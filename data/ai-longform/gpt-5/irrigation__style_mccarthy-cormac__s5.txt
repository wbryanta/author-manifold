They came up the lane before the sun was level with the cottonwoods and the dust hung behind the pickup in a pale ribbon and the air smelled of iron and of dry weeds cut long ago. The orchard lay mute in the slope of the ground and the trees raised their tired crowns from the scabbed earth and the leaves were dusty and curled and you could see in the thin wind the finer powders lift and go and not return. A hawk turned and unturned in the blue and there was no sound but the sound of the tires on the washboard and the engine’s last mutter when he killed it and they sat for a moment and were still.

Joel stepped out and looked down the rows and he turned and took two shovels from the bed and a coil of hard black line and a sack with couplings and a can of purple primer and the blue glue that would burn your hands. Levi came around and he shut the tailgate with his palm and he stood with his hat brim low and his eyes narrow as if to read the orchard for signs that were not there to be read.

He said all right.

Joel nodded.

They walked in among the trees and the ground told where the water had run in other years and where it had not and the dust in those places was not dust but a sort of scurf and if you pressed it it took the print of your boot and did not spring back. The sky was white toward the east and the cottonwoods at the creek were green in a way the orchard could no longer manage and the creek itself was a little mirror stretched thin and gray and not saying much.

The pump house was cinderblock and the plywood door had swelled and stuck and Levi put his shoulder to it and it came and they went in and it was cool there and smelled of mice and paint and old water. The pressure tank rose like a fat blue bottle in the dim light and the pressure gauge with its cracked face had stopped some months ago and lied to whoever cared to be lied to and said thirty two and meant nothing. The switch for the pump was on the wall and the relay with its bronze contacts had been filed straight sometime and it would arc and spit when it closed and a small black scorch showed there like a tiny fire had been set and quenched again and again. On the shelf were jars of rusty screws and nails and a hammer with the head wired on and an oil filter still in its box for a truck that did not run and a leather glove without a mate gone dark with a history of oil.

Joel opened the valve and the old stem creaked like wood. He said you see that?

Levi did not speak.

The sound came in the line like something traveling down a long pipe in the night and finding its way to you only by that small hesitancy in the ground and under the trees a dozen intervals there came a spitting and a coughing as if things long dry and at peace were asked to quicken and would not and were asked again. They listened and then they heard it at once, the suck and blow and the small sharp hiss that is a break under pressure and they stepped out into the heat rising.

There it is, Joel said.

He pointed and the dirt there was darker and a little plume stood like a thin white flag and fell and it was a waste and they both knew it.

Levi stood a moment and then he stepped off the grassless wheel track and walked to the break and he stretched the toe of his boot and nicked away the dry crust and the water came up and darkened the leather and a smell rose that had been laid away for years because the ground had held it and then it was let out like a ghost. He set the shovel and drove the blade with his heel and the ground was so hard that the shovel bounced and rang and he set it again and leaned and the handle groaned and the dry ground cracked like pottery. Joel came and they dug in their shirtsleeves and the sun made little hot marks along their shoulders where the shirt moved and rolled and the wind was a listless thing that came and went like a set of brief and undirected thoughts.

They took turns on the spade and when the hole was wide enough Joel knelt and reached and the water broke over his forearm and he winced and fished in the silt for the line and found it and lifted it and it was like a bone in the ground wet and slick. He saw the gnaw and the toothmarks. Levi had seen it too.

Gopher done it, Joel said.

He held the line up and it drooled and hissed and he closed the end with his thumb and the force was like a small animal. The gnawed part was chewed to lace and the marks were twin and neat.

How a man can hate a thing he aint never seen, Levi said.

You seen em. You just dont see em when you want.

Joel let the line fall and it spit and then laid. He reached and sawed the line clean with the hacksaw and the grit screamed in the teeth and he wiped the edges with his palm and the grit cut him and he did not take notice and he purpled the ends and the smell cut the air and he slid on the coupling and he blue glued it and turned it so that it wouldnt back and the glue flowed and pooled and he wiped it with an old rag and the rag grew stiff with it. He said turn it a lick.

Levi went to the pump house and threw the valve just a hair and the line filled and tightened and the coupling held and stuttered and then was sound and the water went on down the row. He came back and they stood and watched it wet the mounded basins under the trees and watched the dry earth turn and the dust die and the smell that rose was like bread in a poor house. The tree there it was a peach and its leaves hung like tired hands and the fruit set were small and bitter and stonehard and the skin of them was fuzzy and gray with dust and you could put your thumb to it and it would not give.

Levi said you think it'll throw this year?

Joel said it aint never thrown since he went.

He meant their father and Levi knew it and he nodded a little and did not look at him.

They moved down the rows and there were other leaks and the gophers had not done all of them. In some places the roots had reached and lifted and pushed and the line had risen and sun made it brittle and wind and time had set their fangs and would not quit even now. They dug and cut and glued and the glue got on Joel’s hands and made a green shine like an oil slick and then turned brown and flaked. The sun rose and the world’s edge went white and the tank in the pump house kicked and the relay spat and snapped and went on again.

In the third row there was a break where the line had been joined perhaps three or ten years ago and the coupling had a hairline in it that only told when it was made to and Levi lay in the dust and he turned the thing to light and he could see the crack and it was like a fault in a plate and it said that under some pressure that the hand could not provide it would part. He said this here aint worth it. He tossed the coupling and it lay there like a shot rabbit.

Joel looked at him and then looked past him and there was a hawk lower now and on the wire and the hawk’s eye was black and it saw them and did not see them and it caught some small brown thing and lifted and the small brown thing’s legs ran in the air. There is rectitude in the hawk and no apology and there is a mercy past any telling because there is no place there for blame to rest. Joel watched the hawk go and he wiped his hand and he took the new coupling from the sack and he primed and he glued and he set it.

Levi went and leaned on his shovel and looked at the house on the knoll. The house was clapboard gone silver and patched with new mismatched pine where rain had chewed the old boards and the roof shingles lay like scales and lifted at the edges and the window screens were patched with duct tape and thread and wire. The big locust there had split and was braced with a timber and the timber had sunk and the brace was now a fiction and every wind from the west told it so. He stood and looked and he said you know he would have sold it if he had of had the money they pestered him for back then.

Joel said he would not.

You dont know it.

I do know it.

He would not have had nothing to look at here. You think he would set and watch some other fool water his peaches.

He would have followed the money like any man.

Joel clipped the last of the line and tapped it and it sung dull and he tipped his hat back and his hairline showed creased and bright and a white salt lay there and turned more white in the light. He said you say it so you can hear it said. That dont make it true.

You want it to be a monument to him. You want it to be like a grave you can walk.

You talk like you aint never lived here.

I aint blind.

He aint asking you to be blind. He is not asking nothing.

Levi took off his hat and ran the back of his wrist across his forehead and a black smear there made his skin that much whiter and his hair was gray where the sweat had darked it and he put the hat back on and he said you know you cant keep it, Joel. You know you cant stand up to what it will take.

Joel crouched and he did not look up and he said what it takes is work. Not all that other.

You think I aint worked.

I didnt say that.

Levi quit leaning the shovel and he stuck it and dust rose in a little ring and fell. He said I got the paper in the truck. The lawyer says we stand to do better if we dont wait.

Joel looked at him then. He said you brought it out here.

I brought it because you dont never come into town and you dont never answer a thing you dont have to and because you will not sit still across a desk from a man and hear what it is he has to tell you and so here it is the whole desk right in the bed of my truck if you like.

I dont.

Levi made a small sound not laughter and not anything else and he kicked at the dirt and a bug there that had died and gone dry and hard he sent it skeetering and Joel saw it and he felt a thing in his chest like a bird trying to get out and he stood and he said he aint cold yet and you are ready to sell.

He is cold, Levi said. He is cold and in the ground and the ground aint even took to him good and yet.

And yet.

And yet you are going to stand here and make a song about it. You got any money.

I got the money I got.

That aint money, Joel. The bank aint going to take what you feel for tender. The water people got theirs last week and the lights the week before that and you think a man can eat on principled notions and be full.

Joel said what kind of man is it eats and is full in a house he paid for with his father’s bones.

Levi looked at him. He had gray eyes and one of them had a little cap of darker color like a storm at its edge and he had had that since he was a boy and their grandmother had said it meant something and no one now remembered what she had said or she had said it to keep children quiet. He said you talk like he never borrowed a dollar. You talk like the man never put up the place for collateral when he thought he had to. I aint the devil you make me to be here. We aint in a book.

Joel said I know what we aint in.

They worked on and the sun passed so that the shadows under the trees now fell on the wrong side and to the west the clouds had come up like animals that had been sleeping in the grass and then rose and shook themselves and said their piece and the wind that came smelled of iron and a little of rain and brought no rain. When they came to the end of the fifth row Joel crouched and looked along the black line and there was a fine mist there that you would not see if the sun were in a different quarter and he put his hand out and the cool touched his skin in a place he had not thought to be touched that day and he smiled and he looked at Levi and Levi did not return it.

At noon they went to the truck and sat in the shade of it and the shade was a poor thing and it crawled under them like a thin dog and would not stay. Joel got out the old tacklebox with the latch broke and inside were two sandwiches the bread wet and meat with the fat gone see through and a green bottle of tea and a warm waxen apple and a pack of crackers. They ate in quiet and the flies found them and went off again to look for better work. Road dust drifted and hung and fell like snow. In the ditch a lizard ran like the hand of a clock when no one is watching and stopped as soon as you did.

Levi chewed and spit a seed. He said Marie called me. She said the roof in the back bedroom is started in. She says it will not make the winter.

Joel drank the tea and it had turned and he did not show it and he said I can get tar.

Tar aint a roof.

You got any other ideas.

Sell it.

Joel said you dont quit.

Levi put the last of the bread in his mouth and chewed and swallowed and he looked toward the far end of the orchard where the ground fell off and in the low place there was the beginnings of reed and willow and the pastureland beyond was a tan hide with the bone of the earth up under it and in the far distance a white house new and with a roof that all lay as if it had been fitted by an eye and not a hand and a car there in the drive stood with a shimmer about it like it was a hot thing in itself. He said you see that.

I seen it.

They will keep coming. They will come until they are here and past here and you can either sell to a man will walk the ground with a hat on and ask you what name the trees had when you were young or you can sell to men will take a dozer to it and make it into lines on a paper and they will conjure houses up where these stand and those houses will throw off light in the night like a mosquito hatch and it will be done. There aint a third way.

Joel said there is a third way. You stay.

You stay and do what, Joel.

You make it throw again.

It aint going to throw like it threw.

It will or we will all break our hands on it and it will know we tried.

It wont know and it wont care. You set a dollar in the dirt and plant it and water it and sing it and in the end it is a soft green thing and then it is brown and then it is back to dirt and you are a fool. This is the state of it. Scarcely even a sorrow can be wrung from that. Only a sort of grim mirth maybe.

Joel looked at his hands and his nails were black with primer clotted and glue and his knuckles were cut and bright. He said you remember him out here in the light with that old cap on. He would raise his head when the pump went and know if it was right or not by the song and he could tell the morning by the way the trees moved their leaves and if there was water in the ditch without looking at it he would feel it in his teeth.

Levi nodded. He said I remember him standing in the door in the winter with a cup and the frost in his beard and that old coat with the cuffs wore off and he said the frost is a kind of blessing if a man has a mind to take it that way and I did not know what he meant.

Neither did I.

I think maybe he did not either.

Joel smiled and the smile was tired and left him and he looked around and he stood and said all right.

Levi said all right what.

All right to your paper.

Levi wiped his hands on his jeans and he said well. He looked at Joel. He said we can go up and look it over.

Not now, Joel said.

Then when.

You put it back in the truck and you leave it there and when the day gets done if I can look at the lines and know they are sound to the degree that a man can know I will take it out and look at what it is you have got to show me and if I cannot then you can take it and drive it to town and put it on the desk for all the damned lawyers to shuffle and you can tell them that your brother is a fool and a stubborn man and he will not sign and they can set about whatever the law will do to a fool who will not sign and we will see.

Levi said that aint a plan.

It is the plan I got.

Levi did not speak and the two of them left the shade and walked the rows again and there were more breaks and there were places where the water went and did not stay and in those places the ground was all rocks and light and at the ends of the lines were valves with the old plastic handles that had cracked and gone to white crystal and cut your palm like a bottlebottom. They opened and shut them and the water bled toward the creek and in the lower pasture a few cattle stood with their heads low and their sides heaved and the flies were a black seam along their eyes and in the shade of the hedge at the fence a dog lay and he was old and had the eyes of a man who expected nothing further and he watched them come and he watched them go and his tail made a halfhearted broom in the dead grass as though to say he knew the forms but lacked the energy for the ceremony.

They came to a place where the line had burst not with the small malice of teeth but with a sudden failure and the water there had cut a small arroyo and the edges of it were undercut and hung and the little rivulets ran and made their tiny statements and then were gone. Joel stood and watched it and he shook his head and he waded in and bent and felt and his shirt turned black and weighed him down and the line had blown at a glued joint and the stub there was like the stump of a limb a saw had taken long ago and had not been finished proper. He shut the water to it and the last of the leak bled out and returned to the earth and he cut it back and squared it and he set the new parts and held them and the glue burned and his eye watered and the wind had gone down and the orchard was loud with its quiet and with the little rattling in the leaves and the calls of small birds without belief. He stood and wiped his face and Levi was gone.

He looked up and saw him standing at the fence looking toward the road and a dust there and at the head of the dust a white truck and in the bed of the truck were boards and a ladder and the man driving wore a white hat and he steered with his arm out and at the gate he stopped and stepped out. He was not young and not old and his face was the color of dirty cotton and his hands were fine and did not show work or they showed a different kind of work than this. Levi went and opened the wire and the man came through and he said something that Joel could not hear and he stopped and set the brake and turned and pitched a look down the rows as if he’d come to buy melons and had found nothing ripe.

Joel stood still. The man came up and the man smiled and he looked at the wet knees of Joel’s jeans and the mud on his shirt.

I been trying to get a hold of yall, the man said.

Levi said I told you I would bring it to him.

Joel said I aint never seen your face and I aint lost nothing for you to find.

The man held up a hand in an open way and nodded as if to some music. He said now I aint come to blow the dust with you today. I told your brother that I will not press a man who has got his hands full and that aint my way and if you know me you know it. I come out because I was already in the vicinity and I thought I would set eyes on what it is we been talking around.

We aint been talking around it, Levi said. We been talking right at it.

Joel said we aint been doing nothing with you. You and he aint me.

Now that is so and I will not argue the point. My name is Sloane.

All right, Joel said.

All right.

He looked past them and looked at the house and at the barn with its tin roof gone brown with the years and at the windmill that had not turned since his father’s father’s day and he looked at the cottonwoods and the creek and the far pasture a dun quilt with the threadbare parts showing and he looked at the layers of old machinery in the shade by the barn that had been sat down where it failed and had become a species of iron serpentine there and he nodded again as if to that same inner music.

He said I put a number down and your brother has seen it and he may not have told you all of it. He may. It is not my place. There is a price and there are terms. You are holding paper now that is heavier than what I give you and we can make it so you will not be leaned on any longer. You got repairs here that you will not do and I do not say that as any manner of slight but as a man who has seen what you got to contend with. These trees are not the men they were.

Joel said I am listening to you but I hear my brother.

Sloane nodded. He said I hear him too. I like him. He has got the measure of this place. I told him I would keep the house up if you wanted to stay for a time. I told him he could come out and walk about like all them old men do on pieces they once called their own and I dont reckon I will run him off and I dont reckon the law will allow me even if I had a mind to, which I dont. I told him I would see to it that the graves out behind the cottonwood would be left unmolested and not built over and he could come and tend them if that is the kind of thing he has a mind to do. I am a man with his own people to lay up come the time and I dont see any profit in stepping on yours.

Joel looked at him a long time and then he looked at Levi and Levi’s expression was a mask that would not presently let in a light. He said you keep the orchard.

Sloane smiled and the smile by now had the look of a habit that he had learned and had found some small practical use in. He said I am going to put houses upon it Mr Joel. I will put them with a care and I will put them for people that may not deserve a thing and yet will get something nonetheless and that is an economy that is bigger than either of us and I do not say this to pluck at your strings but that you might not make for yourself a story that will not hold up in the night.

Joel said you will cut them down.

Yes.

I will not be a party.

He looked at Levi as he said it and he said it without anger and it landed as it would land.

Sloane looked from one to the other. He turned his hat in his hands and put it back on and tilted the brim. He said all right. I will leave you to your deliberation. I aint a man to stick where I aint wanted. But yall know where to find me and it aint on no map. I am in the place where the ledger stays open past dark. He smiled and he reached and tapped the brim and he walked back and he climbed into his truck and he turned it and went and Levi closed the wire and stood looking after and then he turned and came back and he did not come all at once but went and stooped and picked something up and set it on the fence and then came on a few steps and stopped again and Joel watched him and did not move.

You could have said nothing, Levi said.

I said what was true.

He said names. Remember how he did. He named the trees like they were hounds and some were old men he knew and some were the names of things you dont call a tree. He used to say go take a bucket and go give Lazarus a drink and I would not know what he meant. I thought as a boy that the man had more names in his head than a schoolteacher and more nonsense than a parson. He laughed when he said it and it was not a laugh that sat good in any ear but his. He made a music to it.

I remember.

Levi looked away and then he looked back. He said this aint that time. We are the men here and there aint another one coming if we call.

Joel turned back to the line and he put his finger on the glue and it had set. He wiped his hands and he said that is why I am here too. Now shut the valve at the head and we will hold the new. He went and knelt and he pushed the line and the glue squeaked and held and he looked up at Levi and Levi nodded and walked off and the sun had moved again and the light in the world had changed so that the color of things had flattened and it was a hard light and without depth to it and he heard the pump howl and catch and the line twitched and took to life and then stilled and the water ran and down the row it ran in its careful quiet and around the trees and through the cracked earth and down into the small places where it could hide from the need.

They worked in that heat and in that light and they wore the day down like a man wears a stone in his pocket when he is worried in the dark and must worry at something and cannot be still and the stone rubs against his thigh and there is balm somehow in that and lesson. Joel felt his back and his shoulders and in his mouth the dry copper taste and he spit and the spit was brown. He thought of nothing and he thought of everything. He thought of his father once standing in that very place and looking at the ground and waiting as if to hear a voice and there was none and then the voice came anyway and it was his own and he said a small word to himself and went on. He pictured in his mind the house pulled down and the trees pulled down and the ground scraped and measured and marked in chalk and stakes and string like the territory for a show that had not yet come and the day of the show a hundred trucks and men in hats not like his walking and pointing and calling to each other and the air hot and the beeping of a machine and the push of a blade and the earth’s own smell tore free and thrown up to the indifferent air and it all troubled him and he did not know how to hold it in his head and not let it break him.

Toward afternoon in the last row they found the line buried deeper than the rest and the break in it was under a root like the white bone of a deer lodged half in the dirt and the root ran like a rib and had the slow stubborn true of a thing that had not moved for fifty years and would not move now and Joel dug along it and he cut and pared and the root bled its thin brown water and smelt of tannin and he said sorry to the tree and Levi looked at him and raised his eyebrows and said nothing and Joel cut the line in on the far side and fed it through and made it whole. He said I aint going to sign.

Levi did not speak.

He said I will go to town and stand there and tell them I will not and they will do what they do when a man says that to them and you can go with me or not.

Levi put his shovel down and came and knelt across from him and their heads were near enough that each could see the sweat like rain on the other and the small scars that boys get and never think of again until one day they are old men themselves and must explain to a doctor the star on the leg or the line under the chin and he looked at his brother a long time and he said people go into town and they stand still and they say all manner of thing and the men at the desk with the ties who know which drawer the paper is in stand and they say something back and there is a flurry and then there is not and the man goes out and gets in his car and he lays his head on the wheel because his heart is a little seized or he weeps because of the shame and he drives home and the man in the tie drinks a soda and eats a candy bar and orders more paper because they are out. There aint no change that comes of that.

I aint going for change. I am going for the sake of it.

Levi nodded as if to say that the sake of a thing had its place same as a lid on a jar has its place and he would not be the one to break that news to anyone. He stood and he said do what you got to do. But come tonight you sign or you dont. There aint no half a thing here.

They finished the rows and in the low places in the shade there was a slow and certain cold that had not been there in the heat of the day and in other places the heat rose off the ground with a sound to it you would swear though you knew no such thing could be. In the pump house the relay clicked and clicked and the pump thudded and even with the new you could hear the old in it and every time it started Joel thought perhaps now and then it did not and then after all it did and you could count the starts and know to the day when it would quit and you would be standing there with your hands in your pockets watching and it would be what it would be and no part of you would be able to remedy a thing.

They went to the truck in the evening and the sky to the west was a yellow leather drawn tight and beyond that a bruise of blue and low and the cottonwoods made a black cut there and the first bats moved and Levi took the manila folder from behind the seat and he set it on the hood and smoothed it with his palm and the paper inside slid like pigeons. Joel stood with his hands at his sides and he looked and did not look and he had the feeling of a child who is brought before a thing and told that it is his and he cannot fathom what that could mean.

Levi opened it and he laid out the pages and the wind would have had them but he had set a wrench and a pipe dope can and a piece of scrap iron there and they stayed. He said this is the number. This here is the date. These are the ways the money goes where it has to go. These are the parts that say it will be as said.

Joel read or he let the lines move before his eye and he did not read and he heard in the high cottonwoods the sound of the leaves and the first nightcreatures and he heard in the ditch the soft talk of the little water there and the words lifted like birds and were gone.

Levi said not now then?

Joel took the pen. He held it like a thing he had not been taught was not a weapon and he turned it and rolled it and then he set it down. He said I got to think. Give me till morning.

In the morning it will not be any different, Levi said.

That aint the point.

Levi gathered the papers and put them away. He shut the hood and he set his hands there and leaned and looked off. He said you recall that time he made us burn the brush pile down here us two and he said dont neither of you put a match to it when it is like this and then he lit it anyway and it went off like a thing that was always supposed to go off and we had to carry water in them old buckets that leaked and the wind turned and it come back and took half the fence and the edge of the field and we thought we had done the end of whatever we would ever do and would be beat to bone and he come down and looked and said well I told you didnt I and then he smiled and he never did one thing to us. He said you cant say you aint been warned and that was what he called it square. His warning. Meaning to himself. I think. Meaning to a thing bigger than himself if you had a man in mind. He said it like a prayer. You cant say you aint been warned.

Joel nodded.

Levi pushed off the hood and then he stopped and he cocked his head and he said you hear that.

From the pump house came a sound like a man coughing in a small tiled room. Joel straightened. He said hell.

They went at a fast lope and the pump house was dark and the relay clicked and sputtered and there was a smell of scorched insulation and Joel hit the switch and the relay asked once and Joel hit the switch again and the noise quit and there was a small fine smoke and there was a silence so sudden that the world seemed to step back from it. He stood with his hand on the switch and his arm shook and he was not able to still it.

Levi put his hands on his knees and bent and then stood and then smiled and it was not like any smile he had given that day and he shook his head. He said that aint a message from nobody but it sure comes like one.

We can pull the cover and file the points and make it go.

We can, Levi said. And then we can do it again on some other Tuesday and again and again till we are the boys we were.

They stood a moment and then Joel reached and opened the breaker and the dark there deepened to the eye though nothing about it had altered but the mind and the birds that had nested in the rafters shifted and made small voice.

I aint signing, Joel said into that small house. He said it again.

Levi listened and at last he nodded. He said all right. He walked out and Joel followed. The light outside had gone the good late kind that makes a mercy of all things and in that light the orchard did not look as condemned as it had and the house did not look so poor but looked like a line someone had drawn between the open plain of things that were and the small country of a man’s will and someone had to stand there or else the line would vanish and not because it should.

They drove into town in the dark and the highway ran before them in a black band and the white lines came and went and they did not say a thing and the world on both sides of the truck had all gone to silhouette and the lights of the houses were out there in their little human pattern and the moon came up behind and then slid off and did what a moon does with no quarrel.

They went to the diner first because the lawyer was home and would stay that way and the diner was near enough to a temple as they allowed themselves. The woman there brought coffee and two plates and meat that had the look of meat and the taste of salt and they ate and looked at their hands and looked at nothing. While they were there a boy came in with a girl and they laughed as they came and they slid into a booth and she looked at him and he looked at her like they were the only two people in the world and they believed it and it was not wholly wrong and Joel felt a thing in him that a man should not talk about and has no words for anyway.

After a time Levi set his cup down and he said we will tell him to go to hell then.

Joel nodded.

All right.

They left money on the table and walked out and the bell above the door rang and the air on the street smelled of hot tar cooling and of riverwater and of lilac and a dog somewhere barked not in anger but in declaration and was answered and the town held them as a town will hold you when you are born to it and know its stones and its shade and have bled at its corners and in winter have walked its length with a scarf drawn up and the skin of your throat raw and on some summer day slapped your friend there and been slapped back and laughed and run. They stood a moment and then went and found the truck and got in and drove home.

The night at the orchard was full and not in any way that a man could explain. They walked out to the pump house and Joel pulled the cover and with the light of a small failing flashlight he filed the points and blew the filings with his breath and put the cover back and threw the switch and the relay clicked and the pump came and the old noise came and went steadier and he stood and watched the gauge that did not mean and he smiled and he went and heard the water in the lines talk like a thing returned and he shut two valves and opened three and he stood under the tree named for a grandfather’s joke and he looked up into the dark until his neck hurt and he did not know if he wept or if the sweat went and he did not care. Levi sat in the door of the pump house and did not speak and you could see the red of his cigarette down inside like a little watching animal and the smoke blew out and then was nothing and the night was left whole.

They did not look at the manila folder under the seat and they did not sleep but in the morning when the sun came like a blade they were already up and the line had held in some places and not in others and they did not speak and they went and fetched the shovels and set about it as if they had always and forever and would not stop till bid. The hawk came and went and a coyote showed and passed like a shadow and the dog by the barn raised his head and set it down again. The world said as it always said a word that men only hear if it is their time and will not otherwise and to some it would have sounded like sell and to some it would have sounded like stay and the truth was both and neither and the wind that came after noon smelled of places that were not there and never had been and could not be and it passed across their faces and went on. They worked, and the water that ran in the black lines was not theirs and was and there was no reckoning it. If a man could have stood looking from the edge of that place as the day waned he would have seen two figures bending and straightening and carrying and pouring and lifting and walking and the dust rose around them like breath and fell and was nothing and they were nothing and they were everything the world would bear witness to and it was enough.
