By the time the ferry lets go of the bollards and its ropes slither down into the seawater like shed snakes, there is a thin glaze of frost on the painted railings and the breath of the deckhands comes out in small private clouds. The gulls, who have been sitting on the neighboring pierposts like elderly judges, lift and wheel and cry, then settle again as if nothing has changed. Diesel hum fills the hull and climbs into the bones. People are still finding seats, still lining up at the cafeteria to buy white mugs of coffee and cardboard boats of chips for their children who are not yet tired enough to lie down. I stand along the starboard side a while with my hands in my pockets, the left one curled tight around my phone, as if the cold glass could anchor me. The town slips backward along the edge of the channel. The orange sodium of the wharf lights smears on the black water and a sharp wind pulls at the fringe of my scarf and makes my eyes run.

I hadn't planned to be here. That is the sentence my mind repeats with every small movement of the ship through the harbor, with every creak of the steel and clatter of a loose chain, with each long horn that rolls over the roofs and away into the hills. There is a way in which any journey starts months earlier than it seems, with the first life you did not answer, the first letter you threw out, the first thought you did not allow. And then there is a more literal way, where a man with a damp voice and a name I did not catch phones you on a Tuesday at two in the afternoon and says, am I speaking to Simon? and you cannot recognize the tone of your own voice when you say, yes.

He said there had been a body. He said it had washed up on the stones near the old factory site, half along a ledge and half in the water, the tide giving it back and then trying to take it again. A male, early forties, no wallet, no keys, no rings, which was its own kind of saying. No one on the island had been reported missing this week or last. There was a list of names in the drawer. There was my name at the top of that paper, penciled in by a woman with small arms crossed tight against the draft of an outer door. No one had known where else to look, he said, and the screen door behind him had lifted and let go and lifted and let go and the sound of this — snapping, easy, like a broken heartbeat — had made me think of a place I had not stood in for a long time.

I said, I don't know what you want me to do. He said, if you can take the ferry, if you can come up overnight, we'd like you to look. He said a name I had not heard out loud in twelve years, one syllable, clean as a thrown stone. I stood there with the phone buzzing warm against my palm because I had not noticed it continuing to vibrate long after he'd stopped speaking, because I had forgotten to breathe, because names come with doors attached to them and if you open them the air will change.

An hour later I was putting thick socks in a bag, as if socks could decide anything, and my neighbor was waving across the low hedge in her sensible coat. The frost last night, she said, her voice fogging to the same gray as the afternoon. I said yes and drove to the terminal and lined up with the men in high-vis who have started taking their wallets out already and the women with elastic-sided boots and the tourists who are always surprised it is this cold. By the time I had found my place by the rail we were reversing out into the strait, the slap of the bow waves loud under the rounded snout of the hull, the town growing miniscule, then another series of blinking lights replacing it, and then only the dark and the lights of the next boat laboring through it.

Other people's journeys are easy to understand from a distance. I can look at the dozen or so juggernauts chained in the loading bay, the way their beds shine with wet, and imagine where they were six hours ago and where they'll be in six, the slow patience of movement across dark water and then across a winter road. I can watch a woman in a red hat read to a boy on a molded plastic seat and know as a fact that the boy will fall asleep on the consonants and the woman will carry him to the cabin and lay him down and sit there a while with the engine's thrum under her, her throat thick with relief that she does not have to answer any more why tonight. I do not travel lightly the way I did at twenty. Too much has been hung on any one decision, the weight of what you might bring back with you.

Inside the cafeteria, there is heat that smells of chips and onion and something burned up long before any of us got here. I take a mug of tea that is too sweet because the woman at the counter is distracted, her hair pulled back with a red band and a rune tattoo peeking at the edge of her sleeve, and I do not correct her. There are slot machines against the end wall whose lights flash for no one. There are the same photographs of humpbacks and keels and winter storms that always hang on walls like this, printed out on good paper and matted in white so they look like proof. I carry my tea to the far side and sit at a place where I can see both the gangway and the glassed-in darkness beyond it.

He would have told me to go to the bar, if he'd been here, or if he'd known how to ask for what he wanted. He would have said, this tea is going to be a film in your mouth for hours, let's go somewhere with beer on the carpet and a man with a knife tattoo on his neck. He would have known the fastest route to the door everyone else was avoiding. He was like that, my brother. Or he was like that until he wasn't. He could get into places that were closed for the night because he had a gift for making a bouncer believe that what was about to happen was going to be a favor for both of them. He could make a mistake look like a plan if he smiled at you and asked you about your day and made that look like something he actually wanted to know. The first time he left home he wrote me a note on the back of a shopping list (bread, milk, sponges) that said he would be gone three days and that I should cover for him with Mum and that he was going east for the sea. He came back after five days with a cut over his eye and a smell of wood smoke that wouldn't lift out of his hair with shampoo for weeks, and he looked older by some measure that had nothing to do with the way his bones fit together.

We were not unalike in childhood. We sat under the same kitchen table and watched the same mouse run from the bread bin to the oven and we held each other's hands the first time the lighthouse beam swung across the living room in winter in such a way that every picture frame made a white eye. I was the one who remembered when gym days were, the one who bought the ticket the day of the exam. He was the one who cut the legs off broken chairs and used them as giant pencils to draw circles in the dirt so big you couldn't see the circumference. He once put a spoon in his pocket for a week to see if he could learn the pocket without looking. He once held his breath until he went blue while lying on the bottom of the bath and smiled up at me through the water when I banged on the tiled wall and shouted, come up. We learned our father like other boys learn weather. He was there and then he wasn't and then he came in through the door like a low pressure system that surprises the man in the slicker on the pier.

It's strange how you can sit with a hot mug in your hand and see it as if your hand belongs to someone else. It's stranger that you can breathe in the same rhythm as the sea and know that you have that in common. It's stranger still to think about what is left when a person who looks like you has been put in a drawer and covered with a white sheet in a room in which the windows are painted shut. I stared into my tea and put my tongue against the metal rim of the cup and tasted a ghost of something bloody where my tooth had left a nick there years ago. After a while the tea got cold.

Back outside there was more dark than whatever is one step up from dark. The ferry has a way of concluding the harbor behind it, a way of making the land recede with a speed that looks like mercy. I stood by a coil of rope and watched another sea go under us and then slide away and thought of the way he would stand with his left shoulder slightly forward, as if he were about to walk right through the person in front of him. I thought of the last time I saw him in a real room under a real light.

It was after the funeral when the house was a small library of other people's sympathy and our mother was wearing a cardigan that had belonged to her grandmother and that she had set aside for years for this very thing, as if by saving the garment she could hold off the occasion. The minister had said words about our father I did not recognize but could not contest and the kitchen was full of paper plates and the smell of ham. I had driven from the city and had not slept more than a few hours a night in the month since the call and there was something in me frayed to the point of light. He came through the back door at four, five hours late, his eyes bright, his mouth already ready for a fight. I could smell the kind of liquor that is poured with one hand while the other is already looking for a fight in a pocket. He leaned on the sink as if he meant to embrace it and looked out at the clothesline like it was someone who owed him an apology and said, so this is how we do it now, and I misunderstood everything that followed.

The thing about fights is that they have a topography. There are small paths in a ridge of land that you know from childhood, and then there is the way those paths make the high walk easier and the gorge in between deeper and the fall harder when it comes. He said I had left first. I said he had left worst. He said at least he had come back. I said he had a genius for scheduling. He said our father had not been a monster on purpose. I said he had been a monster because he had an appetite and a bottle. He said I had always been Mum's son. I said that had saved him too. He pushed plates to the floor and a fork that had belonged to our great-grandmother went skittering under the table and he said, you don't know me and never have, and I said, that is the one thing you have made easy, and there it was. He looked at me with something that was part film of spilt beer, part boy who had once built a fort with me on a day that rained non-stop for seventy-nine hours, and he lifted his mouth into a shape that might have been laughter and then he went out the door. He did not change his mind and come back in. He moved to the coast to work on the boats for a while and someone wrote me a letter about seeing him in a bar out near the headland and then, the letters stopped.

A man in a yellow coat brushed past me with a hook on the end of a pole and said, watch your sleeve. I looked down and saw that I had got oil on the cuff, a crescent the size and shape of a bruised moon, and I rubbed at it with my thumb and made it worse. The ferry slid into a part of the strait where the navigation lights seem too few and the sense of how deep it is under us makes the skin at the back of your knees ache. You could lose anything out here. You could drop your name into this water and it would go down with everything else and you would stand there on metal with wind in your hair and be the same and not the same.

There is a place on these ferries that is sheltered from most of the wind, between two stacks of life rafts lashed in white cocoons. Three teenagers in black coats with studs in the lobes of their ears and a tall tin between them had found it. One of them looked at me because I had become, to them, middle age all at once when I put my hand out against the wall to steady myself around a corner. He said something I could not hear and then he looked at his friend and they both laughed softly and I felt a pang of something that was not embarrassment. It was the inverse of envy. It was like experiencing the cold you once stood up to and realizing you cannot stand up to it now, at least not in the same way, not without putting on a hat. I raised the mug in a small salute and they looked at me with a shared glance that said, in ten minutes the drink is going to make us warm, we do not need your approval.

The tannoy cracked into life and told us, in a voice that was careful not to sound weary, that the bar would close in one hour, that the gift shop was open, that breakfast would be served from five thirty, that due to weather we might be fifteen minutes late, that we should stay off the vehicle decks until told otherwise. The voice had the accent I had forgotten he had before he rounded it off with years away. I could still hear him saying, as a joke in his young years but with a thread of actual warning, that up north you always bring your coat. If you'd asked him why he always spoke of there as if it were a separate country, he would have said because in a place like that you stand farther apart. He would have said it to make me argue with him. He would have liked that I couldn't outrun him then.

In the gift shop there were calendars of harbors and keychains with small green islands made of soft plastic. I stood there without a reason and picked up a pack of playing cards that had whales on them and put them down again. A girl of eight with a purple knit hat above eyes that would have made an old woman stop in the street said to her mother, do whales sleep, and her mother said, probably not like you do, and the girl said, do they dream then, and the woman said, one can hope. If we were standing in a different story I would have followed the urge to say to the girl, yes, they do, in nets of slow blue. But we were standing in this one, and I had a job to do, and the cards made me think of other decks of cards and of how he would cheat with finesse and a look of sweet concern on his face and laugh when you caught him because you were supposed to have caught him. I left with my hands empty.

My cabin smelled faintly of damp fabric and the lemonish detergent they use for the blankets. It had two bunks, and above the lower one a poster with the names of constellations I could never see through the deck lamps at sea. On the table bolted to the floor was a folded-up sheet of instructions about donning a life jacket. The instructions had been translated into four languages and close reading revealed that in one of them the word for straps had been replaced by string. I lay down without undressing and waited for the lights that do not turn off for anyone to take away their edge. The movement of the ferry was easy at first. We moved through a part of the strait that had no interest in noticing us, then into a place that wanted to turn our backs to itself and see what happened. The engine changed its tone the way a man clears his throat before he says the thing he has been thinking. Somewhere above me heeled steel squealed against a chain. The smallest rattle from the second bunk struck a spot in my head that I did not know could ache. I closed my eyes and the shapes on the poster blurred into any sky.

I dreamt like an old dog, I always do on these crossings, with my feet twitching. I dreamt a beach of black stones that clacked like teeth when the water ran out of them. I dreamt of kneeling there and trying to gather flat skipping stones when no stone would stay in my hand. He was there, always fifteen in my mind even when we were forty, his hair still too long over one eye. He said, throw it with your whole arm, no, your shoulder, no, from your ribs. I said, you can't teach me how to be someone who never quite leaves. He looked up, and the sky had the pale pale blue of a month of Sundays when we were seven and eight and we had permission to get lost in the cow field as long as we promised somehow to bring each other home. He said, I would have, you know, if it had been me, and I said, would have what, and he did not answer because the stipulations were already written down somewhere by someone else and we were actors who didn't know the plot. I woke with the engine's throb feeling like fingers pressed lightly around my throat and turned over and lay there until the insides of my arms went cold.

At five the tannoy said we could have breakfast. I got up and brushed my teeth with water that came out of the tap faster than I expected and spit into a small stapled-together metal sink, and shaved badly because the ship's mirror does not want you to be your own enemy in its eyes. The cafeteria was a line of men with red faces holding toast on cardboard plates. There were eggs that shone like they were made of lacquer, bacon with its edges curled, mushrooms that had given up, sausages making small greasy films that will outlive all of us. A truck driver with a T-shirt that said DO NOT DISCUSS FAMILY OR THE DOG had a tray with three of everything. I took a bowl of porridge that had spent the night in its own company and sat alone under a porthole. Outside the slice of sea that the porthole chose, the water was old pewter hammered thin by someone with long strong arms. A woman across from me stirred tea that did not need stirring. She said to the air, my sister always gets seasick, and then she smiled at me as if I had asked, and I smiled back because sometimes nodding at strangers is all that saves a day. The porridge had the taste of mornings when the window is not yet open and the house thinks no one is awake.

At six the island appeared, not so much out of the dark as out of its own way of not existing until it needs to. The line of low hills ought to have been blue, but they were the gray of exhaustion. The pier was a chip of concrete knocked off a big block by a hammer and somebody's knee. As we came in there was a man in a small fluorescent jacket who waved his arms and then stopped waving them and put them in his pockets and watched us with an expression I could not read. We bumped the last small run of water out from under us and the whole ferry sighed and the ramp went down with a sound like the first time you have to raise your voice to someone you love. Everyone stood up and went looking for their bags in the same two places bags are always not.

There is a police house on the island that is so clean you could eat off the step, but you would not. It is painted with a white that is slightly wrong for the sky and a blue that is right for a bruise. Inside, there is a small lobby where a woman takes calls until her shift ends and then she hands a small notebook to a man with a salmon mustache and says, yours now. He had a name I could not fix. There were signs that asked you to close the door and signs that asked if you needed to talk to someone and a sign that said you would be seen in order. He said my own name and I said yes and he said another name and I winced and he saw that I had and marked it somewhere behind the gray in his eyes. He said, thank you for coming up. He said, I know it's a difficult thing. He said, we can do this quickly if you'd like, or we can sit down a minute first, up to you. His voice did not know how to travel. It stayed a half step behind his face and watched his mouth and did as it was asked.

I sat and signed a line that said I had been offered support and declined it because I am an idiot of a particular kind. He said, while that's being sorted, can we ask a couple of things. Did your brother have any distinguishing marks, burns, tattoos, that sort of thing. I said, he had a tiny scar by his ear where a dog nipped him when he was eleven, but you could only see it if you turned his head a certain way in sunlight. The man nodded as if to say, we can arrange sunlight. He said, anything else. He had a chipped tooth, one of the front ones, not quite in the normal place where everyone chips the same tooth. He had a crescent white scar on his shin from catching a spike when we were building a fence that never got built in the end. I do not know if that helps. He wrote everything down as if the writing would hold the skin to the bone a little longer.

He said, we found him on the west side, by the old works, fishermen from the co-op thought at first it was a seal. It was clear that he had not been in the water long, a night, two. He said, there was no water in the lungs. I know what that means and did not want to know what that means. He said, sometimes in the cold you go in and come out even if you shouldn't. He said, we're not judging any of this. He said, you'll see for yourself. He said, ready?

There is a room in the back that used to be one thing and is now another, which is what death does to rooms. It has green paint and a bench of metal with drain holes, and it is warmer than you expect until you touch anything, at which point everything is colder than your life ever has been. He was there under a sheet that had once been a sheet for sleeping. The man with the mustache stood at my shoulder like a friend you didn't think you had, and the woman from the front came in and stood by the door as if by having someone who had made tea in the room it would help, and a second man I hadn't seen came in and did a thing with a form that required three signatures. He asked me if I was ready and I thought of what that word means now and then I nodded. The man took hold of the top of the sheet and, the way you uncover a cake or reveal a present, he pulled it down.

There are things the sea does to you that have nothing to do with fairy stories about turning everything smooth. It had made his skin colorless, the gray of flour after a mouse has stepped in it. The hair that had been his vanity was there but wrong, matted in directions that argued with one another. His eyes were closed in the way they only are when the muscle has been told not to bother. The lines at the corner of the mouth, those were right. There was a scar by the ear, as if some small dog had understood the script a long time ago. There was a chipped tooth but it was not the one I remembered, and his teeth were the worst of it. There was a whiteness along the gum that did not belong to breathing. The left shoulder lay wrong, which made some small slow part of my head say, there must have been a fight, or a fall. I looked at his hands. They were not the hands of any man I had shaken hands with in twenty years. They were soft in that particular way after being cold and then warmer, and one of the fingernails was split far back and then mended by someone who had not loved him.

If you had brought in a stranger and told me, this is your brother, I would have said, I can see what you mean. If you had brought in my brother two days before the last words and said, this is him, I would have said, thank God and lifted my hands to him and laughed. What stands in a room like that and makes you decide is not wholly what is under the sheet. It is the weight of the air you have breathed under the same roofs. It is the particular letter your mother wrote that summer that he did not answer and the way the paper came back to her a month later with the envelope softened by ten people's fingers. It is the way he used to put his jaw out when he was concentrating. It is the day he stepped off the pier when we were nine and ten and did not come up for so long I saw the shape of the rest of my life like a long corridor until he burst up next to me and howled, and I thumped him with the flat of my hand and he laughed so hard he made other people laugh, too, who were furious at the same time.

I put my hand, because I could not not, on his shoulder. The bone under the blanket was not the shape I remembered under cloth. I put the back of my finger against his cheekbone. The skin was colder than any possible winter and colder than the time we had found a dead thrush on the path and he had said, we should bury it, it'll be a game, and we had taken empty seed packets and cut them into flags and stood with pieced-together sticks as if we were two small embarkations of soldiers. I leaned down because there was something in me that had tried to get up from my ribs and run toward him for so long I had to see if it was going to survive its own habit. I said his name and the way it sounded in a room like that was frankly unacceptable.

The woman by the door said, take your time. The man with the mustache looked at his shoes. I could see on his face that he had other jobs waiting this morning and that he would like this one to be done. I could also see that he had a brother, or had lost one, or had thought about it, because there are places your eyes go that take you to a particular edge. I said, I don't know. He said, I'm sorry. He said, we can give you more time with him, we can ask someone else, we can look for dental records, we can do the other thing. He did not say autopsy. He did not say likely drown or suicide or misadventure because there are rules about not telling a story with your mouth until you have typed it first. I said, he had a chip like that, but not that, and he had that scar, but it was longer, and his shoulder wasn't, or maybe it was, he fell off a fence once, I've told you that, and we both laughed when he lay there because he had tried to ride it and the fence took that personally. The man nodded like a man in a pew.

I went outside to stand by their smoke area where a little bucket of sand had four damp cigarettes stuck in it like the new growth on a tree. The light out there had its own mind. Wind came around the side of the building and went into my clothes and lifted them an inch from my skin and did that again and again until it found a way to me. Across the road, a fish box hung by a nail to the wall with NOTHING WITHOUT A TICKET written across it in blue. A woman with a dog stopped and then went on. I realized then, with a sort of horror and relief, that some part of me had come here wanting it to be him. Wanting the job done. Wanting the door shut and taped and the cold brought in to finish the job that shouting had not finished. Then I thought of him at nine, swimming under me and touching my ankle to make me jump, and the way he had come up and leaned his head into my shoulder and done nothing for a while like that was hugging, because that was how he did that then.

I went back into the room. The man and the woman stood, because they were practiced in mourning and did it lightly, like birds on a wire. I stood at the foot of the bench and I said, I cannot tell you yes or no. He said, that's okay. He said, we ask because sometimes it's obvious and that's a blessing for someone. He said, sometimes it's not. He said, we will go the other route. He said, we have a list of dentists, we can ask if any of them have seen him. He said, if we could have an address he might have used last we have on file. I said a street I had last written on an envelope ten years ago. He wrote it down. The woman made a phone call. I said, do you have anything he was wearing, anything that was his. The man said, we do, but it is not fit, it's been in the water. He said, there was a pocket with paper in it. He said, if you like, we can let you see it later, we are drying it carefully. He said, we try to do right by everything.

There is a cafe on the island that is the color of the milk at the bottom of a bowl of Weetabix and the inside is full of old ship pictures and a clock that runs two minutes slow. Two teenagers in shepherd's check shirts run it when their aunt is in bed with her back. I sat by the window and watched a van go from one end of town to the other and then back again. An old man with a cap that had seen more weather than me came in and ordered the same breakfast he had every day at nine when he had a good morning. He sat behind me and spoke to someone invisible about the petrol being dear and the ferry sometimes being late and about one of the co-op men marrying someone from away. I ate a fried egg on toast because I did not know what else to do and because it anchored my hands to something. While I was eating a woman came in who looked like someone I had known, which is not the same as being someone I had known, and she stood looking at the board like a person at an altar. She turned and saw me seeing her and raised her eyebrows, the invitation to not talk, and we did not. The clock did not bother anyone.

He could have sat in this cafe and eaten this egg and watched that white van. He could have. That is a sentence that can get on a ferry and stow away every day of your life if you let it. He could have been the man outside in a heavy sweater looking at the gulls as if the gulls owed him money. He could have married someone from away like the man the old man behind me laid his life over. He could have changed his name to something his mouth liked to say more. He could have walked into the police house this morning smelling of cold water and called me a fool and a saint in the same breath. He could have. He could have.

When the tea came, and I thought I ought to because it would be warmed again and that would mean at least one thing had done what it said it would do this morning, I lifted it with both hands and felt how much I was shaking. The woman behind the counter put the little cardboard teapot down and said, you alright, love, as if she had always been allowed to call me that, and I said, I will be, and she nodded, not because she believed me but because what else can you do. The ceiling had flies that had stayed all winter in small perfect triangles in the corners. The window seam had curled paint like eyelids. It is the least interesting thing about grief that the details in a room get so sharp.

I went back to the station at eleven. The man with the mustache smiled at me the way a man smiles when he has to see the rest of a thing through. He said, we've gone as far with the filings as we can go with what's on file for now. He said, we can release him to the city. He said, if you think it would be best, you can sign as provisional next of kin. He said the words in a tone that would not hurt anyone. He passed a clipboard across the desk. I looked at the place where I was to put my name. I thought of writing it. I thought of what he would do if he were standing behind me reading over my shoulder. He would whistle tunelessly. He would rest the side of his hand on the top of my head. He would say, make it look like a doctor's prescription so they can't question it later. I thought, if I write, I choose. If I don't, I choose something else. I do not know which cruelty is the kind with more future in it.

They had given me the bag. It was the kind of hospital bag you get as a kindness when you were not supposed to go to a hospital. It had two things in it. A belt that had not been on a waist in a while and that still had the shape of a waist in it, like a letter pressed under a heavy book, and a piece of paper the size of a postcard that had been washed, then dried, then remembered as paper again. On the paper there were scratches that once had been letters. If I put it up to the light and closed one eye and moved slowly I could find the bones of words. There was a number. There was my name. There was a space where others had been torn off by the water. He had written me a letter that had maybe been on its way to me for days and maybe had been in his pocket because he could not post it. He had written, Si—. He had written something like, the boats here are not like we thought. He had written something like, do you remember the time Mrs. Mac went mad at us for putting the washing line through her cat hole. He had written something like, if you come up I will show you the steps down to the black pool that only locals know. He had written, I'm sorry, or maybe he had written, I won't be, and there was no way to be sure which had been under my finger because the water had its own alphabet.

I signed. It felt like doing a small treachery that was also a rescue. I signed because I knew our mother could not be carried up the stairs to such a room, even in her absence now, even with the cardigan that had been held for years in her drawer for this day. I signed because there are ways one brother decides for the other that are in the job description and because someone had to. The man took the paper with both hands though it was not fragile and he set it where he set it and he nodded in a way that meant, thank you for holding the line here. He said, we'll take good care of him. He did not say, you will get a call tomorrow. He did not touch my sleeve. He looked at me once, like a question he had asked twice already, and then he went into a different room and did his job.

I went behind the post office and up the path that leads to the headland because I could not go straight back to the ferry with those rooms still going on inside me. The grass up there has learned to live with wind and all of it leans, not in the direction you think it's going to. I walked until I was at a place where the land thinks about ending and then does. I looked down at the rocks that are all different once you are close to them, the way faces are all different when you are in the same room and not looking at them in a photograph. The sea was throwing itself at the ledge the way a boy throws himself at the one barn door that opens and will not, for a few games yet, pretend he is not wanting anything. It is a lie that the waves all look the same. It is true that you cannot tell any one from the next once it is past. I stood there with my hands in the pockets of my jacket and thought of all the times he had stood like this, alone or not, and spoken into the wind words that could not lodge anywhere and fall out later to be found by someone else. He might have stood right here yesterday morning or he might have gone down the metal steps to the place where the locals go to fish in the eddy where the wrasse are. He might have gone along the cliff with the tourist girls who come here to take photographs of their boots and the horizon. He might have. I had a long conversation with a gull that wanted my attention for some reason I was not going to discover. I had the sudden craze to throw my hat into the wind and pretend it was a coin and that I could call heads and mean anything by that. I did not throw it.

On the path back down I met a man with a dog that had the sweet sad eyes of all dogs that have been walked in rain. He nodded to me because we were two men outside on a day that had not decided whether to dampen us yet. He said, are you from the ferry, and I said yes, and he said, all the faces change and then they don't. He said, were you the one they called this morning. I said, I don't know how to answer that. He said, do you know Mick from the co-op, and I said no, and he said, Mick found the poor beggar. He said, it happens most winters. It brings home whoever it likes. He said, sorry for your trouble, in the way people say it in places like this where the sentence doesn't have to be heavy because the air will do that for it. I said, thank you. The dog looked at my hands and seemed disappointed that they were empty.

I made my way to the harbor and stood where the ferry's ramp made its slow argument with the gradient of the tide. Across on the pier two men were arguing in a way that men argue when there is nothing else to discuss that morning. A child flicked a plastic cup into the water and then watched it float unguiltily toward the open strait. I could see from here the police house's small top window and I knew that under that roof a drawer held a small line of string that had been a shoe lace and a wallet with nothing in it but a bus ticket and the paper with my name and something like an apology on it. The wind moved around behind my ear and then across my mouth like a hand. The first truck rolled into place on the deck below me and a man in an orange jacket snapped two fingers into the air to show the driver how far to go. The tannoy said we would be leaving in ten minutes, that I'd better make up my mind now.

Before I went up the ramp the woman with the red hair band who had poured my tea last night was walking off, her shift over, her face pale with that heat that comes from other people's bodies all night long. She smiled at me with the professional courtesy of all night workers who see someone who is clearly not a tourist. She said, it was cold last night, like a verdict. I said, it was. She said, the lights were on a bit, not the big ones, the small ones, you know. She made a wave with her fingers to show me ghosts. I said, I had slept. She said, we carry all sorts, don't we, as if she and I both worked on the ferry and had not just been carried by it. Her face was tired. She did not know I had put a name in a book that would go beyond this morning. She did not need to.

I stood on the forward deck as we left. The island pulled back, or the island held perfectly still and we did the pulling. There is a point at which the land stops being detail and becomes outline and then a word and then a feeling. There is another point at which the feeling stops being two and becomes one, and it's not relief. I untied my scarf and then tied it again because my hands needed a task that felt like salvation. There were six other people on the deck and we all pretended we were alone. The ship cut a line in the winter water that closed over, and middle distance turned into a future we were too small to carry but would have to anyway. I held the rail. It was colder than what I had touched in that room, not because of temperature but because it had learned how to be held by strangers forever.

I did not know if he had been under those sheets for him or for me. I did not know if the man with the mustache had watched me and seen what I had not said. I did not know if the paper would yield another word when it had dried entirely and was read by someone with better eyes than mine. I did not know if a fisherman would go home tonight and put his hands in hot water and tell his wife that he had seen worse and then not believe himself. I did not know if a man somewhere up another coast was standing in a kitchen just now and not knowing that the man he had laughed with on Tuesday would not laugh with him again. I did not know if I hoped he was out there somewhere and would call me next week and say, I'm still here, you fool, and if that were the case I did not know what I could possibly say back.

What I did know was how to stand and watch a patch of water and think I understood it for a minute. What I did know was the geography of certain rooms and certain fights. I knew how to hold paper under the right light and find my name and recognize the humor of that. The gulls came along behind the ferry with the courage of small criminals. A boy held a small plastic car up to the wind and made a sound like a motor. The engines moved us with the unembarrassed strength of men doing what they were built to do. A woman took her hat off and rubbed both hands over her hair and put it back on. The cold did not know around which of us to flow first and so took us all at once.

By the time we reached the middle of the strait the land behind looked like someone else's story, and the land ahead looked like the one I had left without my permission. I could feel the night I had spent on the ferry already turn into something that the rest of my days would lean against. We passed a buoy that rang once, the small sad sound of a bell you have to make yourself with your own wind. I looked down at the line we left and it was closing, and I imagined laying him down in the corner of our old small room the day he came home from the hospital with the scar by his ear, the way he had looked at me with one stunned eye, the way I'd wanted to promise him everything. I said his name into the wind and the wind kept it for a while and then it didn't. We went on. The engines did not lose faith. The cold outfaced my hands and stayed with them anyway. The harbor before me lifted its blue hands, and I went to meet whatever it thought it had for me.
