He had to put his ear practically into the dirt to hear it. A damp stitch of soil darkened along the row like the line of a stitched wound, but the sound was deeper, the steady, spiteful hiss of pressure forcing itself into places it had no business going.

“Here,” Eli said. He held up a hand without looking at his brother. “Listen.”

Nate stood a few feet back under the bowed green weight of a peach tree, hat brim whittled thin in the middle where the sun had eaten it. The June light made a silvering of the peach fuzz that was still weeks from softening into sweetness. The leaves whispered like paper. From the county road, miles away, a semi sounded its horn. Out here, the air held heat like a bell holds tone.

“I hear it,” Nate said. He’d known there was a leak even before Eli texted him, before the pressure dropped yesterday and the back corner withered into a gray thirst. He leaned on the shovel and watched his little brother press his head into the world as if he could burrow through it by listening.

“Thought so,” Eli said, getting to his feet, swiping the side of his face with the heel of his hand. The thin soil left a line of mud under his ear like a bruise. “Give me the shovel.”

Nate handed it over. Eli put the shovel point where he thought the sound was fattest and jumped his weight onto it. The dirt here had a skin of weed that gave up easy—foxtail and puncturevine and some annual grass that pressed flat and cut your fingers if you were careless. Under that it was already damp enough that the shovel didn't need help, and when Eli flipped the first square of it aside they both saw the glint of black polyethylene and the slick wriggling of a dozen beetles surprised into light skittering crazily over their hands.

“Jesus,” Eli said, flicking his fingers. Clean dirt ran into the ditch from the trench like dry sugar. It made the hissing noise louder, a wet, obstinate whisper. Eli dug again, knelt, dug with his hands like a dog. The water feeling wasn’t cold. Not yet. It was the temperature of bodies.

“I should shut the valve,” Nate said, already turning away along the row to the box with the green lid. In the world before the drought, before the pumps coughing up sand, before the water district sent letters that made your throat burn even before you knew what to do, you could turn a knob and the sound would stop and you’d work in quiet. You had the luxury of turning things off in those days. He went to the valve box anyway because it was the right movement to make, and lifted the lid. The stagnant ankle-sized pond inside the box let out two mosquitoes and the smell of algae and minerality. He reached into that and cranked at the rusted gate valve and the hiss went away down the row with a sound like a lung letting out its held breath.

“It’s the coupler,” Eli called. “No, Christ—look at that.”

Nate came back with the hacksaw, the can of primer and the slippery can of cement with a brush glued under its lid and a handful of couplings and clamps and a small roll of blue tape because his father had taught him to find uses for things that had been given one purpose and held to it by the manufacturer’s imagination. All men were limited by combinations of what they were told they could do and what their hands learned when no one was looking. His father had said that too, in more practical tones. If the county had put one use on the can, it was a good day when you could make it hold three.

“Gopher chew,” Eli said, touching the edge of the hole lightly with one forefinger as if testing a mouth. The poly had a crescent cut clean out where an animal had gnawed. “Or ground squirrel. Look at these little bastards.” He flicked the bead of teeth marks with his fingernail. “What do they know about mortgages. What do they know about care homes.”

“Same thing we know about their tunnels,” Nate said. “We go through each other."

Eli sat back on his heels and looked up at the row. Each tree carried its crop like a private burden, green and heavy, summer’s promise in small fists. They had thinned them two weeks ago, carpeting the ground with baby fruit like bones. Their father would have whistled when he did it, then looked down and made that quick adjustment he always made in the decision to leave three instead of two. As if it was a perfectible thing. As if the right number of peaches in girl-palm bunches under the leaves could guard any family from loss.

Eli’s T-shirt was dark down the middle and pasted to his chest. He had always sweated heavier than Nate. He pulled it away from his skin with one hand and took the hacksaw from his brother with the other, pinning the black tubing with his knee and cutting through the wet, giving, chewing sound. He set the chewed-off portion in the pile beside him as if he could not help sorting the broken enough to see—damage making categories the way fruit makes sugar. Good pipe here. Junk there. The scar of a gopher on the scrap like a hostile signature.

Their father would have said, The day you stop putting the junk out where you can look at it is the day it starts to own you. His voice made a ghost under the cottonwoods at the ditch.

“Hand me the coupler,” Eli said.

Nate did. He worked the primer cap with a practiced twist, ring finger and thumb, the cheap lid flexing. Purple bled into the brush. He could smell it even before the sun pulled it out—sweet and chemical like fruit before the birds got to it. He swabbed primer inside the coupler and around the freshly cut mouths of the line and then reached for the cement, which always had a feel he liked, gunk but necessary gunk, a glue more honest than the words he’d had to say at the bank and the last hospital.

“You hold for fifteen,” he said, because that was what his father had said, the glue needing a man’s pressure because time alone wasn’t enough to make two things stick.

“You hold,” Eli said. “You’re the one can count while your head elsewhere.”

It wasn’t unkind. The jab was part of the talk. Nate jammed the coupler onto the near piece of pipe, a twist and a push, then repeated the cement on the other side, and set the second mouth over it, shoved the whole thing hard until it bottomed. He looked at Eli and Eli held the line steady with both hands and they leaned their weight onto the join, like two men shoulder to shoulder at a bar resisting a push that could have been a fight or could have been a friend.

“One one-thousand,” Nate said. He breathed purple and petroleum. His forearms shook a little and it wasn’t just effort. The glue bit, the cement setting, but the memory of things failing before they could take made his wrists uneasy. He breathed again. Ten. Eleven. Twelve. Fibers knit. Thirteen. Fourteen. The line held. He let go like relinquishing a child's hand outside the school gate.

“Okay,” Eli said. He sat back again, wiping the sweat with his forearm, leaving a stripe in the dirt like the smear of a comet across a toy sky. “Now just a thousand more like that.”

“There aren’t a thousand leaks,” Nate said.

“What do you call this?” Eli turned his head, showing the rows beyond. The near field was green enough; the far, where the pressure had cut out, was a dusty olive, leaves turned edge-on to the sun. The square of the orchard, unrolled years earlier like a bolt of green cloth over the valley’s dusty body, showed its frays. Along the fence at the county road, men had put a flapping white sign: Development Opportunity. The sign showed a cartoon house with a triangle roof smiling in lines even the architect would know were a lie.

“Don’t look at that,” Nate said. He stood and went to the valve. He didn’t look down the line to where the sign had been zip-tied through the chain-link. He didn’t look at the long half-built wedge of concrete blocks across the road where Whitman Orchards had been last summer. He caught his boot toe on the hump where his father had buried a shitty barn cat because nobody puts even the worthless thing that lived at your edges out with the trash. He cranked the valve open rough, impatience and fear making him jerk it more than was wise, and from the cut ends of the failed rig shoes of water coughed at his ankles before he could scramble back and the new glue thought about betrayal.

Eli laughed at him and then flinched, because the coupler held and the leak was now a diffuse wet under the roots, and the clods the water made made him think of his father’s hands.

“Good,” Eli said. “Path of least resistance.” He grinned at his brother. “And to think he wanted me in theater.”

They watched the line until the pressure settled and most of the hiss had become a thinking noise instead of the panic of a wound. In the next tree, a bee shot by their heads. A dragonfly hung in the air a few seconds, flicking its transparent paper wings as if the effort were for show and then sliding on. It was that time when every insect felt born for what it did and shameless about it.

“Now flush the end,” Nate said. “Take the cap off.”

“I know how to flush a line,” Eli said. “You think I’m twelve.”

“You were twelve ten years after I stopped looking,” Nate said.

Eli made his face blank for a second before he flinched like a man pulling a big splinter. He walked the row down past three trees and ducked under the branches at the T where the lateral connected to the next. He threaded past wire cage around a trunk where their father had tried to keep the raccoons off one year with something he’d seen online he said would work better than traps. The world has a way of telling you what you don't want to know. Eli squatted at the end of the line and undid the figure-eight clamp their father had used because the way of finishing a piece of pipe didn't have to be the way the catalog told you. He unlooped the black tubing and then held the end up a little so when Nate opened the valve farther the sediment would come in a dark rush not right at his face, and what came was a brown soup with things that had once been something. Fine root hair, algae, a slick of iron. Earwigs spat from the end and crawled upstream against the flow because God had made them stubborn. Eli held the end and shook his head.

“Look at that,” Eli said. “Whatever this is, it can’t be what it’s supposed to be anymore. That’s the thing I can’t keep getting past.”

“You’ll get past it if you keep your eyes on the end of the day,” Nate said. “Open more. Close. Open. That’s the rhythm. Like lungs.”

“Not like lungs,” Eli said. He kinked the pipe with his grip to stop the flow, petting it as if it were a living length of something that could understand praise. He un-kinked and let the water go, uncoiled and sprayed again. “Lungs pull in air like we own it. Water goes where it wants.”

That’s not what our father said, Nate started to say, and then stopped. Because their father would have said it was both: air for the price of walking upright; water for the price of men fighting over law. Because their father had kept a file cabinet in the office under the seed catalogs with three drawers of paper you’d have to be a patient lawyer to love. They might not have money but they had rights, he’d liked to say. He said it as if rights were a thing you could put your palm on, stroke, say good boy to, expect to have them bring you the newspaper every morning.

Eli finished the flush and held a shorthand conversation with the line itself while he got the figure-eight on again. He liked the ways his hands did what they did no matter who he had disappointed.

Across the row-line, the neighbor dog started up and then trotted down the fence with a look on its dog-face like it had new business. A truck rolled past slow on River Road, the dull baffle of gravel and the weather-sound of tires on heat and dust. Even without looking, you could tell by the rigidness of the silhouette behind the windshield whether a buyer or a friend drove by. Buyers wore their shoulders like they were protecting their father’s suits. Friends slouched.

“You going to answer Whitman?” Eli said, wiping his hands on his jeans.

“He’s dead,” Nate said.

“Not Whitman Whitman. The son. Casey. He called again this morning.”

“He called you.”

Eli bit the inside of his cheek and looked away. “He texted me. He’s offering ten thousand an acre over last week if we give him the right of first refusal by Friday. He says they—his group—wants to lock down this side of the road before Midsouth Water moves the pipe.”

“Midsouth Water isn’t moving the pipe,” Nate said, because that was what the last letter said, and saying what a letter said felt like faith because the ink and letterhead were expensive. He imagined the office cool in a valley town with lawyers and a girl answering the phone saying hold please with a voice like swimming pool water, safe and sweet. “They said they’re ‘exploring.’ We’re all exploring, Eli. That doesn’t sound like trucks. That sounds like waiting."

“Lock it down,” Eli said. He liked to roll buyers’ words in his mouth before spitting them. He looked at the trees. He looked at his brother. “You going to call him.”

Nate liked turning a wrench more than he liked answering his phone. It was easier to make parts fit than to make language fit lives. He’d been putting that call away like a man pushing a bill under a placemat, telling himself he’d get to it when he had the money. He said, “Let’s finish this run.”

“You always want to finish the run. All you own is runs. Runs and the sense behind your eyes that if you finish enough of them a river will come of it. I want to talk about the things we can do while men like Casey Whitman and the county board do what they are doing.”

They had finished the join. They had cleared the end. Now Nate walked the line, squatting every third tree, putting a finger under a dripper to feel if it was giving. If he couldn’t get a finger under, he pulled the emitter and swilled it in a little dish of acid water he carried like his father had, then pushed it back in, twist, click. If that didn’t give flow, he poked the hole with his nail. If that didn’t give, he cut out that few inches and put in a new piece. If you did this for three more hours, and then walked the next run, and then the next, you could keep a day from showing you its rot.

He and Eli didn’t argue so much as rehearse something they had been speaking in the gaps between words since their father had the first headache and refused to say the word until the day he couldn’t not say hospice. They didn’t have a pattern like morning prayers but they had a shape: Eli would say, We’re drowning, and Nate would say, There’s a platform here if you watch your feet; Eli would say, The platform is just a flat piece of a sinking boat, and Nate would say, If you move your weight right, the boat gets you to shore. Then they would unscrew a cap. Then one of them would mention their mother like an apology.

“She fell again last night,” Eli said, and opened both hands in the air as if asking the sky why that fact refused to be novel. He kicked lightly at the crown of foxtail, releasing its sharp seeds, regretting it even as he did. “Did you tell them to put the alarm back on her bed.”

“I told them to put the alarm back on,” Nate said. He had driven there after last light the night before, the orchard a shaking mass of invisible presence around him as he pulled out in the truck, the sky low with the smell of dry. He had stupidly honked once as his grandfather had when leaving a long irrigation change, the little beep a kiss tossed behind him. He had parked in the spot in the care home that said Visitor like it knew you were a better man than the staff because you were here less often. He had sat on the little fake leather chair and watched his mother sleep, her breath busy with its roughness, and when she woke she had said, Oh you, and then she had said her own brother’s name. And then she had said the name of an orchard hand fired in 1998 who had broken his shoulder falling from the bed of a moving truck. She had said that man’s name like a prayer. He had said, I’m Nate, Mama, but he’d said it carefully, like a person naming himself to a dog that will not be forced to believe. He had brought a cold cloth from the good sink to lay it on her head and she had said, Stop. Enough cold. It’s June. And then she’d slept so hard there was drool at her lip and he had folded the cloth and lay it on the nightstand like that did anything for anyone anywhere. He said, “I told them to change the meds. I told them to call me if she wakes again.”

“Like they will.”

“They might.”

Eli bent to a dripper and unscrewed it and shook it and blew it once, once, the way his father had blown into his palm in winter to warm it before peeling tape. He pushed the emitter back and watched it piss out a jittering stream not unlike his father’s those last weeks when the catheter clogged and a nurse had said something practical that made them feel both saved and outraged. “You put the drip line down here. You fix this leak. You make two men who wrote your names down have jobs. But the last money we had sitting and pretending to look like money is gone. Even if Whitman was offering a dirty silver coin for every acre and a chick for every other acre, I would call him. Close your eyes and say father father to yourself, Nate. It doesn’t matter. Better is not coming. Not for this piece. This isn’t a story with a flattering end. This is weather and law and men with signs that smile like your dentist while they say there will be a cul-de-sac named after your father, as if that makes sense to anyone who ever watered.”

Nate sat back on his heels and felt the white sun heat beneath his shirt. He watched the way water, even in a leak, even in a necessary open end, finds a pattern, arranges itself into its one set of tricks. The orchard was an organism, but it was also a net he had inherited with his faith in it knotted and mended and knotted again. He had a sense of being looked at by his father even now. Maybe the gaze was just habit. Some mornings he shot awake in the bunkhouse at the edge of the yard, his chest hard with the feel that someone had called him by his name in the dark, like a boy again being told to get up for frost or the county water officer.

“Finish the row,” he said again.

Eli’s hand gripped the line hard. Then he let go, like men let go of things in wars, because the letting go was not a promise but at least it was an action. “Fine.”

By the top of the row they had righted it enough you could look and not feel ashamed. The lower branches hung like a woman’s hair too long uncombed, but under them, the PVC and poly were straight, the drippers shot, the coupler held. A coyote had trotted down the far fence some time earlier and left a loping line of prints they could see between the grass heads. A hawk perched on the post where their father had set a bluebird box and never got any bluebirds, only starlings with dark oil-slick throats.

Nate heard a truck come up the drive. He didn’t look. Eli did, scrunching under a tree for shade and wiping at his ear where the sweat pooled. “It’s Whitman,” Eli said. “The kid. He’s got his father’s mouth.”

“I’m not,” Nate said, stubborn as a stand of alfalfa where it wasn't supposed to be. He let the hawk hold him, like a thing you could stare down into to get your spine to stop thinking the bad thought it had been thinking for two weeks: that there was a world where this made sense. He stood and dusted his hands on his pants, then heard the door of the truck slam, footsteps not committing too much of their weight to the ground yet—buyer's steps were always like that; they named their willingness to swear with checks while secretly believing the ground would honor them for free.

“Nate,” Casey Whitman said. He put his hands out like an actor playing a man with two things to offer and neither of them a stake. His shirt was the color of a sky people paint in their apartments. His sunglasses made his eyes not real. “Eli. Fellas. You working. Great to see it. I love to see it.”

Nate kept his hands on his hips like if they held his toolbelt there he would be a man not required to put that belt down and face the hundred versions for sale of his own past ten minutes. “We’re busy,” he said. He didn’t even try to flatten it out into civility. He wanted to be the man his father had forgave himself for not being thirty years ago, all salt and old Spanish and gun in the truck. That swagger had impressed him when he was eleven. It had seemed to him, as a kid, that men who spoke like lines in movies and hammered iron in their yards could do anything. Now being that kind of man felt like choosing to be useful only to yourself.

Casey smiled and let it sober as if he had meant to come just to share some father-adjacent reminiscence and not to salt the earth behind him with legalese. He had a folder in his hand and a white sheet peeking at its mouth and Nate saw, could not help seeing, the way his own name typed there would look. “I brought—well, you know, an update on the market. And yes, full disclosure, my associates and I, we’re trying to get ahead of—”

“Whatever you’re calling it this week,” Eli said. “The weather. The board’s decision. The thing where farmers find a new way to swallow their own teeth.”

Casey held his breath and then exhaled in two pieces. “We’re all in it. But people are excited about neighborhoods here. I mean, really excited. I’m not going to blow smoke. I know your father would have told me to take my hat off and then told me to get off his lot. You boys aren’t your father and that’s okay. You’re—” He stopped. “What I am saying is I know there are costs. Long-term care. Property taxes escalating because of last year. Power for those old pumps. Payroll. It’s a lot.” He had the sense to direct the list into the trees and then at his own shoes and not at their faces. “My offer has a number that respects that. And you keep the house. The shop. I’m not heartless. We can do that.”

“You can’t do that,” Nate said.

“Sure we can,” Casey said, light. “Easement it out. Kitchen and apple trees. You can rent the shop to me if you need. I know a framer wants to stage there. You’d have monthly income without the risk.”

“We keep the house,” Eli repeated. He sounded like a man teasing his own hunger. “And the blackberries by the ditch.”

“Hell. We’ll bring over a Bobcat and put in raised beds for you,” Casey said, and then shrank, intuiting that he had said something like a slap. “I’m kidding.”

“We’re not,” Nate said. He knew they were. Everybody in this county told jokes to edge up to the thing they couldn’t say without crying.

Casey had the decency to let silence come snag him. An airplane passed. Somewhere a diesel engine throbbed until a man took his foot off it and it fell silent like a sore mouth biting on a toothpick. The hawk left the post in a drop and then lifted and the tail flashed like something metal.

“I’ll leave this with you,” Casey said, flapping the folder. The top of the white inside showed Nate a number with zeros that made his chest bark like a cough. Eli took the folder because he was good at the theater of things and how you could act one way to get a different kind of time.

“I know the—” Casey touched his own forehead as if locating a word under his skin. “The—” He had, or pretended to, feel for grief. “Your mother. Everyone knows. I told my guys. There’s room to move on that even before closing. Look. Take a day. Two. I’m not a monster. But I am also going to be frank: we have other offers too. And the county—”

“We know what the county will and will not pretend,” Eli said. He had the folder under his arm like a baton. He was trying to remember the face their father made when someone came to sell him something—a little blankness and a playful cynicism that made the man talking think he might be worth listening to if only to see what kind of nonsense he would spin next. Eli wasn’t his father and wasn’t interested in being. He did not have the unearned freedom of those performances. He looked at the boy in front of him and saw a man who’d grown up with a pool and a father who wore boots on days when the newsmen came with cameras. He let some generosity enter his face because he was a useful person by training and some part of him felt sorry for all the ways a guy like this one had lost out on the kinds of tenderness working hands could teach you. It made him hate him less. It didn’t make him love him. “Thank you,” he said.

And because the world still ran on tiny courtesies even in the middle of big betrayals, they stood and watched him back his truck out of the yard carefully so he didn’t crush the rake they’d left by the shed. Eli lifted his chin in a valley-shrug that might have meant hello, might have meant go to hell, and Casey did the same and drove slow down the rutted drive, one hand on the wheel like he’d been taught to look like he belonged wherever he was.

Under the tree their father used to call Eleanor for reasons he never explained to anyone, a sun-bleached five-gallon bucket lay half-filled with black O-rings and half an inch of sludge. Nate kicked the bucket upright and sat on it because he needed the feeling that something even as unreliable as a used bucket would hold him.

“He’s offering more than I thought he would,” Eli said. He said it softly enough that a stranger might have thought it was a confession of infidelity. He set the folder on a little hummock of grass the drip had made when it leaked, explaining with his hands to the air like he was offering a table of contents. “You see this line.”

Nate didn’t look at the bowl of paper. He took his hat off and set it on his thigh and wiped his forearm across his brow. Salt stung his eye. He let the sting move through him, then kept looking at his brother. He had pissed Eli off often enough to recognize the face that said the piss-off was behind for now and what sat in front of them was a tool heavier than either of them could pick up without breaking their backs. “I see this row,” he said, slow. “I see that in a week I need six guys in here thinning and I don’t have six guys. I see that the water bill was more than the mortgage this spring and the pump man says the wellhole has silt at the bottom and his nephew says he’s got a cousin runs a crew in Lodi he can get us on to for cherries but that ain’t peaches and I don’t know how to plug the gap, Eli. I know if I sell every piece of equipment we don’t use twice this summer, we get three months of our mother with people who wipe her mouth when she falls asleep and don’t make us feel like pieces of shit for not being her all day.” He looked down. A crow called from the fence. “But I can’t see going to Lathrop some Sunday in ten years and driving the kids past a gray cul-de-sac with my father’s name on it like someone made him into a Sims thing. I can’t stand there and tell myself I did something brave.”

“I’m not asking for brave,” Eli said. “I’m asking for not foolish. I’m asking us to consider that a tree is only a tree if its feet drink water. After that it’s wood that might hide a man when the cops go by.”

A wind came up and rained a small sifting of breezeway through the leaves, dry grit on their necks. They both looked at the ditch because it made the small music of water going by when all this part of the world wanted to train you to believe that music wasn’t for you anymore. The ditch had its own schedule, older than their father and older than the county, back when men with belts made of rope and hats that could catch water had come out here and seen a flat place that could be tricked into believing in oranges and eaten up the labor of anyone you could bully.

“We could lease,” Nate said, the thought like a fat fly buzzing around his head every day for six months. “Not to him. Not to developers. To Chuy. He has the crew. He takes the risk. We get enough to keep the house. Mama keeps her bed with the alarm. We get this year. Maybe two.” He said, less sure, “People are paying for sugar now like Yankees. Maybe prices go up. Maybe we get a wet winter. They say El Niño.”

“People always say El Niño like a person saying grace after they ate already,” Eli said. But his shoulders let down a piece. “Lease it to Chuy, yeah.” He chewed the inside of his lip. “He’ll cut costs. He’ll rip this whole stupid spaghetti of drip out and put in those new Octa-lines with the built-in regulators and think he’s made water.” He looked as if he could see it: the black slickness of new poly snaking over their dirt, the tidy black hats of emitters like eyes. “He’ll lay men off after harvest and on Monday morning he’ll bring them back to prune for three days and then lay them off Tuesday again. He’ll make it work.”

“He’ll keep the trees alive,” Nate said. “They’ll still be here when I drive past on the road.”

“For a while,” Eli said. He tipped his head back and looked through the leaves like a kid used to under trees, a boy who had loved hiding until their father, pretending to go mad, would run scraping a rake along the dirt and sing a slasher tune he’d made up to make them squeal and come out of their tree with their hearts sore from laughing. Eli blinked. “Call him.”

Nate did not want to make that call. It would be a new way of telling the world what he couldn’t do. But he took his phone from his pocket—crack across one corner, a smear of glue on the back because he’d fixed everything he could fix in the way he was able—and pulled up Chuy’s number. He felt sudden, unearned pity for his father who hadn’t had to confess this kind of failure because death licked him first. He felt mean about that pity, and then he felt it anyway.

Chuy answered on the second ring, his voice high with the static of his own yard in the background. Nate could hear a radio. He could smell, in his mind, the thing that had permeated the inside of that truck since the side yard where Chuy did his own mechanical work, three kinds of oil and topsoil and the sweetish rot of something he wasn’t going to name. “Nate,” he said. “My son. Finally. You got my message.”

“We got it,” Nate said. He looked at Eli. Eli sat his chin in his hand in a way that made him look momentarily like their boy-cousin again. “We want to talk.”

“You ready or you still dragging the chain,” Chuy said. It wasn’t unkind, but you didn’t get friendship with Chuy uncut. “I know. It isn’t simple.”

“My brother is here,” Nate said.

“Tell Eli to stop laughing at me,” Chuy said. “You two think you invented all the feelings.”

Eli leaned in, his mouth against the place where the voice came out. “We’d sell to you if we were men for selling,” he said into the phone. “We aren’t. We’re men for—what are we for.”

“Fencing,” Chuy said. “And staying too long at the river. And wiping your father’s face with a hand towel and never telling anyone who did it. Lease me your headache, amigos. I’ll pay your mama’s roof and yours and I’ll bring my own boys. You can come smell the cut wood all day and cry if you want when I burn your father’s old tires. There. I said the thing. I love you. I grew up in this place while your father pretended to own me. I will not cut her down.”

They stood there under Eleanor and agreed to things that would be written and then said by men who wore short sleeves. They did not have the paper in hand, but they had each other’s fathers between them, and that counted still in a county not yet burned to the level of memory. They told Chuy they would meet him at the office Tuesday. They told him that they would be men in that office. When they hung up, both brothers turned to look toward the care home though it was miles away, as if there were a light that could guide them from here to her.

They ate the sandwiches Nate had made that morning when he still thought they might not talk. They sat on the tailgate of the tired Ford with its paint worn transparent on the bed rails and its red turned ghost-pink under their father’s back and their own. The bread was warm now. The ham sweated. There was grit in the lettuce that popped in the teeth. Eli drank the water from the cooler too fast and got hiccups. He hiccupped until he laughed and then he laughed until his face went wet. Nate put a hand on his back and pressed there the way his mother had when he was a boy and swallowed wrong.

“Remember when he made us dig up that whole run because he said the grade was wrong,” Eli said. Talking about their father in second-person present had come like weather; they couldn’t stop it, as if he were at the far end of the row and could turn and look at them if they handled the verbs in the right order.

“He was right,” Nate said. “That run was shit.”

“He was right,” Eli said. “He was always right. Then for one year he wasn’t right about anything and then he died and now he’s right again. That doesn’t make sense.”

“It does,” Nate said. “You just hate it.”

They went back to work because work was the part that made everything else either possible or impossible. They moved up to the second block where the pressure drop had gone another way, under the bigger trees they’d grafted sixteen years ago when their father had still laughed at men who planted almond. The black lines here were tangles made by men without time. Nate knelt again, the same shape of his back as when he was nineteen and thought he would do this for six months before he ran off with his girlfriend to Florida. The girlfriend had left. He had stayed. Eli had gone. The math, simple as it was, did not tell the story. He pressed his digits into soil and then plastic. He worked the glues. He said, “One-one-thousand,” under his breath and felt Eli’s hands with his.

At one point he jammed the coupler home and the cement slid on his fingers like slobber. The joint held for a breath and then hissed and gave and black water burst into the air and painted a sideways arc across his shirt and face so that for a second he was eleven again, the day their father had found them sweating at the midsummer line and had opened the head deliberately to douse them both, laughing from his throat and the tender cartilage of his ears glinting with hair in the light. He spat and laughed and swore and Eli said, “Hold,” in a voice shaped like their father’s and they both held and then the plastic took and the solvency set and Nate signed his name to the invisible little weld and for a second he felt a thing that might have been ease.

They worked through the afternoon, heat stacking itself around them in sheets you could swipe but not lift. They said less. They said just what was needed to finish this, then this, then this—little words like pass and here and wait. They were better together at this than at any other thing. Sometimes that hurt. Mostly it made things doable.

At dusking they crouched with their forearms on their knees and looked at what they’d done. The repaired run purred to itself like a living creature digesting. The tops of the leaves were unwet, but under, the world went dark where the drippers hit the same spot thirty times a minute. The ground there gave a smell that was the opposite of the smell of each afternoon that month: this smell was promise, the admission of a secret. It made you think you could be forgiven for other kinds of thirst. It made foolishness and faith look like two sides of one leaf.

From the house the phone rang. It was the old phone, the line still tethered, the wire still carrying the sound of its ring like an animal calling in the distance instead of the little rectangles in their pockets buzzing like a nerve. The old phone rang with its rounded, real bell—which their father had loved, had refused to change to a digital alarum because it proved something to him: if they could make plastic sound like metal why were they making plastic. The bell rang. Nate drank. He stood. In the doorway of the house, the shadow of something made it look like their mother was there already, tiny in a chair. It was only the mop he’d left after the last night he’d tried to make something in there clean enough to count as care.

“I’ll get it,” he said, and then because he knew it might not be changeable news, because it might be the nurse again saying, She’s up, she’s up, she’s singing to a boy who is not here, he softened his face before the badness could surprise it into meanness. He walked to the house with the posture of a tree that had seen worse winters.

Eli watched him go. He listened to the run making itself right. The hawk that had watched earlier had brought a mate with her. They sat, two black commas on the post, and when one lifted off and turned their bodies wrote a word he didn’t know he knew how to read. He thought, not for the first time, about the time their father had left them in the far block at dusk and gone to get diesel and told them to shut it down and bring in the tools and he had not come back because he had run into a man on the county road whose tire had blown and both of them had tried to be each other’s help and got to know one another for the first time in thirty years and came back with hands shaking from the amount of nice the world sometimes demanded. He and Nate had waited and fought over the hack saw like it was their inheritance. Eli had cut his thumb and bled into the trench and watched how quickly the dirt had taken it and drunk it down. He had thought then that there was something beautiful and terrible about the way soil did not give a shit what it was asked to hold. Now, older, he thought maybe it did. Maybe there were better and worse men growing crops out of themselves and it mattered to nobody but their particular bodies whether they picked the better thing.

In the house, the phone stopped ringing. The little quiet that followed had a rawness in it. Then the slam of the screen door hit and then Nate’s voice, full with something that could be grief or relief or both, cut across the yard. “She’s up,” he shouted, and then softer as he approached, “She’s good. Your girl’s with her. They’re sitting her by the window. She’s talking about Uncle Manny’s goat.” He grinned, and it came over his face like something that had wanted to be there all day but had waited out of politeness. “She called me Nate.”

“She always calls you Nate,” Eli said, but he meant it the old way, the way that said sometimes no one called any of us by the names that mattered.

They stood with their hands on their hips and looked at the rows. They were not done; they would never be done. They would turn off the valve in an hour and the pressure would drop and in that last ten minutes, things would reveal themselves like men that had waited politely all day to make their needs known. Then they would touch each of those things, each of those needs, or they would not. Some days, the work was to triage. A body is a world. An orchard is a body. There are only so many things to sew shut before the light goes. Eli turned the coupler under his palm. He found himself patting it like a horse. He thought of the folder on the hummock, its pale face catching the last scrap of light, a number sitting there like something you told your son when you wanted him not to worry for at least through dinner.

“We’ll tell him Tuesday,” he said. “We’ll lease to him through harvest. If Mama is bad by winter, we look again.”

“We are bad now,” Nate said. He wasn’t arguing. He was naming the shape of a present thing. “But okay. Tuesday.”

They slid the green lid back onto the valve box and stomped it with their heels so it went flat. A mosquito, clumsy with the last good hour of its life, scanned the space near Eli’s neck. He flattened it with two open fingers and saw his own blood on his skin and wiped it away with one clean, casual sweep.

“Let’s see the end,” Nate said. They walked down the row to the figure-eight, and when Eli loosened it again a last flume of water came and with it a small dark body washed up and out and landed at his feet. He bent and looked. A vole, dead, swollen and sweet. It had made a house under their house and died there, the water at last giving it back to their sight.

He set it gently off to the side under the loose grass. He touched the clump of its wet fur with two fingers and the chill of it came into his hands like a lesson. He put the figure-eight back on the line. He pulled the tail of poly through the knot until the cap cinched tight. He and Nate stood then, and because they were their father’s sons and their mother’s, they looked a while without moving and let the last of the day lay itself down in layers on both of them until they could stand to walk back up beneath Eleanor and close the valve. When they shut it, the sound that left the orchard wasn’t silence—it was circulation, pause, a stranger kind of breath. They walked the center of the row, their shoulders almost touching, and in that space between their shoulders, every tree took one more drink.
