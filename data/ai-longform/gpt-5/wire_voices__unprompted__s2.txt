On Sundays the switchroom was a soft green cave, a hundred rectangles of light winking in steady rows, the cords drooping like braids, the air smelling faintly of warm dust and something metallic that clung to your hair even after more than one rinse. Sundays were supposed to be slower; the chief would sometimes lean against the glass to the supervisors’ station with her novel, and the girls with steady boyfriends would hum under their breath and write their initials in the frost on the windows when the air went thin in January. But after church and before the late news, when supper dishes were drying in racks across three counties, we came alive with a peculiar kind of longing. The clocks were set; everyone knew what a minute cost.

We didn’t have hands; we had fingers that could pin two plugs one-handed without looking, a wrist that could roll the rate card down to eight p.m. and read off “Muskogee to Rochester, person-to-person, first three minutes $1.35, each additional minute forty-five” like a lullaby. We listened the way old-time piano teachers used to balance a penny on the back of a child’s hand to teach them steadiness; we balanced our heads under our metal bands and heard without hearing. That’s what they trained us to do: assure connection, avoid content. If someone spoke directly to you, you could speak. You didn't insert yourself. You didn’t become part of it.

Even through the discipline — the whispered reminders in training class, the warning signs on the wall about privacy and professionalism and your duty as a representative of the Bell System — the voices found a way under your skin. There were lunch pail voices and Sunday coats voices; there were brittle voices with a shine on them like the lacquer on a drugstore Easter egg. There was the man who always cleared his throat three times before saying hello to his daughter in Pontiac, like he was putting on his shoes. There were the sisters who had survived three husbands between them and laughed as if I were another sister.

When I plug in and say, “Long distance,” and a voice gives me a number, I move it where it belongs. I imagine the thread. From my desk to a trunk, from that trunk to the border of a county, straddling wheat and winter and leachfields, hopping a river, crawling up a pole, crossing lines in someone’s garage of a central office, and down again. Some nights all the threads hum like a cat’s throat, and I sleep at home with a phantom ring circling my ear.

I knew their time because of the shadow it made. Seven-twenty on my Sunday readings, between a man in a blue-collar county who swore and dropped his nickel in my ear over and over and the new mother whose baby breathed milk-burbly on the line while her mother-in-law tsked. I came back from my supper break — meatloaf in a triangle of Pyrex at the vending machine table, coffee with that sweet film that skin forms when the urn’s sat too long — and made sure to get a seat near the center boards. You can feel which slots carry, after a while. It’s like an old checkers player glancing and, without meaning anything by it, taking the chair that gives him the high ground.

Seven-twenty-three, usually; sometimes seven-twenty-five if my timing wrist had been slow. “Long distance,” I said, and there he was.

“Operator, I’d like to make a person-to-person call to Miss Lila Crowley in Philadelphia. The number is… hold on,” a breath, the sound of paper being unfolded, of a room that was large and emptier than most men’s rooms are when they live alone, “Area code two-one-five, KEystone 7-4-3-2.”

He always said the exchange like it mattered, like those two old little letters were bars of music you didn’t just skip, though most people had been trained by now to say seventeen for KE. He spoke clear and slow, like he worked with numbers and wanted them announced right, and his voice had a quiet in it I came to know as well as my own kitchen.

“What is the name you are calling?” I said nonsmiling — don’t put emotion in it; don’t make your voice extra sweet; it sets them off.

“Miss Lila Crowley,” he said again, “person-to-person, please. And tell her it’s Thomas.” He paused. “She’ll take the call.”

Sometimes they didn’t, you see. If you’re charged only when the party you want actually comes on the line, you make it personal. If you’re estranged, you game us with false names. If you’re saving your only two dollars until the end of the week, you say everything in a rush in the arranged second where you tell him he’s “Frank,” and I’m supposed to say, “Frank’s not there,” and we all skate away uncharged and filthy with the lie.

But when he said “She’ll take the call,” he sounded not cocky but… well, as if there was a door between them only I could open, and he’d been waiting for the key.

I got Philadelphia. I got a ring that scratched like fine thread when it first came up and then smoothed as if someone had run their hand over it the way you smooth the nap on velvet; then the operator’s voice there, the vowel-tallness of her vowels. “Philadelphia,” she said, without question.

“Miss Lila Crowley, person-to-person,” I said. “KE 7-4-3-2.”

“Hold your line, please,” Philadelphia said, and I sat there with my thumb on the timing key and my little pencil with the eraser end chewed by someone before me tapping on the edge of my desk. One of the girls two seats down was telling the girl beside her without words — eyebrows and lips — about a dress she’d seen in the window on Main Street, a yellow one with a sash that would look wedding-bell good at the VFW on Friday if you had the right figure for it. The chief came out of the glass box and bent over Peggy’s shoulder. The rate card clicked cold under my little lamp. And then Philadelphia’s voice slid back into my ear. “Your party, Thomas,” she said, with a sound to her tone like someone handing you a wrapped package you already knew you were getting.

The connections can be clean as a glass of water or full of snow and switches and far-away poles whining in wind, but sometimes, rarely, you get a sound at the other end as clear and near as if the person were cupped in your palm. “Lila?” he said, and the “-la” of her name caught on the line for a second like a thread on a thorn. The breath on the other side gave, and then the thing they always did, the dance people have in them even if they’ve driven tractors all their lives: a doubling.

“Thomas? Thomas, can you hear me?”

“I can,” he said. “I can hear you just fine. Can you hear me?”

“Is there an echo?” she said. “I hear something. Say something else.”

“I’m here,” he said. “It’s Sunday.”

“It is Sunday,” she said. “You sound… well, you sound right next to me.”

“You too.” He lowered his voice a half step when he spoke to her, as if someone had put a hand to the back of his neck. “How are you?”

We are taught to clear off after the answer party has answered. Between person-to-person, you time and you go away. But “go away” means your electromagnetic heart remains there, beating. I started my timer and, because the line was as clean as a new penny and because the weight of their voices didn’t pull me back into the circuit, I pulled aside and reached for the next light. But I let them run right in the place behind my ear where someone telling the truth will always start to tingle.

They talked about nothing. The heaviest conversations in the world sound like nothing on the wire. That Sunday he asked about “the elm outside,” and she asked him how he’d done up the two-by-fours with that sticky stuff he’d been talking about, and he said you had to lay the paper, lay it, like you were ironing a shirt. She said her landlord was a pain. He laughed every time as if she had dropped something small and glittering at his feet. She laughed at him as if she were biting a caramel and it had stuck to her teeth, sticky and sweet and slow. The timer quoted the minutes to me. My pencil wrote them. I wasn’t supposed to keep anything not official, but I wrote his county on a scrap and her name in my cash drawer so I would find it each Sunday and not forget, as if forgetting were possible.

The second Sunday I recognized him before he gave me the number. “Operator,” he said, “I’d like KEystone 7-4—”

“Just a moment, sir,” I said, and it wasn’t until I had the Philadephia girl again that I allowed myself to say, quietly and to myself, “There you are,” as if I’d found a leaf I recognized in a river. The third Sunday she was breathy with a cold and he told her to put mustard around her ankles, and she laughed for three breaths and told him he had the sense of someone’s grandfather in a print on a diner wall. She asked him about a soldier I pieced together was his little brother. He asked her about her mother, which I gathered was brittle and serious and had already arranged her stories in a way that pressed thumbprints where they didn’t belong.

I didn’t know where he was — when he said the number, he never gave a town, just digits — but the rate card told me. We had a way of triangulating by looking up what it would cost. He was somewhere in Illinois, somewhere between Decatur and the big place where the river bends its mind. She was Philadelphia, but not Center City; you can hear the bus on some lines, the cars with their horns slapping sound around, and hers was leafier and with an older rhythm. She taught, I thought at first; the way she corrected him once about “ten items or fewer.” She ran a library desk, I thought after, and then felt foolish because the people who run library desks in my mind look a certain way and this one’s laugh would have broken the quiet.

I went home and boiled eggs and ate them standing, the yellow sticking to my tongue in a chalk way, and thought about the names my mother had tried to give me when I was born — Agnes and Ruth — and the way my father had said, No, let’s let her be Martha. It surprised me each time how much those voices had to do with me. They had nothing to do with me; that was the whole point. That was why the company liked to hire us very young and then again very old. We are the geography. We’re the shadow the voices pass through.

The Sunday after Dr. King died, the room was hot even if the windows were frozen shut with April wind, because people had been calling like kites in a storm since Thursday. The mood at the benches was careful. Most of the girls smiled like you smile at a child who has fallen and you aren’t sure if she is crying yet. My mother had called to say if the riots in Detroit came here, we were going to go to Aunt Liz’s out near the grain elevators where nobody burned anything but trash in barrels anyhow. That Sunday he spoke softer to her, and she was too quiet for a long time and then said, “I went down to Broad Street.”

“To the church?”

“To the parade,” she said. “Not a parade. It wasn’t a parade.” A sigh. “When they carried him. It wasn’t him, of course. But it was him.”

“I couldn’t go,” he said, and you could fill a cuff with the regret he poured out. “I thought I would go and then I didn’t go.”

“You would have had to drive a long way.”

“That’s a reason,” he said, “but not a good one.”

The Sunday after Mr. Kennedy was shot, he didn’t call until seven forty. I sat with my hand on the cord and made a show of being matter-of-fact. The chief had scolded me that afternoon for chatting with Mrs. Dolan when she called the office — Mrs. Dolan had a nephew at a paper in New York, and we all were feeling a little bound up with history in our chests. I kept my face clean. When he said “Miss Crowley,” my fingers worked. All over the room you could tell there were pairs trying to find each other, to say, I was awake when they said it, I was at my neighbor’s, I was at my Thursday night bowling, I was at the sink washing a knife when the radio cut that program off, we are the sort of people this happens to. Lila said in a small voice, “It came on right away. The anchor had tears in his voice. Did you hear it, Thomas? Did you… God, I’m tired.” She didn’t say “I’m scared,” because some of us were raised not to bother people with that, but it sat on the line like another operator, listening in.

They had a way of saying nothing that made me feel steadier in some part of myself that had been teetering on an edge since glad-rag girls had started coming in in their mini skirts and their hair going soft instead of shellacked and talking about protests like they were church suppers. I come from a family where you don’t stir up. We fixed what was ours to fix and didn’t phone to make the waves bigger. My brother teased me about my job that way, said, “You’re the quietest woman I know,” and I said, “It’s because I spend all day lending other people my ears.”

By July I had rearranged my supper on Sundays so that I did not have to be scraping my fork at seven-ten; I was always settled before their call. I glanced sometimes at the line of little lights at the very bottom that no one liked to get because it meant the Indian reservations way out West, where the switches did something odd and the echo could make you motion-sick if you sat on one too long. I tried to place each of them like I was building a map. The rate cards gave hints, and the calls themselves gave more: the way the sounds came off the paper in his room, the way hers had the far-off smash of a trolley once, the sound of someone hurrying upstairs in a house where the steps had been varnished and feel sticky in summer. I thought she had a roommate for one week because I heard a female voice say, “Tell him I said hello,” as if I were the one passing along the message, and the laughter on both ends of the line came up and met like two birds crossing an intersection. I thought he had a coworker named Don who told filthy jokes from the way Lila said, “Don’t let Don make you say that,” and he said in a grin of voice, “Darn,” and she said, “Don’t you ‘darn’ me, mister,” without it being awful.

I had other calls, of course. Delores, with the heavy makeup that left round marks on her cigarettes, got the funeral home calls because she could say “I’m sorry for your loss” without sounding like an actor. Eleanor, who was older and whose hair was always pinned crisp as a hymn, did the farmer calls because she talked to them about the weather in a way that made them feel they were not wasting their nickels. I did the boyfriend calls by accident because boys liked my voice, and I had to keep it flat because otherwise I would be asked by the chief to go to training again and sit with my knees together in that room the color of oatmeal and be told that I was not a girl on the call; I was a bridge.

Still: seven-twenty-something on Sundays belonged to Thomas-and-Lila. They came to me, and I gave them to each other, and I was a bee in their room, not necessary but buzzing, and when their voices moved, I moved with them, so when the time slid by to seven-thirty-two one Sunday in September and slid past as if it were merely slick with rain, my hands did not do their job on some pieces of light because I was accounting for their absence.

If a call does not come, you cannot will it. If the chief could have willed the “out-of-service” lights to stop winking when lines were down after a big wind, she would have been the queen of some place more interesting than our little city. You learn to soften your stomach, like a dancer who manages to lower herself to the floor rather than be dropped. I adjusted.

At seven-forty I was in the middle of collecting for a hotel-to-phone call and explaining to an irate man what the rate was and why his on-account did not apply when I caught a wish of his voice behind another light. There’s no sense to this thing: you cannot hear who talks to the girl two benches down, you do not get a private sound that summons you, and yet it happens: a piece of air lifts in your ear and you turn your head as if someone has walked in.

“Operator, person-to-person for Mr. Thomas Merrick, please,” Lila said, and I had to stop my mouth from answering the other man with her name. I completed his transaction in a voice that held back all its color, and then I turned to her.

“What is the number you’re calling?” I said, only English in my mouth.

She read it off. It was him, you see, by the rate I had triangulated without meaning to; it was the same as always. I could say his number in my sleep by then, though the rules would insist that I could not. There are so many ways to do wrong in this job by loving it slightly. The chief was not unkind, but she was a machine that could crank down on you like a boiler if you stepped your foot where you shouldn’t. But I let that warmth in my voice slip for just the syllable of “please” as it went back to her.

“Hold your line,” I said. I held my line. I rang.

The line rang in an emptier house than it used to. If you think I am anthropomorphizing the ring, then you have not rung the same number every week for months. The ring has a belly in it when a couch stands against the wall and a coat is hung with its weight on the one good peg and the second-to-top stair is loose. It has a flat to it when the rooms hatbox themselves. This rang into no fabric at all, a suitcase left open on a bed, the window letting in the sound of whatever tree is out front shaking its hands in a September breeze. The cadence of ring is twice for us, then wait; the one that hears it in the room clocks it differently. I rang the allotted number of cycles. No one answered. I tried him again.

“Operator,” she said in my ear, calm in the way of someone those rules had been taught to in infancy without a switchboard present, “keep trying, please.”

“Of course,” I said.

Somewhere down the boards a coin toll girl was having a fight, her hand cupped at her ear to make the nickel-thin aviary of her world sharper; somewhere a baby cried; somewhere, because it’s always the case, someone was learning how far fury could travel. I tried him again. No one answered.

“We can keep checking your line,” I said, because that was the language in the book. “If Mr. Merrick answers, we will call you back to the line.” Another operator might have asked her to hang up so I could clear her slot. It the Sunday slow under the hum in the room, and I could spare it. “We can try him. We’ll call you back.” I did not say, “I will,” because then there is a finger of yours inside their home.

“Thank you, operator,” she said. I wanted — in a foolish part of myself that had been eating boiled eggs at night and had decided to care about two names that would never think about a person named Martha two states away — I wanted to ask if she wanted me to try a different number. I wanted to ask if Mr. Merrick drove an Oldsmobile and if he was the sort of man who left early without saying goodbye because he couldn’t bear the goodbye part. I wanted to tell her the ring was coming through hard and hollow. I kept my jaw where it belonged.

We have a form for person-to-person when the party does not answer. You stamp it; you tuck it; you put it somewhere. I put hers higher than I should have, so it would not get the coffee ring someone would inevitably leave that night. I tried him again at seven-fifty. At eight. Eight-ten. When she came back on at eight-twenty-four and said, “Operator? Anything?” I did not make her say it twice.

“I’m sorry, there’s been no answer,” I said. “Would you like me to keep trying?”

“Once more,” she said. “Please.” The second please of the night, different from the first; a different wish. “And then… could I leave a message?”

“We do not deliver messages,” I said, the book in my mouth. My hand did a small thing by itself, which was to lift to the quiet jack we used when a supervisor was to monitor a trainee and set it down next to my card. It is a neutral gesture. It is a nervous sort of thing to do, like a smoker’s flick. “But if he answers while your line is still engaged, I can return you to the connection.”

“I’ll wait,” she said.

We are not a waiting service, but we are the object you end up holding when you are waiting. At eight-thirty-two I offered to place her on hold while I tried his number again. She said yes. When I brought the hollow ring back to my own ear I suddenly thought: he could be sick. It was not the first thought you should have; you should think he is at a movie by himself, he is at a diner two towns over, he drove to see his mother and forgot his watch, he is at bowling, he is somewhere. But sound can draw pictures and the picture of that empty room was exact. It had a light bulb hanging, and the shade was missing because he had taken it down to pack it and then put it back up unshaded because he had gotten used to it. He had left two glasses in the sink. He had walked barefoot for a second on the kitchen tile before remembering to put his shoes back on. And then I thought: gone. Not “dead,” the word I do not let on my board. Gone.

“I’m sorry,” I told her again when I came back. She said nothing for a moment, and when she tried to speak again, the sound your throat makes when it remembers it exists made the first noise.

“Can you tell me his line is connected?” she said.

I checked my reading by the little metric on my desk I’m not supposed to speak about like I am not supposed to speak about a lot of things. “The line is working,” I said truthfully. “There’s no technical difficulty indicated.”

“Thank you,” she said. There was a sound that had not been in their calls before — the way a hand sounds when it rubs across a face and changes the shape of it and then lets the flesh go back.

We finished the call in the proper manner. I closed her line. I slipped the top sheet of her paperwork shaped exactly like all the other top sheets into the slot in my drawer and closed it and counted a dollar from petty cash and paid for a coffee at the urn I did not drink and stood with it by the window because it gave me something to hold that wasn’t a taxicab coin in my hand. I could see the alley where the lamp made a cone and the moths fell through it like snow.

The room went on. It always goes on. A girl cried because her fiancé called her by mistake and then apologized for it, said he’d meant to call his mother first because he needed to tell her everything and then he would tell his fiancée everything and by then it had become a quarrel; a man asked if I could connect him to an operator in Danville who would know the girl who sang at the Legion because he thought her name was Sarah and he wasn’t sure; somewhere someone asked for a coin to be returned that had not, in fact, fallen; somewhere a child told his father, “Say hi to Uncle Tommy,” and then the father said, “When he’s here,” and then both of them exhaled.

At nine, the chief pinched her cigarette in half in her ashtray and told me to go to break, and I went, and instead of going to the vending machine basement room, I took my coat because the air had turned in that way it does in early fall and walked down the back stairs onto the cinders. I was not the only one. Eleanor was out there, and she said, “Can you believe this nonsense?” and told me about the new rate thing that had come down from the city and how no one would be able to understand it and they ought to hire people who understood human language to write the memorandums. She patted my elbow and asked me about my mother’s cat and told me she had seen the young ones out on Saturday night at the dance hall off Route 6 bending in a new kind of way. She stirred the world with her words while I stood in it.

When I went back to my board I glanced automatically at the time. I would be on one more hour and then Dolores would take over and wear her lips to the end of their Sunday usefulness. I did my work. I tried not to listen with the part of myself that had turned, this summer, to hearing. But at nine-thirty-eight, when I had leaned for the fourth time to catch a man telling all the numbers in his wife’s family birthdates to the operator in Topeka and telling her to “just try them,” my light tagged “Philadelphia” came in in that side-of-the-head way again, and without looking I pulled it. If the chief had been watching, she would have seen nothing — this was my work, I was doing my work — but if I had been watching me, I would have seen that I answered that light as if someone had touched the thin skin under my arm.

“Long distance,” I said.

“I’m calling again for Mr. Thomas Merrick,” she said, and her voice had been ironed. The breath that had pulled apart the earphone between us earlier had been put away in a drawer. She gave me the number.

“He has not answered,” I said.

“Try again,” she said with a politeness that put paid to anyone’s thinking she was a woman who took things lying down.

I tried. The ring rang. The moths flew through the alley light I had seen earlier because now when I am at my desk and I hear the wind through a line I picture the moths, not the wind-blown Kansas. Someone picked up.

“Hello?” a boy’s voice said. A neighbor. It was too clear to be a boy on a farm; it was the milk of a hallway in a four-unit building. A boy after his bath, his shoulders smelling of soap, his hair parting with a comb for school tomorrow. I didn’t know if I was allowed to love anyone I connected to this much; the little hole where my training sat seemed to be filling with the feeling anyway.

“Operator,” I said. “Person-to-person call for Mr. Thomas Merrick.” The boy repeated the name, credulous, as boys repeat the names of the men who live in rooms they are not allowed into. “Is Mr. Merrick available?”

“He moved,” the boy said, with the relish of telling. “He left last Tuesday. The man took his bed out through the back and it hit the stair rail and he said the bad word and his mother said, ‘Don’t you say the bad word,’ and then Mr. Merrick laughed, and I waved, and he said he didn’t know where he was gonna go yet. But he had a suitcase.”

He had said he. The room in my ear had no furniture in it. I did what I have been trained to do when a boy is telling me more than I am supposed to be hearing and there is someone waiting on the other jack to be told whether to pay. I said, “Thank you,” and I asked, “Is there anyone I can speak to to confirm?” and the boy called his mother. Her voice came on, kind but careful, and I said the thing I am supposed to say — “Is Mr. Merrick available for a person-to-person call?” — and she said, “He moved,” with an adult spare shoulder. “We believe he left a forwarding address with the postal, but we don’t know it.”

“I appreciate your assistance,” I said, and the boy shouted, “Tell him Jerry says hi!”

I came back to her. In that half second, before you bring one party back, you look at your own face in the reflection in the glass of your light and you decide what your face is going to sound like. You make it the book. You make it something you could put on the wall. You make it not be your face at all.

“Your party has moved,” I said. “There is no forwarding number available at this time.”

Silence. I could hear a bus outside her house or a car on a distant street; you learn to tell the difference by how the sound collects. A bus is a big animal. A car is a stone skipping. I thought she might say, Could you call back and ask the woman — I would have; I could have — or that she might tell me to look something up. All of this would have been bending the rules. There are ways to bend in the world like ours. We are not the law. But she surprised me by doing what the book would have written her to do if the book knew who she was. She drew a long breath and spoke slowly so I would get every word.

“Thank you, operator,” she said. “Please cancel any further attempts.”

“Do you wish to place any additional calls?” I said by rote. She did not. I settled the line. I wrote “Moved” on the top of Thomas Merrick’s slot in a pencil as light as I could manage so that the chief would not see I had written anything that was not numerals. I went to my next light and my next.

I do not go looking for other people’s names when I am off my board. You are not supposed to. I have not been the sort of person, even before this job, to call anyone up and ask for secrets. But after my shift that night I did not go home the way I always go home. I walked a block farther down, to the pedestal on the corner with the phone in it with its door that sticks now that the rain has dried inside the wood, and I dialed information for the city in Illinois that matched the rate card closest to his. “Merrick, Thomas,” I said, and when the girl there told me there was nothing listed, I waited a beat and then said, “Any Merricks on the street that begins with the letter H?” She said, “Ma’am, I can’t—” and then stopped, and I heard myself exhale and I knew that she was doing her own bending down at her own board, and she said, “A. Merrick on Hartley. J. Merrick on Jackson. That’s what I have.”

“Thank you,” I said. I did not write the addresses down. I went home and made myself toast in the pan and ate it without butter because I had taken a coffee I did not drink out of the machine and I felt the need to square my accounts with the night.

I slept and woke twice with the feeling that something had been dropped at the end of some distance and was calling to me like a penny in a well. In the morning, I folded my uniform blouse and pressed the collar with the side of my thumbnail and pinned my hair and went in and sat in my seat and did not look at the “Moved” penciled faint as dust over the top line of that slot because in the daylight things should be what they are.

She did not call on Monday. She did not call that week. She did not call the next Sunday. A gap is a different animal from never. Never has its own peace to it; you can take your sweater off if you are never going to go outdoors. A gap makes you get up twice to see if the kettle has boiled. I shifted my supper the other direction, forward into the hour when my mother called and told me about the cats that came to her back stoop, the yellow one with his left ear notched as if someone had clipped it, and the gray one with her belly hanging low as if she had had too many litters too young.

I did my work. I stopped writing letters on my slips — no private marks, the chief had scolded when she saw a little star I had made next to a funeral call so I could pay extra attention — and I took the calls that came to me without recognizing half of them for months in their socks. But she was in my frequencies. You cannot un-ring a bell; you cannot un-hear a tone once it has set itself in your head. Every time a Philadelphia connection came through I stood a little straighter at my board.

In October the leaves start dying in New England in spectacular ways that have no business being called dead. Here, it’s slower and lesser, but you still get a red like a polished nail now and again, and it makes you want to look up even if you do not have anyone to look up for. One Sunday before the time that had always been theirs, a Philadelphia call came in with a distinct thrum to the line like there was a truck idling under the window of the party. I took it. “Long distance,” I said. A woman not much older than me — you can tell, you can always tell — gave me a number and said, “Person-to-person for Mr. Nathan Crowley.” Crowley. The chest is a box and then something knocks inside it; you are supposed to keep your hands at your side when that happens.

“Your name?” I said, the form in my bones enough to ask.

“This is his sister, Lila,” she said. “He’ll be home. Tell him it’s Lila.” She said her name with none of the drag her voice had pulled across the wire with Thomas. She put on something bright to talk to her brother and she wore it right, like when you put red lipstick on not to see if you can but because it matches the day.

“Hold your line,” I said. I got their house. A woman answered — older, the sort of older you know because there is flour on her hands even if there is no oven, the sort of older who does not let flour dry on her hands, who wipes it off but you hear it anyway — and called for Nathan, and he came to the line and yelled, “Lila!” like a boy again and told her that the splinter he had gotten in his palm that morning had come out and that tomorrow he was going to try to put the shelves up in the mudroom even though their father had told him he’d ruin the plaster.

I timed. I made a note of their charges. I started the clock and watched the red of the minute creep because that is the thing you learn to do to soothe yourself — to watch something inexorable proceed. I did not linger. I did not bend. I connected and withdrew and made my neat notes. I listened for my other lights. And yet: when Lila laughed, there was something in it like a jar had been set down with care on a shelf you had been certain would be left empty. They talked like people who have obligations. They talked like a brother and sister who have to tell each other all the news before someone else tells an edited version. They said, “We should get the curtains before the first frost or else we’ll never do it in time,” and “Did you get the sugar? Because if you didn’t get the sugar, Nana will act like you forgot on purpose.”

And then at a break in the flow where I could idly raise my hand to stretch my fingers without bumping another key and while both of them were laughing over the remembered phrase their father had always said when he dropped a tool on his foot — something about “cats and crows,” which must have been family code for cursing — her laugh did a thing it had never done with Thomas: it slid down a step. She said, “I don’t know if he’s living like he thinks he ought.”

“Who?” Nathan said.

“Thomas,” she said. She did not say his last name. She would not have said his last name, in a house with flour hands in it and a brother on the line, in the presence of a woman she did not know at a board. Her voice was very quiet and completely without drama, the way you say that if it freezes by Wednesday you’ll bring the plants in Tuesday night. “He didn’t answer last month. He didn’t answer the next week. He’s gone off in his head and I don’t know if he’ll land where he can be reached.”

Nathan, in the way some men think first with comfort and then with intelligence, said, “Maybe his car broke down,” and she laughed in that way this line had taught me and said, “Maybe the world flipped over and we all look silly for thinking it’s the same thing.”

And then she said, “It’s fine.” In that most womanly sentence. It’s fine.

It’s fine really means there is a small kettle boiling somewhere you are ignoring. It really means I will not use my breath to describe a thing you cannot fix. It means, to people like me who have spent their lives being the hinge, you must not lean.

I didn’t lean. I finished my timing. I told them their charges. I closed the line. I put my pencil in the tray. I took her first name out of my drawer where it had been sitting on the piece of odd paper I keep there to write things on when something in me will not be content until I have written something, and I opened my palm and tipped it into the trash can. It lay there unhappy with the coffee grounds until I went back to the lunchroom after my shift and dumped the whole trash can into the bigger one and watched it go, a little line of L and a little curl of a.

Sometimes when you open the board in the morning, there are lines that hold old heat. I put my hands on the keys and felt ordinary. The whisper under the cranked-out hum of the room was what it always had been. It was late enough in the year that the window at the end of the row showed the light short as a cigar, and I thought, not for the first time in months, of my own brother in a barracks too many states west, and whether he would call my mother collect, like he always did, and whether he would only talk to her and never ask to talk to me because our family had arranged itself in that polite way that keeps the songs going but makes nobody dance. I thought of the boy in the hallway who had told me Thomas had moved and his mother who had been kind but careful and wondered if they had lightbulbs out in that hall at night and if the boy would be scared to climb the stairs, and whether anyone would diversify the stock at the drugstore now that the pharmacist had lost his wife and the displays had gone shabby.

In the weeks after we get a new kind of absence, the air in the switchroom changes in an unprovable way. People call and the calls are themselves, and yet… there’s a layer of muslin in the air. There’s a grit as fine as flour. The chief turned the radiator up one day and turned it down the next and said, “Do you feel a draft?” and we all lifted our shoulders and said we didn’t. We did.

People walked in off the street with coins for the pay station in our lobby and asked to use our desk because the pay station ate coins like a boy eats candy and would cough them back up with a tinny burp. I would watch their mouths move through the glass and think: if I took a step I could be an ordinary woman handing you a receiver; if I kept seated I could be the machine. I stayed seated.

The first snow always smells like itself and not like anything on earth. That year it came early, fat, wet, with a taste like tin on the back of your teeth if you stuck your mouth open toward it. I walked out of the building with the girls at ten on a Tuesday with our coats buttoned up and our hats pulled down and stood there for a second because the air was so clean it seemed insulting to go anywhere in it. The jail across the alley had its one window lit and a man’s shadow crossed it and I wondered if his mother ever called him or whether he was in a place where the calls only come at fixed hours and then are given to you like you’ve earned a sweet. I thought of the thing I was not supposed to be thinking of, the two voices on Sunday nights as if you could hold on to the thing that was gone by thinking it exactly. People speak into a phone with their lips against the wrong place sometimes; they hold the thing wrong, as if it were a harmonica and not a machine. I had loved correcting that all my life; I had loved telling them to slide their voice to the correct opening so it could go where it needed to go. You cannot correct the air when it carries loss. You can say nothing at all, and let it go through you.

On a Sunday in November, a man called person-to-person for a woman in our town, and when she answered she said, “I told you not to call here,” and he said “I didn’t have a choice,” and she said, “You always have a choice,” and then both of them started to cry. I felt the burn of the edge of my earpiece dig into the top of my ear where the skin is tender from the metal, and I held the timing lever like a hand. I thought: that is their rope, that crying, not mine. That is their ceiling, their floor, their walls; I am the mousehole. I thought of Lila and her brother, now a rope for each other. I thought of the trees on her block in Philadelphia shaking their hands at the bus as it went by. I thought of a man somewhere with a bed that had banged a bannister and a suitcase and the knowledge, which sometimes is enough, that on Sundays at a particular hour a bell would ring in one room far away because his number had been called.

When I was a girl, my father used to take me to the railway yard on Saturday afternoons if my mother had gone to her sister’s. He would lift me to sit on a fence and we would watch the couplings. The men down there had a way of making what was heavy look as if it remembered being light. He would put his hands in his coat and say, “Listen,” and I would listen, and he would say, “You can tell where they’re going by the sound. Empty runs lighter.” It was never true in the literal way he meant but it was exactly, marvellously, true of the world. He died the year I finished training, and the day after the funeral I sat at my board and waited for a light, any light, to tell me who I was supposed to be, and it came and it said, “Number, please,” in a voice that had no judgment on my grief at all, and for the first time all week I did exactly as I was told.

The Sunday after Thanksgiving, turkey bones simmered for soup on stoves in three states in my ear. I had brought a slice of pumpkin pie wrapped in wax paper from my mother’s and I ate it leaning over a white paper napkin to catch the flakes. The church bells at Saint Mary’s had rung at six and the sound of them had followed certain calls like a persistent dog. At seven-twenty-one, an Illinois call came in, a different number, a man with a voice too fast, telling me he needed to reach Philadelphia and he needed to do it tonight. “It’s the Crowley home,” he said. “Any Crowley. Try them. Please.”

I tried the number Lila had used that day for her brother, because it was what I had and because if the chief had been standing square behind me I could have explained it as “the last known.” The line rang, warm, like a sweater hung to dry in a kitchen where soup is simmering; it rang over a floor where a child had just run across it and then back and slid; it rang into a sentiment I had learned belonged to that house and those people. The older woman answered. I said, “Philadelphia operator,” because that is what we say when we reach another operator’s region and we play-act being them in order to keep the line simple. “I have a person-to-person from out of state, for any member of the Crowley family.” She gave a short, efficient, loving sound.

“Nathan!” she called. Laughter, footsteps. He came to the phone and I did the hand-off. I did not know, could not know, had no right whatsoever to know — and yet I did, in some little tucked-away part that lives off scraps — that the man on the other end, in his too-fast voice, was looking down at a scrap of paper in his own hand, one where someone had written a number in a hurry, where there was a coffee ring touching the corner, and on it was the Crowley home and the words: for Lila.

They spoke, two men with a woman between them like a place. “She’s not here,” Nathan said. “She’s been out walking.” The other said, “I can call later,” and Nathan said, “No, don’t. She’ll be back, and if you call back she’ll answer, and if she answers she’ll say hello, and if she says hello the world might figure out what shape it has to keep to hold itself together.” He did not say that last part, of course, but it was there in the room with the soup. He said, “Call back at eight.”

The man said, carefully, “Tell her Thomas called.”

I hung up the line. I waited with my hands where they belonged. I did not get up from my board or say someone else could take my next light. I did not tell the chief I was going off to stretch my legs. I did what you have to do when what you want is to be a girl in a doorway rather than a woman at a panel. I sat. At eight, which is to say precisely when the rate changes, precisely when the penny becomes two and the old men in suspenders waiting near the pay station slap their thighs and say, “Now,” the call came again. “Long distance,” I said. “Person-to-person for Miss Lila Crowley,” the man said, “from Thomas,” and I gave him to them, and the Philadelphia operator came up on the other side as if the world had put itself into our hands with the certain knowledge that it belonged to everybody.

“Lila?” he said.

“Thomas,” she said.

Between them, the lines crossed a river, a road with the first snow on it turning to Knife and then to Sugar, a boy’s hallway with a light bulb burnt out at one end and a slipper like a little waiting boat beside a door, a kitchen that smelled like soup, a house with a woman in it who remembers to wipe the flour off her hands, and the room he stood in now, wherever it was, the kind of room that is either the start of something or the end, with two glasses in the sink. They said each other’s names as if this were a table they had set together once when they were young and still set together now without fighting over where to put the forks.

I started my timer. I did my job. I did not uncase the soft animal in my chest. I did not lean. I did not stay. I tapped my pencil against my shelf in a rhythm only I know. But sometimes, when I am very old and my hands have gone to parchment and the machines I sat at are in a museum behind a rope that children reach under to touch, I will be able to close my eyes and put my ear to any door and hear the particular way she took his name into her mouth and made of it the sort of word you can live inside. I will remember that I was there, that the circuits went where they were supposed to go, that someone asked me only for what my hands could give, and I gave it.

After their call that night, I untopped my pencil and erased, very sharply and slowly, the pale “Moved” I had written over his slot. I did not write anything new. A cardinal had been hitting itself against the windows of the switchroom lately, crazed by its own reflection in the glass; it came again while I sat there and thunked its body once, twice, and then flew off in a huff to the telephone wires that were doing their work like old women knitting in a semi-circle. My shift ended at ten. I took my coat. I went down the stairs and out to the alley and tilted my head up. The night had no moon. When a far-off train made a sound along a bend I could not see, I listened, and by the weight of it I decided I could tell that it carried something home.
