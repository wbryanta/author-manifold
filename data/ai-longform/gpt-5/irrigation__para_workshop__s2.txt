By eight the trench had him up to his hips in damp dirt that clung to his jeans like a second skin, and the June sun hadn't yet found its bite. He was down there with a shovel angled between his boots, one hand on the cool length of Schedule 40, the other raking a cave into the wall to lay the broken line back as if he were tucking some child to sleep. The cut had split lengthwise along a seam, a white gash with the edges feathered and rough where the PVC had grown brittle and surrendered to pressure. Two rows of apricot trees flanked him, leaves making a sound like paper rubbed between fingers, a sound he couldn’t hear without thinking of his father’s arm reaching out the window of a Ford and brushing at them as he drove. The trees were older than Luke’s first trip away to the city, older than the little scar under Ben’s eye. They needed water and that was what today was.

A truck rattled up along the end of the row, gravel skittering from the tires like thrown marbles. He didn’t look. He knew the gait of the engine by the way it fell off the idle near the pump shed and came on again; it had always sounded vaguely embarrassed about itself, as though the rattling were personal.

“You look like a gopher,” Luke said from above him, a shadow falling over the trench.

Ben straightened in the hole and felt the fan of his back crack in a succession of dry pops. “Bring the couplings?”

“I was going to ask if you wanted the good primer or the purple stuff that stains your fingers and makes you look like a teenage boy with Kool-Aid addiction.”

“Either. Both.”

Ben stepped on the shovel and the shovel cut a clean slice and he pried free a clump of heavy soil. Luke set a box on the rim of the trench and crouched. Sweat had already printed half-moons under his eyes and darkened the curve between his shoulder blades. He had not yet found a hat. His hair, longer than it had ever been in high school, stuck up as though he’d surprised himself by appearing in the field and didn’t know which way to comb to get back out.

“They make any smaller?” Luke said, pulling out a length of slip coupling and holding it near one eye. His hands were city hands, soft on top, though the knuckles were nicked as usual; Luke had never managed to be a creature that moved through the world without bumping into it.

“We’ve got three-inch line and three-inch couplings,” Ben said. He reached up and took it. The sunlight through the plastic made a pale translucence, like bone held against a bright sky. “You bring cement?”

Luke reached and produced two blue cans with sloshing insides. “And coffee,” he added, as if this were the actual salvage piece of the morning.

Ben wiped a damp forearm across his forehead and left a streak of gray. “Set it there. I’ll prime.”

“You want to do it, huh?”

“I want it to not blow out when we charge the line.”

“Got it.” Luke put the coffee on the post by the end of the row, the lid already beading sweat on top of sweat, and swung himself down into the trench in one quick movement that made the dirt walls crumble. He landed with a soft curse and his boot heel slid into the muck. “Fuck.”

“Language,” Ben said, out of habit.

“Nobody here but us and the gophers, Reverend.”

Ben reached and steadied the pipe with the toe of his boot. They had done this any number of times when they were younger, never on the mainline then, only on the thin black laterals that fed the drippers. With those it was cutting tape and pushing barbed tees into pliant rubber. You could do it with your hands and it took you no deeper than your knees. The mainline had a different gravity. You felt it even in the ground near it, the way the earth turned slick in the top few inches as if the pipe had sweated over time.

“Dad always said smell would give you cancer,” Luke said, and then he grimaced. “I didn’t mean—”

“I know what you meant,” Ben said. “Hold it steady.”

He uncapped the can with his thumbnail and the smell of solvent came clean and sharp as a slap, sweet in a way that made the better part of him lean toward it even as his nose tried to turn away. He dipped the dauber and ringed the inside of the coupling with purple, the thick line of it wet and absolute. He turned to the broken edge of pipe and painted that, too, then did the other cut and the other coupling. His fingers bloomed with stain; there was no way to keep it off. The older he got the more he moved toward his father in that, in the acceptance of mess.

They pushed the first coupling on together. Luke held the seam lined up with the seam on the pipe as if aligning something with something would make a whole. Ben felt the resistance and then the give, that pop that says yes, it’s seated, you can breathe. He corkscrewed his palms to seat the fitting deeper and felt his hands slip on their own sweat. He let the coupling go and picked up the cement. “Ready?”

Luke nodded. He was staring at the long brown row that ran past them, his mouth set. The apricots were small and green-hard, an olive color, the fuzz just visible if you put one close to your eye. A shrike flicked from one branch to another and the world held its breath for that flying weight.

“You thinking about it?” Ben said.

“About whether it’s going to hold?”

“About selling.”

Luke’s eyes came back and met his. They were still the same inconveniently clear blue they had always been, the color that made coaches pick him for teams and women smile because they felt seen. “Jesus, Ben. Morning.”

“I don’t want to get to the end of this pipe and pretend we’re asking less important questions.”

“We’re not even at the elbow. You going to start in this trench?”

“Seems as good a place,” Ben said. He daubed cement on the purple and the purple smeared and became something beachy and garish, a color that belonged on a child’s bathing suit. He pushed the second coupling into place and felt the hot pressure flare along his palm. His heart showed up in his mouth for a moment and then went on. The elbow would come when they had installed the short bridging length. It would take cutting the pipe back further; it would take measuring and being sure.

Luke picked up one of the couplings and sniffed it. “We’re already here,” he said, and he wasn’t talking about the plastic. “This is a day about fixing a line in a field so that some water can reach some trees. It’s not a day about lawyers and appraisals.”

“It’s about both.”

Luke’s mouth softened with sympathy that Ben resented without being able to name it. “Ben, you’ve been here. You don’t have to make this trench do two jobs.”

They set the short bridging piece between the couplings and Luke held it while Ben painted and twisted and felt the clean urgency of getting pieces together before heat made them seal prematurely. A collection of barn swallows stitched the sky above the trees and small ants moved along the cut edge of the trench as if the line of earth were a border they had been waiting for someone to draw. The smell of the river hung faintly in the air, even though the river itself had been a thin ribbon since April, more mud than water by the time it hit the bridges.

At the pump house a magpie landed on the roof, head tipped. Ben could hear the faint chink of the float valve against the tank, could hear his father’s voice in his head listing the sounds worth your attention and the sounds you took as weather. Cavitation, that bubbly rattle at the throat of the pump when the water came too shallow. The fsssst of a split micro-sprinkler throwing onto bare dirt. The false hiss of a tape whose end had worked free so it chattered without purpose under the leaves. There was a game to be played in finding the broken piece of a system, and it had rules, and they were fair. You went down the line and you listened and you paid attention. The rules had always pleased him.

They climbed out of the trench to let the cement take before they backfilled. Luke moved like a man who had come from elsewhere. His footing was strange; there was too much ground under him and it made him choose where to put his weight. He went to the coffee and sipped and made a face. “Hot.”

“What did you think it would be?”

“Not this.” He set the cup back and looked at the orchard. The trees marched in twenty-five rows to the slope of the irrigation district ditch to the east, then turned again and again to the far fence that separated them from the highway. Beyond the fence were the fields the Cartwrights had planted in walnuts ten years ago when everyone said almonds were done, and beyond those the first faint suggestion of a housing addition that had stalled when a second drought came and money got shy. The addition had a sign that promised a community pool and pocket parks. The sign bleached and flapped and had a bullet hole punched clean through the promise of the pool’s blue square. “How much do you think?” Luke asked. “The developers. What do you think they’d really pay?”

“Does it matter, if you’ve already decided to say yes?”

“I haven’t decided anything.”

“You were already saying words like escrow on the phone,” Ben said. He leaned an elbow on the post and his shirt stuck where it touched anything.

“That’s not a sacred word,” Luke said quietly. “It’s a word that means we would get a check and Mom wouldn’t have to worry about a pump that sounds like a person choking to death in the middle of the night and taxes that hike every season because some bureaucrat thirty miles away thinks orchards are a luxury item.”

“Forty-seven years,” Ben said.

“What, of your life? You aren’t forty-seven.”

“Forty-seven years of that pump. That pump carried water for apricots, peaches, plums, and then back to apricots. Dad said the first one died and he and Grandpa hauled this one in on a dolly they stole from the rail yard and a prayer. Forty-seven years,” Ben said again as if the number could build a wall the way dirt mounding back into a trench can hold.

Luke took another drink of coffee and then gave up again. He set the cup on the post and it jittered, crowding a ring of sugar ants that had been exploring the flat top. “You want to be the man who sits beside it when it stops?” he asked. “You want to be listening to it and watching the river drop and you want that to be the thing that tells you that your life is over?”

“Don’t do that,” Ben said. He didn’t hear himself raise his voice until he saw Luke’s eyes harden and flicker. “Don’t take everything and put it under a big sign that says life. We can’t be something and not do something.”

“You want to keep doing this. I’m trying to say I don’t think doing this is going to make a life that you want.”

“You don’t know what I want.”

“Don’t I?”

They didn’t speak for a long minute. The air stirred and a tight bloom of cotton fluff drifted from the stand of trees by the ditch. A tractor coughed two fields over, gear teeth complaining, and then fell silent. In the neighbor’s pasture a horse swished its tail and cannoned sideways because a fly had walked over the exact wrong piece of its hide. There was a slowness to the way late morning came on in June; it always surprised you because it should go in long white strides, but instead it came like someone you knew well, pausing every twenty yards to look up at the sky as if it were deciding whether to keep going at all.

“Remember when he used to carry us in the bed of the truck to the stand?” Luke said. “Lids off, your face every which way with peach fuzz.”

“Dad would go out in his socks to pick before it got this hot,” Ben said. “He’d tell us to take the truck and he’d just go—” He couldn’t help half-smile at the image. His father had a particular gait in his socks, careful and unembarrassed, like a man crossing a stream on stones, though the ground was only grass. “He’d hand us a basket of seconds and say, go trade that to McCallister for a sack of nails.”

“And we’d want to go ourselves to McCallister’s because they kept that old dog,” Luke said, “the one with the one ear like a flag.”

“Which bit you when you tried to kiss it because you were four and thought all dogs were for kissing.”

“Still a betrayal,” Luke said.

“I told you not to.”

“You told me not to because you wanted to be the one to kiss him first.”

Ben surprised himself by laughing. When he stopped, it wasn’t because laughter had run out. It was because his throat caught on something hard. He put his fingers to his neck and pressed until he swallowed past it. Luke saw and looked away. He crouched, his legs making a triangle of shade on the pale dirt, and knocked a small rock with the back of his knuckle.

“Mom took a nap at the table yesterday,” Luke said. “Just put her head down on the wood like a kid. I stood there like an idiot with my car keys.”

“She doesn’t sleep. She tosses. She always tossed even when Dad was here. She can’t take a nap in a bed.”

“She always acted like asleep was something she had to talk herself into a little bit,” Luke said. “Like it was someone she was dating and didn’t want to be too eager with.”

“You hit way too many words with jokes,” Ben said, not unkindly.

“Occupational hazard.”

“You can quit that occupation.”

“Can you quit this one?”

Ben slid down into the trench again and began to pull dirt back over the pipe with his forearms, careful not to knock the couplings as they set. The mud slid and stuck and made cool kisses all down his forearms. He pressed and packed. The physical act of it put his breathing into some rhythmlike pattern, like the one in the old hymns their mother still knew by head more than heart. He could have stayed there a long time without needing words again. He felt rather than saw Luke move to the opposite side and do the same, their hands meeting in the middle sometimes like men reunited through a dumb wall of earth. When they got to the top Luke climbed out and kicked earth with his heel and tamped it. They put a couple of flat stones on it for memory, not for need.

“End furrow’s got weeds,” Ben said. “If I turn the water up too much, it’ll wash. We’ve never had enough flow again to carry a real head.”

“Do you have to say it like a eulogy?”

“I don’t know how to say anything else,” Ben said.

They walked the row. Luke broke off to tug on a micro-sprinkler at the base of the fifth tree and then the seventh, habit making him look for the cracked ones and the ones that had clogged with grit. The trees were set with the old-style spaghetti line buttons still in some places, the kind you could pop out with a pocketknife and seat again with a thumb. There were places where gophers had gnawed the lateral where it came close to the surface. Green leaves made blue shade. Here and there a fruit hung lighter than the others, wormed or damaged or early split. Ben saw all of it the way you can see an animal’s skin as a map if you have been around animals long enough: this is worry, that is grooming, this is a scar that matters and will matter again.

At the pump house Ben threw the latch and the door stuck halfway down and then went with a stuttering metallic sigh. The corrugated tin roof made the air inside smell like pennies and spiders. The pump itself sat like an old heart on the concrete, painted once and then painted again, the coats flaking in wide curled chips. The belt made its letter-shaped track between the motor and the shaft. On the wall was the old green panel box, dials and toggles that had come from an era where you built boxes that could outlast your grandchildren. The padlock on the box was new and cheap by comparison. Ben had bought it at a grocery store in town on a day the old lock had simply decided to keep its own secrets.

“You can wait a bit if you want,” Luke said. “Eat before we turn it on. Pretend there’s any order in any of this.”

“A little order,” Ben said, because he needed it.

“Do we pray to it?”

“We say thank you to it when it behaves,” Ben said. He raised the plastic cover on the float regulator and checked the level. He checked the pressure gauge nailed to the canvas-wrapped standpipe. He opened the air bleeder a finger’s width. He lifted his hand and set it with something like a blessing on the metal cage around the motor’s exhaust. The motor was cool. It had a good day in it, maybe.

“Ready?” Luke asked.

“You bet me,” Ben said.

“What are the terms?”

“Peace for five minutes.”

“Done.” Luke set his palm by Ben’s, just briefly, not touching him because he knew the old sign. He reached with his other hand and flipped the big switch. Electricity takes invention and it also takes faith; you can know every inch of the cable from here to the pole by the road and still be surprised when the world leaps like a horse as wind goes through a sycamore. The motor came on with its sound, a low growl that rose into a hum. The belt caught and wrote its letter. The pump took a second, then two, to consider what sort of day it might like to have and then it joined, the deep wet throat of it starting and then settling. The gauge quivered and lifted. The bleeder hissed and spat a little water and then gave a steady breath of air. No cavitation.

“Look at that,” Luke said softly, as if he had been lied to by people and was relieved to find an object that could be believed.

“Give it a minute,” Ben said, worn in his caution like a man is worn into a jacket.

They stood with their shoulders nearly touching and listened to the machine work. It vibrated a little and the vibration came up into their bones in the way of all things that move faster than you expect. You could feel the water wanting to be allowed, wanting to move. If allowed, it would go out and give drinking to each little emitter, it would make tiny arcs under the leaves like handwriting, it would sink and be drawn up and made into apricots swollen and gold with a sheen that looked like oil. If not allowed, it would: nothing. It would bank itself against the gate in the ditch and wait for someone more deserving.

“There’s going to come a day,” Luke said, “when you can’t make it do that anymore. When the river’s a rumor. When they’ve cut our electrics to time-of-use and the cost to run this for five hours at noon would buy a man’s shoes.”

“It’s noon you remember,” Ben said. “It’s June afternoons.”

“They told us to use drip because it saved water. They told us to switch to low-chill varieties and we did, and then there were years the chill hours didn’t come and there was nothing to switch to. They told us to stop spring irrigation and we did and then April was dust. They keep telling us. I’m tired of hearing them. Aren’t you tired of hearing them?”

“Them,” Ben repeated. He tasted the word like a cherry pit taken into his cheek, too big to be swallowed and not quite sweet enough to comfort. Them had always existed—county men, district men, men who came from places thirty miles away with a map you could fit in your pocket instead of the kind who had the map in the soles of their boots. His father had listened and sometimes had agreed and sometimes had not and now he was dead and there was not a single decision he had made that could be reversed.

“Mom would be okay,” Luke said. “If we sold. I’m not trying to pitch you. I’m trying to tell you I’m not trying to pitch you.” He ran his hand over his head. A streak of pale dried primer had already transferred itself from palm to hair and set there like chalk. “I don’t think I even know the arguments I’m making, exactly. I know that sitting with the Realtor last week felt like a joke because she acted like she had dressed up in a Realtor costume and came here to say the exact words we imagined she would. ‘Your property is unique,’ she said, ‘and your proximity to town enhances value though the ag-zoning limits use.’ She said, ‘You could do an agritourism thing. Cider. Weddings.’ I don’t think she knew the words for apricot.”

“You drank a cider at Dad’s funeral,” Ben said, to make him feel worse, and hated himself as he said it.

“Because it was what was in the cooler,” Luke said without apology. He went on, low. “Because there was a cooler that someone filled with what was at the grocery store, and there were ice packs that had been used for a sprained wrist last summer. Because there was a house with a counter where we could set cups and there were people who came and told us their favorite story about him and then asked if we wanted them to help stack chairs. Because he died and that’s how that day worked. It wasn’t a day that was about anything except that someone whose hands smelled like pipe cement and bursage and Ten High whiskey wasn’t going to stand under that tree anymore and tell us we’d misread the sky.”

Ben looked at Luke and then looked at the open door of the pump house and the yard beyond it and the trees and the line of the irrigation ditch that made a lean green cut on the land. June had a different color, high and a little bleached but deep too, as if the earth were working at two things at once: offering and withholding. It felt like he was always being offered a version of leaving, and always the picture included a smaller piece of himself.

“I want to try,” he said, surprising himself because it was so small a word. “I want to see if this year we can bring them in and make a little and not just keep from losing. There are markets I could take to. I know you think those are pictures out of a magazine, but I went and I saw a stand in the city and they were selling ours—they weren’t ours, they were theirs, but they were apricots that could have been ours—and they were charging four ninety-nine a pound and people were buying them like they were buying some piece of a story they wanted. I wanted to be the person who handed that to them and told them what happened to blossoms in the January freeze.”

“You wanted to be a poet,” Luke said, not sarcastic, only naming it.

“And you wanted to be a person who moved and kept moving,” Ben said.

“You are allowed to be the thing you wanted at six,” Luke said, “more than anybody else I know. Okay. Then we try. This season. And if it breaks you?”

“Then we sell,” Ben said. The words tasted different in his mouth than they did in his head. He had not allowed them residence there before. He set his hand again on the cage of the motor and it was hot now, but not yet too hot to touch.

“You’d say that?”

“I just did.”

Luke nodded slowly, as if making himself memorize what a person looked like when they said something hard and said it simply. “Okay.”

They stood there together while the line charged. The rows hissed, life making the small noise that good work makes. They walked back up the row, watching for the arcing line of an emitter gone. They saw a few and stopped and bowed without words, one piece at a time. That posture—the kneeling to pull clods away from a tree’s base, the tightening of a clamp on a barb fitting, the set of his shoulders while cutting and gluing—was one he had always known Luke by. It was a posture you didn't associate with money. It belonged to something older, a kind of acceptance.

By ten the world found its summer pace. A buzzard arrived like a slow hand over the clear part of the sky and made half circles as if casting something. The distant highway yawned and closed. The float valve clicked and shut and opened again in an almost moral rhythm, the equal measure of a machine built in a decade when machines were meant to accompany you.

They broke at eleven and went to the truck and sat on the tailgate and unwrapped sandwiches whose bread had already gone obscene in the heat. The meat inside lay flat and rallied salt like a demonstration. The groceries store had slid two tiny packets of mustard into the paper; Luke stuck one on his forehead like a third eye while talking; Ben tried not to laugh and failed. They ate. They used the dust of the truck bed as a plate. Ben looked at the hill beyond the ditch and saw a coyote standing there looking at them, not curious, only taking a census. Its mouth hung open not in a smile but a pant. It flicked an ear, turned, and was gone into a fold of light.

“Remember when he told us we could move the world with a bigger lever?” Luke said with his mouth full.

“He said, ‘Give me a place to stand,’” Ben said.

“What he didn't say,” Luke said, “was that the place to stand feels like a secret you only get told once and you have to hear it perfectly or you spend years guessing bad answers.”

“You can choose a place and call it that,” Ben said.

“Can you?”

“Sometimes. If you stay.”

“Probably,” Luke said. He picked at a splinter under his thumb where the primer had already turned the skin purple. “I always think about that night with the frost,” he said. “When we were fourteen and the spray wouldn’t keep up and we drove the truck all night and you kept leaning out with the flashlight to see if the tips were freezing and you fell asleep anyway with the light in your hand and it went sliding under the bench. And he just—he drove and didn’t wake you. And I talked and he didn’t tell me to shut up even once.”

“He was good at that. Being with two of us and making it feel like he was only with one at a time and in the right way, too. He was bad at some other things,” Ben said, because it wasn’t fair to make him and only remember the part where he was the kind of father you could take out and polish.

“Like saving money,” Luke said. “Keeping records that made sense. Not thinking that a man who made a big promise in a hat wasn’t going to screw him.”

“That,” Ben said.

“He wanted us,” Luke said suddenly, simply, the way a child announces the weather and it rearranges the day. “He didn’t want the orchard only, he wanted us inside it.”

“So what do we do with that want when we’ve outgrown the space it left?” Ben asked.

“Make a bigger space? I don’t know. He died and I got a call in line at a coffee shop and the world went on behind me and some people were mad that the kid at the front of the line was just standing and not giving his card to the reader like a good boy and I thought, I am not a good boy. I thought, I don’t know how to tell you all that a man who taught me that streams can be read by the sound they make around small rocks has just left the earth and now none of our rocks sound the same. I paid seven dollars for something with cardamom in it and the woman wrote Luke on the cup in nice sharp letters and I wanted to throw it like a shot put under a two-lane bridge.”

Ben didn’t want to cry. He felt a sharp bloom under his tongue instead, a particular ache like the one after you drink cold water too fast and it finds the wrong nerve. He pressed his palms against the seat of the truck until his hands shook with doing it, then he stopped pressing and he swallowed.

“We finish the north run after lunch,” he said, because this was what he could do for them both.

Luke nodded as if you could bow to a decision that didn’t look like a decision. “I went by that housing sign last night,” he said, almost casually. “The one that says ‘pocket parks.’ Someone wrote ‘FUCK YOUR POCKETS’ on it in spray paint. I laughed so hard I nearly pissed myself.”

“You couldn’t help yourself,” Ben said. “The thought of a pocket of grass the size of a table.”

“Meanwhile we have this,” Luke said, and swung his hand wide. “And we are idiots that we have it at all.”

“It feels sometimes like it’s having us,” Ben said, his tone half-dare, half-prayer.

In the afternoon the heat settled. The first grubby cloud of the summer built itself over the far hills and then lost heart. The dog from the neighbor’s place came and lay in the shade of the second tree and panted and wouldn’t be moved. They worked their way along the north line and found a place where a cheap fitting a man had used ten years ago had come loose because the idea of ten years hadn’t been in him on the day he’d put it in. Ben cut and fitted and in cutting took off a piece of the inside of his thumb where it met the blade. The cut welled and pulsed. Luke put his hand on Ben’s wrist as if you could stop blood by telling it so. They wrapped it with an oily green rag Luke pulled from his pocket because he is the man who has an oily rag in his pocket no matter where he lives. The rag smelled like the inside of a bus. It held.

“Gross,” Ben said, lightheaded.

“Don’t lick it,” Luke said.

“Don’t make me laugh.”

“I’m giving medical advice,” Luke said, pretending to be aggrieved. He picked up a clamp with his other hand and tightened it with his good thumb on the driver. “We need the bigger screwdriver,” he said, just to say something.

“I’ve got it,” Ben said and reached and it wasn’t there.

They both stood then. The argument found them without really being invited. It came like a small hard chunk in a mouthful of stew and you had to spit or you would bite on it and break your tooth. They argued about the screwdriver and where it had been left and who always left tools and who always made lists and who enjoyed being the person who knew where things were. And then of course they weren’t arguing about any screwdriver. It came up the way summer might find ground water under a line of cottonwoods: steadily and then all at once. About Luke leaving and then arriving back like a man clutching news that had been written by someone else. About Ben staying and wanting thanks for it and not wanting to know that the thanks wasn’t enough to pay anyone’s mortgage. About a woman in Portland who was sometimes texting Luke still and a woman in town who had stopped texting Ben two winters ago. About Ruth at the kitchen table with her head on the wood and the way she picked the threads of the potholder because she would not let herself pick the skin of her hands.

“You think forgiving him is not my job?” Luke asked at one point, wild.

“What are we forgiving?” Ben asked back, louder, half a shout. “What is there? He did his best and then he died.”

“And sometimes his best was not listening,” Luke said. “Sometimes his best was deciding to pretend you were him and that was the end of the discussion about what you wanted.”

“He gave us this,” Ben said and swept his hand like he had earlier, the same gesture, bigger, meaner.

“He gave us this,” Luke said, mimicking, and then caught himself and looked stricken because he had heard how cheap he sounded. Silence swung hard between them and then hung.

They stood there breathing like men who had come up out of the water and did not want to go back under. A long band of heat moved through the leaves like a snake. A blue heron rose heavily from the ditch and then made itself into something that could sail.

“Okay,” Luke said. He didn’t often say that word and mean it. He meant it this time. “I am sorry for making him into a poster right there. He’s not that. He’s not a stand-in for what made me move to Portland. He was a man who believed that you could teach someone a thing by doing it in front of them until they flinched and then they flinched less. And he loved Mom.”

“And us,” Ben said, because he needed it given to him right then like a glass of lukewarm water.

“And us,” Luke said, immediately, penitent, as if Ben had put the rope back and he had grabbed it and it had kept him from sliding. “I am also sorry about the screwdriver. That was me.”

“You are not sorry about the screwdriver,” Ben said, but it had made him uncoil a little and he knew Luke would feel that under the words.

Luke lifted the truck’s toolbox lid and dug. He did something with those hands then that made Ben remember a night in freshman year when Luke had fixed the broken handle on the backdoor with a spoon and a twist of baling wire because he wanted a girl to be able to sleep on the couch and feel like she was safe. He found the bigger driver and he raised it and made his eyebrows into a show of apology and then they set about the clamp and the pipe and the elbow. The cut piece fit. The cement held.

The water. The machines. The work. It went on.

A man in a flatbed from over the hill pulled up alongside the fence around two. Ben recognized Earl Sherman’s neat bristle of hair under his ball cap. Earl bought walnuts in from farther north now that his own trees had aged out into a kind of aristocratic dryness; he was not a man for whom the word failing could be applied to any object he touched, only to his marriage in 1996 and perhaps to the dog he’d once tried to teach to come to its name when it was in love with the smell of the road.

“Boys,” Earl called. “You alive?”

“More or less,” Luke called back. His handle on town was the casual friendliness of someone given a free pass because of who his father had been.

“You better get your bunds cleaned out or the head ditch’ll run over and you’ll be the ones the district fella comes and knocks on.”

“We’re on it,” Ben said.

“I seen a coyote gone under your fence this morning,” Earl said, conversational.

“We seen him,” Luke said. “He thinks we’re dumb.”

Earl nodded as if this were ordinary philosophical ground to stand on together. “I hear you got offers,” he said, and then wished he hadn’t said it, you could see him want to put the sentence back, to swallow the words and the slight dryness under them.

“We have a year,” Ben said. He meant a season. He meant: we have this day.

Earl looked at him and looked at Luke and then did something kind: he didn’t offer advice, or numbers, or stories of someone who had gone one way or the other and regretted not doing both. He said, “I saved you them air bleeders from that place we tore out.” He reached and handed three small brass things down to Luke that looked like the parts of a clarinet. “You take ’em. Always good to have a couple.”

“Thanks,” Luke said.

“You boys holler,” Earl said, and tipped his head in a move that was short of a nod and more than a shrug and more like a blessing. He drove away. Dust lay down. The dog under the second tree did not lift its head.

They worked until the sun angled and then gentled and the air made them remember that a night would come. The pump had a different note when the shadows lengthened. It might be nonsense, it might be that the metal cooled and that changed the ring, or it might be that they put their longing to hear it into it and the longing inflicted its own sound on the world. They took the truck and backed it to the end of the row and filled a blue tub with water from the tap and put their feet in it as if a person were allowed that kind of kindness without anyone weighing the water’s better use. The first shock of cool was exquisite. They let themselves moan like men in bad jokes. The dog came and drank without looking at them and then lay down and put its chin on its paws and pretended it didn’t want to be touched.

Ruth came outside, a small woman with shoulders that had tipped forward in the last two years in a way that made her blouse hang different. She carried three green glasses and a pitcher that had stopped making its own noise years ago; it held lemonade, the cloudy kind with seeds. She put the glasses on the tailgate and poured and did not comment on the fact that the glasses were probably dirty because the tailgate probably was and the last thing in the world that mattered was a little dust in your sugar.

“You running it,” she said. Not a question.

“We are,” Ben said.

“I heard it,” she said. “I like that sound. It means you boys are where I expect you to be, and I can put my head down for fifteen minutes and not wake up to somebody knocking.” She handed a glass to Luke first, then Ben. He watched her hands. Her wedding ring was too big and slid, and she wore it anyway. “Mrs. Clevenger called,” she said. “She says hello and she says to tell you her lilacs finally set and she blames me because I told her to move them by the north window last year. I did not tell her anything of the sort.”

“You’re always telling people what to do with lilacs,” Luke said.

“You’re thinking of your grandmother,” Ruth said, and smiled for real for the first time in days, a smile that made Ben think of how she had looked at a pie cooling on their wedding day like she was falling in love. “She didn’t know anything about lilacs either. She decided the one we had ought to be cut to a nub and it never recovered. Mean woman.” She said it tenderly. She leaned on the tailgate beside them and put her head against the side of the truck and closed her eyes. “That Realtor is coming again tomorrow,” she said after a moment, as if she had spent the last fifteen minutes trying to say it and couldn’t, and now it came like a small cough. “I told her we don’t know anything. I told her you boys were arguing and that I would make pie and hide in the pantry while you did it.”

“Don’t hide,” Luke said.

“I may anyway,” Ruth said. “It’s only fair. I hid all the rest of the arguments I didn’t want to hear through the kitchen door. It’s nice in there. It smells like flour.” She opened her eyes. “Your father was a good man and he also made me want to lock him out of the house some nights,” she said carefully. “I’m telling you that because you need me to be a person and not your mother for one minute.”

“Okay,” Ben said. The tailgate was warm beneath him. The metal had that finish it gets from years of good use, as though it had learned manners.

“Also, he had a letter on his dresser with some names and figures on it the week before he died,” she said. She watched their faces to see whether that was a blow or a balm. “I read it. I’m telling you I did that. He had called a man. He had asked a number. He had done a thing he always told me he didn’t think we would have to do yet. I don’t know what that means or what you are supposed to take from that. I only have this information and I’ve been walking it around the house for six months like it’s too heavy to set down.”

Luke made a noise. It was not a laugh though it borrowed one of laughter’s muscles. “He would have done it differently than us,” he said.

“Of course,” Ruth said, and sat up and sipped lemonade and looked east. The light had become the kind that moved even when you weren’t. It was the color of peach flesh. The rows of trees took it and made it green gold. The pump wore it like a coat.

“I want to try this season,” Ben said. He had not told their mother yet. He felt the words fly and then land while he watched.

“Okay,” she said. “I like watching you try.” She turned to Luke and raised her eyebrows, as if there were a quiz that called for only one answer and it was the one they had all known since they were children. “And if that doesn’t—if we get to August and it looks like fools, we can make a different choice. We can make a choice no one would think was us, even. You can shock the people at the grocery store. You can sell it to the men with the hats. You can go to a city and listen to people be mad in line about tiny green things in their coffee. I will live in a small place with two chairs and one of them will be for you and one of them will be for your brother. I will have a rug that makes my feet think nice things.”

Ben set his glass down and put his head in his hands briefly and not because he was sad. “You’re terrible,” he said around his smile. “The rug.”

“I have learned to want in a way that can be satisfied by a rug,” she said. “It’s a relief, in its way. Your father always wanted something that didn’t fit in a house.”

Luke reached and put his fingers on Ruth’s wrist and she let him. There were only a handful of men who could touch her like that and not make her feel like she was a shelf to be leaned against. He was one and his brother was the other and their father had been the first.

They sat until the lemonade went watery with ice. They put their shoes back on. They walked to the head again. They adjusted the bleeder and watched a small spit of air coax itself into water. The pump was steady. The line they had mended held.

In the last light the orchard sounded like rain. It always did when you first ran water in June, even if it was only low-volume emitters clicking and rods of dark water licking into bowls they’d dug. The birds believed it, and came. The air cooled. Somewhere, far to the west, a freight dragged itself toward the line of river. The town took on its shape in the near distance: a water tower with a teenager’s name scratched on its leg, the dome of the courthouse that had not judged anyone important in a decade, the lights that came first on Main and then out on the highway where trucks went to other fields and other families.

They collected tools, which is to say they piled them in one place and told themselves that was a kind of order. Ben went along the row and tied the deadfall in the third tree tighter so it wouldn’t split when the fruit set weight. He did this without thinking and then realized he had done it and had a small flinch at the hip because the body knowing the orchard better than the mind meant the mind could feel displaced, as though displaced equals replaced. He adjusted the tie. Luke watched him do it and then went ahead and did the same with the next two because he saw him and wanted to be with him in the seeing.

Evening came, the slow way it does when the day has been built on one repeated act. Ruth went inside and turned on a radio that came a quarter-second later to the yard because of how sound moves through a screen and a room and a person. A voice sang something about a boy and a river which was either a lie or exactly right and there had never been a way to tell.

“Turn it off?” Luke said, standing by the pump house door.

“Leave it,” Ben said. The pump hummed and the voice threaded through it and something like a chord happened, one good thing pressed alongside another and making a third not quite nameable thing. That was the best they could do. They could lay their work alongside their wanting and see if a sound came that they could live with.

They walked back toward the house by separate rows and then turned at the end and met, as though they had traced something and found it had not been a circle exactly but a path back toward. The repaired line was damp to the touch where the couplings had sweated themselves into place. A bead of water stood on the lower edge of one seam, round and exact, a small world in which the trees were upside down.

Luke put a forefinger under it and the bead slid obligingly into his skin and was gone.

“One season,” he said.

“One,” Ben said.

They went inside to tell their mother what they had decided for a day, which is the only unit that meant anything you could hold. The house smelled like onions and cut grass and the clean chemical tang of the primer that had traveled on their hands and put itself on the edges of their lives. Their father had once said, this is how you know something real has been done: the world wears it, and your wife can smell it, and your children won’t forget. That had been before he learned another thing: that the other way you know something real has been done is when you let it not be done anymore because your hands are not the only hands. The letter in the drawer had told Ruth that and she had told them in her way. They went to eat and to tell her and to listen, and outside the trees took into themselves what the pipe had carried and the water moved invisibly upward in a green-quiet system older than them, and the sound of it under the leaves was small, and it was enough to end a day.
