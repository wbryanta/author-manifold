By the time the first woman with the canvas tote bag stepped carefully across the threshold, Ellen had smoothed the little orange stickers on bowls and lamps and dresser drawers with the flat of her palm until her skin smelled like adhesive. The front door was propped open with a brass doorstop shaped like a sleeping cat, the screen door unlatched and humming a thin, high note every time it swung. The air inside was already the complicated air of a lived-in afternoon: lemon oil from the wood, a sweet starch from old curtains, the faint trace of her mother’s powder that clung obstinately to the blue carpeted stairs. On the card table by the entryway, under a hand-lettered sign that said Cash Only, she had set out a metal box with slots for twenties, tens, fives, and one stack of ones that leaned against the corner like a shy guest. She had written the prices on the stickers the night before at her kitchen table one town over, the numbers pitched somewhere between what things were worth and what people would pay. Even then, at midnight with the television muttering, she knew those numbers were a pretense, a structure to stand in for the unwieldy heft of wanting to hold on.

The woman with the tote greeted her as if they were acquaintances at church and then kept moving, eyes already skimming the open living room, the line of sun across the piano lid, the wicker plant stand. Ellen had watched this in other people’s houses, the way shoppers moved like water finding cracks, heads tilted, calculating. Now, in her mother’s house, their motion looked both jaunty and invasive: people bending, lifting, prodding her mother’s life as if looking for a soft spot. She fought a small impulse to be apologetic, to explain that the chair with the curved arms had held all their backs through flu seasons and prom night hairdos, through the year her father left, through three decades of shows watched and arguments held and feet up and quilts thrown. None of that mattered here. The sticker said fifteen dollars.

There were driveways like this all over Mapleton on summer Saturdays: gravel packed into hardpan by the tires of the faithful, a little origami sign taped to a mailbox. She could hear the hum of a lawn mower down the block, the clipped laughter of men in shorts discussing the parts of an engine. Somewhere, a dog barked. If it had been any other Saturday, she might have been out herself, one of those women with a coffee cup, her body bent in that same way, her eyes going greedy and then soft with recognizing something. She had learned to do it without thinking: see the value, ask nicely for two dollars off, make the woman minding the cash box feel seen. “What a pretty garden,” she would say as she counted out quarters. She could not, at that moment, imagine being pretty about this. She licked her thumb and straightened a pile of dollar bills.

“What’s your plan for the piano?” someone asked, a man with a ball cap that said Kubota in orange. He stood in the entryway with his palms on his hips and gazed at the upright like it was a stubborn calf. He was the kind of man, Ellen thought, who had wrists like rope and always found himself the first to speak. She said, “It’s on hold,” because she had said it without deciding to say it a dozen times already to other strangers with other caps, men who tested the pedals with their steel-toed boots and told her it would be a bear to move.

The piano had been a houseguest long before she was born. Its brand name—Kimball—was not anything anyone bragged about in the music world, her childhood piano teacher had told her at some point, the teacher’s tongue pressed meanly to the side of her mouth as if these were dirty facts. But it had been her mother’s insistence, this thing of piano lessons, this upright presence that took a bite out of the room. “A home without music,” her mother used to say, “doesn’t know itself.” Ellen had not known how to hold that sentence then. At seven, she had thought music would make the house warm in winter. At thirteen she had pounded the keys resentfully with hands trained to chop beets and fold laundry for two. At twenty-two she had come home for a Thanksgiving when her mother—unlaced by a bottle of something her older sister Sheila had tucked into the cupboard above the stove—had put on a jazz record and laughed for once in a way that made the corners of her mouth split with surprise and asked, “Do you still remember the Clementi?” Ellen had plunked obediently and had felt that old sense of being measured return like a small, clever enemy.

Kubota Cap nodded, eyes still on the thing. “Shame,” he said. “It’s in good shape. Bench too?”

“The bench too,” she said. Under the padded top lay a world of old sheet music, fingerprinted and curling, Beethoven’s furrowed face on cheap paper, the Baptist hymnal with lilies of the valley embossed in gold ink. She had opened it that morning in the gray hour before the first car doors slammed in the gravel and looked for an imprint of her mother—the pressure of her script, the faint indentation of her pencil where she had marked something important. She did not find anything that felt like a message or a decision. She closed the lid and put a sticker on it like a bandage.

Now they came, a woman with a child who had braids like licorice whips; a tall high school boy with a sunburned nose; a man in a shirt with pearl snaps who was looking for a sander; Mrs. Yoder from the corner who had lived there since Ellen was in pigtails and had outlived two husbands beside. “Oh, honey,” Mrs. Yoder said under her breath, a private thing meant to cover the whole house and not just Ellen, and squeezed Ellen’s elbow with four dry, competent fingers. Once, when Ellen was eight and had disobeyed her mother and ridden her bike down to the creek, losing track of time and fear, it was Mrs. Yoder who had found her in the weeds with her knee skinned and sat with her on the curb, passing over a Kleenex and a square of Juicy Fruit and saying nothing about the red rage skimming off Ellen's mother like heat from a stove. When she tried to thank her—how do you thank someone after forty years?—the older woman shook her head once, lightly. “We take care of our own,” she said, and with a small, defiant glance at the display of crystal animals on the walnut curio cabinet, plucked the glass hummingbird out from among them with a finger crooked like a hook and said, “How much on this one, sweetheart?”

“Five,” Ellen said, guessing. She did not want to say two. She did not want to say ten and hear Mrs. Yoder make that quick sucking sound that would announce it and her both as ridiculous. The hummingbird was worth nothing. It was one of the few things Ellen had been allowed to touch as a child in this living room, when the rules of the house were laid out in that invisible chalk her mother somehow smeared on everything: this is not yours, and neither is that. She would pick the painted thing up by its heavy glass tail and tip it into the light until its wing caught sun and became, for a second, a color that did not belong in Ohio. Mrs. Yoder unsnapped her coin purse and handed over a five like a handshake, and Ellen put it in the metal box and breathed a little easier, as though a transaction of this exact nature answered some old equation.

People carried her furniture out the way wrestlers carry each other, both careful and predatory. A man with shoulders like jerky and forearms that made his T-shirt strain took the maple dresser from her childhood bedroom under the careening guidance of a woman who called out the degrees he needed to lift from the opposite side. Going! the woman said, triumphant, when they had found the angle that cleared the newel post. The dresser had been blue when Ellen was five and the room had been papered with a riot of tulips. That was before the year of beige, a year her mother declared the house had to be made sensible, and everything that spoke too loud got quieted with something called wheat or mushroom or bone. The knobs were still porcelain, each with a little hand-painted rosebud that had chipped off in a predictable pattern, so all the roses looked like they had been eaten by moths. “Twenty-five,” the man had said gracefully when he had set the thing down by the table to count the money out of his wallet. He smelled of honest sweat and peppermint. Ellen had meant to ask for forty. She nodded at twenty-five. He tucked a few dollars into her tip jar—the one she had labeled on a whim as If You Feel Like It and, now that a stranger had endorsed it with a few dollars, she would not be able to get rid of—and gave her a little salute on his way out.

A heavyset woman in florals that would have embarrassed Ellen’s mother back then asked for the Revere Ware pots for a song. “That’s not copper, you know,” the woman confided as if sharing a piece of gossip. The bottoms were indeed dingy and not the luscious gleam of the ads her mother had clipped into a folder and stared at as if they could teach her something. Ellen let them go at a price that made her cheeks feel thin with failure. The woman was right. The good set had gone to Sheila, who lived in a suburb of Cleveland with a kitchen full of things meant to match, the kind of kitchen that had made her mother purse her lips and say, “Who needs a pot that tells you its name?” as if offense could be a meal. She could hear Sheila’s voice in her head now anyway, not that Sheila had offered or been asked to be here today; how tender she would be, her tenderness a second skin for her judgments. “You let those go cheap,” she would say later, chewing the inside of her lip and pushing back her hair. “It doesn’t matter,” Ellen would answer out loud, sometime in her own kitchen when she told the story of this Saturday to herself before telling it to anyone else. “It’s not about the pots.”

A young mother came in with a baby on her hip and a toddler attached to the hem of her T-shirt. The baby was wetly delighted by the smooth world; she reached and grabbed whatever was nearest and took it to her mouth with the little smack of a fish. Ellen watched as the woman settled herself in the chair her father had loved to sit in after work, a roll of something kept in the chair’s zippered side pocket for years—hard candies, puzzle pages from the Gazette with the crossword half done, spent tissues like little off-white grubs. The young woman unlatched a bra strap with one practiced finger and gave the baby the square meal of her attention. It felt wrong and kind to see someone else inside that frame of a life, making use of the chair’s curve, sighing as if it had been made to hold her body specifically. The toddler nosed the corners of the room, trailing her palm over the tabletop and running a swivel coaster in demanding circles. Her hand reached through the bars of the staircase and pressed her face between them to look up into the living darkness of the hallway, and then she said, “Echo,” experimentally, and the house obliged with the smallest repeat.

Ellen put the chair down as sold in a zip of Sharpie on her yellow pad and felt a little lurch, nothing dramatic, a wave of hunger or something like it that had nowhere to go. Her mother had not wanted the chair back upstairs, even when the year came that the stairs were their own problem. It had belonged to a man who had, in the end, become part rumor, part ache, part story. He had gone out one day in the typical drizzle of a March, closing the storm door so carefully the spring did not make its usual bark; he had gone to meet a woman whose existence found its way into their lives by the little bits that stick to you like burs when you walk through a new field. He had left one suit behind, two wrenches, three socks that never turned up in any later laundry. To see someone else’s feet up on the stool, to hear that little exhale of relief that a chair draws out of a tired woman, was to see that the shape of a thing made just for one person was a kind of lie. The chair was a generous object. It had not been faithful to a single set of bones. It had been waiting all along for any human curve.

The sun shifted, burning a line in the dust on the TV armoire, and the house became briefly lit in that harsh way that makes every mark show up like a confession. People pointed at smudges on vases, at the web of hairline fractures in china, at the water ring on the oak coffee table where she had set a sweating glass on a summer day in 1979 and then had learned new words from her mother to describe bad behavior. A man asked if the framed photographs were part of it. He pointed with his chin up the stairs, where the hallway set of school pictures traced through the steps of sweatshirts and turtlenecks and the evolution of bangs. She said no without thinking. He shrugged, good-natured for someone who had thought he might find a thrift store’s worth of sepia strangers and then go home and market them to his people as usable ghosts. She had meant to take the photographs down, had meant to replace them on the walls with nothing, which would be more honest, but that hour had not presented itself. All these things were like phrases in a language that had outlived its country.

The man who ate sunflower seeds dropped the shells with surprising neatness in a Styrofoam cup he carried with him from room to room. He was looking for tools. In the back bedroom where the sewing machine table sat with its metal knee plate peeking like a grin from the cutout, he crouched and peered under the bed and came up with a long-buried collection of spools of thread gone to dust and a jar of buttons that Ellen had thought might make someone pause and light up with the same sense of possibility as the magazine spreads had lit her mother. No one did. The man stuck his hand in and let the buttons sift through his grip and chink down in a little musical train. His nails were neatly trimmed. His cuticles were a rough line of winter. “You selling the machine” he asked, his voice a fraction thick. He had a slight accent she couldn’t place. It was the kind of thing her mother and her mother’s friends would have tried to locate and name by the shape of his R.

“My grandmother had one,” he said, without waiting for an answer. He nodded at the Singer. His grandmother, he said, had worked at the plant making the seats for Chevy trucks, years when her hands ate oil and came home stained with work no soap could scold away. She had sat at the machine on Sundays and made hems for church dresses and curtains so the neighbor’s boy couldn’t see inside when he walked by at dusk and thought he couldn’t be seen. She had gone deaf young, he said, but she could feel the machine like a heartbeat and that was company enough. “How much?”

Ellen had thought she would keep the machine. It was one of those things you imagined you wanted to know how to use. She had no desire to learn to sew and never had. What she had wanted was a continuity she did not believe in and could not help measure herself against. “Fifty,” she said, and then, because the button jar in his hands looked suddenly like a small city someone would have to learn from scratch, she said, “Take the buttons too. No charge.” He held her eyes a beat. “Forty?” he said softly, as if to let her take part in doing a reasonable thing. They were polite to each other in the way people are when they are both imagining other lives for themselves in the same breath.

In the kitchen at noon, a teenage girl with hair the color of a penny tugged a Pyrex bowl up into herself like a prize and smiled so completely that Ellen did not need to look at the sticker and say anything about the price. The bowl was the loud jacaranda blue that had clung through dishwater and most of the seventies without apology and had outlasted a dozen chipped plates. The girl cradled it the way people cradle an idea they have not even had yet and will hold onto for years. She looked at the lid with something like worship. “It matches two I found last week,” she told someone invisible, and then she looked up at Ellen with a start and realized she was supposed to justify her tenderness. “I bake,” she said, chin lifting. Her college hoodie said Toledo, so she would be going away in a few weeks, stepping into some place that did not know about this street. “My mom says I can’t take any of the good bowls,” she said, and then flushed as if she had betrayed the wrong party. Ellen wanted to say something real and motherly and exact, the sentence she had always been searching for out of her mother’s mouth, one sentence that would have held the meaning of something whole. Instead she said, “That one makes good potato salad,” and they grinned at each other like a hand had reached across a chasm and found its partner. “Three dollars,” Ellen said, and the girl offered to pay five, and then somehow they were both relieved that the exchange had both a market and a gift inside it.

Behind the garage there was a table with a spread of simple hand tools that had all known her father’s hands. It was just open shade and more crushed gravel out there, and a sign stuck into a bucket of river stones that she had written in block letters: Everything In Yard. The man who had been in the service—a sinewy thing a year older than Ellen, with a sun-creased face and the kind of eyes that told you he had seen all kinds of skies and had decided not to be impressed with any of them—hovered over the pipe wrench like he was seeing some particular memory in its teeth. He touched it and did not pick it up yet. “I used to have one like this,” he said without turning around. You didn’t have to say much at a sale like this; the script allowed for a handful of openings and then silence as punctuation. “My dad said not to use it on bolts unless I wanted to get yelled at later,” he went on, and Ellen saw in that flash they were children again, both of them wanting to be right and not be corrected by men who came home full of something that had nothing to do with meatloaf and girls’ homework. He paid two dollars and she put them into the slot for ones and then the man said, “My condolences. Your mom, she was the one who made those buckeyes for the fundraisers, right?” He was someone who kept the file cabinets of the town neatly inside himself. His voice had the careful touch locals used when they peeked into each other’s private rooms. “She was good at those,” he said. He lifted his chin like a goodbye and walked away with the wrench held like a baton, and Ellen’s chest hurt with the sudden smallness of the detail. If that was the thing you knew about Marjorie Day of Maple Street—the buckeyes, the old, simple candy that made middle-aged women cackle and lick their lips and say they were sinful—maybe that was enough. She had been particular about chocolate and gloss and the set of a peanut butter sphere on wax paper. She had done that. She had fed people who needed the pleasure of sweetness to go on voting and working and walking down hundred-year-old sidewalks that buckled over roots.

People peered into drawers. They peered into the drawer of the telephone table in the hall, where the phone had hung for fifty years and the slot for the directory had once housed the big book with its onion-skin pages and the mesh of humanity printed thin in November. Inside that drawer lay two decks of cards with old motel names on them, three rubber bands turned to paste, four pennies with dirt in their dates. There were also, in the bottom back edge where Ellen had known to reach without looking, letters. They were not love letters, no trails of adult passion set down stupidly on paper, no oversharing. They were simple letters that arrived when Ellen was living elsewhere: a card with daffodils on the front and short notes inside that said things like: Remember to wrap the pipes. There was a check once for thirty dollars when she was thirty-one. There was a list clipped to a note that said fish fry Thursday and then the list had been of carrots and potatoes and ant traps. Ellen took the letters without ceremony and slipped them into her own canvas tote under the table and saw the man with the Kubota cap watch her and decide to be the kind of person who said nothing out loud.

By two o’clock the house had a different echo. The emptiness began to assert itself like a voice in a choir moving to the front. When the high school boy and his friend took the back out of the couch and then took the cushions and then took the bones of the thing that had held her family in frightening proximity for decades, there was a string of pale carpet under it like a virgin road. Ellen found a dime and a button, a baby’s pacifier that could not have been from any baby she knew, and a crumpled shopping list in her mother’s small backhand that said tin foil, Comet, corn. She put these into her pocket the way a girl picks flowers as she walks home with dirt in the frill of her socks.

They lifted the dining table the way they teach you to lift—a boy at each leg and one in the middle—and one of them made a noise that wanted to be a man’s noise and was not yet. The table had grown out of the floor under their lives. It had their birthdays ringed into it by the heat from pizza boxes and the tap of forks. It had the mark from an upside-down iron that she had made in a rush before a school performance of The Ransom of Red Chief. It had it all written there so delicately you could rub everything out with the side of your hand. The woman who bought it bargained hard without any meanness, as if this was sport she had trained for. In the end, Ellen named a number even she did not expect, then said okay to less because the woman exhaled in that singular way of the finally-satisfied. The woman’s husband looked relieved as if they had averted something. That had been a particular fatigue in her parents’ house: averting something that might or might not have been there in the first place.

At three, a boy in a faded polo shirt with a college bookstore pen held in his teeth took away the typewriter that had been downstairs on the closed-off porch for as long as anyone remembered, its ribbons desiccated, its cover vaguely webbed by old cotton. The brand name on the face of it tugged at Ellen’s mind without landing—the big American companies of mid-century that assumed they would be needed forever. He told her he wrote his papers on typewriters now, and he hit the keys too hard to show her, and they made the sound that had once been the underbeat of brainy women and men in every city practicing how to say things directly into the world. She smiled indulgently even though she wanted to take the typewriter and hurl it down the cement path so the myth of an uncomplicated old time could bleed out into the grass.

In the late afternoon, when the light had the amber color that television commercials use to trick you into thinking you could be certain of something, a couple in their late twenties pulled into the driveway in a compact car that was missing one hubcap. They got out and stretched in sync. They moved together like people who had walked at the same speed awhile. The woman went straight to the back step where the ceramic planters were set out in a little half-circle in case someone felt about planters the way some people felt about orphaned cats: not exactly love, more that electric jolt of responsibility when something stares at you from a willing position. She touched one, thumb in its nick, her gaze softening toward a yes. The man stood in the foyer and let the house wash over him and then touched the wall with one palm like it was a person’s cheek and he needed to confirm it was warm. He looked at the painting of a barn in snow in the hall and smirked enough that Ellen knew he was noting the naive brushwork, not to be cruel but on principle. He moved in through the living room in a loop and stopped at the small glass bowl full of matchbooks from diners and funerals and vacation cabins that her mother had thought said something about romance. He lifted one. The motel had been in Michigan. The red print tried to look French. You could smoke in those rooms, Ellen remembered, the way people had been able to do anything whenever they wanted until you knew better. The man set the matchbook back like it was hot. “My grandma had a set like that,” the woman by the planter called out, and Ellen laughed, and the woman laughed too, because all afternoon every person had said a sentence that began that way and ended with a grab—all our grandmas, she thought, had the same grandmother in a house with a bowl of floating soap petals.

She had held certain things back. The quilt that her mother had started when she was forty and had kept folded like a promise in a trunk all these years. The black-and-white photograph of a picnic, all of them in shades of gray, their mouths full of chicken and their eyes almost closed, ridiculous and alive, a blanket of checkers beneath them that would come into the world later as a cliché. The blue glass vase from a daytrip to Marietta when Ellen was twelve and her mother had felt like a version of herself that wore bracelets and bought two cones and did not talk about meat. These were the sorts of things you carried out of a life in a small box or two, things that told you what to remember when memory went savage and preyed on the wrong bits. She had meant to keep the recipe box too—the one with the sticky yellowed cards, the shape of her mother’s hand in those swishy little notes to herself that said bake hot and – more sugar if company. But at ten, a woman had come in with her own mother and her hands had landed on that box and the woman had said, “Oh, look,” and the way she said it had been more devoted than Ellen could promise herself to be for the rest of her life, and so Ellen had watched as the woman took the cards out of their slot and fanned them lightly like a house of worship programs, and Ellen had said fifteen without breathing and the woman had nodded and the old woman had clapped softly like someone had learned a bit of piano. Then, later, after they had gone, Ellen had sat briefly at the kitchen table on one of the ladder-back chairs that were much narrower in the hips than people were now and had let herself sob once, just the push of air, not letting it go feral, just letting the pressure equalize so she could go back to being the person who made change.

You had to weigh it. That was the secret nobody told you that Saturdays like this made ordinary: the difference between what something had meant and what someone could take—time, money, a shelf in a new life. The young wheat-haired woman who bought the quilt stand but not the quilt because she did not believe in quilts, she said, in her own joking way, the woman whose politics were braided with sincerity, who lamented not having room in their small house for any art that did not do anything, like the barn-in-snow painting, she reminded Ellen of the ways years cloak us in rationalities. Her mother had made rules because she had been hungry and scared and wanted a house that was not going to fly apart. Ellen had made allowances because she had learned to like the smell and sound of flying apart. In the middle of the afternoon, when the light slid sideways through the slats of the blinds and striped the far wall like jail, the older woman who sold Avon for thirty years and had a certain presentation of her cleavage leaned a forearm on the card table and said, “You know, if you’re not quite ready for that piano to go, I have a nephew with a basement.” There was understanding written into that offer the way there is sugar in fruit.

She had not been ready for the bed. The bed did not leave until four, when two men who did this without fanfare came in with dollies and straps and looked at it as if it were just a square thing that had to wobble through a narrower square. They did not say condolence or memory. They had on matching T-shirts with a logo ironed over a ripple of chest hair and they were cheerful in a way that had nothing to do with her. They would take the bed wherever it was supposed to go. Ellen had stripped it in the morning, the mattress cover tight as a sailor’s promise, the sheets laundered at the laundromat last week after she had stood waiting while a woman with a beet-pickled laugh said loudly to no one in particular that death had a smell, and Ellen had noticed not everyone had nodded in agreement. She set the pillows aside on the floor and they looked immediately like orphans. How many nights was it that you could lie there and believe the person who had slept in that bed could be called back by a phone ringing, by the doorbell, by the jangle of keys? It had been six months of that, and then one month of a thing between knowing and being, and then a day when the hospice nurse with hair the color of gravel had stooped and smoothed the sheet with a practiced hand and said words that made everything constant. Now the bed moved down the hallway with the sideways dignity those men and their straps insisted upon, and as it passed the shadow under the telephone in the hall it breathed, the way beds breathe now that science has shown us lungs in strange places.

The late light turned the dust into sequins. A small boy picked up a photograph of Ellen as a middle-schooler in a sweatshirt that had announced something sparkly about her that wasn’t then and wouldn’t be. He stared and then looked at her the way small children look at adults as if they were shapeshifters and this proof changed nothing. A woman with a gentle face and a harsh purse took the top off of a baked enamel pot and said that the lid hadn’t dented up half as bad as hers had, and Ellen pictured a hundred roast chickens giving up their skins in a parade of households where everything had to be coaxed and then forgiven. A man asked if the doorknobs were for sale and it was the only time all day that Ellen raised her voice in a way that made her mother’s tone ring behind it. “The hardware stays,” she said. She did not even know what she meant by hardware and it did not matter. You had to leave the house something to keep itself closed with.

At five, the traffic thinned. The signal at Maple and Birch switched its cycle with the same indifferent practice, and the shadows on the front walk lengthened in deliberate procession like a cathedral deciding what time it was. There was a small collection of bruised things left: the ceramic frog with a chip out of its eye; the stack of VHS tapes everyone thought was both hilarious and sad; the crocheted afghans that could be had now for pennies in every town in the middle of the country. Someone came in and, by the way she moved her feet over the threshold, Ellen knew she had been here before when the house was not for sale in any of its parts. The woman’s eyes ran the room and softened not into greed but into recognition. “You’re Marjorie’s girl,” she said, and the scabby edge of Ellen’s name in the woman’s mouth amazed her: that she existed in people’s minds as something attached to a woman who would not—in the oddest phrasing that seemed locally correct—be with them much longer. The woman said her maiden name, which made Ellen dizzy with time travel. She had played jacks with this woman in the same room where they were standing, at a moment when both of them had believed that the future would be something they could think up in the safety of each night. They had taught each other how to flip the pile of jacks into a single neat net. They had learned to look away at the right moment so the other had room to be good at something. “I’m so sorry,” the woman said now, and it had to come from someone who had been in that room when the house was noisy with someone else’s bacon, when the curtains had held the smell like faith. “I brought pictures,” she said, and took out of her big bag a little envelope from the summer drugstore that had gone out of business in 1996, and slid onto the card table half a dozen photographs of girls in big shorts and boys with thick hair. There was Ellen, and there was her mother younger than Ellen was now, with a waist and a blouse and a look on her face that made Ellen step back, briefly. It was not beauty that took her breath away—although that was there in her mother’s youth like a coin under a layer of dust—it was the look of someone unburdened by what would come. It made her want to reach into the photograph and slide into that June afternoon and whisper something like “Call in sick” into her mother’s ear, as if anything so small could change the slope of things.

“You keep them,” the woman said. “Your mama always kept our pictures safe.” Ellen took them under the table with the letters, and this felt like a kind of justice. The woman left fifteen minutes later with a doily she had not wanted and a casserole dish someone else had left two weeks ago and never come back for. She paid too much and would be proud of that later.

She kept finding things. In the back of the pantry, behind the tower of paper bags that could become a hundred sack lunches if the world asked nicely, there was the tin of unsmoked cigars her father had kept in his last months there. She had not known they were in the house. They were three brown bullets lined up like a dare. She touched one and it softened under her regardless. There was the way he had leaned against the porch railing and looked off down the block with a kind of quiet astonishment after one of those last dinners when he had not eaten much. “You girls,” he had said, and then either said nothing or said something no one remembered because of how completely her mother mouthed the air like a fish. Ellen put the cigars back into the tin and put the tin into the trash bag with the cracked Tupperware and then took it back out and set it on the top shelf where someone else would not see it and probably, later on, could not find it.

A crow set up on the streetlight outside and made the one note it had make itself important. A breeze pushed dirt over the kitchen floor in a square of light. The line of ant traps along the baseboard—new—made her think of the last summer when her mother had called about the tiny pilgrims making their way along the counter to the sugar bowl. “They’re scouts,” she had said into the phone, as if the ants had intention and the intention had to be respected for the contest to be properly organized. “It’s not like in the War,” she had added, to make sure Ellen understood that history could not be casually borrowed. “This is not metaphoric.” Ellen had laughed and then felt immediately sorry in a childish way because her mother’s mind had been better than everyone else’s for so long and now it slipped like a person on algae at the river.

There were still two plastic bins of books. People’s eyes had skittered over them in that way we have left now. The spines were old library discards and grocery store paperbacks, the kind that once lived in car windows and brought their readers to tears or more. There were four Bibles, because in a house like this the Bibles replicate like cells, then stay. Ellen had set the three most generic ones aside to go to the church and had left the one with her father’s name on the presentation page out because she was messy sometimes where there should have been devotion. It was just a book with paper and a binding he had cracked in church on a dozen Easter mornings, the white of lilies carving out a place for a grief so bright it made you laugh first, as a safety measure. A man with a gentle Afro and a daughter who wore her disappointment publicly because there was not a dog for sale here, he picked up the English Standard Version and cradled it like it might weigh out in his hands differently than the one at home. Ellen did not watch what he decided. He had the kind of hands that do not break things. His daughter found a stuffed bear in the back closet with one eye stabbed out in the course of its service and brought it to the front like an animal who had been in the woods too long. “How much?” she asked and made her eyes do the thing that meant she knew what to do in the world. “On the house,” Ellen said, and the father frowned without meaning to, pulled between an impulse to say thank you or teach his daughter that nothing was on the house. He balanced both and tipped his head, and the girl walked away with the bear’s ear in her mouth, because the world begins again every afternoon whether you are ready or not.

By six, the metal box had enough small bills in it to make her feel like a petty gangster, and the bigger bills were folded and kept in an inside pocket of her purse, because of her mother and because of the women who had taught her at lemonade stands that the thing stolen would be that which you had not thought to guard. She sat in her mother’s kitchen on her mother’s chair—no, not her mother’s anymore, a chair with a CAUTION: SOLD sign on its back, a chair that jockeyed lightly back and forth as if wondering what home would carry it next. The house creaked its evening creaks. A fly threw itself with spastic love at the oval-bubbled glass of the screen door. She felt something like joy and something like failing at a test she did not know she had signed up to take. Her hands ached in the center of the palms where so many small coins had rested and turned, small planets with their wear patterns like weather.

A man came to the door and then backed away because he had seen in her face the look of a person near the end of a day, and he had the decency people in towns like this keep in their glove compartments. “Tomorrow too?” he called, though the sign had said One Day and she could hear in the question a little prayer that she would say no and let him be off the hook. She said, “No. Today.” She did not want to do this twice. She watched him watch the paper sign that had headed up a lifetime of sitting, the one she had taped on the front of the Kimball piano. She watched his hand reach out and touch the edge of the bench she had let no one sit on for the first hour because she had thought it would somehow hold in it some last staggering breath. He tipped his head and smiled at her like he understood that he was not taking a piano from her, he was manufacturing air in her house so that she could breathe.

When the last stranger left—a boy with a beard not yet seeded fully in who carried, triumphant, a stack of National Geographics from a year that had taught the country its greed—Ellen stood in the front door and put her palm against the paint. There was this feeling of having been spaded up and left ready for something. The house whispered in the way houses do when they are between sentences. She could see the imprint of furniture rubbed into the carpet like the shadows of big mammals who have gone on. She could smell the end of dish soap and the clean line of outside air in a home that had been inclined toward inside. She could hear, somewhere far, the rumble of the train going through the notch west of town, iron seducing iron in the old song that had threaded this county since the Civil War.

She picked up the broom. It was a thing to do, and women in her line had kept themselves upright in that way all their lives. She brushed the entryway, the motion circular and then arrowed, and pulled into the dustpan a little world: hair in its fine thread and its coarser wire, soil in dignity and grit, a peanut shell, a twist tie, a flake of wax she could not source. She threw it all away as if this were easy. She wiped the thin film of a day’s touch off the table and watched each path of her rag change the shallow shine.

In the living room, where there was mostly air now, she stood at the window and looked down Maple Street in both directions. The Yoders’ bungalow wore its porch like a hat. The oak at the corner had, over the years, taken the shape of that one storm; it had a tilt to it that would carry the rest of its life. The house across the way still had the odd toy squirrel nailed to the porch post, meant to startle. The sky had the pink bruise to it that tells you the next day will be hot again. A boy on a bike pedaled arms-out past like Jesus and made the move that boys make, small and arrogant and designed to make the eyes of every person in a house along the street lift from their plate. In the late-century of their small town, everyone had learned the art of leaving other people to it unless they were bleeding. She could feel, suddenly, that the room had been full of that patience, that one of the things she had misunderstood about her mother was the degree to which she had wanted to be left alone to make her lists.

The refrigerator ticked. The freezer motor hummed off and then on. The emptiness made its own sound, like the inside of a seashell you hold to your ear to confirm the ocean. There were not many things left now to claim by fiat—the things she would pick up and take in her car later when she had the energy to make a third trip. Her hands went out to the blue vase without meaning to. It was an old glass, thick and bubbled, heavier than it looked. She remembered the day: how the shop had been cool with fans overhead stirring the air and the men blowing glass had been serious and soothing both. Her mother had watched them the way cartoon animals watch a rolling pie on a windowsill and said to no one, “I always think there will be much more blue in the world than there is,” and then had bought the vase and set it in their house with a sigh, as if it were an argument with them both and also with the light. Ellen held it now and saw its thin places and its fat, the way the lip curved slightly to one side to say I was made by hands. She thought of how many times she had thought in her own life that the thing she bought would be the thing that fixed everything. She did not put the blue vase down.

She did not need to lock the door yet. The house would be naked and open for exactly this hour and then would have to be shut up again and listed and emptied of the last of its parts until it was abstract and ready to be passed to strangers with money and plans. She thought of the young couple with the missing hubcap and pictured them in a house not unlike this one, with a rug from Target and an argument about money and a late-night making up on the stairs in the half-dark. Someone would live here and they would not be the people who had learned their particular hum in this hall. The door would open on winters and springs that had nothing to do with anyone who had been measured at the mark on the kitchen doorjamb. She felt herself step into a room where ceremonies were accidental: washing your hands one last time in a sink that had seen your dirty palms since you were a person who fit on someone’s hip; running your fingers along the wall at the height your mother always missed with her dust rag; flipping off the light and then, because you are a woman of your time, turning it on again to make sure you had not left the iron on even though the iron had gone down the steps on a hand truck an hour before while you pretended you had not heard it bang.

She went out onto the front porch. The steps had a rhythm to them her feet knew better than any other set of steps in the world. She sat. The night would come. The little solar lanterns Mrs. Yoder had stuck in her flower bed would light up all by themselves as if miracles had been democratized. Across the street, someone turned on their kitchen radio and a baseball game leaked into the street like a true thing, the announcer’s voice like melted butter over the low-held heat of the crowd. The world would keep its appointments, with or without her looking. She put her elbows on her knees and her face in her hands and then she laughed, once, real and sudden, because the squirrel nailed to the neighbor’s post had fooled her—as if it were an animal with a message—and she could hear her mother say in the back room of her skull, “Oh, for heaven’s sake,” and then say nothing more.

You think the things will articulate the loss, and then you find that what articulates it is the ordinary. The shape of the air. The way a room smells when it is emptied and holds onto everything in a thinner, more honest layer. The way money looks, counted, and then looked at again. The glance of a stranger at your old piano. The exact tilt of the light at six-thirty when there is nothing left to buy and someone in a hurry pulls into your yard anyway and you have to stand and go to the door and be kind and say, We’re closed. The way a stranger will then look up at the house that once knew everything that you did not, and say, without realizing it, that he loves the bones. And because you are a person who learned to be alive here, you will nod in recognition at someone finding the thing they like best.

She left the door open to let the house breathe and then, because she could not help it, she went inside for one last thing. In the hall, on the small telephone table with the spot where the earpiece had chipped the varnish, there was a dime taped with ancient tape to the underside. She had put it there at twelve, a flair for drama you are allowed in a house where things are scarce but stories are free, in case of emergency, a tradition inherited from her mother who had inherited it from a woman who had whispered to a neighbor through a phone in a bedroom when the roads were washed out. Ellen had forgotten it and then remembered and then forgotten again. Her mother had never used it, or if she had she’d had the decency to replace it with another dime of the exact same year. Ellen pried at the thing and let the tape squeal and come off in a long tired peel, and then the coin was in her hand with its portrait and its trust. She did not think anything as big as this is the last small thing or as small as what am I supposed to do with this in 2025. She put it in her pocket with the button and the list and the other implement of her afternoon, and then she went to the door and closed it partway, so the screen would hold the exact amount of evening, and she sat again, and breathed, and watched three boys with an orange basketball take turns making runs at the hoop someone had nailed crude over their garage, each of them lit up by their own bodies like the house had been lit, and she did not cry, or she did and it ran coolly into the collar of the last clean T-shirt she had brought, and around her the small Ohio town kept its schedules and its secrets and its way of letting go of everything by calling it ordinary and giving it to strangers to carry out into the world.
