The sign had been in the garage rafters for so long dust had turned the letters gray. Lydia wiped it off with the tail of her T-shirt until ESTATE SALE TODAY could shout over the quiet of Maple Street. She set it in the yard at nine minutes to one, when the sun was hard on the flattened grass and the pavement shimmered and the town made the low humming a town makes when people are out after church, after lunch, unhurried, interested. The paper taped to the front door read NO EARLY BIRDS in blue marker even though there already were two of them hedging the walkway, pretending to tie shoelaces and adjust sunglasses and consult a phone.

“You open?” one of them asked anyway. He was that middle-aged with a goatee that made everything he said sound like a dare. Cargo shorts, thin calves, a fanny pack strapped like a holster.

“On the hour,” she said. Her mother had said it that way, always in little clicks of time: at the hour, on the hour. Never “in a minute,” never “soon.”

The goatee man grinned as if she’d delivered a line he’d been waiting for. “Can I just take a look in the garage? I saw tools.”

“On the hour,” Lydia said again. She made her voice steady, not sharp; it was a trick she had learned from twelve-year-olds who misread tone faster than they misread anything. If you made your no soft, it tempted a harder yes. If you made it mild, it stood. The repetition—on the hour, on the hour—helped keep the rest of her from taking inventory of everything inside that could be taken out.

She fit the key into the front door and turned past the lean of the wood. The lattice of the screen had a place your finger had to sneak through to unlatch at once; you had to press and lift. Her mother had shown her that last winter, when the lock had frozen with a January snap, both of them laughing on the porch until their teeth hurt.

It smelled like the house: hot dust from the ducts, the sweet skin of old wood, mothballs ghosting the closet hall, the faint metallic tang of the dead air conditioner. The centerpiece of the living room—the upright piano, rich and black with her father’s polished pride—held the scrap of tape with $300 written on it, blocky and clean. She’d made herself write the numbers as if that would make them true.

There was no reason not to sell it. She had practiced scales until the oil in her wrists ached on that piano; she had cried on the bench with her hair hanging down, the keys muffling her, when a boy named Mark Stevens had called her “Lyds” and said he’d call later and didn’t. Her mother had dusted it every Thursday, as if that kept notes in their places even after Lydia had quit. The last time anyone had played it was at Thanksgiving two years ago when the neighbor’s granddaughter banged out Heart and Soul and everyone clapped as if generosity could make talent real. There was no reason to keep the thing. There was no home left for it. She had a third-floor walk-up in Worthington with a window box and a bookshelf. She had a small car.

The sale started at one. That meant she was the one to unlatch and say hello and watch.

The first arrivals came like birds. Not a flock, exactly. A deliberate scattering. Two older women with the gracious hands of women who fold church bulletins into fans, who stack pies on wax paper and leave them in the kitchen for the funeral committee. A man in a John Deere hat who had lost his eyebrows somewhere in the past decade. A teenage girl with hair purpled on purpose, her mother wincing at the thought of hair dye on towels. The goatee man, pushing in at the corners the second she moved her shoulder from the doorway.

“Cash only?” someone called.

“Cash and Venmo,” Lydia said, pleased at the modernness, then unexpectedly sad about being pleased. She’d downloaded the app last night, sitting on the edge of her mother’s bed listening to the box fan, thinking about the time her mother had written a check at the pharmacy and apologized for writing a check and then apologized again for apologizing, and the girl at the register had said, “It’s okay sweetie,” and her mother had pursed her lips into a stern straight line that meant don’t you “sweetie” me.

The man in the Deere hat headed for the hutch. “Heavier than it looks,” he said to the nobody who had asked. He slapped the wood, the way men slap wood to coax out something. Lydia flinched. “My mother had a hutch just like this,” he said in a tone that meant his mother was a different person for him than Lydia’s had been for her.

“Of course she did,” she said, and smiled.

She’d tagged everything last night until the part of her brain that named colors and prices and functions had gone gray. A ring of tags like a chain in her hand. Yellow tags for kitchen, pink for linen, blue for tools. She had sat on the floor where the couch had been and written amounts that made people feel cheerful and charitable and eager to carry something heavy. She had argued with herself about the wedding china that they never used, about the sad stacks of Tupperware with the lids curled like tongues. She had decided yes on the Pyrex with the cornflower pattern her mother said was a wedding present, no on the ceramic bluebird that had once lived on the windowsill near the sink. She had slid that into her purse. It had surprised her, the swiftness of that decision—bluebird in purse, the way her hand had moved by itself—and the safety of it, the way the small bird had warmed there, as if it had pulsed.

“Is there a basement?” the goatee man asked. His voice carried the way opportunists’ voices carry, a little intentionally too loud so that you can’t say no privately.

“Back by the kitchen,” she said, and he was already heading there, zipper on the fanny pack rattling. She wanted to call after him, Don’t go down there without shoes, the cement flakes, watch your head, the light is a string you have to pull, the bulb is dim, my father hit his head there and swore. She did not say it. She let him know the way mothers let their children know one last thing with their eyebrows.

“Condolences,” one of the church women said, and put her hand over Lydia’s hand for a brief press. “I always liked your mama. Tough as nails about the prayer chain. Don’t you put out gossip on it, she’d say. You save the details for God.” She laughed a soft little laugh, almost a cough.

That was one of the new things of the past week: the small stories people told you like pennies. You were supposed to cup your palm and take the coin, feel its weight, examine the face and the year and say, Oh, yes, that’s her, Heads or tails. She’s within the same metal. You were supposed to feel more of a person that way, and often you did. At the grocery two days ago, Mrs. Gutman from the pharmacy had said, “Your mother came in with a list big as the phone book and I said, Are you feeding the whole town? You know what she said? This way if something happens to me, I can feed whoever’s here.” And Lydia had thought, My mother had this plan? For death? For company after it?

The afternoon unfurled even as Lydia gathered it. There was the small white table where she had learned fractions by cutting cookies into quarters. That one went to a young couple in matching shirts printed with sunflowers. “This is for our breakfast nook,” the woman said as if the words themselves were blessings. They will drink coffee and leave sugar rings the way I did, Lydia thought, and before she could decide if that thought should push her to say, Take it, love it, care for it more than I did, or to add five dollars, the man was folding cash into her palm.

A boy with shoulders too narrow for his T-shirt pulled a letterman jacket off a chair back. Yellow H’s on emerald sleeves. Her father’s jacket had had those colors, that heat of wool, that smell of sweat made into history. The boy held it up and laughed aloud and then looked guilty because Lydia was watching.

“How much?” he said, and to her surprise she said a number far lower than the one she had taped to the collar last night. He swallowed and nodded and shrugged into it. It looked good on him, the way old things sometimes wake up when you give them to the right person. She thought of her father pretending to dislike being sentimental. He would have pointed and said, That punk better have a wicked fastball, and her mother would have shushed him and then smiled with him when the boy went out the door.

By one forty-five, the kitchen had grown two new holes. The chrome-edged table was gone and the chairs with their vinyl seats: one had a cigarette burn troublemaking luck had brought it when Lydia was ten and thought flicking ash into the drips of candle wax made her a bohemian. A woman with a low voice bought the bowl that belonged to every gingerbread man, every birthday cake. Lydia had watched the bowl cradle egg after egg and flour dust, white handprints on clean aprons. When the woman said, “I’ll put it on my counter just for show,” Lydia wanted to say no. To the show of it. To the bowl orphaned from the wrist that had creamed butter in it with patience. But the woman’s eyes were light and wet at the edges, and the way she traced a chip in the glaze with her fingertip was not a collector’s possessive little lizard lick; it was tenderness. Who was she to decide what got put to show and what got put to use? Both were a kind of use. She named a price. The woman paid it and said, “I was sorry to hear. Your mama was ahead of me at the post office last winter and insisted on loading my boxes, big as they were, and I said, Now, Helen, you let me get those, and you know what she did? She said, I got two of the first floor and arthritis in one hand so I might as well even the two out.”

“She’d say something like that,” Lydia said, and for the rest of the hour she held that line like a rail. She’d say something like that. I can hear her saying it. She did hear her saying it, too, her mother’s vowels wide as plains: Helen was a small name made round.

Her phone buzzed in her jeans. Her brother John, his name moving like a fish under the glass of the screen. She hadn’t expected him to call. He had been at the funeral on Wednesday in a suit he wore as if it were a borrowed language. He had left after hugging her twice and saying, “You’ll handle it, Lyd.” On Thursday night he’d texted to ask, Is there any chance we could keep the piano. Which was more about distance than pianos. He lived in Phoenix and believed in wide driveways.

She pressed Accept. “We’re in the middle of it,” she said, and in the background the low thrum of the room sounded like a field of bees.

“I thought it wasn’t till next week.”

“No. Today.” She had told him. Twice, with dates.

“So what’s going?”

“Everything that’s not nailed down. People are very... thorough.” She watched a heavyset man in a brown work shirt open every drawer of her mother’s dresser and then close them again. The carefulness lodged in her throat like a seed.

“Is the piano gone?”

“Not yet,” she said. The word yet felt like a dare to herself. A test turn of the wheel. She felt sweat at the back of her neck. “If it—if there’s an offer, I’m going to take it.”

There was silence. Then John said, “Christ, we could have shipped it.”

“To where? You have room?”

“That’s not the point. It’s—Jesus, Lyd. That was Dad’s…”

She stopped the memory before it gelled. Her father’s hands on his glasses. Her father’s face in that funny serious near-music concentration. His sneeze. She pushed money and flyers into the cash box, lined up bills face-up like a small green field.

“I know,” she said, and looked at the piano with a love that was more like the ache after a muscle cramps. “I know what it is. But I can’t keep it. I don’t play. It doesn’t fit. We can’t keep everything.”

“I’m not asking to keep everything,” he said, and it was true: he wasn’t asking anything, really; he was accusing her of not being able to split open and become a storage facility for ghosts. “Just the big things.”

She laughed, and it came out like a cough. “That’s the problem, John. The big things are heavy.”

“Oh for—okay. Okay. Don’t get all…” He wasn’t sure what he meant. They never were. In childhood, whenever their mother had said, “Don’t get all excited,” it had left the both of them wary of intensity. In their family, tears were a kind of bragging. You must think you’re very interesting, their mother would say if someone sobbed in the cereal aisle.

“If you want anything,” Lydia said, “text me. I can set it aside. And, John—” she did not put his name on the end of things often because names made people stop and she wanted him to not be startled—“You could come. Next time. It’s not like we’re selling out of the trunk of a car. You could fly.”

“Lyd,” he said, and there it was. His plea that had nothing to do with flights. He was not a person who looked directly at pain; he liked to approach it sideways and snatch something before it knew he was there. “It’s easier if you handle. You have your summers.”

“I teach summers,” she said. “Everything doesn’t stop just because it’s hot.”

“Okay,” he said, and then, almost sweetly, almost as if he meant it: “You doing okay?”

“I don’t know,” she said, and it was true, and the comfort came not from his question but from that little permission the sentence gave to her own mind, a pass to not collapse yet. “I’ll call you later.”

A man appeared at the card table set up as a cashier. He set down three paperbacks by Rosamunde Pilcher, a lobster-shaped bottle opener, and a framed print of ducks taking off from a pond. He said, “How much for the lot.”

She named a number. He said, “How about,” and named one lower.

She looked at the cardboard box beside him, his pile growing, and she saw his ring finger was naked to the first knuckle with a tan line like a bandage. “Okay,” she said. She wondered where the ducks would go. Bathroom? Den? She pictured a den with a recliner that shuddered like a split animal when you kicked back. She liked to imagine other people’s houses. It made this easier. Her own could stay uncluttered and sleek; other houses could be generous and heavy.

“Is this Pyrex local?” a woman said near the folding table where Lydia had spread baking pans. Local, as if the casserole dish had grown in the Ohio clay beside the dusty soybeans. Lydia nodded as if that were possible. It was that kind of town: things had origins not merely in manufacture but in lineage. You either knew who had owned your object before you and how many holiday green beans it had seen, or you were a stranger.

Two boys came in kicking their sneakers against the frame of the door as if to knock. They were not quite men and not yet careful. They went straight for the records in the crate near the radiator and pulled out James Taylor and Billie Holiday and the Ohio Players, and one of them turned a sleeve over and had the open light of delight on his face and said, “Dude, original pressing,” and then folded as if he didn’t want to show that happiness in front of a person older than his mother. “How much?” he said softly, not looking up.

“Two dollars,” Lydia said, and the boys froze, the way wild things freeze when a tone is wrong.

“We can do three,” the impulsive one said, and Lydia wanted to hug him, and then realized maybe that was the first wanting she’d felt all day that wasn’t about the past.

A cross breeze found the hallway and moved a strand of hair against her cheek. She had kept herself in the entry purposefully. The front room where the sale was anchored, the kitchen just over the low pass-through, the short hall to the bedrooms where she had taped off the closet with masking tape because she could not let anyone see the place where her mother’s clothes had hung until Tuesday. She had gone into that closet Tuesday morning and had stood looking at the cloth that had held her mother’s outline until the air smelled so richly of starch and fabric softener and a woman’s saved breath that Lydia had had to step backward and close the door.

A woman with a baby strapped to her chest swayed in the living room and touched the edges of the framed high school portrait of a girl from 1956 Lydia did not know. Lydia thought it must have been her mother’s friend who went to Akron and became a nurse, until she realized it was her mother. The hair, that baby-lift of curls; the chin that said I am gentle until I am not. Lydia had never liked that picture, which meant she had not looked at it long enough to make it either hateful or dear. The baby sneezed and opened and closed a fist and the woman asked, “Are you selling any blankets?”

“In the bedroom. Bed two.”

The woman stayed. “I’m sorry. You must be getting this all day. I didn’t know your mom well but she looks like she was kind. Just how she looks.”

“She was many things,” Lydia said. She did not mean it as refusal. The baby hiccupped. The hiccup was a bubble.

Her friend Eileen came at two with a thermos that smelled like coffee even when it was coffee. Eileen had been the town librarian since Mrs. Decker had relocated to Florida for her husband’s heart. She had those librarian shoulders that sloped inward in perpetual intimacy. Everything about her was a kindness except her directness. “You eating?”

“Do I look like I am?”

“Which one’s your snack,” Eileen said. She had known Lydia since the fifth-grade spelling bee, when Lydia had choked on the word “lavender” and wanted an e before the a and would not give it up until she cried. Eileen had mouthed the right letters from the audience and later confessed, “You made me cry with how you fought for that e.”

“My snack is this,” Lydia said, and held up a handful of small things: a jar of buttons, a small tin of needles, a set of shot glasses Lydia was sure her mother never used. She thought of a hundred ways her mother had not used things. How many gifts had stayed in boxes because they were too nice.

“You choosing?”

“I think so. I can’t keep a dining set. I can keep a jar.”

“They’re buying the dining set?” Eileen’s mouth made a circle.

“A young couple. They’ll probably throw it into a U-Haul and scar the legs and I’m going to decide I like that.”

Eileen unscrewed the thermos and poured into paper cups she had brought, and the coffee was bitter and clean. Lydia drank and felt the bitter as if it were a rib under her tongue. “They’ve said beautiful things,” she told Eileen. “About her. All day. I don’t know what to do with that and the fact that sometimes I wanted to run out of this house until my feet bled."

“You do nothing with it,” Eileen said. “You let it set up and become something. Jelly or cement.”

“Useful.”

“Hard,” Eileen said. “Both.”

On Maple Street out front, boys on bikes dragged their feet on the pavement to brake. An argument two blocks away lifted over hedge tops and blew away. There was that particular Ohio sky of July when everything is available and slightly haloed. The courthouse clock in town chimed three, distant, like somebody else's punctuality.

The goatee man reappeared from the basement with a tray of mason jars under his arm and a milk crate stacked with lengths of wood. He had cobwebs in his eyebrow and a spider like a punctuation mark on his shoulder. “Your dad had some good stuff down there. Old nails. Nails, you know, used to be real.”

“Everything used to be real,” Eileen murmured.

“Two dollars a jar,” Lydia said. She had been braced for him since he stepped onto the lawn. She was not braced for her sister. She didn’t have a sister. She had this woman standing near the piano like a sister who might have been, forty-fiveish, with a haircut Lydia associated with no-nonsense and good lotion. The woman was reading the music propped on the rack—Jesu, Joy of Man’s Desiring—as if the notes meant something beyond an arrangement of ovals and sticks.

“Did she play?” the woman asked without looking up.

“She taught me. Until I refused to be taught,” Lydia said, and the woman laughed the way women laugh where they want to be different from each other but are the same.

“I’m looking at this for my daughter,” she said. “She’s taken the school pianos apart with her ear. I’d like to give her a hostage that can’t be taken back.”

“Do you have a truck?” Lydia asked because it was easier to ask that than to say yes. It had been easier all day to ask about parking, stairs, strong backs, cash. It had been easier to be prosaic than to be elegiac.

“I can get one. And three men. I have a brother-in-law who owes me a favor because I let his wife stay with me when he was being a jerk about the dog.”

The price was three hundred. The woman touched the lacquer with only the back of her finger, as if the piano were a sleeping thing she didn’t know. She said, “That seems fair,” and when she reached into her bag and pulled out a fold of bills with the elasticity of someone who lives with teenagers, Lydia had to look at the curtains. She watched a fly land on the sill and celebrate its own suicide for an instant and then try for the screen.

“I can’t help you load,” Lydia said. “I can’t stay in the room while it goes.”

“That’s your right,” the woman said, and her voice was both matter-of-fact and kind, and Lydia loved her for the way she didn’t add, I’m sorry, or, I can imagine. She filled out a receipt because this woman would need to justify this to someone—money is always a discussion later—and Lydia wrote, UPRIGHT PIANO: $300 and the o in “piano” looked like the head of a pin.

The men came at three-thirty, and there was a lot of talk about ankles and lifts and angles and the dangerous welcome of stairs. Lydia went into the hall bathroom, shut the door, and sat on the closed lid of the toilet and listened to the complicated ballet of it: the rope of the strap pulling, the grunt and chuckle, the command of the man who had done this dozens of times. The first thud against the doorframe, too small to be lethal, almost intimate. The scrape of felt over wood. The woman’s voice saying, Careful not to catch your hand, and the thin coil in Lydia’s middle that meant a cry was the blade of a molecule away. She let it happen. It wasn’t the first time she had cried in that bathroom. The wallpaper was a pattern of lavender sprigs that had absurdly begun to peel at the seam where her mother had cut it too shy.

When it was over, it was over cleanly. She washed her face in the sink where she had banged her chin when she was seven and first learned the word “ridiculous.” She looked in the mirror above the sink and then flinched because the mirror had been taken down on Thursday and carried to the garage to go to Eileen’s niece, and this was air. She had seen her face against air. She pressed a towel to the wet where her cheeks had lifted without permission and left a little moony imprint on the towel and then folded it, as her mother would have, into a neat rectangle and put it back.

In the living room, there was an empty rectangle where the piano had been. The floorboards were lighter where the bench had sat, like skin under a bandage. There was a dust of felt, a smudge. Lydia ran her finger in the smudge and lifted it and thought with something like innocence: look at that.

The sale dwindled into the neighborhood’s twilight hour, when the people who think they don’t need anything stay away because they are cooking, and the people who know they don’t need anything come because need has nothing to do with it. A boy with a soccer ball came in and acted surprised there were things to buy, as if the sign in the yard were a joke. An old woman, hunched in that old-woman way where the body no longer trusts itself to be vertical, asked about yarn. “She had needlepoint upstairs,” Lydia said. “I put the canvas aside for a neighbor who asked. I can go get—” and the woman said, “No, let me just look,” and shuffled out to the porch and sat on the steps where the air was little more merciful. The porch had hosted bridal showers and ice cream parties and thick quiet at night when the lightning came sideways. That it could hold birds and women like that both was one of its glories.

A man brought up an aquarium from the basement with a trail of mildew like a comet tail and said, “Ten?” and Lydia said, “Sure,” and thought of the guppies her mother had tolerated for exactly a week and then had given away to the second-grade room at the elementary school, saying, Fish are for people who leave the house less.

A kid with a skateboard under his arm bought the bottle opener in the shape of a lobster and told Lydia he was going to hang it in the new place he and his girlfriend got. The new place was behind the Feed and Seed and had a view of the grain elevator that made the sky look like it had punctuation.

“Her name is Harmony,” he said, and Lydia could not help it: she loved the world savagely for a second because they were going to hang a lobster and a girl named Harmony was going to open bottles and feel like this was something.

At four, Eileen stood in the kitchen and said, “Do you want me to make a list of what’s left?”

“No,” Lydia said. “Make me a list of how to eat when your kitchen is gone.”

Eileen touched her on the forearm, the way friends in this town did when words were likely to make something worse. “Pizza. And then eggs the next day. And you eat on paper plates until you realize you care more about the planet than your sanity and then you wash one dish and dry it and assign it a name. Frank. Frank the plate. He will be your companion.”

“Frank,” Lydia said. “I always thought I’d pick a girl plate.”

“Oh, grow up,” Eileen said. Then, after a minute, “How are you doing with the thing.”

“What thing,” Lydia said, but she knew. The thing of it, the leaving. The particular piece she had been avoiding since the first price tag, since the first small. The dresses.

They were on the bed like people, laid out with their arms open, their sleeves waiting for anybody’s shoulders. The wedding dress in tissue—sleeves poofed from the ‘60s, lace like cake frosting someone had piped with iron hands. The black suit her mother had demanded to be buried in and then not worn because in the end the idea of what you’d wanted to do with the last outfit you wore trumped the reality of the body warming the notion. Lydia had put the suit away with her eyes half closed. The dresses, though, the others—summer cottons the weight of a sigh, collars crisp, buttons that were the size of coins in those years when currency told you about a nation—they were what caught her.

A teenage girl found the dresses. She hooked a hanger from its wire triangle and slid the hem between her fingers. “Oh, holy,” she said. “Do you think she’d care if I cut one of these down?”

“What would you make it,” Lydia asked in the tone of a test: saying nothing and waiting to see if this girl revealed intention or desecration.

“A skirt,” the girl said. “Or—oh, this one. This one with the little cherries. A shirt. I’d crop it and wear it to the fair.”

“You’d cut it,” Lydia said and felt, not for the first time, the mix of wanting to rename pain as fashion and anger as brightness and permission as revolt.

The girl met her eyes. “If you don’t want me to, I won’t. But looking at it is making me crazy. It wants out of the closet. I’m sorry, that sounds stupid. If you tell me no, I’ll say okay.”

“She would have hated that,” Lydia said. “The maybe.” She held the dress up. That pattern—cherries in pairs, stems crossed like shy girls—was from the summer when Lydia had made Eileen swear on a Bible she’d die before admitting to anyone that Lydia had bled through a pair of shorts at the county pool. Her mother had washed the shorts in cold water and said a thing Lydia had not recognized as kindness until last week: “There is no stain that can’t be made smaller.”

“I don’t know what to do,” she said aloud. She had been saying that sentence in her head in so many dialects that to hear it in her own voice made it simple and new.

“Take one,” the girl said. “Keep it. Then give someone else permission to love the rest to pieces.”

“That sounds dramatic.”

“It would. I cannot help it,” the girl said, and grinned. Her hair was cut in a bluntness that suggested she had gotten up from a kitchen chair and said, “Enough.” Lydia loved her too, for that. Resilience was one thing. Enough was another.

She ran her hand over the cherries. The cloth was warm to her palms from the girl’s touch and from the window light. She imagined cutting. She imagined leaving it whole, the dress lying forever like a waiting. She imagined herself carrying it home across the half-state and hanging it in a closet where it would not belong. She imagined the jar of buttons already in her bag, and the bluebird warming, and something in her chest opened like furrowed dirt when a hoe finds it.

“Forty dollars,” she told the girl, and then, when the girl flinched, not in greed but in the way a budget grieves, “Or ten and you bring me a picture of what you made.”

“Do you live here?”

“No,” she said. “I used to. I did. I don’t.”

The girl held out a narrow hand. “I am Maeve,” she said. “I come from up near Vanlue. I can bring you a picture. I will bring you two. I will wear it to the parade so everyone will know I ruined an adult thing.”

“I would like that,” Lydia said, startled by the way the present tense sat for the first time all afternoon like a bench that did not wobble.

An old man bought the fishing rods. He had the hands of an old man, swollen flowers, petals of skin. He said, “Your dad and I went to Indian Lake one summer of ‘sixty-one and caught nothing and then went to the diner and had pie and lied about it. I will tell that story on top of these rods until I’m gone.”

“Tell it loud,” Lydia said, and saw, for a ridiculous second, a pie the size of a table.

A teenage boy with acne like an alphabet across his forehead bought the stack of postcards her father had kept in a shoebox—Hocking Hills, Niagara Falls, a mountain goat on a road not within five states of Ohio. He paid with sweaty dollars and said, “I like the way the people talk like they’re friends with someone they aren’t friends with yet,” and Lydia had to swallow twice.

The light moved. Afternoon slid into that trough where it sits in Ohio, late in July, like a liquid you can cup in your hand. She stood in the doorway and watched people carry out the furniture of her childhood. It really was like that: the shape of her and John’s knees dented the cushions, the table rings like moons under the polish, the coat hook that had only ever held one red scarf in winter because everyone else wore practical colors and her mother said, That scarf is a show. A man tightened his grip on a bedside table and Lydia had the irrational thought Oh, I left the earplugs in the top drawer. She almost called out. It was like catching her mother mid-nap. Don’t shout. She will startle.

“What about this,” Eileen said at her shoulder. “What’s this box. It looks like a cigar but it does not smell like cigars. Which is a blessing.”

There was a red rectangle of wood that Lydia had not opened in at least twenty years. It had been on the high shelf of the pantry where the phone lines had been once, the tangle of cords making everything in the house feel connected. Lydia lifted the lid and there, like little soldiers at war with moths, were recipe cards in her mother’s slanted, forever surprising hand. Not cursive. Not print. The place between. Orange card for Jello salads—why orange?—green for cakes, white for things with eggs. Her breath made a small cheap fog over the first card. “Lazy Pierogi,” it read, and underneath: “Use butter, not margarine, don’t be silly.” She laughed and then put her hand to her mouth as if in church. Eileen leaned in and said, “Oh, there she is.”

“I said I was keeping the birds and the buttons,” Lydia said. “But I lied. I’m keeping this. I’m keeping this if I have to put my couch on the roof of the Honda and drive like a Dukes of Hazzard extra.”

“I think they call that a sign,” Eileen said. “From management.”

“Which management,” Lydia said. “Heaven? The part of my brain that knows what will feel like her when I’m gone?”

“Yes,” Eileen said simply. “All of that. Also your mother left notes on those. She always did. She always had an opinion about nutmeg.”

“She had an opinion about everything,” Lydia said, and for the first time that afternoon opinion felt like gift and not like shove.

By five, the rooms had been edited into their school year selves. Sheets of light fell where things had stood. The house did not look bigger so much as honest, the way someone looks in a candid shot they have not practiced for. The baseboards revealed the accumulation of lives in a line: the shadow of dust, the place were the vacuum had not gone because it could not have fit and now could. Lydia thought of the time she had run the vacuum with such fury on a Saturday morning in anger at her mother’s neededness that she had burned up the belt and the whole house had smelled like marshmallows gone to hell.

People left with their arms full. They balanced lampshades like floating hats. They pressed coins around the neat cardboard circle of a croquet set. Their mouths framed apologies for their comfort. Their eyes were shy and fond and invasive, all the ways a neighbor’s eyes can be when they are in your house.

A woman Lydia recognized from math class in 1979 but not from anywhere else held out a folded twenty and a Skyland High penance. “Helen babysat me when my mom had me young,” she said. “She taught me to make toast. Don’t laugh. Some people don’t know toast. She said, You watch and when it gets to the place between golden and brown, you slam the lever. She was a slammer.”

“She liked control more than machines did,” Lydia said, and the woman laughed and then angled herself through the screen.

The quiet that fell was the quiet that comes when the theater empties of the audience and the actors have gone and the stage still holds their breath. Lydia stood in the living room and waited to feel the relief she thought might come with emptiness. Relief didn’t come. It wasn’t grief that came, either. It was something around the edges that felt like clean.

“Do you want me to run anything to the Goodwill tonight?” Eileen asked. “There’s always that post-rush purge. People leave and you look up and say, Why did we think we needed this when what we needed was toast.”

“Tomorrow,” Lydia said. “I have to eat eggs and name a plate first.”

She went to the kitchen for the last time that day. The counters were a map of endings: a box of canning rings, a wooden spoon with a thin part where a child had worried it with her teeth, the last of the visitors’ paper plates in a chip bag clip. She pulled open the drawer where the dish cloths had been, knowing it would be empty. It was not. In the back corner, the one made inaccessible by a loose screw and her mother’s stubbornness, there was a mound of elastic bands. Fat ones, thin ones, the ones from produce and the ones with that impossible sticky powder on them. She held a handful and looked at Eileen and said, “Do you think anybody ever in the history of the world has run out of these.”

“Yes,” Eileen said. “Men.”

Lydia put the elastics in the Goodwill box. She wanted to believe in the distribution of small salvations. She turned to the wall beside the doorway, the inner side of the pantry where, under the two coats of latex paint her mother had insisted on after the divorce, you could still see the shadows of the pencil marks where heights had been recorded. There it was: “LYDIA—age 8—four foot one.” “JOHN—age 6—three foot eight.” “LYDIA again;” “JOHN again;” the attempt at fairness. Her mother had drawn tiny lines with dates, the numbers slanting up as if height could lean into the future. Lydia touched her own mark. If she pressed the pad of her finger against that odd little depression long enough, would it remember back at her. Would residue get into her.

“Do you want to take that board,” Eileen said, which was the kindest thing anyone had said. It is a human impulse to want to put a hinge on a closet with no door.

“No,” Lydia said. “I want it to stay. I want some things to stay. I want a woman buying this house—she is not a math teacher, she has a dog, she wore a bracelet I loved—I want her to put her hand right here and feel stupid loving what she will love.”

“Done,” Eileen said, and if she could have signed a legal document at that moment with a pen made of the soft part of a friend’s heart, Lydia might have.

There was a last customer. There is always a last. He was a skinny boy who moved like a rabbit and had the uncontained smell of outside. He looked as if he might ask for something sharp. He held a small, heavy thing in his hands and pointed it at Lydia. It was her father’s hammer. The wooden handle had the wear of a man who did not mellow his grip. The head was scored from where he had missed his rage and made a hole in a place he did not mean to.

“How much,” the boy said, in a voice so soft she had to ask him to repeat.

“How much,” she said, equal in softness.

“I like the sound it makes when you hit stuff,” he said, as if that were both a confession and a negotiation and a translation of boy into man.

“Two dollars,” she said, and he brightened. He gave her two dollars in singles, earned in who knows what hours, and walked out of the house with the hammer pointed away from him like good luck.

The quiet after this last was thick and green for a minute and then dissolved into something else. Lydia stood alone in the space between the living room and kitchen and looked at the house in both directions the way you look at a friend moving away from you in a crowd. She stepped back and then forward and then back. She turned off the kitchen light and then on, because who knew what rats of sadness might scurry in the dark, and then off again because why light a room that has already done so much.

She picked up the bluebird from the place she had hidden it on the top of the cabinet, tucked where her mother put things she pretended to forget but paid interest on. She put the bird in the pocket of her bag without drama. She slid the recipe box on top of the bluebird, and the jar of buttons beside them made a comforting small glass clink. She found, on the counter where her mother had kept clippings and coupons, a note her mother had written on the back of an envelope in April. “Ask Lydia about her summer,” it read, as if that were a reminder to be a mother and not just a woman who had learned to manage lists.

She put that in her wallet. She sat on the floor where the couch had been, where the imprint of the couch still pressed the carpet into obedience. She folded her knees. She thought of how her body had grown on those lines on the pantry wall and how, once you stop measuring something, it gives up growing and tries to find a way to be seen in some other dimension. She sat until she could name her sit: not collapse, not worship, not delay. Sit. She took out one recipe card and read the ingredients and saw her mother’s annotations: “Don’t use garlic if you’re bringing this to church.” “Don’t be impressed by people who buy their crust. Store bought is fine.” “You can always add more lemon.” She traced “lemon” with her finger, L’s climb, m’s hum.

Eileen came back in from the porch with the last empty laundry basket on her hip the way a woman carries a child and set it down on the floor. “I told the last car we are closing.”

“We are closed,” Lydia said.

“Yes,” Eileen said. “This iteration.”

They walked through the rooms together like tourists. Lydia named where things had been. Eileen named where things could be, not here but in the ordinary after. It made the house feel not sad, exactly, but not accusing. Lydia stopped in the bedroom doorway and looked at the pale square on the wall where her father’s photograph had hung, his face toward the light so that his left ear had looked like porcelain. She closed her eyes and saw it and then opened them and let the pale square be the only thing to look at. That rectangle seemed like a lid lifted from a cookie jar: everything accessible and no cookies left. It was a shape that admitted the absence. She did not know why that felt like a comfort.

At the front door she held the banister ball in her palm. It was loose. It had always been loose, a little, and her mother had said, “Leave it. Every house needs one flaw as a way for the luck to get in.” Lydia tightened it anyway, on instinct, and then loosened it one half turn.

Outside, the cicadas had started their low thrumming that made afternoon sound like a held chord. She propped the door open and let the twilight in one last time. She could hear from somewhere down the block a girl singing nonsense. The smell of someone grilling—fat and sweet, that awning of summer’s breath—floated up Maple. She could stand here all night, she thought with a small panic. She could stay until the light was gone and the porch was secrets, not chairs. That was not the point of this: to sit vigil forever. The point was to make the first motion toward after. To lean and breathe and let the door close while you were still the one holding the knob.

Eileen took the sign from the yard and balanced it under her arm. The rectangle of flattened grass where it had been looked like a shallow grave for something too small to bury honestly. Lydia glanced down and then, without thinking, scuffled her sneaker across it until it looked less like a mark and more like yard.

“Give me your hands,” Eileen said. She did not ask often; she asked now. “I’m going to be some kind of religion here for a minute if you’ll let me.”

“You can be a religion. But I refuse to kneel,” Lydia said.

“Whatever your posture,” Eileen said, and held Lydia’s hands in hers and said nothing, and the nothing was both the prayer and the end of the prayer.

They turned out the lights. Lydia locked the front door. She stepped across the threshold the way people step across the cracks in a sidewalk if they have read a certain amount of poetry when young. She put the key in under the frog on the porch, the one with the open mouth that had been a joke and had become a practice. She did not know if the new woman would find the frog funny. It would be alright either way.

They stood on the front walk and looked back the way you look at a place you have loved and loathed and been built by. The living room window showed only a room. It did not show any scene, any story. That was decent. The town was doing its early evening cruise past the house, not to gawk—these people did not gawk at sorrow; it was too familiar a farm crop—but to be sure she had got through it. An old man in a truck tipped his fingers off the wheel, that small salute men gave a hundred times a day so the air would know it knew them.

Eileen put the sign in the trunk of her Impala next to a sack of green beans she would intend to can and then not because she would end up eating them all raw. “You come to my house,” she said. “I will make eggs so good they will stop your heart."

“I’m going to stay for a minute,” Lydia said. “I’ll walk. Will you pick me up on the way back from—wherever you go after this. The ice cream place. The sun. Know that I’m okay. I am. It’s just the last—”

“Sure,” Eileen said, and got in the car and left, the engine a sturdy hum, the Impala square in the way old sedans were, as if they promised you a right angle every time you wanted one.

Lydia walked to the end of the walkway and put her hand on the mailbox. It had his name and then her mother’s name in vinyl letters on the side, because names on mailboxes had been the fashion and because her mother liked the way letters looked when they took themselves seriously. She turned and faced the house and breathed it once. She did not say goodbye out loud. She said it to the place on the porch where her mother had kept a plant that would not thrive no matter what seasons of lecture it received. She said it to the rake that had stood on the side of the house like a soldier who knew only one order. She said it to the small wildness that would grow under the eaves in August, the little ferns that looked like freckles.

A couple walked by with their dog and nodded and said, “Sorry for your loss,” and she nodded back and did not say, You aren’t talking about loss, you’re talking about a location. My loss is many places. She thought of the recipe card. She thought of Maeve’s cherries when the parade came and the band went past and a girl would stand on a curb and feel herself the kind of person a crowd looks at. She thought of the hammer and the boy finding the right sound for hitting. She thought of the piano moving out, the way something heavy and important is still vulnerable to an angle.

She waited until the street quieted to the place where you could hear people dawdling, which is its own sound: steps that do not belong to a destination. She walked to the corner and turned, out of habit, to look back. She didn’t bother to memorize. The house had been smaller inside for years than it was on the street. It had been bigger in memory than it could be in person. Both those things were true.

Three girls sat on the church lawn up the block with their phones glowing and their knees up, talking not to each other and entirely to each other anyway. A train sounded far off and then closer and then threaded the town. Trains in Ohio are like sermons: you do not always want them, they demand themselves, they go on past when you think your attention will.

By the time Eileen’s car pulled up again, summer had tempered by a degree and the sound of forks on plates had taken over the block. Lydia got in with the recipe box on her lap and the bluebird opening its beak in the pocket of her bag, ready to be placed on a different windowsill, the buttons riding like currency for a country she did not yet live in. She closed the passenger door and set the box between them and Eileen put a hand on it lightly, like you might touch a book when someone is telling you the story of it, so that the story knows everything it needs to be said.

“You hungry,” Eileen said.

“Terribly,” Lydia said, and then added, in the instant before Eileen could turn the key, her mouth tasting like metal and lemon both, “Ask me about my summer.” Eileen looked at her and then began to laugh, not loudly but in the way that shakes the head and sets something right, and Lydia laughed too, and then they drove away, and the house behind them did what houses do once you leave: it held, it stayed, it let you go.
