By eight the sun had undone the last of the morning's thin, milky fog, and with it went my excuse to linger up on the dam road, looking down. The reservoir lay slack, pulled back to a shape I didn't recognize, with bleached ribs of shoreline curving like a creature turned on its side. The water had never looked green from up here, not the way it was in men’s brochures when the dam was new, but this summer its surface was slackened tin, flickering with a skin of gnats and heat. Beyond it, the basin widened, a spread of silt and caved-in walls, and the meander of something that used to be a street, all of it so light the gulls could make shadows on it just by gliding. When I killed the truck’s engine, the noise dropped out of the world. No motors out on the lake yet. Just locusts starting up, the distant scream of a hawk. The concrete parapet of the dam still wore the same stains I remembered from when we first drove over it a lifetime ago, the russet tide lines standing six and ten and twelve feet higher than the water now, and some teenager had scrawled an apology in chalk where the tourists like to stand: I DIDN’T MEAN IT.

No clouds. No wind, not yet. It would be a hard day for line work, but the job wasn't going away. There are some tasks no one thinks to do until drought writes them into necessity, and this one had my name on it before the memo ever got sent. I could have begged off. I could have said, send Tyler and Kendra, they climb better; or, wait until fall when it cools; or, it's not a good time, my mother is sick. I said nothing. I signed the requisitions. I kept quiet about how the job and the place made a circle that had started a long time ago without asking me.

I set the base station on the high point of the old spillway overlook, where a coin-op viewer used to be, its metal stump still bolted into the slab. The bolts were furry with rust. The overlook had been the end of a fenced-off terrace then. Now, the chain-links were in a pile, cut and coiled on the ground like dead eels. I braced the tripod legs, checked the bubble, leveled until the eye was perfectly ringed, did the fussy things so later the points would hold and the lines would not wobble when someone else opened the file at a desk far from here and wondered why the star of points had gone soft, why the centerline bent. My receiver caught sky and muttered until the flood of talk between satellites and ground relaxed into a number I could live with. I named my session what the county would want it named, not the name I'd have given it if I were the only human being ever to read it. Project_Lacombe_Drawdown_Streets_2026. Not, Unable to Sleep, Returning.

The handpick, the flagging tapes, the prism, the mini-prism, the scrub brush for knocking clay off boots, the spare batteries, the spare of the spare—they went into the day pack. The tablet went on its lanyard. I clipped on the radio and the horn. I took a minute to look again before I slung it all over a shoulder. From up here the exposed town was a test of the eye’s honesty: what you took to be lines were cracks; what you thought were gullies were once gutters; what you were dead sure was a foundation was a shadow. But some things said their names plain. The double arc of Sycamore Lane, though the sycamores were long since felled. The pale, straight edge where Commerce had been wide and stoned. The two staircases of First Baptist standing alone like teeth set in old gums. The little rectangle of the school garden where we'd grown beans and marigolds to keep pests down, as if marigolds could keep anything out. And a flat square toward the northern slope that could be any of a dozen homes, any except the one that it was.

On the dam road there were already cars pulled halfway onto the gravel shoulder. A pickup with a Confederate flag license plate; a crossover with a baby stroller folded in the back. Dog people and camera people and people who had nothing to do on a Wednesday morning except peer off into a story they'd never been told directly. One woman wore a t-shirt that said SPOOKED TOWNS. She leaned over the parapet with her phone at arm's length and then pulled it back like the scene might reach for her. Two kids played a game where they tried to spit as far as they could into the dry. Their mother told them to stop, that lady was trying to work. I wasn't, yet, but I nodded anyway. The air had that lake smell, the combine of dead algae and damp rope and fish scales drying on a dock, and under it, the sour bottom-silt smell I've never been able to describe to anyone who hasn't had to live with it: like old vegetables left in a plastic drawer and opened in August; like wet pennies; like whatever a river remembers of its old town when it is called back over it.

On the way down the access road I passed the closed-down bait shop at the bend, its sighing coaster sign: Minnows. Worms. Ice. Mr. Banner had boarded the windows when the lake went to nothing but drought in the eighties, then unboarded them when the water came back and the bass tournaments swung their grills around to point at his lot. He'd died sometime after. The sign hadn't moved, but the individual letters in the window that used to spell OPEN OPEN OPEN had slumped to read PEON. There was a stack of bright orange barriers by the downramp and a deputy posted at the sawhorses. He looked bored, looked young. He asked to see my badge and I showed him the worn laminate, County of Lacombe embossed in blue. He said, "Stay off anything that looks like it wants to collapse."

"Yes," I said.

He looked over my shoulder like maybe the whole dewatered town would get up and aim for us. "My granddad," he said, "he told me about the time they set fire to the houses. I guess they thought it would make the ground cleaner. I guess it did not."

"They pulled all the pipes, the wiring, the copper," I said. "They took the doors and the good boards. Burning was faster than hauling what was left."

"You from here?"

"Somewhere like it."

We both knew a dozen somewheres like it in the state. The dam-building was a story you could trace across the map with a ruler, find the places where the river had been mustered into obedience and towns were told to hold their breath as water rose over their heads. The county liked to call it progress. The paper had printed a photo last week of a girl in shorts sitting on a square of pavement the caption called a ghost Cadillac lot. It was not a ghost Cadillac lot. It had been the feed store where my mother could get a bag nailed shut with a string that pulled quick as a flight of sparrows.

The deputy was still staring; he didn't want the talk; he wanted the lie, that it all happened to other people. "I'll be careful," I told him, and started walking.

On the mud there was a crust, and then below it a layer that sucked. The trick was to step where sun had found your footstep to dry. The gulls had gotten ahead of me, had already decided that if the humans had turned the lake down like a faucet they could use the new flats for their assemblies. They stood like executives in their meetings, all oriented toward a speaker with his mouth open and his wings slightly pitched. They made their rail-yard noises until I got too close and then, with a hiss of breath, popped themselves up into the air. The other thing that moved was crickets. A disaster for crickets. They made a noise like cardboard wheels. I wanted to tell them what I know children want to be told: that the water would come back, that the great wide shining they remember in the way insects remember will lay over all this again, and the sun will travel across it all day, and the night will throw stars on it, and their chirr will be a good sound. But I have told that lie as a professional too many times. It's in the way we do the maps, the pretty color gradients for low and high, the arrows that show flow, the bright blue lines the public can understand. The water will come back, yes. But not because it is owed this basin. Not because the crickets ask.

Ahead of me the curb line ran taught and straight for sixty yards and then, at an intersection with what had been Elm, bulged outward like a lip in the place where cars had slowed and bumped their tires up to it and then down again. That told me two things before I set a prism: one, the concrete had been poured in seventy-foot sections; two, someone had had the idea to widen the entrance there in the 1950s when cars themselves had gotten wider. I opened the channel on my radio and checked that the receiver on the overlook was listening. The tablet chirped faintly against my chest until it silenced into numbers.

Once I set the first shot, the job fed me. Spine into elbows into hands, sighting along the staff until the bubble centered like an answer. Point: curb; feature code: CBLFT; note: Intersection Sycamore/Elm, station zero. Beep. A glucose pellet for my animal brain. Something I could want again and again. My eyes let go of the whole picture and I was in it at scale, my own two feet my measuring sticks, the prism reading back my tiny unforgivable human speed. I moved south along Sycamore, the curb making a long clumsy S around settling and pop-outs where tree roots had pushed up then rotted and left their ghost upthrusts in the cement. A piece of rebar stuck out rust-black and crocheted with algae. I used the brush and kicked silt out from the joints to get the inside corners of the curb returns, because right angles are fewer in the world than maps pretend, and these, too, after sixty years and the weight of a lake, had pivoted inward or spread. Bits of blue-and-white tile lay on the surface of the clay like fish scales. I did not pick them up.

At Third, where the drugstore had once stood, I could see the form of its basement, the concrete notched where the staircase had been. The drugstore had been where Mr. Clary had cut his own hair in the little mirror propped in the window and then swept the trimmings into an envelope for his wife to stuff a pincushion with. The front steps came back to me with a feeling in my calves. I can still stand in places in this county and know in my body if I was a child there, not because of memory of thought but because my muscles tell me how high up a step had been, how they had to heft me. The red and white sign had come down during clearing. The old men who formed the clearing crews had tried to convince each other to save this or that—the record store’s neon, the bell. In the end, too much work. Better to burn. My mother had cried when we drove past the piles of black and her voice was the only sound in the car. She had a scrape on her forearm from a nail. That image, too, is stiff in me like a small bone: the dirty white edge of the Band-Aid and the way she pulled it off and later didn't look to see if blood had restarted.

The sound of another voice is always startling when you're working alone and your edges have been loosened, and I was a dozen shots south and lighting a new cigarette when I heard, "You with the county?" It came from the left, from the low side. A man was letting himself down the cut with his hands and the heels of his boots, keeping his ankles turned to press, not to slide. When he got to Sycamore proper he stood and looked left and right like it was a new street he meant to cross. He wore a baseball cap in the shape of a ball cap and not the stretched fashion thing, and his mouth had those radiating bracket lines on it I recognize in people skilled at kindly gossip. He had a plastic bucket looped over his forearm.

"I am," I said. "Stay out of the hard holes. The silt looks like stone, and then it wants you."

"Does it." He took a step; it held. Another; it held. He inhaled through his nose. "Smells like an old boat cushion had a child with a catfish."

"That's not wrong."

"I'd never have guessed you could just see it like this," he said. His eyes were telling him things his voice didn't yet trust to say out loud. He squinted southeast. "Is that First Baptist? Is that the Steps?" He said it like a capital letter. I followed his look. It was first Baptist. It was the steps. Looked small from here. Small and lonely. You can't see the rest of a church because churches are mostly absence: open space, echoes. The steps were—if you are kind—an index.

"They're on my list," I said. He stared like he should have one too. "You live around here?" he asked, not quite making it a question about me.

"Upcountry," I said. Names are cheap. Self-story is more expensive. "You're not supposed to take stuff," I said, nodding toward his bucket.

He looked at it as if I'd reminded him it was on his arm. "Everybody's taking something."

"You could crash through a soft place hauling a cinderblock."

"A man can't pick up a nail?"

"You'll cut your hand and then you'll dip it in the reservoir and you'll be a headline."

He thought about it. "I brought gloves," he said, opening the bucket to show me a pair like oven mitts from late last century, thick, like they'd been used to handle logs. He closed the bucket and did not set it down. "Where'd you say you were from upcountry? Whose people?"

"Whose people are here?" I said.

"Oh, honey, not mine. Not one person as a person. Names, though. My mother would name roads by who was at the third house on a curve. That takes a county to keep alive, and the county didn't."

"That's true." It was the truest thing she'd said and she was he but he had said honey. I didn't mind. He had the tone. "I knew people with those hands," I said, nodding at the gloves. "Charhauling hands."

"Ah," he said, light coming on. "Your daddy one of those? Break'em down, load'em up, burn what you can't haul?"

"My father was a mason," I said. "He did not get hired." That was gentle in my mouth. The truth had teeth I'd learned to keep covered. Meanness in this place reproduced well and hid its young.

"You come to gawk or you come to work?" he said, all curiosity made soft by age. He had to put the questions into the air so they wouldn’t have to sit in his head and begin to change shape and itch.

"Work," I said, and raised the prism pole in my gloved hand. The bubble was still centered. "Go see your stairs."

"My stairs," he said, pleased, and he pushed off along Elm, not my Elm but the one that dropped. He didn't sink. I'd done some of these descents so often years ago my legs had their own maps. He had the beginner's luck. He would find a patch that wanted him and then either think to back out slowly or he would fall in up to his thigh and panic and we’d throw a rope. I imagined the rope across his white glove and the black clay making it blacker.

When I came up on First I paused not because it was work but because it was my Sunday school, which is like saying my first hours of sitting still and being taught to be small were done on a bench where someone had written Hee Haw in the wax of the wood as if it were a prayer. The steps were not only steps but also the porch that once had double doors and stained glass that made a bruise of color in your hand if you put your palm up into the light to check the blood vessels in it. Now the porch had two upright stumps that had held the hinges and were sawed off clean. At the foot of the steps was a scatter of rounded pebbles; because they were out of place here I recognized them. They had once filled the baptistery, to let the water drain out through their spaces without taking mud with it. They had rolled when the building came down and a few had a patina line halfway up their sides where sixty years of lake level had kissed them and then stayed. I leaned to press a finger to the line. It was a false sense—they were just cleaner there—but it made something in me rest, seeing how long a kiss could last in silt.

"Is that a church?" a boy asked behind me. He was sticky pink from sunblock, a summer boy with narrow shoulders and wide feet, the shape boys have when they've only just realized their limbs can betray them if they try to show off and the trick is to make it look meant. He held a snake's skin in his hands like a scarf. The skin was almost whole and it made a tender noise when he breathed on it. Behind him was his mother with a wide hat and a look of someone regretting the summer but knowing what it is for. "He can't go up, can he?" she asked me. "Something about this," and she waved at the steps, "tells me it’s going to slough."

"It's stood for sixty years under pressure," I said. "It won't consider him special. Stay on the solid parts." I thought of the marigolds we'd planted along the front walk in spring. Mrs. Cutter had brought starts in egg cartons and said marigolds kept rabbits and lessons out of sprouting. It hadn't worked, of course. The rabbits had eaten the marigolds and then run through our legs when we tried to chase them around the side.

"Is this somebody's town?" the boy said. Above his lip was a streak of the white kind of sunblock that won't rub in, lining out the shape of his mouth like he had a mustache made of milk.

"Yes," I said.

"Whose?"

"Mine," I said before I could not say it. "It was mine."

He looked at the steps differently. Then he looked at me. "Where was your house?" I pointed north toward a place that was and wasn't, not because I missed the place where I'd slept exactly but because I woke sometimes and found I'd been measuring a house in my dream, walls into corners, corners into the secret places behind the couch where pennies fell, and the dream always refused to tell me how many times a person has to go away before the place stops expecting them. "Can we go look?" he said. His mother began to say no but I could see she also wanted to be where the boy was already turned toward.

"It's slippery," I said. "And the ground does strange things."

"She works here," the boy told his mother as if I'd asked his permission. "She can do it. Also you can go back to the car if you want. It's boring."

"It's not boring," she said with her mouth and rolled her eyes with her whole head and smiled to let me know she was not angry.

"I guess it isn't," the boy said after sampling the words. He looked at the staircase. "Was it scary?"

"When the water came?" I asked. "No. The dam kept it slow. It was slower than you think."

"Did it splash?"

"It lapped and then lapped higher and then lapped until it lapped where you are standing," I said. I looked at where he was standing. My brain did that trick it can do now where it overlays a line, a contour, a bench mark, some little number that says this is as high as something had been, and I saw where it would come up to on his pale shin. The mother watched my face, reading what we look like when we are recalling the real.

The boy set his snake skin back on the steps with care, patting it like a blanket.

"Don't take things," his mother said with relief in her voice, a child's obedience containing in it absolution for her, and I looked away to give them their moment while I put a new dot on my tablet and labeled it CWBOT—church west bottom—and in my notes wrote round stones, still with tide mark, because I have learned over years that writing for the future-me like she is a stranger is a generosity, and knowing this stupid small thing again one day might be what coaxes her through the afternoon.

They went their way, down slope, and I went mine. Sycamore opened onto Commerce and there, at the corner, my feet expected one more step because that was what savings of momentum had taught them. There had been a ramp down into Woolfson's, the store with the fan that made its own weather, and on the fan a chain of paper tabs the girls had cut with scissors to look like pennants. When you were small and sweaty and your mother had spent too long talking to Mr. Woolfson about cloves for the jar and screws—he always wanted to tell her three screw sizes when she only needed one so she would feel safe—your mother would raise her finger to warn you not to and then say go on, for goodness sake, put your hands in front of that fan, and you would stand in line with the other children and bless the person who had first made a circle of blades to push heat out of your hair. In floods, in a drought, a fan is a promise someone once made you and then kept. The ramp was there. It had kept its slope. The handrail jutted up out of its concrete jacket like an arm, the wood gone a long time since the clearing crews had used it to patch a barn, the iron still insisting. Across the way the bank's marble floor had erupted in a neat way I recognized from the places where the city's pipes had been pulled and the hollow left behind had collapsed like lungs sighing: the squares of tile canted, settled, nothing so messy as a real break, everything respectably out of plumb. I did the rims of the curb returns, made a string of points, and the form of my folder on the tablet grew, from one dot to ten to a thread to a line.

By the time I turned north my shirt had given up pretending it was its own thing and simply lay on me like I was a ridge it had to inhabit. The insects hummed in octaves. Out on the flat a heron stilted, its gaze in that fish-narrowness I want to call concentration but is probably simply otherness: the bird has a job and that job is older than the jobs my kind gives names to, and I don't want to be the person who calls it something just because my head requires everything to be named. On the far hillside eucalyptus the county had planted years ago because they grow fast were starting to tip from drought root-rot. Their bark peeled in long papery strips like skin after a sunburn. Farther north, dark squares that were basements with wood frames still hugging in around incredible damp air. I put my pole down and went slower. The air cooled my teeth when I breathed through my mouth because there was so much water trapped there below my nose. An old stovepipe jutted sideways like a telescope. Water spiderlets—out of place—skittered. I stuck to the humped, dry parts. A few steps I knew even blind from long memory: here there had been a patch where the sidewalk had heaved and my father had tried to level it himself with a pry bar because he could not stand to walk by it twice and let a thing like that act unaddressed. He had cut the toe of his boot on the edge. After, we had sat on the porch and he had put his finger in his mouth and then held it to the cut, and he had made a noise when the wet met the leather that sounded like he was both cussing and laughing, both of which he would have told us not to do in church.

When I saw the crack, I knew it. Of course I did. The crack in our porch step had been the map my child’s eyes used to find the threshold. It had been the shape of our state, which is an elongated hexagon no one outside of here recognizes until you hold your own hand to the outline enough times that the mind tucks it into its drawer of shapes it can call up at will. The crack was smaller than in my child's mind and exactly its size. Something about the heat had widened it. The line of it was clean and hard, the concrete edges shorn neat like a part in hair you make with the side of a comb. I put my boot beside it and felt that fierce readiness we feel when a part of the past that is not an object but a place in our bodies comes back: shoulder height against the doorjamb, the little dance your foot makes to find the place where one more inch of length in your leg will make this thing yours.

They had knocked our house down before the water came up. I had gotten to see it after, when the foundation was only itself and the pipe that had carried the gas was stubbed up, and the men with their hands had stood around and said we could not play on it because we might break a leg. They had turned around two minutes later and we had played on it. There was a wet cigarette end resting on the porch. The cigarette had lipstick on it, a red more orange than women wear now, and a half-moon of teeth print. The lipstick was the exact color of the marigolds. I had wanted to pick it up, and my cousin had slapped my hand.

"It leaches," I said out loud now. "Don't put that in your mouth." And I laughed then for a sound to answer me. It came back off the little walls in a hush and I knew the hush as well: the way anything spoken here quiets and soothes, the way this ground had always been a good sounder-off.

I stepped over and onto the slab. It tilted, slightly, the way it had when my father, the mason, who could not afford a mason when it was our house, had done the work himself with a level that was missing a bubble and a spirit I thought so immense nothing in the world could trouble it. The indented oval where my mother had set a chair leg and moved it and the leg had not found the divot again and then she had tried to knock the divot back level with a rock, and then she had felt silly and set the leg there anyway. The square patch where a square of linoleum had lifted in our kitchen. The low threshold in from the little breezeway where my cousin Jonah had grabbed a hornet with his fingers by accident and come up like something sprung. The steel dowels sticking up at the corner ready for a wall that never came back. "House of Ruth," my father had written in carpenter's pencil on the first day he started framing it for the second time, which he did because he believed in starts, not because he didn't believe in endings. The damp of my eyes pooled in the exact locations it had when I had watched the men sign the thing that meant this went away.

I put the prism pole down and took a shot on the center of the threshold. I wrote in my notes not Threshold, which is a clean word, but the town's word: Stone-sill. I put a second point at the crack with the name I should not have let myself assign to something that must be neutral and replicable in an office: PORCHCRACK. I did not add, shape of the state, but my hand melted against the foam of the pencil in a way that told me I had thought it.

Above the slab, the air was a little cooler. It would be my imagination to say that the slab itself was still holding the house's temperature, all these years later, each room assigning to its area the micro-degrees of a kitchen full of boiled rinse-water or a bedroom's sleep-cooled corners. It was imagination, but this is what imagination is for; it keeps me in arms with the parts of the world that don't need me. I walked where my parents' bed had been and my mother's vanity. The place her jewelry box had sat in the top drawer, the velvet worn thin. I crouched to look under the place her dresser had been though there was no under. The clay pressed my boots. A thin square of yellowed paper lay to one side, and I reached in my gloved hand to pinch it up. It was a card from a pack, the Ace of Spades, the spade blackened. It had half of a bitten corner like a child had taken to it cud-chewing. Tight to the paper was a bit of silt, like fur, reluctant to let go. I put it back. You do not fill your pockets with a place you cannot afford to lose.

Someone skidded in the silt above. The sound pulled the hair up on my arms. A boy's yip. A woman's breath in, quick. The clatter of a chunk of a curb. I took the pole and stood. The slope north of us had always been gentle and this quart of sun and summer had turned it into something else, a set of little landslides that didn't so much travel as melt. I went up not because I thought I had to but because standing downstream of panic and watching it bristle has always made me feel like the kind of child who stands aside when the class lines up for a fire drill. The boy had been the one with the snake skin. He had not gone back to the car. He had found a hunk of tile that was a color the county had liked for bathrooms in the spring of 1953 and was proud of the weight of it, proud like a fisherman is proud of a fish that paid him for his waiting. He had stepped wrong with his load.

"I told you not to go down in the tricky spots," his mother said, a sentence she had sworn she'd never say because she believes in letting boys be brave and has learned that ninety percent of bravery is luck. The boy had one leg down to the mid-thigh in a place where the moisture had undercut the crust and his knee bent in a way that called to your own knee like a warning from the past. I set down the pole and took the boy by the wrist and the mother by the hand and said, do not fight it, you will lose your shoe if you fight it, and pulled up and back slow so the mud thought there was still a leg in it. His shoe did slide; he yelped and laughed and when he toppled back he made a circle in the silt like an impact crater from his butt. He pointed down into the hole like there would be something there at the bottom—bones or a marble or the answer to time. The tile cracked when he dropped it.

"You can't take those," I said. "They want to be in their own place." I did not say what I meant: that if we started to civilize this taking then we would never stop. What I wanted for this place is what you'll say a snob wants for any place: I wanted the lookers to look and the takers to leave empty-handed until I had done what can be done for a thing like this—take its measurings, tie it to this way of keeping what rivers pretend not to understand: the words of those who say them over and over until they are what is still said after us.

"We're not," the mother said, too bright, making the rule shape itself between her mouth and the boy's ear so it could be obeyed and then turned into a story about the way they are when they tell themselves who they are. We do not steal. We take only pictures, leave only footprints. We were there when.

"You from here," the boy said, not asking. The snake skin still lay laid out on the steps like it might keep itself warm. He had a smear of black on his shin and he used two fingers to lift it off and smear it thinner. "What's your name?"

"Mara," I said. I did not give him my last name. My last name here is a kind of address you can place your palm against to be warmed. Most people won't put their hand on another person like that nowadays.

"Mara," he said in the voice children have where they are smoothing syllables so they fit into the slots the summer makes in their throats. "When it comes back, will it be fast?"

"It will start up there," I said, and pointed to the mouth of the creek where it had always come in. "It will make a wet then a slick then a sheen and then you will say, mother, I think it's a lake again, and it will get into the places you are seeing now and then your snake skin will rise up off those steps like a prayer and the paper bits will get damp and then the teeny fish will come and bump with their mouths at the things and test them."

"They will?" His face said he did not think a fish had a mouth that could bump.

"They will," his mother said, and then added because she is a woman and knows what to say to make the lie less of a lie, "if it rains. If it rains and rains."

"If you are patient," I said, thinking of how patience sometimes is simply a word we use when we mean else: if you cannot control a thing, if you have exhausted other possibilities, if the power has to come from somewhere else. I had stood on the hill when the water came last time and it had been like watching a bad temperament enter the room: you wanted to will it into goodness and instead it had glared up at you like, I am not your child.

After they had walkedeast toward the middle school yard that had never been a middle school yard, after their shoe and shin prints had marked the mud in scallops, I took four clean steps deeper into what had been our kitchen and pressed my heel into a place where I knew there had been a pop of tile that sang when you stepped on it. Someone else might not have noticed that place; I did. Under the layer of clay and silt and softened cement, my heel caught a flatness that gave and I felt a tremor in my calf. I wanted to say, there it is. I said it out loud in case there was a person nearby who needed to hear what we say when we are where we thought we'd never be again and it turns out some of it is waiting and a lot of it is gone. There it is.

I spent the rest of the morning on the streets. At Maple a teenager had spray-painted a date in white, 1971, though the town had been drowned in sixty-four. I aimed for the anachronism of it, the way his wrongness would be a little building block of the place the county will later say is lacuna—holes in data—misinformation—my job to fill. I took curb to curb at the corners, then mid-block, then edges where the slope had given. Where Center Street ran down to the old ferry landing there was a swallow's nest under the overhang on the one chunk of the ferry office's wall that remained upright, attached for no reason except it had been poured thicker there. The nest was new. Swallows build with spit and mud. They were happy in the new world. Under the nest the wall wore all the little half-moons of their brush, the stucco built up in lapping tongues. "How long did that take you," I said into my radio, to no one. "What is your wage."

At the feed store's east wall two boys had lined up rocks and were knocking them with bigger rocks. When a small rock cracked apart soft inside, they groaned. I wanted to tell them not to break the creek's garnets; I wanted to tell them how my father could look at a wall and say, if you pull that out the rest will go, leave it, leave it and the thing will stand. The urge to instruct ran hot in me and I swallowed it and let it cool in my throat. Every job gives you a time scale to live on while you do it, and then you get to go back to the one you share with the old lady at the store and the girl on her skateboard and the truck whose muffler is rattling on its last and you are kinder to the truck for having once forced yourself to think like stone. These boys would break a hundred smooth rocks today and stand up smarter. We don't get to grow things in people with the speed we want to.

Somewhere, a bell. Impossible. A trick of the ear. It was just a single clang, and then another, separate. A gull dropped a shell and then dropped it again. It fooled me twice before I laughed out loud and for a flash saw myself the way a drone would see me: woman in a black hat and a shirt ringed dark at the back, standing in the white geometry of a grid, talking to herself or the birds, small. The only thing that gives the bird's-eye view its moral is that we presume it is the eye of something that might judge. If you think it's only a bird, you can laugh again.

By noon the brush of voices from the dam told me the bus had come. Channel Nine. People love a drought not because they love harm but because the bottom of the reservoir is a wound that is easy to look at and say, there. There it is. We are doing this to ourselves or it is being done. There are ghosts you can almost point to. A reporter with hair too perfect for this county had on waders and a man with a camera bumping his battery at his hip was trying to do a squat in clay. "We're not allowed to," he said to her twice when she asked him to go out farther, toward the riverbed that was slick in ways a flatlander fails to read. The deputy had been joined by a man from County Parks with an orange vest that made his skin look wrong. They were telling people the difference between curiosity and trespass.

I kept north. The thing I hadn't wanted to do now was the end of the oval of my path, and you can't set an oval and leave the last little curve unmeasured. In the yard of what had been the Russes' place a crab apple had taken itself back into the earth and left behind a grid of voids where its roots had been, each yet filled with silt that had flowed into the negative spaces like a spill into a mold. The pattern was like lungs; the pattern was like deer hooves; the pattern was like something I'd seen on the underside of my eyelids when I'd rubbed them too hard and then seen the shapes the rods and cones make for us when we turn them off and then ask them to keep working. At our neighbor's there had always been a jockey statue, white with a red jacket, and a little painted whip. They had taken it with them when they moved to a raised ranch on Ainsley Road, but there was still a circle in the splay of the front walk where the statue had stood, and the circle was cleaner. Around the circle the red grit that was the county’s favorite pre-mix for sidewalks in those years showed. In the circle it was gray. The absence had been covered; the covered had been undercovered; the undercovered had been watered. It is a funny life, to think in layers like this, and I am a boring friend at a party. I tell stories about utility easements and the way something that looks like a field is in fact a plaza full of right angles and ceremonies for what is underneath. "Drink your beer," someone will say to me, kindly, and I will remember the room and the people in it and that there are men whose hands still do this work and we are not simply clerks of the past.

At the end, under the brittle green of weeds that had made a go of a month, the square of the septic tank. I didn't expect to see it, and when I did I had to step back. Some humiliations you think you finished bearing. The concrete lid was cracked as if someone had put a car too close to it and rolled. A dragonfly stopped and started above it, sampling the moisture. My grandmother had stood over that lid the day we moved and said to my mother, the man at the diner told me they put their septic next to the drive and when they had a party certain people would make sure to use certain bathrooms and I told him he was too proud to marry our girl. That was not the day for it. But people have their one way to ride a horse and this was hers. I could see my mother's mouth, a straightness. She had wanted to throw salt over her shoulder after the doc signed the paper about my sister and she hadn't. Her mouth had been not the mouth of a woman moving from one house to a nicer house but the mouth of a woman who had learned how to have a mouth men took to be agreeable.

There is a picture of me at three where I am standing in this yard. There is a smooth tin hoop in my hand that my father made by bending a strip and hooking it through with a nail and he would roll the hoop and I would chase it and every time I did I thought this was a thing we had invented for our small two-person world and then he took me to Mr. Banner's and I saw a boy do it in front of the minnows and my heart cinched like a belt. And also it leapt. The heart is a muscle that prefers honesty, even if it hurts it. There is a picture and when I saw the picture as an adult the way the light worked in it made me think, oh, that is how afternoon used to be. It was not warmer. I had simply been. The slab reflected a kind of knowable that now resides in pipes and fiber and my job sometimes is to make my way between the knowns the big systems think they have and the knowables your foot can still find by stepping on them.

I crouched. At the edge of the slab by the crack there was a rusted end of a nail long as my pinky, pointing up. It would be the way to catch your work pants and tear them wide, and then you'd have to hold your thigh and swear and go back to the truck and get the duct tape. I took the hammer out of my pack and gave it two taps with the butt. The nail bent, fell where it had come from, lay down in its future. The smallest things we do sometimes are the only things that will be noticed by someone in fifty years when drought comes again. Some woman then will step and her boot will miss catching and she will go on and call it luck.

On my tablet, my points had made a shape that you could hand to someone and say, this is what was underneath, and they would say the words we always teach in school about pattern and network and system. My points were not a street of my childhood, though. They were a plot of a way through it. They were what we do with grief when we have a job that asks it of us. I named the last point HOME_EAST and chewed my cheek, then renamed it SYC_145_STA because one day some clerk will kill a file because she sees a word like home in it and thinks we have sentimentalized our work, and then someone else will show up and there will be nothing for her and she will step wrong and sink. The job must be done for someone you will never see.

The wind had lifted. A sound came up from across the basin that could have been a truck on gravel and was not. It was water at a distance. Not here. Up at the infeed, a notch had opened in the shade and the bright part of the creek was shining like the side of a minnow. Two years of missing rains and the county's allocations jacked around with and this is what we get: a shiver of sound telling me, in a week the bottom will not be this bottom and in a month this language will have to be installed in the ears of another who will come and say to a boy who wants to hold tile, not today. I stood to say goodbye, not to the slab or the creek or anything like that, but to the privilege of seeing it like this. Any work worth anything has this inside it—something like kneeling at a window, and then standing. If I say it to you like this it sounds as if I think we are moving through these places with more grace than we are. We aren't. We are making a rack for our errors and hanging them up, neat, so that we don't trip.

On my way back up I passed the First Baptist steps again. The snake skin had darkened with damp, a single line curved like a draped cord, its pattern of scales way the river consents to an imitation of itself. The pebbles at the bottom of the baptistery still had that lighter line. I took a picture and then did not, because everyone else thinks the picture is the thing you did, and I wanted the map to keep the place for me instead. Kids had added their shoe prints to my pole tip prints and the quick stamp of a dog. Someone had drawn a heart with a stick in the skin of silt, and inside it written two initials I did not know. The heart cut through the thin top dry. The heart would be gone with the first good gust. That is how hearts in this silt are. That is also how they are on the street in summer chalk. You bless them for their willingness to be the thing that does not need to be tough forever to be seen.

On the ramp up, the Parks man had added two more lengths of orange barrier. He recognized my hat and raised a hand. The news woman with the hair had traded her waders for sandals and was on the phone in a voice halfway too bright. A man in green pants with a Jensen & Sons patch on one knee put his heel against a barrier and leaned until it squeaked. He spat into the dry. He said, out loud to no one, "Huh," and for no reason that is explicable, that made me happy. It is the huh that saves us, the small grunt that is not condemnation, not praise, not fear. A huh makes a brief house around a thing, big enough to kneel in.

At the top, at the overlook, my base was still talking. The numbers were still right. On my way past the coin-op stump I put my palm on it and it was hot. The truck had been baking and then the first hiss of air conditioning was the best thing in the world, even in a world with a baptistery and steps and a crack in your porch. I wrote a few more notes in my file not because I needed them but because I distrusted the day and wanted it to not be able to take from me what small hards I had hammered into it. I added a description under Sycamore/Elm: pronation of curb, roots, feeling in calves; and under HOME_EAST a note that I let live just as a line—"stone-sill," nothing more—because that is how we keep a human thing in a file that one day a person three counties away will open when it is their day to be sent. You don't explain it. You mark it with a word that will sound workmanlike and ordinary to anyone else and will make those tendons behind your knee draw tight like a plucked cord when you are old and one day hear the river again where you were not expecting it.

On the dam road the crowd had thickened. A man had a paper coffee in a white heat sleeve and had the posture of someone whose heart beats too hard and he doesn't yet know why. Two women were arguing about whether that was the feed store or the bank. The deputy had his thumb under his belt. A teenage girl had taken off her shoes and had her feet up on the parapet and the soles were pure and not touched and she was letting the heat polish them. The hawk that had cried at nine had found a thermal and was making a loop and then another. The orange of the barriers thickened to something like a sunset around the edge of the asphalt.

When I got in the truck the radio spilled out the weather. Seven percent chance of isolated thundershowers after three. A long-term pattern we should call a pattern. The woman said it with the little chirp in her voice the station's brand must have required of her. My mother, who refuses to cross this dam, had told me last year on the phone she thinks a man can be broken by work that doesn't leave dirt on his shirt. "You come by and clean my gutters," she had said, confessing it as a hydrological anomaly. I had gone with a ladder and my old pants and when I was done I had leaned on the fence and looked at her lawn and said, the slope is wrong, mother. Water is going under the house. She had said, I know. I used to have a husband to talk to about that.

When I turned the key I watched my fuel needle swing like a metronome and then land past half. Ahead of me the road went the way it has always gone. The town lay out, denuded, rectangles and shards, a skull with its thin places exposed. Someone behind me honked for a reason I couldn't see. I put my hand out my window and made a soft little wave to say, I do not take it personally. I turned my head to fix the map in the place inside me where the map is not something I take to a boss. The past is not a place you walk away from. It stays and either hardens and hurts or, if you're lucky and careful, becomes something with walls that stay up even a little when the rains come. I tapped the edge of my tablet with my nail and the file shivered into its saving. On its face were all the words that make the work into something someone can use: names of nodes, codes for curb and stair and slab and ramp. In my mouth, unnamed, was the taste of that day my father squinted down the length of a level with one eye closed and made a line that didn't care what the water would do. Outside the window the hawk cut its circle into a smaller one, then let it be. We talk about elevation as if it is the only thing that matters. The last thing I did before I pulled away was to write, with my pencil on a strip of flagging that I tied to the base tripod because it seemed right that someone else should see it too when they came to pull the gear, the old street's name spelled the way my first teacher had insisted it must be and no one gets right—Beech, not Beach—and the letters stood loopy and pale in that hot wind and then laid themselves down when the draft off a Channel Nine truck hit them. I drove and left them to it. In the rearview, the lake gave back light that hurt and then didn't. The sound of the creek at its mouth sharpened and then fell under the pitch of the engine. Ahead, the road curved as if it would take me around again, around and then around, but of course it didn't; it simply took me up and out, like water does.
