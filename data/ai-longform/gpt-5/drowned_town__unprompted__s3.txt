The first morning the yellow of the centerline flashed through the skin of water, I parked by the chain-link gate the county had bolted across the old boat ramp and signed for the keys from the deputy in the white SUV. It was early, thin light waking the bulrushes into pale froth along the rocks. There was a smell like a cow pond and old pennies, and the faint sound of the dam’s spill gates, a far machine-sigh that has been background here for as long as I can remember. I signed my name twice on a grease-smudged clipboard, tucked the keys into my pocket with the field tablet, and shouldered the orange tripod with one hand and the survey pole in the other. The deputy, a stick of a man with a soft lilt to his vowels, said to watch for silt pockets—knee-deep, he warned, and laughed like he couldn’t imagine how two feet of anything could take you down.

They had told me in the office that the lake was at sixty-year low, lowest since they first closed the gates and backed the river up into the hollow where our town lay. Reservoir people say “lake” because it sounds gentle, blue and ringed in pitched-roof vacation houses, boats like toys and girls on inner tubes with sunburnt noses and a cooler full of light beer in the stern. I am a surveyor and I say basin. I say pool elevation and drawdown and bathymetry. My job was to take a lattice of points across the newly exposed ground—foundation to roadbed to culvert mouth—and stitch it into a surface our engineers could drape old plats and aerials on, to draw up hazard maps for the weekend walkers who would inevitably pour in to look at the rib cage of a town left to rot and darken under water and then, like a trick of mirage, be revealed again.

I am not religious, and yet when I stepped off the black, kayak-scratched concrete of the ramp and onto the slick gray silt, I felt something that could have been a catechism, the naming of things in their right order after long silence. I said to myself—Cherry Avenue, Cortland, Bartlett, because our streets had once been named for fruit, for what grew in the valley before hydro and tourism, named in that slow, hopeful way of towns that imagine themselves permanent. I said—foundation at 394.125, give or take, elevation not the address number my mother wrote on letters, but a ghost of it, a number you could tie to sea that had never come here.

Between the reeds and the dull sheen of water, the old asphalt shone through. You could see the paint lines if you squinted—the centerline a jaundiced curve, the shoulder rough with the cinder we used to spread in the winters when it iced. An island of concrete rose like a whale’s back where the post office had been, four steps fanning out into mud that held prints of raccoon and deer, and the round heads of rivets glinted where the old stop sign had been sheared off years ago underwater. Folks had been out already, you could tell; footprints, the shredded graze of sneakers and heavy boots, a half-sunken lawn chair planted like a flag at the foundation’s corner, as if somebody had claimed it the way you would a towelled square of beach.

I set the tripod on higher ground—a little shoulder of gravel that would hold—and set the base, leveled the bubble, turned on the receiver to catch satellites through the thin cover of dawn haze. The logger made its familiar series of beeps, the count of seconds until it found enough birds to her first fix. Six satellites, seven, eight. I breathed out and watched my breath not smoke in the warm morning, and I thought of the first time my father put his thumb on the top of my head to measure how much I’d grown—against the pantry door, pencil blunt, his hand steady. I had been nine that day and angry in a way that went to bone because my mother had told the man in the tie that we would go, that there wasn’t a choice, because the checks had been cut and the last notices sent and the bulldozers were coming. He had measured me anyway because we had done it on my birthday every year since I could remember, black marks going up like rungs on a ladder, H. 7, H. 8, and that year there had not been a place left to make a mark because the door had come free of its hinges and been loaded with the last of the kitchen things into the borrowed truck. My father held the door while I stood straight and then leaned it against the porch and made the mark there, along the frame where the light used to creep in at night after traffic on the state route went thin, the only time our house was quiet.

The base station settled into its work. I took the pole and the rover and began to walk. Each step let out a slow kiss of silt releasing the sole of my boots. A fish bone like a comb lay in a cup of water where a divot had caught the last night’s whisper of wind. It would have been a bass or a carp most likely; kids couldn’t keep their hands from tossing foreign species into the reservoir, and even if they did, the lake would have made its own decisions, a new ecology thrown over an old valley. There were the dark stumps of the orchard trees, sawed off half a man’s height after the federal surveyors had come through with ribbon and stakes and rights-of-way, the stumps that years of water had not softened. Our trees had names we called them by—Bing, Granny, Bartlett—and it made sense to us as kids to run and hide behind an always named thing. I put the pole on the first stump I reached, top flat and scalloped, and took a reading. The tablet bloomed a number—X, Y, Z—and I named it in the file: “Stump 1.” Later it would be point 5037 or 7084 depending on where I started the count, a tick in the endless ledger the world is to government. For this morning, it was the place my brother had tied a rope to and learned to climb, his shins grubby with sap stain, because the rope seemed to reach the whole sky and if he could not go over the reservoir like the geese or the sun, he could at least swing crazy over the dirt.

I had not been back really since we left, except as an adolescent to watch boys try to impress one another skimming rocks, except as a high schooler to drink from a thermos with a friend and name the fishermen by their jackets, except as the young woman who thought she could leave this county and never touch its clay again. You can’t truly leave a place when your parents are there, and when your mother insists on reminding you that your father had wanted to be buried high, not near water, because what had been forced on us should not be forced again in death. She had said it as if the undertaker might get bright ideas. When I graduated with the certificate that said I could take bearings and hold a line against the slope of the ground, I found work two counties over until the commute wore the edges off my patience and I took the job at our own county’s engineering office. For years I measured the stainless curve of guardrails and the bellies of culverts and the edges of asphalt that needed another two inches of binder. I kept my head down when a map of the reservoir crossed the desk because I did not want to be the woman in the office with a particular history. And then this year the water went away faster than they could release it and crops burned in fields to the south and electric people phoned water people and money people, and engineers phoned surveyors, and one of them said my name like a solution, and I let it be.

There were others already down below me on the flats, although the gate at the ramp said closed. The county has always tolerated trespass when it pleases to; fishermen trample and teenagers kiss and nothing breaks in particular. Today’s walkers were dressed in shorts and T-shirts despite the hour, one old man with a hat like a crumpled envelope, two women with big clear sunglasses, and a boy carrying a stick like a spear. They kept a distance when they saw me with the orange tripod and the tablet; the authoritarian color scheme keeps a person safe in all the ways that matter and none that do.

I skated across a pocket of goose shit and the texture of the ground changed underfoot, went from the heavy heave of lakebed to the rasp of something laid down hard and level in the old days. The asphalt made a suck noise as it yielded the water it had wicked up in sixty years. You could draw lines across it with your heel and uncover a black that had not seen day since there were cars that ran through our town on their way to somewhere the state thought mattered. On either side of the road, the slope fell away—drainage ditches, a gradient someone had so carefully put in with a grader blade coming down a slope from the main street to the lower. I could see Monmouth Street ahead, or where it would be as soon as I crossed Cherry. They change the names when they drown a place; call it so many acre-feet at max pool, call it Survey Tract 18, Parcel 7A. But pieces keep their old words like burrs that won’t comb out. Names live in mouths until the day the person with the mouth does not.

I stood where I thought the crosswalk had been. That was a younger me’s safety under the same rules of a different order: between the thick lines you were magic and a truck would not hit you. Now there were faint ghosts of lines, like chalk after rain. I took a point at the corner of Monmouth and Cherry because corners anchor not just the geometry of a map but the geometry of how we walk, and a person who has known enough loss will grab at anchors that can be had. The tablet showed me the cloud of points I’d already recorded. I made a mental picture—my whole job is making mental pictures and then disciplining them with numbers—of what this would look like draped with the old plat we’d scanned. The county clerk keeps the records in a room that smells like headstones, cool and a little sweet, oil in oaken drawers. The mayor’s block, Map Book 3, Page 12. When I set the crosshair over the basemap and the points matched up enough that you could say with good conscience we had tied the world together, I would send it on to our GIS woman and she would turn it into a tidy layer on a website with pan-zoom functions for the curious, and a father would pull it up on a phone and point to his child and say, “That there was the bank.” If this were a different kind of place, there would be an interpretive sign with a good artist’s attempt at the way it had been before our roofs went dark under the daily churn of waves thrown up by the slow wind.

The foundation where the bank had been was thick and square, poured in a year when sand and gravel were brought by train. We had not had much of a bank—savings and loans, a place for mortgages small enough you knew the man’s wrist hair who held your deed down with his palm while he tapped totals with his other hand. The concrete had spalled from decades of freeze and the sharp word grind of water. You could see exposed aggregate, river-rounded stones bigger than my hand sitting in the face of the wall like eggs set in mud. The mud inside the bank’s outline had settled, level as a poured floor, and somebody had already scraped their names into it with a stick. Chickens had left their branchy prints. I took a point at each corner, then a point in the center, then at the base of the three steps. When the lake returns to herself, she will cover this again in a skin of muck. I was surprised at the tenderness I felt when I thought of that—the world filing this thing away the way a person boxes up what she does not yet have the heart to discard.

Past the bank there was the low rectangle of the barber shop. My father had been a stubborn man about many things, not least the cut of his hair. He shaved the back of his neck with a straight razor on Sunday mornings and then let his hair grow to his collar until my mother told him he looked like a horse trader and then he would take me to sit under the center fan while Mr. Gagliardi clucked him to the chair and set the white cloth across his chest. Mr. Gagliardi cut all the men’s hair the way you cut peonies from a bush—calculated, cheerful violence and then, suddenly, something lovely had been left behind. In the shop a century of swept hair had probably made a mat under the plank floor that you could spring on, and for a long time underwater it must have been a kind of reef, hair and dropped pennies, rusted scissors, a paper sign curled into a C reading Bus Sched. When they notified the business owners they would flood, our barber had wrapped each of his mirrors in newspaper and eyelet tape and carried them to his cousin’s place over the ridge. He returned one mirror to himself before he died, everything behind his face doubled, the room his widow moved into gleaming a little more for it. What I am saying is that not everything was lost, but enough of it was.

Cherry bent toward Bartlett, and the hill that had been the schoolyard rose gently, a holdout island in this pooled bowl. Geese had claimed the crest. Their calls came across the exposed streets and hit my ribs with the memory of morning assembly in the gravel, the teacher who produced a pitch pipe from her pocket, and the way all of us made the Pledge like we were promising ourselves anything could hold if we said it every morning. The school had been moved plank by plank to a church camp up in the pines. Kids cut their feet on slivered boards that first summer, and the girls took to wearing socks even in the heat. The basement of the school was what had been left because why move a basement, why think of anything lower, when the water would do a good job erasing what a foundation had meant? I put the pole at the base of the old steps, a squat run of concrete leading into nothing, and hit the measure button. The receiver chirped like a bird; I love that sound, the world telling me it can be known.

Downriver a pulse went through the anchored tin buoys. A small release. The reservoir is not a static thing. It is a held-in, let-go. It breathes ledgers and kilowatts and summer afternoons on a rented pontoon boat. If you stand close enough to its skin for long enough, your own breath tries to match it. I caught myself sucking air shallow and shook it off. The trick, my mentor told me when I started, is to let land tell you where it’s good to stand. Mud is a language. Water is a liar. Rock can be cruel, but it will tell you what it intends if you spend long enough in its company feeding mosquitoes with your shins.

The point where our house had been was not far, a three-block walk, because my mother had loved to be near the post office and the grocer and to this end had chosen not to move higher up the hill where a good view would have given her a different kind of proof of ownership. She said it made her feel like she would miss less if she kept herself where someone would tell her the news. The squat rectangle of the grocer’s steps was there still, the slot cut into the step where a metal strip had been so you would not slip in ice. Inside that rectangle a hundred bottles must have broken in a slow way when the water came. Sometimes kids diving near the boat ramp came up yelping with brown glass shards in their fingers, and mothers said where did you think you were swimming, in a pool? The grocer’s foundation held water still in a belly that smelled of lilies and rot. A frog skittered from one shadow to another. There was a signboard still staked in the ground at the corner—pelican-bright plastic from the nineties or the early aughts warning about zebra mussels and the fine for introduction. It had been driven in where a hardware house had once held nails in coffee cans and a bin full of gloveless hands.

The lot line between our house and the Hulls’ had been, in the last years, a fast debate—fence squabble, my father said, and laughed because he was clever at cutting men down when they grew silly. The surveyor hired by the county put his stakes in and my father and Mr. Hull had measured with a ragged spool of tape themselves and then accused each other of being kind of stupid about it in a way that meant they had their peace. The stakes were long gone, but the lip of the sidewalk survived, and the broken teeth of a retaining wall that had held a maple in place like a cuff. That maple had been my first tree. I knew the knots of its bark by heart. I’d learned to swear into its creases, to kiss there as the bark pricked the backs of my knees. There was a black stump where it had been, softened to a sponge over decades of rot. I tapped the pole against it, as we had tapped its trunk when we ran past it, always striking wood, always asking something to keep us from harm. I was not a superstitious child, but I had been a kissed one, and that had been enough.

I had imagined that reaching what had been my doorstep would undo me. You build the day in your mind until it is a cathedral and then it turns out to be a stoop. Our front porch was a rectangle of poured concrete the color of putty. It had suffered worse underwater than some others because the top was friable, the way it goes when water has enough time. I bent and pressed my thumb against the edge and it gave like cheese. The bolts where once had been a railing stuck up and I wondered if someone had cut those down for safety before the flood, if my father had been that kind of careful. He had been and he had not. He hammered smoke alarms into the ceiling after he watched a television program and then he left the kerosene can by the back door in summer like he wanted a fight with fire. He had believed, I think, that you could say no to a thing just because you had always said yes to yourself. The day the men in neat shirts came to say again what was happening, he had leaned against this very porch post and said a line from a Western I did not know, something about pried from his cold hands, and everything had spooled out from there into the days we packed and the days he drank and the days my mother took the car herself to the courthouse and signed.

I put the pole on the doorstep and took the point. A number is a number until you give it a name. I named it Home Step because it was easier to breathe that way. I walked the outline of the foundation, which had been low because a basement here meant water in spring. My parents had put their energy into the edges of things, into what you could see. My mother had planted four lilac bushes between our kitchen window and the neighbor’s because she wanted the smell in May more than she wanted a patch of lawn to lie on. The bushes, of course, had not survived a lake, but there were four black stumps in a line where their hearts had been, so perfect I had to lift my hand to my nose and trust I didn’t smell them because the body is a bastard that way. The steps down from the kitchen run were there. The earth had swallowed the last tread and the second was half-caught in mud. I imagined my mother stepping out here with a peeler in her hand, tapping cigarette ash onto the top step, and me underfoot, the way children haunt their own mothers, hands out when she would not give me what I thought I deserved—an explanation, another slice of apple. I squatted and reached into the mud and came up with a smooth, white disk. For a second my heart slapped in my chest. It was a jar lid, the kind with a rubber gasket, broken free of its ring.

I admit that I looked up, a child again, sure that if anyone saw me find something, they would remember the rules before I did—no removal, the signs had said when they locked the boats out. Leave artifacts where you find them so that others can understand. The county had given me a bright vest and a list of guidelines it had copied from a neighboring state that had had its own bath of drought and revelation. I turned the jar lid over in my hand. It did not carry a story as small and singular as I wanted. Sometimes the world is too large to get your arms around, and sometimes it is the exact size of the thing you wish to come out of the mud.

“Looks like it belonged there,” a voice said, and I startled, half-rose, and turned. A man stood at the edge of the black yard where once our lawn had been crabgrass and stories. He carried a cane but did not put weight on it; it was a prop or a measuring rod. His hat made shade down to his nose. He was old in the way that makes a person much younger all at once, because all the child that had ever lived there in them sits forward in their face. “I knew the people who lived here,” he said, and pulled the brim of his hat up as if the introduction required a reveal. “You’re Lissa May’s girl.”

No one calls my mother that anymore, except people who were thirteen when she was. Startled, pleased, hurt all at once, I nodded, and the nod made me feel that I was on a string.

“I am,” I said. I had the pole in one hand and the jar lid in the other and both hands seemed foolish. He stepped closer, careful, and then he tilted his chin out over the porch as if the house had returned and he were admiring the tuckpointing. “I was Eddie Hull,” he said. “Junior,” he added because that mattered here, where we knew some men by only the half of their name they gave each other in bars and at church breakfasts. “My father was—” He gestured toward the black rectangle next door.

“We argued about fence,” I said, and smiled. He smiled too, pleased to be remembered for the precise shape of a human silliness. “We did,” he said. “Your father would come home and your mother would have cooked, and you children would sit on the back steps, and I loved your mama’s voice when she called you in. It made me feel like I might be somewhere good one day if I married right.”

“Did you?” I asked. He laughed, a rusty sound, and leaned on his cane, and I had the ache of a church picnic at the back of my tongue. “Good enough,” he said. “She’s gone now. She died four years back. Heart. I never did find a voice like your mama’s, but we made do.”

It is greedy to want from a stranger your own precise missing thing. I looked down into the little round pool inside the foundation, where the back room had been, where the patch of tile had been laid crooked because my father had kept his left eye closed when he worked around mortar smoke. Perhaps the tile was there under the film of brown water, perhaps lilies had struck roots between it, perhaps their disintegration had filled the pattern places and the world was an unsolvable mosaic. “Are you out for the look?” I asked him, waving my hand at the general sightseer’s joy of what had been brought up for examination.

“Part of it,” he said. “Part of it I had to see if the lie was true.”

“Which?”

He lifted the cane and drew a line in the air between my house’s edge and the Hulls’. “They say you can hear the bell some mornings.” He peered toward the schoolyard’s ridge, geese wheels bright as pennies. “They say when the wind goes toward the dam after rain you can hear the bell from the old church. But the church’s bell never got drowned. They took it to the camp. It’s just a story folks tell to comfort themselves that what’s down there maybe doesn’t mind.”

“I have heard that story,” I admitted. I had rolled the jar lid in my palm so the rubber left a faint clean streak where the mud scraped off. The church had been moved up to the ridge near the pines too, because a donor had offered his hay wagons and the whole thing had been rocking down the road with all our mothers walking behind, like a wedding procession and a funeral all at once. I had not followed. I had been in my closet packing my paper dolls into a shoebox into a larger box into the borrowed truck. A person cannot walk behind everything she will lose. “But I have heard you can also hear the train whistle some mornings,” I added, and smiled.

“Now that,” he said, “is true.” He was the kind of old that tells a young woman with authority that something impossible is true and she will take it. “You think you cannot miss a thing until you stand where it will come from and look toward where it is going.”

We stood for a moment, hands deep in our own afternoons. He shifted and his cane pierced the gray the way the pole had. It came up wet. “You’re working,” he said, suddenly abrupt, and half-apologetic, and then something softened in his mouth, the part that had loved my mother’s voice, perhaps, or the way he had been a boy with rope burns on his palms. “I remember you,” he said. “You run with your knees high, like you’re stepping over cats.”

I laughed properly then and let the sound go to the lake. “Knees high,” I repeated. “Because my father always left things on the floor.”

“He did,” Eddie said. “I’ll leave you to your numbers, miss. I’ll look on toward the church.” He lifted his cane in a not-quite-salute and started up the slope. His legs bent and straightened like the hoes of a good farmer. The sun pushed its elbows out from behind a bank of thin cloud and made the whole arc of the reservoir flash, a sheet of metal beating.

I moved through my grid, taking points: corner of my bedroom, where the dresser had stood, the edge of the closet that had held my paper dolls. I thought my hand would shake when I measured my mother’s room but it did not. I put the pole on the spot where their bed had been and it was just a crumbly place with a water bug doing his slow, polled centuries. The part of me that is all math—the girl who learned to pace a hundred feet in sixty-seven steps and then sixty-six in high school and wrote it in a field book like a lover’s tally—was calmer here among the measured ghosts. The part that cannot is not the part anyone sees when I point the gun and call out rod height.

By midmorning the heat began, honest and persuasive. The basin is a bowl that traps summer; even when there is water to blunt it, you can feel the sun as a slow weight. I took out my canteen and drank enough to not need to again for a while, and ate the apple I had packed, and the sugar under the sun made me feel unreasonably good. The first time we had come to see the lake full, my father had lifted me onto the hood of the car and given me a Word about the cost of things and the way the world would look you straight in the eye while it did what it had planned to do with you. He had graduated from mottos to axioms by then. I had believed him not at all and entirely. He had placed his hand on mine and pointed toward the center where once the water tower had been, now a peculiar swirl when the wind went certain ways. “That there,” he said, “is where they swallowed your room.” He had said it like he was offering proof he had lost something too. I had made my mouth go straight because tears felt politic, and a hot, remarkable rage had run into me like I had been injected with a sky.

The apple made me think of the orchard again. Stumps like moored boats across the flats. I went over and climbed one because I had forgotten how the sap fell under my palms and I wanted to remember. It was springy and soft and smelled of a damp lumberyard. I looked out from that little hill the way you look when you know an expanse is empty and want to fill it for a second. A turkey vulture carved a lazy line across the far arm of where the town had been, using the warming air to lift. If I had been inclined to say it, I might have said I wanted a religion that believed the things we had lost might lift like that too. My mother believed in duty and a nice china plate. My father believed in naming the wrong thing the enemy and then going to bed satisfied if he had shouted at it enough hours.

Back at the truck I clicked from one file to another to check the number and arrangement of my points. I had zipped my mouth closed with a talc line of silt, and when I licked my lips, the taste was like coins again. Folks were lining the dam’s parapet with their elbows, looking over to where certain columns of wood showed—those would be the girders of somebody’s back porch. The rumor always starts: there’s a car at the bottom of the reservoir with a body tied in the trunk; there’s a safe that someone tossed in a hurry when the sheriff turned down Bartlett; there’s a school bus full of the drowned because the world did something unfair. I had known all those stories as a kid and they were the kind that made you a little higher in bed at night. The deputy made a slow pass down the service road in his white SUV to remind folks that this was not the fair, and when he saw me, he held up his coffee cup in a toast. He was the kind of man who would be at my mother’s wake because he had known my brother from basketball. He understood that some respects you pay and some you cash even if the teller window is there long after the bank has shut.

By noon, other people with more formal vests had started to arrive—pale men with drones and women with clipboards and an archaeologist from the university who wore a floppy hat that said she had spent stages of her life in deserts she had forgiven. She asked if the county had a plan for signage, for ropes, for brochures that would say do not fall into the idea that this is your place to take pieces of. I said that we had cones and bad judgement and that I would draft a handout with the line about leaving things in place salvoed on the front. She laughed like a shovel into gravel. “You from here,” she said, not quite a question, because something local had tangled my vowels again. When I nodded, she said, very gently, “I’m sorry for your loss,” with an odd emphasis, as if the loss were still happening in the present and was not a thing that had happened and been happened over. “Thank you,” I said, because the way you carry something politely is still a way you carry it, no matter if the straps cut into your hands.

We walked together toward the church steps because everybody had seen them from the parapet and wanted pictures, and because geese had made a ridiculous monarchy of themselves up there. The steps were raw edged, concrete with pebbled stones read into it by the first man who had slammed a wheelbarrow full of mix down and scraped sweat off his face with his wrist. The imprint of a dog’s paw showed in one corner. Eddie Hull was there already, his cane leaned against the middle step, his hat off, his scalp a map of old sun. I did not say his name. He was the kind of older man you can ruin an afternoon for by calling him anything younger than Mister. The archaeologist pulled out her camera and took photographs with a slowness that softened the light she seized. When the geese raised a racket, she laughed. “Though I do not want to have my knees pecked by a queen in a feathered gown,” she said, and sidled.

Eddie’s eyes went from me to the archaeologist and back to me. “She’s going to take your things and catalog them,” he said, like a ritual tease. The archaeologist smiled, unbothered. “We like our things better when we know their names,” she said. The man made a noncommittal noise. He had names he did not want catalogued. He put his hand out and laid it on the top step. It was a tender gesture on a thing not tender, and I felt something flutter in my chest, a memory not of the church itself—my mother had believed more in potato salad than in Scripture—but of weddings and the rehearsal dinners that always broke out in tears, the long space between “I do” and the literal carrying off. I had stood once on that step in a pink dress with a bow I had been told not to pick at and watched two adults who believed in permanent things with their mouths say so in a way that made the whole town soften.

“I hope they keep it up when the water comes,” Eddie said, and we all looked at him as if he had suggested the obvious. “Just this,” he said, palm flat on stone. “Just so you could come down here in a boat and reach your hand down and find it. I think that would make people nicer somehow.”

“Nice and equilibrium are not words in good conversation right now,” the archaeologist said, the hat shading her smile, and then, “But I like your wish.” I liked it too. I do not know if that’s because I held a good slice of altars in my head just then, or because the thought of permanent handles on the world steadied me.

After the steps, I left the archaeologist and Eddie, and I went to the old culvert under the road. As kids we had learned to squat and call frogs out with our hands like trumpets. The culvert’s mouth was a concrete circle, its lip ragged with impact and frost, and inside the dark showed not the light of the other side—it was filled for some twenty feet with silt, turned into an inland, buried thing. A dragonfly throbbed past, impossible gem that it was. The stream that had run through our town before it was taught to lake had had a good curve here, and you could catch shiners in the damp and let them flash from your palms if you crouched still enough long enough to forget your ankles were biting. I took two points on the culvert rim and named one “Culv N” and the other “Culv S.” It is how we fix what we can; give it a label, do not ask it for anything. I thought absurdly of putting the pole inside the culvert and I had the immediate, feral fear of things that would live there—snakes, a sleeping animal with its ribs making the small skin breath. I had never been much afraid as a child here. I had fear enough when we left.

I made another lap of my own foundation in the afternoon. The sun had softened what had been crisp—edges of squares and the shadows they had made. I had points, plenty, more than we would need to anchor the old map, but I kept taking them like a person picking up a last set of plates from a table no longer in her house. It occurred to me then that my file would be a thing someone could request one day—from the county, or from me if this is the sort of place and life that comes to a private asking—and that I would have to hand it over and it would tell the exact outline of my mother’s bed. It would be a thing that would show the distance from the step to the corner where we kept a dented soup pot and a wooden spoon. I did not mind, which surprised me. I have never been stingy with my lost things, perhaps because their abundance makes me believe that the act of taking by another can never make them empty.

On the way back to the ramp in the late day, I stopped at the place where the corner of the old diner had stood. Its sign had said Pie and then in smaller letters Our Famous Coffee, and I had grown up believing that famous in this instance meant anyone in this town could name it. The diner’s edge was a long, low slab stained with coffee in the daylight—it must have been coffee because it had bled in brown arcs and points, and because the place had always smelled not like breakfast or lunch but of something in between, the way good places do. I bent to lay my palm on the slab the way Eddie had once the church step, and a little flurry of insects rose up, disturbed, their bodies whirring like light machine noise. I apologized to them in my head the way you do when you step on an ant hill as a penitent child with no gods. I straightened and saw, half-sunk in the mud near the slab, a glass marble, blue as July, the kind that makes your thumb look smaller when you press down on the glass. It was so perfect I almost laughed. I had lost my marbles the week before we packed. I mean this literally. They had been in a tin lunchbox and I had taken them out to choose which to take because my father had told me I could bring one box of toys and no more. Things had a weight limit as well as a life limit in those days, and if you cannot hear a parallel there between toys and the people my mother would leave, then you have not met anyone human. The lunchbox had tipped and the marbles had shot across the kitchen like planets minding their own orbits. One had rolled out the back door and down the two treads and into the yard and then into a frantic thing of a mouse’s hole or a crack near the retaining wall; I had searched on my knees until my palms went green and then I had cried because it felt like this one lost meant all losses were fated. It had been blue like this one was blue. One day I will learn not to expect the old gods to play tricks for me that neat; until then I will allow myself these marvels.

I looked up at the ramp. The deputy’s car was not there; he had gone to his cousin’s to check on the cows. Up on the parapet, a couple pushed a stroller with a child with sunglasses on. A man in a green golf shirt shaded his eyes with his hand to see past the glare out to where I was. The archaeologist had a folding table now, with a cloth on it and a stack of handouts that asked people to look and not touch and to plan their steps. Eddie was seated in a lawn chair someone had thought to bring him, his cane threaded through the arms so it would not fall. The world had folded itself into a set of small kindnesses almost without me noticing.

I bent and levered the marble free with the tip of my trowel. I should tell you I carry a trowel in my kit; it is not issued by the county, but it comes in more useful than the wrench. The marble came up with a sound like a kiss peeling away from somebody’s cheek. I rolled it in my palm and it came clean with none of the stubbornness the jar lid had. It was the most ordinary miracle I have known in years. I held it up to the late light and the blue swam in it. I set it down on the concrete slab of the diner and then picked it up again. My job, the signs, the archaeologist’s patience, the county clerk’s cool drawer of map books—all of them said leave. My hand and my chest said take. I balanced the marble on my palm and it lay there like the day itself decided to stay still a second in the pond of my hand.

I set it carefully in the corner where the slab met the lip of what had been the diner’s porch and turned to walk away. I took four steps and turned back. My mother had taught me that you can risk nothing by leaving things in place but the moment you need to point to it and say, “There.” The marble glinted. It would be under water again within months because storms would come, because there would be an early snow up high and then a long rain, because we are not immune to cycles even when we are in a trough, because I believe in return the way a fish believes in muscle. I looked at the blue spot spot of it and I had the strange defiance of a child: I picked it up and slid it in my pocket.

On the walk back to the ramp, a boy of about nine—the age I had been when we left—stepped from behind the bank’s foundation with a fork in his hand like a prize. He had mud on his calves and a clean white T-shirt gone brown in great arcs. Behind him, his mother called with that particular worried cheer that means both I will let you live and I will tell you to be more careful because you cannot; she had a baby on her hip who wore a hat with ties under his chin and was chewing on one. The boy saw me and the pole and the tablet and scuffed his foot. “We’re not supposed to take it,” he said. There was a skitter of pride in his voice. He had understood the rule and held it in his hand long enough to decide on it.

“It helps if you can tell a story about where you found it,” I said, conscious instantly that this is not the speech of a surveyor but some other occupation entirely. He looked at the fork in his hand. “It was by a step,” he said. “I could tell because I saw the dirt go down like this.” He lifted his hand and made it a plane, then dropped the plane two inches; when his hand reached level, the tip of the fork shivered. He had the gestures of a person who will grow to build things. “Then I seen this.” He held it up. The fork had four tines, one bent, the pattern on the handle a little faint cascade of leaves.

“If you take it,” I said, “you’ll remember where you found it. If you leave it, someone else might make up where it was from. What do you think about those two facts?” His brow furrowed, and my own voice hummed in my ears because I had not planned this little trial and it seemed like the world had tossed me a coin and told me to call it in the air.

He rubbed mud thumb to palm, a slurry. “If I take it,” he repeated. He put the fork back in the mud next to the step. “I have a mother,” he said. I laughed. “That’s a good answer,” I told him, and he grinned, flattered by his own humor, the smallest of architects, and ran for the top of the steps, two-footed like a rabbit.

At the truck, I took off my boots and tapped mud out of them. Gray slid away onto the pavement in slow tongues. I wiped the soles with rags I kept in a bucket, because if you drive with silt on your pedals you risk leaving a little of what you care about on the county roads forever. The deputy swung in again and rolled down his window. “Get your data?” he asked brightly. I held up the tablet and waggled it. “All the numbers,” I said. “Plus two.” He grinned, not asking.

On the ridge, the light angled into the reservoir and made the silt gleam like silk. We are a place of brown and green and sudden yellow when the cottonwoods give up. We take a long time to decide to call a bad year bad and a good one good. We are not the coast; our losses do not make the world’s evening news. We are the county line you cross and say did you hear they had to close Monmouth Street for whatever this week; we are the kind of no that says come back another time if you need me. From the dam, I looked down at my town and thought that a map is a picture of things in a way scaled to usefulness, and that what we had made today could be read by a child and that was something. The drywall of the day thinned. Across the way, the pines took on that impossible black they do at dusk, where you think if you walked into them you would stain. The light on the rocks by the dam took on an abrupt sincerity. A man shouted to his wife about dinner and I heard the words pie and chicken and the part of my brain that is so muscle memory I do not question it set a plate and folded a paper towel on top.

On the tailgate, I brought up the file with my points and scrolled through them. Second column numbers, third column numbers, fourth column numbers. There was a point called Home Step. There was one called Culv N. There was one called Church Step Dog. A person who did not know how files are often named the way you write on your hand in the dark would think perhaps that they were whimsical names. They are not. They are the hooks you hang your life on so you can get home in the dark, so you can find your way back down a street that is now a striated rind of muck. I sent the file to the server from my little sheriff’s office of a cab and the blue arc went complete. I pressed my fingers to my pocket once, the useless superstition of checking for the thing you just put there. The marble knocked under my knuckle against the denim.

I sat for a long minute with my hands on my thighs and watched the geese bicker at the crest of the schoolyard and the slow slip of someone’s canoe out toward the island where the tree stumps made a crooked crown. In the water you could see the soft shouting of the day, the little wind-scallops, the turn over on itself of the lake where it reckoned with sun. If I stayed sitting long enough, the light would go and I would be one more silly person calling her mother late because the day ran longer than she thought and driving in the dark along the hairpins with her eyes still full of what she’d seen. I made myself stand up. I made myself start the engine. I put the truck in gear and steered it toward the gate, and when I reached it, I stopped and stepped out and closed the chain and ran the padlock through and clicked it twice in a way that made sure. I put the keys in my pocket next to the marble. They were heavy together and for a second the weight felt the exact same as the weight of childhood, which is to say a necklace you did not know you were wearing until a hand reached to lift it and you were suddenly bare and wild with the feeling of the air. I ducked back into the truck, turned on the headlights because details are the only practical piety I know, and pulled away, the tires hissing on dried silt, ash leaves skating down across the hood in the small wind that had risen as the evening cooled the basin and the surface of the lake, what was left of it, lifted like the back of a sleeping thing that had not forgotten how to breathe.
