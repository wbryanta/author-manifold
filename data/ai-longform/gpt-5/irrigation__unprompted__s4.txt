By the time we crossed the ditch and took the rutted track toward the east block, the sun had already pressed the dew off the grass and turned every shadow into hard-edged geometry. Dust lifted off our boots and came up to my knees. The trees stood in their ranks, the outer leaves tremoring with the light breeze, slender sickle-shapes of shadow wobbling on the sand. The scent of hot green hovered over everything, even this early, the kind of cooked chlorophyll smell that only ever seemed to exist in June, when the almonds were thumb-sized and furred, and the orchard breathed like a sleeping animal.

Noah swung the orange five-gallon bucket from his left hand, the one with the cracked thumb he’d smashed in a fence gate twenty years ago. He had a shovel over his shoulder and the spool of Teflon tape wedged in his pocket so it flashed white when his shirt lifted. I had the PVC saw and the coupling fittings rattling together in my bucket like dull bones. The purple primer and blue glue nested together, the cans sticky and furred with grit, their brushes matted like neglected hair. I tried to walk in the truck’s tire spoor and kept stepping out of it, the earth giving a little, the top powder hot and the compacted beneath damp, making my sock heel feel clammy through a hole in my boot’s welt.

“You sure it’s this section?” Noah asked, squinting at the line of trees. He was breathing lightly, the way he did when he’d already been awake for hours, when he’d honed himself down with coffee and a run and a shower and was more or less untouchable inside his skin. He had been staying in the old house a week and already had a routine carved out of the mornings that did not involve the birds holding court on the power line or the pump’s gizzard grind coming up the draw.

“What do you think?” I said. “You see where the ground’s gone dark?”

He stopped and leaned over his knees and found a gopher mound with his boot, spaded wet where the water had carried it, and said, “Okay.”

It was a hundred feet more to the leak. It had soaked a rough circle the size of a truck bed, an afternoon’s worth what with the day’s heat and the thirsty ground, but you could still see where it had bubbled up in a little geyser through the sand the night before, spraying a cinnamon fan outwards. The PVC ran here at twenty inches, the lateral feeding the row with six-dollar micro-sprinklers that should have been knocking a halo of water into the root zone but weren’t, not now that the pressure had gone south. The last good rain had been March, and even then the orchard took less than it would once have done, more of it running russet-stained out of the end drain than going down, because the soil pan had compacted with years of our father’s insistence on keeping the same tractor path.

“Smells like a busted aquarium,” Noah said. He had his hand on the wet dirt and then wiped it on his shorts absentmindedly. He had never worn the right clothes when he came back out here. He was all calf muscle and college T-shirt and a little gold cross glinting when his collar went crooked. It had been that way when we were kids, too. I had the ratty jeans, the belt with the imperfectly punched hole, the hat that had been Dad’s before he sweat-salted the brim, gave it to me, bought himself another and pretended he’d always had two. Noah had socks with stripes and white shoes that stayed white through feats of will or luck.

“I’ll dig,” I said. “You check the first riser to see if anything’s blocking.”

He shrugged and pivoted on his heel and walked to the riser upstream, fingers in his hair. His hair had the first threads of gray in it now, or so I’d thought last night when he had leaned down to take his mug out of the dishwasher and the kitchen light had limned them like webs. He had his mother’s hair, lighter. Mine had gone coarse on me, and the heat only made it feel like I had a hood on.

I took the shovel from him and sunk it where the soil went from dry to damp. When it hit PVC three spits down I felt the shock all the way up my forearm and let the shovel lean, and then went in with my hands because the shovel would hack the boss of the line off if I wasn’t careful. The wet sand slid like dough between my fingers, a good sign. The ground had been that way in our best years, heavy and holding, so that you could cut a square for a valve and the walls would stand like cake even if you pushed at them. I cleared the line, the poured-white curve of it like a bleached bone, and found the crack by the shimmer of a little jet there, not much more than a grasshopper’s spurt, all it took to kill pressure across six acres.

“My guess?” I called to Noah, who had his hand on the riser. “Gopher chew, maybe. Or the tractor nicked it.”

“Those couplers are cheap,” he said. He unscrewed the cap, squinted, and put it in his back pocket like he was going to keep it.

“And you know better because—”

“I don’t know better. I just know Dad always bought the cheap ones.”

I sat back on my heels and blew hair out of my face and did not answer. The little jet continued to tick-tick like a lawn sprinkler’s first hesitant tries. Beyond us the orchard carried on with its tiny miracles. I had to be careful, always. You could feel the whole place as one system if you let yourself—branch tips with their new leaves desiring their measured ration, light sifting down in particular densities according to where limbs had been cut back, the flour of dust inside my mouth keen for water too. The years when you could afford to think of it as one thing had been better. Lately I made myself break it down to units: this lateral, that riser, this valve, eligible for a repair, a replacement, or death.

“What the hell,” I said softly, when I eased the pipe a little with my hand and the crack sprang wider. There was movement under the ground. Something had nosed at this spot, or the earth itself was giving up.

“I don’t want to start an argument,” Noah said behind me, not turning around until I did, “but we should just cap this tree and move on.”

The words settled over everything like ash. He dodged back into a smile. He always did that, said the truer thing and immediately softened it, looked to me like I might agree if I didn’t think I was being attacked.

“Cap this tree?” I repeated. “It’s twenty feet to the next. You cap one and the pressure gets weird downstream.”

“We can regulate the downstream with clamps.”

“With what clamps? Those bent sheet-metal things? We used those in eighty-nine.”

“There’s a bag of them in the shed. I found them when I was looking for the tar line.”

“You found those but not the eight box fans? Mother of God.”

He laughed, a little. We were making too much noise for an orchard morning. It reminded me of when we were boys and Dad was teaching us what to listen for—the difference in sound between water rolling free out of a broken line and the more percussive plinking against clods that meant a smaller leak; the particular whine the pump made before it seized; the hiss of air in a cracked line if you turned a valve on too quick. He’d made a game of it, so when he leaned back on a tree box and shut his eyes, sweating, we’d crawl around with our ears practically to the soil and shout out what we heard like we’d learned a foreign language. We had laughed too much then, too, and he’d said, “Listen, you clowns,” and then he’d laughed at himself for calling us clowns, amused by a gentleness he did not always allow himself.

“We have to make a call,” Noah said, more quiet now. “About all of it.”

“We are making a call,” I said. I held up the blue can and shook it. The ball inside thocked against the metal. “We’re fixing the line and getting the water on, and you can talk to me about philosophy later.”

“It’s not philosophy.”

“What is it then?”

“You know what it is.” He slid the shovel blade under the pipe bed and lifted. Hot water ran over my knuckles. We had turned the valve off but the lines bled like sheep long after you thought they wouldn’t.

“We should cut here,” I said. “Two inches on either side. Set a slip. And I’ll wash out the line to get any grit. Will you get me that towel? No, not that one. That one’s for the primer.”

I had a small, savage feeling of satisfaction each time I found the right tool in my bucket without looking. My hand found the saw by the tape-wrapped handle and the rubber of it squelched like something living. I set the teeth where the paint ended on the PVC and listened, counting out strokes, doing my best to keep the blade level by noting where the teeth ate too quick, how the pipe tried to roll. There was a line of sweat at the base of my spine that felt muggy as a thumbprint. When the saw skipped and the blade bit my finger because I’d been too quick, I swore in a way that brought a hawk off the top of the sycamore, just the shadow of it going over the row.

“Don’t bleed on the glue,” Noah said. He took the saw from me and I let him. He tended to what requested him by way of being broken, but he had never liked bodies for their mess and their inability to listen.

“Kid gloves,” I said. “That’s what I always envied.”

He sliced the other side, easy. He had always been good with his hands when he let himself. He laid the cut piece down on the damp price tag of earth the water had made and flicked a small brown spider off it with the edge of the saw blade. The pipe haloed the little cut log of itself with the trickle it still held. My hand throbbed impressively for the smallness of the cut.

“You got Band-Aids in your truck?” he asked.

“Use the tape,” I said. I found the Teflon tape in my pocket and tore it with my teeth and bandaged myself with unnecessary care, wrapping the finger pink and white until it looked like another person’s.

Noah said, “When Dad cut himself he’d put dirt in it if there was no sawdust around.”

“He stopped doing that when we learned the word tetanus.”

“He stopped because Mom stopped him.”

“She stopped him because her hands always burned from the bleach she used on the kitchen counter and she didn’t like seeing blood on it.”

“She just didn’t like the look of it on the rag.”

“No,” I said, suddenly uncharitable, sudden as how a bull will lean and then swing its head with no added warning, “she didn’t like it because she saw too much of it from cleaning for Grandma when she got sick.”

“Okay,” Noah said. He jammed the slip coupling over the lower half of pipe. He was good at the glue, too: primer sweeping inside the coupler until it sounded like a muffled palm smack, the blue stirred one way for luck like our father had told us, this myth he’d made for himself because some days it looked like going to church and he had claimed there was no harm in pretending the universe could be swayed. The glue smeared itself in blue lips on the PVC, and the rubber of the slip creaked when he twisted it, blue rings forming in the grooves.

We kept the silence between us while we did the next steps. I’ve always thought certain tasks insist on certain kinds of quiet. The right silences are as ritualized as any liturgy. The pump house requires cussing. Planting asks for singing, if you’re the kind of person who sings. Dad used to hum “Red River Valley” when he thinned the peaches and his lips moved when he counted, and I learned to count from that, better than I’d learned it in class, because numbers in air aren’t much help to a person like me and numbers on knuckle joints, apple against flesh—that was something. But pipe work like this requires a held breath, slow, a listening to the way the slip slides on and stops, whether it meets so true that you can be forgiven for believing in physics as a kind of magic.

He slipped the upper end and eased it down, cupping it close to the lower like he was cradling a fat fish that did not want to be consoled. We waited the minute and a half we always waited, no matter what the can said about set times. The smell of the glue huffed up and cut through the scent of resin and hot leaf. Sweat ran a clean, cold line down my back, my skin surprised at the temperature difference in this heat. The world went out until it was just that blue curing in the shadow we made.

He sat back, finally, heels in the dirt, knees up. He brushed his palms on his shorts and made two dark prints. He didn’t look at me.

“I can get you a number,” he said. “He buys out in cash. Gives you time to move your equipment. You can rent it back for a season if you want to make sure, all that handshake stuff.”

“Who?”

“Guy named Sandoval. Works with the larger investors.”

“Sandoval.” I tasted the name like a new variety and filed it in the wrong place on purpose.

“Wants to do a solar install on the west quarter. That’s where the best southern exposure is, he says.” He huffed an aborted laugh. “Listen to me. Talking about exposure.”

“I thought it was just developers who came sniffing.”

“They do. They are. But he’s the only one who sounded like he’d be decent about it.”

“If I’d wanted decent, I’d have been a librarian,” I said.

“What the hell does that mean?”

“It means we were not raised to think about decent when money got in the room with us. It means Dad always said you take what you can get and you keep the part that matters out of reach of everybody.”

“Did he say that?”

“He said it when he was sober and he said another thing when he wasn’t. I got to do both those educations. You left when it got interesting.”

“Don’t,” he said. Not sharp. The sound of the word was flat as a Christmas light that should have lit and didn’t and you unscrew it and screw it back in and it’s still dead.

I closed my mouth on what wanted to come out next. It had been three-quarters forming for years. I’d gone to say it any number of times, and then I’d hand him a pair of gloves or ask him to pass me the wrench instead, and he’d tell me something about his students’ parents or the dog in his apartment building with one eye and how it watched him like it knew everything anyone did in the hallway, and we’d go on like we’d made a choice to stay on a road that ran between tall things we couldn’t see.

“Let’s test,” I said.

We stood up with the low, collective grunt men make without deciding to. You can tell a lot about how often you’d moved in company by how natural that sound is. We turned the valve by degrees the way Dad taught, so air didn’t rush and sand didn’t buck and the pressure didn’t give us lies. Far upstream you could hear the first sputters at the nozzles. Closer in, tight, reliable pss-pss as the arc found its range. No new leaks in our seam that we could see. We watched the blue coupler marry itself into anonymous usefulness.

“You still want to cap that tree?” I asked.

“I want to talk to you before we spend the afternoon repairing ten more things and we’re too tired to hear one another,” he said. He walked, slow, picking his way over the little spurts, fiddling with a sprinkler that had clogged with a mosquito raft.

“It’s barely ten o’clock,” I said, which was not what I meant. What I meant was: I can do this all day if I have to and I can do it all the next day and the day after, but I cannot have the other conversation and then swing a shovel with any grace. What I meant was: the first conversation makes the second one impossible.

“Let’s go look at the pump,” he said.

We followed the line that led there as if it was a steadying hand. The pump house was a squat cinderblock building painted a color it had no right to be named: Navajo White. The paint had pulled away in long reptile strips where the sun was worst. The door tilted into its frame and thumped like a pool cue. Inside, the air hung low and wet and made the back of my throat taste of iron. The heat of the motor was animal, a breath. Dust clung to the curves of the intake like a velvet.

I put my hand on the casing and felt the faint hum through it like a remembered song. It had been a better hum once. The pump was like any other living thing in that regard: it would tell you what it felt if you gave it your ear long enough. There was a new roughness. The bearings were near to going. The packing in the gland had blackened under the drip. The little jar washers that those labels said you were supposed to replace at intervals that did not align with our money had all splayed outwards like finger bones.

“We keep this another year and she’ll burn down the valley,” I said.

“What does that mean?” Noah asked.

“It means your decent man will have a pile of scrap and we’ll have less water than we do now.”

“It means we can’t afford to replace her.”

I nodded, because there was no arguing. Even if you could get the parts, even if you could bolt this one apart and nurse it through the worst of August and the way September always came at you hot here no matter what the newsman said, we did not have the money for it, not with the made-early payment on the loan that gave us a better rate and the taxes and the way the well itself had silted these last years. The canal water wasn’t a sure thing this season. The allotment was a fraction of what the old men still at the diner—every one of whom would list their COPD as a badge and their irrigator’s patent as a confession—would say a man deserved in this valley.

“This is the difference,” he said, very soft, not to be cruel. “This is why I keep thinking—why I can’t stop thinking—what are we doing? It’s not just stubbornness, Caleb. Even if there weren’t buyers lined up ready to shovel money at us, it would be—you said it yourself. Physics. The pump will fail. The well will go bad. The trees want more than we can give.”

“The trees want,” I said, and had to put my mouth against my sleeve because I heard myself and did not want to sound like a lunatic in a desert muttering to its inhabitants. I have always been too much in love with the idea that wanting is just a larger behavior of water. He watched me and did not flinch and that, more than anything, made me angry with him, because he refused to take the easy path. I wanted him to scoff. I wanted to be able to feel singular.

“You know what we could do,” I said. I did not look at him. I leaned into the pump’s heat like it would do something to my back, which had already started to seize around the place where an old injury lived as if it had a little house there red as a barn. “We could let the west block go. Just cut it off at the valve. The trees past the ditch—we could do it today. That’s the worst soil anyway. Re-calcify and you can’t do anything with it.”

“And that makes everything okay? That not being able to afford to water our trees is okay as long as we choose the ones to die?”

“You don’t do this thing because it’s okay. You do it because it’s yours.”

“You don’t do this thing because it’s yours. You do this thing because it can live under your hands. I can’t—” He stopped. He closed his eyes, then opened them again and looked at the floor like there was something down there that would argue back. The light through the aluminum screen made his face a regular patchwork. He put the back of his hand to his mouth and bit it and left a little crescent with his teeth.

“We could put in pistachios,” I said, the way I had been saying it in my head for a year, though every time I’d run the numbers I had stopped before the end. “They’re salt tolerant. They suit this heat. They—”

“Take six years to bear. Seven. Eight,” he said. “And the upfront cost—Jesus, Caleb. We can’t even—” He shook his head. “Why do you stay on the treadmill? Why not step aside?”

“Because somebody will pick it up if I don’t,” I said. “And they won’t be careful.”

“Who are we being careful for?” He asked it not as an accusation but as if he hoped I’d say a new thing.

Dad. The word flared in my head like a welding iron. Dad, dragged back into this valley the way his father had dragged him, as if the boy of him had been a caught thing on a string. Dad, who had known the names of the birds by their calls but not the names of his own exhaustion. Dad, who had made himself in this dirt and then made himself hard to everyone in it, the way he’d thought he had to.

“He thought he owned it because he paid for it,” I said. “I know better. It owns me because I love it.”

He let out a breath that had edges. “Everyone thinks their love is a reason. Sometimes it’s the weight that pushes the seesaw down and pins you there because you think you can hold the other end with your chest.”

He looked, at that moment, like our mother. It wasn’t his hair or his nose but something in his face like it had softened to hold someone else’s burden without entirely identifying it. Mom had had those eyes when she’d fold the towels in long thirds and watch Dad through the kitchen window. She had recognized that love is record-keeping, and it will do what mathematics do to people who do not really choose the discipline but find themselves in it nonetheless. There are always remainders.

“Come on,” I said, because I was the older brother and had been saying that since I was five and he was three and put a pea up his nose so far you could only see the green through an impossible place. “We should flush the filters. The sand is full of plankton this time of year.”

He laughed, low, not committed to it. He took the bucket from me and carried it outside and set it by the filters. We shook the filters. Mud coughed out and made the smallest delta at my boots. The sunlight that came into the pump house narrowed to a single bar and lay across the motor casing like a hand. The heat of it made my fingertips ache. I spit in the dust and the little wet crater stank of iron and something like algae, an old pond turned on its head.

When we’d done enough to satisfy the morning’s list we emerged into the flattest part of the day and immediately regretted it. We had been born and raised under this sun and still, every year, it felt like an error in judgment when June reminded us what was possible. The wind the weatherman called a breeze had picked up blessedly and came off the canal like a blessing from a religion I didn’t subscribe to but envied. The light turned every particle in the air visible. That is how I think of June: every invisible thing is cataloged and made legible. Including, unfortunately, how tired we were.

At noon we sat in the thin shadow of the truck, our legs out, canteens between our knees. We ate the awkward lunches we had packed like we were each twenty and tasked with proving to some unseen judge we were capable of feeding ourselves: peanut butter on stale bread, uneven coils of beef jerky, half a dozen apricots between us that had the dust on them still and tasted like the sound that sweet sings when it finally meets mineral and decides not to mind it. I sucked on a pit and rubbed a thumb against the flesh-meal and thought about how Dad had kept the jars of pits on the mantle one year like someone who had been robbed would keep the evidence of the thieves on display. He had claimed he was counting. I think now he wanted a record, something to heft when he was having a day he did not think he had worked enough.

“How’d you sleep,” Noah said, which is how men in our family ask Are you thinking of overtakes and is your heart doing that thing.

“I slept fine.”

“You were up at three,” he said mildly. He had the decency not to say: I heard you walk. The old house carried every sound like a church.

“I was listening for the pump,” I lied.

“I thought you didn’t worry until August.”

“I’ve been practicing.”

He took an apricot, pulled it in half with his thumbs, and passed me one side without looking at my face, as if the fruit had made the decision and not him. His hands were clean for a person who had been in dirt all morning. He must have rubbed his palms together like he did, as if holding an invisible bar and trying to start a fire with friction alone. He looked out over the orchard the way a person looks at the lowing of something they do not understand.

“You know,” he said, “it’s not only the money.”

“That makes it worse, you saying it like that” I said. “Say it is if it is.”

“It is,” he said. “But it’s also—” He tapped the ground once with his heel as if to make it a bell. “Do you want me to say it in my language or in yours? In mine it sounds like my life is not here. In yours it sounds like it’s a kind of math I can’t do.”

“In yours then,” I said, because I was feeling perverse.

“My life is not here,” he said. He let the truth sit there, flat as an extra piece in a kit you wish you had figured out a use for without it being a doorstop one day. He braced his hand on the ground and left a print he covered up with his heel as if he was embarrassed to have made an impression.

“You think mine is?” I said.

“I don’t know,” he said simply. “Some days it looks like it. Some days it looks like—habits strung on a line. And when I picture you old, I picture you here because that is the movie my brain has made and runs when it cannot sleep and that terrifies me because what if it’s a kindness to tell you I can pay the penalty on the loan and we can sell before you have to be the man who becomes a spare part to the thing that owns him.”

“I thought you weren’t quoting yourself at dinner any more,” I said, and it was not a nice thing to say, and I regretted it and then the regret arrived late like a dog you’d called while you were still angry, after the bad humor had passed.

He chewed. He did not correct me. He could have. He could have said: I don’t know anything anymore. He could have said: I panic that all I know how to do is move to where the money is and spend it on the curation of a life and then feel false because who am I to have the chance to be false. He could have said: every time I come up that road I have a new plan for you and for me and by the time I get to the house the only plan I have is that I will step into a bathtub and put my face in the water and listen to the dull ringing it makes in my ears until I am brave again. He did not say any of that. He looked at the tree line where Mrs. Hale’s property used to begin and then did not begin anymore because nothing begins when it is an empty field staked to be a development. He ate an apricot as if it were medicine.

We drank. We were not gentlemen about thirst: our heads thrown back and our throats working sweetly. The shadow of the truck gave faithfully its six and a half inches of shade. Flies came and went in their halting, purposeful ways, landing on the salt of my shin like small messengers from an afterlife I could in good faith claim we all deserved. Between the rows I could see where a coyote had come and gone, the prints deep where the irrigation ran, delicate where the ground rose. That coyote had a den somewhere near the cottonwoods. I had watched the pups this spring skitter like boys in their first stiff shoes. I had wanted, briefly, to be a person who could sit all day and take notes in a lined book. I am not that person.

We rose when the heat should have kept us where we were. We rose because that is what we had learned to do no matter what the day demanded. The next leak was in the main that ran the shallow side of the gulch. The soil there was tricksy even in years when the rains replenished and no neighbor laid down a layer of chemicals thick as faith. The pipe had pulled out of the elbow there in a way that made me suspect the last man to fix it had been too sure of his sleeve glue and not careful with his primer. I had better words for people like that until I remembered it had been me, three Octobers ago, when my head had been all haywire with the weed whacker’s hum and the way the rain had come so fast it had sounded like someone hosing the sheets on the line. I had primed the shallower side and then someone had called me and I had picked up the phone and Dad had told me a story he’d told me already three times that week and I had pressed the elbow on with hope instead of certainty.

“I hate this,” I said.

“This particular job or this pattern we are repeating every hour and a half of our lives?” Noah said. He had his hands on his hips. He looked just like Dad right then. Dad used to stand that way in a hardware store in the electrical section having wandered there out of some misplaced sense of adventure and stare at the rows of something he knew nothing about and want very much to want to know. He had taught us by accident the pleasure of admitting what you didn’t know. He had also taught us, by accident, the danger of pretending you did and letting the pretending harden into a sorry kind of protection.

“This particular job,” I said.

We did it quick because we had finally let our bodies do it without letting our brains note it all the way. I cut the line clean. He held the slip just so. Our arms looked the same in the light, I noticed: gross, sinewed, the hairs on the wrist gone blond. We snapped the new el and pressed. He used his thumb against the wet blue like a man taking a thumbprint of a child he should have been there for the first dozen times more than he had been, which is not to say he had done anything wrong with his life, only that different lives choose different absences to collect. The line slicked itself into place. We held it. We counted. A lizard did a neat press-up on a rock and then scuttled.

When we turned the valve, the sound roared in that way water does in the places we cannot see. I like the idea that a kind of thunder lives underground and we make a profession of it. The orchard spit and blinked. A rabbit bolted and took a hard question-mark left then stopped because its brain told it to stop and look.

“Did you ever think,” Noah said, above the new hiss we’d just built, reluctant, “about selling when—like when Dad was still—”

“Yes.”

“When?”

“When he smashed the pump timer with his hand and then cried because he had the wrong watch on, and it told him he’d been somewhere that he hadn’t, and he believed the watch and not himself.”

He didn’t answer right away. There are gradations to grief’s quiet too. This one was the kind where you watch water and say the name of it in your head as many times as are equal to the number of days you think you could have made different if you had set your body between someone and the thing they were going to do next.

“I would have said yes if he’d asked me,” I said. “You were gone. I wanted somebody to tell me that keeping it was a story I could choose to stop telling myself.”

“I could have told you to sell.”

“I know,” I said, which was not the same thing as I wish you had. The heat pressed hard. He looked at me and saw all of it there and did not force it. He said instead, “Let’s eat the rest of those apricots.”

“Now?”

“Yes. Or I’m going to faint.”

“What a delicate flower.”

“You can carry me like a sack of almonds.”

It should not have been funny. It was.

We ate standing up, like two men taking communion from a plate left on a windowsill too long in the sun. A truck went down Hale Road hauling something that knocked and sang and then the sound was gone and the silence unstacked itself. A meadowlark sat on a post and made the machine of its throat into a bell that has never needed to be tuned. A dragonfly the size of my hand paused on a sprinkler for seven full seconds and then zipped off as if embarrassed.

By three we had collected under our belts three more couplers, an O-ring, and the memory of Dad’s last words to each of us individually the way you carry a tool without knowing it is in your hand until you need it. Noah’s had been better. That is how my brain puts it even though there is no magic to it and no justice. Noah got “Make sure she eats.” Just that. He had meant our mother, who was refusing food in the way she does when she thinks her appetite is too loud for the room. He was not, I do not think, talking about me. He was not talking about the orchard. I got: “Don’t let them,” which is worse than a prayer and no use at all in a courtroom, because I had to live with a ghost who believed there were still thems in the room. I followed that order longer than I should have, I think, given how quiet the thems had been of late.

By four I had the streaked forearms of a man who should not be out there in that heat and still is. My head had a particular hum in it I associated with the year I worked every shift there was at the packing shed, every position on the belt until my hands did not know what it was to hold a fork for dinner without my fingers creeping into that grip. I had never had the shoulder for the shed. Dad told me that often enough. He told me in the way he thought a man talking to a man was supposed to. The sentences pretended they did not know where my heart was. He taught me how to stop a story at the end of the sentence. I have spent years relearning how to let it run into the next.

We walked the rows together at five thirty and looked for the new wet places that meant we had not been as careful as we thought. The sun had dropped only enough to admit that it would have to, eventually. The light had gone wider. The cottonwoods along the ditch had invented their own night. Frogs inside the irrigation box had begun their absurd honking, little businessmen with umbrellas shouting about nothing.

“So where do you see it going?” I asked. The question surprised me. I had intended to keep it to myself until the season turned and my body was tired enough to turn it into simple nouns and verbs.

He didn’t pretend not to know what I meant. “If we don’t sell to a developer, or to your man with the solar panels, and if we don’t replant, and if we just—what, keep on?—the numbers get worse. We borrow or we don’t. If we don’t, it’s easy to see what happens. We cut what we can cut. Maybe we make one more year. Maybe two. Then we go to auction. It hurts. We move out. The house goes like it was never a house anyone loved. If we do borrow—” He looked at his hands. “The thing about debt is it tells you it has made you strong and then it puts a muzzle on you. The only way I could come to peace is if I saw a way to—what?—carve out a part of this that can live. If you said, let’s keep two blocks and lease the water right on the rest, give me a little income from somewhere else, I—”

“Income from where?”

“Teach,” he said and shrugged. “There’s a high school ten miles away and they need everything. I could drive. I could—” He shook his head. “Listen to me. I turn every sentence into a plan the way you turn every faucet into a valve you’ll rebuild one day.”

“That’s not a bad plan,” I said. I had found myself walking slower where the shade touched me. I moved again. He kept pace. Our feet were the exact same size; I had never noticed.

“It’s bad because in it you are doing all the same you’ve been doing except with me standing next to you, and then one day you die and I’m stuck with the thing I didn’t choose and I resent you even as I love you for not leaving me alone.”

“You resent me already,” I said.

“Yes,” he said, not looking at me. Then, after a beat and a shrug, “But not in a useful way.”

We came to a wet place so round and new I knew it had blown only just now and I let the laugh come out of me more like a bark than a human sound. The ground had taken the water and made nothing of it yet. It glinted at me like an eye.

“You get the feeling,” I said, “that we’re characters in one of those Greek things? That a god is watching us do this badly and is getting ready to make an animal for the purpose of watching us better?”

“I don’t know from gods,” he said. “But yes.”

We dug the sand away down to the line. The sun had softened, beautiful as a woman who is about to ask something hard of you. The leak was a pinhole in the elbow we had used from the bucket three hours ago. The new box of parts had a flaw. The whole box, maybe. The metal had skimped to pay for the better paint in a plant far away where no one knew the particular costs of June.

“I want to call him now,” Noah said. He had his phone out and the number already cued, and then he put it back again. He put one hand on his knee and looked like he was praying.

“I could call him,” he said into his thigh. “I could say not yet. Then he would wait and you would breathe. And then what, Caleb? We do this again next week. We shift from this break to that, from this season to that. The grove throws us a year that looks like what I want to see because the rains were strange and the money man at the bank had a bad day and gave us a better rate because he wanted to feel like a person. And then—”

“And then?” I asked. I had my hand back in the dirt. The cool of it had ceased to be pleasant and turned into the kind of chill that lives behind your kneecaps when you realize you’ve forgotten your keys.

“And then you die,” he said evenly, as if I had asked him how the story ends and he was not about to be bullied by the question. And it was the right thing to say. It was awful and argumentative and right. And, too, we do not get to be the person who says the right thing in that tone and then steps back into a life where nothing puts us against the shoulder of a pump and asks us to press.

“Let me talk to him,” I said. “Let me be the person who says no.”

“You are always the person who says no.”

“Then let me be the one who says not yet. One more season.”

“For what?”

“For what we started with,” I said. The light had found a way to make the glue glisten blue as if it were water itself and not the stand-in for it. “For putting our hands in it one more time.”

“That’s church,” he said. “You’re going to have to show me a sacrament.”

I pointed to the micro-sprinkler beyond, to the one with the exact spin I like: a steady dandelion of spray, not too high to evaporate in the hot air, not so low that the tree’s suck won’t find it. The water had found the pollen on a leaf and made it bead. It is a stupid thing to insist upon. It is everything.

He looked at what I was pointing at and he saw, because he is my brother and he has the same eyes I do on alternate Tuesdays. He let out a breath. He nodded. He sat back on his heels. He took out his phone. He held it like a sacrament.

“I’m going to ask him for a month,” he said. “One month. If in a month you have a plan that makes it so I do not throw up when I look at it, we try your way. If—” He shook his head again, to clear a thought. “If we’re just a patch on a patch on a patch, we stop being a thing that thinks it’s speaking English when it’s just saying its name for water, and we sell. If he doesn’t wait—”

“Then he’s not decent,” I said, and I had to let the smile into my voice. It made the ridge line look younger. It made the cottonwood on the ditch throw off a few leaves like it was applauding.

He made the call, standing, looking into the rows. He used his mannered voice, the one he used for his principal and for my mother when she was near the end and refused to accept the end had anything to do with her. He listened, his face making several different calculations at once, all of them scrupulously private and only one of them edged with hope. He very nearly laughed at something the man said. He said, “No, thank you,” when I would have said, “You can go to hell,” and it did not count as a fault in him.

He hung up. He swallowed. He looked at me. There are moments when you take a brother into your mouth like an oath and carry him there without rashly saying anything out loud.

“A month,” he said.

“Okay,” I said. Which could mean anything.

We finished the repair while the light went to amber and then to something almost pink. The slip came true. We turned the valve. The rush started again, obedient as ever. The tree took the water and made a sound in my body that was not a sound and therefore could not be shared but still was real as a tally. The orchard breathed. I heard the breath I have listened for since I could understand that a grove is a system in more ways than one.

We walked back up the row slow. The frogs were louder. The hawk had positioned itself like an idea in a tree that did not want to hold it. The truck waited with its bed full of our failure and our small, exact work. I could feel the ache that would live in my shoulders tonight the way other men feel the ache of a woman they no longer have any claim on. The house was at the far end of the long view, small and immodestly bright because Mom had chosen that yellow and the sun had been kinder in those days and held up its end of the deal.

We did not speak for a time. We did not need to. The line of trees ahead of us had their own way of wanting. A bee lifted and flew in the carriage of my voicebox and I swallowed and it was gone and it meant nothing and it meant everything. We stepped into the truck’s shadow and out of it. We stood. He wiped his face. I put my hand on the new primer stain on my jeans like a man will put his hand on a scab to keep himself from picking at it. A wind came up and went away and came up again, persistent and then not. There is a word for that in some language. There must be a word for the way you think you have chosen and then you find out you had only turned a degree and the rest would always be coming.

“You’re going to need a new pair of boots,” he said suddenly, lightly, with the specific exasperation of the younger.

“I like these,” I said. The sole had torn away at the toe on the right foot in a gash that made me look like a surprised fish. He had been watching it all day. I could feel, inside that small problem, all the other ones.

“I’ll send you pictures,” he said, as if boots were a species that responded to being looked at with reverence. “You don’t even have to come to town.”

“Don’t send me anything with stitching on the toe. I can’t abide it.”

“Christ,” he said. Then he laughed. It surprised me and made me laugh too. The frogs paused and then shouted louder because that is what frogs do when they think they are having the last word.

We went to the house. We ate what there was. We kept the lights off because the heat stayed near the ceiling and because there is a mercy in sitting in a dark room with your brother and bruises on your shins that match and letting the day go at the pace it wants to. The orchard did its night work. The pump whined and then settled and then found its song again and then sighed and shut down on the timer like the most miraculous, stubborn, loyal thing. The frogs argued. A mule train of clouds came over like something that knew where it was going and would not be told otherwise. When we stood to seal the door for the night, when I moved the brick to lean it closed because the latch had gone the way of all small mechanisms in houses where men pretend they will fix them when they are done with other work, I looked down the row and saw the apron of wet that meant we had missed another leak. It was a small one. It shimmered to me across the dark.

“Tomorrow,” I said.

“Tomorrow,” he said. He pressed his palm against the door like a seal and it held. We went inside the house our parents had believed would hold more years than the valley itself. He slept in the room he used to sleep in, the one with the poster over the bed of a mountain he had never climbed. I lay in my old bed and listened to the water we had set in motion and to the spaces where it did not yet run. I lay there and thought of a month, of what you can ask a grove to give you in a month, of what you can say yes to if only you think yourself bigger than the little blue ring of glue pressing itself into a shadow and refusing to let go. I lay there thinking of the men who had stood in that dirt before us, their hands the same hands, and of the men who would come after if they did, if they must. I lay there and held both of those truths in the kind of grip that makes a person believe they have done something because they have thought it, which is a lie and also, on some days in June, the only way to be any kind of honest at all.

I woke at three because the pump’s pitch changed four degrees and I had learned this in the small instruments of my body. I lay there and counted my breath and the rolling in the pipes. At four the birds started, insolent. At five I heard the truck door open carefully like a novel. If you had told me I had dreamed it I would have believed you. I rose anyway, put on the boots I refused to replace, and went out to the yard. The sky was a skin we could not see through yet. Noah stood there, his back to me, looking at the orchard like a man looks at the sea when he has learned to swim and does not know if he will again. He did not turn when I came up. He did not have to. He said, “The leak?” and I said, “Tomorrow,” and we both knew tomorrow would be today and then we were walking again, our feet in the spoor of yesterday, the smell of hot green lifting into the day's throat like a song neither of us had written and both of us knew the words to already.
