They called last Tuesday, a thin island voice that carried a wind I could hear before I knew where it came from. Is this Daniel? the voice said, and my name sounded as if it had crossed water to be said. I'm phoning from the Northern Division. We have a body recovered at Lunna Voe. There was a wallet, mostly ruined, and a note with your number. We think it might be your brother. We need someone to make a formal identification.

The ferry was the late one, the overnight crossing north that left as day drained and arrived after breakfast. I drove down to the terminal in the fading light, past the dim squares of houses and the cobbled quay with its new glass waiting-room where the gulls walked like clerks in the pale soup of the air. I hadn't packed much. You don't bring clothes for seeing a dead man. I had a spare shirt that didn't smell of the petrol station and the same coat I'd worn for years in winter, cuffs polished with use. In the glove compartment I'd stuffed a set of letters my brother left in our father's shed, letters to no one, things he'd written down when he'd had no one left to tell. I didn't know if I'd need them or if he would have wanted me to bring them. It was a foolish token, the way you bring flowers to a grave you've never seen.

On the ramp, the man in the high-vis jacket waved me forward, and I felt the roll of the ferry underfoot before the car had moved at all. The stomach knows these things before the mind. We were folded into the belly, cars hunching close to each other, doors opening onto damp air. Lorries breathed diesel warmth and the sharp ammoniac tang that must have been from the road grit piled in sacks along the wall. I turned the key, killed the engine and sat for a second in the sudden mechanical silence punctured by gulls and the shout of some deckhand above. Then I took my small bag and followed the painted arrows and the smell of hot oil up the steel stairs to the passenger deck.

The lounge looked like a waiting room someone had taken out to sea. Low ceilings, bolted chairs in ranks, tables too small for elbows, a cluster of slot machines blinking like domesticated stars. Children ran on the soft carpet in an ellipse until their parents hissed them still. There was a dog glimpsed under a table, a border collie whose white feet stuck out from under the muzzle of a long-haired man with a ring in one ear. I stood at the window and watched the dockside lights smear and begin to float as if someone had lifted them on a spoon. The tannoy said something about safety. It said something about crossings being subject to the conditions. It asked us to watch our step.

I bought tea in a paper cup and watched steam turn to its own weather in front of my mouth. A girl in a blue polo shirt told me it was one pound twenty, then didn't wait for me to give her the coins before she had smiled at the man behind and began to pour his, too. We were moving now. Slow at first, as if there were something that didn't want to let go, and then a fraction faster, and you feel it truly in your knees when you look at the quay falling away. A man in overalls below backhanded a coil of rope as if shooing a fly. The ramp lifted, the ship turned with a sour stutter of sound and the night came forward to meet us like a shape we'd been walking toward all day without seeing.

When we were outside the breakwater the ship gave a small animal shiver and settled into its going. The grey of the water became a dark that had weight, and on the horizon a last pale band refused the night. We all stood at the window as if we were witnesses, as if something had to be witnessed, and then one by one we fell back into ourselves. In my pocket my phone buzzed with a message from an unknown number, a taxi service on the island telling me where to find their rank when I arrived. The policeman had texted me the night before with the arrangements like you would lay out a day-trip: meet at the mortuary at ten, ask at reception, he'll come and get you. He had a first name I'd forgotten between the terminal and the lounge. I tried to make a mnemonic and it slipped away.

At a table near mine two young men were discussing an engine timing belt. Their hands moved like they were holding a phantom tool. I watched them for a while because it was easy to look at mouths forming words that had nothing to do with death, and because the engine's thrum beneath us had a lullaby in it that pretended you could sleep if you trusted the ferry to keep going.

The screen on the far wall said we were halfway between land and water even when we were just leaving the harbor, a looped video of whales and islands and a man pouring whisky over stones. The whalebone arch in one shot looked wrong somehow, an empty gate in a place without walls. I thought of my brother and gates and how he couldn't pass one without climbing it. He hated the slight humiliation of passages, the way a door demands you alter your momentum and ask. He walked through things like he wasn't supposed to be there and had paid for it so many times that it had become a habit too. Once when we were small he had kicked at a door because my father had locked us in after a shouting and the door had flung back and the handle had struck him on the eyebrow. He wore a thin white crescent there forever after, like a line of chalk. For years I watched people's eyes go to it. They would glance at the scar and then glance at him to see what sort of man would have that mark and call it his.

I took my tea out of the lounge and walked a circuit. Past the black plastic chairs in the gaming corner, past the darkened shop with tea towels printed with maps no one could fold away, past the sign pointing toward the cinema room where a film no one had asked for flickered on a bulbed blue sea. A group of elderly women in the corner had luggage like pale coffins. They had taken off their hats because it was warm inside and their hair lifted as if a breeze had gotten confused and joined them. They were talking about the light in June and all the different words they had for it.

I found my way up and out. On deck the wind had teeth and found its way under my coat as easily as if it had been invited. We cut a white stitch through a cloth too dark to name. The metals of the deck hummed and sang under my shoes. On the starboard side a man smoked and his cigarette end glowed a small furnace in the wind's ancient human war. He nodded at me like men do when they know what you're doing and they'll not ask you for it. I nodded back. I leaned on the rail and old salt lifted into my face and settled on my lips in powder. There come nights with sea in them and there come seas that are like the nights they grew up with, and this was both. It was as if the place I had been made in had sent me a message, and the message was nothing more articulate than the word more. More cold. More dark. More wind pressing itself against you until you either stood or let it carry you into the deep.

My brother once told me that water has memory and will go where it has gone before if you let it. He was mending something then, or pretending to, sitting on the step with a coil of fence wire. It was the summer after our mother left and we had nothing to do with the day. He could say things in a way that made them sound borrowed from men much older and far more tired. Then he would grin, swerve the conversation into an alley, and steal your wallet like a magician returns what you hadn't noticed leaving you. He said he was going north, that time. He said it casually, as if he had always been planning to and now he thought to mention it. He took a map from his pocket that he'd marked with all the places called with the same names as our school subjects—Geography, History, Chemistry—and he said he would find a place called English. I told him there would be no such place and he said that was precisely his point. Then he was gone. He wrote for a while. Out of a stony place comes a letter that rings if you drop it. Then a year went by and I saw him on a street one night, late, when the shops were shut and the neon of the chippy turned our cheeks the color of meat. He said he was fine. I gave him forty pounds and he gave me back two jokes I have used ever since.

On deck, the man with the cigarette asked me where I was bound for, and I said north without saying where because the sea felt like one place and looking at him I could see he thought that too.

North, he said. He held the cigarette cupped in a hand as if it were a flame he had to protect. He had the broad wrists of men who drive things for long times. I'm on my way to Lerwick with my rig, he said. He lifted his chin toward the lower deck where the lorries were sleeping. He told me they had loaded him wrong and he felt it every time the ferry took the swell. You can sense these things, he said, when you've done it long enough. I nodded like I had the right to nod. He offered me the cigarette and I shook my head.

You from up that way? he said. I told him I wasn't, then I told him I was going to see someone in the hospital. For some reason the lie came out soft and neat and wrong. He looked at me and the wind flattened his hair in a way that showed the geometry of his skull. He didn't press me. Men on ferry decks invent their lives for the length of a crossing and then lay them down when they step back onto land. He told me about a time he had driven a trailer of crushed stone onto a ferry in a storm and the whole crossing he could hear the stone talk as if it were loosening in its cage. He told me about his brother too, how long it had been since they'd spoken. He said it casually. He had the voice of someone who waited for the other man to joke and make it trivial and unimportant. I didn't. He smoked, and then he put the stub out and flicked it over the side and we both watched the dark return to itself as if nothing had ever interrupted it.

When I went back in, the lounge had shifted. People had unwrapped blankets they had found under their questions. The bar had closed. The slot machines had shut their brightly lit eyelids as if suddenly embarrassed. A teenager slept with one foot on the seat and one foot tucked under like he'd only fallen ten minutes into a plan to be awake forever. At the far end, under a television with a rolling text about delays, a woman held a baby and whispered to it in a constant stream as if her voice was the surface it needed. She didn't look tired. She looked soldered to that task and nothing else. The baby's red hat was the brightest thing on the ship.

I had not booked a cabin. The idea of a bed with a window looking onto black had not seemed right, like a hotel room in a fire. I found a place by the window where the glass was not quite cold enough to burn and not quite warm enough to be a joke. I took my bag into my lap like someone might take it if I did not. For an hour I tried not to think about the shape of the morgue, about the idea of pulling a sheet back and looking into a face I had loved and hated and sometimes, often, refused. Instead, I thought of little things because the mind, like the sea, has currents that take you off the line you meant to travel.

Our family had a winter hobby of naming things we could see from the front room window. There was a tree we named the Telegram because it had three wires that met in its crown. There was a roof we called the Yawning Roof because the man who lived beneath always stood in his doorway and yawned when he came out with the milk. There was a cat that tumbled on the low wall that everyone knew and no one managed to get hold of. When our mother left the names became a way of saying goodbye to the town while we were still in it. My brother took to calling the horizon The Stepfather. He said it was because you didn't have to like it but it would be there every morning whether you wanted it or not. He could be cruel with a smile. He could be kind when you were looking away. He was tall in the way that looks like it might hurt if he were to fall. He had hands that always looked as though they needed to do something. And he had the scar, the chalk line on his brow, which made his face look as though God had made a practice cut there and then lost interest in the whole head.

The ship moved in long mariner's breaths. Every now and then there was a tilt and a slosh as if the night had coughed. People shifted in their chairs and then subsided like islands in a seismic storybooks. I closed my eyes and for some spare minutes I was in another room, a smaller, older, colder room with a leaking plastic window and the smell of soup. In that room my brother had been making me laugh with a story about a man in a suit trying to fix a burst pipe with an umbrella. The umbrella had been black and absurd and the water had been strong and had won in the end. He told it with voices, with a whole chorus of men you wouldn't believe could come out of one mouth. I laughed until I forgot I was in the room with the window that leaked. I laughed until I felt that someone was looking through the leak at me and wishing to be where I was because the story there had become good.

I woke to my own hand on my face. The ship had moved in a way that made the picture frame on the wall in front of me shift minutely and then stop. It was a night of nights. The clock on my phone said one twenty-seven. In the window the world had narrowed and widened, a whale's eye. Even with the engine's song there was a hush I recognised and couldn't name. I stood. My legs didn't feel like mine for a step and then they remembered.

Back on deck the wind was thinner, like something had tired. There were more stars now, and the line of shore we'd left had been replaced by a dark of a different kind ahead. I couldn't see land but I knew it was there. The deck was washed in yellow light and a fine spray like silver hair wove itself between the rails. For a moment there was a picking of color in the north like someone had knocked over a glass of green paint and it had frozen in air. It was faint and if you looked at it it pretended you hadn't. The aurora is something that isn't and is, a trick of charged things in a dark you imagine is empty. I stood and watched it and thought of my brother dancing under streetlights, his shadow taller than him and stepping out of time.

In my pocket my phone had one bar and the old messages were stacked like stones. There were ones from him I had not thrown away. They were not kind messages and they were not cruel. They were messages like weather reports called in from somewhere you thought no one lived. They said things like Eat well, idiot and London's all scaffolding and The seal watched me watch him and we'll never be so lucky as that again. Once, three years ago, he had messaged in the middle of the night a photograph of his boots and a slice of sky and one long paragraph about a woman whose hair did what it wanted. He hadn't said where he was. I had not replied. Or I had replied in the morning with something so safe it could have been written by a man to a civil servant. Not even that. I had sent a thumbs-up. I had been too busy being righteous to be useful. My righteousness had a polish to it like the cuffs of my coat.

The moon came out of whatever it had been doing and washed the deck a different white. The ferry's wake went back a long way, a lace torn and thrown behind. I counted things because I needed to. I counted the lifeboats held in their orange cradles. I counted the grated steps up to the bridge. I counted my breaths until they were no longer counting, just the place I lived. The cigarette man reappeared, nodded and moved on. I stayed alone until my cheeks had no more sensations left to give and my heart had calmed from its panics.

When morning came, it did so by way of making things very slightly more complicated. The dark did not leave but it became a dark with information in it. Shapes acquired edges. Water became a thing with thoughts. A thin bruise of light sat on the underside of the horizon and did not move. The ferry adjusted. Somewhere, plates pitched and men swore in short phrases. The baby in the lounge cried for a minute and then was comforted by some pact only the two of them had agreed to keep.

By the time breakfast opened, the windows had turned from night mirrors into the kind of glass you see your life through when you admit you're not going back the way you came. The cafeteria had the smell of bacon and of a sweetness that was everywhere and nowhere. Men in orange sat with men in navy and didn't talk. A woman with a ring of keys like a sailor's belt cleaned tables as if they had offended her the day before. A man stirred tea so slowly I thought he would stretch it to the island and beyond. I wanted to order something that felt like an act of taking part in the day but the idea of putting anything of the world in me that wasn't salt or air seemed a trespass. I drank coffee. It sat in my mouth like a dark that pushed back.

A woman with knitting took the seat opposite. She didn't ask. She looked at me in the frank way older people look when they've lost enough to stop pretending. You're pale, she said. It was not unkind. It was a fact appraised without interest by a professional. I told her I hadn't slept. She patted the place on the table where a plate would have been if I had ordered a plate. I looked at her hands. They were square and confident, pink on the knuckles, as if they had been turned on a lathe. The wool ran through them like it had a mind of its own but gave way to her in the end. Going up to work? she said.

For the day, I said. I'm there the day.

Aye, she said. She clicked another stitch into place. There was a funeral of a cousin I hadn't seen for years, up Firth way, she said, not uncheerfully, as if this was as good a reason as any to make a passage. It had been a good year for weather. The silage had been brought in early, and the terns had not been as vicious as sometimes. There are years the birds hate us and years they forget, she said. This year they forgot. You've the look of someone going to a kind of appointment though.

I nodded. I'm going to identify a body, I said, and then I couldn't believe I had said it out loud and to a stranger of all people. The word identify felt like a weight I was not strong enough to lift. She nodded back and didn't make the face I'd prepared for, didn't offer platitudes as if they were passes to somewhere you didn't want to go. She said, softly, and with a respect like a hand closing a door against a draft: The sea returns what it borrowed in the shape it pleases. The rest is ours to understand the best we can.

We docked with a grinding that set something in the back of my teeth singing. The island town was a scatter of low houses and sandstone blocks with windows that had watched so many days they had become part of the weather. The ferry shrugged its ropes at the quay like a horse presenting a shoulder and the ramp came down and we were let go like water from a gate. I went down into the underbelly and my car felt both very small and like something I could hide in. Outside, the air had the thinness of new coins. The light was a sheet of aluminium and the sky lifted in one great humourless expression. It didn't rain. It didn't threaten to. It simply existed and in that existence said: do what you came to do.

The taxi rank was two cars and a man with a smart jacket who pretended to be bored. He had a moustache that would have been the pride of a younger decade and he wore it like an apology. For hospital? he said in the same voice people use in lifts. I gave him the address. The road curved by the harbor and a queue of vans took fish to places that would never see the sea. The hills beyond wore heather and power lines like a shawl. A wind farm lifted its arms in a gesture you could take as welcome if you were so minded.

We reached the small hospital where stone had been asked to be glass and had mostly refused. At reception the woman looked up and down at me with the same assessment as the woman on the ferry with wool, but this one was administrative, a cataloguing of a form and its boxes. She asked my name and some questions about addresses I didn't belong to anymore. Then the policeman appeared with those steps policemen are taught, quiet somehow, as if they practice in rooms where people are sleeping.

Daniel? he said, and his hand came forward and then thought better of itself and became a hand again. He was very young or he was very old. His face was both at once in a way that allowed me to like him and hate how much I liked him. He said: I'm sorry for what you're here for, and he was. He led me down a corridor that smelled like lemon and dust. He said some things that were, in a way, a continuation of the call: found on the shore at Lunna, some days gone, the weather hasn't been kind, we can't be certain unless you can, we found your number in something, a piece of paper with ink that must have once been black. I'm sorry about the state, he said. We have to ask. There's a ring, he said, and I felt my stomach do a small wrong movement in my body and resettle.

He stopped in front of a door with a round window and a sheet of paper taped to it that said: Authorized only. He said We take this seriously like it were a kindness I could not have expected and that I didn't know how to accept.

Inside the air was colder and held chemicals the body knows are not for it. The room had a table with its wheels locked and lights that only knew how to tell the truth. A woman in a blue apron stood with a clipboard like a picture of herself wearing a blue apron and holding a clipboard. She nodded in a way that folded the terrible into a ceremony and I thought all the shows on television had got that nod very wrong.

This is Mr. Muir, the policeman said to the woman. He is here to— and he did not finish the kind of sentence people don't finish in these rooms. He looked at me as if a thing I might be saving would be reduced if he took it out of my hands.

The woman said: Are you ready? and then, because I didn't say, said: There's no being ready. Will you tell me when to stop, and I will stop then, and you can take as long as you wish, and we will not leave you alone unless you ask, and if you look and you find that you cannot bear to look we can stop. She had done this before. She understood the grammar.

I nodded. I'd say I was ready but that word had ceased to mean what it means to men about jobs and sports and keys and trains. I nodded because I had nothing else.

They drew back the sheet, and there he lay, and there a man lay who could have been my brother or could have been, precisely equal to the last decimal point, not my brother at all. The water had been both kinder and crueller than I had expected. The skin had lost its narratives and acquired the story of the element it had come from. The hair lay to one side like a thing returned from the idea of hair. The lips had become the notion of lips. The eyes were closed in the way eyes can be made to be closed by hands, and I was grateful to whoever had done that small thing like they had shut the eyes of an animal in a ditch. The smell of him was not entirely the room's chemicals. There was a sweet in it that was about time and meat.

But the scar. There was the white half-moon on the brow where the handle had made its mark when we were both minutes from boys. It was there and it was exactly where it should have been and its paleness was this body's only touch of the familiar. I'd thought I'd look for that almost absentmindedly, and I had not thought of it at all until I saw it, and when I saw it I felt my heart change its weight and I said a sound that could have been yes except the word yes is a door and I was still standing in the hall. There was also, on the left hand, the second finger was missing its last section. When we were children, he had held his finger too long in a hinge of a shop's back room and the door had shut on it and the tip had been lost and the nail grew like a joke after that, too small and always looking surprised. Here the hand lay and the finger was whole. I remembered that wrong. For one hot minute the wrongness of my own memory made the scene become a thing in a mirror. Then I looked again and saw the shortness of the nail, the old bite on the knuckle, the way the pad of the thumb was too flat like he'd taught himself hammering with no apprenticeship.

His jaw, I thought. My brother had a way of setting his jaw when he was about to say no that could stop men rushing down a street. This face had a jaw that did not know no or yes. It knew only a math of softly moving out of the shape a face takes and into the shape water gives back. His ear. My brother's left ear had been nicked by a little knife once when he had knelt on a rug and cut up a lemon for a very small party and thought he was making a big gesture. There had been blood like an idea of sympathy from the ear that had frightened a girl and made her go home. This ear had a nick. My stomach tightened again and went cold, colder than the room.

The policeman said, quiet as the fabric of the sheet itself: Is it him?

I took a step forward without intending to. I touched the sheet near his shoulder, and the material had the texture of a thing that has forgotten it was made out of plant. I did not reach for the skin. I did not want to put any heat into that cold. I stood with my hand resting on the sheet and I thought about how there is a stage, in anything that ends, where it is no longer possible to say who stands in for what. What does this stand in for, I thought. Does this body stand in for that boy, and does that boy stand in for the man, and does the man stand in for every argument we had about how to be a person in a world that refused sometimes to be one? In front of me there were facts and there were details and I had to understand that one could be solved and the other never could be.

I looked at the scar again, the chalk line. It was stupid to think it could have been drawn on by someone who thought that was him who wanted me to think that. It was stupid to think that the sea would return another man's head with the exact same careless signature. Yet the world is full of stupid accuracies. I thought of all the men with the same ears and jaws and scars. I thought how if I said yes the apparatus of the state would pull levers and make phone calls, and if I said no another waiting would begin, a worse waiting that would stretch into a horizon flat as a sheet of windless water. I looked at the hand again. On the third finger of the left hand there was a line where a ring had been. The policeman had said ring earlier like someone might say lighthouse. There was a ring on the table in a clear bag. It was a cheap thing, the gold worn thin at the palm, the skin's idea of it left on it like a patina. It had a Saint Christopher sainting no one. My brother hated saints but he loved making fun of them. He would never have worn one unless as a joke, and jokes become habits if you wear them enough.

The woman's eyes on me were the kind of attentive you give someone walking on a high place. The policeman shifted his weight once. The throb of some machine in a room beyond permeated the quiet like a far storm never ends. Somewhere a trolley wheel clicked faintly with each rotation.

He looks like him, I said, and the sound of my voice startled me. It was the voice of a man who has been instructed for his own and others' good to speak clearly. He looks very like him. He has the— and I could not say the word scar because the room had so many now. He has the mark on his head from when we were idiots. He has something of the left ear. But his mouth is not his. His mouth is gone. It never said anything and now it never will. My hands were doing something I couldn't watch. They had made fists inside my coat sleeves and pressed themselves up against my forearms until the skin remembered they were there. I loosened them. The nails had bitten me as if to make sure I were still a living person.

The policeman nodded like he'd been told to nod at this moment at whatever men were likely to say in front of a dead stranger or a dead brother. Take your time, he said. There is no rush here.

But there was. There was the kind of hush that has the shape of a rush. Events want to fall downhill and flatten out somewhere that isn't your house. I thought of my father, the only time in the last fifteen years he had said my brother's name without an argument attached; he had been on the phone to the utilities, and the woman at the other end needed his son's date of birth for an account in some old name, and my father had given it, in a voice like he'd found a coin on a beach and was showing it to someone who would take it away. I thought of my mother, who would have been the only person to hold any of this in both hands and make it stay for a second so the rest of us could look at it and then put it somewhere it couldn't hurt us so immediately. She had been gone so long that even the holes she had left were gone too, patched over by plaster and painted with other colors until people came in and thought the walls had always been like that.

My brother and I once had drawn a line down the middle of our bedroom and discussed very quietly what kind of person would ever cross it. Later the line faded and then became a faint idea of order that we let become blurred and then a joke we told to girls like we were sociologists of our own private country. It's strange to know that the last real thing between us was not something like that. It was a look. He had come to see me once during the year my son was born, this was years back, and had held the boy as if he had been made of some flammable material my brother had never worked with before. He looked at me and said nothing because he knew I was waiting for the joke, for him to say that he had dropped it, that there had been a switch on the way from the kitchen. He didn't. He gave the child back and he left with a look I couldn't translate for a very long time and I did not follow his back down the stairs because I thought there would be more looks to practise my translations on. There weren't. That was the last one I got.

I thought of the phone call and the ferry and the man with the cigarette and the woman with the knitting and the old men not talking at breakfast and the baby in the red hat and the way the aurora doesn't care if you see it, it keeps doing the thing it does in a sky where men are moving from one set of work to another, one set of promises to another, bringing the same hands with them however the sea is behaving.

I leaned forward and for the briefest, least instructive second, I smelled his hair. It smelled like the sea but it also smelled like something human, like the space behind an ear you once kissed when you were too young to understand what it would mean. I closed my eyes. The stupidist things we remember are not the ones that would help us in a room like this. You remember the way he would peel an orange like an apple and criticize people who had never learned to do it with a knife. You remember his throw, the way a ball left his hand with a small clap of noise like he was letting it go only reluctantly. You remember the way he would not end a sentence if someone cried in the middle of it. You remember the smell of cheap soap on his clothes when he had tried to make an effort for a court date. You do not remember whether his left ear had one nick or two. You do not remember whether the white mark on his eyebrow slanted exactly like this.

I opened my eyes and for a second the man on the table looked exactly like my brother asleep. It was the slackness. It was unbearable. In sleep Cal would make a face like he had been disappointed by an idea. He made a small tut sometimes, a tiny admonishing sound at the way days fail to be as excellent as they promise in the morning. He had done that always, even as a child. I don't know where he learned that sound. Perhaps men pass these things down in a chain not unlike a chain of islands you cross on ferries until one day you're not in the place you started.

Is it him? said the policeman again, more gently, as if there were a way to ask it that would make me say something different and he was trying them all.

It is, I heard myself say, and at the same time, as if another man had spoken in the room—no, it isn't. It is and it isn't. It's the man the sea brought back and in him are the parts of my brother the sea took all those years ago when he left and went into something I'd never make peace with. The woman in the apron lifted one drawn-on eyebrow as if to say: we are here, you must meet us here.

I took the paper and the pen the police offered and I signed the line with my name on it because a line is something men make when they cannot make anything else. The pen squeaked faintly, a small ridiculous sound like a toy not yet thrown away. I wrote my name as if it had been given to me to keep safe. When I had done it the woman put the paper on her clipboard and covered the man again, as if that, finally, was a kindness I had earned the right to watch.

Would you like a minute alone? she said. No one ever says: with the body. Bodies are other things, they become euphemism quickly because the truth is coarse. Would you like a minute alone? I'll be outside, the policeman said. He placed a card on the edge of the table as if the man would need it. He slipped back with his quiet shoes. The woman went. The door closed with a click I will think of whenever anything very small is put back perfectly where it belongs.

I stood there. The lights hummed. A tap somewhere dripped with the same stubbornness an old tap uses to ignore men. My breath made a storm in itself. I put my hand on the place where his shoulder was under the sheet. It was colder than I had imagined and yet it was not alien. It was a temperature you could get used to if made to. I said his name, my brother's name, and the way it moved about the room and struck the tiles and came back to me was the strangest of all the strangers. I said I'm sorry, out loud, because there was no one left to hear it and because it was the opposite of righteous and there were so many cups of that left unpoured in me.

Later, in the corridor, the policeman said the words of protocol and condolence, and I nodded the nods of receipt. He said they would be in touch about arrangements, about release. He said: Are you alright? and I said I was as far as that word would stretch. He said there was coffee somewhere and I said that was a very good thing to hear. The woman at reception looked at me as if now I had entered a new category and she would write it in a book later under a letter no one would ever ask to see. Outside the air hit me like a sentence.

In the car I sat with the engine off and watched a gull walk in a deliberate line across the tarmac as if drawing a line we might respect this time. I wanted to call someone. No one arrived. My father's face came to me with the force of a wave. I could see him at his chair with the television on too loud, the remote not quite balanced on the arm, a glass of water beside him like a pill that has given up. I would tell him because who else would I tell and because not telling him would be another act in a long-running play no one wanted tickets to. I would say: It was him. Or I would say: It was what's left of him. Or I would say: It was a man I have called him because the world asked me to call him something and I had run out of names.

On the way down to the ferry that would take me back, the hills gathered their shadows and let them sit like animals that had finally found a place to rest. In the harbor, men moved like they always moved, like day workers in a painting never end up going to bed. The sea was not different. It had not been altered by what it had given me. It will take and give and stand there for as long as there is any standing to be done.

I went to a bench by the water and let the cold enter me in full as if this were the only honest thing left. A woman pushing a pram stopped to fasten a blanket more carefully around a small set of moving legs. She hummed something, a tune made of nothing more than patience and air. I thought of my brother under the sheet in a room where light did its job all day. I thought of him in all the rooms he had been in, rooms with men and with women and with unpaid bills and with stolen oranges and with music nobody else paid for. I imagined him on a ferry deck somewhere with a cigarette he did not smoke because he never could keep a smoke lit in the wind. I let all those images talk to each other until the gulls found something alive to argue about and broke the spell.

When the next ferry pulled in its horn made the noise of a god yawning. I stood and took my bag and walked toward the ramp like any man walking toward anything he's decided can't be dodged. I stood with the other people who were crossing as if this were all there was and all anyone could do. The wind touched my face with a hand I'd come to know in a long night. Above us the flag did its dumb impatient flapping. I put my hand on the rail because it steadied me and because I wanted to feel something that held the ship up hold me, too. The sea was the same, a persistence, a levelled thing and yet with a mind. I stepped forward when the man in the jacket waved and thought, foolishly, about names again. Mine. My brother's. The names men call water when it is in certain states. Swell. Chop. Slack water. Neap tide. Spring tide. All the ways a thing moves toward you and away. All the ways you move toward it and away. I went aboard and I did not look back because you cannot look at the place where you decided who a person was and drive.

On deck, later, in the small square of wind I made with my body as we left, I thought that maybe I had not identified him, not in the way the forms understand, not like matching a figure on a list. What I had done was another kind of act. I had taken the version of him the sea had made and said: This is the one we will carry. This is the one we will speak of in the rooms our lives happen in. It is not precise. It is never precise. The women with the knitting know this and the truckers and the men with the quiet shoes. The ferry slid between two pieces of dark with as much grace as a machine can manage. The island fell away and returned to its place in the map that opens in you when you close your eyes and think of going anywhere at all. On the horizon the light shifted by a fraction like a thought you finally find the right words for, and then lose, and then find again, and I stood with my tea cooling in my hands and let the ship go on doing what it does until there was, finally, nothing else for it to do.
