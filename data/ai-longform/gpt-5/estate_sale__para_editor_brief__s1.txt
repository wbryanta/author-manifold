By one o’clock the stack of ones in the caramel tin was damp from her fingers. The tin had always been on the top pantry shelf, with a faded painting of a cathedral on the lid. She could remember her mother standing on a chair to get to it, clucking her tongue, the chair scraping linoleum. Now the tin sat on a folding card table under the carport. A strip of blue painter’s tape sagged at one corner of the white poster board sign that read ESTATE SALE TODAY—CASH ONLY in her blocky, careful Sharpie. There was a cooler with store-bought water bottles, the kind she wouldn’t have bought if she’d had the time. There were brownies on a paper plate that Dot from next door had carried over at nine and replenished at noon, the corner ones cut unevenly, still warm the first go, now glossy with the humidity.

Claire could see the living room through the open door, people angling around one another with lamp shades and ottomans and boxes of old cookbooks. The screen door bounced on its spring, bang, click. Shoes on the porch steps; the concrete was cracked down the middle from a winter two decades ago when the ice froze in it just right, her father used to say. Cicadas sawed at the air. A man in a Cleveland Browns cap grimaced, trying to shoulder the oak rocking chair past the campaign chest in the hall. He had the chair’s back under his armpit, his face pushed up against one of the slats, and he held out a wad of cash toward her as if she were a tollbooth. “Sixty,” he said.

“Eighty,” she said. That was the price she’d written last night and stuck under the seat, thinking it a small, private defiance against the way people believed everything in Ohio should be cheaper just because the sky was a little grayer.

“Seventy,” he said, already halfway down the steps.

She glanced at Dot, who was in the lawn chair to her left as if anchoring her to the spot, her legs in their floral capris crossed, her gray hair sprayed to helmet. Dot shrugged and pressed her lips together. “He’ll spend it at the VFW anyhow,” she said, low.

Claire put out her hand. “Seventy,” she said, and the man peeled off bills and wedged the chair into the bed of his pickup next to a weed whacker and a plastic child’s play kitchen. She watched the chair settle into a new geography. She remembered her mother’s feet in white socks, rocking there at four in the morning the night she’d waited for the call that her father’s Ohio River tug had finally come into port in Wheeling. Not a tug. He’d never run a tug. Bulk carrier. Towboats pushing their boxes of coal. She could hear the creak of the wood, the way the house received and answered the motion.

“You need more ones?” Dot asked, peering into the caramel tin, then at the cooler like a general surveying supplies.

“I’m fine,” Claire said. The rubber band around a lopsided stack cut into the side of her smallest finger. She had given the last of her quarters to a girl for a handful of costume rings and watched the girl slip them on, two on one finger, her nails bitten to moons. A dealer had come first thing, a thin man with the kind of flat curiosity that scraped her nerves raw. He’d moved through the rooms as if he were smearing butter with his eyes, already calculating resale. He had tried to buy the whole box of her mother’s reports cards from 1939 to 1946. “People like ephemera,” he’d said. “The handwriting, the grades, the teacher’s comments. It’s elegiac.” He’d said elegiac in Ada, Ohio, as if it were normal to declare elegies over addition tables. She had said no, that those weren’t for sale, feeling then like someone declaring something too late, like she was scribbling Not For Sale on a map after the borders had moved.

Now the flow of bodies had settled into its afternoon pace. People drifted in with Kroger bags for their scores and iced coffee cups sweating down their wrists. They talked to each other like conspirators, then turned to her like supplicants: Will you take five? Will you throw in the stand? Is this real? Does this work? Where is the other matching one? And all the while, they were carrying her—no, not her, she told herself, not her—carrying the furniture of her childhood out the front door into the washed-out light.

Inside, a little dog yipped. Someone shushed it. A question: “How much for the Pyrex?” Over the heads in the doorway she could see the primary colors lined up on the buffet like obedient children. They’d been in the lazy Susan cabinet by the stove for as long as she’d known the word cabinet. Sundays had been casseroles in those dishes, the red one for chili mac, the blue for scalloped potatoes. She could see the atom pattern on the nesting bowls that had seemed futuristic when she was little, space age glamour, their orbit lines now wobbled from somebody else’s scrub pad.

“A set like that?” Dot clicked her tongue. “Thirty-five is fair. Forty if they hem and haw.” Dot had been a school secretary for forty years. She spoke in policies and lunch counts.

“I wrote thirty,” Claire said. She had a notebook with the bigger items listed. Piano, two hundred. Dining table with leaves, a hundred. Maple dresser, sixty. Those numbers had seemed tentative there on the page, a breath she hadn’t released. Now they sat like fact. A woman with the Pyrex sidled up to the table.

“Thirty,” the woman said. “Cash.” She flapped the bills so Claire could see the President as if to remind her that money was serious, that humans had faces.

“Thirty,” Claire said, and the woman grinned, satisfied, as if Claire had learned something in that moment that mattered and she, the woman, had taught her. The woman had a butterfly tattoo that popped bright against the soft of her wrist, just above a bracelet that said faith in hammered script. “My grandma had these,” she said, and for a second, Claire was ridiculous—she wanted to say that it mattered that they’d been here, that they had belonged to a particular person in this place, that they had known the rattle of this particular stove’s pilot light, that their usefulness was not generic but shaped by her mother’s biscuits—mine only if you can eat them with your hands, she’d say, no forks—and the gravy sweat on a July night. But the woman had already swept them up and turned away and the living room swallowed her, and the thought dissolved like sugar into the air.

Dot squeezed her knee. “How you doing?”

“I’m fine,” Claire said. It was so easy to say. In the past year she had said it the way she’d once said hello. Her mother had been small those last months, her cheeks pouchy, the skin on her hands papery. The winter had stolen sounds—fallen leaves drawn over like a muffler—and the spring had made everything smell like damp concrete and bleach. “It’s just a house,” Claire had told her daughter, Jules, on the phone three nights ago, when Jules had offered for the third time to come down from Ann Arbor to help. “We box some things, we tag some things, strangers paw through, we close the door.” She’d been sitting in the dining room at midnight with a row of glass birds lined up by color on the table, sunlight to cobalt. The little blue one—hm, how much?—had a beak chipped so precisely it hurt.

“Want me to go check on the piano?” Dot asked. “That music teacher from the high school said she’d be by after lunch with a girl who takes lessons.”

Claire smelled the piano before she turned to look. Or not the piano—its shellac—and the absence that surrounded it. When she was nine, she had thought the piano held a kind of breath, that when the fallboard was down it was holding it, and when you slid the fallboard back the room exhaled. The spinet had sat against the front window as long as she could remember, the window that looked out onto Pansy Street’s single maple, which had used to be two maples, the other cut down after the ash borers came through. It was absurd, but she had been surprised four nights ago to find that the piano had a shade of darker carpet beneath it, a rectangle of time. She had expected the floor to be neutral and found it marked, the room keeping inventory in its own way.

“It’s fine,” she said, then, “Yes, if you don’t mind.”

Dot laced her fingers together and pushed up out of the lawn chair with that soft grunt all her friends made now when they rose, as if they were accepting a fact about themselves. She step-thumped down the hall. Claire watched a man come tipping down the front steps with the pinkish lamp that had been in her mother’s bedroom. He had it tucked under his arm like it was a wide baby. The lamp was full of shells encased in resin, a Florida souvenir bought in Daytona the one spring break her parents had taken after her father retired from the paper mill and they drove to see the ocean in his rusted Crown Vic, a cooler of Schweppes between them like a promise. The lamp always looked sticky. He held it as if it might ooze and he might lose it.

“Ten,” he panted, testing her.

“Twelve,” she said.

He looked wounded, then delighted when he could hand her a ten and two ones as if she’d kept him from some kind of moral hazard. People like to be corrected, she thought. They liked to pretend at decency, to be stopped on the way to taking too much. He tucked the lamp into the crook of his elbow like a football and hustled off. Something in his run said he was late for something else, had fit this dash for a lamp into a day already made of errands.

Inside, the babble swelled and thinned. A small boy in a Batman T-shirt came out holding a gravy boat like a spaceship. He drifted to the end of the driveway and tilted it to the sky as if on a long mission.

“My sister says the writing desk was hers,” came a voice, sharp and distant. Her phone shuddered again in her back pocket: Dan. She had nearly set his ringtone as a bell tower peal, the way he phoned now like a reminder of something he had put in storage and wanted to make sure remained in storage somewhere.

She fished the phone out and saw the thread of texts he’d been sending since morning, in his seesawing punctuation: How’s it going ? Any dealers ? Save Dad’s tie rack if you find it ? Fell asleep on couch last night, sorry I didn’t call. Did you find my Cub Scout kerchief? She could hear his voice in every question, a voice that never really landed. He was in Dallas now, his unit of American life measured in planned communities and communal pools with cabanas. He would not be here; he had said it plainly at first and then softly every time she’d spoken to him, the way a person apologized to a waiter for an unavailable dish.

Pictures, she texted back. She took three: the line of people in the hall between the dining room and the bedroom, the card table with its tin and brownies, the living room from the corner nearest the cold-air return, the angle she’d sat at with her knees drawn up when she was eight and had gotten to stay up late on New Year’s Eve. The piano’s bench was pulled out. She could see a girl there, hair gathered in a messy topknot, her sandals dangling from her toes. The music rack was up, empty. The fallboard was open, keys exposed like a row of smiles of different moods. The piano’s name, Baldwin, was on there in gold under the dust. Dot stood at a diagonal behind the girl, as if that might direct a better price into being.

A car rolled by slowly, eyes inside appraising. The civic mourning map had unspooled since her mother’s obituary ran, a small paragraph between the DeLongs' lost dog and St. Paul’s rummage sale. Does the paragraph fit the person? she had thought when the funeral home emailed her the proof. Preceded in death by her husband, survived by two children and one granddaughter. Volunteer at the library. Avid baker and gardener. Her mother was more and less than that. Preceded by: a sleepy boy who once put a sewing needle through his heel and limped for a week without telling anyone because he thought the pain was punishment for the nail he’d stolen from the hardware aisle at Ben Franklin. Survived by: a daughter who changed her shirt in this very hallway every Sunday morning when she was thirteen so she and her mother wouldn’t fight about the neckline. It was all so ordinary and that ordinariness had felt like a fence that kept them alive.

“Do you take Venmo?” A woman with short hair and an angular frown thrust her phone at Claire like a weapon, her glasses sliding down her nose to make room for indignation.

“Cash or check,” Claire said. She held up the sign as if proof of writing were proof of a code. The woman exhaled, a sound of disapproval that made Claire think of her mother tapping the side of a mixing bowl with a spoon, then the noise of batter being scraped up from the bottom. She wondered if this woman’s mother, somewhere, listened to her breathe, understood that what she meant was not really annoyance but fear, or longing, or hunger.

“Fine,” the woman said, and produced an old checkbook from the bottom of her bag as if as a demonstration. “Fine,” she repeated, and turned on her heel back into the house like a person who had won a small thing.

Dot reappeared, flushed across her cheeks. “She plays ‘Fur Elise’ like she’s been born waiting for it,” she said, tilting her head toward the living room. “The teacher says the girl’s people can come with a truck at four if you’re willing. Two hundred is fair, I told them.”

Claire nodded, but her throat ached. It was so humiliating, the ache. She had always pruned her own softness, kept it balled in her fist. The piano had been insisted upon. It had been the argument and the compromise. Her mother, who had grown up with one in the parlor and who had loved to say parlor with her own eyebrows up, had declared that her daughter would play. Not enough girls got music anymore, she’d told Claire’s father after supper the night she’d taken her first envelopes from sorting books in the library’s basement. The tips of her fingers had been gray with dust that night. It was not that she thought Claire would be a pianist, or that she wanted recitals; it was a bulwark against a life where there were only chores and school and overboiled coffee. “You need something else in your head,” she’d said. “Something to lay your hands on that isn’t work.”

Claire had laid her hands on things her whole life: steering wheels, grocery carts, her daughter’s hot scalp the night Jules had thrown up sausage and peppers into the green bowl and sobbed because some boy named Martin had called her a bitch at the ninth-grade dance. She had laid her hands on her own mother’s elbow to help her up from the toilet last winter while the heat clicked unpersuaded from the vent. She had not laid her hands on the piano in twenty years. And yet it had been there the way a mountain is there, shaping the weather in the house. How could you measure such a thing? In the deepening of the carpet, the rectangular shade where sunlight had decided for four decades. In the nick on middle C where Dan had dropped a quarter as a joke and then cried because he thought he’d broken the most expensive thing in the world.

A girl’s fingers tripped on the keys just then, a tumble of notes. The sound was tinny, the spinet a container of sound not meant for big rooms, but the music had a molten center, and somehow every person in the house stilled a little, even if they didn’t know they were stilling. Claire stood up from the card table without realizing it. The screen door hit her edge-of-thigh when she walked through, a sting. People glanced at her and away. A man bent over a box of National Geographics paused with one in his hands and the yellow border made a frame around his bent head. The girl at the bench had two wisps of hair that had escaped the clip that held everything else up. Her feet were bare. Her pinky fingers were cocked up a little like birds listening.

“That’s nice,” Claire said, as if praising a school project, and then hated herself for the ridiculous thinness of it.

The teacher, a woman in a denim jacket with two lapel pins—eighth notes, black and glossy—touched the girl’s shoulder. “You could play the Schubert,” she said. “Just the first page. Let her hear the sound.”

Claire said, “It doesn’t stay in tune like it used to,” and the teacher nodded with a shared knowledge of age and its humiliations.

“We have a guy,” the teacher said. “He works on the Steinway at the university. He’ll come out.”

There was something embarrassing about the word Steinway. It felt like bringing a summer guest into the room where you’d stored the Christmas wrapping paper. But the girl’s fingers had found something gentle, then, almost embarrassed themselves, a run that made Claire feel for a second the weight of her daughter when Jules had been an infant and had fallen asleep on Claire’s chest in the blue chair by the window while the late afternoon light put a wet sheen on the porch. The girl turned her wrist and the air changed temperature. The house held it.

“We’ll come with the truck at four,” the teacher said again, softly. “If you’re willing.”

Claire put her hand on the edge of the piano where the varnish had worn to a matte from a lifetime of touch. The veneer had always had a ripple, as if it were showing its own grain in three dimensions. When the piano movers had brought it in in 1978, her mother had fed them ham sandwiches at the kitchen table and scolded them for trying to lift with their backs. Those men had been softened in her memory by the usually sharp people in her life, softened by the way they grinned at the sound the piano made when the dolly went over the threshold, that dull open-throat noise. One of them had swiped his palm across his forehead with the heel of his hand and said Whoo, like a prayer.

“All right,” Claire said. “Four.”

As if she had given permission for the afternoon to unspool, the house resumed its bustle. The boy with the gravy boat zoomed it across the porch railing and made pew-pew sounds and then set it carefully into his mother’s open tote like a landing. A college-looking couple, both in white sneakers and tenderly ironic T-shirts, fingered the macramé plant holder that had been in the kitchen window with a pothos in it for twenty years. They said mid-century to each other with reverence about things Claire knew her mother had bought at Rite Aid.

On the hallway wall, behind where the coat rack had been, was the shadow of the house. Claire had stood there at midnight two nights earlier, her hand pressed to the yellowed paint, the plaster cool against the tips of her fingers. The marks were quiet facts: the shape of something that had been, the absence of it now. It occurred to her that an estate sale was also a map: this had gathered here, that had been held there. She’d found a hardened nail under the lip of the hall carpet that Dan had hidden as a boy to play at being a cobbler. She left it there, a secret under other people’s feet.

In the bedroom, two women in nurse scrubs pulled the cedar chest from the foot of the bed, one at each end, and murmured to each other in a conspiratorial hush. “Lift with your knees,” Claire said because it was impossible not to hear her mother in herself in situations like this. She hissed the words through her teeth as if that would keep them from sounding like bossiness. They grinned at her in unison like guilty sisters and tucked their knees in and pushed up. The cedar chest had belonged to her mother’s mother and smelled like the inside of an old closet, dry and a little sweet. Inside were four afghans, all made in the seventies, each afghan a sentence that her grandmother had thought the house would understand. Claire had priced them absurdly low, hoping they would find homes where someone might hold them to their face in winter and inhale the stored springs.

“Excuse me,” the taller of the two women said, and held up a floor-length garment bag she’d found standing like a sentry by the closet. “Is this for sale?”

The bag was light gray, the kind dry cleaners sent you home with, meant as a favor and then a reminder. Claire knew without unzipping. “No,” she said, too quickly. Her wedding dress was in there. There had been a time, soon after she and Tom had separated, when she had thought of taking it out into the backyard and setting it on fire. She had imagined the crepe and lace surrendering in a rush, an undoing like furrowing a field. Of course she had never done it. Her mother had cleaned it after the divorce, had fluffed it as if preparing it for a new appointment. Her mother had brushed the sleeves as if smoothing a child’s hair. “Not for sale,” she said again, quiet, trying to pull her voice back from the edge.

“Sure thing,” the woman said, cheeks blotching faintly. “Sorry.”

“You’re fine,” Claire said, and then felt again the warm humiliating rush of being a person about whom so much could be said: you’re fine, you’re not fine. She folded the top of the garment bag down over itself and pushed it into the back of the closet so only the gray of it showed like a cloud.

Back in the living room, a man had the maple dresser halfway out of the hall. Claire could see the outline of his stomach pushing at his T-shirt, at the words Mechanicsburg Mustang Pride. The dresser slid on a furniture skid she didn’t know they still had. He had to angle it to get it past the door jamb without taking a chunk out. As he leaned into it, something thumped inside the drawer, a subtle soft sound. He paused and slid the top drawer halfway out with the toe of a boot. A thin envelope lay at the back, unassuming. It was the color of manila folders. It had a red border around the edge, like some flirty airmail letter from the movies. The man reached for it and then stopped, eyes flicking to Claire, as if waiting for a cue that didn’t exist on the poster board on the card table.

“Probably junk,” he said.

“Probably,” she said. She wanted to say Let me see that, with a bright smile that did not contain the blood rush of panic she felt, but she couldn’t find the tone. He offered it then as if he had been practicing this gesture all his life, and she took it as if the paper might crumple in her grip. In the upper left-hand corner, written in a handwriting she did not know but that was both neat and a little swaggering, was E. No stamp, no address. The flap was tucked, not sealed; it looked as if someone had slid it just under to keep it from opening its mouth.

“You can keep it out of the price,” the man said, with what could have been gallantry.

“It’s not—” she started, then stopped. She did not know what it was. She slid her finger beneath the flap and unfolded the single sheet. The paper was lined, blue lines faint from having been in the dark. The words were in that same hand. They didn’t float or push; they had settled there. Before she read more than two sentences she knew she would have to sit down. She perched on the piano bench tiles of light dappled on her forearms. Someone behind her was debating a quilt. The letter was dated June 27, 1972, three weeks after her nineteenth birthday, a date that had made no special imprint in her memory. She felt as if she were looking up to see if it was raining and found that it had been, for a while, quietly, unexpected.

Ellie, it began. The name her father used in his jokes, the name strangers reached for when they wanted to claim a closeness. Ellie. I will be at the Greyhound in Columbus Tuesday night on the 8:45 if you want to go like you said. I have the money for two tickets to Cincinnati and from there we’ll see. There’s a room someone knows of. There’s a job where I am. You could type, you always said so. You said you would bring one dress and one you could wear and we’d buy the rest at the store. I can carry your case. If you don’t come, I will go. No questions. I don’t want to be here when he comes in from the boat. You said—well, it doesn’t matter what you said. If you don’t come there won’t be anybody to say it to. J.

Claire had to look up from the page and into the room to keep herself from sinking into the yellow of the letter. The girl at the piano pressed one finger experimentally into a low note and watched the hammer lift like an animal sniffing. Outside, a truck downshifted; the sound had a summons in it, a step-there, step-there, like the sound of trombones on a high school field. Someone called, “Can you take the front leg off that lamp?” The sunlight had found the dust in the air in the cleft of the late afternoon. J, she thought. June 1972. Before the picture of her parents in the kitchen where he is slicing tomatoes for BLTs and grinning like a man pleased by a simple invention. After the picture of her mother in front of a Chevrolet with a scarf tied over her hair, the scarf’s ends pointing like hope.

She looked back down. Without thinking she flipped the page to see if there were more, a postscript, but there wasn’t. She flipped the envelope and ran her finger along the inside lip as if there might be crumbs of more words. She had a thought, absurd and rooted: what if it is for someone else, another E. There had, after all, been other E people in Ada in 1972. Elaine, whose house had the brick porch and whose father ran a tire shop. Edna, who scolded the wheat from the bread until it was only white. But the dresser had been her mother’s from the beginning. It had been moved up these stairs by men who wheezed at the top and joked about collapsed lungs. It had been used for scarves and folded slips and the nightgowns her mother had kept when they went out of fashion, mock turtleneck and floor-length, with white crocheted collars that held their shape the way the women had held theirs. It had not, to her knowledge, held other women’s letters.

“Find something spicy?” Dot sidled up, drawn by the closeness of any two people over an object. Her face softened when she saw Claire’s mouth. Dot had that uncanny ability to read the emotion rather than the words.

“It’s nothing,” Claire said, which was the strangest thing she had said all day because it was everything. She slid the letter back into its envelope, nested the flap, and tucked it under the rubber band of her notebook, as if inventory were a way to keep it from blowing away. She felt silly then for wanting to tuck it into her shirt like a breastplate.

The man corrected his grip on the dresser and shouldered it forward. “Want me to pay for that out there?” he called back to her over his shoulder, balancing his rear end against the door frame to pivot.

“Sixty,” she said, and he nodded and groaned with a kind of satisfaction she recognized. Why was physical pain so satisfying to watch when something was being accomplished? Why was labor itself such a proof? She thought: she will never know what choice she didn’t make. She thought: maybe she didn’t go to Columbus. Maybe she did and came back on the nine in the morning. Maybe J stood with his hands knuckled into his coat pockets and watched the door swing and knew a fact about her mother her daughter had never been permitted to know: that she had the capacity to hurt someone in order to remain where she was.

By three, the heat had settled over the porch with a weight that made silences click. The sun slid behind the sycamore across the street and threw a wobbly shade over the last of the chairs lined near the driveway. A teenage boy with his hair like a bush had put three sports trophies—basketball, bowling, and a gold plastic horseshoe—on one of the kitchen chairs he was trying to buy as a set. He wanted to tell her things. He was bursting with telling. “We’re moving into a place with two living rooms,” he said. “My mom said they call one a family room, but only if the TV is a certain size. She said if you call both their real names together, ‘living family,’ it’s too on the nose.”

Claire smiled and wrote down on her pad: chairs, four, thirty. He handed her three tens neatly; he had a wallet with a clear section for a school ID. He held it between two fingers like a trick. “You need help moving anything else out?” he asked, breathless with his own willingness.

“Thank you,” she said, because what else could you say to someone so eager to slot himself into usefulness. He takes money that clinks, she thought, remembering the feel of allowances in her jeans pocket as a girl, the way coins had a shape that told you their worth without a picture.

Clouds rose behind the white gazebo in the park at the end of the block that the town used for midnight fireworks every July. Last week a sign had gone up under the gazebo: School supply drive here. The week before, a sign about the swim team car wash. In the grocery store, women waited in the freezer aisle and said, “Have you heard?” as if there were only one thing to hear, and there always was. Last spring it had been Mrs. Comstock’s knee. In winter it had been a boy two streets over who had been found in his father’s truck, cold. “Good boy,” the cashier with the bob and the superior laugh had said, shaking her head, as if by pronouncing the adjective in past tense she could make it weigh more.

At ten to four, the music teacher returned with a man and a teenage boy. The boy wore one of those harnesses that made him look like he was there to be safe more than he was there to pick things up. The man had a dolly that spoke of trauma and care simultaneously, all those straps and squeaks and the respect it demanded. He tilted the dolly back and forth on its big rubber wheels to show it he was in charge.

“You sure?” Dot whispered, as if this were now a secret.

“I’m sure,” Claire said. She didn’t want to watch, and she wanted to watch. She went to the kitchen and picked up the magnet with the Cedar Point roller coaster on it and took it off the fridge and set it on the counter, then picked it up again and tucked it into the back pocket of her shorts. She ran the faucet and then turned it off without getting a glass because the water that came out of these pipes still had a copper aftertaste, a taste she both loved and hated for the way it said home. She could hear the men strapping the piano, hear the boy’s exhale every time they tipped it even a half inch, hear the teacher say, “Watch the corner.”

She leaned her forehead against the door of the fridge and felt the cool aluminum through the magnet residue. The letter under her notebook tugged at her awareness like a small child at a hem. You said, J had written. My mother had said. She’d said, Claire thought. She might have said, I will. She might have said, I want to go. She might have said, He doesn’t love me. She might have said the word want, which Claire could count on one hand the number of times she’d heard her say. Her mother had wanted things like hot coffee and a soft nightgown and the garden club to approve her compost bin. Her mother had not said what she wanted in any way that required someone else to bend to her wanting. Maybe she had not gone to Columbus because she could not say want in front of the bus.

“Got it,” the man said, and the room made the same sound it had made the day the piano came in, only this time the sound went in the other direction, a hole opening, the gravity reversing, the air glancing off the door jam as it went out. The men pivoted and for a spilt second the piano floated in space, a thing that, in its leaving, revealed how it had been not only an instrument but a wall, a weight, a companion. Claire put her hand on the frame of the kitchen doorway and felt the house vibrate with the effort. The corner of the piano left a half moon in the summer-sealed paint and the man who had been so careful swore his apology under his breath, and Claire said, “It’s fine,” and meant it in that moment with a generosity that surprised her.

On the porch, the dolly protested and gave in. The teenage boy leaned into it with his whole body, his toes dug into his hoses for purchase. The teacher put her hand on Claire’s arm as if to tether her to now. “I’ll send you a picture when she plays it at her recital,” she said, and something in the specificity of that—her recital, as if there would be only one—undid the knots in Claire’s chest for a second. She pulled Dot’s extra tissue from her pocket, blew her nose as quietly as a person crying can.

When the men came back for the bench, the boy, grinning as if he had just done a stunt, said to Claire, “Want me to take those hymnals that were in it?”

“You can have them,” she said. The hymnal had been stolen from St. Paul’s in 1963; her mother had laughed every time she had pulled it out of the bench for a guest’s sheet music. “They can afford it,” she’d say, as if God were a big ledger with pluses and minuses and this particular theft, this vengeance of music and use, was in the black.

After the piano left, the room shed itself in a rush, like a person taking off heavy jewelry. The picture rails looked indecent without their frames. The rug looked smaller as if it had stood taller to meet what had stood upon it and now could lie down. The cash tin was three quarters full and then, mysteriously, up and down as the afternoon eddied. People paid, and Claire stacked the bills without looking at the Presidents, their faces an abstraction of currency rather than people who had once held letters in their own hands and tucked them under a rubber band.

At four thirty, a woman with damp hair and a precise, controlled smile set the white cookie jar on the card table and tapped the lid with one fingernail. It made a noise like a bell in the wrong room. “My mother had one just like this,” she said. The jar had been a permanent resident on Claire’s childhood counter, fat and cheerful, with a rooster painted on its belly. It had held oatmeal cookies and then buttons and then, in the last decade, nothing at all; it had held the idea of cookies. The woman put twenty dollars in the tin and then hugged the jar to her with her upper arms as if to keep from dropping it and in the process made it an infant. “This is the last one,” she said softly, and Claire didn’t know what the sentence meant but it felt biblical.

Thunder muscle-flexed in the west, not loud yet but present like a neighbor’s bath running through the pipes. The sky above the maple had that strange golden color it sometimes got before an evening storm, a light like honey poured into the bucket of air. People looked up when they walked to their cars and said to strangers, “Uh-oh,” and smiled the way people smiled in grocery stores when something tiny and satisfying connected them—a melon selection, their carts threading—and then dissolved.

“Take any last looks you want,” Dot said at five, coming in from the porch and setting her hand flat on the wall at the height of her own chest as if saying goodbye to the house herself. “I can’t look at that spot where the piano was without pulling at my eyebrows.”

“It’s just a floor,” Claire said, and then, because she was infected with the day and could not stop herself from revealing in small ways, she added, “I never thought it would smell like that under there. Like a cardboard box.”

Dot nodded as if this were a known part of a known ritual. “I brought you a casserole,” she said. “It’s in my Frigidaire. It’s lasagna. You can put it in your Frigidaire and take it with you, and then when you come back tomorrow with the charity haul, you’ll have something besides whatever it is you eat when you get home and put your arms on your table and say, ‘Well.’”

“I didn’t know I did that,” Claire said.

Dot patted her arm. “Everybody does that,” she said. “We’re all beating the same drum,” she added cryptically, which was one of her Dot-isms that Claire had learned to accept like round stones along the path.

Outside, a woman in a baseball cap with an apple embroidered on the front loaded the dining room chairs into a Subaru with a hatch she had propped with a broom handle. The woman’s son, maybe twelve, held the broom as if in a ceremony, solemn with his job. Claire found herself wanting to bless them in some way, to put a hand on their shoulders and declare passage. She settled for writing down on her pad: table, 100, and drawing a line through the word as if canceling a prescription.

When most of the people had gone, the house rang with a new terrible music. The hum of the fridge was too loud. The air conditioner sighed and seemed to say you there you there you there. The clock over the stove ticked with a hubris she resented. She went into the bedroom and opened the top dresser drawer all the way, then pulled the drawer above it the two inches it would come, so she could squeeze her hand into the back of the first drawer and find a lump of something. As a girl, this had been a delight, finding the secret compartment of her mother’s underwear drawer where small things were kept: a stack of S&H Green Stamps, a pair of cuff links that had been her grandfather’s even though she had no memory of a shirt to hold them, the keys to the shed. Now there was nothing there but dust, the color of it lighter on her fingertips than in the drawer, as if the place had been letting go of its own a little at a time all along.

She took the enamel measuring spoons from the kitchen drawer—the red had chipped off the tablespoon to show the white underneath, the half-teaspoon’s handle was bent—and set them in her purse without ceremony, without the part of herself that needed to narrate all her moves. “What? I’m keeping the measuring spoons,” she would say to Dan when he asked her about the big things like the bed. “What, you wanted them?” she would say to him, smiling, and he’d say no, of course not, and he’d mean it, and then later he would think of making pancakes on a Saturday, and he’d wish he had thought to ask.

The storm finally cracked and then opened. Rain came in huge fat coins that plastered themselves to the driveway and then smeared and decided to be rivers. The light went white, then green, and then sated itself. Dot appeared in the back doorway with the lasagna under a little tin foil tent in both hands and an umbrella stuck under her arm. She looked delighted and appalled in equal measures. “Time to bring the house its bath,” she said.

Claire took the casserole. It was heavy in a way that did not annoy her, a way that said human generosity comes by the pound, here, take it. She set it on the counter and the rain drummed hard enough that the noise from the park became a wall. The gutters had long since decided to be purely decorative. Water fell from the eaves in beads and then strings and then a sheet. Someone’s trash can on Pansy Street went by, riding the water like an animal that had remembered it could move.

She walked through the rooms then, as if she could gather them one last time and push them into her pocket like lint. The guest room that had not been a guest room in fifteen years. The little bathroom where the mirror had a brown spot in the lower right corner like a permanent bruise and the turquoise tile her mother had used to perennially declare as “retro” when new friends looked around and said oh. The closet where the winter coats had hung their prayers. The hall where, when Claire was a girl and was put to bed before nine, she had lain her head at the foot of her bed so she could listen to her parents watch Johnny Carson in the living room, the applause from the TV fizzing like a bottle opened after midnight. She had thought then that adults chose adulthood exactly for those moments, the hush, the loosening, the sound through the wall that was proof of being included, if only in the listening.

She sat on the living room floor finally, the carpet flattened, the rectangle where the piano had been its own shade of living. She put her back against the wall, her toes pointed toward the paradox of the doorway, the place that had been so much threshold in her life. If she craned her neck she could see the water ribbon down the top step. For a second, by the light of the storm, she allowed herself the theater: that the house itself was relieved to be shucked of so much, to be a shell with only itself to keep company. That it was like a body after a bath, that the ache it felt was good ache. She thought of the letter again, of the nine o’clock bus and the woman whose future took two tracks and finally chose only the one. The letter was a fact under a rubber band, and also it was a story, which would be its second life now, inside Claire and, if she decided, inside Jules.

Her phone buzzed. Jules. Everything going okay? You want me to come down tomorrow and help with the pick-up?

The rain dappled the screen. She heard Dot in the kitchen opening drawers, the way a woman does in a house that is not hers and yet is also forever somewhat hers. Claire typed, It’s okay, baby. Bring yourself next weekend, not the stuff. We’ll go to the lake.

She looked up as she hit send. The light off the rain in the front yard flickered a little, blue, and then steadied. A cardinal landed on the telephone wire across the street, soggy and adamant. Somewhere, faint, she could hear the clack of a truck bed closing, laughter—it was always laughter at that pitch, a little too loud to be actually happy and then, so, honestly trying to be. She pressed the envelope flat with her palm, felt the soft tug of paper resisting the smoothness. She did not open it again. She knew the sentences by heart already, the way any person who has ever waited at a particular time at a particular door knows the air in her lungs.

When the rain slowed to a generous drizzle, she stood and stretched and walked to the front door and set her palm on the place people touched without thinking, the place that had wobbled under possibly thousands of human tips of fingers leaving or entering. The paint there had the faint shine of oils, the way contact polishes what it loves. She turned the brass knob; it was warm, and then cooled in her grip, ready to be any weather’s. She stepped onto the porch and listened to the water as it slid off the maple’s leaves, as it pulsed from the downspout and found the little river it always found along the edge of Pansy Street, gathering with all the other day’s spendings.

Dot said, “Lasagna?”

Claire laughed, a short bark that contained, unexpectedly, the start of delight. “Lasagna,” she said. She lifted the foil corners and let the steam kiss her face and closed the door behind her with her heel, the latch catching the way it had caught all her life, a sound like a hand clapping once in approval.
