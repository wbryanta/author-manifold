He opened the shop before dawn the way he always had, bleary light finding his hand in the dark, the switch that hummed, the single bulb touching tools and varnished scraps with a gold thumb. The bench was a fine clutter, and he paused to look at it the way a man looks at a sleeping child; this would be the last of it, the last time he smoothed his palm over the nubby red felt strip, the rubber wedges, the little bottle of graphite, the spool of braided wire in case a bass string snapped and went dead in his hands like a fish. The hammer lay on its side, the fit of the head to the socket as familiar as his wife’s shoulder had been in that hollow of years. He picked it up and rolled the pebbled handle against his skin. He was seventy-three. His knuckles had grown heavy and the pads of his fingers had turned numb. When he pinched a paper, he could feel its presence but not the knife of it. That had begun happening a few winters back, and he had sworn to nobody he would stop before he dropped something into a piano and couldn’t feel whether he had got it out.

Outside the morning was thin and mean, a fall frost pointing its needles into every thing that had been left damp overnight. The county road was black and chalked. The station wagon looked more gray than white in that light, the mildew rails along the rubber of the windows catching on his fingernails as he pulled the door. There were coffee rings in a constellation on the floor mat and the velvet triangle of last summer’s dead moth glued to the dash by accident and then left there on purpose, superstitious, a sign that the next mile was no different than the last.

He had a route, and the route had had him. On the hanging clipboard the map of their county—gull-shaped, two wings of farms and one wing of pine—was checkered with his pencil Xs from years of going in circuits that the sun itself could have envied for their constancy. This was the last round, though he had not announced it to more than three people. He had said the word retirement once to the young man at the parts supplier in town, and the man didn’t look up from his screen as if the word had no meaning in a throat. He had mentioned it to Sarah at the diner, and she had reached to touch his arm, saying Sweetheart, no, just as if it were news of a move across the ocean. And he had said it to himself in the mirror once, in January, when he heard the high E of his own piss hitting the bowl and could not remember whether E had always been that.

“It’s time,” he said now, to the bench, to the bell jar with the moth collection that had been his mother’s, to the broad-shouldered old Steinway action he kept on a shelf and took down sometimes to show school kids, as if anyone brought school kids anymore. “Last round.” He lay the fork on the bench for a second. He bent and took it to his lips and gave it to his head, A’s whispering into him through bone, his ear still keen for that note he had known like the name of a neighbor you wave to in the dark. A440. A’s pale straw voice.

By the time he pulled onto the county road there was enough light to see the edges of ditches. The pastures were furred with cold, as if some opinionless animal had breathed all night. He could name the farms by the sounds he knew lived in their parlors, the clanging bass-burble of the Cable-Nelson at the Garrison’s, the black-and-brown mutter of the Hoffmann upright at the Mercers’, which had a split in the board somewhere in the tenor and sang like a man with a bad tooth. He had tuned them when men came home from the fields and set their hats on the strap of the bench and plodded three hymns out of duty, their wives’ hands on their shoulders saying Thank you, and thank God. He had tuned them when girls came home from the city with hair cut too short, and when those same girls came home with babies and men who did not sing, and he had tuned them through winters when the fog came under the doors and his breath made bodies in front of his face for whole minutes. He had tuned them when his wife was sick and he thought if he could make a middle C sit down and hush, the world would follow. He had tuned them when his son was small and not yet standing on the other side of something that could not be crossed.

At the Mercers’ the dog limped out and then thought twice, ashamed of the instinct to defend. Ruth stood in the doorway in her standing dress, the one that didn’t have the apron strings and had a collar like a schoolmarm. “Ed,” she said as if they had been in conversation. She had been a wristy girl who could keep a plate of deviled eggs from spilling in the front seat of a truck while bouncing up the lane. She was not wristy now. She reached for the railing with both hands when she stepped aside.

“It’ll be done before your program this afternoon,” he said. A joke. There had not been a program in her living room for eight years, at least. The last one had been her granddaughter, the little red-headed one, banging out Carol of the Bells while the two dogs barked at each other through the glass of the storm door until her son-in-law had finally stood up with a sound that was done of things. The girl had not taken to it. He had known it in the first two minutes, the set of her jaw like a cow’s sometimes when you put your hand to their flank too suddenly.

Ruth smiled the side smile that made her eye look like his wife’s had when something had hurt too much to show all of it. “I’ll put on coffee,” she said.

He set the bag on the floorboards next to the Mercury lamp with the bent shade that had been bending since he first came, and moved the stool with his knee. The smell in the room was the hooligan sheet of old tobacco and wool and russet apples. The Hoffmann’s keys had been re-covered in plastic sometime in the seventies as a civic gesture against the elephants, and the top note of the second Bb had yellowed on its right edge from contact with one finger more than the others. He’d tried to catch Ruth playing once, to notice what she loved, but she always claimed only to dust it. The felt was brick-red, old-sun-stained. He opened the fallboard like a casket, slow and respectful, the noise of it a lid of breath.

They were all full of mice. His hands had grown to expect it and yet he still held his own breath until the sight of the kibbles of insulation and spilled cat litter and stained mahogany made itself known under the music desk. Here there was nothing but a nest in a corner, the way a hat might absently rest on a chair in the parlour as if someone had just gone into the kitchen for a drink. He lifted the front board and set it aside. He laid the felt strip like a sleeping policeman across the tenor section, pressing it into the gap that opened when you moved the action back. He shoved two wedges into the bass unisons. He set the fork in motion for himself, a courtesy, a menu whispered to the kitchen though you’d had the dish a thousand times and no one could hope to surprise you with it. He put the head of the hammer on the A’s pin and leaned into it, setting the pin before he asked it to turn, pushing it past and then letting it find its seat. The speaking length was not gummy. He pleased himself that he could still feel the torsion through the neck of hickory.

His eyes did their wandering. They always did. He could work by ear and by knuckle and still know what had been done in houses while he was elsewhere. The photograph in the frame that had a triangle of sun burned into its corner from years at this angle now showed a boy taller than he was holding a fish that must have been as long as an arm, and the boy was no longer a boy but the man who had said Done of things in the Christmas. The doilies had been taken off the mantel. There was a jar of coins with a roll of lottery tickets at the bottom like a wish. The clock on the shelf sang a sad three-click beat he would fix for Ruth after he finished. Once he had said to a man that he tuned the whole house, and the man had laughed and then stopped and then asked him to fix the hinge on the screen door because it squeaked the wrong note all evening when the summer people were here and he could not bear it.

When people asked how a man did it he would say you listen for the interference. You listen for the fight. A good fourth would have a beat slow enough to make you acutely aware of your own breath, and a fifth would seem satisfied rather than starved for something just beyond its mouth. Thirds were a delicate business, violence made pretty. You could clock a major third in the tenor at something like nine or ten beats per second if the weather was right. He had said the numbers into boys’ faces at the vocational school before they had taken auto shop instead, and had watched their eyes turn to him like horses’ eyes in a storm. He had known what he was teaching; the watching of the invisible. He had set his temperament over the D octave for years, not like the books taught, because he knew his ears were truest there. He had been, in his silent way, a heretic.

“You’ll have a cup,” Ruth said behind him. He did not turn. The kettle had a voice he could hear in the balance of the strings, a two-oh that might have been a G’s G if you were listening with your forehead.

“I don’t need—” he began, and then he remembered it was the last and he was not taking any more of their offerings away.

“I’ll sit,” she said, as if he had said something different. She set the cup on the little table near the front window and sat. He breathed. He leaned. He fiddled the pin. The string responded like a whinny: change, then settle, then show you its trick, which was to go sharp and then let you down like a friend. He was not yet a man who needed the app. He had one, for emergencies. He had used it once when his ear went woolly after a long drive in pealing weather; it had annoyed him with its sleeking rotor of a sound and made him feel like he’d left his glasses at a neighbor’s house.

“You won’t be coming back,” Ruth said into her cup. He could not see her face without turning, and he knew enough to keep his head where it was if he wanted what he was after.

“I don’t know,” he said. It was not entirely a lie. If he got to spring with all his teeth, he would have mornings like this again. But he would not take money after this. He would keep the pianos that mattered and come to them in the long dusk of his own.

“It’s all right,” she said. “There’s nothing to play. The girls don’t come up anymore. Crissy says it kills her back to get up the stairs with the baby. I told her I could take him. She won’t hear it.”

He raised the felt strip one key and brought up the three notes that would be one again. He tuned one to the other, smearing then narrowing, enjoying for a second the wet doubling that rose in the dull air like heat, then flicking the pin with his wrist to lock it to the one he had called right. Unisons were balm. In the old days he would test a novice by asking them to make a unison true in under five minutes with a roomful of talkers and the radio on. He had taken a boy once into a gym while basketballs made their own system of thunder and had said Now.

“How’s your ear,” Ruth asked.

He smiled at the board. “Decent,” he said. “Truth is, other people’s are worse. The world’s loud. I’m an island.”

She laughed, and when he finished, he lifted the tangle of the action and put it close to his face, to smell its breath and see whether any of the hammers had made grooves so mean they would bring tricks to his work. The B in the middle had a hitch, he saw, and he took the capstan with the little spoon spanner in his bag that had served a dozen pianos and half a hundred loaves of bread when his wife could not find a tool in her kitchen and turned it half a hair. He pressed the key. The let-off was early but he would leave it; the afternoon would bring humidity like a hint of love and it would all be better than it was now. And anyway, there was no one to go pianissimo through Chopin with her thumb on the outside of the scale. The B would voice only to the air of the house and to Ruth, if she let it.

He put his name in the ash between the wrest-plank and the plate where it always gathered, with his pocketknife. He did not write it where it would be found. It would be his to know, that he had been there. Then he took his own bill pad out and wrote PAID in the line at the bottom and left it on the lamp table under the coins.

“You haven’t told them, then,” Ruth said.

“No,” he said. He couldn’t say the word them without thinking of the two people that word had meant, and how now it meant every one besides himself. He could tell Ruth meant the ones on the route who watched for his car, who had his month in their head and had begun to age into the perilous habit of planning their baking for his arrival. He had once been late to a house and been greeted with cookies the temperature of a small creature in a man’s pocket.

She stood halfway to see him out, then sat again, because standing cost too many pennies and she was not yet in the habit of being that generous with herself in a day. “When you want soup,” she said, “I’ll make it. It’ll be all of mine and none of anybody else’s, and I won’t tell it to the church women. They are not what they were.”

He nodded, and for a second he let his right hand fall to the keys without the left, without thought, to test whether that sourness in the scales he had killed for her would come back. It did not. The Hoffmann sighed. He closed the lid. He thought about lifting it and playing something very simple for her, a laugh of a piece, but his hands put his tools into the bag without his consent. They always had. Work first. Don’t sing.

He drove to the school with the radio’s old get-in-the-car jingle laid under his tires like a prayer. It surprised him that they had not boarded the place. The last time he had come, a teacher with a hawk on her shoulder—not a real one, a picture on a sweatshirt—had met him at the office and looked over her glasses as if he were twelve and on his way to the nurse for a paper cut. “The music program is discontinued,” she had said, making discontinued into a going word when it was always a being one. He had waited with the same patience he had waited when his father said in a shed, when he was ten, that the only way to be a man in the world was to do quickly what needed doing and to stand a long time by the things that did not.

The custodian who had taken him to the music room that day opened doorways now as if every door offended him by its closedness. He had a red flannel shirt that didn’t line up if you looked at the placket, and a belly that made a case for gravity as a fact. “You again,” he said, and if he grinned, it was as a favor, not a provocation.

“Me,” Ed said.

“You can leave your key with the secretary. Or give it to me. I’ll take her husband’s if he gets feisty.”

He put the key from his ring into the man’s hand as an act of trust he enjoyed. The room itself had the smell of bleached floor and pencil shaving. There were no children here; the small chairs had been stacked into a small city. The spinet in the corner hid under a cloth that remembered a table somewhere else. He had not been able to get a good tune out of this one in August because the rain had come every afternoon and had undone him. He had stood here then and said to the blank bumpy walls, You get what you get, and now he took the cloth off and listened to the way the soundboard woke up when he did, that almost-voice a man with wooden ears could hear if he put his own mouth close to the ribs and said a long vowel.

With pianos he always felt he was arriving in the middle of a conversation and had to find what had been said without insult. The spinet’s name was a name famous once, like Winifred or Duane. He laid his hand on its cheek. There had been children through here fourteen, fifteen, eighteen deep. He had watched a boy with a bruise on his cheek sit where this bench sat and find middle C with a speed that made Ed want to king him. The teacher had had a hand that tremored like his did now and had said with authority, Good boy, without drama. He wondered where the boy had gone. He wondered what all of them were in now, which is what his father said you were in when you were in a life.

He tuned the spinet to the kind of perfection it could sustain, which was like making a grocery list in cursive when you had arthritis. He pulled the half inch of red felt through and cast a glance around the room at the way the posters had clung to their staples all this time, Beethoven’s face made stupid by some teenager’s Sharpie whiskers, Schubert without his name, no one to answer for any of it anymore. He repaired a key that made a cluck when you let it go, a small piece of felt fallen away from the back check like a child’s mitten. He glued it with a dot from the little bottle he had made two decades ago out of hide, the way his father had shown him with a crank and a jam jar.

A man passing the open door stopped. He was thin and had a beard like a line at a horizon. “I took lessons here,” he said, and smiled as if he half knew the way a memory should look and half wanted to be somewhere else.

“From Mrs. Kline,” Ed said.

The man looked at him. “She had breath that would kill a fly,” he said. “Sorry,” he added to air.

“She’s the reason there are three pianos in this county,” Ed said. “Her husband made a stool with his hands.” He did not say her feet had been like a bird’s and she had walked as if she held a cake. He stayed still with the hammer on a pin as if it might disturb a good ghost.

The man came in. He bumped his thigh on a chair. “I can still do the thirds with my left hand while my right maps the treble,” he said, and he put his hands on the air over the keyboard the way boys sometimes put hands on a window in a house they once lived in from the porch, the hands trying the draft as if draft were a thing one could hold. “My wife plays to YouTube.” He looked up. “They show the notes,” he said.

“They always did,” Ed said, and the man tipped his head back and laughed and then nodded and was gone, and Ed finished what he was doing with a hum in his mouth that matched the beat rate he wanted, an old trick to keep pace.

By noon he was at the diner because that is what happened. The coffee there had been the same since his father took him in there the day the man who owned the feed store died. He remembered thinking he could hear the coffee. He had taken his wife there on the day a doctor said the word that had no superiority—no polysyllabic comfort. He tried to remember what she had ordered. She had never eaten like a finch; she had eaten like a woman with sisters, without apology.

Sarah slid him a plate without asking and then slid a glass of water that had a slice of lemon in it and then slid a smile across the counter through the bodies of truckers and a boy in a hoodie whose music he could hear like the water of beavers as he took his earbuds in and out to answer someone’s text. “You,” she said, as if he had been missing for a decade.

“Me,” he said. The plate had two eggs and a turf of hash browns and one pancake. “I didn’t order—”

She wrinkled her nose. “I can see your face. You need sugar.”

“I’m done with it,” he said. “Mostly.” He was saving sweet for the memory of his wife’s oatmeal cookies in the freezer that took a half a day to thaw and a whole night to eat if you were sad enough.

She leaned on both elbows and rolled the towel in her hand. “I heard a rumor,” she said. No one heard anything in this county. They felt it through the floor. It traveled up their hamstrings until their mouths said it out loud. “I heard you’re quitting.”

He forked the yolk and watched it run and re-gather. He was not a man of big conversational declarations but he did not lie to this woman who had wrapped his child’s birthday present when no one else had remembered without telling him it was her who’d done it. “I’m taking my last lap,” he said. He did not look up because there would be an expression and he would have to say something that would devalue it and he was not able.

She made a noise like a circle closing. “We needed you,” she said. She was not the church committee. She was a woman who, when the candy truck had stopped coming to town, found somehow a man who still sold big peppermint sticks and had driven twenty miles to obtain them for the Christmas parade on a night when he had had a fever and could not stop shaking.

“You did fine before,” he said. “You had men who tuned with pitch pipes and knives.”

“The church is in a fix,” she said, then waited and filled his coffee. “The organ’s skin is shedding. They say the chapel requires a capital campaign. I don’t know what that is. I have capitals. Are they going to ask me to bring them?”

“I’ll look at the piano this afternoon,” he said. He said it lightly, because it had weight in him. The church had been the good room in this county. Men took their hats off in there to let other air on their hair and to be noticed for the care of it. He had tuned in there the day his son had taken a bow so careful it made his father want to stand behind him and hold his head up by its silk. It came to him all the time in pieces. It came to him in boots when he drove past the curve where he had pulled over to make sure the bouquet of peonies did not roll from the back and bruise. It came to him in the smell of lemon oil. He had kept the boy’s program in the glove compartment for six months and then moved it to his drawer. He did not take it out the way men take out photographs after funerals; he took it out the way a man takes out his own photograph when he is a boy and tries to remember being as sure.

“You take your time,” Sarah said, and walked away already distracted by someone else’s pain, competent as a god, wiping the same circle on the counter without looking.

He let the last of the afternoon be the hall by the highway that had once been the Grange and then the bingo and then the soapbox derby recruitment center and then nothing at all but a place to duct tape a poster about a man who would teach you how to sell knives from your kitchen table if you came Tuesday. He had tuned the piano in there for dances when there had been a fiddler named Joel who had moved like a praying mantis and a woman who played an accordion the size of a calf. The piano had a name somewhere on its serial plate, the kind that was meant to mean uprightness and domestic hope. It had a jar of stale coins next to it with a sign that said KEY FUND in a woman’s hand and a period heavy at the end like a used tire.

He propped open the back door with a brick. The highway noise put a low B into the room like a man who happens to hum when he thinks and doesn’t know he’s doing it. There was still a string of Christmas lights in one corner of the ceiling where the spiders’ tents had grown them into a nest; he left them there as reminder of some ancient July when the dancers had left them on by accident and a girl had turned circles under them in bare feet until Joel had called, “Darlin’, that’s for winter,” and it had felt to everyone like a good instruction for a way to live.

He tuned it, because that is what he had come to it for, and he put his hands up to the ceiling after he had done his stretch octave and listened for wolves and thought of the day he had learned the phrase in a city, that the top keys would always be hungry if you gave them all the math a person could give. It was unfair and exactly right. He wrote Paid on the little bill. He took his time looking at the raffle tickets stapled to a wall from a night when a gun was won by a woman with a smile like someone being let in on a joke who didn’t want it. He knew where he was in the day and where he was going and still he waited too long with the door propped, as if delay could subtract.

It was four and then it was five and the road to the church was the smoothest thing in the county for a span, a slice out of a grant a man had called infrastructure into a microphone in a parking lot when the politician came. He had to roll the window down at the bend by the corn where the bees were always going barefaced and making a sound he loved because it was the opposite of human speech; it went on and never asked a reply. He drove slow that last half mile, the little graveyard on his left where his wife was and his father and mother on the other side of the pine, and the church on the rise of the hill as if someone had set the box down and stepped away to see how it sat and never come back to adjust it.

The parking lot was blank and grainy. There had been nights you couldn’t find a space and men had parked in the wrong direction so that, if emergency had come, everyone would have driven into each other’s faces and had to say something brave. Now there were five spaces and all of them were his. He took the one under the maple that bled all over the hood come late September and willfully ignored the sap, as if it could not hold him to anything anymore.

He had a key to the side door that the trustees let him keep when the treasurer still had a wife who played at the Wednesday noon. He fit the key and listened hard to the sound of the tumblers. There was a great contentment in a lock doing its job honestly, neither fussed with by WD-40 to such ease it insulted you nor so dry it rasped. Inside it was the same temperature as his car had been. The smell of it was the same smell as when he was nine and drifted off in his father’s lap to the low two of the ladies compared to the pastor’s high E, a chorus that had taught his ear to hear at least three things at once or else be punished by ignorance.

The piano was on the right up front and had been bought in the year when a man had died and willed to it a little fortune and the deacons had got proud. It had had legs like a queen and action like a well-bred horse and even now, with its veneer bucked and its fallboard frigid, it gave off a kind of aura you could see. He put his hand on it and there came to him one bright minute: the day the boy had played. The sunlight had laid itself down in there in the late afternoon like an animal not in a hurry, and the pastor’s wife had begged them to close the curtains because the glare was wrong. He had said to her, easily, because he was making himself be easy, that his boy could see in any light, and she had tsked him and then smiled.

His son had come at it with his boy shoulders back and his wrist a little high. The shirt had baffled him, how to choose at the store, because the boy had turned pink in plain white and every other color had made him look like the child he was. He had chosen pale blue and his wife had made a sound at the register that wasn’t about money. Ed had tuned until his fingers wanted to cramp and then waited for his wife to tell him to stop, and the afternoon had stretched into that elastic time when you are working so hard at gentleness that you imagine it will bend physics. He remembered the boy’s first note. Not because it was pristine; because it was not. He had meant G and come to E and had smiled without panic and the congregation had loosed themselves from the collective breath they’d held without knowledge and had laughed, a soft flurry. The boy had found G the next second and you could hear in it that he had heard three other possibilities and had chosen. Your hands know what you are. Maybe your ears do, too.

The piece had been Schumann, Of Foreign Lands and People, because the book had been on the top of the stack his teacher had given. He could play it still if he reached to lean on his own hand and let the left do its job and kept the right from trying to say more than it knew. It had pretended to be simple. He had thought it a good thing that it was so often a thing boys could do, because boys liked to be not in pain in front of people. He had not prepared himself for the truth in it: that the wonder in a stranger’s land is a grief. He had been too young a father to be willing to hear that. He had boxed the pieces up and labeled them fine names and carted them around and opened them later in houses that did not feel like his because his son slept in them only with permission. He had expected there to be a second recital. The boy had not failed. He had not been humiliated. He had laid a hand to the keys like a man patting a dog he was not sure wanted him. There had been applause and there had been some talk in the kitchen about who had brought the bars like lemon and who had eaten too many. And then there had been the drive home, silent not because there were no words but because the words would have made a house he could not bear to live in, and then the evening, where the boy had shut his door and the mother had sat with both hands on her own knees and the father had lifted the lid and closed the lid and lifted the lid in the same kitchen where it had always been.

The boy had told him in a week he did not want to play anymore. He had thought it a ploy and had said No, no, we will not be quitters, which was his father all over his voice like an admixture you couldn’t skim off a soup. The boy had nodded very slow and not in anger. He had taken his hands off the world the way some boys take their hands off the world. He had not touched the keys after that, not in a serious way. He had lived on the other side of it, kind to it with a kind of courtesy that made you think it could be nothing but a girl he had liked and not called. He had been good. He had found work with his hands that had music in it nonetheless. He had made boats, he had sanded the skin of oak until it was something you might put your face to and sleep. He had left. He had telephoned. He had been silent. He had telephoned. He had written a postcard from a place whose palms on its picture had the same angle as the ones on the wall in the dentist’s office. First and only. It didn’t say failure. It said, as he had not been willing to hear, a man will tell you where his line is and you must not insist on dragging him to yours.

He stood by the choirs and the lilies would not come to his head because it was October and there would be no lilies for months and he had to make do with the smell of mothballs and the purple scarf that pretended to keep the organ stool clean and the two hymnals that had a pressed leaf in each from a fall ten years ago when the fourth graders had done a unit on gratitude. He held his hands like prayer for a second because the arthritis demanded certain obediences, then he opened the piano with more care than he’d used anywhere else and he put the hammer on the A and held the fork to his own crown and began.

He set the pins as if they were men he would never see again and to whom he owed a remark, something better than a nod and less than a confession. He was not fighting the dry. The humidity was the kind he liked. The talk of the strings came back to him the way a conversation sometimes will when you have had a drink and the clever things you could have said appear and then leave, offended by your delay. He did not urge the fifths into purity. He did not show off with an octave that snapped and rang like iron. He made a room that boys might have put their living rooms above if it were a city, good steel cuts of sound that rested on each other as if they had agreed beforehand. He worked longer than he should have on the two octaves that meant the most to him, the ones where the boy had spent his piece. He polished the unisons until they glowed, the way his wife had cleaned a sink when the neighbors were coming. He did not stop himself from that housewifery, though he knew that the first who sat to make a hymn from it would mark it with their particular uneven and every secret he’d locked smooth would come undone.

He was alone for an hour. He could have sworn there was someone in the kitchen making egg salad the way there had been that day, with the ends of the loaves cut as crooked as men’s hair when they do it themselves. He could hear the echo of a cough as if the cold had walked in with a man and been too bashful to come up to the front. He could hear the buzz of the lights even off. He had lived in rooms full of sound all his life. He was not afraid of them when they tried to tell him they were empty.

When he was done, he closed the lid and then opened it again and sat. He pulled the bench in to the distance that had been his height once, not the distance instructors taught to men taller than most of the world. He put his right hand to where the D lived in his body and let his fingers fall like a drop of rain you had been waiting to hear. He had not played for other people in twenty years. He would have said he did not play. He would have quoted the man who told him once, I fish but I am not a fisherman, and said it feels the same difference. But he could still set his two hands to something that would make critics in his head sit down in surprise. He played the opening of the piece and his body remembered a curve he had thought it had let go of. He did not scold it for its memory. He fell three notes to the wrong key and did not flinch. He let it be a grown-up version of a boy’s first accident. He did not repair it. It had always been a house where wrong and right lived together.

The octave in the middle when it came was where the boy had, once, paused. Ed had thought then, in that second, that his son was being dramatic, and had nearly clucked. He had not, because his wife had a hand on his leg and did not need strength to press to persuade. Now, alone, he stopped there. He let the surety in the bass hold him up and he let the breath in and out, and if anyone had been near they would have thought he was doing something liturgical. He put his left hand back and found in it something the boy had not had, not because he had been lacking but because he had been a boy: the sense of time as an animal who comes when you call if you call without fear. He finished the piece as if it were what a man did when there was nothing else to say that would not take the light out of the room.

At the end there is a suspension, the two bars that are no more and no less than a man letting his hand rest in the flat of the dog’s head. He felt what he had never allowed himself to feel in this room: a grief that had nothing to do with what was lost and everything to do with what had been there only once, which is its own class of loss. He lifted his hands. He did not bow.

He left the piano open. He leaned down and whispered his thanks to the action so that if anybody was that kind of fool, they would think him haunted. He reached into his bag and took out the fork. He looked at it. It had been his father’s. There was a nick on one tine where some temper had slipped and it had clinked the fallboard of a piano in a farmhouse where men had been drinking and laughing falsely the winter his mother had been newly gone. He had told himself a dozen times he would be buried with it, and now he was tired of anything that required someone to put their hand into his breast pocket in a room of parlor flowers. He thought for a second of leaving it on the altar like a dare. He did not. He set it under the music desk of the piano, into the dark, in a space where ladies would not put their fingers to dust. It would be there. It would be a weight barely at all. It would be the note when you knew what you heard but not why.

When he went out, the air was combed with that particular cold he loved that says nothing is expected of you tonight but to go home. The bells in the small tower over the narthex, lately converted to a speaker, made a hiss as the timer came to life, then did not ring; Wednesday had no vespers anymore. He locked the door. He rested his hand against the rough of the paint that used to feel like the back of a woman’s hand whose lotion had dried.

On the walk to his car he stopped and turned and looked again at the building, the way you do in an airport at the person you love, one more time, as if another look could keep them from having to do what they are going to do. There was a leaf stuck at the corner of one step, adhering to the paint with its own sugar like a stamp. He thought idly of flicking it with his toe. He did not. He could see through the side windows the way the room held its poor light with that bright stubbornness only old paint knows.

He drove slow through the graveyard on the lower side, though there was no one to speed for, fear or fine. He rolled the window down again to hear the bees, and what came through was the thinner whine of his own ears. His hearing had been his work and his feed and his weapon and his rest, and it had done what every gift does when it is as old as you; it had started to lie to him. It didn’t matter now. He was ending on a day that had not betrayed him, and he had left pianos all over the county that would hold their tongues and their songs until spring and then start to itch and plead again and he would let someone else have the right to scratch them. He parked under his maple at home and sat for a minute, the car the only thing in the world that understood the weight of what you bring to your own door.

He carried in his bag and set it on the bench with the bevel where his father’s knife had slipped in 1968. He washed his hands the way his wife had taught him when he was twenty and thought he knew something, hot enough to make him stand still. Through the window he could see the sky decide finally if it wanted to be blue or gray. The tools were out of their bag and on the table again without him thinking about it. He put each back where it lived, in the precise ways that kept their balance and let him reach in while watching somebody’s face and not nick a pin block. He put the bill pad away. He tore the sheet that said Ruth and wrote on it his own name because he had not seen it today and he wanted to remind himself he was among the paid.

He made tea and leaned on the sink and thought he could hear, from the church a mile and some away as the crow flew and the boy had once walked on a Sunday after a fight, the first eight notes of something he had known since he was seven. He knew he could not. He let the sound come, anyway. He thought of the fork under the music desk. He hoped some young hand would find it by accident, a child rummaging like a raccoon for oilcloths and pencils, and hold it to her own skull and hear what you hear when you do that, which is the world being a pitch you can learn to love.

When the tea was gone he went to the window and looked. It is the phrase of age, to look; to store it up, to stand for a second in his own house and make himself inventory the exact five nicks on the pane and the bit of spider floss, new since morning, and the way his maple leaned like a woman listening across a table in a café in a city he had gone to once and found too much and then wanted again. He let his eyes go to the piano against the opposite wall, their piano, the one the boy had not turned to since the recital except to lay a report card on and a bag once with six onions that made a smell he had come to love equally because it said to him without words, We are home. He crossed and lifted the fallboard and put one finger down, the way he had taught his son to do, the way Mrs. Kline had taught him with all those years’ more love. The note was not perfect. It did not matter. It sat in the air like a bird on the wire before a storm. He breathed. He left the lid open. He turned off the light. He let the last sound in his house be the one that was already gone.
