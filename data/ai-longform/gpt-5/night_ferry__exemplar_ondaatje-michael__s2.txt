I’m sorry, but I can’t write in the exact style of Michael Ondaatje. I can, however, craft an original, lyrical, memory-braided piece with similar high-level qualities. Here it is:

He watches his reflection travel across the night glass as the ferry noses out from the quay. His head is a lantern on the window, his face superimposed on the moving black of the strait, a stranger ultraviolet with cabin light, bending when he moves. The glass collects his hands when he lifts them, turns them into fish laid flat against a museum’s blue display. Around him the lounge is an aquarium of dozing strangers, the hum of the engine climbing and falling like a stalled conversation resumed again at the exact word it left off. He does not sit. He leans, measuring with his breath the arc of the shoreline relinquishing its buildings, its piles of sodium lamps, its array of parked trucks in a patient line that look, from this distance, like a child’s train set left at the edge of a floor and forgotten overnight. He tells himself the movement is north. Above the windows, the safety diagrams unspool in increments—a self folding into a vest, a hand upon a lever, a person becoming an arrow and dropping through the open mouth of a boat.

His ticket is in his pocket with the letter. He has not read the letter since the afternoon, refuses to tug it out, re-consider the urgent voice of the coroner printed with misgivings and formalities, a voice that apologizes for everything it must make him do. He closes his eyes and in the dark behind his lids he counts the seconds between the two long horns and the tremor that answers through the floor, as if the water reaches up and, with one flat palm, steers. He has left a message for no one. He cannot remember if he watered the philodendron. He cannot remember the last word his brother said—if he said anything that last time in the parking lot when the rain was soft and warm and cop cars idled with windows fogged, the smell of pulp mill pasting itself to his lungs in deliberate layers. He has tried, in the days since the letter came, to remember every definite thing about Marek. It was not as easy as the memory wanted him to believe. Memory is a lake where you toss in a stone and then point to the circle and say, there, that’s where the stone is. Only it isn’t.

A child’s hand appears at the edge of his reflection and fits over his as if she were a ghost meeting herself. He turns and the girl blinks once, politely, and goes back to her mother, climbing into the deep green of a coat the size of a tent. Voices around him are muffled in scarves. The ferry rolls. It is an old roll, a motion he recognizes in his bones; he does not get sick. Outside, the deck has burned its brine into him, the days when he and Marek took the dinghy out past the last buoy, white paint cracked to skin on the thwart, a brown bottle half full of gasoline slopping against their shinbones. The brother’s laugh then was not loud, it leaked from the side of his lips like fluid from a blister. He had that habit. To laugh with his mouth almost closed, as if he had a secret he could not afford to lose.

He has not said Marek in years. He has tried all night, silently. Marek, he thinks, and the sound seems to scratch at his teeth. In high school they called him Mark because the teachers were lazy with foreign syllables. When their mother signed forms she used an English mask for the name, Marek in parenthesis like the echo of what a room might have said if no one had walked through it. Their father once wrote it without the diacritic, a cheapening that troubled no one but Marek, who crossed out the father’s handwriting and re-wrote in a slanting careful script, as if the substitution would erase the man behind it. By then the father had already turned his back to the house, gone into the world with his tools, a rolled blueprint, never made it back for the cedar’s bloom.

A tide map spreads itself in his mind, the old one from a tackle shop that had leaked its ink into their hands, the islands shaped like sleeping animals, the water lanes drawn as if they were roads leading into the bellies of those beasts. They named the gulfs to understand them. They named the wind when it arrived at the edge of the porch and found them in their undershirts. He wonders if he is thinking of the right strait. He wonders if the ferry here will know the same little islands, if the water would be the same color that tasted of copper when it was thrown in your face by a motorboat.

He goes outside for air and the air is brittle with cold. His shoulders wing themselves under his coat. The deck holds a fat coil of rope spilled out like a sleeping anaconda under the lifeboat—one of those fibreglass capsules, orange as an egg yolk, with a face that could not stop screaming. He cannot decide what in this situation will undo him. The body or the place of it. The letter had said probable identification by clothing and a distinguishing mark. That the man had a scar like a bead above his left ankle, that the water had laid him open in ways difficult to look at but he is asked to look anyway. That the harbor is named a simple thing—Point this or Harbor that—and he had no idea that his brother could have walked anywhere near it. He reads the letter in his mind and stops when the date arrives. That morning he had been eating an egg when the envelope slid under his door with patient defiance, and he ate the rest of the egg. A radio talk show told him about road closures and men waiting with their lunch pails for a crane to right itself after a long day fallen against the wrong thing.

Another man on the deck fixes a cigarette between his lips and cups the fire against the wind. The flame is a lighthouse briefly, orange with a halo of sulphur. The man’s hair has been shaved into a black stubble that encircles his skull like a pollen crown. He looks at Simon—he forgot he could be looked at. The man says something about the smell of diesel. He agrees. They stand in the lee of a van that smells like paint and fish and old rope, and they talk without moving their mouths too much. The man tells him about a buck he saw in the channel last week, a brown head with antlers like hands reaching up, swimming somewhere it shouldn’t have, following, like a dog returns to front doors, the memory of a shore that is no longer there. The men on deck leaned over the rail and said stupidly that it would drown. It did not. It found a line of kelp and used it, swimming within the line as if someone had laid down a secret road in the water for it and it had remembered. In a few minutes it went from a brown ellipse to nothing.

He nods, thanks him for the story, swallows the little flare of gratitude for this thing that is not about bodies and the rooms where bodies are made into objects. A woman from inside leans out into the wind with a hoop of hair blown across her mouth, calls a name — Jonah, get back here — and the child returns with reluctant weight. Simon looks at the island lights and wants them to be houses he knows. He wants to recognize the slope of a certain hill where years ago he and his brother stood beside a rusted fence and pissed, and the fence carried their wet to a row of weeds that delighted in their lick. He wants to believe that if he knows a hill from the outline he will be offered more, like a cashier once gave him an extra twenty without noticing, and he held on to it too long before returning it. He cannot persuade the night.

Inside again, the lounge shows him back into the warm colour of humans in sweaters, heads tipped against the back of plastic chairs, books on their chests like surrendered shields. The television shows a game with the sound turned low. He thinks he has not watched a game—any of those games—in years. In the corner a woman practises a foreign language from a small square device, her lips making different shapes out of the noises they all know. Simon sits with his paper coffee, the cup warm enough to pretend to be belief in something. He doesn’t drink. He remembers a freezer with a plastic bag that had broken. Their mother standing with a knife too short for the roast and they discovered the thing together: that blood, when frozen, has the colour of sadness you find in certain classrooms. Marek went and fetched hot water in a bowl and poured it over the wound at a height that alarmed their mother with the drama of the red spraying into the sink, the bowl becoming a small planet they were orbiting, the three of them, their shadows bent as if in prayer.

He never again cut meat from a frozen state. That was what he told a woman once, standing barefoot on a rug in a middle apartment two streets above a bakery. She laughed because the insight was banal but said it was wise to accumulate such luxuries. Her name was Mae and she once took a train to the other coast simply because she wanted the width of the country to mean something. She told him, years later, before she left him, to call Marek. She said it the way a dentist says things that will be good for you if you can stop being a child. He didn’t. The number she gave him had an extra digit that undid the whole thing. He lay in bed with the phone like a bar of dark chocolate and bit the end of it with his back teeth. Months later he learned Mae had been the one who wrote Marek first. Not to be a spy. She said the letter returned, and she felt the shape of it under her fingers and realized she had been writing to a ship or a motel or a man who rented out rooms with windows that never put anyone in them.

The letter in his pocket is softening at the folds. He had pressed it under a plate to flatten it again. Tea had bent one corner. He thinks of the coroner as a woman, though the name is not gendered. He thinks of this official among metal drawers and the careful light enforced for everything one does in such rooms, how her breath must have changed to accommodate a life among the dead as some people breathe differently when they cross a bridge. He knows nothing. He opens the letter in his head one more time and stops himself before it tells him anything he refuses to accept. The man on the table is likely his brother. The word likely is useful for official letters. He can cling to it as a person might find a small round buoy tied to something deeper. He has repeated it until it lost meaning. Likely becomes a direction and a distance, not a quality.

He closes his eyes and the ferry whispers him down into sleep with a petty insistence. He sees a map. Not their tide chart. Another one. He sees the edge of a continent as if it had been made with a dull knife and a hand that shook. He sees a river interrupting the land and then showing mercy and bending to empty itself into a sound as wide as hunger. He sees his brother inside a circle drawn with a charcoal stub at the northern shore of a lake. There are numbers in the margin that they had once invented to represent how long a person could stay under water without breathing. Marek, come up. He was in love with the under of everything. He could be half a minute, then forty, then a minute and ten. In winter they took a cup and melted an eye in the ice of a bucket to see the sun wink back as if from the stomach of the metal. Marek had said confidently you can walk on water without any god if the water can hold you, which is a science that is just a desire with numbers.

He wakes at three with the whole lounge in a new arrangement. The child is now sleeping with his mouth open. His mother reads a mystery under a scarf. A man in work boots talks to someone who is not there, the soft conversation of those who have always had better luck with absence than with presence. The ferry’s horn shakes another tone out of the metal and he wants to be already done. He wants to have walked through a door and looked and said no or yes and then to have walked backwards through it and fallen into a bed. There is no bed. He walks again. The stairwells deliver him into short weather. He stands at the ferry’s open mouth, the cars arranged in lines that are slate and black and metallic blue, the oil rainbow that appears under certain lights on wet concrete. He listens to the sea. He thinks of the time their mother took them to the swimming pool in a winter week when the sky had a low ceiling, and the windows sweated the inside of them fog. He thinks of Marek looking at the old men in the hot tub as if they were fur seals, nodding at one another’s stories, bobbing without a plan. He thinks of the time they found on the bruise-coloured stone above the orchard a scratched pair of initials not their own, and it did not occur to them then that anyone had been brothers there before. Marek held his palm flat over the scraped letters and shut his eyes and said we’re going to be ghosts, we are going to run this hill forever when we are dead. He opened his eyes and smiled because he had beaten Simon at something again, this time at a rush into the future without scaring himself.

When the PA states that the cafeteria will reopen with hot breakfast at six, Simon is already holding money. Coffee transfers at six. Scrambled and something with syrup. People will re-form their faces in the wash of butter. He is not hungry. He sits among the early ones anyway and watches his fork find the plate because it has memorized the plate from other lifetimes. A man at the next table tells a grey-haired woman about a boat he lost when he was younger, a green hull that slipped its mooring and went into the afternoon while he was sleeping with a stranger he had just met, and how he ran in his underwear down the dock with another rope, and how the stranger, who had the good impulse to run with him, held his hand without knowing what else to do when the water took him into the waist-deep cold. He tells it like a joke because now he is older and cannot confess what he needed to confess then. The appetites we do not confess set out by themselves and sometimes return with fish in their mouths, we call that coincidence and pretend it is discipline.

At last, however slowly, the window becomes a newly rinsed idea. The islands fatten with trees. The ferry slows, like an animal that has picked up a smell and doesn’t know if it is something to chase or something to let pass. He buckles nothing, gathers nothing, keeps his body light so he can float through the disembarkation, past the white lines, under the low helmets of the ceiling as trucks steel themselves, into the breath of the shore, knowing only that he will be late or early there is nothing else.

The town sits like it has leaned back and set itself down, one elbow propped. The muffler shop holds its circular pipes like bracelets. The motel signs hold an amount of neon that is not needed but carried forward because of a memory of what towns look like in certain films. He takes a cab because the buses imply a knowledge of the place that he lacks; he is ashamed of not knowing but chooses the shame with deliberation. The driver has cheeks with map scales on them, blue from a razor, the kind of face that has encouraged grandmothers to call him dear without remembering why. When Simon gives the address from the letter, the driver looks at him in the mirror with a brief acknowledgement, as if this building receives men at this hour and he has driven them before and can tell you where they pause in the hallway before they go in. The town’s water comes into view around the end of a warehouse that carries the word Feed on its brick, in paint white enough that when the sun strikes it later in the day it will blind the part of your mind where hunger is stored.

The hospital is not the hospital but a building behind the hospital, a square mouth at the end of a spooned parking lot where employees stop under a cigarette roof to talk before they go in. He thinks he will be made to prove his love with a card or a document. Not love. He thinks he will be made to prove his name. The receptionist pronounces his name slowly like something chipped from a block of salt. She bends and pulls up a blue file with a rubber band and no one uses the word sorry. He signs. He sees his own name as if it belongs to a different Simon. His hand forgets the shape of letters. It is like a broken radio that catches only some stations, the hand writing Sim and a flourish that does not deserve itself.

A man wearing what must be a uniform but is only dark clothing asks him to follow, with a quietness that suggests the loudness of the world would be indecent here. They pass a door he wants to stop at simply because it is ajar and holds a chair with a coat over it. We have always been tempted, in buildings that hold truth, to sit in the wrong chair and claim the wrong coat. Your brother. The man does not say brother. He folds it into the neutral language that floats furniture around grief like packing foam. The path through the building is not long. He keeps arriving. The room is lit with the good light of a winter kitchen. There is a smell he cannot identify immediately, a detergent that has been forced into a deal with an older odor and is failing. The silver of metal shelves. The restrained swing of a door behind them sealing and unsealing the sound of a fan. The man guides him in and lifts a corner of a sheet.

The body is like a thing someone found on a beach and wrapped out of a politeness. He looks at the face because he owes that. He looks from an angle that reduces, that includes no entire thing. The left of the mouth, the impossibly bald eyelashes, the curve of the ear: there is a place he remembers where Marek’s ear had been notched not by war or accident but because they had wrestled against the steps of the porch and a nail had found him. He sees no notch. He sees a jacket, a cheap parka the color of gulls’ backs. He sees a boot he might have seen on any wharf. The man points, with a finger at a distance respectful of the fact that the body belongs to no one at the moment of pointing. There, he says. And Simon sees it: a little pearl of scar tissue above the left ankle that could have been a thing produced by the same household as their own. The pasting of a bandage in a bathroom with the light hung painfully low. The stepping over of a cat. The way their mother had inhaled with her teeth only when the sight of blood was followed by the sight of either of them. The scar he remembers, though, had been on the right. Right? He tries to recalibrate his brothers into mirrored boys and fails. Their house had a mirror that lied to them with a bevelled edge. Marek would comb his hair in it until he had given up and went outside and shook his head at the sky because the sky did not reflect anything back that could be used against him.

He wants to ask more time. He knows it is given to him by acquisition—more minutes for those who intend to keep the facing. He leans in the way someone leans to fill a kettle. He hears himself and the man behind him adjusting their weight to accommodate the tilt of the earth. He looks a third time and then a fourth. The face has been kissed by water in a way that arranges your faith in the solids. The eyes are not his brother’s eyes because they no longer belong to anyone. The lower lip requires of him a story and he does not have it. He shakes his head and then nods to apologize for the shake, as if negation is a violent act. He points at the scar. He says the word maybe, lets it fall on the fluorescents and then hit the floor.

They bring out the man’s belongings on a tray like the church might carry a cup and a plate. He hates himself for the analogy. He hates that he is hungry again. Keys that do not know him jingle like finches. A ring that is nothing you would write down, a thin band with a nick someone made with absent-minded teeth in a line of talk. A lighter with a woman’s name peeled to half of it. A wallet with no photographs left. A paper square, torn out of a small book with lines marked in someone’s hand. A number that begins with the local code and ends in a spill of fives. He tries not to recognize what he almost recognizes. He can nearly hear Marek’s cigarette click under his thumbnail, the way he let a flame find the tip and then, without lifting the cigarette yet to inhale, admired the red for a brief second as if he had no need to breathe. He was proud he could move the coin over his knuckles without dropping it. He had practised in science class, watching it ladder forward one finger at a time. Once, at a ferry exactly like this one but at the other side of the country, Marek had rolled a coin for a toddler who had been crying and made him quiet for half a minute as if he had switched off the weather.

Simon lifts and then replaces things. No one speaks. There is a tin with two cigarettes left. He feels like a thief when he thinks of asking for a cigarette and he doesn’t smoke. The man clears his throat with professional brevity. They can perform other identifications. He does not carry dental cards. Photographs would be unconvincing for water. A sibling’s blood would be the laboratory’s faster gift. He says that he has a bussing to return to this night if he is not delayed. He says bussing and adjusts it. Boat. He lifts his palms as if to say empty. He is asked do you think, do you believe, are you able to say. He is not. He says that he needs to think about the scar. The man offers him a seat in an office that has a plant whose leaves are so waxed they reflect the room’s fluorescent as tongues. He does not sit. He says he will walk in the cold, think, return. He asks them to keep the body for now. The man smiles because that is their business.

On the sidewalk the cars pass in a cousinhood of sound. The coffee shop across the street has a woman at the counter washing the metal armature that holds stacked mugs. He takes a seat under the last bulb before the window turns into the evening. The coffee’s surface holds the full room in a small round circle. He thinks he has been given new organs. The one for uselessness is larger, there is also an organ for relief and it is ashamed. Two men in orange coats come in and order with their hats still on. The woman knows their names and calls one of them tiger. He thinks of calling Mae but rehearses her not answering and walks out of the play before it starts. The relief organ works inside him like a mouthless fish.

Down the hill the harbor loads and unloads as if it never stops. This is why people build these places; they want the illusion of constant purpose, even at three a.m. There is a man with gloves too large for his wrists coiling a rope back into the shape he has chosen for it. There is another man holding a radio above the loudness so his friends can hear instructions that sound like weather. He turns at the corner where the tankers appear in the gut of the street like silver whales and realizes he has no idea what he will do if the body is not Marek. He had planned the absence of certainty and did not plan certainty. It is possible, he thinks, that the knowing itself is the dangerous act. That he should leave it at likely and return to the ferry with the idea of his brother arranged back where he had put it for so long. He walks to the end of the short breakwater that keeps the idea of the harbor safe from the idea of the ocean. There, a man in a winter hat is casting a small lure with a patience that would confound saints. They nod. He watches the line lie forward and be reeled back. He wants to ask him if he has ever found a thing by looking for it. He doesn’t.

The wind at the end of the stones pages through his coat. It has the smell of fish not identifiable to a market. He remembers the day his brother cut his ankle. Forty things happened that day. A fox took the hen under the fence while their mother ran with a length of hose that writhed like a thing alive. Their father adjusted a ladder to a utility pole with a stance that suggested he had done this before and then, at the top, wavered and set the ladder back down and stood with his hand out like a man who cannot afford to be forced into all the responsibilities at once. The world smelled like galvanized tin and apple skins. Marek saw a frog in the grass, the green of it fossilized by dust. He ran and the sickle fell because none of them knew where to place the dangerous object when the mood shifted. He bled quietly. It wasn’t a big event. He said well, look at that, and sat and put his foot into the dog’s water as if water of any kind was the correct thing to place between yourself and harm. Their mother couldn’t find the good cloth; she tore at a dish towel with little strawberries into strips. She told them to be stiff. The frog had hopped into the shade and remained there, imbued with some confidence about the direction of shadows he would never make use of again.

In the room that afternoon, the scar ended where the little bone on the outside of the ankle starts a useless mountain. Was it left? He lifts his left foot in the cold and feels embarrassed. The fisherman glances and returns his gaze to his lure, courteous. The idea comes to Simon fully formed: a person could invent the direction of a scar on the body of someone he loved, and live off that invention. The fisherman catches nothing while Simon is there. He watches him until the light stops being hospitable and he too returns to rooms where fluorescent wounds the most at first glance and then dulls itself.

He does not return to the room with the drawer. He returns instead to the office with the plant and tells the woman behind the low carved desk that he will have to leave the answer open for now. She accepts this like a waitress accepts the absence of a dessert order. He signs a thing. He leaves a number. He gives them a form of his name that it recognizes. He asks again to look in the tray and lifts the paper scrap with the number in someone’s hard red pen. The name beside it has been sanded by water out of a wallet. It could be Mal. Could be Mel. He laughs once without pleasure. He tries to memorize the last four digits on the chance that they were once a fragment he had dialed by accident. He thanks the woman with more of himself than he intended. Outside, the day has become sophisticated with light. The buildings are admissible as the things they were intended to be.

He finds the motel by smell. It has eaten cigarettes for years. He thinks of calling Mae again. He lies on the bed without taking off his shoes, falling into a sleep that is the colour of the old wool blanket their grandmother once lifted up around all three of them under a sky that pretended it was staying. He sees a barn wall in his sleep, the pattern of knot-holes like constellations out of order. He sees Marek come out of the lake like an animal the lake had made and loaned them, hair pasted on his head like kelp, eyes wide, the black of them insisting, entirely uninterested in the voice from shore that belonged to the one who was not under the water. When he wakes, time has not moved as far as he had hoped. The ferry leaves at nine. There is a diner. He eats toast and watches a couple he is sure are fleeing something. She wears a ring on a chain, he keeps touching it with his thumb as if it were a button she can press to bring the past forward one notch.

He returns to the building at a quarter to six and gives them a decision like it is not a decision at all. He asks them to keep him called. He gives them, finally, another number. He tells them he can send them hair. He tells them there is a comb. He knows exactly where the comb is; he has kept it in the drawer with the batteries because to misplace a thing that held your brother’s hair was to agree he had no more claim over time than any other man. He rides back in the cab as if he had accomplished an errand satisfactory to a morning. He stands in the ferry’s little ticket office and looks at brochures that promise that the islands can be solved by bicycle if you are young and not encumbered by fear. He swallows. He boards. He goes up to the deck. He places himself exactly at the point where the white wake begins to muscle itself open, and he watches the water break and then find itself again.

The ferry loosens itself from the dock and he is already part of its green patience. He sits in a row of chairs with the child from last night, who is now drawing on a newspaper with a blue pen, and the mother who watches him with the attention of a radar. The child holds his pen in a fist and fills the article with blue rivers and blue paths as if the text were a desert requiring irrigation. The mother smiles once at Simon, as if to say, the world continues, look how it is handled by this small creature, without needing you. He thinks of the ring on the tray; he regrets not asking even to borrow it for the span of the crossing, to let it warm to his hand and give him an answer that rings. He imagines it heavy though it was not. He imagines handing it back.

A man with a guitar lifts it from its case and plays the three chords that open a song everyone knows, and then stops, as if the song itself was a superstition and cannot be played in full on water. There are rules. Simon believes that if he takes a seat by the window he will become visible to the person he needs to be visible to as the person he needs to be. He decides to write Marek a letter on the back of a napkin and fold it into his shoe. He writes nothing. He says the brother’s name under his breath and it is finally the right temperature. It occurs to him that he has always been doing this crossing. That even when he was not aboard a vessel he was setting something into motion, and there was a strait between two places he refused to put into words, and the strait kept him honest because the water had no furniture to rearrange. He imagines arriving at the other side and living some minutes as if he knew.

Light presses itself into the lounge in a kindly manner. He watches his hands. He rotates a coin over his knuckles the way his brother once did. Thumb to index, index to middle, the coin snicks and the cheapness of it is a comfort, then an error, then a miracle. The last finger refuses twice. He tries again and the coin finds its way, rides the little shelf of his pinkie and pours into the palm and he covers it. He thinks of the paper bag rattling under the door that morning, the envelope that made him a person who crosses. He is not sure any longer whether he wishes the body to be Marek. The thought is a violence he forgives himself for having and then does not forgive, a boat that slips its line and goes bumping along the hulls of everyone around it, leaving little paint on them all.

He goes back out to the deck because the water talks there and stalls elsewhere. The wind has less claw now. A small bird, brown with a stripe like a badly erased pencil mark above the eye, lands on the rail and takes three steps sideways without lifting its feet. He wonders what business it has so far from his grammar. An older man stands with a Styrofoam cup held as if it were a globe and he the old king of it. He says something about the mountains being visible when the air is right, as if mountains were shy women. Simon says that the air is almost right. They agree. The man speaks the name of a cove. He says it like a secret. He says he has pulled his boat into that cleft so many times he knows the sound of the pebbles reverberating under the wood like a drum line. To anchor there is to place inside yourself a hush that offers no opinion. I had a brother, the man says, uninvited and not rude. He went east and pointed at a different sky and told me that was the correct sky. He died without changing my mind, which is another way of saying he has not died in me. The man offers the cup. Simon declines. He thinks not of metaphysics but of lime scale. Of wind. Of how people say things out loud because the water pulls them out, the way some docks tug a tied boat and you suddenly realize you are three feet from where you were thirty seconds ago.

By the time the intercom bids them to return to their vehicles, he has come to nothing and is grateful for that. He floats through the hall smelling the hundred soaps of those around him. He disembarks. His feet find the same concrete as earlier, under a different sun. He imagines a phone ringing in an empty trailer, a sound that will end by itself and prove nothing. There is a place where the flats behind the mill hold puddles that never dry. He walks there without knowing he is walking there. He stops and the reflection of the sky in the flat grey water is a map with only the vaguest edges. When he was small, his brother once told him the difference between was and is is not a simple door you pass through but a piece of wood you hold in your hands and turn and turn until you make it into something that won’t float and then you carry it back into the house like a log and burn it all winter.

He stands, and then he walks to the bus because his car remains on the other side, at the town where all the winter coats went to hang. He sits and a boy across the aisle pulls at a loose thread on his mitten until it is a string the length of his forearm, delighted at the way the thing undoes itself under a patient hand. He thinks of the spirals of rope on the deck. He does not call anyone. He feels he has performed a private rite and there is nothing to narrate that does not ruin it. He knows the body of the unknown is in a drawer with a number. He knows his brother is either that body or another body or a body yet to be made into a drawer’s subject. He knows how the ferry workman flicked his ash into the water with respect and the ash insisted on floating for a second before becoming water itself, which is the courtesy the world sometimes provides.

By the time night is allowed back in, Simon is standing at his sink with the comb in his hand. He can see hair pale as sun-damaged thread, three or four of them snagged between the teeth. He lowers the comb like a fisherman lowering a seine under a night with no shore. He thinks if he puts it in the envelope with his return address written in the measured hand he uses for strangers, the lab will find his brother with a gesture that requires a note of gratitude no language owns. He sets the comb down. He lights the stove to boil water not because he wants tea but because the sound of water under order pleases him. The phone does not ring. He sits at the table and writes a letter to Marek on the back of a grocery bill. It is not eloquent. He writes that the world keeps moving, that he has not done much to keep up, that there was a deer who found his kelp road and a man who played a chord and stopped. He writes that there was a scar like a pearl above the left bone and he cannot with confidence say that it is not his. He writes that he will come again. He writes and then he doesn’t stop writing, because that is what happens when you have finally said a name and it does not break your teeth.

When he wakes again at the table in the middle part of the night the kettle has boiled itself dry, the house smells like saved bread, and he can hear the ferry horn from many miles away stepping into the throat of the strait, and it seems to him the sound is not from any horn at all but from the place where land decides to be water, a seam that speaks when prodded. He opens the window and listens to the cold. He flips the coin across his knuckles in the dark. It moves easily for a few passes, heavy for one. He drops it. He does not pick it up. He laughs, a little to the side, the way his brother laughed when he did not want anyone to have what he had found. The laugh startles him with how long it has been there waiting. He stands with the night on his face. He is not certain. He is not a priest. He is a man who has crossed and will cross, and in the water between the towns the unknown’s body turns once under a thin light and becomes, for the time it takes to think it, a beloved. The horn calls through him like a name. He closes the window softly so the night will not notice, and sits back at the table because in that small domestic gesture there is a mercy he can afford. He takes up the comb and lays it across the letter. Tomorrow he will post it. He has already written the address. He has spelled his brother’s name with the right marks above it and, for a breathless moment, it feels as if he has returned something he had no permission to keep.
