A man she has never seen before wedges his fingers under the lip of her mother’s green wingback chair and rocks it forward with a grunt. The fabric is rubbed thin at the arms where cotton shows through, and when he hefts it the chair exposes its undercarriage, dusty staples and the small black biscuits of felt her mother glued on to keep it from scratching the oak floor. He is wearing a ball cap with a seed company logo and has a face like a wrench—functional, bored by its work. “Can you hold the door?” he says, and Evelyn, who lived in this house until 1986 and has never in her life carried that chair more than a foot, cups the knob with her palm and sets her shoulder against the heavy door, pulling it wide. It catches on the throw rug and snags. The man shuffles, twists, clears the jamb. The chair scrapes the wall and leaves a pale crescent, a kind of goodbye bloom. He doesn’t notice. He doesn’t know to notice. Out on the porch, the late-September light flattens the green of the upholstery, turns it drab.

She follows him halfway down the steps and then stops, because there are three people behind him coming in, a woman her age with a straw tote, a man with a clipboard she assumes belongs to the estate company, and a teenage girl in a wolf T-shirt. The driveway is already lined with cars, the small square of her mother’s front lawn bent with tires from someone who couldn’t parallel park on Maple Street. The sign she taped to the mailbox—Estate Sale Today, Cash Only—is soaking up humidity, the tape slowly surrendering at the corners, making the cardboard gape like a dry mouth.

The man with the chair stumbles in the grass and regains his footing. Another man appears and takes the other side. Two strangers with her mother’s chair between them. When they go to slide it into the back of a pickup, the chair catches the sun and for a blink Evelyn sees the way the cloth has been smoothed by hands, by knees, by feet thrown over the arm during long phone calls. She sees the afternoon light opening in an arc above the cushion and her mother’s slippered feet on a footstool and the ashtray that used to stand on a bronze rod like a little satellite of the chair, the way her mother would punch out a Chesterfield and suck the last bit of smoke down in a small hiss that had always made teenage Evelyn think of someone else at the other end of a long tube.

“How much for the Pyrex?” the straw-tote woman calls, already halfway to the kitchen, which is the heart of all the houses on this block, and anyway the place where the cheap gems of a Midwestern life sit: bowls, jars, the metal potato ricer nobody uses anymore but everyone recognizes by touch.

“Three dollars a piece,” Evelyn says. “Or ten for a set.”

“What about the blue? The cornflower?”

“Five,” she says, because her mother’s hands had loved that one superstitiously, and because somewhere between setting prices and the first stranger walking in, she had decided that making small, arbitrary hierarchies might keep her sane.

She has a folding table set up where the old television console used to be, a gray metal cash box at her left hand, a pad of carbon-copy receipts that she bought because writing anything down makes it real, and a jar of ballpoint pens that will all, she knows, run dry at the worst moment. She wears a short-sleeved shirt because the afternoon is like a held breath and the house holds heat like a mouth. She can taste plaster dust on the back of her tongue. In the front room, Miss Carpenter’s oil painting of sycamores is already gone from the wall, leaving four small holes like the corners of an old photograph in an album. She feels like she should cover those holes with something. Tape a napkin over them maybe. She does not.

The estate man, a bland person in a crisp shirt named Kyle, lopes around with a dot of green stickers. He affixes them to plates, to an exercise bike from the early nineties that sat in her mother’s sewing room holding a pile of denim half-slips, to the ceramic swan that had, for forty years, been filled with hard candy and then dust. Every time he places a sticker on something, Evelyn has the impulse to ask him to see his palms, to check whether they are clean. He had met her in the driveway that morning and called her ma’am twice before she asked him to please stop. “You can just…just call me Evelyn,” she had said, and he had nodded and written it down as if to avoid any future argument about her name.

The teenage girl lifts a high school majorette’s hat out of a box and plants it crooked on her bristly hair, looks at herself in the mirror that is still nailed to the newel post. The plume wobbles. She bares her teeth at herself, grins, bares them again, deciding if this counts as irony. Behind her, a man is squeezing the sofa pillow with two fingers, considering a fifty-cent purchase with mournful intensity.

People move in currents through the small rooms, pick things up, put them down, lift them high to see the underside and the little circles left by felt protectors. Some talk to each other, some talk to themselves, some talk to Evelyn as though she has been keeping these items in a storage unit expressly to deprive them. “I came here for the yardsticks,” an elderly woman with a helmet of blue hair tells her, triumphant, and holds up three wooden yardsticks with the names of long-dead local businesses: Pickett’s Hardware, WQLC Radio, the Dairy Barn. “For my granddaughter’s science fair,” she adds, and because she has included family in the first sentence, Evelyn gives her all three for a dollar and adds the cracked one for free. The woman pats her wrist in thanks. Her skin is very thin and cool. It makes Evelyn think of wet tissue.

She is efficient. She did not expect to be. There is a way to do this and it is simply to move through decisions like a knife moves through cake, decisive and repeating. People hand her bills and she makes change without looking away. A man in a shirt that reads Jesus is Lord tries to talk her down on the laminated bookcase. She feels herself rise on a small wave of temper and then lets it set her back down on the other side. “It’s five dollars,” she says, and he blinks, surprised she won’t bargain. He buys it anyway. She hears, from the kitchen, the scrape of a chair being moved and almost says, “Watch the floor,” but stops. She has to learn how to speak inside herself, to keep her warnings in her throat where they can do small good for no one.

There is traffic outside like there hasn’t been on this street in years. The grain store added a new lot and built higher silos last spring and trucks favor that side of town now, dodge the rickety bridge near the junior high. Today, the vehicles curl in and out and park half on lawns, half in the street, doors left open, radios leaking. A man she knew in high school parks a bucking pickup with a misfiring engine and limps up her mother’s walk with a cane. He looks at her and then elsewhere in the way of small towns, in which recognition can be an ache you do not want to call out. He circles the dining room, thumps the sideboard her father once sanded until his hands bled because he hadn’t been able to find the good sandpaper down at Ace and was too proud to borrow any from Mr. Brody. He runs his hand along the inside of a drawer as though looking for loose change. He snorts when he doesn’t find any. He catches Evelyn’s eye and nods, a small defense, and buys a box of canning jars for three dollars and leaves.

“You’ve got some nice stuff,” someone says, a woman with a haircut like a smooth helmet, as though she and the yardstick woman choose their stylists in the same small circle. “Not like some of these where it’s just junk.”

“It’s her mother’s,” the teenage girl with the hat says, not turning, as if Evelyn had told her, which she had not.

“Well,” the woman says, her mouth bowing as if this justifies something for her. “You can tell she cared for it.”

She did and she did not. The same like all houses. There are the things you love because you touch them every day in the same manner at the same times. There are the things you love because they bypass touch and exist in a light you don’t control. The mixing bowl, the yellow one with the little crack like lightning down the side, had been handled, cooled, left in dishwater to drift and bump the other bowls like boats. The paperweight with the bubble of glass inside that had belonged to Aunt June was dusted and set back on a shelf by hands that did not believe in its usefulness. The table in the kitchen had worn through three sets of varnish with the salt of meals and elbows and pencils pressed too hard in arithmetic. The cedar chest had been lifted twice, carefully, for floods that had never risen quite high enough to justify the way it had uplifted them.

In the early afternoon the train goes through the edge of town. You can hear it from Maple Street if you want to. It’s a quaver at first, the way a cough in another room seems part of the air until you notice it, and then the horn opens out its wedge of sound and pushes a hole in the humid air, and for a second you can pretend your life is that large, that it demands a clearing. The girl in the wolf shirt hears it and says, “Cool,” into the mirror and the hat’s plume nods as if agreeing with her.

A boy, maybe five, hides under the dining room table and whispers, “I’m a turtle,” to his own knees. His mother browses and cannot see him and calls him out loud twice and then a third time and the boy says, “Mom, shh, I’m a turtle,” with the aghast authority of someone explaining their nature to a world in error. Evelyn catches his mother’s eye and smiles and bends to look under the table. “It’s a good shell,” she tells him, because it is. It is the table under which she read books when she was nine and snuck cookies when she was twelve and kicked her sister when they had been told to be good girls that summer her father left for a week and nobody used the word gone but everyone could tell it had come into the room and sat down and taken a plate. It is the table under which her mother lay in 1977 when the tornado siren went off and they had all run into the kitchen instead of the basement because the dog had been barking so panicked at the screen door her mother thought someone was on the porch. They had crawled under this table and held on to each other and laughed so hard during the all-clear that she had hiccups until midnight.

A man lifts the lid of the cedar chest and inhales with his eyes closed, which she understands and hates him for. He runs his knuckles along the corrugation of scent, sits back as if it had told him a story. He asks if she can do sixty. She says seventy-five and he agrees and she has the thought you have at such moments—that you have mispriced not the object but your past—that she pushes aside like a door. She writes the receipt in two copies. He loads the chest into a beige minivan and two girls in the back seat clap for it like it is a pet.

The Pyrex goes fast. It always does, apparently. Kyle had told her that when he came to do the walk-through the week before, the way doctors tell you how often you can expect side effects so that when they arrive you feel educated rather than cursed. “Your mother had good Pyrex,” he’d said, and it is true, and it touches Evelyn that he would use good in that way, as if praising berries at a farm stand. Blue cornflower, avocado green, snowflake, golden wheat. Stacked on the counter like a pastel geology, they shrink as the day seesaws, and when the last one goes—a lime green with a white lid to a young man who pronounces casserole like an apology—Evelyn sees her mother holding it against her hip like another child, half-casual, half-cradling, the way she would transfer anything hot to the table, moving her body and her strength around it as if she were a pulley.

She had intended to hate these people more. She had rehearsed it on the drive this morning, the anger and the performative derision, the reading of intent into their hands. There is a kind of ease to loathing that she had counted on. Instead, she finds herself pulled into their uses. The teacher buying every mason jar, running her fingers over the faint raised word Ball and setting them aside with the seriousness she reserves for her lover’s antique whisk collection, will use these for her class maybe, for marigolds in spring, for idiotic Pinterest projects in which children glue sequins like tiny wounds on glass. The college boy who has taken his mother’s station wagon and now stands in the living room swaddling a lamp in a flannel shirt like a monk is going to light that lamp in a place with cheap carpet and no stories at all and the lamp will begin to lay down hours for him, small hours, reading hours, sick hours. The man with the cedar chest will probably store Halloween decorations in it and never think again of her mother’s heavy crocheted afghans, of the way that smell announced them like a season, but that is not for Evelyn to correct.

There is a woman from church, of course. There always is. Today it is Marlene with the rigid posture and the scarf pins. She arrives after two and stands in the doorway as if to bring the air of condolence with her like a shawl and looks over the crowd approvingly, because a large turnout both pays respects and lowers prices. “Oh honey,” she says, and hugs Evelyn in full view of the room so it can be recorded; someone will mention later that she was there and that she did what was correct. Marlene has known Evelyn since she was six and she rubs the top of her shoulder with two fingers in a way that would have soothed a child. Evelyn submits and then moves out of the embrace like a boat edges off a sandbar. “Such a pretty home,” Marlene says, though she knows exactly what it looks like and has for decades, and then adds, to Kyle, whom she does not know but recognizes as a professional to whom she wants to prove herself superior, “Lorraine did keep a lovely home.”

“She did,” Evelyn answers, while looking at the ceiling, which needs washing.

In June, when her mother died, the house had seemed both small and full, a walnut you could not crack without losing the meat. It had been hot then, too. The hospice nurse had come in denim and a ponytail and had sat at this table and written things down while the ceiling fan moved the heat around like a shuffle. Her mother had been kind in that general way of the dying. She had nodded a lot, as if answering a rolling series of questions that were being asked in another room. She had followed Evelyn’s hands with her eyes. She had asked for ginger ale and then hadn’t drunk it. The last thing she had said clearly had been in her sleep and it had been nonsense, lovely nonsense, a list of tree names like someone reading down a page for a word and finding only what they loved: sycamore, tulip, willow, plane, linden. Evelyn had put her hand on her mother’s wrist and had felt everything go through that hand—history, correction, spoilage, forgiveness—like water through the slats of a livestock fence.

“You keeping that pitcher?” a man asks now, reaching over her cash box to point at the pale blue glass pitcher behind it that her mother kept like a shrine on the top shelf all the years Evelyn was in school, unreachable for lemonade and equally unreachable for her daughter’s scorn. The pitcher has a spout that looks like a bird’s mouth and a handle with a groove just large enough for an adult thumb. It holds exactly the amount of iced tea that gets drunk when you say “Stay for tea” to one friend. Evelyn had seen it and simply set it behind her box, an instinct, not a plan. “It’s not marked,” the man says, expectant. He has a tan so honest and excessive that it can only have been earned on a roof and not at a beach.

“Yes,” she says, and touches its cold curved side with her pinky like she is soothing an animal. “I’m keeping it.”

He shrugs good-naturedly. “Worth a shot,” he says, and finds himself a whisk in the kitchen instead, poetry happening in a different drawer.

The living room empties in a way you only notice if you stay in a room for four hours and watch it inhale and exhale eight times. The couch goes, leaving a clean patch on the rug like a skin peeled away. Someone unplugs the table lamp by the door that has been there so long it has a birthright and winds the cord around his elbow like a skein. The black-and-white photos on the wall are not for sale, Evelyn has decided and written on a paper sign and taped above them. A woman with sharp shoulders asks anyway. “Even this one? With the carnival? Oh, it’s adorable. Look at the hats.” Evelyn finds, briefly, pleasure in telling her no, as if there’s a joke only she and the house share. The woman tilts her head and makes the face of someone being charitable. “Well, bless your heart,” she says.

The flat crack of a plate reaches her from the kitchen and she flinches. The straw-tote woman lifts her hand in a small apology and holds up a saucer edge like a coin. “I’ll still take it,” she says, but Evelyn shakes her head. “Two dollars for the rest?” the woman asks, calculating how much of catastrophe must be paid for. “One,” says Evelyn, and the woman nods and opens her tote and slides the plates in at an angle. There is always breakage. It would be hubris to think otherwise.

In the middle of the afternoon, a thunderhead unrolls over the cornfields west of town and then decides to hold off, as if it had been summoned and then told it would be inconvenient today. The light goes pewter and the sweat on Evelyn’s neck cools. When the high school custodian—she remembers him, never knew his name, remembers his keys like a ringed planet at his hip—comes in dripping from his car window lowerings, he shakes water from his hands and beelines to the box of tools near the refrigerator. He holds up her father’s ball-peen hammer without seeing it as anything but a hammer, and something in her, not quite a grief, bumps at the inside of her ribs. “Three dollars,” she says. He hands her five and tells her to keep it, and the magnitude of his inconsequence, of all their small generosities, makes her ache.

The cardinals on the windowsill—her mother had a cardinal period, a mutiny of cardinals—go in pairs to a woman with a red purse, then to a boy paying with nickels he has worried flat with his thumbnail. “My mother collects them,” he says, grave, and Evelyn cannot help it and asks, “Your mother, not you?” and he looks up and answers with a small frown, “Well, she always says she does,” and Evelyn gives him the smallest cardinal, the one with a chip on the crest, and he takes it in his hand like a bird you would not dare to press.

When the man with the cane returns and asks about the workbench in the basement—“How much you want for that old brute?”—she hesitates, and he sees her hesitation and nods like someone seeing a fieldstone he remembers in a field. “I get it,” he says. “Was thinking of my boy. But I remember your daddy putting that down there. Got stuck in the stairway, right? Had to take that piece off.” He taps his own knee with the crook of his cane. He does not wait. “You could keep it. No rule says you gotta—”

“It’s forty dollars,” she says, before she can change her mind into a person. He pays her in tens and she can imagine his boy, not a boy at all now, leaning into the same wood, tightening the same vice, gluing things none of them actually need to be glued, the way you glue for the satisfaction of pressure and the knowledge that you are set, that you have placed two things together and made them one briefly. When the two men he has brought carry the bench up the basement stairs, they have to tip it and strip a bit of paint by the lintel, and Evelyn does not swallow, does not gasp. The gouge is small and bare, a rebuke and then an absence.

Upstairs, where hardly anyone goes because she has directed them kindly elsewhere and because upstairs is not for sale in the way stairs and light and thresholds are never for sale, she goes to her old room to breathe. The bed is unmade. The spread is a dated blue that would be called dusty on purpose in a catalog. On the dresser is a comb with three hairs in it that she had left there in July and meant, strictly, to throw away the last time; it felt like vandalism then. This time she wraps the hair around her own index finger and drops it in the waste basket. The small act releases something sour and tender in her chest. There are two items in this room she cannot watch go, so she has not put stickers on them. One is the brass music box in the shape of a locket that opens on a yellowed picture of Paris and plays a tinny version of La Vie en Rose when you wind it. The other is the quilt her grandmother pieced, the colors sun-bleached past their intention, a bit of a dress from a firehouse picnic, a bit of apron from a strained spring before her mother’s youngest sister was born. She takes the music box and puts it in her purse as if smuggling a prayer.

A small man with a large voice hollers from the bottom of the stairs, “How much for the ladder?” and Evelyn leans on the banister and says, “Five,” and he says, “Four,” and she says, “Five,” and he shrugs as if she has betrayed him and says, “Okay,” and carries the ladder out on his shoulder like a rifle.

When, at three-thirty, a thin woman with hair the color of honey and a mouth chapped into cracks asks to see what jewelry, if any, there is, Evelyn brings out the cigar box from under the table without thinking, and then realizes she does not want to show it, and then decides that this want is not allowable. The woman opens the lid the way you open something that might be empty and finds it is not. There are clip-ons with their little teeth, plastic beads cool and light as rain, bobby pins, collar stays, a ring with a peach stone that is not a stone, it is a trick. “She must have loved pink,” the woman says, and Evelyn examines that sentence like a fern unfurling. “She loved wanting,” she says aloud, and the woman flinches, the honesty making a small breeze where she had expected a polite lie. Evelyn picks up a silver locket that is not silver and holds it an inch above her left palm. She remembers it caught in the light on her mother’s chest while she gestured for emphasis at a reunion, the way it had thudded softly like punctuation. “Three dollars?” the woman asks, and Evelyn nods, and the woman’s hand is so grateful and quick that the box shifts and a brooch flips and a snap-on earring slides on its own to the bottom. The woman laughs. “Slippery,” she says. “Like they want to stay.”

The afternoon gets thin in the way afternoons in houses do, the edges distancing, the center revealed. People come at this hour who are not early birds but also do not want to be bottom feeders, who hope for a deal that is also a small grace. A couple in their twenties in matching T-shirts from a brewery walk around with the word “together” propped somehow between them like a plate. They pick the battered colander with two holes and show it to each other as if to underscore their tolerance for batteredness, their willingness to be delighted by things that do not require paint. A girl from the next block over, grown now into a woman with her hair pulled back as if expectation were aerodynamic, stands in the hall and looks too long at the wall where, because of the summer’s damp, a vine of shadow has lifted the paint. “I didn’t know,” she says to Evelyn, and they both pretend she is talking about humidity.

“You want the dining table?” Kyle asks, pen poised. He has learned the inventory of her grief and speaks with medical precision. “If not, you got three offers.”

She looks at the table where the turtle-boy had made a shell. It is lighter without its leaves. It has a sugar burn on one end that looks like a coin. Once, she pressed her cheek to this wood when she was nine and the earth felt like it had tilted hard and would maybe not return. She closes her fists and opens them. “Sell it,” she says.

He writes a green sticker and affixes it to the underside of the table like a secret.

In her mind, there is a list—small, domestic, a receipt of a sort—of times she has left this house. When she went to college and her father had put her suitcase in the trunk of the Oldsmobile and then stepped back and made his face wooden because he did not have the language people in magazines had for such days. When she came home to this house newly married and unafraid, and she and David had slept in the guest room under a ceiling fan that ticked like a clock. When she returned, less married, heart dry like a riverbed, and sat in the chair with her legs under her and watched her mother peel a peach without breaking the peel, and the peel had dangled like a ribbon, and she had thought, If anyone were here to see that they would call it grace. When she brought her boy, shaky and high-fevered, and her mother had soaked her washcloths in a bowl of ice so that they had something to exchange for hours. When she came back because of the tumor in David’s chest, and the way dying can turn a person opaque. When her mother fell and the word hip broke open in the family phone like a shard. When she arrived for the hospice bed, and the house smelled like oranges and ammonia.

“Everything must go,” the sign had said in the ad Kyle placed. Such an apocalyptic insistence for a town that still dug out dandelions with spoons like it was the 1950s. And yet. And yet today it is true. Everything that can walk walks.

“Is there any Christmas?” a woman asks, as if the season itself might be stored in a bin. “Downstairs,” Evelyn says, “in the back,” and adds, because it comes over her like a small weather, “the nativity is missing its wise men. And the baby’s hand.” The woman stiffens, as if insulted by the defect, and then softens, because she has known babies and hands. She goes down anyway. She returns with a ceramic snowman cigarette lighter that does not work. “I’ll take it,” she says, and Evelyn nods, trying and failing to remember the sound this snowman used to make when it worked, the flint scratching the wheel, the small victory of flame.

At four, the world begins to lengthen its light into evening even as the heat tucks something away. The cars thin. The neighbors who came only to stand in the yard and fold their arms disperse. The mayor’s wife, who wears perfume you can smell four chairs away at Rotary, buys an ashtray shaped like Ohio and says, “Well, she won’t need this where she’s at,” and smiles in the smile of those who know and want it known. “Where she’s at,” Evelyn repeats, tasting the grammar as if it had seeds.

Kyle, relieved perhaps because there were no heirs to accuse him of theft and no cousins to accuse each other of greed, begins to stack the unsold in categories on the porch: lamps, linens, a basket of unmatched lids that will go by the end to some person who collects lids as a philosophy. He has a ladder of fatigue laid on his face and he yawns without hiding it. He looks at his watch and then at Evelyn with question marks; there is a time you close these things. Evelyn nods. “Half an hour,” she says, and he writes it on a piece of cardboard and tapes it to the mailbox sign like adding a tail to the end of a squirrel.

After that, people hurry. They must return to the lives that had let them spiral out to this other person’s living room. They take slow last looks and then speed up. A girl with a braid buys a stack of records, Beethoven and Dolly Parton and one hymnal she will use for a wall collage and not for an off-key Hallelujah. A man who has been in twice and touched nothing that costs over a dollar comes back and buys the crocheted potholders, color atrocities that will not match any kitchen, and puts them in a paper sack with his careful fingers, as if they could bruise. The turtle-boy returns and asks if he can have the shoebox with buttons. His mother starts to say no, then looks at him, then at Evelyn. “Two dollars,” Evelyn says, and the boy’s mouth falls open as if the box itself were a mouth. “They used to be on clothes,” he says, not quite a question, looking at the brown and the shiny and the pearl, at the two-holed and the four-holed, at the black with a crescent missing like a night sky. “They still are,” Evelyn says, and feels how true it is—that the past does not unbutton itself no matter how greedy you are.

From the back of the kitchen, the slight shift of the house—a settling, a sigh—makes the plates in the hutch shim like minnows. The light on Maple Street has turned to the angle that in her childhood meant please come in, wash your hands, your father will be home soon. This thought does not open into a sob. It slides along the floor and under the doors like cool air.

A neighbor from the other side of the alley comes in and wraps her arms around empty air at the foot of the stairs because the grandfather clock is gone and for a second she believed she’d ascended to a world where time was absent. “Oh,” she says, and then laughs at herself in the mirror with the girl’s hat feathers still stuck in it, and then looks at Evelyn and shakes her head. “It’s hard,” she offers. As if placing a table in your truck is hard. As if grief were a dresser you got a friend to help you muscle down and then realized you had mismeasured the door.

“Do you want this?” someone asks, and holds up a book. Not a good book, not a loved book. A Reader’s Digest Condensed with a tired cover. “No,” she says, and the way the woman’s eyebrows wing up, planning to tell this story later, makes Evelyn add, “No, thank you.” The woman nods as though the manners have made a difference.

The last half hour is like the end of a county fair, something celebratory gone tired, the light on the Ferris wheel seeming alternately triumphant and cheesy. Two men carry out the dining table and she stands out of the way, her hands at her sides. They bump the doorjamb and one of them says, “Watch the—” but does not finish his sentence, because either he is kind enough to swallow it or because he does not know the word jam, because he is from somewhere else, and in that small gap she sees an entire ocean of childhood language that is not the world’s. Jamb. Wainscot. Eaves. The words have lived in this house without calling themselves to attention. She wants to lean her head against the cool of the hallway and put those words into it like screws: jamb, eaves, lintel, sill.

“Okay,” Kyle says later, softly, like a bartender telling you it is last call but you are the only one at the end of the bar and you are not drunk, only untethered. “We’ll leave the card table and the chairs so you can sit. I’ll have the hauler come tomorrow for what’s left. You got someone comes by to pick up the Goodwill?”

She nods. She has written all of this on a list that has traveled with her all summer through hospitals and banks and trust offices where men explained to her the committee of her own family’s money. Goodwill. Hauler. Realtor. The appraiser whose measured greed had been a consolation. She has always been good at tasks. She knows this is both virtue and crutch.

The house, without its things, is a house. A line of shadow runs where the couch had been, a slight high-water mark of dust. The kitchen table’s absence makes the light from the back door expand as if a ditch had been dug and immediately filled with sun. The noises change. With fewer soft surfaces, every word on the porch is crisp. A stray voice floats through from the neighbors bullying their dog the way some men bully the wind: Come on. Ah, come on now. She hears three gnats bumping against the screened porch, which means, she supposes, that for now there is someone in the house who hears gnats.

When the last man leaves with the cracked mirror and says to be careful because “seven years is a long time to risk,” she is alone, except for Kyle, who takes a last lap, checks, makes a note, nods. He tells her to call if she remembers a thing she wants pulled from the Goodwill pile tomorrow, and she says thank you in a voice that makes him look at her, as if any thanks at this hour might be suspicious. “You did good,” he adds, and it surprises her that she believes him. He picks up the little cardboard sign and starts to pull the tape from the mailbox and in the end leaves one gummy edge because it is stuck, and he waves, and is gone, and the house yawns, not dramatic, but necessary.

There are still things, of course. Houses hoard. There is a curtain rod closet no one found with three stakes of tension rods. There is a shelf of cookbooks with indexed cheese ball recipes and pages stiff where batter has made the paper turn to something like cloth. There are the photographs that make the house look as if life is contained under glass. Evelyn walks to the hall of photos and looks at the carnival picture, the one the woman wanted. It is the year she and her sister had begged to go on the tilt-a-whirl and lied about having already eaten. In the photo—taken before, so the lie had not yet made them both sick—her sister has the roundest eyes and hair cut in bangs too high because it was hot that day and their mother had been impatient. Behind them, their father holds a paper cone of peanuts and the man in the booth leans forward as if thinking about how to steal an extra car back into gear. Evelyn touches the frame and says out loud, “I’ll take you,” to no one’s surprise, and lifts it off the nail. The wall has four uncovered spots of paint that did not yellow because rectangles had protected them, and these pale parts look like new ideas you can’t quite own up to.

On the radiator in the front room there is an old penny pressed flat into an oval—someone has elongated it in a souvenir machine at a zoo and it reads Cincinnati in a stretched scrawl. She does not remember it. She picks it up, holds it in her palm. It is warm, which is impossible. She drops it in her purse with the music box and the blue pitcher will be heavy in the other hand and the quilt will be rolled into the back of her car like a gentle animal for a ride.

On the kitchen counter, under a curled bit of the newspaper, there is the bowl of ginger ale her mother didn’t drink. Not really, of course; that is months gone, poured down the sink in June with the solemnity of people playing at ceremony, but she sees it, the small fizz turned to flat surrender. She sees the good scissor her mother hid in the flour bin one year to keep her daughters from using it to cut poster board. She opens the flour bin. There is no scissor, just the smell of old bread. She closes it and knows that when the hauler comes and when the realtor in her efficient shoes reaches for a pen and when she carries the last of the folded towels to Goodwill and the girl there asks if she wants a receipt for tax purposes and when she drives out of town and crosses the river that has always been so pathetic and then so fierce and then so pathetic again, every bin in her mind will open for five seconds and then shut, and each time the clang will be small and significant, a series of closings like drawer pulls in an empty house at dusk. She is learning the choreography of closing. She is not bad at it.

She sits on the floor where the couch was and watches the light shift on the picture rail and thinks of how her mother used to wipe that ledge with a damp cloth and then pull her finger along it to be sure she had gotten all the dust, a gesture that had offended Evelyn for years for its fussiness and now seems like dedication, seems like worship. She is not crying. When she does, and she will, it will not be here, she thinks. The house has watched too many tears to be impressed. It has its own leakages.

Someone knocks at the front door. She startles. It is the boy with the buttons, back, frowning at the porch floor as though accused by it. “I found this,” he says, presenting a nickel as if he were an emissary from a poor country. “In the yard.” The nickel has a dark divot of weathering. “You can keep it,” she says. He stares at her and then at the nickel and then says, “Okay,” with a speed that tells her generosity is not required to be refused to be valued. He runs back to his mother’s car, which is pulled up in front without its engine on, the windows all down, a kind of midwestern trust she recognizes but had forgotten to miss. The mother calls, “Say thank you,” without looking, and the boy yells, “Thank you,” into the air as if that were enough. It is.

She goes to the back porch and stands where the flies gather and listens to the next street over. A mower starts, stops, starts. The smell of gas is sweet and the cut grass is sour and both of them make her hungry. She pulls the string that turns on the bare bulb above the washing machine and the light shivers and then comes to itself. The basement will be black in an hour. The spider in the corner above the old sink has made such a geometry that she would pay for it if she saw it in a store, but here it is free, and so she gets a broom and lifts the edge and the spider runs, and she says out loud, “Sorry,” startled by how quickly the apology can be made to an enemy before it can be made to a friend.

In the kitchen she takes the yellow mixing bowl and walks it to the sink and runs water into it. The tap splutters as if outraged and then smooths itself. She thinks she will wash this one thing. There is nothing in it, no batter rim, no salad oil shine, and still this will be an act. She watches the meniscus climb the inside and stop. She puts her hand in and scoops and sets the water over the dulled paint. She hears her mother’s instruction as if broadcast from the radio on the windowsill—scrub but don’t scour, no need to take off the history—and is surprised by the clang of disagreement she feels. She takes the green sponge and presses it hard, as if the more the past is buffed the glossier the present will be. She rinses and tips the bowl to the side to dry and only afterward remembers the crack. Water slips through and beads under it and spreads into a slow, irreversible ellipse on the counter. She moans with laughter that makes no sound. She sees herself, tomorrow morning, wiping around that bead on the laminate because the hauler will arrive at nine and she will not be there.

The doorbell clicks with its tinny insistence and she goes and it is a man holding two votive candle holders she recognizes but cannot place anywhere in her mother’s habits. “I think I forgot to pay for these earlier,” he says, embarrassed less by the crime than by doing a correct thing late. “You didn’t,” she tells him; she cannot lie about that, she knows every dollar that crossed her palm today, they had felt like fish. “Huh,” he says, and looks at his hands as if surprised by their cleverness. “Well,” he says. “May I?” He sets two crumpled singles on the edge of the table and retreats, and she is suddenly in love with the entire idea of a cash economy.

She shuts the front door. The latch has always stuck. It catches halfway and you have to push past a stubbornness that lives in the wood, not in your wrist. She uses her hip and fits it into place and flips the deadbolt and the sound of metal sliding into carved space is obscene and satisfying as always. There are still items in the corners. There always will be. This house will never be truly empty in her mind. It is an organ, not a structure. You do not move out of your liver or your heart. You learn to live with it or without it and both are hard.

She takes the pitcher with her left hand and the picture with the other and slings her purse across her body so that it bumps the place where her ribs turn to soft. She stands in the front room and looks at the place where the TV had been and at the corner where the lamp’s light had made a pool every winter night and says into the open air, because saying into closed things is pointless, “Goodbye, Mom,” in an even tone, without asking for audience. The room breathes. She thinks, in a flash that is giddy and then solemn, of the way her mother had said her name in a grocery store when she had wandered off once to look at the animal crackers box with the circus cage bars. “Evvie,” her mother had said, a coil of worry and scold and affection, a portal in a single name. There is no reason to believe that making that sound now would have any effect, no reason to expect an echo, and yet she stands at the door and waits a second as though traffic must pass along an empty street.

Out on the porch, the heat is less aggressive. In the maple across the way, a squirrel hangs upside down and nibbles something bitter and then flips upright and runs along the branch like continuation itself. Someone down the block plays the radio too loud. It is an old song. She cannot pull it into focus. She goes to the mailbox and rubs the last bit of sticky tape off with her thumb until a wrinkle of cardboard lifts and then falls. She puts the cardboard sign in the trash can at the curb, because ceremony is for the living.

She does not look back at the house as she walks to her car. She picks up a small stone from the walkway and tucks it into her pocket as if pockets were new again. She sets the pitcher in the passenger seat and belts it in and laughs at herself without hiding it from the street. She sits for a minute, not turning the key, watchful in a way that looks like hesitation but is not. It is attendance. Her hand rests on the steering wheel while the afternoon sifts into something else, not evening yet, but after. Above the grain store, the cumulonimbus that had promised drama earlier has dispersed into threads, caught and unhooked. The telephone wires sag in the heat and hold the sound of insects as if they were tuned for it.

Driving away will be its own act. There will be, before that, the long reverse out of the driveway, and the check for traffic from the right and then from the left, and the moment when the rear wheel will dip a fraction of an inch as it climbs the rut where the Maple Street tar patched last winter has settled. There will be the left turn and the slight jolt as the car crosses the seam in the road. There will be the stop at the corner where the stop sign on its aluminum leg has been twisted by a truck’s back end and still does its job, valiant and undebatable. There will be the last sight of the house in the side mirror, small as a thumbnail. There will be the train again, later, at the edge of town when she does not have time to be held by it, and then she will stop and be held by it anyway, because there is no rushing that will prevent this or anything else.

She turns the key. The car stutters and then catches. The air conditioner blows what it thinks is cold until the car believes her intention and then shifts to provide it. She pulls to the end of the drive, the peppery leaves of the maple tree overhead not moving at all. When she stops at the corner, she looks down, and next to the brake pedal there is a bead, a small red bead, snagged in the rubber, escaped from some string, probably from the box the boy took. She digs it out with the edge of her shoe and it winks in her palm. For a moment she considers flicking it back into the footwell. Instead, she puts it in the little dish in the cup holder where she keeps change and the melon-flavored lip balm she never uses. It makes a small sound, plastic, ordinary.

The house behind her sits and does the ordinary things of houses. It holds its breath and then exhales. It presses its hand flat to a floor and feels its own warmth. People on Maple Street sit on their porches with glasses of melted ice water and do not say aloud that it is too hot for the time of year. A dog drags its leash, relieved, toward a patch of shade. The grate of a skateboard makes the sound that most resembles distant thunder that is not actually thunder. And in the driveway, for a time, there are the sun-shaped marks of all the furniture feet that have left. They will fade; rain will take them, and the tire tracks, too, will go virginal after a few days of dry weather and then a good, heavy, correcting storm. But for a few hours, they will remain, testimonies neither heroic nor cheap, round little vacated territories on concrete, each one a memory of something held and then let go. She could, if she wanted, go back and stand with her toes in those empty rings like someone trying shoes on in a store. She could say, here. And here. She could, but she does not. She turns the wheel and takes the left, and the sound of the blinker is like someone gently telling her yes.
