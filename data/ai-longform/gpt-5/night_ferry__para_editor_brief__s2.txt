The ramp lifts behind me with a metal cough and a ropey wet sound, drawing a bite of night along as if it were a net. There’s a smell of diesel and brine and fish that has dried and then been made damp again, and beyond the glow of the loading bay lights the sea is an unmade bed, shivering. I keep my small bag close under my arm as if I had something vital in it—I don’t, just two shirts, the razor, the old phone I don’t use—but the grip steadies me in the way that gripping things does. The ferry’s skin hums under my feet. The men in reflective vests wave us away from the reek of the car deck, up to the stairwell painted a tired mint green, the paint buckled where boots have pounded too hard. Everyone has their own version of making this crossing, and even I, who haven’t done it in years, know how the steps pinch your ankles, how the wind squeezes through the joints of the steel and makes a flute of the vessel.

On the passenger deck there is a lounge with upholstered seats you can sleep in if you have to. A handful of people already have their feet tucked on cushions, shoes in plastic bags tucked under their heads like strange gray loaves. There is the low glow of a television with the sound off and a live crawl along the bottom that no one watches. A child makes the sound of a bird in sleep, a deep swallow that breaks into a small whine. I pass the vending machines—tea, coffee, hot chocolate—all from the same spout, and the hot food counter closed for now with a metal shutter. I go to the bar, not because I want a drink, but because it is open and warm. The barman is drying a glass with a white towel that has purple edges from too much machine wash. He nods at me like he’s been expecting me and then, seeing me hesitate, draws a finger along the taps with their bright, fake icy mantles.

“Tea?” he says.

“Please,” I say. My voice comes out cracked, like an old plaster wall. I clear it and say, “Tea would be good.”

“Sit. I’ll bring it.” The kindness in service of habit embarrasses me a little. I sit at a table with a porthole that looks like a flat moon. The window shows nothing. The deck lights reflect my face back at me, a pale pearl of me in a round ring; when I shift, the picture lags.

The ferry has been my brother’s place more than mine, in the telling we had of ourselves. He always preferred being between. It suited him, the weather and the machinery and the idea of motion for its own sake. We crossed as boys in summer, once, on a family holiday when holidays had just become permitted by the budget and our father had gotten his overtime that year repairing something in the north. My brother had stood at the bow and let the spray pin his shirt to his chest while I sat inside, nursing the warm core of a cocoa. He came back with his hair salted into spikes, eyes shocked and wild, saying the dolphins were riding the wake. I said I didn’t see them. He said that was because I didn’t come outside.

The barman brings the tea and I put my hands around it but don’t lift it yet. The heat is a kind of proof. Behind me, the boat shifts and someone’s glass finds a lip of deeper sound near the sink. The barman glances at the television and then at me and says, “Rough up top. You want a cabin?”

“I thought I’d try a chair.”

He makes a small face. “Well. If you need, ask. We’ve sold most of the singles, I think, but there’s always a room when somebody’s sick. You know how it is. Winter crossings.”

I nod. “You all right?” he says, and then, as if to make it smaller, “You look like you’ve had a day.”

“I had a call. I have to be on this one,” I say, and then stop. I don’t want to say anything more of it, not to this man who has a finite number of nights he can carry other people’s stories.

He nods again, like he does know, a courtesy we offer for things we cannot afford to understand. “Well, if you need something.” He goes and polishes another glass.

I sip the tea and put the mug down and watch my hands come away with small crescents of condensation. My phone buzzes in my bag and I let it. It buzzes again. After a third insistence I take it out. The number is unfamiliar, the country code the right one. I answer.

“Hello?”

There is the smothering sound of someone calling from a room in which the heater is faulty; then a voice, brighter by comparison, young.

“Is this Mr. Haines?” the voice says.

“Yes,” I say.

“This is just to confirm you’re making the overnight crossing,” he says, and gives the name of the station in the harbor on the other side. “We’ll meet you at arrivals. Morning. Eight thirty if she’s on time. Weather permitting. Could be nine. I don’t know if you’ve crossed recently.”

“It’s been a while,” I say.

“Right. Well. We’ll be there. Just—to go over it again, sir, and we can go over it again in the morning. The deceased has certain features in common with your brother, according to the description we have on file. The age range. The general build. The hair. And—um—the teeth.”

“Yes,” I say. “You said this.”

“I did,” he says. There is a flustered paper rattle in his voice. He may be turning a page, although I don’t know of what. “We would like you to come to the, eh, the facility adjacent to the hospital to see if you can confirm. We’ll take it very slow.”

“Thank you,” I say.

“Would there be anyone else traveling with you, sir? Anyone to meet you on arrival?”

“No.” I have to decide whether to add, There is no one or I have not told anyone. I add nothing.

“All right. We’ll be at arrivals from half eight. Don’t worry about baggage. Do you have any questions at this moment?”

“Is there—” I say, and stop. I don’t want to ask if there is a tattoo. I don’t want to ask whether the body is whole. I ask, instead, “Where was he found?”

The voice brightens with relief. “Just outside the north bay. The path that goes to the iron cross, you know? On the rocks below it. Someone walking their dog.” He clears his throat. “It’s not the worst place, in winter, if you know where you’re going.”

“It wasn’t him then,” I almost say, but swallow it. I say, instead, “Okay.”

“We’ll see you in the morning, sir.”

When I hang up I put the phone face down so that its black flat doesn’t attract my eye. I know the iron cross. My brother used to bring it up in his calls, when he was sometimes drunk and sometimes very earnest. He’d say, It’s a stupid thing, an iron cross in the wind, nothing to say what it’s for, just there, rusting, like something the sea coughed out and the islanders put upright and said, There, that’s our thing, that’s our mark. He drew it for me once in a letter, a dismal little matchstick thing white on blue ink. I put the letter in a shoebox that also holds our father’s old driver's license, the photograph where his moustache is trying too hard. I have not opened that box in years. When I get home I think I will put it in the bin and then change my mind and put it under a different bed.

I used to call my brother when our father was dying and say, You should come. He would say, I am working and besides, what would we do? Sit and wait and time will do what time does. He had that conviction then that all things would happen whether or not he was there to see them. Or he had a fear of small rooms with ordinary light. It didn’t matter, in the end. Our father died on a Wednesday afternoon while I was in the kitchen heating soup and the radio said there had been a small earthquake far away. I left the soup to burn and called the care nurse and then the undertakers and then, at the very end of the list, my brother. He did not answer. He called back three days later and said, I'm sorry I didn't answer, I was at sea. I said, He is gone. He said, I know how that is. Then he said, There was a whale that came up right alongside us yesterday, close enough you could have touched her if you’d wanted, and all the boys just went quiet. He said, It's a kind of mercy when something is that big, that close, and just chooses to do nothing except watch you for the length of a breath.

On the ferry a boy with acne is taping a closed sign to the slot machines. There is no one at them anyway. He holds the tape with his teeth while he stretches it across the glass. He sticks the ends down and then peels and resticks until he gets them at the right angle. He wants it to be clean, to be right. When he finishes he looks up and sees that I am watching and flushes pink above his collar and zips his hoodie. I give him a small nod that is meant to be nothing and he goes away fast enough to be a run.

The tea is gone, though I do not remember drinking it. I stand and take the mug back to the bar and set it there, and the barman says, “You all right?” and I say, “Yes” and we both pretend that means something. I walk to the aft deck door and test it and it opens and a fist of air comes in, huge and shock cold. A woman shrinks away from it in her sleep, uncoils her feet and then repins them.

Outside: the kind of cold that has its own grain, that goes like sand over your skin and finds the seams in your coat and saws at you there. A net of gulls floats in the ferry’s wake, white knots in a wet dark. The air tastes of metal. The stern shakes its bright flags of light over the churned sea and those flags unroll and curl and vanish. Past the rail, the edge of the world is close and low; night has a bottom here you could strike if you were not careful. In the far black, two red lights climb and pause and climb again; a trawler working, or the lights on the ladder up to an oil platform, a false horizon.

When we were small we would lie on our backs at the bottom of the stairs and call out our names to hear what the house did to them. His came back full and mine came back thin. My brother had a chest that did something with sound, that set it even without trying, and I was always trying. He sang when he worked, when he washed, when he mended the wire in the kitchen while mother was at work and father was asleep before the late shift. He sang the same song until the words became nothing except shape in the mouth.

I go back inside after my face begins to lose sensation in the skin below my eyes. The woman who shrank from the cold before has a baby clamped to her now, the baby a small angry heat source visible only by the seam of a hat. A man across from them reads a thriller in which a gun is being described to me at length. The flat gleam of the porthole shows me all of us in a faint reflection, like a family portrait where no one arranged themselves properly. I look too long at the blur that is me, so that I become dizzied by the double world, and then I concentrate instead on the seat fabric in front of me, the repeat of tiny hammers or anchors or sprigs of leaves, depending how you angle your head. The pattern becomes a low-lying field and I move across it, slow and heavy as a cow.

In the middle of the night the ferry tilts toward a memory of itself and I have to stand and brace. A bottle rolls on its side and bumps at brief intervals against the foot of a chair. The only good way to be on a boat like this is to consent to be the boat, the way you let your knees be the street when you cycle. A man says, “Jesus” under his breath and someone’s prayer beads strike a click inside his pocket. I wonder if the boy with the closed sign has found a place to lie down, whether the crew quarters have curtains or just compartments, whether he is lying with his hood up, a little cold at the feet, the boat telling him secrets as it tells them to all of us, the same secrets said a different way to each.

I book a cabin after all. The barman looks at me and says, “Ah,” as if I were a swallow who had turned before the wind and found the right ridge of it. He takes my card. The cabin number is written on a printed paper. I go down a corridor that narrows and narrows until I think I will not fit, and then the door is there and it opens toward me and the room is a plastic miracle: a bed folded down from the wall, a bolted chair, a small desk with a fixed notepad and a pen on a plastic chain. The bath is a cubby with a long silver hose for water. The window is a circle, two hands’ spans, and it is pitch outside but I stand there anyway and look. There is a coat hook and it takes my coat as if it were a friend I am introducing it to. I sit on the bed and the mattress makes a noise like rain. It will be warm later. I know the heat of the cabin will be something to climb out of if I sleep, and that it will be most terrible to be half asleep and fighting with your coat in a hot room because the ferry is a pitching animal.

I doze. In sleep I am eleven and my brother is fifteen and he has borrowed a small boat from a man with viscous eyes who lives near the harbor. We go to the headland, where the tide is a fat, playful thing with a vicious core to it. We have brought bread we think is for us, then see it is for the gulls, and we throw it and the gulls come, bright and shameless, their orange eyes the color of weak yolks. My brother takes off his shirt, as if to prove something, and goes into the water to feel how strong he is. The tide pretends to gather him up and then does, and he wants to be seen taken, so he laughs and shouts and looks back at me on the rock, and then he is further out than I can get to without going in too, and I feel the whole water shift its attention, and I step back and then forward and then back. He makes a circle of his hand, meaning I am fine, and disappears into the dull green and then comes up with weed on his head as if he is making himself a joke. That night I go home and put my hands on the table and still can feel the pull of the water through my roots. In the dream, which pretends it is a memory, he comes into my bedroom late and sits on the bed and tells me not to be afraid of drowning. "The body knows more than you think it does," he says. "It is like a dog. It swims home."

I wake with sweat damp under my arms and my hair damp at the neck. The cabin is hot in the way a room gets when the heater is doing only one thing. I strip the duvet, sleep under the sheet, wake again to the kilning smell of myself. It is three in the morning by the porthole clock. I get up and run water in the small basin until it goes cold and splash my face and the back of my neck. The ferry strikes a swell and the water leaps with my hands and makes a child of me: I'm a boy again, splashing in a dim light. The metal underfoot sings in couplets I do not know the language for. I look into the mirror and see the man I have carried to this place and he is no one I would say hello to on the street. He would not say hello to me either. He is one of those men whose mouth has learned to tuck in on one side to hold what he is feeling in reserve.

When our mother left it was not a leaving people could see. She stayed in the house but took herself somewhere else, and we had to guess at the unpeopled rooms she now preferred. She kept our dinner warm in the oven and arranged the plates the same way but sometimes you would see her in the shed staring at the tools as if they were a set of maps. Father thought he could fix it by fixing other things. The lawnmower had its belt replaced. The gutters were cleaned. The hammer got a new grip. There was something murderous in how much he repaired. Nat, my brother, would go out just then and find a reason to be late back. He would come home smelling of someone else’s smoke. He would look at the ceiling while father talked and I think that is part of why father grew the moustache, to see if he could cast a little shadow there on Nat's bright denial. It is stupid to make a story of these particulars and say: this is why he went north, this is why I stayed. We were not instruments of a logic. We were boys, and then men, and we did what we thought we knew how to do, which was less than we needed.

I hear footsteps in the corridor, padded, respectful, as if all the shoes have agreed not to shame anyone asleep. The ferry makes a low complaint and then booms, in its gut, and then is calmer. Outside the window there is no line yet to say where night lets morning have a little. I get into my coat again, leave the cabin, shut the door quietly like I have a sleeping thing inside to be kind to. Up on the deck it is less cold than before because something is beginning to happen in the air, some loosening. The sky is the color of the belly of a dead fish. The stern wake is still white as the inside of a tooth. I lean on the rail and feel the rail’s life through my coat, the minute high songs of its vibration. I close my eyes and count to sixty and open them again to see if the horizon is more itself. A gull that has never left the boat for the whole night—how do I know it is the same gull? I don’t; it just is—is perched on the light just as if it belongs there and has a job. It looks thoughtful, which is to say it has a cruelty about it that is organized.

My phone buzzes again and it is a message this time from a number I do know. It is a woman I have sometimes had an arrangement with, which is too crisp a way to say how we kept each other from being too alone on Thursday nights last winter. She writes: Are you all right? and then, because this is a conversation of sorts, writes: I heard there was an accident on the line today. Are you traveling? I text: On the ferry. Will call later. May not have reception. She writes a heart and then writes, later: I dreamed you were a tree last night. I looked out the window and you were a tree in the neighbor’s garden. You were trying to come in the door sideways and you could not because both your arms were branches. I smile out loud at this in the dark, the kind of laugh you do that is more breath than sound. I do not tell her anything of where I am going or why. She and I agreed to know only the parts of each other that appealed to us and leave the rest.

The sky phases blue in a single gesture, like someone has pulled a sheet under it. Then pearling begins at the rim. Then there are actual shapes to the cloud, and I see the land’s first line come up as if the sea is making it, as if the dark is a great loaf being sliced. The ferry announces itself with the ship’s horn, one two, listening for its own echo. People wake under piles of coats and look irritated with the idea of arrival. The hot food counter opens and the smell of bacon complicates the morning. I go and line up with the men and women with thick boots and order eggs and bacon and a fried tomato and black pudding, and then do not finish any of it, knowing that there is something coming that will not be helped by greasy stomach. I watch a dock hand in a photograph on the wall—there is a framed set: the ferry, the port, the cliffs—his face so out-of-focus that he is all emotion, and I decide he is in love with someone who does not know it yet.

We come in under the bluff with the weather mast that looks like a great radio exploded in a careful way. The harbor has that cold closeness that all harbors have in the morning: everything over-ready, the ropes sorted, the nets mended, the chains all pulled into obedient loops. The ferry turns her hips to the ferrule of rock and the gap of the dock, and another horn sounds, a shorter one, a heraldic little bravado. I put on my bag and go down again into the green stairwell with the others. Our collective breathing has a sheepishness to it. On the car deck men in yellow shout to each other in an invented language of vowels and point with flat hands. The ramp drops and there it is, the wet bright of land, and the smell of the harbor, which is metal and fish and the thin old iodine of weed, and people are already ready with cigarettes on their lips and cars that stink of dogs, and we pour down into it like the ferry is a boot emptying itself of small stones.

He is there, the policeman, because who else would wear that much dayglo at this hour and stand perfectly straight in it. He is a boy, just as his voice said, in the way all policemen are now boys to me, which is both age and a private fear of being told by a child what I must do. He has someone with him, an older man with a gut, who is the sergeant and knows it. The boy says my name to make my attention a thing, and I make my face into attention. He looks relieved as if a sum had worked itself out and he had been worried of and for it.

“Mr. Haines,” he says. “I’m Callum. This is Sergeant Blay.” The sergeant sticks out his hand and I take it and it is a stubby, businesslike hand, the grip not too much, not too little; he has shook a lot of men. “We’ll take you along now if that’s okay,” the sergeant says.

“It’s okay,” I say.

We walk to the car, which is a small hatchback, absurd in its effort to be a police car, its idea of authority standing on tiptoe. I get into the back. The floor behind the front seat has grit that has crushed last night and become this morning. Callum drives and the sergeant sits with one hand on his thigh, the other on the dashboard as if the car might run from them. The island is plain in this light. It has not made plans for us, and will not. The houses are crouches. The cemetery is both too big and too small. A dog looks like a man with low arms from behind, and then is a dog. The road throws itself toward the town and then away from it again in the way roads do on small islands when they are all connections and no destination.

“We have a facility,” the sergeant says. “As I said on the phone last. Not fancy, but…” He waits, but I don’t say anything and he goes on. “We will go slow. There’s no rush. We took photographs as well. Sometimes the photographs are easier. You can say stop, or ask us to take a break. There will be no pressure. We know it can be… We know it is.” He stops because he cannot find the part of the sentence where the verb ought to go. “You knew your brother well?” he says, which is the question that people ask in these contexts who have never had a brother, I think.

“We were not speaking,” I say.

“Right,” the sergeant says. “Right. That’s—right.”

“It’s been eight years. More.”

“Fine,” the sergeant says, as if we have agreed on a neutral setting for the sun. “And your last address for him is the one we had on file, at—”

“He moved around. He found work where it was. He wrote sometimes but he didn’t like to say where he slept. He had—” I stop. I almost say, He had pride and then think better of it, because it isn’t that. He had a fury that he disguised as something else. He had a way of finding the parts of himself that hurt the most and polishing them.

“Right,” the sergeant says again, yet it is a different word each time he says it, he has that at least: he modulates.

We pass the iron cross. It is exactly as described: a cross made of upright beams of iron set in concrete, rust weeping, a thing some men made one day and named a marker, not a memorial, not anything except itself. Two gulls sit on it like thoughts. A man in a red hat walks a dog that is the kind of dog that people call good and the dog is being good, which is to say it is doing what it would have done anyway but now it’s a reward for it. I lean my head a fraction toward the window to honor the place without doing anything as much as gesture. The car hums. Callum checks the mirror because that is what he has learned to do. When I was eighteen, checking the mirror seemed like the act that would keep me alive. Now it is as useful and as little as anything else.

We pull into a car park beside a building that could be any building anywhere there is weather. The walls are pebbledash, the windows old. There is a ramp. The sign is in both languages, the island and the other, set one above the other, the island letters like fence posts. We get out and my legs do not complain though I thought they might. My bag is very light and I want to leave it in a hedge. The sergeant holds the door in a way that says the door has held itself for years and will be insulted, but I am grateful anyway because of the shape it makes of things.

Inside: heat that has been too strong for too long and now smells of dry mop and the faint stink of a drain that should have been attended to. A woman at a desk with hair the color of straw cut at an even line across the bottom looks up and says my name with care and then says, "There’s tea," because this place knows it must offer something, something for the hands, something for the mouth. I say, "No, thank you," and then, because of the way her mouth pinches with disappointment, I say, "Yes, please," and take the chipped mug and do not drink.

There is a corridor and at the end of the corridor, a heavy door. We do not go to it yet. They put me in a small room that has a plant in it doing very well, some kind of fern, as if it eats the things that we breathe out and are too heavy for us. There is a poster on the wall that says, in the island letters and the other language, "You are not alone." I have an unkind thought about the accuracy of posters. Then the unkindness flattens into tiredness. The sergeant sits in a chair that is slightly too small and does an elaborate folded thing with himself so as not to seem baffled by it. Callum remains standing. The sergeant says, "We can show you some photographs first, if you like. Sometimes it’s less—" He lifts his fingers and makes a small fizzing motion with them, which is, in all honesty, exactly right: less.

I say, without thinking, "How long was he in the water?"

Callum looks at the sergeant, who gives him one second of permission, and says, "Not long. Maybe the night. It’s cold. It keeps things. There were—" He stops, because there is a word he will not use, a word for small things that are not small in number. "There were birds. It’s the time of year. They’re hungry. But we think—it was not very long."

The sergeant looks at me. I nod. I have always liked the fact of birds. It is useful to be blunt about their appetites.

They bring a folder. The woman with the straw hair brings it with a severity, as if she is making sure it does not escape. She puts it on the table and then lays her hand flat on it for a moment, not looking at it, and then takes her hand away. I think of all the hands that have rested on other folders in this room. The sergeant undoes the elastic and opens the cover. There is a smell of old paper that has taken a little moisture into itself. There are the photographs. They have not done that thing where they print them on glossy paper and make a thing of it; they are simple printouts, flat, the pixels visible if you move your face close. The sergeant turns the first one and turns it again so that it faces me.

I do something with my face to make it a face that looks at pictures of bodies. I had thought that I could practice this and then do it, but you cannot practice this, in the way you can’t practice falling: the thing itself occurs and reoccurs without you. The photograph is a man on a gurney with a sheet to his chest. He is so pale the light goes through him in places and turns them blue. His eyes are closed, one closed better than the other. There is a cut on the lip that is a mouth in a mouth and looks like it would have hurt to drink hot tea. The hair is longer than my brother wore it when I last saw him. The mouth is a different mouth than the one I last saw because I last saw it with laughter in it and this one has nothing in it, all the furniture moved out. The nose is my father’s nose. The eyebrow on the left breaks and makes two strokes like someone wrote an unfinished word. The fingers, visible at the bottom of the frame, have their own stories I cannot hear yet. There is a small tattoo on the shoulder. It is a bird. It has been bled of definition by salt, so it might be a bird or it might be a sail, you cannot know.

"Take your time," the sergeant says. "We can put it away. Or not. We can do both."

There is a clock on the wall that almost ticks but the sound dies inside it. The fern lifts one of its fronds and lets it fall again with an importance that is not important. The room is built for the kind of silence that rushes away when anything moves and then rushes back again. I look at the second one. In this one there is a ruler by his head to show the measure of it, because men love to measure. The ear is a good ear. When we were boys, my brother would rub his ear with the back of his hand when he lied, and the ear would flush as hot as a coal. When he first came home after being away for two years, and stood in the doorway in winter wearing his new coat that no longer smelled of the house, the ear colored and I thought, He is still mine in there somewhere. Now the ear is a pale shell and I cannot see whether it would flush if I spoke his name.

I say, "I need to see him."

The sergeant looks at me to be sure I want it, and then he nods to Callum and Callum nods to the woman with the hair, and there is a choreography to grief in official places; everyone knows their steps. We go down the corridor to the heavy door. The woman with the hair punches a code into a panel and the door unlocks in the same way all doors in all buildings unlock—there is only one kind of hum, always—and then we are in a small cold room with nothing on the walls and a trolley in it covered with a white sheet. The room smells like old cold air, like the inside of your hands in winter when you push them under your armpits. The sergeant says a thing, but I do not hear it because the room is the way rooms are where the shape of what will happen is so big it fills them.

I walk to the trolley and put my hand on the rail and it is colder than the room. The woman with the hair takes hold of the sheet and folds it back a little. She is gentle in the way people are gentle with bread and babies and nothing else. The face is the face from the picture but it is also a different face because the light is different and because I am here making air with my lungs in the room. The eyes are open a little—not a lot—and there is a whiteness to the white of them that is like the backs of spoons. The mouth has fallen and has not fallen back. The hair has dried into a private wind shape. There is a mark at the collarbone that could be where the skin rubbed against a rope when he hoisted something. The throat is so defenseless. I have never seen my brother’s throat like this. I have seen him sweat at football, I have seen him laugh and curse and sleep open-mouthed in a bus with his cheek on a stranger’s shoulder, I have seen him cut his lip on a glass and touch his tongue to the cut with that strange tenderness we always have for the parts of ourselves that hurt, but I have not seen this: the tall lovely gab of the throat peeled of its noises. The smell is human and salt and something like metal that is not metal. There is a bruise high on the cheek that might be new; there are freckles I do not remember. The ear is right there.

"It’s him," I say, and the words make a sound in the room that changes the shape of it. Then, immediately, without thinking, I say, "It isn’t." The words both exist, they hang between us like washing. I put my hand to my own mouth and then take it away again. "I don’t know," I say. The part of me that is a boy in a garden is here; the part of me that is a man who signs forms is here too. My breath fogs a small part of the cold air and for a moment blurs the line of his forehead and then vanishes and the forehead is the same. The woman with the hair stands very still.

The sergeant says, "It’s all right." It is very possible he says this every day to someone, but it does a thing in me anyway. "We can take prints," he says, "we can do dental, we can do the whole. We just have to start somewhere. Sometimes, it’s the shoes," he says, as if that is a story. "Sometimes people know the shoes."

"They took his boots," the woman says, a fact offered into the room with the clean hand of care. "We had to. They were wet through. We dried them, in case."

"Can I—" I say, and then don't finish it.

"Yes," the sergeant says. I had not yet asked the question, but he knew what all questions mean here. "You can touch," he says, and it is obscene and grateful how I feel it.

I put my hand out and touch the hair first, because it is a thing that has always been touched. It is stiff where the salt has made it a machine. Then I touch the shoulder, above the sheet, with one finger. It is cold, but not like stone. It is like a wall that has not seen sun. My finger stays and then my hand, and then I take it away. I have not said his name. I do not know which name to use. After he left, he tried on names like coats. Nathan, he said, call me Nathan because I am not the Nat who had to clean father’s tools and look for mother in the breakfast chair. Then later he said, call me Nate, as if the name might help him climb into a country where no one had known him. I kept calling him Nat on the phone and he did not correct me because it would be a lot of work and we didn’t like work with each other in that way.

The woman covers the face again with that gentle exactness. We stand and do not look at each other. We stand as men do with a problem with their hands: they have hands and nowhere for them. The sergeant clears his throat, which is both an act and an apology for the act, and says, "We can give you some time. There’s a room. There’s—" He runs out of furniture. He wants to offer biscuits and cannot. Callum puts his hand on the door handle as if to say, Say when.

I take a breath and somewhere inside the breath is a small, solid place where a fact sits: if it is him I have said a thing into the world now that will not be unmade, and if it isn’t, I have also said a thing, a different thing, that will make other things happen. I think of a game we used to play when we were boys: he would hide in the house and I would call and he would not answer and I would cry, because it is in me to cry when a person I love does not answer. He would come out then and laugh and say, You’re so soft, and because we were the ages we were, he would say it to be cruel, but he would also put his hand on the back of my neck because it wasn’t only cruelty. He would say, We will be dead for most of our lives, so there’s no need to practice. I had no idea, then, that he was making a vow I could not hear. Or maybe he wasn’t. Maybe he was just a boy being a boy, and I have carried it, as we carry the wrong things sometimes for years.

"Can I see the boots?" I say. I do not know what I think will be in them, some imprint of him. The woman with the hair nods and goes away and then the door makes its smallized hum and then opens again and she is there with a blue plastic tub in which are the boots, heavy, dark, a size I know without looking at the label. They are old enough that the leather has the patina of a thing that has bent to the same shape of foot many times. There is a scratch on the left one, arc-shaped, from where he would have dragged it against some iron to get up a ladder. I remember, at a barbecue one year when he came with a woman named Lorna who had a plait that came all the way down her back, he took his boot off because a coal had jumped and gotten down around his sock, and the boot lay on the grass and a bee went in it and we all laughed and he said, I’m going to be a hive, and then he overturned the boot and shook it and the bee came out mildly angry and we apologised to it in that human way of ours that means nothing to the bee at all. These boots could be those boots, or a hundred other boots. There is a grain of sand inside the right one, stuck in the join.

"Are you all right?" the woman asks.

"I don’t know who he is," I say, suddenly furious with the room’s arrangement, with the way all this will become a document. "I haven’t known who he is in years. He—" I am about to say, He refused to let me know him, but that would be a lie of a kind. We refused each other. We did it as if it were a thing the world wanted, one of us to stay, one to go, as if we were not allowed to want the same long hall of ordinary days with the same light falling on the same chair and the same noise of the kettle on. We did it because we were matching each other, step for step, without ever looking down.

The sergeant does a thing I have only ever seen uncles do: he leans forward and puts his elbows on his knees and holds his hands together loosely as if he is giving his hands a little rest on each other. "Let’s do the rest," he says. "Let’s do all the pieces. We will take your time from you and give it back to you at the end. You won’t know what to do with it, but we will give it to you. We will find out if he is your brother or if he is someone else’s brother. Either way he is somebody’s. He is somebody’s even if that somebody is gone. That’s what we do here on Tuesdays," he says, and I want to laugh because it is so ordinary and so impossible.

He stands then and reaches for the folder again, and I look past him through the small high window that shows only the sky. It is the kind of sky that looks made of sheep. The iron cross is somewhere behind us, rusting, being itself, the gulls landing on it with that certainty they have that they have not done wrong to us because we are not in their story. The ferry, back at the dock now emptying or filling, will have a boy taping a sign again and getting the angle wrong and making it right. The engine’s hubbub will be a nest for another hour for people who must sleep like thieves. The strait we crossed all night will be making its low noises, and somewhere on its surface a thing will be surfacing with nothing on its mind but air.

"All right," I say. My voice comes out of my mouth and returns, a smaller animal. "All right."
