The glass of the revolving door held my reflection and a blurred world of lilies behind it, both of us wavering as if on water. A bellman in gloves pressed his palm to the panel to slow me, and I stepped into cool air that smelled of lemon and starch, the clean press of money.

In the lobby, the ceiling rose in a white vault dotted with new sprinklers like beads. They caught the light and gave it back. A piano was playing a song that sounded like water poured into a glass and the hush of hundreds of ice cubes. The lilies were as tall as my thigh and leaned as if they had been listening for a long time.

“Welcome,” said a young woman behind the reception desk. Her name tag, slender and gold, said Zaybe. “Checking in for the Reinhardt wedding?”

I had rehearsed a smile. It fit without practice. “Yes. Two rooms, Santos and Wexler.”

She tapped and smiled back and slid key cards across the stone counter as if dealing in promises. “We have you on seven and eight, city view. Elevators are just there. Please enjoy the chilled towels.” She gestured toward a tray with damp, cloth-wrapped disks like surgical packs, and a man with a bar cart in miniature asked if we’d like a glass of something grapefruit and botanical.

Will took the towel, the drink, the keys. He was ahead by six polished tiles before I was moving again. He reached for my bag and I held it tighter.

“I’ve got it,” I said. It was a small bag. I had learned to pack as if rooms could burn, as if you might be called to a stairwell with nothing but your own breath to carry. Will kissed the top of my head as if I were resting there.

“Fancy,” he said, low and appreciative, the word moving through his chest.

On the wall opposite the elevators a slab of pale stone was inset, names chiseled in an exacting hand. There was no dust in the cut letters. Someone had been here with a cloth this morning. The title said In Remembrance, the way they title waiting areas and lounges.

My hand moved of its own to the litany of surnames. My finger didn’t fully touch but hovered the way you hover your palm over a stove to feel the ghosts of heat. It was a list the length of wet hair: Akers, Bhatia, Clements. There were the Vaughns, two of them. There was M. Ruiz. There was C.C.—no full names for staff, I’d heard, for their privacy. There were some I didn’t know or couldn’t match to faces, and there was a space in the list where the lowercase letters gathered.

“Hey,” Will said, his voice careful because we both had agreed, in theory, to careful. “You cool?”

I nodded and slid my finger down to the bottom edge of stone, cold and softened as if it wanted to be thumbed. “Seven and eight?”

“We’ll do your room first,” Will said, looking relieved to have directions. He liked a plan and a set of stairs on paper. He liked buildings and renovation. He’d sent me photos of this lobby months ago when the wedding venue was announced. “Looks like they knocked out a wall,” he’d said, eyes full of diagrams, and I had nodded into the phone in a room that was then someone else’s.

The elevators were quick and mirrored. They made a small sigh for each floor and then a careful chime as if someone had matched forks. As we rose, my stomach went weightless with the old trick of air in a box. I looked away from myself and into the seam where the doors slid. Already I could map the elevator’s path in the hidden shaft, feel the square of black it traced up and up, the border of the place I had known and the new skin around it.

In the room the air was cooler, drier. A stripe of city cut through the window; beyond, the river’s close shoulder. The bed was white and squared like a gift, the sheet corners knife-sharp in the way my hands remembered. There was the arrangement on the nightstand: a bottle of water with an excavated droplet of condensation, a ceramic dish with two wrapped chocolates, a pencil not sharpened. The bathroom’s little bottles were arranged from short to tall so your hand found them in the dark: shampoo, conditioner, lotion with the serif labels that could have been perfume.

My body, trained like a dog, wanted to straighten what had been straightened. I tucked my hands into my elbows and looked at the place where a housekeeper with smaller hands than mine had left a filament of hair, pale as a remnant of a web, near the base of the lamp. My heart ticked hard once in pleasure at the imperfection. It said: people are here. It said: hands.

Will had set his bag on the bench. He walked to the window and spread his palm on the glass. “I told you they made it better,” he said. “It looks like the photos. Maybe nicer.”

“That’s their skill set,” I said, and my voice was too sharp, like a nail clipped too close. He turned.

“I know,” he said. “I know, babe.”

He had met me two years after the fire, after I had moved across the river and taken a job at a bakery where the ovens were electric and person-high. He knew the outline of my story in the true way someone learns it if they didn’t live the heat with you; he knew where I coughed in the night when someone was grilling down the block. He knew the sound of my back against the kitchen cabinets at the smell of bacon. He didn’t know the shape of ceiling plaster above you when your lungs decide they are pockets.

The room key was a warm rectangle in my hand; the insert and draw of it into light was the same as it had been—then a red wink before green, a permission the size of a pinhole.

“I’m going to rinse,” I told him. “Then you can shower.”

It was a joke, and he caught it, and we were better.

Under the water, I watched my shoulders go from the chalk the city gives them to something washed. There was the small hurt of heat on the thinnest skin, the release of steam into my head. I closed my eyes and did not see dark. I had trained myself for that. I had practiced looking at the backs of my lids as if they were sky.

When I came out in a towel, hair hung to drip down my spine, Will was sprawled on the bed, his shirt off, his fingers tracing the brocade of the coverlet like he was reading it.

“So, we’re not late for the thing tonight?” he said. “Rehearsal dinner, six-thirty. Cocktails at six.”

“We’re not late,” I said. The time was a blade I could hold with two fingers, cold and manageable.

He kissed my shoulder as he went past me to the shower. There was a little bit of him that carried soap always, a ghost note of it in the pocket of his chest. When he had gone behind the door and the water’s change in sound told me he was beneath it, I went to the closet and pressed both palms against its frame, my forehead to the edge, and held myself as if I might come apart along my seams.

The old clock in my head began its litany without my asking. It spoke in the voice of the supervisor I’d had at twenty-one, a woman named Teresita, who swore she would never answer to Tess. Look where you are when you close a door. Keep your back to the hinge. Don’t get boxed. Leave the cart at an angle in case. Restock and rest when you can. Don’t fall in love with a view. Use your hip to push.

I let the voice run and wash out. I had not been back here since. That was the point of tonight: not penance, not bravery, just a return with a dress and someone else’s last name on a place card.

When Will came out, hair slicked into dark spears, I was sitting on the little bench that let you see down into the street’s movement the way a tall child can peer into a parade. He toweled his head with sharp quick movements like a dog banishing swim.

“You’ll tell me if you need to go,” he said.

“I will,” I said.

We went down on a river of other bodies. The elevator ad screen told us what menus we would eat and what spa packages would change us into various types of petal. At the bottom the lilies were now leaning in a direction I didn’t remember. The light through the glass was different, gold and wool-soft, all the hard edges in the day’s air dulled.

The bar that had once been the service closet now had a gleaming brass rail and stools upholstered in navy leather the color of new bruises. Where I had once parked my cart and used my gloved hand to keep the closet door open with the heel of a laundry bag, men in thin jackets leaned with a curve to their backs that suggested they were used to being built sunlight.

The Reinhardt party had taken the far half of the dining room, which opened into the ballroom with a wall that would slide if you pressed a button. There were white tablecloths flocked with silverware and parents in dresses the color of real fruit. A cousin with a blond ponytail had her arm hooked in the groom’s as if he were a lucky door. The bride—Sam—was in citron silk that made her skin glow like something living underneath oceans. Her laugh was clear and unpracticed as if she had not yet said anything wrong to anyone.

Will’s arm at my waist was light, a ribbon at most, a thing I could unwrap and not drop. He steered me toward the group like a man bringing a horse to a new fence.

“We’re not really going to know anybody,” he said behind his hand. “I’ll stick to you.”

“You can go be social,” I said. “You have faces you’re meant to make in your phone.”

“Only your face,” he said, and his eyes were kind, untrained in the way of witnessing made public. He kissed me as if to prove his sentence and not to make a bouquet out of me.

I let it be. The piano turned from water to a series of small falling things; a server passed with a tray of spoons cradling something pink and raw that wavered like tongue. I took a spoon. The spoon was the perfect weight, the fish clean, the starch on my tongue immediate and old, and then the server was gone and my mouth was empty.

Introductions rolled like marbles. Will was friendly with a groomsman whose beard made his face look borrowed. There was a woman with a stark collar and a laugh that cracked like ice; she introduced herself as the designer of the invitations and said, with double irony, that the color of the lettering was Smoke. Another_guest, a man who looked like a plowed field, said he worked on the rebuild.

“Ninety-four weeks, thousand trades,” he said, leaning to be heard over the piano. “We didn’t sleep. The sprinkler heads? Custom. The glass? Tempered to within an inch of armageddon.”

“That supposed to comfort me?” I said, and he smiled at me as if I were making a joke.

“Phoenix,” said a woman behind me, patting my bare shoulder where my dress didn’t cover. Her hand was cool and perfumed of pear. “My son said it’s like the myth. Rising from it.”

“You cut off the head of a snake and call it a poem,” someone said not far away, and men near us laughed as if they all understood myth.

The new general manager rose to speak a welcome. A woman with a microphone introduced him with the words beloved cornerstone and community, and he stood with his hands placed as if blessing a loaf. His hair was white the way sugar is before it has touched anything. He spoke of the years of dust and blueprint and steel and of how good it was to be back, to be home.

The word emptied my chest. Home was a thing you cleaned for people whose children colored with expensive pencils on laminated placemats. Home was where you went to sleep with the smell of another person's orange shampoo in your hair. Home burned no matter whose key ring you had.

I excused myself. I slid around a table with a centerpiece elevated on a glass stem, lights strung like stars around its bloom, and found the corridor that led to the restrooms under a bank of framed archival photos that had never existed until the rebuild to suggest a past. A hostess with a black book gave me a bright nod and a smile practicing for when she would warm into effortless. The corridor was long and carpeted to quiet the tabs of heels. The restroom’s air was cooler, moved by a different lung. It was all mirror, making sameness as an answer to mess.

I bent over the sink. My palms made islands of water. In the mirror my mouth was the color it had been when I was twenty, when my lips were bitten down because an apron string had been pulled too tight or because someone had laughed at my English, which gapped and forgot under the pressure of offhand talk. I touched my throat. A woman in yellow passed a tissue to me as if I were weeping.

“Just taking a breath,” I said.

“Big night,” she said, and floated out like a wafer.

In the corner of the hall where the lights fell less brightly there was a door with a keypad—no sign, or only the discreet suggestion of Staff—and a hairline crack at its edge where a hinge would give. The door had a smell around it that wasn’t lobby; it was the smell of cold machines and laundry, of new concrete and whatever scent replaces bleach when bleach is a horror. Old air lived under doors like this: caught, remembered.

My finger, traitor, knew the code. I didn’t know it but my body did the old automatic count: one-two pause three-pause, the rhythm of access. The new code wasn’t that but the pause held like a breath you forget you’re holding until your jaw aches.

The door opened by luck with a push from the other side; a woman came through with a plastic bin held against a hip. It made a squeak where it rubbed her. She was older than me by ten years maybe, face set to a neutral that work gives you. The uniform was slate gray, a T-shirt under a jacket with a badge that said Hotel and below it her first name: Rosa.

The hair came up on my arms the way cats’ hair rises when the vacuum starts in a far room. She paused, accommodating my body’s surprise, and then her hand came off the bin and found my face without thinking. She touched my cheekbone and then my chin as if learning if I were surface or bulb.

“Mara,” she said. She had not said my name in six years and it came out like the first word a child says in a language you didn’t know they had learned. “Santa Maria,” she said, and laughed and cried in the same small breath. “Look at you.”

The bin slid down her hip and made a little sound like an animal being set to nap. Her palms framed my face as if I were of glass. I pressed my hands to her arms, the hum of muscle and life there.

“You’re here,” she said, as if the word was a question and an accusation and a blessing. Her hands fell to take mine in a grip that spoke of buckets and weight. “You look good. You look—different.”

“I sell cherry turnovers to people who think they are buying a feeling about being children,” I said, because there were too many sentences that could go where the space was. Rosa’s laugh was a low rolling thing that warmed.

“You always had your mouth,” she said. “And your eyes.” She glanced down toward the door, back-of-house, where someone had said her name. “You came for the wedding? We have seventeen tablecloths that will never see this kind of food again.” She tipped her chin toward the door as if to say, come.

I followed her into the space my skin knew before my eyes did. The floors were smooth and painted gray and held the wheels of carts like an agreement. The air had the constant whirr and hum of a body asleep under a thin sheet. This was the place of keys on retractable cords, of lists with boxes to tick, of a coffee maker older than any of the staff with a stain under it the color of weathered walnut. The bulletin board had a flyer about CPR in both languages and a clipped comic where someone had pasted the heads of our old supervisor and manager onto a drawing of ducks in hats.

I saw, on a low shelf, a pile of folded terry cloth with the edges aligned the way I used to align them so that they fit people’s hands without catching. My own fingers twitched like fish.

“We talk about you,” Rosa said. “Even the ones who don’t know who you are, they know. They think you’re myth.” She said the word without condescension. It landed like a dish placed very gently on a table. “The one who went room to room and pounded like a cop, who pulled a man out by his elbow while he tried to take his suitcase. The one who would not go home after, only sat and held Antonia’s mother’s hand and made sweet tea all night until the worst was over.”

“I did what anyone did,” I said, and it came out quick, a reflex, a shield with a hole in it the size of the shape that I had failed to save. There was a list of those things in me that did not have names on the hotel’s slab.

Rosa’s eyes flicked to the side. She crossed herself lightly in the way of habit rather than belief. “Not anyone,” she said. “You were a runner, you always were. Your legs. The way you didn’t wait when the alarm bells went crazy.” She swore in Spanish. “We are similar that way, you and me. Something breaks, and we move.”

I let myself sit in a chair that wobbled by a bolt’s width. Rosa poured coffee from the stained maker into a thick white mug that had a crack not all the way through. It tasted of something bitter and unafraid, and it burned the back of my throat like a child sure of herself.

“Your cousin used to swear she’d never get married,” she said, and I blinked until the world in which Rosa thought this thing existed matched the world in which Sam was no one’s cousin of mine. “No, no, not yours.” She laughed at herself and made a motion as if to flap away confusion. “They use us up here. The ones who know where you can hide a broom and still find it in the dark. The ones who know which rooms smell like men. You want to see downstairs?”

I did, and I didn’t. Downstairs was the place of the laundry, the heat that rose from sheets even when the air was cold. It was the place where Teresita had taught us what chemicals ate what stains and how to triage your anger when a lobby man called you sweetheart because he could.

“Maybe tomorrow,” I said. “Maybe in the morning.”

“Ay,” Rosa said, hand lifted. “It is always tomorrow with the pain. Okay. You got a dress in your room? I want to see.”

I told her the color and she clapped her hands together as if I had invented a generous thing and given it to her. She touched my shoulder where my dress was bare in its V. The skin there was rough from sun, from ovens, from time.

“We thought you went away,” she said. “Like some of them, to other states. To churches. Some people do not go, you know.” She cocked her head, bird. “They stay and become ghosts at their own stove. I am not made for that. I have to move. When you are born standing up to reach the pan, you do not sit later.”

“I tried sitting,” I said. “My legs kept arguing with me.”

She snorted. Someone called her name again, sharper, and she rolled her eyes in the way of women who have had their names thrown like balls at them all their lives. “You go,” she said. “Eat rich people food. Stand where other people can see you. Come back in the morning. I’ll be here at six.”

“I’ll bring something sweet,” I said.

“Not those bakery things,” she said, mock-serious. “Real sugar poop. The candy from the bodega. I want my tongue to hurt.”

“I have a stash,” I confessed. “If the van makes it across.”

Her hand squeezed mine. “Ay,” she said, a word in which a thousand untranslatable things lived. “You always make me laugh. I’m glad you’re not dead.”

We said it like weather: the possibility, the gladness.

Back in the light of the public corridor, the piano had stopped. A woman’s voice was doing the rhythm of a toast, the up and then the settled down, the laugh before the story of a childhood misadventure that would make the adult now at the front of the room feel loved. It took a second for my eyes to widen around the new glare. The bar cart came very close and tilted its whole ocean at me; I set my empty coffee down as if I were in my own kitchen and the server with the carmine mouth hid a smile behind her hand, the secret handshake of service hiding in plain sight.

Will found me with an ease that I had not expected to be breath, his face bright with relief and beer. “You good?” he asked. “I kept a chair. There was a story about the groom and a goat that will probably never get told again.” He cupped my elbow, his palm full of warmth and bone. “Hey,” he said, looking at my face more closely. “Your color’s better.”

“I saw a friend,” I said. “From before.”

He searched my face for worry as if he could pluck it off me with his fingers and put it in his shirt pocket. “Does it feel like the same place?” he said. “I mean, I know they—”

“It doesn’t not,” I said. English is good for two negatives. It can braid them. “There’s hate in the walls. And love. And cheap paint. And better plumbing.”

He laughed, shaky. “You could list tangible assets,” he said. “Capital improvements include. You okay to stay for dessert?”

I thought of the kitchens, the wheeled racks of steel. The torching someone would do of a brûlée with a small fire you could keep in your hand. The sound that sugar makes when it fractures, soft as a scolded child.

“I can stay,” I said. “If we go back up on the early end.”

“We’ll Irish goodbye,” he said, and when the servers came with plates of cake in different, careful colors he took two and sat me away from the bulk of the room in a small curve of space near a ficus that made a performance of leaf in a column of light.

The cake was almond and something else, the crumb springing. I swallowed joy like a baby does: large, all body. Then came the billow of speeches, the father of the bride with both hands on the sides of the lectern as if it might tip otherwise, and his voice cracked at new and then again at always. The groom spoke of devotion without music from any church; the bride said a thank you to the hotel staff that was real, standing with her glass at the end, the slice of her shoulder bare, her hand open and warm. The staff lined the back wall, a seam of gray and black, and when she said the word all of them tilted slightly toward the light, a field of sunflowers obeying weather.

After, the music came like an open door. I saw the DJ’s hands light and precise in air the way a conductor’s are; I saw a little boy in a jacket hold both arms out like wings and tilt, making the air go under him like a lift. Will pulled me out and we did the old dance we do when we don’t have to count. His hand was respectful on my back and then it was not, and I let him have both, the good and the better. At one point, between a woman in a sequined dress with a hem made to be stepped on and a man with a tie around his head like a story weapon, I closed my eyes and the lights made a field against my lids that was not smoke but the afterimage of joy in a bulb.

When my chest tightened and my mouth went dry between one song and the next, when the DJ said at the mic the city’s name with a flourish, I excused myself and went to the stairs, not the elevator. The fire door at the end of the hall was heavy and set more firmly into its frame than it had been ten years ago. I opened it and set my foot on the first concrete slab. The air in the stairwell was cooler by degrees. It moved as if under a low sky, high enough to be free but not distant.

The stairs used to have a scuttle of cigarette butts in their corners and a smell made of lemon disinfectant and old gum. Now: nothing so human. A faint, almost sweet smell I could not place, maybe from the new paint that had tightened its skin over the walls and remembered the physics of a fire and the way heat will wake a color.

I sat down on the fourth step because that was the place I had sat once, ten years ago, where the smoke became a palpable thing that shaped your mouth. I had braced my feet against the step above and leaned my head back and counted to one thousand because someone said counting would keep your mind from turning yourself into an element. I had pressed my hand over my nose and mouth and listened for the frequency of the alarm because they say you can follow in that shriek for a time until you cannot.

It was quiet now except for the small steel ping in the pipe overhead where water sat ready. I could hear down below somewhere the faint voice of a woman and the gentle clank of something brushed against a metal face. Even the hotel at rest speaks, like sea.

It is a strange thing to wear your body where it lived a war. It is stranger to wear a dress and be in that space. I pressed the skin of my arm where my dress made a small, pretty gap. I leaned forward and put my ear to the cinder block wall. In childhood I believed you could hear ocean in a shell because the shell had once been meant for ocean. I listened now for anything those walls kept and let slip.

I heard, faintly, the laughter of a person I didn’t know, maybe a server making a joke between stacks of plates. I heard the sigh of the building itself, the way a large thing breathes without thinking. I did not hear fire. The absence of that was a howl in its way. It left me tender where a burn would not.

Upstairs the song changed, and the beat of it reached like the arm of a child under a door. Sometimes even in a mall or at the movies or on a corner where women are walking with paper cups I hear that reach and my throat closes. Now, instead, it opened, because it was the beat of men and women being foolish in their shoes, in their sequins, in their unhard arms.

I went back into it. In the corridor a server brushed past me with a tray of little balls of fried potato like music. She said, “Sorry,” and I said, “It happens,” the old call and answer. In the mirror by the fire extinguisher my face was flushed in the way of people who have danced. I touched my cheek and it was hot, the good heat.

We left before midnight. Will, true to his word, slid our bodies along the edge of the room and out the glass door as the bride threw her head back and laughed at something the bartender said. In the lobby the lilies looked wild, something set in a vase with a preliminary order and then allowed to go where they wanted. I touched one with a finger, the velvet of it. The night bellman nodded at me as if we were co-conspirators; perhaps he had invented a romance between me and my fear and was glad to be any kind of midwife.

In the room, Will bent and unbuckled his shoes the way boys do when they have been taught to put things in places. He lay back on the white bed and his mouth became slack in seconds, a good sleeper. I brushed my teeth until the foam was a clean chem and then rinsed with water colder than I expected from a hotel. In the dark I opened the curtains a hand’s width. The city made its soft talk. The river wore a sash of light like a girl at a pageant, all pageantry and still under that the smell of mud and fish.

I lay with my arms above my head, the way you lie when you want your lungs to open to receive. I sleep in positions a medic might approve. I almost drifted and then rose again, my brain like an unbroken horse you coax with sugar and patient hand. At last I slid sideways from the bed and opened the door to the hall on bare feet. The carpet was a polite hush. The exit sign at the end was the old red I recognized from a thousand nights and six long hours. It had the color of Catholic cards. Beneath it the door to the stairwell waited like a mouth in profile.

The elevator chimed somewhere like a bell at a small church. A cleaning cart rolled at the far end, the squeak of its wheel the same as the squeak in the dream I had in the first years, a sound that came just before the room filled with rising brown water, not fire but another shape of hunger. The woman behind the cart had headphones in; she was singing under her breath to something only for her. I could have waved. I could have said a word in the shared tongue of women waking in hotels to make things so.

I put my palm on the cool metal of the stair door and pressed, very slightly, as if the door and I were in conversation. It relented with a sound I knew, a hinge that had been told to hush. I slipped inside and did not descend, only stood on the square at seven, face lifted to the red glow of the exit sign, red like an old pulse. I leaned my forehead to the cinder block. The paint there was satin, a finish made to wipe away any greasy hand. I left no mark. I did not need, any longer, to leave the salt of me as proof.

In the morning, the city was a chalk drawing someone had gone over with water so that all the lines had feathered. Will was awake before me, which almost never happens, and he was looking at me like someone who had watched you sleep and learned something he would not use as a knife.

“You were gone a little,” he said.

“I was in the stairwell.”

“Yeah,” he said. “I heard the door whine. How was it?”

“Empty,” I said, and was surprised to find that the word did not hurt. “Rosa’s making coffee at six. We’re late.”

We grinned at the smallness of what we would be late for. He pulled on a T-shirt and shorts and flip-flops that made a sound like agreement. We went down to the staff corridor carrying a paper bag with gummy bears and sour ropes and the hard brown candies you used to buy at bodegas for a quarter, even when the quarter felt like someone else’s whole wage.

Rosa was where she said she’d be, hair pinned up with a clip that had bitten too long and was now a little toothless. She took the bag like a sacrament and took out the gummy bear, yellow, and held its head between finger and thumb and looked into the tiny transparent face.

“Salud,” she said, and ate it, and closed her eyes, and then laughed as if someone had done something indecent in a field somewhere. “Mmm,” she said. “Ay.” She put her hand to her heart. “Mara, I love you. Sit. Tell me about your ovens. Tell me if the new bread makes you curse. Tell me—no. Let me guess. The owner has a wife who doesn’t come down to the shop even if she says she will.”

We sat. The coffee was mean and black. The hallway hummed. A man I didn’t know with a baby-blue cap that said Security passed and said morning in a way that included nobody, everybody. Beyond us the racks went on into a little dark that made my hands itch to swing the flash of my phone out like old times. The pipes overhead wore condensation at their elbows like jewelry.

“Antonia,” Rosa said then, her voice altering, railroad switch. “Her mother calls sometimes, you know. We talk of things that live in the air and cannot be caught. Of soup. We talk like widows even though we are not. She forgave,” she said. “Not the hotel, not God. Maybe time. You know, sometimes she says she dreams of work. Not her own work. Of laundry. Soaps. She sits and dreams the way a hand holds a cup in the dream. She wakes up and her house is so clean she wants to cry.”

I held the mug. It leaked slightly at a seam and made a brown crescent on my finger. It looked like the line some people have on their palms that fortune might trace. I rubbed my index finger over it and it bled into my fingerprint.

“I do not go to church,” I said. “But sometimes I go to the river. I stand where the path is broken and look at the water with trash in it and feel that something is okay. Not right, but okay.”

Rosa nodded. “I go to the grocery store in the night,” she said. “Three a.m. I buy plums like people take lovers. I put my face in cilantro. I touch everything that will not complain.”

We didn’t need to say the other things we did in private. We did not need to say who under the slab had been ours.

“I thought when they opened the hotel again,” she said, “that maybe we would all move like actors in a movie, you know? That the memory would be a camera and we would play. But no. What we do is wipe down real tables. We put water glasses at the right angle to the knife. We get a blister in the same place and we stop and tape it and then we keep going.”

“Somebody last night called it a phoenix,” I said.

She smacked her lips. “They love that, the rich, because it means nothing they have will ever be taken from them. They will pop back up as a bird with shiny feathers. Eh.” She made the gross-out gesture of girls disgusted by a bug. “Everything rises from something. Eggs. The sun. The smell of garbage. You come back if your legs will bring you and your work will feed you. Otherwise,” she said, “you do not. But one thing—” She leaned toward me. “You are not a myth. You are a woman. You can leave again. We will still be here. We are not a story a building tells to sell rooms.” Her eyes were gentle now and bitter and lit. “You hear me?”

I heard her. I had heard her the first time she had told me anything, in the laundry room the week I started, when she said, Wear sneakers, not the cheap flats they push at you, your feet are your bank. I heard her then and I had shoes that let me run.

I stayed downstairs until the shift turned over. I put plates into their stack for the banquet, face to face, rim to rim. I did it wrong and was corrected by a boy who was too gentle to be forever in this work; he smiled when he corrected me as if afraid I would break. I told him I had been trained in a different century and he grinned like it was a compliment to him. Some of the old gestures came back to me. The way to steer a cart with your forearms. The way to see a bathroom mirror not as a face trap but as a thing to be made to be true.

Upstairs, Will was propped with pillows and looking at the news on his phone with his lips pinched in disapproval. “We’re supposed to do brunch,” he said. “I told them we might bow out if you—”

“We should go,” I said. “We said we would. We can stop if I—”

“You will tell me,” he said. He had taken a line of my words and made it his.

We did the brunch. It was a narrow table with chairs squeezed too close, mimosas that tasted like perfume, eggs in white sauce, laughter like a crushed can. I watched the bride’s mouth form the word honeymoon and I watched the groom cover her hand with his like someone putting a jacket on a child. I watched game faces and real faces and faces of people who had heard the word phoenix and believed in it. The bellman with the gloves waved at a little girl who wanted to sit on his cart; he let her climb on and ride for one length of the lobby and her mother clapped as if this too were a gift.

We went outside after, into air that smelled like exhaust and wet dog, the universal. I stood on the curb edge and looked up the face of the building. It was smooth and unchallenged, the windows like commas. If there were any scorch marks left, someone had folded them into a story about dirt that would not come off and mentioned a solvent that would remove it, and then someone had bought that solvent, and then someone had applied it with the appropriate brush, and then someone had stood back and admired their labor. The memory was under the paint the way a bruise lives under jeans.

“We can drive back early,” Will said. “Make the bridge before traffic.”

“Let’s go,” I said. Rosa was taking her break at eleven and I had said I would bring her a sketch of the oven at the bakery because she has a nephew who might need a job where heat is not hate. I would scan it and send it later; my phone had a case with strawberries.

As we walked to the car, my hand slid into Will’s like it had always been meant to scoop there. He squeezed and swung our hands once like a joke and I pretended to protest as I always did, to keep the ritual safe. When we reached the parking garage, the lights buzzed in that way a building tells you it too is alive. I turned and looked one more time.

Through one of the glass panes of the lobby I saw, just for a moment, the line of gray uniforms moving like a school of fish, coordinated not by order but by alertness to each other. Rosa, short and full and wearing her cracked-lip grin, had her head tipped back as if to swallow sun. She laughed at something and, though I could not hear, I knew the rhythm of it in my bones. Above her, three levels up, the sprinkler head above our room winked with light, a small metal bud sheathed in promise.

On the drive home, Will drove with the radio low as if to not bruise me with a careless song. I watched the city slip behind and then return and then slip away again in the rearview, like something indecisive. My breath went in and out. The hotel became small and then small and then smaller until it was only a box in my chest that, for once, would shut. Where the highway lifted onto its long span, the river was wide and familiar, its color an unspecial gray. A gull hung in wind like something tacked to air. I thought of Rosa’s hands around a mug, of the boy who stacked plates like prayer, of the bride with her shoulder bare thanking those who made the world shiny for her. I thought of my body, which had not burned, which had carried linens and suitcases and a child once from a hall into noon. I let my spine rest into the seat the way you do when you trust the person at the wheel.

By the time we crossed the water, the lilies in the lobby would be loosening, dropping their pollen like gold onto the clean wood of the table. Someone would sweep it up with the corner of a cloth, careful, not to break a stem. Outside my window at home, the maple held last year’s nest, dark in the crook of two branches. A city bus exhaled at the corner. In the back of my mouth, sugar lingered, a small ache. I swallowed and the ache went with me where I was going. It did not mind. It was a good traveler. It had learned to rise when I did.
