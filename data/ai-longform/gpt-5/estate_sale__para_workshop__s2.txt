By noon the stairs had a bald stripe where the runner used to be, and Ellen had watched strangers lift the furniture of her childhood through the front door as if her life were a set of props to be refitted on another stage. The front door stuck in the heat when it closed and made a damp sucking sound when it opened, like a mouth. There was a yellow paper taped to the glass—Estate Sale, Saturday Only, 12–4—her daughter had printed it at Kinko’s and remonstrated that no one called it Kinko’s anymore. The church up the street had rung the quarter hour twice now, and the black flies were suiciding into the bowl of lemonade she’d set out by the cash box.

The house smelled like a closed piano. Under the lemon cleaner, some note of dusty velvet, mothballs and old smoke. It was funny, Ellen thought, how smell retrieved a place in her like a dog bringing back a slipper. The air outside had a bleached, Ohio sort of heat, heavy with ragweed and mown grass and the sweet rot of the maple tree in the front yard that had been dying in slow, gentlemanly fashion since the winter her father put tire chains in the trunk and left them there until May. Men she didn’t know shouldered bookcases and end tables past the hydrangea the color of bad gums. A skittish teenage boy had bought her mother’s box of 45s for three dollars, and when he held the stack to his chest it looked like he was holding a small animal.

“Is the price firm?” asked a woman with hair the color of a penny. She held up the crocheted afghan that had always lived on the back of the plaid couch. It was the only couch Ellen had ever seen a cigarette burn through, on a Christmas Eve when her uncle fell asleep during It’s a Wonderful Life.

Ellen looked at the sticker she’d written in blue pen: $10.

“I could do eight,” she said. The woman had a face like a teacher’s, the kind who barked your last name in a hallway. She’d already put the afghan in the crook of her arm. If the woman had demanded the paint from the walls, Ellen thought she might have peeled it off for her.

Behind the teacher-hair woman, a man with the complexion of a weather report fingered the set of CorningWare, blue cornflowers marching in military formation around their bellies. He lifted one of the lids and frowned at a chip. “These old enough to be worth anything?” he asked, not quite speaking to Ellen.

“They’re old enough to have fed me a meatloaf a week for twenty years,” she said. He squinted at her as if recalibrating the angle of his question.

He had a way of being in a room and purchasing it with his gaze that reminded her of men on real estate shows, the ones who palmed the marble like it belonged to them for the right mortgage rate. Mother would have disliked him on sight, would have sensed the indifferent appetite in him, the way he would cull from her house the things he could resell, would have said to Ellen under her breath he looks like a vulture poked itself into a cardigan.

“Five for the lidless one,” the man said.

“Five for all three,” Ellen said, as if she were making an apology in a foreign language. The kitchen was suddenly the wrong size. Every time someone asked the price of a thing, she felt as if she were pricing a decade. “Take the lidless one for free if you buy the other two.”

He nodded, unsurprised to be winning.

A squat refrigerator magnet—an Ohio State “O” her mother had once affixed to the deep freezer out in the garage because “I like knowing we’re the champions of something, even if it’s only football”—lay on the table by the cash box. It had demagnetized some years back and slid listlessly down the freezer door like a badge that no longer belonged to a uniform. Ellen picked it up and, acting on an impulse that felt like a small theft in reverse, slid it into her pocket.

“Ma’am?” a tiny voice piped. Ellen bent and saw a girl in a pink shirt holding a ceramic rabbit the size of a potato. The rabbit had always lived on the windowsill above the sink, where her mother had lined up the small worlds women keep by wells—sponges, a bottle of blue Dawn, a small dish of rings. One of the rabbit’s ears had been mended with Elmer’s and patience. The crack line looked like a vein.

“How much is the bunny?” the girl asked.

“Fifty cents,” Ellen said, and then because the girl’s bottom lip was caught between her teeth in the particular harrowed way of children who have to ask permission for everything, “A quarter.”

“We only have dimes,” the girl said, relieved to have been given the test and to have an answer. Her mother appeared behind her, the skin under her eyes creased by years of not enough sleep, and held out a palm of change, the coins warm from being in a bag or a pocket, the color of old mouths. Ellen reached to take them and felt a wash of sudden useless kindness. She closed the girl’s small fingers around the coins.

“You can take him home,” she said. “On the house.”

“But you said—” the girl began.

“It’s okay,” Ellen said. She looked at the mother, whose face had discovered both shame and gratitude at once. “It’s hot. He’ll like new scenery.”

The girl whispered something to the rabbit as if it might change its color. She pressed its cool flank to her cheek.

“I’m sorry about your mama,” the mother said quietly, and then, because this was a town where people collected condolence phrases like they did plastic grocery sacks—folded into triangles and tucked in the drawer below the phone—she added, “She was a sweet, sweet woman.” The second sweet struck a bell in Ellen’s head. Her mother had always reserved that second sweet for people who were simple, livestock or cousins who married men who ate with the TV on.

“Thank you,” she said, and went to make change for a man with a veteran’s hat who’d been staring too long at the framed print of a schooner her father had bought at a flea market on Route 30 because it reminded him of the sea he’d never seen.

Too much air had come in as the door thudded and opened, and the house had swollen with it. The rooms were a mouthful of summers. There was something gauche in the way people touched everything. Ellen knew this was the point; it was why you put a price on the velveted dirt under a house and invited everyone in to trample it.

She had worn the watch her mother had kept in a jewelry box with a stockinged lump of pearls and old nursing pins, knurled metal that had once fastened to the collars of white dresses. The watch didn’t work, but Ellen checked it anyway every few minutes, feeling the cold disc on her wrist as if it had a pulse. Outside, a child cried once and was shushed. A pickup truck came skipping up the road with a dog loose in the bed. Down the block, someone had the gall to mow.

“You hangin’ in there?” Shari asked, appearing at Ellen’s elbow with two plastic cups of lemonade and ice cubes that had turned to wetted glass. Shari would have smelled faintly of Ivory if Ellen had buried her face in her shirt. She had known Shari since they were girls, had slept under Shari’s lilac print bedspread and eaten her mother’s lasagna. Shari had been the first and only person Ellen had told about the time she was sixteen and had stolen twenty dollars from her mother’s pocketbook on a June night when the air was loud with frogs so she could pay the cover to get into the Haymaker and watch a band whose lead singer had a tattoo of a teardrop before she knew that meant anything. Mother had found the twenty missing and made a ledger of suspicion and made Ellen return it anyway from her babysitting money two months later, saying “so it all comes out right in the end.” It was not a sentence, Ellen had learned, that described the world.

“You don’t have to stay,” Ellen had said on the phone that morning.

“Like hell,” Shari had said. “I’ll bring ice.”

Now Shari touched her elbow and handed her a cup and said, “I snagged the lady who wanted to buy the end tables without the lamp. She’s taking the lamp.”

“God bless you,” Ellen said, and drank. The lemonade tasted like childhood, salty almost, the way lemon did when it accompanied yard work and not restaurants.

“That your mom’s clock?” Shari’s eyes had found the metal disc on Ellen’s wrist. She thumbed it lightly as if she could encourage the second hand by touch. “Still no good?”

“Like time itself,” Ellen said.

“Don’t get philosophical on me. There’s a man in your father’s chair. He’s asking if it reclines.”

Ellen glanced across the room. The chair was the color of coffee with too much cream. Mother had always hated it. “It’s a man’s chair,” she would say, meaning it as indictment and benediction both. The chair had gone through two springs, four footstools, and her father’s hospice nights. The mouth of the chair had held him as he’d looked at nothing and then at everything in short succession, as if someone kept turning all the lights off and on in the middle of an errand. The chair did recline, with a noise like an admission.

“Ask him if he’s getting the footstool too,” Ellen said. “I can’t have the chair’s ghost hanging around with nothing to put its feet on.”

Shari went to supervise the recliner, her hair clipped away from her neck with a clothespin the way she’d always done when they were teenagers trying to learn how to curl their bangs with a curling iron cheap and thin as a licorice stick. As she moved through the living room, a woman with a church bulletin tucked under her arm like a passport detoured to intercept them.

“You must be Eleanor,” she said, as if accusing. “I remember you from the Christmas Eve service when you played the angel.” She addressed Shari without looking at her. “She pinched your arm under that robe, didn’t she. Mary Lou told me afterward. Tina’s girl. Tina that used to live back of the Kroger’s? Except it’s a Walgreens now. She works at the commission on aging. My cousin’s boy does the snow for them. It’s a small world.” She did not stop talking long enough to be corrected. “Your mother was a peach.”

“She was allergic to peaches,” Ellen said, bewildered into it, and the woman laughed as if Ellen had been charming on purpose.

“The Lord works in mysterious,” the woman said. “Did you know she used to bring my husband a loaf of that banana bread. Poor man can’t eat enough of it.” She said poor as if he somehow were without arms.

“People liked her banana bread,” Ellen said. She remembered how her mother’s hands had moved in the kitchen when she was measuring flour, the neat strike-off with the back of a table knife. Her hands had a way of addressing bowls as if they’d done her wrong once and she was only now forgiving them.

“She had a way in the kitchen,” the woman said, and lifted a napkin holder shaped like a goose and made a faint squawk of satisfaction. “Do you take checks? Sometimes at estate sales people don’t like to, on account of, you know.” She opened her mouth and did not say death, as if the word itself might make crumbs on the carpet.

“Cash only,” Ellen said. There was a sign. The woman looked at it as if it had just appeared, then at Ellen, as if Ellen had invented cash on the spot to rob widows. “There’s an ATM at the Speedway,” Ellen added, like a consolation prize. Everything was a direction, or an apology, or both.

On the piano there was a lined piece of paper, ripped ragged from a steno pad, in her mother’s dense precise hand that had carried her through forty-one years of nursing, first on night shift and then on days, from surgeries to senior wellness. Two dozen eggs, dish soap, Scotch tape, 2% milk, Cool Whip, bag of carrots, sweet potatoes, ant traps, call Ellen RE: Thanksgiving, stamps, garlic powder. Mother had pierced the figures on her grocery list with a ferocity that had left little punctures all down the line, like braille left by a woman who’d never learned it and still meant to speak.

Ellen picked up the list and then put it back where it had been, like a hand on a forehead.

Her daughter called at one o’clock, voice sheened with distance, planes flying in it that you could hear but not see.

“Hey, Ma,” Anna said. Anna said Ma now that she lived in Minneapolis, as if she were trying on a Midwestern-ness that had been her inheritance all along and now fit her like a coat she refused to take off after spring came. “How’s it going?”

“Men are stealing your childhood in exchange for singles and a jar of coins, but otherwise I’m doing great,” Ellen said. She meant to be funny and heard it land like a tray.

“Do you need me to drive down?” Anna said, immediate, the way children once obeyed.

“No. No. You came for the funeral,” Ellen said. And you were braver than me, she didn’t say. Anna had stood up and spoken the day they’d buried Mother in a dress from Target that had shed its belt on the car ride to the cemetery. She had said that her grandmother taught her to make deviled eggs and do a Jumble and fold a fitted sheet, even though no one actually could. She had said that her grandmother’s laugh sounded like ice in a glass, and Ellen had looked down at her hands and had known that this was also an indictment and a benediction both.

“I found your grandmother’s recipe card for the banana bread,” Ellen said instead. “It calls for shortening and a faith in God.”

“I believe in at least one of those,” Anna said. “Did you eat lunch yet?”

Ellen had not. She had eaten grief like chewing gum for weeks and found herself sometimes unhungry, sometimes ravenous for a particular taste her mouth could not name. “Shari brought lemon finger sandwiches,” she said. They had not been finger sandwiches; they had been Ritz crackers with store cheese and a green celery shard crushed in like garnish. “I’m fine.”

“You keeping anything?” Anna asked. There was a matter-of-factness in her voice that was like being salted, both tender and bracing.

“I put an Ohio State magnet in my pocket like a thief,” Ellen said. “I’m thinking of procuring a spoon.”

“Hell yeah, crime,” Anna said, and Ellen felt the smile she hadn’t known she had. “Okay, Ma. Call me after if you want. Or send me pictures of weird stuff. I kind of wish I were there.”

“Believe me, you don’t,” Ellen said, watching a man with knees like melon rinds pick up her mother’s paperweight. Inside the paperweight a coral-colored flower made of glass petals was trapped in a clearer glass world. She’d broken it once as a child and then not broken it, in the way memory makes mischief. “Say hi to Ben.”

“He’s here,” Anna said, and there was a rustle and a hello from someone’s throat that Ellen associated with Thanksgiving plates and the jostle of coats hung on a guest-room door. Her daughter was moving with a life in it she could not constantly see, like a river under a frozen field. “Love you.”

“Love you, too,” Ellen said, but Anna had already typoed goodbye and was gone.

A man with a face like a birthday cake—wide, friendly, that slight weaving smile of someone lit from the inside for reasons that didn’t have to do with the day—came holding the barber’s strop that had hung on a nail in the bathroom her entire childhood. Her father had sharpened his razor on it in a long slow slide that had a gravitas Ellen had felt down in her own bones, not yet having bones to know that was what they were for. He had brushed his cheeks with lather and then scraped it away so that he was revealed again. He had never once cut himself, at least not where she could see.

“How much you want for this?” the man asked. He turned it in his hands like a snake a person wasn’t afraid of because they didn’t know better.

“It belonged to my father,” Ellen said, and then heard how ridiculous this sounded in this room where everything had belonged to her father, her mother, and by extension, to her. She felt like slapping a sign on the wall that read: This belonged to my father. And then another under it: And? She put a sticker on the strop earlier that said $3. She had pulled the number out of air, wanting to make an integer smaller than a life.

“Three dollars,” she said.

“You sure?” he said, either kind or greedy, who could tell.

“I’m sure,” she said. He nodded and put it in the crook of his arm, alongside a nest of extension cords of which there had been eighteen in the garage. Her mother had been devout about winding them and snapping a bread tie around them when she was done, as if trying to teach them to be tame.

A woman with a bouffant, the kind that made its own weather system, stiff with aerosol, paused in the living room and gazed around as if she’d taken a dare to stand in her own life for a minute. She had been Ellen’s mother’s friend from church. They had made funeral luncheons together, deviled eggs in platoons, platters of ham that had the solitary ambition of getting in you and being welcomed. She held a bundt pan in one hand and a shepherdess figurine in the other, and you could see her trying to imagine a house in which those two things might live together and appear to be in a conversation.

“We’re gonna miss her something fierce,” the woman said, and she let the bundt pan tilt and clap against the shepherdess’s knees. “You got a dollar for the both?”

“It’s marked three for the pan,” Ellen said.

“I know,” the woman said, patient with her, as if Ellen were slow. “I’m asking will you take one. I need a pan like I need a hole in the head. I’m buying it because it was your mother’s. People’ll see that pan at potlucks and they’ll know who made the cake.”

“Two,” Ellen said.

The woman put down the shepherdess gently on the arm of the couch and let the bundt pan fall to her side as if it were a purse and then nodded, satisfied to have horse-traded her way to salvation. She laid the money down on the coffee table and patted Ellen’s hand once, an infuriating kindness.

In the dining room, under the chandelier her father had bought from a salvage company when a restaurant burned down in Canton in 1979, the walnut table was scattering itself. The leaf had been pulled out and people orbited it as if it contained a magnet. Ellen had put a mason jar there with pencils. In it, someone had left behind a silk carnation from a funeral corsage, white stained brown along the edges like toast. Next to it, midway down the table, an old checkbook lay open, the register scabbed with numbers written in her mother’s hand going back years. Ellen had opened to 1998 and found her own name. $100 cash for Ellen. Then again, $60 Target for Ellen. And then: Ellen’s electricity, $83.60. She felt these like birds landing on her arms. There was no place to put your gratitude where it could keep.

A man in a sweat-stained ball cap ran a finger down the back of one of the dining chairs as if checking for dust. He had a way of touching everything as if the room belonged to him and he had come home after a long time away. He asked about the chair’s wood and when she said walnut, he sucked air through his teeth the way men do when presented with another man’s good luck. He asked what she wanted for all six. Ellen heard her own mouth say the number—a number that felt like a betrayal and a relief—and watched him count out the bills like he had been waiting his whole life to part with them.

“Will you help me load?” he asked.

She would. She would open the door. She would help carry her mother’s chairs, light as ghosts, out across the view she had of the maple and the sky and the road where boys had learned to ride bikes and scrape their knees and take up smoking behind the garage, and she would lift the legs into the bed of his truck, and Shari would tie them down with rope that had a story and a smell, and when his truck drove away and someone else drove up, the void in the dining room would be a ring, a tan line where the table had been all those years.

She did. Her shoulders burned in a familiar way her body remembered from a life of women lifting what needed to be lifted: children, baskets of wet laundry, casseroles the weight of babies, grief.

The man who called himself Mitch—his T-shirt had it printed on the chest under the name of a heating and cooling company—whistled when they wrestled the table to the truck. “Thing’s a beaut,” he said. “Bet you ate a lotta meat and potatoes off this.”

“We ate everything off this,” Ellen said. Homework. Apologies. The long talk when she was nineteen and said she might not ever come back to this town after college and her mother pretended for a minute that her hurt was an injury inflicted by the weather and not a choice. Taxes. Card games. Cutting coupons. Birthdays where the cake was from the discount rack and still tasted like a victory because of the light in people’s faces when they sang.

“I’ll take good care of it,” Mitch said, and in a voice that made him suddenly unbearable and sweet to Ellen, he meant it.

By two-thirty, the good stuff had all gone a little wrong. People were going through drawers now as if their grazing were a right. Somewhere, someone had put on one of her mother’s crocheted shawls and had the look in her eye of a person trying someone on in order to see who fit. Ellen floated between rooms with a roll of blue tape and a sharpie stuck behind her ear, the string of the pen’s cap wrapped around her wrist like a tourniquet. She had wanted at the beginning to be precise, to set prices that honored what things cost and what they had meant and what they were now worth to someone else and what they had been bought for in a place where everything went on sale just before you realized you needed it. But by now, the numbers had become arbitrary, and then they had become a noise, and then they had become mercies. Seven dollars became five, became three, became take it, became just do me the kindness of leaving a little hole where I can stand and pretend this never happened.

In the small bedroom at the back of the house—the one that would never release the smell of old quilts no matter how much wind you let into it—a teenage girl in black jeans sat on the floor cross-legged, filed her thumbnail against the corner of a picture frame, and considered a stack of Nancy Drew books with the ferocity of a thief cataloging a vault. Ellen, in a moment that later she would be ashamed of for its pettiness, decided the girl did not deserve Nancy and then caught her own thought like it was a bat flying too low. The girl had put her gum on a Post-it note while she turned the pages and then returned it to her mouth like a second chance. “Are these first editions?” she asked.

“They’re the ones my mother bought at the five and dime,” Ellen said. “They’re the ones she read to me when I had the flu and had my own vomit in my hair and was too tired to be grateful. That’s what edition they are.”

The girl’s gaze, which had been glinting in that teenagery way, softened, either at the mention of vomit, gratitude, or mothers, who knew.

“Three for a buck,” Ellen said. The girl brightened. The bargain had been done. She gathered the books in her arms as if they were a friend she’d pulled from a pool and handed Ellen a soft handful of money, the bills folded around each other like someone had been trying to make them into a smaller thing.

Shari came to the doorway then and leaned her shoulder on it, the shape of her in the house fitting in the spot where Ellen’s memory about how things were supposed to be sometimes fit perfectly and sometimes wouldn’t go, as if someone had replaced a door on a day when no one was looking and it had swelled overnight.

“What about the cedar chest?” she asked. “We mark it sold?”

A man in a wide-brimmed hat, his beard crisp as a broom, had been looking at it on and off all day, running his fingers over the lid like it was a small lake and he was a boy asking if it would bear his weight. It had been Ellen’s. The year she turned eighteen, her mother had taken her to the furniture store to choose it. She had stroked the cedar—young tree cut down in another winter and planed into something meant to hold quilts and secrets—and had laid her palm on it and felt briefly like a person who would be certain about her life, an Amazon ordering baskets, picking out valances with enviable certainty. Then her mother had said, “Many women would be grateful. Many women know what they’re for,” and Ellen had felt the lift of fantasy go out of her like a breath. She had put it in the house anyway and filled it with blankets, stacks of college T-shirts at the top that still smelled of laundry soap and campfires. Once Anna had climbed in and pulled the lid down until Ellen reached a hand under and jerked it back up with the same panic you feel when someone you love goes under the water and doesn’t come up when your body thinks they should.

“Not sold,” Ellen said.

“You keeping it?” Shari asked.

“I thought so,” Ellen said. “I think so.”

Shari nodded and looked at her in the way superior friends do, which is to say in a way that acknowledges the indecisions and loves you anyway.

On her way back to the living room, Ellen passed the bathroom and stopped herself. Above the sink, the mirror’s edges had gone tame with age, the silvering pulling back like old fog. On the back of the door hung three towels, two thin as Bible paper, patterned with pastel seashells, and a thicker one that had belonged to her father. There was a dented tin of Noxzema on the shelf and a blister pack of Hall’s still with three brown buttons left in it. She had meant to throw most of it away. The ways a person survives are ungentle. They leave behind evidence. She straightened the Hall’s, for no reason she could name, and, seeing her face in the mirror with its edges receding, moved a hair behind her ear the way her mother used to, an automatic correction.

She heard the front door and the sound a body has when it makes itself small to come in where it’s not sure if it’s supposed to be. When she came back to the entryway, a man was there whose beard had turned more face than beard, and behind his glasses his eyes blinked with the manufactures of old grief. He looked like someone from the part of life where you get called Mister by clerks who are twenty-five and break your change into your hand like trust. He held his cap in his hand.

“I was hoping—” he began, and then stopped because the breath would not go. “I wondered if you had anything of your mother’s that’s—” He looked around wildly as if he could find the word for what he wanted in the room. “—handwritten.” He bent his brow as if hiding it would make what sprang from it come more easily. “A recipe maybe. My mother passed last year. We didn’t—” He shifted his cap to the other hand. “I don’t know what I’m doing here.”

Ellen felt all of it blow past her—people and their wanting and their bargains and the cash box and the sticky lemonade and the list of items her mother had needed that week, carrots and ant traps and stamps and garlic powder—and held out the steno page with her mother’s grocery list on it that had called to her across the room. He took it like a chalice and held it gingerly on the edges of his fingers so his hands would not eat it with their oils.

“This is perfect,” he said. He touched the piercing where her mother had made too hard a dot on the “i” in ant traps and then ran his finger down the right margin where the long list of numbers had added up to a story of costs. “Do you know what this is?”

“It’s a week,” Ellen said. “It’s a life.”

He nodded, and the relief on his face split open something in her like a packet of sugar. He took out his wallet as if to pay for a thing like this were to break its spell, and she shook her head.

“Please,” she said. “Just take it.”

“No, no,” he said, and he laughed the sound of a man disciplined by women all his life as if by happy dogs and good luck. “Let me give you something for it.”

“Give me—” Ellen searched for the exact amount. “Fifty cents. That’s what carrots cost when I was little.” She did not know if this were true, and it didn’t matter. He put a coin on the table and looked at it like a monument.

The natural selection of an estate sale means that by three there is more on the floor than in the air. Someone had knocked a bowl and it had broken. A ring of dust announced where a lamp had been for so long that the lack of it was a sudden truth that could not be argued with and could also not be borne. The corncob in the salt-and-pepper set had lost its lid and sat there like a half idea. A child found a spool of thread and unwound it as far as it would go and then was in distress at having unraveled something too completely to put it back.

In the lull, Shari went and started the coffee on the drip machine that had ground the same burr brown for a human generation. The sound of it filling, little hiccups of water, was unexpectedly balm, like a small animal licking its own paw. Ellen took a Styrofoam cup and held it inches from her mouth just to feel the heat and not to have to answer questions for a moment about tablecloth sizes or whether you could wash a wool blanket in a machine that had been known to work miracles and also to bite.

She thought of the first time she’d written her own mother’s number into a new telephone—on the landline that had lasted until last year and then finally had been pried from her by economics and the collusion of buttons you had to press to get a person. The red coiled cord had always wrapped around itself and then thrown itself free. Once, at fourteen, she had taken a friend call up to her room and lay on her bed and was speaking to a boy and had not been careful with her voice, her mother had picked up the extension in the kitchen and said “you do not lie in your own bed and talk like that” and hung up in that chunky, terminal way that phones had, leaving Ellen hyperventilating and wanting to be someone different immediately and in a way that would last until she died and not just until morning. She had laughed about it with Shari at school and rolled her eyes, and that night she had, for the first time, made a secret her mother did not know to ask.

Now, by the refrigerator, a woman with red lipstick gone jagged at the edges held up a Tupperware canister and squeezed it to see if the lid held on.

“These are the old kind,” she said, approbation in her voice. “Best kind.”

“They used to be cheaper,” Ellen said, and the woman gave her a look of pity that said she had somehow missed the way the world had priced itself over time while she’d been busy nursing people and buying on sale.

An old man walked the hallway and paused at every doorway as if to pay respects. He was the Benton down the street who had put up his flag in the yard every day for twenty years and then one day stopped and no one asked because sometimes asking made you responsible for what people told you. He had a limp the size of a word that had been misapplied and then stuck. He touched the doorframe with his knuckles as if the wood had a fever and he could take it. Ellen had known him as a girl when he’d had a wife who waved from the porch with a hand to her chest like someone grateful to be waved at at all.

He stopped at her mother’s bedroom, where the bed was half-on, half-off its demise. The mattress had been lifted like a skirt to look for lost things. A quarter and a bobby pin lived in the valley where the slat met the frame, nation-states that hadn’t known they were neighbors until today. Benton leaned his head in.

“She used to sing in the mornings,” he said. “Used to hear her through my kitchen window over the coffee. Not words. Just rising and falling. Like you do when you’re making eggs and you forget you’re singing until you turn and your own voice surprises you.” He smiled. “That’ll be a hard thing to lose.”

He stood for a second and then flowed back into the hall, the hip coming along at its own pace, and Ellen reached out and touched the doorjamb too. It was soft under her finger where hands had worn it. A flat place among all the small grains. The smell of the room lifted—powder and starch and something with the tinny sweetness of a cough drop in the pocket of a Sunday dress—and hit her so hard in the chest that she had to sit down on the edge of the box springs for a minute and count in the way she had taught herself to count when her father died, when she could only eat toast, in sets of five, because the way numbers made squares of the air calmed the part of her that didn’t want to be in the same room as the day.

In the little room off the back porch, men had taken down the rack of tools her father had hung there with a discipline you could see like a grid. His old level, green as a Malachite slab, lay on the floor near the garbage can. She picked it up and peered through the glass at the bubble in its little lake, forever negotiating its position, not sure where home should be. On the shelf above, there were jars of hex screws and fuses and a small can of gasoline that had somehow never evaporated, defying science and good sense. Each jar had been labeled in her father’s thin quick print, capital letters as if all words should be able to be seen from across a room in a crisis.

“Will you take two for this?” asked a young man, appearing beside her. He had prematurely despairing hair and a shirt with sleeves rolled up because it was the sort of day that gave you no choice. He held the wooden salad bowl Ellen remembered her grandmother rubbing with oil every March, the month the air got a little easier and the back porch became a room again. There were cut marks in the bottom of the bowl where knives had kissed it. A long time ago, table forks had been banned from the salad bowl as if they might steal from it.

“Three,” Ellen said. “And you take the little tongs with you or you’ve committed a felony.”

He grinned, and it made him look younger and less likely to perish of whatever his hair suggested. “Deal.” He held up six fingers in a peace sign and then, catching his mistake, added another digit. “We just moved here,” he said. “From Toledo. I don’t know, it felt—” He cast around for words the way planets look for a sun. “Right to get something that had already known a house.”

“You a Buckeyes fan?” Ellen asked without needing the answer, which was to say she knew what the bowl wanted from its next life.

“By marriage,” he said, and made a helpless face. She laughed.

By three-thirty, the house rang. She hadn’t known that rooms could ring without pianos in them. Sound changed when a place was unburdened of its particular accidents. A clock ticked faster than it ought to, as if it had unrolled itself from the wall and then discovered that the story it had been telling about minutes was flimsy. The coo of a mourning dove outside took up more acreage than it had at the beginning of the day, and the maple leaf shadows fell in a new geometry on the floor of the dining room where sun could land now uninterrupted by the table.

Shari came and leaned against the door frame, pressing the heel of her hand into her lower back. “I think I yelled at somebody’s grandson,” she said.

“I think I told someone to take the sheet music for fifty cents,” Ellen said. “Most of it’s church songs. What will they do, corrupt a hymn?”

“Let ‘Em In,” Shari said, and the both of them laughed too hard at that, the way people laugh when the word that was someone’s idea now sounds like the world.

“Do you want me to bring the Goodwill truck by?” Shari asked.

“Lord,” Ellen said. “Not yet. I can’t bear a truck. Anymore trucks and I’ll lay down in the road.”

“What about the ring?” Shari asked then, low, in the tone she used when she feared she was going to step into a patch of meanness and hoped to do it in socks. “You want me to—?”

“I looked and looked,” Ellen said. “I can’t find it.”

They both knew which one. Her mother’s wedding ring, not the plain gold band she’d worn to work because she was always washing her hands and because the hospital swallowed rings like a thing from mythology, but the one with the small pearl and two shallow chips of diamond that could not cut anything. Ellen had wanted—Lord, how she had wanted—for the ring to show up in a pocket of a coat or fall out of a book at her feet and for her to hold it in a palm ringed by her own life and to recognize in her hand the way the past made the present into a small flame you had to cup from the wind.

“I’ll look tomorrow,” she said. “When the air isn’t so full of other people.” It was a stupid sentence that sounded like she had lost the vocabulary for big things and was traveling by small nouns only now.

They watched a man carry out the TV stand that had always looked like a concession to a too-modern world, with its glass shelves and its too-clever angles. A woman in a denim skirt bought her mother’s scarves and wrapped them around her waist and throat as if in an early stage of drowning. The shepherdess left with a child who made her walk along the porch rail for good luck.

By ten to four, the men with slow hips and the women with eyes that could reconcile a broken saucer with the rest of the set had cruised through and either purchased or failed to. A teenage boy with shoulders like he’d been born waiting for his real body to arrive had asked for the price on the metal Buckeyes sign. Ellen had glanced at Shari, who lifted a shoulder, and had said “free” in a voice that, to her own ears, sounded both miserly and generous. He had put up his arm as if to fist bump and she, to her own surprise, had met him halfway.

After the last man in the last ball cap of the day had said “you have a blessed—” and left the sentence with its rear end hanging out the door as far as grammar and hope would take it, the house went quiet all at once, like a person holding its breath under a wave. Ellen stood in the living room with her hands on her hips like her mother’s ghost. There were rings like cheap wedding bands left in the carpet from where table legs had stood and compressed a small circle of the world for years. She realized with a sort of detached wonder that she could see through to the back of the house now with a clarity that had never been available to her when she was a girl and the rooms were full of themselves.

“Want me to bring the vacuum,” Shari said, and Ellen heard the end of the afternoon in her friend’s voice heaving itself up.

“Let’s sit a second,” Ellen said.

They sat on the floor, their knees up, two women in their fifties who had become girls again by the absence of chairs. Lines on their faces had been written in by decades of wind and weather and kindly unkindness, like mapmakers who had enough time to draw both the rivers and the creeks. They sat a long minute in the quiet, the only sound the hum of the world outside doing what it does—bird, leaf, train at a distance, lawn mower, child, a dog two blocks down with opinions about everything.

“Remember when your mama banned us from chewing gum in here?” Shari said, because what do you do but tell a story when you outlive someone who stored all the other stories inside herself.

“I found a Juicy Fruit under the table once,” Ellen said, “when I was sweeping. I was eight, and I took it outside and buried it like a mouse.”

They both laughed, and the laugh went around the room and touched the corners, gentle, and came back. Then they were quiet again.

“I’m not going to keep the cedar chest,” Ellen said. She realized it as she was speaking. The man with the hat would come back, and she would sell it to him for a number that made him feel like he had done her a kindness and had not robbed himself, and he would fill it with blankets that smelled of the home he made with someone else and once, a long way off, a child would climb into it and someone would panic and someone would laugh and everything that had ever happened to Ellen around it would not be lost, because the world isn’t a ledger you can keep, but it would be elsewhere. She said, as if saying it would make it an intention and not just air moving through her throat, “I’ll keep the watch. And the silly little Ohio magnet. And the steno pad with mother’s handwriting about garlic powder. And maybe the paperweight, if it doesn’t get away from me.”

Shari nodded, slow. “You could keep the smell,” she said.

“It won’t keep,” Ellen said, steady. They sat another minute.

When Shari had gone—to get the vacuum from her trunk, to get her purse, to go home to the dog Ellen was half-sure she allowed up on the couch when her husband was away trucking—Ellen rose. She wandered the perimeter of the living room like the guard of a museum at closing, her hand lifting to touch the air where a picture had been. Give yourself time, people had said in cards and baking. Time for what, Ellen thought. Time is so arrogant. It imagines itself sufficient.

She went to the kitchen and opened the junk drawer as if she were going to find the thing she always found there—black twist ties, a pencil sharpened down to a nubbin, gum in its winter coat of aluminum, a birthday candle broken as if used—and she did. Some of the things moved around so much you could always find one. There was the pack of matches from a bowling alley in Orrville that had closed in ’91. There was a crocheted coaster that had miraculously escaped wicking coffee into itself. There was the key that opened nothing in this house. She held it in her hand with a recognition that was equal parts rage and tenderness and put it back.

On the latch of the back door there was paint flaked off in the shape of Ohio. She pressed it lightly with her thumb, as if to test whether it would come loose and whether she wanted it to. It held. A fly battered itself against the screened porch and then, as if embarrassed to be alive in such an articulate way, found the hole in the threshold under the door and went out.

A car pulled up, then another, latecomers who always came to these sorts of things at the end in search of the thing fate had overlooked. A young woman with hair the size of a set of curtains stuck her head in and said, “You got any mason jars?” And Ellen smiled and said, “Ten cents each,” and the woman looked at the clock and back at Ellen and set her mouth to haggle and then, seeing the rooms like a sock turned inside out, said, “Two for a quarter?” and Ellen said, “As many as you want.”

The hat man from earlier came back as the yellow light of the late afternoon came obliquely up the street, flipping a border of brightness onto everyone’s cheeks so they looked for a minute like they were all glowing from their bones out. He took off his hat at the threshold to the back room where the cedar chest lived and asked with his hands, palms up.

“Fifty,” Ellen said, and he hesitated, made face that was only habit, and then nodded. He went out for cash and came back licking his thumb to separate the bills, a gesture that had been handed to him by a father. He looked at the chest as if it might undo him, and when he touched its edge, Ellen was swamped with the certainty that whoever sat on it next to tie their shoes would not know how many mornings had happened at it: her father lacing and re-lacing his boots for the first season at the factory, her mother sitting down heavily the winter after he died and then standing back up and going to work the way women do because it is what is required, Ellen doing up the shoelaces of a foot long-gone to another city, and her daughter, those impossible years with the sun in her hair before it thickened with jobs and men and the charity of her own red heart—kid afternoons, chalk on the sidewalk, Popsicles melting into the palm, shoes kicked off everywhere.

They loaded the chest into the man’s pickup in silence. He put it on a quilt in the bed of the truck and strapped it with the seriousness of someone who moves a thing because moving it is a form of prayer. They shook hands, and his hand was callused and hers was small in it. He drove away and did not look back.

The shadow from the maple had reached the porch floorboards in lovely bars and the dead bit of it had shed a limb that would need picking up. Ellen stood in the doorway for a long minute and looked at the porch’s gray weather—its wrinkles, its freckles—looked at the line her mother’s foot made under the swing where she had sat and moved herself idly back and forth for so many days that the floor had been carved a little into a valley. You could put your toe there and feel it fit. She did. She let herself move in that small valley, forward and back, forward and back, like the kind of thinking you do that is better than decision but not by much.

When the last car had gone and Shari had called a see you tomorrow up the walk and a neighbor had waved though they’d never spoken in twenty years except to wish each other an umbrella, Ellen walked the rooms and said goodnight to no one out loud. Upstairs, the indent in her mother’s pillow had partly risen and partly not. Ellen smoothed the pillowcase, the linen still smelling of dryer sheets with the undertone of someone’s warm head. She opened the window and let the day come in, the smell of mowed lawns, of the caramel note summer adds to evening.

At the bottom of her mother’s dresser drawer, under the winter socks, there was paper. She lifted it in a cluster and set it on the bed. A letter from the VA about her father’s benefits. A postcard of a donkey from Mexico that someone had sent in 1973 and no one had thrown away. A recipe clipping for something called Tomato Surprise. And in an envelope, loose like a tooth, the ring. The pearl was slightly scratched in a way only a mother would notice, and the diamonds, possessing more light than they could hold, winked foolishly in the last of the day.

Ellen stood with it in her palm a long time like a person learning a new country’s coin. Then she put it on her smallest finger, where it rode up against the knuckle like it had found a home that wasn’t what it was made for and was still right, and walked back downstairs. The house had become a container that had nothing in it but her, Shari’s hollered goodbye still living in it like a smell, the lines in the carpet where the couch had been like little graves.

She carried the cash box to the kitchen table that wasn’t a table anymore and counted the money there as if adding could be a sacrament. It was all ones and fives and a few tens, and change that had sweated onto the bottom of the box. She wiped a thin film of lemonade from the counter with a paper towel at the exact minute the church bell up the street struck four. When it finished, the day exhaled and redid its ponytail and stuck its tongue out to touch the last drop of sweat off its lip.

Her phone rang and for a second she imagined it would be her mother. Her body did this every once in a while still, the pointless leap—leaving messages for the dead is like dialing the weather. It was Anna.

“Hi,” Ellen said, and her voice had the calm in it of a person speaking in an empty church.

“How you doing, Ma?” her daughter asked. There was kitchen behind her voice, and a chair leg scrape, and the muffled cutlery of someone like Ben putting an onion in a bowl.

“Tired.” Ellen looked at the watch on her wrist—the face was blank, true to its word. “I sold your grandmother’s cedar chest to a nice man in a hat who knows rope knots. I gave away a rabbit for a quarter twice and then for free. I kept a magnet and a bowl of glass that has a flower in it. I found her ring under socks.”

“Keep the ring,” Anna said softly, which sounded like both a command and a permission and something from fairy tale. “Keep all the rings you can.”

“I will,” Ellen said.

“Are you hungry?” Anna asked, practical and plain as bread.

“I might be,” Ellen said, surprised to have a body again, to feel in it the need to put something in her mouth that wasn’t memory. “I might go down to the diner and see if they’ve got rhubarb pie.”

“That’s not in season,” Anna said, and Ellen could see her face making the skeptical shape it had made since she was three.

“It’ll have strawberries in it to pretend it’s rhubarb,” Ellen said. “It will be fine.”

“Send me a picture,” Anna said. She was crying a little. It ran out at the ends of her voice like seams. “Of the ring. And of— I don’t know. Of the living room.”

Ellen lifted her phone and took a picture of the emptyness, the beautiful strangeness of it. Light came in sideways, unimpeded. Dust hung in the air in columns and made a cathedral of little shining things. The tracks in the carpet looked like a pilgrimage.

“I’ll send it,” she said. She was quiet a moment and so was her daughter. “I am not all right,” she said then, as if confessing to someone who could forgive her.

“I know,” Anna said. “Me either.”

“Okay,” Ellen said. The admission didn’t fix anything or mean anything; it only put a line around how the day had been and allowed it to stand up. “I’m going to lock up.”

She went to the front door and stood with her hand on the knob, feeling in the bones of the house the way you do when a storm is miles off. The maple made a sound in the wind like someone in the other room saying something you couldn’t quite make out. She pulled the door closed against the heat, and the suction sound it made on the jamb was somehow a comfort. She flipped the deadbolt, not because the house needed protecting from anything that would come in, but for the satisfaction of finishing an action that had once meant bedtime, safety, ordinary, the blessed mechanical ritual of being everyone under one roof.

On the way down the porch steps, she put her toe in the worn valley beyond the swing and pushed herself forward and back once, wide enough to feel the momentum and not long enough to have to call it escape. The sky did not decide anything. The day had done what days do and gone to where they go. She walked to Shari’s car to take the vacuum from the trunk and thought, in a small private space that had opened in her alongside the grief and made room for itself like a person shifting in a pew to let someone else sit down: tomorrow she would go into the garage and find the cane with the rubber tip that had lived there and throw it away. Tomorrow she would take the magnet out of her pocket and stick it to her own refrigerator and it would slide down an inch, because that was the truth about magnets now, and she would press it where she wanted it anyway.

At the curb a mother took the rabbit from her daughter’s careful arms and put it safely in the backseat where it would not break. The girl blew on its ear like you do to see if something is living. The mother looked up, met Ellen’s gaze, and in the brief fraternity the day had bestowed on them made a face that said we all carry something out the door. Ellen nodded. The woman buckled the rabbit in.

After she’d wiped the last ring on the carpet with her hand as if a circle made in pile becomes less true if you smooth it with your palm, she locked the door and tucked the key into her purse. She stood on the porch a second, the evening stirring, the blackbirds arguing and then falling asleep all together in the maple, a congress. It was a small town. People driving by lifted their fingers off steering wheels without taking their hands off the road. Everything had been given away that could be given in that one small afternoon. Enough remained that you could still say there had been a life.

Ellen walked down the steps and into the bright street, carrying the vacuum and the ring and the day, which had finally again become light enough to lift.
