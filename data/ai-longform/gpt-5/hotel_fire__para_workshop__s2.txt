“What you doing?” asks a voice that has tugged my sleeve many times.

I turn and Marco is there, which is impossible. But he would be ten by now plus the years since, and this boy is sixteen perhaps, and he has the same narrow wrists, the same set to the mouth that says I will eat the world like an apple and spit the pips at whomever I choose. The illusion skitters and rights itself. It is not Marco. I have found a boy who is also a boy. He looks at me as if I am an aunt and not an intruder. “Watching,” I say. “My cousin’s party.”

He makes a face that is unimpressed by parties. “Good tips,” he says, adjusting his tray. He sets a used plate on a rack that was not built for it and grins at his own wrongness when the plate sits canted, then, with a small showman’s sweep, re-seats it properly.

“Careful,” I tell him, which is the dumbest thing anyone can tell anyone in a place like this. He laughs and is off, a bird through a set of hands.

Back in the ballroom, the first dancers have become damp and hairless like newborns; the last dancers have let themselves be coaxed from their napkins. Sari dances with her new husband in a way that makes her taller than she was. They both look at once elated and like they have been told the secret of an excellent sauce and are too high on it to share. I think of my mother, who would have leaned against the column with me and whispered, Mar, the way her voice went there as if to ask for bread. She had arms with names: the one that went around me was the banana arm, gentle; the one that pulled me out of the street was the hook.

When the sparklers come out—because of course someone has decided that you cannot be wed without small handheld combustions marking your passage through the created world—I don’t go outside with them. This is not because I am afraid of sparks. It is because I do not approve of parties needing fire. The party doesn’t notice me not noticing it. Out on the terrace, their faces glow with young choices. Inside, I pick a winter cherry from a garnish and bite it and find that it is terrible and that that is a kind of joy.

Near ten, Aunt Maureen puts herself down in a chair like a sack of flour dropping into a pantry. “I am done,” she says into my ear. “Take me up in the mechanical room.”

“The elevator,” I say. “We can say elevator. They will let us use it.”

She makes a face at the ceiling. “You and your sayings,” she says, a nonsense reproof she keeps in stock for when she can’t think of the real one. I walk her down the hall; the elevator arrives like a gentleman in a tux. We enter. The doors close. There is a moment when I forget to be brave, and then the moment opens its hands and claps for me anyway. Because nothing happens. Because we arrive at the tenth floor where Maureen has a suite with a couch that I know will be unusable in three generations because of the way she drops full weight onto it without ceremony. I settle her and make her take off her shoes and tuck the throw around her calves as if we are old-time mariners, and I say, “I’ll check back,” as if she were a sleeping child and I am the moon, which is another lie, because I will go downstairs and drink a little and maybe dance again and then I will sit in my room with my shoes off and listen to the air conditioner coax the room into a climate it prefers.

When I return to the ballroom, I see my cousins by the photograph booth returning with strips of themselves, holding them up like eels. I sit, then stand again. I think of leaving. I don’t. I will stay through to the end, I decide. I will see what the building looks like when a party ends. I will not leave as one of the women in red did thirteen years ago, with one shoe. There is dignity in repeating oneself differently.

Around midnight, the staff appear with their practiced erasures: clearing glasses in armfuls, rolling away the bar, wiping the rings left by kindness on the tabletops. A girl in the pale uniform of the night shift enters the bathroom with her bucket and sees me washing my hands a third time. She nods to me as if to say we have been alive tonight, haven’t we, and now we will corral it into the appropriate bins.

I tip the servers again. I do it because it makes some part of me widen like a door that had no hinges before. Pilar catches my eye from the corridor and gives me a small salute with her chin as if we are in a movie where spies recognize spies. The DJ announces the last dance in a voice that has been tired since it was born. We move through it. The light brightens by a shade. The room is a small universe again with its own gravities.

It is after one when I push through the revolving door and stand on the sidewalk because there is a part of me that belongs outside of any building no matter how beautifully it breathes. The street is a river drained of its fish. A taxi glides by like a shark. The boy with the red shoes from the bus could be any child asleep in an apartment above me. I light nothing. I lean against the planed stone of the façade. It gives me back a small portion of my heat.

Up above, one of the chandeliers is still on. Below, in the well of the lobby, the night desk man speaks quietly while referring to a piece of paper. A couple in their thirties, his tie undone and his shirt out, her bare feet carrying her shoes like a harvest, stop to argue about the route between the elevator and the hallway that will be their marriage for the first time. “Left,” he says. “Right,” she says. “Left.” They go left. I know the way is to the right. I let them be wrong. That is another kind of blessing.

When I go back in, I go up to my room and take off my suit jacket and leave it hanging over the chair like a second person reconsidering. I undo the short necklace I wore that my mother gave me and put it on the white dish the hotel thinks is for rings. I stand at the window and consider opening it, but it doesn’t open more than an inch because buildings have decided we can’t be trusted with our own heights. I put my palm against the glass and write L I D I A with the pad of my little finger bent and then breathe on it to fog it, to make the letters appear. For a second, they are there in the wet, and then they are gone because that is what fog does and also what time does and if you want permanence you must write with a nail on stone and then the building will call security.

I get into the bed carefully as if I am a performer in an act called The Woman Who Sleeps In a Place Where She Once Polished Sinks. I lie on my side, which is the logical opposite of the way you should lie when you are prepared to get up and run. The bed is unreasonably good. My feet find their places among the cool. At some point, I must sleep, because I wake.

It is early. The light is the color of an egg. The room hums its private chords. Outside the door, a vacuum trundles with an enormous dignity. The housekeeping carts are out. I hear the low call and response of the morning women: You done in there? They used to call to me like that, voices that braided into a rope you could hang the day from. “You done in there,” I say now, a benediction from my pillow to the hall. I am someone who has slept in a hotel after thirteen years of swearing I would not. My lungs feel, if not larger, then less surveilled by the past.

Sari will be asleep until noon. Aunt Maureen will eat toast in that suite with its couch like a ship. I will go down and drink coffee that tastes faintly of cardboard and lemon. I will put ten dollars in a small envelope and leave it on the desk with a note that says, simply, Thank you, and a drawn flower that is not a foxglove because I do not know how to draw foxgloves. Maybe a daisy. A daisy is honest. Honest flowers would make bad hotels.

On my way out, I take a detour to the service corridor just because I can. A maid is at the closet distributing sheets, her hands quick as mothers. She nods, and I nod. She smells like the morning.

“You know the folds?” she asks, joking, as if we are at a party and someone has asked whether I still dance.

“I know the folds,” I say. “I could do them asleep.”

She laughs. She holds a stack of pillowcases against her hip and then switches them to the other because hips tire even early. “I’ll tell you a trick,” she says to me, generous to a fault. “Don’t chase the edge. It will run from you. Hold it here and pull from there. The corner will come.”

“I know,” I say. She smiles wider as if I have seen through her and approved of what is there. “We’ll take care of your room,” she says, which is a sentence the young cannot hear as a blessing and the older understand as an oath. “We always do.”

“I know you will,” I tell her. I mean the plural. I have known it the way you know the streets of a city you have lived in, not from maps but from your feet.

Outside, the day has been made fresh by a parade of trucks washing the gutters. The phone pole leans as always. The bakery window is a theater of sugar where the croissants have arranged themselves as if they were waiting for a curtain. I stand on the sidewalk with one hand on my bag. For a second, I look up at the building, at all the windows, at my own small square where a shade goes up slowly as if a ghost has found the cord. I think, Lidia, listen, as if she is at my shoulder and needs help with a hairpin. “Okay,” I say, not to anyone who will answer. “Okay.” The word tugs tight as a sheet corner. The street opens before me, unmade, and I step into it.
