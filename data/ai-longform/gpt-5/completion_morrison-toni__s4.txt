Jacob Vaark rides inland to collect a debt from Senhor D’Ortega, a Catholic planter whose showy household and crumbling order sit atop brutal labor. Vaark dislikes both slavery and D’Ortega’s sanctimony but wants payment; D’Ortega pleads misfortune and offers people instead of coin. Vaark refuses to take human beings as settlement, yet during the tense visit a woman from the quarters appears, barefoot and desperate, and silently begs Vaark to take her young daughter, Florens. The wife and priest smooth over the scene, but the mother’s pleas strike Vaark: he senses a “mercy” that has little to do with him and everything to do with getting the girl away from a predatory household. He leaves with the child as partial payment, uneasy over the compromise but convinced he has been asked to rescue, not to purchase.

The trip north is long and jittered by ferries, tidewater, and cold. Florens, unused to shoes and to any kindness from a white man, is alert to Vaark’s reserved gentleness and to the strange openness of the landscape compared to the overseen confinement she has known. When they reach Vaark’s small holding in the northern colonies, Florens is folded into a household of women and two hired men: Rebekka, Vaark’s English-born wife; Lina, a Native woman whose village was destroyed by disease and fire; Sorrow, a damaged, dreamy girl found shipwrecked; and Willard and Scully, bond laborers who do much of the heavy work. Each woman studies the newcomer and measures her story by the seams of her own loss. Work is constant, the farm precarious, but the air is less charged with fear than Florens has known. The people here are not kin, but they move in a rhythm of mutual need that sometimes feels like family.

From there the narrative widens and braids. Flashbacks show how Rebekka crossed an ocean to wed a stranger, how Lina survived the burning of her people and re-learned the world through observation and skill, how Sorrow wandered half-feral with only an imaginary companion for comfort. Vaark trades, farms, and dreams bigger: a grand house rises in his mind and then on a hill, expensive and defiant, the emblem of a man who believes he can build safety out of wood and iron. A traveling blacksmith arrives to forge hinges and gates; he carries a self-possession that ignores rank. Florens is drawn to him with a force that feels like rescue and fever together.

Illness comes as the grand house climbs. Pox rides the same paths as commerce, and the household’s bonds tighten and fray in its wake. Vaark’s ambition meets an old-world contagion; the women must steer the farm, their bodies, and their bargains with the world. Rebekka turns toward a survival built on the rules at hand, hardening into them. Lina tries to shield who can be shielded. Sorrow, long scorned, begins to gather herself. Florens is sent out alone to find the blacksmith, to bring back remedy and skill, and that journey becomes her crucible. On the road she learns which terrors are named by law and which live unnamed in the brush or the minds of men; she also learns what hunger can turn into when it is refused.

What follows is the reckoning of desires and claims: Florens’s love and fear, the blacksmith’s measure of freedom, Rebekka’s calculation, Lina’s warnings, Sorrow’s stubborn hold on her own life. The grand house stands empty of the promise it was built to contain. In the end, Florens sets down her own telling—carving it where it cannot be easily burned—addressing the man who would not keep her and the mother who would not. The “mercy” that named her passage north comes to mean many things, none simple, all sharpened by the world’s new laws and old cruelties.
