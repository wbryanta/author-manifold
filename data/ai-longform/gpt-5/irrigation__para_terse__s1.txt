By the time I turned into the gravel drive the sun had already found the angle where the windshield goes from bright to a hand over the eyes, and I remembered how to creep forward with my head tilted toward the top left corner of the window, like an old dog nosing through a hole in a fence. The house looked smaller than I remembered, the paint chalky on the eaves, the windbreak of eucalyptus sighing beyond the barn. When I stepped out, the dust stayed in the heat like a ghost before it settled. I could taste the morning already brined with noon.

He was sitting on the porch steps, forearms draped over knees, a brown coffee mug on the top step beside him. From his back I could see the sweat darkening his shirt along the spine. He hadn’t shaved. The mug, I noticed as I climbed, was Dad’s, the one with the blue stripe that had held roofing nails for a while before being brought back to coffee after someone washed it in steel wool.

“You’re late,” he said without turning. The man had always been good at speaking in directions.

“My flight got in late,” I said. “Or the rental car was slow. Or I should’ve left the airport last night and slept on the shoulder of 99 with the truckers and coyotes. You pick.”

He turned then, and the years had done strange work around his eyes. “If you’d slept on the shoulder a coyote would’ve stolen your shoes,” he said. “And you would’ve had to dig with your hands, which you would’ve hated.”

“I don’t hate digging,” I said. “I just hate doing it with you.”

The smile came across his face slowly, like someone testing a board before they put weight on it. “There’s coffee inside,” he said. “Light’s busted over the sink, so don’t lean on anything with glass.”

“Just like I remember.”

“This place isn’t the same,” he said.

He meant the orchard but he also meant himself, and I wasn’t going to give him the satisfaction of asking.

In the kitchen the air moved as if it were remembering what breezes used to be like. The tiles were cracked in the single-file line you get when a house shifts its weight. That thin stripe of carpenter ants by the baseboard had found something sugar-hard within the wall; their slow disciplined traffic made me feel more alone than the empty house did. The coffee was burned in the way that happens when people forget to set the clock and it just keeps going and going. I poured two mugs anyway.

When I came back outside he had already loaded the wheelbarrow: shovel, pick, hacksaw, pipe primer the color of my tongue after that summer with the grape popsicles, a can of PVC cement with its brush stuck in the lid like a horsehair caltrop, a handful of couplers rattling in a Folgers tin, a spool of Teflon tape, two hose clamps that looked too rusted to trust, lengths of 3/4-inch PVC with one bell-end each, a coil of black poly with barbed fittings zip-tied to it. The kind of load that makes the wheelbarrow wobble in your hands and twinges a muscle in your lower back when you’re not looking.

“You made me coffee,” I said, holding the mug out.

“You’re a guest,” he said. He took the mug and drank as if the heat of it would keep his teeth from falling out. “We should fix the break on the number four lateral first. The pump coupling’s fine; I checked it last night. There’s a wet spot sixty yards west of the fire road, after the kink.”

“How big’s the wet spot?”

He sipped, squinted into the sun. “Big enough to have wasted water we don’t have.”

“It’s June. No one has water.”

“That didn’t used to be true.”

We started walking. The gravel steadied beneath my boots more than I expected; when you’ve been away from a place you forget how to put your feet down upon it. The orchard rose in gentle terraces, the rows bending slightly along the contours, each tree anchored by its little mound of dust. The leaves didn’t cup as high as last year, he’d said on the phone, but there had been a week of a heat dome and then the allocation cut in half, and the wind did this thing in May where it blew warm and sapped the edges out of everything green. Now the old ladder trees—the Bartletts we used to pick rungs into our shins on—looked like fatigued animals, shoulders bowed. The newer trees, dwarfs with their tidy scaffolds, still tried to look like hope. There were glints along the poly where emitters caught the sun.

When he and I were small our father used to touch the soil under each tree by putting his palm flat against it and counting, softly to himself, to six, and then he’d make a sound in his throat that meant either we were okay or we weren’t. It wasn’t until later that I knew he’d been checking for cool. A twist in the earth, a whisper of what lay beneath.

“So, this is what we do,” he said as we cut between two rows and the orchard swallowed us in its more than and less than. “You dig and I cut, or I dig and you cut, but we’re not trading on the cutting. One of us should do it all so we’re not blaming the other one later for the angle.”

“I’ll dig,” I said. “I don’t want you telling me how to hold a hacksaw.”

“You still do it like you’re buttering bread,” he said. “You have to let the saw do the work.”

“I’ve let other people do work for me,” I said. “Maybe the saw can join the club.”

The wet spot was a crescent in the dust, dark as spilled tea, and the ants had already mapped it. Flies lifted and settled. He bent and thumbed the edge of the dampness, then touched the inside of his wrist with the same thumb.

“Cool,” he said. “So it’s today. Good.”

“What else would it be?”

He shrugged. “Sometimes you dig and it was yesterday, and you feel like a guy who keeps arriving late.”

We cut our way in, scraping the shovels under the crust of silt that had made itself king since March, prying it up in plates. The top three inches were grains that moved as one brittle muscle; below that, and I could feel it through the shovel’s handle, a tamp to the ground that has been forced to be still too long. The poly lateral was there, black snake in a bed of ash, and thirty inches down the PVC mainline ran like a spine. He squatted and pulled the poly up to his knee, slid a hand along it until he found the split, a smile of white under the black.

“Gopher,” he said. “They like to chew where it tastes like somebody else did a job.”

We used a barbed coupler, wrenched the line over its two ribs, warmed the poly in the sun until it agreed to give, then pushed the halves together and wormed hose clamps over and tightened them with the flat of the screwdriver. The ambient sound when you do this—clamp, wheeze of stretched plastic, the way grit crunches under your knee—is a music you can only love if it taught you your first swear word. We fit back a few emitters, shook the line to see if it held, then let it lay.

“What’s it feel like,” he said to me, “to come out here and work the land for a day and then get to go back to your building with a doorman.”

“I don’t have a doorman.”

“A guy with a cap who knows your name and whether you like your packages left at the front desk.”

“I like my packages on the kitchen table,” I said. “Sometimes the mailman lets himself in.”

“You sound happy,” he said, and smiled again, and I understood then that if I let him, he’d fight me with charm before he let me fight him with anything else.

We dug down to the mainline with picks because the shovel wouldn’t go clean. The earth here was clay that left your wrist watching itself. When we reached the PVC he knelt and brushed it with his palm like a priest dusting ash off a printed word. The break was at a coupling; Dad had spliced it there one winter, I remembered that day, we’d been home early from school because of a flood, there was water twenty feet wide in the ditch, and Dad was soaked to his chest, he’d laid the cutout pipe on the hood of the truck to keep it clean and glued it under the green tarp while rain hammered on the tarp’s drum. He’d had a thermos of black coffee on the fender and he drank it standing there and steam came from his clothes and I did not think then that a man could be poor and holy at the same time.

He touched the break and a bead of brown water swelled and ran over his knuckle. “Depressurized,” he said, which meant the pump was off. If it had been live we would have both been trying to put our fingers in a throat.

“You shut it down?” I asked.

He nodded. “Last night. I heard the racket. Woke me up.”

There’s a sound a pipe makes when it’s torn, not when it snaps, but when the plastic has thinned under some old sunstroke and the last threads let go. It’s a cough in your yard. He had gotten out of bed, then, and gone barefoot into the bright dark. He’d shut the pump off with his hand, the iron switch box cold and familiar under his palm. He’d stood in the orchard, and the stars had watched him, and I had been asleep two hundred miles away, my mouth open, a fan turning the same slow arc over my bed, those white knives of light moving over my ceiling like oars.

He handed me the saw. “You can cut,” he said. “If you mess this up I’ll bury you in it.”

I laid the fine-toothed hacksaw in the groove, took a breath, and let the first draw be light. In my head I could hear Dad, the sentence that was his life: slow at the start, boys, then let it find you. The blade bit and fled grit, drew white powder into my eyelashes. The pipe oscillated and set, and I kept the angle of the cut square, reading something in the resistance the way my brother had read the damp with his thumb and wrist.

“Not bad,” he said, when the end fell. “Use the reamer.”

“I would never not.”

He laughed, the sound low. “I forgot you could do this. All those years and you forgot everything useful and somehow this stayed.”

“You’re not the only one who got something imprinted on him.”

We covered the fresh ends with purple primer because the solvent wants to push back against you if you walk into the room without saying hello. The smell took me someplace ugly and bright: the back seat of the pickup when we were kids, our knees glued to the vinyl, Dad’s shirt off, his body shining under the sun, the purple drying on his fingers while he drove with one hand and ate a peach with the other, juice down his wrist. We buttered the inside of the coupling with glue, swabbed it like your mother would a skinned knee, primed the outside of the pipe, the bell of the new section, then slid them all together while counting in our heads to forty-five because if you let go too soon, hydraulics gives you the finger. We held it like nurses hold a thing that trembles.

“You married yet?” he said, eyes above the pipe.

“No,” I said.

“You going to be?”

“I don’t know.”

“You always talk like that,” he said. “Like you’re doing me a favor by answering the question without content.”

“You’re not married,” I said. “Do you want to be?”

He looked down the line of the buried pipe as if he could see the wheezing of it, the map of its cracks. “No,” he said, and I didn’t believe him, and we each, in our way, believed ourselves.

We waited for the glue to do its work because we knew too well what happened if we didn’t. He took off his hat and stood up and the wind touched the bowl of his hair as if measuring grain. Beyond, the hills hunched light-brown at their shoulders, the dotted oaks hunched with them, and an airplane dragged a line across the sky that looked like a scratch someone would fail to buff out. The irrigation district’s canal, if I walked to it now, would be a concrete V with nothing at the bottom but dust and a tangle of tumbleweed bones. A killdeer ran in front of us, shrilling, faking a broken wing to lure us from a nest we wouldn’t have found even if we tried.

“You talked to anybody yet?” he said.

“I’ve talked to everybody,” I said. “Hendricks. The developers who bought Ramirez’s place and turned it into those boxes with the fake shutters. The city manager who says the city wants to buy a corner for a park if someone will gift it the half-million. The water guy who says if we sell the land without water the land is worth what it will cost to take down the trees.”

“This is your tone of voice when you say a lot of facts to avoid the part where you say what you want.”

“I don’t know what I want,” I said.

“That’s bullshit.”

We hadn’t said Dad’s name yet that day. The sky seemed aware of it. Sometimes you hold a word like a bird in a box; you can feel it moving but you don’t want to take the lid off because then it will either fly out or die. Our father had been dead since March. In the last week of February, when his breath had been thinner and the dry of his lips was a different color, he had asked me to change the channel while Ruben—my older brother and the man in front of me—slept in the chair by the bed. He didn’t actually want the channel changed; he wanted my hand to perform an act in the room, to be busy near him, to show him that attention could take a shape. There are people who leave; there are people who stay; there are people who stay and have to watch. When he went, he went between us, as if some invisible line we had been arguing about since we were children ran under his body.

“Okay,” I said. “I want not to have this fight with you the way I had it with him whenever I drove back here for a weekend and pretended to hate it the whole three hours on the road and then pretended to hate it again the whole three hours back because if I admitted I liked being here I’d have to admit that leaving was a mistake. I want you not to end up like him, which is to say—”

“Which is to say what?” His eyes, through all those years, had kept the color of bark.

“Which is to say bending yourself down to the size of this place until you don’t know how to stand up in any other room. Which is to say dying here like a post.”

“He died in a hospice bed ten miles away,” Ruben said. “We were both there. You don’t get to edit that.”

“You know what I mean.”

“I know what you think you mean,” he said. “This place wasn’t a prison. He told me once he wished I’d gone. I told him I didn’t want to be the thing he missed. Both of us were wrong, and both of us couldn’t stand the idea of the other being one inch right.”

I sat back on my heels. The glue had had enough time. The coupling stayed. We cut an equal length on the other side and set in the new section, our fingers touching when we held it together, a little sweaty static between our knuckles. He looked at me then like a man thinking about the way another man he loves drinks water when he is thirsty, and I saw—because I needed to find it—the boy who’d taken a torn-out page of the National Geographic to the bathroom and whispered to it because the picture had a whale in it bigger than a house and he wanted our father to build a place that could hold a thing like that.

“We can sell to Hendricks,” he said after a while. “He’s not a developer. He’s a farmer. He’ll probably rip out the pears and put in almonds and sell the water. But he’ll keep it in rows.”

“You say that like it matters.”

“It does,” he said. “It matters that the wind still has something to whisper to. It matters that the ground isn’t being poured into. I don’t want to look out and see cul-de-sacs where the rabbit lights used to be.”

I pushed dirt back into the hole around the pipe, held it in place with the shovel blade while I stepped on it, tamped it down to a firmness that told you not to expect much, that said here are your limits, do not brag. “And if we don’t sell,” I said. “We do what. You do what.”

“I keep it going until a year that isn’t all bone,” he said. “We make the loan payments with the crop and I pick up a crew job with Nelson’s shop in the off months. You don’t have to be here.”

“You can’t make the loan with this crop,” I said. “Half the fruit is buttoned because of the leaf scorch, and the packer’s paying a number that says he’s done with us. I saw the way the forklift guy looked at you last week like you were a kid selling baseball cards. He never looked at Dad like that. It made me want to punch him and I’ve never wanted to punch anyone.”

He lifted the wheelbarrow handles and rolled until his boots found a rut. “You always say never and mean sometimes,” he said. “You want to eat? We should eat before the heat works the pipe like a worm.”

We sat on the tailgate of the pickup with our legs out. The sandwich he handed me was bread with mayonnaise and a half-fist of smoked turkey, the way Dad had made them the day a neighbor’s dog came and ate an entire row of fruit in the lower forty. There was a dill pickle in wax paper. We ate looking at the rows. The sound of a bees’ republic came from a place that turned out to be above our heads and not in our ears. Dust devils were gesturing politically in the far field, soft columns that twisted like a debate and then fell apart without resolving anything. A crow walked under the nearest tree like a minister doing inventory of the pews.

“You remember,” he said, “that time Dad had us lay the black line and I got it wrong and he didn’t yell?”

“He didn’t yell often,” I said. “He just looked like someone had knocked on his door at midnight and told him they loved him and he couldn’t think of a way to say it back.”

“That’s bleak.”

“You were probably hiding in the truck bed eating sunflower seeds then.”

“I don’t hide,” he said. “And he did yell sometimes. He yelled at the pump. He yelled at those guys who came with the meter and tried to tell him he was cheating a flow rate by letting his neighbor draft off the line. He yelled when the drought was declared ‘exceptional.’” He made little quotation marks with his fingers and I felt the O of sudden sorrow in the pit of my stomach. “He never yelled at you, though.”

“That’s not true,” I said. “He yelled at me the night I told him I was leaving. He yelled so much I felt like the house would peel.”

Ruben nodded as if conceding a point he did not want to concede. “He yelled then,” he said. “He told me had told you to wait a season and you said no. You said you couldn’t keep waiting for the weather to be fair to your life.”

“That’s not what I said,” I said. “I said I had to go because if I stayed I would be staying to keep him from being alone, not to keep the orchard from being lost. I said I wasn’t strong enough to do both things. Maybe I should have stayed.”

He looked at me then long enough for both of us to get older. “You think if you’d stayed things would be different?” he said.

“No,” I said, and we surprised each other by laughing at the same time.

We took our bites and sips in conclusive silence. I remembered that when we were boys and Dad took a mouthful of sandwich he’d keep it in the side of his cheek while he lit a cigarette, then he’d put the cigarette in the corner his tongue was not already occupying, and from a distance he’d look like a man winking at a private joke. Our mother used to scold him for it while she watered the rosebushes by the side of the house, and he’d tell her it was cheap to get a two-for-one. She had a way of bending the hose, pinching the rubber to make a fan that caught a rainbow at three in the afternoon and made us believe everything was a secret and ours to hide in.

After she was gone and before the orchard started to say it was tired, Dad had planted twenty new trees in her name. You could walk to the end of D row and there’d be a stake with JESS scrawled on it in black marker; the A was smudged where someone’s thumb had gone into a wet letter. Those trees were the last to set good fruit. It wasn’t superstition—I told myself—but I did not pick from them when I came home. It felt like something to be left.

We washed our hands with the last of the water from the jug. When we were kids Dad would spit on his fingers and call it good. My brother shook his hands in the air like a pianist without a piano. “Heat’s bad,” he said. “But it’s not last year bad. Last year it sounded like the trees were whispering sorry.”

“I didn’t know trees spoke English.”

“They speak something like it. They speak what you think you hear when you’re the only thing in a field that remembers sound.”

We returned to the hole. The glue had gone from tacky to church. We covered the pipe carefully, the reverse of what we’d done to get down there, layering clods like you lay worries in a row for someone to step on them without falling. Before we set dirt to its final sleep I laid my palm on the pipe and counted to six. Cool came up like a rabbit out of a briar. “Now,” I said.

He nodded and stood. “Stay,” he said. “In case. I’ll go throw the switch.”

“I want to do it,” I said. “You always get to do it.”

He considered. “Go on then,” he said, and his voice had given me something that made my heel bones feel less like flint.

The pump house was a box Dad had built from leftover barn timber. The lock had been broken so long it had learned to hang its head. Inside, the electric panel was lined with the names of things: well, pump, house, heater, mystery. Only the pump switch had tape on it, wrapped around and around because it had been known to be loose and men like to bind anything that threatens to betray them with tape.

When I threw the switch there was a chirr, a cough, a spin of sound that reached back through years and made my back remember what it felt like to have someone slap it hard because he loves you. The pump took a breath. The air in the line gave its little death cry. For a few seconds that felt like a minute nothing happened, and then there was the slow unstoppable like of water being where it is meant to be, and the line groaned, and the orchard found itself. In June the noise of irrigation is a man reminding himself he has lungs.

I jogged back through the rows, the grass burrs seeding themselves onto my socks like thoughts. As I went I passed the laterals where they ran next to the trunks, each emitter spitting its measured song, a half-moon wetting the dust. Where a line had been pinched, the water told you, skipping the beat. Every tenth tree had a squirrel watching the world with hatred. When I reached Ruben he had his boot heel on the fill, tamping, and his body was a statue given a very small job.

We stood with our hands open a few inches out from our sides, like men figuring out if it’s raining. A moistness reached our ankles as if the ground had decided to breathe out for us. It went along the row with the same steady intensity as a rumor.

“Every time,” I said. “You’d think it would stop being magic.”

“Water never stops being magic,” he said. “It stops being ours.”

We went tree to tree and listened. Where a coupler began to leak a little we tightened its clamp with the screwdriver we’d done penance with since we were old enough to know that slots demanded patience. Where a rat had chewed an emitter into flagellum we trimmed and tied. The sun slid toward us in small angry ways. The lattice of shadow under each tree made a geometry you could lay your face in, a mathematics that asked for nothing in return. My shirt got heavy at the small of my back. Sweat went into my eyes and stung the old cut above my eyebrow.

We argued in the way of brothers working: while looking elsewhere, as if the sight of the thing in your hands would discipline the things that left your tongue.

“You can’t build houses without water,” I said. “Someone will have to pay for it. The city’s already paying more for water they can’t get and the guys with pipes running under the river at midnight keep figuring out ways to siphon off what isn’t theirs. Let’s not be those guys.”

“You’re talking like there are only bad men and good men,” he said. “You talk like the world is a courthouse. It’s a ditch.”

“It’s both,” I said. “You’re acting like Hendricks is a church because he’ll keep rows.”

“I’m acting like if we sell to him I can still drive by this place and not feel like I’m the man who dropped my parents’ bones in a woodchipper.”

“Why do you get to be the man with bones in everything?” I said. “You were five when Mom died and somehow you’re the one with her ghost in your pocket? I’m not allowed to have anything you have?”

He stopped and I stopped because I understood what I’d done. “I didn’t mean that,” I said.

He looked at the ground long enough for a horned lizard to feel safe to move. “I know what you meant,” he said. “I’m allowed to be wrong, Cal.”

“I didn’t mean to say it stupid,” I said. “But she was mine, too.”

“She still is,” he said. He crouched, lifted the poly to find an emitter stuck with grit, spat on his thumb, wiped it clean. “Actually,” he said, “I don’t carry her. She visits.”

“What does she say when she visits?”

“She asks if we’ve been eating,” he said. “She tells me to put a hat on. She tells me not to be like my father and then tells me I should be more like my father and then laughs. She tells me you look tired.”

“She tells you to lie.”

He looked up at me and the intensity was a kind that had once broken a man’s test in the alley behind the bar when Ruben was twenty and didn’t know how to be anything except the protector of small people who thought they should be big. “I’m not lying,” he said. “I won’t sell to a developer, Cal. I can’t. It would be like letting someone turn a church into a laundromat. I don’t mind laundromats. They’re full of people who take care of their clothes. I mind watching a steeple get sawed in half.”

“You keep saying church,” I said. “Dad didn’t believe in God.”

“He believed in pipes,” Ruben said. “He believed in water in them and he believed in laws about how not to break them and he believed in mornings. He believed in the idea that something owed you nothing and you still had to meet it halfway. That’s God enough.”

I found myself laughing then, not because anything was funny but because my shoulders had been holding up the word sell for four months and sometimes when you stop you fall down or laugh. One of the smaller trees had not set much fruit and what it had was misshapen, one pear bulging like a heart that had been drawn by a kid in the back of a class not listening to the lesson. I put my thumb on it and felt how hard it was. We used to call that stone-hard. We had a language for those things like all families have a language for the things that are about to leave them.

He turned the valve on the far end of the lateral and the water made a full circuit, returned to itself, and something in the line trembled and set and then was fine. “Pressure’s good now,” he said. “If the pump holds, we won’t have to prime it in the night.”

“You’re not sleeping anyway,” I said.

“I slept,” he said. “I slept in the chair with his hand in mine and I kept thinking hands should be less important on a man than they are.”

“What kind of sentence is that,” I said.

“It’s the one I had,” he said. “You still think you need to save me.”

“I need to save both of us from this place eating us,” I said. “I need to feel like not everything we grew up with is a version of the thing you read about in the paper where someone inherits a bad dog and then spends the rest of his life writing essays about how the dog taught him to love.”

“A bad dog’s better than no dog,” he said. “You live somewhere with no dogs. You live somewhere that smells like an elevator all the time.”

We were going to keep circling like this until we made ourselves dizzy or one of us fell over. The sensible part of me said: say your number. Make an offer. Tell him how much Hendricks would pay, how much the developer would pay, where the loans stand, where the mortgage sits like a barber’s cape over your shoulders. But numbers would make all this uglier in a new, alien way. Numbers would make us men with calculators. Our father had a little one with rubber buttons that made him look like a gambler. He’d tap it with his index finger and the corners of his mouth would go down and then he would tap again as if the second set of numbers would fix the first set by magic of insistence.

“You could come back for a season,” he said after a while, as if he could say the sentence without meaning it. “You could help through harvest. We could see.”

“I can’t,” I said. “My life is there.”

“Your life is everywhere and nowhere,” he said mildly. “You are the kind of person who would take well to a monastery. Or a submarine.”

“What the hell does that mean,” I said.

“It means you like being contained and you like not being asked to prove anything every day. You like a task that repeats. You like knowing the difference between a leak and a break.”

“You’re not wrong,” I said. “But I don’t want to die here.”

He leaned against the tree, tilted his head, listened to the water in the line as if it were telling him secrets about itself. “Neither do I,” he said finally. “I want to die in a country where the weather knows my name.”

“I don’t know what that means.”

“It means I want to die where someone has called me often enough that the sound of my name has worn a path back to me that I can find in the dark.”

We did the rest of the rows because the problem with orchards is if you fix one thing a second thing stands up and asks to be noticed. A leopard lily hunkered where the tractor would find it in a week gave us its bright face like a provocation. He stepped carefully around it. Overhead a red-tailed hawk pulled one long vowel out of the afternoon and held it until my heart ached. We were passing that place where Dad had tripped in ’03 or ’04, where the root had made a low hump and he’d fallen and cut his cheek open on a rock and when he stood up, bloody, he’d said: well, the ground still has teeth, boys. And we had laughed so hard we made him proud.

Around four a thunderhead built itself over the western ridge and then made nothing of itself by two. Heat on these afternoons doesn’t work the way you think. It goes away and comes back as if it forgot something on the table. The smell came up of wet dirt—some call it petrichor, the word that sounds like a demon and smells like salvation. For an hour the orchard seemed younger. Leaves uncupped a degree, so small you would not have believed it without looking right at them the way you look right into an eye. There is a kind of hope that has nothing to do with outcomes and only to do with seeing something be what it is when nothing stops it.

“You hated coaching little league,” I said, apropos of whatever part of our childhood had been shaking its bag of marbles in the back of my head.

“I didn’t hate it,” he said. “I hated the dads. The boys were fine. They all wanted to turn left when they should’ve turned right and then they all wanted to tell each other what they should’ve done instead of doing it again.”

“That’s what we’re doing,” I said.

“We never learned anything,” he agreed.

The day got quiet in the way days do when they stick their head around an edge and check to see if you’re going to make it to evening. We finished walking the rows and the pump made its little end-of-cycle moan like a cow given a long run. I went to the gauge and read the number. He stayed with the water as if the water might confide, in the last minutes, who it loved best. When we came together again our sweat had turned the dust along our forearms into slurry.

Back at the house the kitchen was the same shade of shadow it had been in the morning, but now the flies traced figure eights in the corners as if auditioning for someone’s attention. He sat at the table. I stood against the counter and picked at a loose thread on my shirt until it grew from thread to ribbon. That was when I said it.

“They offered nine hundred for the lot,” I said.

He looked at me without moving. “Hendricks?”

I shook my head. “Delmar Development. They’d keep the far parcel zoned ag for a while to get the credits. We could—”

“No,” he said.

“Hear me,” I said.

“I’m hearing you say nine hundred,” he said. “I’m hearing you counting already how much of that disappears the second it lands in the account. I’m hearing you making a list of who we owe. I can do that list without closing my eyes. I can see the man at the bank who brought doughnuts the day after Dad’s funeral and said he used a calculator harder than our father did.”

“I can make us solvent,” I said, and he did not flinch at the word, did not smile either.

“Solvent,” he said, almost tender. “Like glue.”

“You don’t want to be like him,” I said. “He died with the loan almost paid off and nothing left but tools that belonged to a century nobody wants honors for anymore.”

“He died with rows,” he said. “He died in the same county he learned to say his first words in. He taught his boys to hold on to a pipe until their fingers got tired.”

“You think that’s an epitaph?”

“I think it’s as good as any I’ve seen on a stone,” he said.

“I could give you a house in town with windows that hold their paint,” I said. “You could sit on a porch that looks at other porches and never have to pull your boots on at three a.m. because a line is bleeding out.”

“And I could watch a man with a little poodle walk by twice a day and wonder what’s growing under my feet besides pipes that carry other people’s pleasure,” he said.

“You don’t have to be mean about men with poodles,” I said. “Poodles are smart.”

“They are,” he said. “That’s the problem. I want to be dumb.”

“You’re not dumb,” I said.

“I want to be dumb the way a thing can be dumb about itself and still be whole,” he said. “I want to be dumb like a rock. Like a field that knows only sun and water.”

“We don’t get to be that,” I said.

“No,” he said. “We don’t.”

We didn’t move. The house breathed its little sticky breath. Outside, a truck went past on the road and didn’t downshift; I heard the rattle of the aluminum ladder in its bed. In the corner by the back door a pair of Dad’s boots still waited like sentries whose man had been gone so long they’d made up their own hours. I had the thought that if I put them on I could go out and be better at this than I had ever been at anything in my other life, and I also had the thought that if I put them on they would cut my ankles.

“I’ll sell twenty acres to Hendricks on the west side,” he said finally, as if he was telling me the weather tomorrow. “The oldest rows. The ones that break first. He can rip them. He can do what he wants. I’ll keep the center. Jess’s trees and the new block. This keeps what’s left of my word with a dead man and this helps you to stop counting your friends to sleep.”

“It’s not enough,” I said, but softly.

“It never is,” he said. “You take the loan. I’ll give you care of it. I’ll pay every month and if I miss you come with a hammer and a priest.”

“That’s an unfunny joke,” I said.

“It is,” he agreed. “We’ll give the city the corner with the old oak for a patrol car to eat its lunch under. That way, when you come back here with a woman who will have you and you bring a kid to see the place you can park safely and tell them we did something that made a story that wasn’t just money.”

It is a particular flaw of mine that when offered a compromise I see every place it gaps rather than any place it holds. Maybe it is why I left: because the world here was made of compromise and each gave way to another and if you stacked them you got a thing that looked like a ladder and I wanted stairs.

“Okay,” I said after a while. “Okay.”

“Okay as in okay,” he said. “Or okay as in I’ll call you Saturday and you’ll have a better idea and I’ll have to tell you again.”

“As in okay as in okay,” I said. “If he’ll do it, and if we can—”

“If he won’t do it we’ll make him,” he said, and let his voice flirt with humor. “I know a man with a backhoe and a sense of theater.”

“I’m not going to ask what that means,” I said. “I’ll call him tonight.”

“You’ll call him tomorrow,” Ruben said. “Tonight you’ll help me go to bed the way men who live together learn to do. You will lock the pump. You will turn off the kitchen light and swear at it for buzzing. You will sit on the front step and try to hear if the water is still moving from this distance. You will fail to hear it. You will say it anyway.”

“Where are we eating,” I asked. “Or is there no food.”

“I’ve got a can of chili with a cowboy on it,” he said. “There’s cheese that could cut glass. There’s a half loaf of bread if the ants didn’t declare independence.”

“I’ll go to the store,” I said. “I’ll get steaks.”

“You’ll buy ribeye and pretend to know the difference between that and our father’s favorite,” he said.

“He liked flank,” I said. “He said it was the only honest cut.”

“That’s because he liked men to chew,” he said. “Bring beer. Bring the brown bread he liked that takes a degree to swallow.”

I went into town in the late heat. Everything shimmered in the parking lot kind of way, the blacktop rippling with the bodies of invisible fish. The cashier at the market was the same woman who had bagged our groceries when I was fifteen and bought condoms with a rage at the register that made her look away and hand me the change like it was rope. She recognized me and said my name like you say it to someone who has turned into a ghost and didn’t know he had. I drove back slow, thinking about something Dad had told me when I was too young for it: that when you give a thing a good ending you don’t make it shorter; you make the part at the end matter more.

At the house I put the steak in a pan with butter and garlic and salt and a sprig of rosemary I’d killed from a bush by yanking it like a child’s tooth. He stood with me and watched the steak as if he were learning a new skill. We ate on the porch with our plates on our knees. The sun went in that way it does where it gives you one last look at its shoulders before it turns. The eucalyptus talked in the slow gossip of tall thin things.

He drank his beer and I drank mine and for five minutes we did not imagine anything. It is harder than it sounds. A rabbit came along the edge of the gravel and decided we were monuments. In the orchard the sound of water had softened so that it was only there when I asked for it. I asked. I heard something like a man sleeping.

We closed the pump house and set the lock with its head down. We went to the bathroom and used the sink as if it were a holy place. When he went to his room he stopped in the doorway and leaned a shoulder against the jamb and said, without looking at me, “You can sleep in Dad’s bed if you want. I changed the sheets.”

“I’ll sleep on the couch,” I said. “I’ll listen for stars.”

“That’s right,” he said. “You always wanted to turn into a night.”

In the dark, with the fan ticking like an insect with a small metal wing, the house felt like it might float off its foundation if we both breathed out at once. I did not call anyone. I did not fill in any forms. I did not write any numbers. I let my brain do what it has wanted to do since March: make a circle that a man I could not save could step into and be whole. I failed. I fell asleep.

In the morning the air was shaped differently. The cup he left for me was clean. The pump had done its work and not cried in the night. We had one day’s less worry than we’d had when I drove up, and that felt like a coin someone had put into my palm when I wasn’t looking. You cannot plan a life on coins; you can make a day.

We went back out and looked at the wet crescents under the trees and he touched one and put his wrist to it and said, “Still,” and smiled in a way that made the years come off him like bark.

The argument about selling had not been won. It was not the kind you win. It had walked with us through heat, through dirt under the nail, through the smell of primer that will always be my mother, through a man’s compromise that might have saved us in one way and pinned us in another. It had reached the point, by evening, where it had tired of itself and gone off to look for water. We stood there and let the sound of the moving thing continue without knowing for how long, like men who have learned to be patient with a happiness that belongs to no one.
