There is a point on the dock where the tarmac ends and the night begins in a concrete line. The ferry’s mouth opens there: all ribs and lights and a throat of diesel breath. I stand with a bag that has nothing anyone would steal—two changes of clothes, a folded letter, a book someone left in the laundromat, a toothbrush, a comb, the small comb you gave me, which is the only beautiful object I own. Someone near me is smoking in cupped hands like he’s sheltering a bird. The woman at the booth has the soft voice that every harbor hires. My ticket slides back under the scratched plastic pane with my name, which I say out loud to myself on the way up the ramp in case this is the last place I could say it without echo.

We funnel into the open stomach. The span of metal above us. Runnels of the day’s rain track towards the dark. It’s almost winter, almost the hard part of the year. These long northern Straights—this one and those beyond—line up across a map like thresholds a person crosses in his life, pulling small cargos of histories behind him, scuffing off the places he came from at each crossing as if his past were something that stains at first and then bleaches.

I find my berth and put my bag on the lower bunk. It is the room meant for two. Sheets creased like paper. Formica, green, a color they must have bought in quantity on some municipal tender in a year when they named paints for plants: moss, fern, pine. The window is an oval that makes the world oval. Through it there is only the angle of the dock with its white lines like bones and men in vests walking into and out of their own red lights. Once the metal begins to tense, once the engines tune their song to the night, it starts to mean something. You haven’t left and there is no going back, two fidelities laid beside each other like spoons.

I think of him. The call came in late afternoon when the light had already thinned into that color old photographs have, silver rising to the surface. I was in the garage where I store the things I use for weather: boots, tarps knotted like the backs of hands, the tin pail for salt. The voice was measured, trained for this. He was careful with the word body the way a doctor is careful, because you do not know what you are naming until you name it and then it becomes a human being again. They found one after three days of a search. A simple rowboat in broken water. There had been fog, and the fishermen had talked on the radio about the returns on their screens not matching what their eyes saw. Three gulls that came and went like a weather system of three souls. The coast guard reported and then corrected. The current moving in a way that did not match a certain section of old charts. This man used to write to us, the caller said, or we have a letter you might want to see because it is written in a hand that looks rehearsed as if for the stage. He gave me the name, as if testing, and when I did not speak immediately, he said the town as well, a town I knew like a place one hears in stories and believes because at some time a cousin vanished into it and sent back a postcard of a house that was too pretty for the weather.

You do not say anything at first. You stand with the wire of the phone in your hand and you watch it like it is a snake that might coil back into you. I had not seen my brother in four years. The last time was at the edge of a frozen ball field in a suburb where boys were playing a sport that required a circle of adults to keep it warm. Their voices came up through their scarves like birds in winter hedges, and Silas lifted his head when he saw me and I thought he might run. I had been waiting in the car until the last of my cowardice evaporated, which is to say I was inside a vehicle whose heater was blowing and I was watching a field where a ball was a white coin no one could find. He walked along the chain-link fence with a zippered parka and hair that looked as if someone had taught it to be a sail. He had smiled, he always smiled first. Then he took my hand in both of his as if it were a document he needed to legalize. The small bones there beat like birds.

He is not one person, he is the cabinet of my blood and the mage of my anger and the man who stands very quietly in the room of my dreams when the room is full of water. He is the person whose voice I can summon when the power goes out; he is the face that rewrites itself every time I think it is finished.

In the cafeteria there is the smell of liquids that did not begin as liquids: cheese made into soup, the air turned into coffee. There is a map of the strait on the wall, laminated, with blue being used too many ways—light blue for depth, darker for rips where the water tears its own fabric. There is a markup with a black pen someone has used to demonstrate a route to a person who didn’t understand. This line swings wider than you would expect, out past something called The Bellows, back in close to a finger of land no one lives on in winter. A child is naming each island, then naming it again, as if there is a way of appointing guardianship with the mouth. A man in a wool cap is sleeping with his head tilted back in the way that reveals the shape of a boy he must have been, some day under a tree. I carry a tray with a bowl and a spoon and a cup and the noise of this tray on the steel rail is the noise that will stay with me when I am eighty, when I place a tray on a different rail in a white building and there is someone else across from me with a number pinned to his chest.

There are people on ferries who look like they only live on ferries, who emerge from their asthmatic dozes when the boat moves and put their coats on and then take their coats off and who can describe the shape of the current in the way a geologist might describe time. There are women with children who carry an extra coat over their arm not for themselves but as a pledge to any catastrophe. There are men with hands like oars who are reading small books about birds. There are students who are sleeping from one corner of the ship to the other, stitching a line of dream across the carpet.

I take my spoon through the cheese like a ship through illogic. The television says news and then stops saying it. A presenter sits in front of a map of far-off wars. A soundless mouth that tries to shape our luck. People do not look up. They consult their own smaller screens which, because they are lit from inside, have more power. The ferry leans into the first real wind and cups the water with itself the way we might cup a face we are not yet permitted to kiss.

On the deck the cold is a pattern. That word that is heavy sometimes like a book and sometimes light like paper ash. I breathe and am entered by my own body, which will keep me alive because it is a machine built in a garden and even now it believes in work. Ropes go to places I cannot. Straps and cleats, a language. A young deckhand is walking toward the bow with a coil over his shoulder, as if he is about to bless the sea with a sacrament shaped like a snake. He nods, because everyone nods at sea, as if we are patting the ship. Every third light is dead because this is not a movie. The town along the shore is just a seam of electricity. The house I lived in when I was ten is there somewhere and if I believed in distances the way I used to, I could point and say, There. Father snoring on the couch. Mother at the sink. Silas under the table with the dog, whispering to it the names of fish as if naming could make them ours.

Once he took me to the river. This is a thing brothers do when a parent is too quiet. He led me into cattails with a jar shaped like a lantern and a piece of bread for the guardian of bridges. He had a stick that was heavy because its wetness was new. The river that summer was widening as if we were making room for it by growing. We did not swim where the fathers swam because our father’s voice had summoned the town to join his arguments and then gone out like a light, and because of this we knew other edges—the culvert, the little cul-de-sacs of brown water where minnows were rehearsing a future. Silas put the bread on the surface like an offering and something took it just with its mouth. He laughed under his breath the way he did when something was too beautiful for noise. We agreed then without speaking that what we saw had been made for us to see.

In the next room—a lounge for quiet reading—a woman is watching me not watch her. She wears a pink hat knitted with intention. Her book is the kind that comes with a city on the cover, a city rendered in grayscale, as if it were better that way. Her eyes lift when the ferry tilts and then settle, recalibrating. She has the face of the mathematics teacher who liked me for trying. I think she knows something about bodies and arrives at this profession by having wanted to. She mouths a word—one of those words that describes birds returning—and I imagine that she has a child somewhere at a university and that this is the season when she visits and rearranges money into smaller envelopes.

It is night then. I mean the night you accept. The light of the ferry is thrown back at itself in brief white sheets as we pass islands that are not so much islands as platforms for making lighthouses. I understand my birth in flashes like this. Someone putting a hand into warm water and finding a world there. People talk in small voices when the world outside is bigger. A man with a beard is telling a story about a storm in a way that removes the storm and replaces it with his hands. He demonstrates the size of waves with one palm and an eye. The others nod as if they’ve been on the storm’s board of directors. He stops and looks out across the night with the face of a person who has lost and found everything—but then a teenager laughs nearby and corrupts the elegy. I am glad.

There is a paper I will be asked to sign in the morning. I wash my spoon and dry it with the motion of my breath and the cloth they give you with coffee cups in places like this. A man will ask me if I’m sure. He will say, If you are not sure, we will take more time. There is time. Then he will say the name of the organ that will tell the truth if we ask it to, and he will have a list of letters in his pocket. If the body is not my brother’s, then I will go back to the other grief, which is of the man who is not dead but has decided not to be my brother. That grief is larger or smaller than the first, I cannot decide.

When we were children and it was April, there would be a day when everything was still wet, and we would collect the names. Silas invented that. He would put a stone in his pocket and it would be a name, and a piece of green glass from the abandoned lot would be another, and a nail would be father and a train ticket would be mother. He taught me that keeping was an art without law. This was in a time when he was still my older brother, walking with the swagger of someone who had been given a room in the world to practice. We were small enough to miss dinner and for it to be all right. When he was twelve he opened his palm to me and there was a bee there that had died of cold and he blew into the legs as lightly as if he were playing a harmonica, and he insisted it would live if we urged it. He said, Watch. And the bee did not live, and I learned to love him for the arrogance of that. Later he took things from us when he needed them and did not return them. He took the camera wrapped in our father’s sweater. He took the chequebook with its carbon copies blue as rivers. He took, finally, the letter I wrote him to tell him that I had stopped waiting, and I saw him fold it and put it into his pocket as if I had died and he had inherited my bones.

There is a bench by the window facing the bow. Someone has left on it three red playing cards fanned face-up as if the gambler had to rise suddenly. Queen of hearts, queen of hearts, seven of spades. This is for me. There are stories that play themselves. A boy in the corner is building a ship out of blocks that fit together in limited ways. He has placed on the deck a small animal from the wrong set—a giraffe on a ferry. His mother is speaking to him in a language that sounds like mountains when the wind is high and he answers in the other language because that is how children keep their countries. At the end I think he will make the animal leap. The ferry shifts and the giraffe topples, and the boy redeploys it to the edge as if that were intention.

The cold comes into the lounge at intervals, a messenger. A woman has taken off her ring and put it beside her book, then slid it half an inch nearer and then back. This moon of vows. She is looking at her own hand as if it is a shoreline she cannot map. She is not my story. I do not have a story tonight that is not the old one. I think of the man who allowed himself rot, the one our mother said had eaten his way into a depth where there is only the sound of your own heart. I think of the letters that were sent addressed to Silas care of whomever we knew he was staying with, each returning to us with a rubber stamp’s cuneiform. Not at this address. Gone away. The curious courtesy of that phrase.

I make my bed a little and lie on it the way you test the water with your palm before you reach for something under it. Sleep comes over me in our father’s shirt, which still smells of something like campfire after all these years if campfire were a cologne old men used to wear in the days when men dressed in their houses to go out and not to come back. The engine arranges me. The ship’s ticking. The subtle shifts, the luffs, the hush when the bow noses into a smoother patch. I listen and collect. Children as they shift in their dreams are audible as birds.

The last time we spoke he was calling from a payphone; you could hear it take small bites of his voice. The wind moved like a new idea through the phone booth, and he kept saying, It is not what you imagine, as if imagination were a gate we could pass. He had been working with a man who painted houses and a woman who ran a kitchen and a boy who didn’t go to high school in the winter because it was a ferry ride away, and he had other nouns for everything now. He said: People who do these jobs have a vocabulary of nails. He said: I could build a staircase from the continent to your house if I wanted. I said, If you come here I will make you soup, and we will not talk about the old thefts. He laughed for the first time in weeks and then said he had to go because a bus had arrived that was willing to be his home for that evening. There was also a girl in the background who was practicing the skill of walking without looking at the ground. And after that he didn’t call for months and then he wrote a brief letter from a place where wind must have begun, the paper wrinkled by the science of his pocket. He said, I am paid and I am unpaid. He said, I saw a man in a canoe carve his own paddle as he drifted and I wanted to steal his tools. He said, If it is true you have learned to live in one place, I would like to be wrong next to you.

There is a horn on the ferry that sings like someone with a gift. When it sounds the night presses into our ears, and the gulls are recommissioned as orchestras. The small child with the giraffe has fallen asleep with his mouth open. Outside the spray is not water but small men with hammers. The deck glistens under a film the light cannot remove. A sailor is carrying a triangle of metal and when he walks the triangle rings at a rhythm set by his body. I see the captain behind glass and he has his hand on a wheel that looks old, the only romantic object in an otherwise unrevealing assembly of dials and numbers, and I wonder if he is thinking of the lover he cannot write to because there would be nothing to say that does not involve claiming a weather system.

Sleep returns in coils. When I wake it is because the ship has changed its angle like a man resigning himself to a love. A bell that had been pretending to be silent is now ringing. There are footsteps in the hallway with the confidence of people who staff ships. Someone down the corridor is crying into their sleeve and then it is over. The sound of a vending machine being opened with a key and a bang. A woman's voice reminding a child that the dream is not real but the voice makes the dream last. I look at my watch and it is a number that belongs to no one. I could still be in the house where I have been living the life that contains no other. I could be in the cabin where I once took a body’s warmth into mine that was not mine to keep. I could be in our mother’s kitchen tying the cords of brown paper into a present of bread.

Silas and I were twelve and fourteen once, then fifteen and seventeen, and each time we thought we were done becoming, there would be another calendar, and on it there would be a day when we did or were done to. One summer was the summer of knives; we learned that knives were not things to throw at trees. Another was the summer of bees’ nests; we learned they were inside the ground as well as in branches. Another summer was the summer of the phone calls that came late in the night with breathing. We learned who had our number. Another was the summer of the forbidden quarry, green water that pretended to be clean and killed a boy with calves like a runner’s. You could hear his mother from the far side, long after, in the squares of air the boys would cross with their backpacks coming out of paper routes and into their father’s cars to be told for an hour they were fools. Silas was always almost in trouble and almost forgiven. He had a way of placing his fingers on the table in front of a man that made the man like him. He had their exhaustion inside him by the time he was eighteen. He had their cheer. He had their disappointment like a second suit layer. He had the shame our father should have carried and did not. In the high school auditorium he stood with the other graduates and when they threw the caps, he did not. He put his in the pocket of his blue gown and went home with it like a small, square animal.

I walk the length of the ferry. The dampness might as well be printed with a watermark listing the names of those who have stood here thinking of the dead. The forward observation lounge has a kind of faded grandeur: chairs in theater rows, lights dimmed to a degree that suggests respect, long windows in which the night is also watching us. Somewhere behind the walls there are two engines the size of small churches. They are counting. They are telling the sea how far apart each turn is. No one is here except an old man who could be a lighthouse keeper on leave, eyes like tongues tasting the night. He nods in the way of people who have nothing to add and are therefore rich. For a moment I want to sit near him and say, Did you have a brother, too? At once this is something I am obliged to know and something I must never ask. Instead I look at the small plaque on the wall that says who the ferry is named for, and the name is a woman’s name and I find that this comforts me as if I had been thinking of saints and my favorite walked in with her sleeves rolled up and said, Enough with saints.

It seems the ferry lifts slightly and then relaxes, a habit I did not know ships had. The boy with the giraffe wakes briefly and says, I dreamed of a building with no stairs. His mother says, That is a good building, now sleep. The boy says, But how did we go up? She says, We didn't have to. There is, in the very bottom of our lives, the phrase we didn't have to, and if I dig there with a spoon I find it full of the seeds the birds did not come back for. I think of a time Silas and I stood in a field at the edge of town watching a fox move from one shadow to another, the way a hand moves from one pocket to the next, with nothing too urgent about it. He said, If you sing they don’t spook. He sang something under his breath—one of those songs the radio played as if it were a news bulletin—and the fox paused and looked at us as if we were not yet finished being human.

In the morning with the dark still not believing we are sincere, there is the smell of toast and ship metal. People move in early manners. They are kind to each other because they are not yet what their day will extract. I drink from a small cup that makes my hand seem huge, and I think of the photograph of the two of us taken when I was five and he seven, in our mother’s front yard, where the sea is behind us like a promise. We are wearing shirts that were once white and are now a new color that will not be named. We look like we lived in an envelope no one sent. His arm is over my shoulder and even this was a dare. We look into the licked glass of the camera and do not blink. Behind us the neighbor’s boat is on its trailer. It is made of wood that has not yet learned the names of paint. The grass has the precise green of televisions when we first had them, and the antenna is pointed at another life.

The port we come into is the last mouth in the string of mouths. There is a tower with a light and a man on that tower in a red hat, and his hand is as good as language, he is telling us to come in. There is a gull with one leg, balanced like the point of a pencil. There are piles of salt like small countries under tarps. The town is a set of roofs and chimneys and a hotel with vinegar in the carpet. The docking is the hardest part, and everyone watches as the rope is thrown and captured, because landing is sex and we are all hoping to be included. The loudspeaker tells us to be ourselves. Vehicles begin their measured ballet. Buses shiver awake. There is a boy with a brass instrument case held like a baby. I go down into the belly and through the mouth again onto the dock, and the wind has not changed its mind.

I walk among the cars like a wrong object in a game for children. My breath is steady; I have told it to be. The coroner’s building—if that is what it is called here—sits at the edge of a parking lot in which trucks have rested overnight in the positions of sleeping horses. The building is a square of wood and windows like a portable at a school. It does not try to be anything else. Men in caps are gathered near the door, smoking and setting their eyes in that way men do when they are thinking of another boyhood. A woman with a clipboard holds the morning like glass. I say the name at the desk and the woman says mine back, to check if I still am. She tells me to sit and I sit. A radio tells someone else what to do with their hands. A clock that has not yet learned its numbers is on the wall. There is a smell like the inside of a new book with something beneath it like iron.

The man I spoke to on the telephone appears wearing a jacket the color of bread. He has the hands of a man who has never done carpentry and never will. He shakes mine and I feel we are both entering a story without preparation. He says, We can take our time. He says, There are things you may not want to see and that is a dignity you are allowed to keep. He says, If this is not your person, there is still the person he is. He says, You can go in and come out and go in again. He speaks as if each sentence is balanced by a thought he will never say out loud, which is the way all important talk should be. I walk with him through a hall that bleeds its own light and can’t stop. We pass a bulletin board on which are pinned notices for flu shots, a poster that shows a small hand washing a big one, a photograph of the coastline taken from the angle of a dog.

He opens a door onto a room where light is not simply light. Things are covered because most of us love our skin. The window faces the harbor and shows the ferry like an animal that has just drunk. The man says a name I do not recognize and for a moment he is laughing and it’s late at night and he has pulled a record from a sleeve and he and his wife are dancing in a square of kitchen bare feet. He is that person, too. He pauses with his hand on the sheet as if he is about to act in a play. He looks at me and I nod and this is how human life is organized, through gestures that are permissions, through surrenders you once vowed never to make.

He folds the sheet back just enough. There is a face which is a face and also not yet. There is water’s work there, not so much bloating as an effect like someone has translated his features into another language and then back. The lips are parted as if he meant to speak one more number. The eyes are closed as if in disagreement. The nose is that shape it always was, a run of bone with a confidence at the end. I cannot say immediately. I am in the field behind the school and Silas has his hand on the back of my neck because a boy named Simon has called me something and we do not want that to happen again. I am in the corner of our mother’s house and he is showing me a knot that makes a loop that holds and falls away. He says, Learn this and you can tie a boat to the world. I am in the apartment where the woman is turning on the light behind him and he is saying he will go to the store and carry anything back that we want. I am in the hallway of the shelter where he is folding a towel with that fast good motion he has even when there is no money and the light is poor and the person beside him is one of those you never can be. I am listening to his voice on a payphone and he is telling me that there are two species of cormorant in our province and he has learned their names so that he can carry them and be less empty.

There is a scar by the ear, the little flicker of white like a remembered lie. There is the notch at the hairline where he once stepped into a doorframe because he was thinking of other labor and the frame did not move and the sharp part caught him. There is the one front tooth whose edge, if you rub it gently, is rough as a coast because he bit a nail when he was seven and the nail came away but made something of him forever in the mouth. These are things I can swear by. Still, I think of men I have known who look like each other when they are wrecked. I think of the photograph newspapers use for the dead because they look like a person who might become important.

The man waits. He is good at waiting. He has not given me the word to say. He says, I could show you the hand. People sometimes know the hand. He says, There is a tattoo. And I say, A tattoo? Because in my story there is no ink on his skin. He says, A small thing. Not recent. It is on the upper arm. He looks at me then because he is the man who shows and takes away sheets for the rest of his days and he knows the weight of my looking and he is attending to it. I nod and he closes the face into the white again and walks to the other side with the care you use when a person is sleeping and you do not want to wake them by mistake. He folds back the cloth and I see the pale landscape of shoulder and the slope of the arm and then, near the place the sleeve would have ended, a blue mark, blurred by years. Two letters. F and J. Or E and L. Or the shapes a boy finds in a book of treasure maps and copies with the point of a knife into the desk. He says, It looks like welding. Sometimes boys take the doorjamb and a hot nail. He says this to see if the world will remain the world while he says it. I did not know of this.

Silas would melt the edge of his name into anything. He liked the permanence of corners. But this is not that. It could be that he marked himself in some year when I had retired from looking, because that is what an older brother does. He says, Look away. He says, I will make my own weather.

We stand so long that time comes into the room with a broom and begins to sweep gently from one end to the other.

He says, You could wait for the doctor. We could have photographs. You could look again later in the day. People do not always say yes at first. He does not say, People sometimes say the wrong yes, because he is not as cruel as that.

The hands. I learned my brother’s hand by losing to it. He would place the coin in his palm and cover it with a second hand and then lift that hand away and there would be no coin. It would be in the other hand. Then it would be in my pocket. Then it would be under my tongue and I would nearly die of it and our mother would say, Enough. There was always a callus on the index finger where a pencil had dug a small well. There was a knot on the right thumb from before we met that belonged to our father, a story in itself. There was a way, when he slept, of folding his fingers underneath his cheek like wings.

The man has the idea and he holds it out in both hands like bread: Would you like me to bring up the hand?

The sheet is just cloth but it is also a border. He shows me the hand. It is not the hand I know, but it is exactly the hand I know. The nails are clean. The little white moons at their base like instruments the dead still use. There is a cut on the pad of the thumb that lifts slightly where skin has learned to make its own door. I touch the edge of his knuckle with one finger and it is like pressing on the ice to see if it will bear you. There is that same unseen script in the tendons, the same writing the body gives itself and then refuses to read. I say nothing. The man knows enough languages to translate silence. He says, I will give you a minute and steps out. The door closes as if it were closing around a poem.

I think, It is him. I think, It is not him. I think, It is a man somebody else loved, and that is enough to make him ours.

On the way here I had promised that I would tell the truth to whatever the morning asked. But truth is an arrant drunk who does not show all his pockets at once. Truth in daylight is not the creature you slept with. I have not cried since the museum I went to with a woman I did not marry, in front of a painting of a winter river with three men in the snow who looked like they had run there just to be struck by light. I stand now with my hands in the pockets of my coat and the cold pushes hard where the cloth gives.

When we were small, there were fits of our father’s that were frightening only in retrospect, then only when we were in houses where such men did not live. He smashed a plate once, and the shards came to the floor like a minute of snow, and Silas looked at me with a face that said, We will survive this by inventory. He kept his own record in a tin lunchbox he never took to school. In it were the objects of our youth as they fell out of orbit: a bent bicycle spoke, the cigarette paper that carried a girl’s kiss, a Canadian Tire slip with the dollar scratched out, two circus tickets from a summer we never attended a circus, the cap from the medicine that took our mother through the month a dog lived in the house, a white stone carried home from the quarry and washed until it had surrendered all the quarry’s story, a postcard of a ruined hotel where our aunt claimed to have hidden in a broom closet while men in tuxedos ran down the hall with guns. The letter I wrote—he burned that. He told me later by holding his empty hands palms up. Illiterate men love fire the way readers love libraries.

There is a bell ringing distantly for a minute and then not, the ferry announcing another thing. The man in the bread-colored jacket comes back and stands still beside me and does not look where I look. He has a way with doors. He says, You can come back. He says, You can say now, and you can say something else after. He says, Would you like a chair, and when I say no he does not bring one. He allows my animal to decide.

I hear my brother’s voice from some year talk around the lump in his cheek from the tooth he never had fixed. He is telling a woman he loves her and meaning it the way a man does when he is about to turn away from that love at speed. He is singing in German to a grandfather of someone else in an upstairs room. He is setting the pieces of a deck of cards on the kitchen table, four equal stacks, dealing out fates as neatly as you would cut bread. He is counting to ten for a boy whose father is not there. He is reading out dockets at a charity auction, and no one can tell if he is raising money or giving it away.

I say his name in the room. I say it like a coin that has gone dull. The name hangs there not because it is heavy but because it is what all the air has wanted. The man writes it down on a form only when I repeat it without question. He does this not to satisfy the institution but to make the morning nod. He says, We’ll need to talk to you after. I say, I can stay, and he says, There's a place. He watches my mouth then because mouths sometimes tell what hands do not. He says, If you’d like to take a moment with him, you can.

I do not touch his face. I put my hand flat on the sheet near the shoulder as if somebody could come along and push our hands together like gloves and it would be a joke. I say something to him that a child would know is untrue and good and necessary.

The door opens and closes. The others in the hall are working at their own ships. On a desk a plant people forgot to water learns another season. When I step outside, the day is flawed magnificence—the kind that makes you think of dying because it is so clean about living. The gull with the one leg has crossed the lot, a small monk. A man in a truck is on the phone saying, I am here. I am here. I would like to be elsewhere but I am here. The ferry is backing away already, its square end filling again with those who are staying, those who will not be asked to say the name of a dead person today. They will be asked to do other things.

I walk down to the water because the water has been my way to walk everywhere. There are rocks, and between them small worlds. Each world is a city without us. Each bit of weed holds its own green. Small birds that do not belong to my vocabulary greet the morning with motions that are more precise than words. The sea inside the harbor is a dog on a leash; it pulls and is pulled. Across the strait a trowel of sunlight is laying down a brighter version of what we have. One of the men who had been smoking near the door is carrying a box into the building with a weight that tells me it is not heavy and will become heavy depending on the sentence we are in. He stops, sets it down, lifts his cap, scratches the part of his head where worry is stored, puts the cap back on and picks up the box again. We share a look that is practical. He is not asking me for anything, which is the greatest kindness.

You can stand near a body as long as you like in some places. In others you are made to leave because the rituals have been assigned to agencies. I think of our mother in the kitchen the afternoon we came home with news about a boy who had not died but wished he had. She sat with her hands under the table like she was holding a small animal that would bite if the light hit it. She said, We are not entirely our worst days. She said, We will sleep on it. Silas laughed then and the laugh was one of those short barks that remove oxygen. He said, I can’t stand when people say, We will sleep on it. He said, Because then you go to sleep and you dream and the dream decides and you don’t have any say and it wasn’t decided by you at all. Mother said, That is why we say it. She said, Because some things should not be decided by you.

Silas is not here to argue with my grief. He is not here to say, Make me soup. He is not here to lay a map on the table and draw the route a ferry takes through a strait that is trying to forget its own name. He is not here to run a finger along the scar that is on my forehead from the day I dove into the shallow part of the river because I believed in my speed more than in water. He is not here to tell me that the girl I think I love should be left alone because she loves being alone. He is not here to tell me to keep the little radio; he will take the big one and teach it the weather.

After the forms there is the small office. In it there is a photograph of the man’s daughters. He does not tell me they are his, but the way he straightens the frame tells me stories that would not do well in ink. He hands me a page with a number on it that will call a woman at a desk that is not this one, and a page with another number that will buy us a plot of earth beside strangers, and a page that has nothing on it but lines. He says, If you need to sit longer, you can sit. He says, I am sorry in a way that makes me want to say, It is fine. I do not. I say, thank you, and I mean it. He has been the correct instrument. I have been the string and the unintelligible music.

I carry in my coat a letter that he sent two winters ago, the last thing I have of him with his voice touched down on it. There is a drawing of a bird in the corner, a bird that is not a type but is the idea of bird. He writes, I have learned to be quiet and to be less quiet. He writes, I have loved people you would not like and I have loved people you would have been so happy to like if you’d met them. He writes, I keep my knife very sharp and my nails clean. He writes, If ever you are north and you see a man in a red cap fixing the railing of a deck with a small girl holding a flashlight, that man could be me. He writes, If you do not come, I will call you a coward in my sleep. He writes, If you come, we will find the boy we lost and give him a better library.

On the dock there is a bus with passengers getting on one after another. They put their coins into the slot like penitents offering small burnt animals. A woman at the end of the wooden pier throws bread to the gulls and then is surprised when they arrive. She laughs and would be beautiful in a painting. An old man moves a lobster trap and then another and each becomes the place the trap was before, then not. The sky has woken. The day will go about arranging itself, which is all we ask of days.

When the ferry’s horn sounds again, I look up with the stupid hope of seeing a boy waving. A boy in a jacket he did not return. A boy whose hair the wind pulled but did not displace. A boy who is also a man standing very still in a room while a man in a jacket the color of bread waits until the living remember how.

I close my eyes and say his name again and this time it is a different thing in my mouth. It has dropped its coin and unpicked its knots and set down its deck of cards and slid its arm through mine. It is heavy enough. It is not the first time I have carried something like this. It will not be the last.

I walk back down the pier to find coffee, to sign the next paper, to call the number that will make a car come that will take me to the house where I will sit with a woman on a couch and tell her the story in a voice that sounds too much like someone else’s voice. On the way I pass the boy with the brass instrument. He sets his case on a bench and opens it and touches the gold as if it is something that breaks, and then he closes the case again and lifts it and keeps walking with it held like a promise. The gulls make their letters in the sky and I cannot read them, and for once I do not mind. The ferry, coming back in, being readied, being unlaced from last night, floats on its own heavy dream.

I think of his hand in mine, the one I put my finger on for a second in that room, the way it would fit the way it used to when we crossed streets because the light was coming for us and we wanted to call it music. I plan nothing. I keep close something an old man once said to me on a ferry crossing a different strait three years ago when we were both too tired to lie anymore. He leaned in, that stranger, with a mouth that smelled like an orchard in winter, and said, The Lord is only a ferry. He brings you across. He leaves you on the other side and goes back for someone else.

If I had to carry my brother across anything, I would choose this. I would choose water and rust and the wind that speaks in small, truthful languages to rope. I would choose morning and the bowl of made soup and the boy with the wrong animal on his boat and the ferry turned just so, the captain invisible and all of us deciding to pray at the same time, for a minute, without telling anyone that we are.
