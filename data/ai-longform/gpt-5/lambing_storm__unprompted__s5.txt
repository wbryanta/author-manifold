The wind had a way of turning the old shed into an instrument. It found the seam in the long side where the tin panel had never quite met straight and it played it until the whole place hummed. Maggie knew that hum. She knew the separate note of the latch on the big door and the skitter of snow pebbles flicked like grit through the gaps and the deepened tone of the rafters when the gusts came hard, as if the roof were drawing its own breath. If the wind had a voice, the ewes had their replies—throaty, full, distracted, and constant, a sound that took up residence in your ears during lambing and went with you into sleep, if you got any. Late April and it was snow, the storm pushing white sideways across the yard and pasting it on to the lean of the hill as if they were back at January and the calendar had lost its place. It reached in under the doors to settle in blown salt-white curls on the black rubber mats. It frothed at the threshold to the pens like some mad ocean. She had closed up the vents and shoved sacks into the cracks and the wind still made its music.

Sam stood with his hood up and his hair damp where it had come free, holding the iodine bottle like an offering and glancing up at the strip-light every time it flickered. He had a bad habit of standing with his feet splayed, the way Hugh had, as if claiming his ground. He was taller than his father had been at sixteen, all bone and outgrowth, a boy pretending to be a man because the world had made him.

“Blue stripe fifty-four is starting,” he said, his voice muffled in his scarf. He pointed with the neck of the iodine bottle toward a pen where a mottled Mule was pawing the straw and worrying at it as if the straw could be made to reveal a hidden door.

“I saw,” Maggie said. She slid her hand along the wall where she kept the grease pot, the jar of ropes, the lube. The ropes—soft, red, cotton loops—were Hugh’s, his knots, the same thickness as his fingers had been. She had told herself this year she would replace them, as if that would be easier. She hadn’t.

Blue stripe fifty-four turned a circle and her bag hung, glistening, one corner of it scraped and cloudy as if she’d been at it awhile. The mule’s ear tips were crusted white with blown snow that had melted and refrozen as she came in. Maggie shook some grains of coarse salt into her palm and held it out across the rail. The ewe lipped at it on her way around, the rough edge of her tongue and the mild, old-lanolin smell of her breath. Maggie wasn’t sure if it calmed the ewes or her to offer them salt at moments like this; it was what she did.

A fresh lamb was crying thinly in the next pen, bright mouth paddling at the air as if he were gulping in the world that had not yet taught him the edges of his hunger. The ewe there was a good mother, attentive, nose working, the sound she made like someone talking in their sleep. “Dip him,” Maggie said without looking. Sam was half a step ahead; he had already knelt and pressed the little belly gently aside to find the wet umbilicus and dunk it in the amber solution, once, twice, holding him still with more gentleness than he knew he owned. He had Hugh’s hands for that, too: big-knuckled and sure when they remembered how.

The shed was warmer than outside. Straw, dung, milk, the close heat of ruminant bodies, that perfume that was comfort and labor both. They had everything they could want within reach. Kettle. Bucket. Marker spray. String. Thick towels ripped from an old bath sheet. A jar with its cracked blue lid of frozen colostrum, the one on which Hugh had written in block letters NOV ’22 with the thick black marker, his hand that last time inked on this winter that would not end. A small gas ring ready on the bench because they’d learned, like everyone here learned, that you made your own power when the hill reminded you who owned it. Her phone lay face-down under the corner of the lambing book, and the lambing book itself was what it had always been—edges softened with years of damp, her pencil scribbles like rapid prayers. 54 blue stripe, scanned twins. Last year, lifted a lamb for graft. Once you got into lambing your mind divided itself into two maps: the animals, and the weather. Everything was pegged to those latitudes.

Blue stripe fifty-four stopped her circles and braced. She lowered her head as if to head-butt the bale, then chose instead to stare into the core of herself. Maggie leaned into the pen over the rail and put her hand on the ewe’s back, the simple human touch of it. “You’ve got this, love,” she said out loud. Saying love to a sheep didn’t make sense and was exactly what made sense. Sam had the head torch in his pocket and turned it on because some shadow slouched across the roof just then and the fluorescent failed to hold the brightness steady. The head torch threw a precise cone that turned the white of the straw violet and cleaned the world down to its details.

“Membrane?” he said.

“Watch,” she said. She could see the ewe’s lips parting under the tail, the ring of slick flesh and inside it a glimmering bubble, and then the front hooves slipped into view like a pair of tongue tips, still sheathed in their soft slippers. Hugh had once called them their shoes, as if the lambs arrived prepared for their first steps with something white and temporary they could leave at the door.

“Good,” Maggie murmured, the contraction easing. She reached and held gently to the little feet, two fingers above the pastern, not pulling so much as being present, letting the ewe feel the promise of the direction. Hugh had always taught patience in this moment—the letting of the ewe do it, the midwife’s art. There were times for being forceful; this wasn’t one. Blue stripe fifty-four bore down again. The cone splashed, and a head appeared, nostrils already working, tongue out. Maggie wiped the lamb’s nose with her fingers and flicked away the membranes. “Hello, little one,” she said. “Come on then.”

Between them and the sound of the wind muffled as if now it were on the other side of glass somewhere far away, the lamb slid out, shiny as oil, all angles and the pin-boned shock of a thing that only moments before had been a theory. Maggie rubbed him with the towel, brisk, the way you got friction into a life. The ewe’s first lick was fervent, her eyes wild like someone waking. Sam had the iodine ready and moved in when the cord dangled long enough. He did not look away from the ewe’s mouth on the lamb. He seldom did, even now. Maggie remembered being that young and being transfixed by the first time-warping minutes when the animal formed its world around another animal and you were allowed to watch.

“Twin?” Sam said.

“Should be,” she said, already aware of the space inside the ewe that had not yet collapsed, the way she breathed—those things you learned without meaning to. Blue stripe fifty-four nosed and cried, low and persuasive, calling in a rhythm that made Maggie’s chest ache. The contractions started again and were easier the second time. The second lamb came as if remembering the path set by his brother. He was bigger and slower and unrolled himself like a thing from a package. Maggie cleared his nose. He sneezed and the sound was the strangest triumph.

They gave the ewe a little time. They gave everyone time where they could. The kettle clicked off on the shelf from the electric, and that noise, that small ceremony—steam, warm water, Maggie thinking of cups—did something to the tuck of her shoulders. She looked at it only to look away, because looking was jinxing. She poured some of the boiled water into the small blue bucket to temper the colostrum thawing there. If she thought of cups and mugs and the house, she thought of Hugh in the kitchen slumped in the chair he had claimed and the old brown teapot sweating on the stove back, the way the house held lambing inside it, too, the way straw rode in on cuffs and socks left wet heaps against the radiator and the radio gave the scores from matches they would not see. The kettle’s hum made her throat tight now; you do not understand, she wanted to say to appliances, how not to be his winter.

Sam handed her the marker spray and she marked the lambs with the same stripe as the ewe’s raddle, brisk strokes down their ribs—blue on white, the claim like a flag. Sam glanced sidewise at her mouth, at the notch in the skin above her top lip that got deeper when she was thinking. He had done it since he was small, watching her face for the reading of the day. She set the spray down where it would not be knocked into straw. The ewes did not care for decorum around chemicals or tools. If they could eat it, they would. If they could step on it, they would. It put them in a fair and basic fraternity with all farm work.

A noise like a sheet coming loose in a storm from a clothesline and slapping itself senseless ran across the shed roof, and then the fluorescent light strobed hard twice and went out. The red lamps in the corners died all at once. There was darkness, and then there was the sound of the generator not being there—an absence, something pulling down time. The lambing shed felt twice the size with the sound gone. Someone somewhere and long ago had put a crucifix up on one of the uprights. A remnant from when the farm was Catholic, or maybe all farms were Catholic if you meant it right. Tonight, it was just wood.

Sam’s hand went instantly to the head torch at his own head and he clicked it on so fast that it was funny and sad. He sweated even though the wind was banging the door and the cold came like a fist. The beam washed the nearest pens with a tunnel of certainty that made the other corners more dark. A lamb in the next pen bawled as if objecting to the lamp’s disappearance, ridiculous, as if the sun had left. “Batteries?” Maggie said.

“In my pocket. Two spares.” His voice came fast. He bobbed his head to throw light into the far right corner because that was where the triplets were, a pale ewe with too many and that tightness between her ears that said she was overwhelmed. He had that instinct already, how you turned your attention to what might break and how you let the easy ones alone. Hugh had that gift and then he had learned it over thirty years, both, the contradiction that farming always was. You came into it with a heart made for it, or it tore you and made the shape itself.

She had the gas ring out and the cylinder under the rough bench. The matches were in the tobacco tin Hugh had kept since he was nineteen, darkened inside by damp and sawdust, marred in one corner by a gouge, the etched sails of the ship on its lid so worn they could have been any weather. Another one she had meant to replace. She lit the ring and that pin rain of blue flame was a comfort beyond itself. You could promise a certain amount of heat with something you controlled, and that counted more on a night like this than sleep.

“Text Gran,” she said, because saying something ordinary would keep this from carving itself too deep. “Tell her we’re grand. Tell her power’s gone but we’ve the gas ring.”

Sam’s thumbs did that moving he could do without thinking, his head bent, the torch sending the beam down to the straw. He would write something that was two words and it would mean all there was to mean. He put the phone back in the inside of his jacket. He shifted his feet and the straw made a dry ocean noise.

Blue stripe fifty-four’s lamb made that little first bleat again and she laughed to herself because that sound was beyond power or light, it was its own weather. She rolled her sleeves higher. Her forearms were red and furred with light old scars, lines of story: the barbed wire at twelve, the angle grinder bite at twenty-seven, the nip of an anxious ewe two years ago when she had reached and been clumsier than she liked. She rubbed a smear of lube onto her hand and wrist and up to the elbow and went to check the black-tag ewe against the wall, the one who had been at it too long earlier—hinged back, eyes wild, no progress, the dread that moved like weather, too. It all came in gusts and then stilled; you learned to read those patterns on the animals the way you read snow on the hill.

This one, Green 142—scan said twins—had that stretch in her mouth that wasn’t screaming but was saying: this is a bolt I’d undo if I could. The swell at her back end was tight white and glossy, no feet yet, and when Maggie slipped her fingers in she felt nothing for a moment and then the slick, warm knobble of a nose with no legs laid along it. “Bugger,” she said softly.

Sam looked at her fast. He always watched her face before he listened to what she said at moments like this.

“Head and no legs,” she told him. “We’ll sort it.”

She climbed into the pen and knelt, the straw wet patches cold through her trousers at once. The ewe pushed and that made it harder, but you could not blame her for working with the bit of the world she could feel. Sam held the head torch beam steady for her without her asking, the cone of light making it into a theatre. Maggie rested her left hand on the ewe’s flank to tell her that someone else was here and she let her right disappear inside again, fingers splayed like a star as she moved along what her palm told her, not rushing. Head here; tongue warm and big; jaw with the milk teeth in it; neck thickening to shoulders. The legs must be back, elbows tucked. If you saw it like a picture you would be lost. If you felt it as the curve of a set of bones and the leading edges of the joints, you could find them.

“Hold her head,” she said. “Not hard, just keep her steady.”

Her hand slid, knuckles against the ear notch, past that little thorn of cartilage by the shoulder—there, the ridge of it—and then the hock spurs, no, too far back. She moved up again. The ewe grunted and clamped and that made everything grip. Maggie breathed deep and slow, loud enough for the ewe to hear it, louder than for Sam. “Come on, now,” she said. “Let me.” She put her hand flat against the lamb’s chest and felt the bird-rattle of a heart. That was good; that made something ease in her throat. She did not say it out loud. She found the wrist—thin bones, the feel like a child’s palm in your hand when it has never worked a day. She hooked one finger over the pastern and pulled the smallest pull that would count, that would bring one knee up and then, with luck, the second would follow. The lamb gasped with his face still in the mucus and she wanted to be three hands and she had her cheek pressed against the ewe’s groin somehow ridiculous and everything in her ached with the work of not panicking.

“Hold,” she said. “Good girl.”

Hugh had said, long ago, on a distant night when rain had hammered as if it had forgotten to stop and they both were twenty-five and had thought twenty-five was the age you had to get everything right forever, that once you were inside a ewe you understood there wasn’t a strong you and a weak them; there was only life being difficult and then sometimes generous. The ewe did not choose a malpresentation; you did not choose to be the person whose hands were there; you got on with making sense of bone and muscle. He had laughed afterwards and on that same night he had slid on a patch of slurry and landed flat, and she had laughed at him, and just remembering that evening now warped the air by the pen like heat rising off a road.

Maggie’s middle finger found the groove of the other foreleg and she strained it out, the lamb jerking like a fish on a line. “Now,” she said. “Sam. When I say, pull steady, not quick, toward the hocks, then swing down. With her contractions. Not against. Keep him straight.”

Sam said, “Right,” and his voice cracked on it and she could see the Adam’s apple jump under his scarf. He braced his feet, not wide now but steady, and took hold of the loops of the little red ropes she had slipped around the pasterns. The ewe moaned and the cords in her neck stood. Maggie lifted the lamb’s head a fraction to free the shoulders that were looking to lock, and the lamb slipped that bit and held because sometimes the world likes a bit of theatre. The next contraction came and they used it, the ropes singing and the ewe grinding and Maggie murmuring and the lamb’s chest passing the pinch. Then he slithered into her lap in a clap of fluid as if he had been waiting on a ledge and had finally dared.

She cleared his nose. His eyes were cloudy with being inside but they blinked and that was enough. He wasn’t right though—pale tongue, too big for himself, tongue lolling and that thick swollen look of a head that had spent too long in the neck of a doorway. “Come on, sweetheart,” she said. “Come on.” She rubbed him with the rough towel as if she meant to start a fire, hard on the chest and along the ribs, then took his back legs and swung him upside down for a breath to drain the fluid, the little body flopping like string. She hated it every time; she did it anyway because she had seen lambs change their minds with that drain, had seen their hearts catch a different rhythm.

“Hand me the straw,” she said, and Sam was there, handful of the cleanest he could find without thinking of the words clean and straw as betraying each other.

She stuffed straw in the lamb’s mouth to gag him enough to prompt a swallow. She massaged his chest. She blew in his nose. He sputtered and then gave a pathetic, terrible, wonderful cheep. She laughed, and she would say later it was a laugh, though it broke something in her throat on its way out that sounded like a person gasping at the end of a long dive. She put the lamb up near the ewe’s nose and the ewe told him in her language that wasn’t words that he was necessary. The ewe had a second still inside; already the spasm in her belly said so. Maggie made herself wait that minute, her knees numb with cold and straw wet soaking into everything, her fingers tacky with fluid. When the next lamb slid out she slipped a hand to feel and found the back legs—breech—and pulled with the hooves flexed to keep them from tearing. That one came quicker, a sleek grey-faced ewe lamb, and you could almost see in the way she shook her head that she was of a different opinion about all this. She sneezed hard and bleated once, offended.

The lights came back then, the fluorescent humming its way to life with a delay that made you believe, for a half heartbeat, that God had done this for you. They were lit a second and died and came back and did the stuttering thing again as if they were not ready and the generator, stubborn, did not put its arm around them. The red heat lamps in the corner under which the triplet lambs had been sleeping did not relight. They hung like ornaments someone had put up for a season and forgotten to take down.

“Sod it,” Sam said, fierce, a man’s voice coming up from wherever it waited in him.

“Get a pen together for her,” Maggie said, because swearing only counted if it did not end in sorry. “Fresh straw. Hurdle. Put her in the corner there where they’ll be out of the draught.” She nodded at a calmer spot, away from the doors, near the stack of small bales where you could hide a lamb if you had to.

They moved on. There wasn’t time not to. There was a ewe in the far left pawing dryly as if she might dig herself out of the weather of her own body; there were two wet twins by the gate who thought they were freezing and who might be right; there was a lamb by the triplets’ pen whose ears were flat and whose mouth felt wrong as he mouthed the world. Maggie bent and touched the inside of his lip and his gums were white with cold. She picked him up, tucking him into her jacket and zipping it partway to hold him there against her, feeling him as if he were a hot-water bottle put in the wrong way round.

“I’ll get the gas ring going more,” Sam said. “We can do a bucket, bottles. Warm him up from the belly.”

“Not too fast,” she said automatically.

“I know,” he said, and he did; he had squinted at the vet’s poster on the wall that had a cartoon lamb with arrows showing the intestines as if laughter would make this better. You could kill a lamb with kindness if you warmed it too fast or fed it straight into a stomach like a lake. He had learned this the way you learn tipping points of everything you love.

They set the gas ring up on the shelf and put two metal buckets on, one for hot water, one for bottles. The blue flame seethed nicely. They filled old fizzy-drink bottles with hot and wrapped them in towels and put them in a cardboard box lined with sheepskins and rags for makeshift; the lamb would go in there if it came to it, a hot-water cave. They would call it the oven because humor made better pen-mates than fear. Maggie unzipped her jacket and slid the lamb into the box. He did not complain, which was good or bad. She dipped her finger in the warm water and rubbed his gums again until they pinked a fraction. He had no mother to feed him from. He was the third of the triplets; the ewe had looked at him and said, in her calm ewe way, no.

“Can we try grafting later?” Sam said, meaning: skin him and put him on a spare ewe if he died, or better, rub him in the slime of a stillborn to cheat another mother into loving him. It was a brutal arithmetic that you did with the tenderest of hopes.

“If we have a dead,” Maggie said, because she had learned never to invite death with a plan for its usefulness.

They were halfway across the shed when the door rammed and blew open a foot and snow came in like foam kicked from a wave. They had wedged a sack of barley against it because sheep and doors could not be trusted. Maggie lunged and slammed it shut with her shoulder, and the edge of the wood ripped her jacket and bit into her upper arm so she hissed without choosing to. Sam’s head was up, checking. It was always checking, always your ears scanning for that cry that was wrong, that silence that meant a lamb had lain forgotten too long. Some people hated lambing for the way it seemed to demand something unsustainable from you for three weeks. Maggie loved it, even when she hated its nights, because it insisted that you put your hands on need.

“Generator?” Sam said presently, breath coming a little fast.

“We can use the stove, the torches,” Maggie said.

He breathed out and the visible steam of it curled in the beam of his head torch. “Dad would—” he started and then stopped and the silence of the half-word was more painful than the word.

“Your dad would be swearing and saying leave it until the morning and then he’d go out in the divil of this and freeze his fingers and make it go,” she said. “And then he’d stand back and say ‘good girl’ to the engine.”

Sam’s mouth tightened and he nodded. He looked older when he nodded in that particular way; he had a line between his brows that Hugh had never had until his late thirties. Maggie dug in the crate where the Jerry cans lived under the bench and found one with a trace-diesel smell and shook it: light, not empty. She imagined going out into the storm and the feeling of diesel on her hands and it drying them to leather and the way that smell clung not unlike iodine. She pictured the generator facing away from the weather, the rope on the starter, the way you had to mind the black wire that had habit of working itself loose and remember to twist it back. She made a decision and it wasn’t full of bravery. “Leave it,” she said. “We’ll be fine with the torch and the gas for now.”

Sam stared at her for a tilt and then nodded once. He would have gone out if she’d told him, and it would have been a showing-off pretend courage or a true one, and in either case she would have had to live with the image of him vanishing into that blizzard. She thought: there will be days we cannot protect each other, but not today.

The ewes edged their bodies to new corners of their pens, turning their flanks to the draught, tucking their lambs into the sweet wedge of space between their front legs the way they do, making a hedge with their knees. That gesture was so old that it hit you like music from childhood. The lambs were all novelty and bone. The two by the gate, the white-faced ones Maggie had worried for, improved under a tea-towel rub, their mouths redder with blood where it should be. She watched one latch clumsily to the teat and understood better than her own breathing the way the ewe’s milk descended—how the teat would let down labored at first and then with a let-go. She could stand there and be no other person.

By two in the morning the lambing book had gained five entries and Maggie’s writing tightened to a cramped rippled row where her hand had stiffened. The wind had found a new frequency that made a nail in the far door cry in a way that raised the hair on your neck. Somewhere, something in the yard that was loose made a rhythmic knock. The cold had eaten the cheap feeling out of the shed and replaced it with something ceremonial—dark and work and breath.

Sam had gently tubed the oven-lamb, which had some warmth back in his splayed toes now. He’d measured the tube from nose to the last rib before he slid it in, had checked for breath from the end to be sure he wasn’t putting it in a lung, and had flushed warm, yellow colostrum from the borrowed ewe into the belly proper. His face had been tight with concentration. The lamb had swallowed and his stomach had rippled and Maggie had lifted his head anyway because old habits die reluctantly, and the lamb had sneezed.

“Name him?” Sam had asked, meaning joke-name him, because the oven had seen a parade of Bread, Muffin, Roast and once, for a lamb with a single black ear, Jammy.

“Furnace,” she had said, and he had snorted.

At three, the black tongue of a cloud folded itself down the valley across the barn aperture. There were no stars. The far hill was not a hill because white-on-white has no edge. Maggie tried not to think of the drifts blowing up along the lane and what the morning would mean for the feed bins and the in-lamb ones still out in the lower field if the fence line gave. She tried not to think beyond the four tin walls because that was what night did—it said, we are the world.

At three-thirty, with her shoulders at their dumb old ache and her hands whitened with dried colostrum, there came a sound out of the long pen that made both of them look up like hounds sniffing. An old ewe, grey about the face, was making a sound that was not the sound of labor or love but the baffled, tinny cry you heard when a cervix didn’t open right or when a lamb was crossways and nobody could get to it. She had a leader’s presence; she had raised three singles in three separate years with a calm that had made her earn a reprieve from culling twice. Maggie had had to fight Hugh for her, both times. Now Grey 10, with her wise eyes, was pressing the small of her back into the board and biting at the air, the water sac burst with a wash over her hocks that made steam in the torchlight.

Maggie went in. “What?” Sam said, already moving to climb the hurdle to hold at the head.

“Feels wrong,” Maggie said, her voice too steady, because if she let the undertow show she would not be able to do it. In her mind she could have drawn the lamb: not full sideways, not breech, but like something had decided to try coming with only its shoulder. She slicked her arm again until she was shining to the elbow. Sam put his palm on Grey 10’s cheek, and the ewe leaned into it for half a breath as if he were a wall and she a person resting. The lamb inside was heavy. If she had not been this cold, Maggie might have sweated.

She worked, and she entered a space in herself where the wind could not make a dent. She moved the lamb’s forelimb, found the way the elbow would not give, pushed the head back a whisper—dangerous because if you lost it you might never find it again—and eased that stuck shoulder to a place where it would be a shoulder. She got the loop on the pastern and felt the ewe spasm and saw sparks in the corners of her own eyes and swore very lightly. She put the second loop on the other foot because she was not an idiot. “Right,” she said. “Now we mean it.” They pulled. The lamb came in increments the size of your little finger bones, then like a jar lid easing and then not, and then like a thing remembering how. He came out like you wanted to cry at something too simple to explain, with a call that was faint but there. He was enormous, a ram lamb. His head was bused from the struggle and his tongue purpled at the edges and she didn’t like that. She rubbed him and he did the thin squeal and then again and then made the noise that had more down in it and she put him to Grey 10’s mouth. The old ewe made a sound then as if she had climbed a hill in her own head and found the view. She licked him with a ferocity that might have rescinded all Maggie’s bad decisions, and the lamb flopped and had the indecency to sneeze like an old man.

“You bastard,” Maggie told him, joyous.

Sam laughed in a sound that was too loud in the lamplit shed. He wiped his face with his sleeve and left a smear of filth across his cheek, a thumb-line like war-paint.

They worked until the kind of tiredness came where your hands became things you looked down at and asked to move for you and where even hunger got bored with you and went away. In between there were small ordinary pleasures like the way a ewe’s teat thickened under your pinched fingers when you checked for colostrum and found a decent line of it, like gold. There was the way the head of a lamb fit into your palm so perfectly that you wanted to punch art in its face. There were difficulties and failures. They lost one, a twin that had not decided to breathe in time, and Maggie wrapped it in a feed sack and set it by the door to keep the dogs later from considering it their business. She would think of it long after and then the thought would let go of her because you learned to hold grief the way you held everything else on the farm, as something that sat on your shoulder while you carried on.

Sometime after four, with the gas ring hissing and the sound of the wind going from confidant to companion, Sam leaned his head back against the bale stack and closed his eyes. His mouth slackened. He looked twelve and thirty. Maggie touched his boot with her own, a nudge to say only, I see you. He started awake as if someone had shaken him and pretended he hadn’t fallen asleep. He reached for a bottle and made that measured whistling noise he made with his lips when a lamb suckled, a sound he did not know he made.

There was a limit on what your body could do and then the list of things that mattered anyway did not care about the limit. Time herded itself. Three, four, then five, the hour that in ordinary life was birds and the first bins on the town street and bakers. Here it was snow still and the wind had gone down by an eighth. Maggie went to the door and cracked it and the wind shouldered its way in and stole her breath with simple robbery and then stood there like a boy who had kicked a ball into her yard and said what of it. She looked out across the yard at the drift that had formed along the tractor shed and it was a clean white shape like bones under a sheet. In the gable of the lambing shed, the little hole where the swallows came in the summer was a black coin. She said the thought she had not had yet out loud to it, as if swallows could hold a woman’s secrets for her. “We’re doing this,” she told the hole, and the absurdity of that sentence made it truer.

When she turned her back on the door she saw Sam had been watching her and trying not to be obvious. He had Hugh’s way of leaning one shoulder into a post when he was spent and needing not to admit it. “You cold?” she asked him unnecessarily.

He made a face at that. “You look like a snowman.”

She opened her mouth to say and then closed it. The thing rose then, the heavy thing that had sat like another layer of clothing, and because the shed was a confessional and the ewes had heard all versions of their keepers’ histories, she let it be a sentence. “I thought,” she said, and then had to wait a beat, because saying I thought, you needed to know what it was you thought. “I thought it would feel like he was just late. Like his boots were by the door and he’d walk in and say the thing he used to say—that same thing, every time, ‘any dramas?’—and I’d roll my eyes. But it doesn’t. It just feels like this.”

“Like what?” Sam said, but he said it gently, not as if he was asking for an answer but like he was asking for the rest of the breath.

“Like it’s ours,” she said after too long, and she had to look up at the roof when she said it so a ewe would not hear falsely what was not condemnation. “Like it was always going to be ours at some point and here it is. And I’d have put it off another ten years if I could have.”

He blew out his breath and looked at his gloves and at his hands in the gloves. “He always could get a lamb out when it was wrong,” he said, and his voice was full of something that was both pride and thieving. “I thought I never would.”

“You will,” she said. “You just did.”

He looked up, quick, like a boy seeing a skylark up out of a hedge. “That,” he said, with the edge of laughter under the word though there was nothing funny here. “I suppose.”

She wanted to say: I knew you would always want to go to sea when you were five and you wanted nothing but the stream by the lower field; I knew you would find your father’s keys and carry them in your pocket for days when he died and not say a word; I know you are not going to stay; I know if you do it will be because you cannot help but love what tears you. She wanted to say: I will not hold you with my grief. She did not say any of that because lambing sheds were honest places where you couldn’t lay down that kind of weight without it getting turned into something else in among the smell of lanolin and dung.

He had that faint off-stubble of the sixteen that he resented on his upper lip because it looked like a dirty mark when you didn’t shave it, but he refused to scrape at it because shaving would be to say he was a man. She wanted to put her hand on his cheek like she hadn’t for a decade. She wanted to put her head on his shoulder like she had never. She went instead and topped up the gas ring and noted with a calm that surprised her that the bottle would do until daylight.

There was a hard case then that would have been the kind you go to the phone for if the phone could bring you a vet and the road could bring you the vet’s little white van. A ewe with a big head single, a Texel cross, that cursed collision of terminal sire and experience, that hard left-hand right-hand think doing the geometry. The head was out blue-tongued and the legs were back and the tongue was swelling under the eye-lids like a heart. Maggie had two choices, both bad. She chose the set that did not involve cutting. She pressed the head back, which made the ewe bellow with a sound that came up from centuries, and she flattened her hand along the lamb’s cheekbone to push one shoulder in, just enough. It took ages that were minutes. When she got the loops on and the legs forward there was that split-second when the lamb did a gagging greasy inhaler-sounding half-breath that would have put a swear in the mouth of a priest. He came. He dropped and was unresponsive and went a colour that was a bad dream. She didn’t think about whether to stop. She swung him, hard, once, twice. You didn’t do it hard. You did it hard. She breathed into him. He gave no sign and then he jerked the tiniest of jerks, the size of a moth’s wing beating, and it was the thing between an ending and an opening. Sam was crying in a tight, snotless way beside her without knowing it. The ewe butted her elbow hard enough to leave the square bruise of a hinge on her skin. She told the ewe, with patience, to mind herself. The lamb lived and would have opinions every day of his life. She held him until his breath steadied and then set him in the wedge and stood up a different kind of tired.

She realized at some point that the wind had shifted. The notes of the building had gone from fear to something like a final verse. The red lamps surprised them both when they flicked and held, so that a soft womb-colour climbed back into the corners of the pens and everything became a story you had told yourself you had heard once. Heat brought its own smell back with it—singed feathers of the old lamp, the sweet iodine always present, a ghost of electricity itself you only noticed when it came. Sam lifted his face to the lamp as if he were a crocus seeking day.

“You smell like a dog,” he said, and she did, of wet and animal. He took a breath like laughter.

Outside, the storm was not over, but it had given up the idea of surprise and settled in to its work the way the sheep had. The yard would be a retold place later, hills newly thrown where no hills had been. The lower field would be a wrong shape. The road would be a hazard with a memory. They would be like that, too.

The last ewe in true labour went into her pen and blew and tightened and then delivered two small like apple halves, no trouble, and they were up in what Hugh would have called the time it takes to boil an egg, which was another of his sayings that had come with him from his father. Maggie wrote in the book the time and the numbers and the note live x 2 and wished there were a place in the margin for all that could not be written, the sighs and laughs and the swears and the way the light came and went like someone remembering you exist and then forgetting and then remembering again. She would write Blue stripe 54, twins, good mum. Green 142, one head-only, one breech, both ok. Black single tex, tough, alive. She would not write that all through this she had felt Hugh in the far pocket of her jacket like a heat you don’t know the source of; that she had not wanted to check whether the tin with his matches was empty or full because emptiness was a trick you could not afford to look straight at.

They would drink tea when they got into the kitchen, tepid from a flask because the power would take the kettle back for itself as if claiming wages. They would both fall asleep in chairs with straw in their socks and wake with that stupid pain in their necks that meant anything that did not fit into a pocket was overambitious. The dogs would step in and step out and haul the snow with them under their bellies. The day would climb in behind the storm with arrogant blue, as if it had never met a cloud, and the women in the village would look out of their windows at kindergarten times and at the feeds and at the way the smoke rose from the chimneys and think: lambing weather. The vet would ring later and say: all well? and Maggie would say as much as could be said—some goings-on, yes, but we’ve managed. Her mother would ring and say: your brother says the A68 is shut and Maggie would say: we have all we need.

Sam stood with his hands jammed up into his armpits under his jacket and watched the last-born try the teat with that hopeless first attempt and then the next that made it seem that one of them had learned a language since the earlier one. He shifted his stance, like his father used to when a lamb got on, a quiet pleased, a man regarding a straight wall. The heat lamps cast his face in red, and he looked like a boy covered in berries. He turned to Maggie without prompting and without asking anything worth words and she felt an old thing dislodge, an icicle off a ledge. She reached and wiped the smear from his cheek with the pad of her thumb and he flinched because he was sixteen and then stood because he was her son and because they had been up all night and there were lambs calling.

“Any dramas?” he said in a soft stupid voice to make her laugh, and it made a cheap ache rip her and soothe it both ways at once.

“Just the usual,” she said, which covered it and was not close.

They went around again then, the twice of it, the always of it, with the wet-straw slither under their boots and the red light haloing and the peppermint of crushed lamb-cud under everything, and outside the wind prised a little at the last drift and the long field lay lacking its corner of hedgerow but shining the way a page shines when you’ve written the past night into it and turned it and decided, without deciding, to keep going.
