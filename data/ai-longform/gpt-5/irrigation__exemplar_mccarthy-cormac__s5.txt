They were out before the sun came up full, when the world was still blue and colorless and each thing stood in its own place like it had declared and settled on being what it was and could never be otherwise. The orchard lay there under a broad sheet of sky with a haze like an uncoiled ribbon rising off the flats, the far mountains reduced to paper outlines like ruins of something failed and old and purposed down into rock again. The peach trees held their leaves like ragged flags. The leaves hung tired. The ground about them was buckled with salts, the earth crusted white where the water had run and run away and left its leavings. There were black drip hoses strung along the rows and buried PVC mainlines under the caliche. A hawk sat on the crossarm of a rotten powerpole and watched.

The older of the two brothers stood in the bed of the pickup with a coil of schedule forty and a tin coffee can full of glue and purple primer and a hacksaw and some couplings and he would call down and the other would hand up what he wanted and they set off across the furrowed lane between one row and the next. Their boots squinched in the powder. The air smelled like dust and old fruit and sunbaked plastic. The younger carried the shovel. His hat had gone shiny with sweat and grease. They both had the look of men who had once been boys in that place and were not long come to being older than their father had ever been.

Where do you reckon. The younger said.

Right where you said, the older said. You cant miss it. You could see the damp from the road yesterday.

He walked with the saw in his hand as if whatever it was they were to cut lay somewhere ahead and there was nothin for it but to go to it. They came to a place where the berm sank in and the peach limbs above had a richer green to them and the dust held dark. The ground was crusted with a salt like snow and a damp glistening rose up between clods and the ants there were traveling to and fro with purpose and in numbers no one had commanded or ever would.

The younger stood the shovel in and leaned on it and looked to the east. The low sun in his eyes sketched everything down to the smallest twig and thread. You think we should go on and call Dale, he said.

The older squinted. Dale aint comin out again for free. We can fix it.

It aint just the one break.

It never is.

He set down the coil of pipe and dropped from the truckbed and took the shovel from the younger and stepped off into the row and started to dig. The sound of the shovel in the dirt was a kind of chewing. He cut through roots like black ropes, old grass, tricklings. He exposed the pipe. It was a white PVC three inch with a hairline fissure down its spine so that where the pump had been on it had jetted like some white animal wounded and blind. He shut off the pump the afternoon before and gone home and rolled out drawings on the table and the papers had told him nothing and he had not slept much.

The younger leaned to watch. Wheres your saw.

Right here.

You want me to cut it.

Go on if you aim to.

The younger took the hacksaw and reached and sawed and the pipe gave up a gritty dust and a squeal and then it fell into his hand and he set it aside. A smell came out of the cut like old wetness, like a smell kept too long and let go of unwilling. He wiped his mouth with the back of his hand and then he looked down the row as if the rows had spoken and his face had something in it that had not been there when he come. The older saw it and did not say anything. He hopped the ditch and stood and looked too.

A black dog trotted along the ditch as if the ditch drawn there was the dog’s own invention and the dog a kind of plumb bob to show the world the true. It stopped and sniffed the damp and sneezed and shook itself and went on. The hawk flapped up and moved to another pole with little concern. A train horn far off in the flat came three times like a thing in pain.

They’d measured yesterday. They had walked and counted steps and stood over the capped risers, the tees and crosses like bone in the earth. The older had made a little mark on the map his father had made himself with a carpenter pencil and an old tea stain on one corner and the map had not matched nothing. The older’s hands were nicked and the sweat had got into the cuts and raised a sting. He had not spoken of the papers in the glovebox of his own truck under the tire gauge and the folded newspaper and the one letter with the lawyer’s letterhead yellow like butter it was so old and the corner chewed by mice. He had not spoken of the talk with the man in the short-sleeved button shirt who had come out in a rental and walked the fence lines with him making little drawings on a pad and then smiling and taking off his hat to wipe his head where the hair was thin and not much of it.

The younger dryfitted a coupling and stood and held out his hand and the older gave him the purple primer and he daubed it on and it ran down into the cut and stained everything like a wound you make for a doctor and then went to the glue and he turned the can and the glue made its little spider webs in the heat and he pushed the coupling on and gave it a twist and held and held and then let it go.

You want to talk about it, the younger said.

About what.

Come on.

The older squatted again and scraped at the dirt and broke it and pulled it under the pipe. About what is what I asked you.

What youre lookin at like you aint here. What you got under your hat.

The older reached up and touched his hat with the tips of two fingers as if to see if anything was under there. Well, he said. You want the truth as I have it or do you want to argue a point which you had already set your stake on before you come out here this mornin.

Say it.

They aint goin to have water for us come July. Not like we need. We are at the bottom of the sheet. They’re rationin and I talked to the board down there and the board man he told me and he said he was sorry like an undertaker. The pump is wore. The orchard is older than we are. The bank dont mind so much that theres trees. It minds that the trees aint money.

He said it and kneaded at the dirt and did not stand. The younger looked out over the orchard again as if lookin might make some new thing upon the horizon. He wiped his neck with a bandanna. He cut another piece and fitted it, the glue can open, the solvent hot in the air. The glue popped in bubbles and he held and counted. His mouth was set in a hard line like he had bit down on something too bitter to spit.

I aint sellin, he said.

It aint you alone to sell or not, the older said.

He stood then as if deciding to do so had been a fight and set his hands on his hips and looked down the row where the treeline ran up to the road and the road ran to the borrowing pits and the pits to the river which was not much of a river anymore but a ditch of consequence that could not be denied. You remember what he said, the younger said. You remember him standing out here sayin it to us.

He remembered. The old man with a straw hat like a hat from a painting, a man with his own wars, who had come from a time when men could get a thing done with a shovel and a length of pipe and a certain look in their eye that said they would be in that ground one day and not afraid of it one bit. He stood and he could see him as if he were yet alive and his hand in the air like blessing his sons and his trees alike. The older said nothing.

He said it aint the money. He said the money dont go to heaven. He said the water goes there, the younger said. He said the water is the shape of God in the world. He said you cant hold it. You can only keep room for it.

You want to lay up with a sermon or you want get this fixed. We aint got long fore this glue goes off and then we need to turn it on and see.

The younger said all right and they both bent to the pipe and they worked. A few flies had commenced in the heat and they worried the glue and then their wrists and then their eyes and the older flapped a wrist at them. A ground squirrel chirred like a toy no one wanted. From the dirt where it had been latched in a kind of crease a small rattler took its leave and slid like oil toward the shade and the younger saw it and stepped back and the shovel came up out of the earth in the older's hand and he set it down like a man setting down what he was tired of carrying and the snake’s head was no more a problem. He hooked it out and flung it into the berm and it lay there spilled like a coil of old hose and the tail buzzed yet, not long.

Look at you, the younger said.

You want to put it in a glass box, the older said. Maybe take it to town and show em. Maybe you can part your hair with it.

He set about filling. He the older. He scooped and tamped and neatened like a man in the business of burying whatever needed burying and would not argue about it. The younger watched the last tremor die and then turned and went for the pump.

They had a little engine house of sheet metal with a floor out of creek rock and a rude bench and a coffee jar with bolts and washers and cotterpins and pennies in the bottom and a picture of some saint tacked up with a roofing nail whose name no one knew, a woman in a blue cloak who looked out at nothing in particular and would be all right when nothing occurred, and in the corner a bag of cement gone to stone and took on cracks like the bed of some dried sea. The younger primed the pump. The priming cap was scabbed with hard water and he took a hammer and tapped it and then his bare hand on the aluminum shell where the machine had a fever in its skin and then he looked up and the older was there with the same look he’d come with and had not discarded.

You aint listened to me at all, the younger said.

I heard you.

You aint listened.

He filled the priming cup with a cut antifreeze jug and he set the cup and tightened and he pulled the choke and he kicked over the engine and it coughed. It coughed and went and the belts took up their slack and made their hush like having been starving and given bread at last. The pump thrummed. There came a little tremor back through the pipe and in a few seconds water stood at the break and then pressed through. A leak bead like sweat stood and then held and then did not and they looked at each other. The older gave a small nod no one could have seen but the young man saw it.

Let it fill and push, the older said.

They walked the rows while the pressure came. They walked with their hands at their sides like men in a graveyard whose kin lived there. They knelt and looked at emitters. They kicked a piece of old cloth covered in white. They came to the headgate on the old lateral where the concrete was spalled and the one tie wire was the only thing that held the board from going to the river. The headgate board had dates cut into it with a knife and the oldest date was not one would choose to put on any new thing. There was a swallow nest under the lip and the swallow’s heads watched with the eye of the damned. The younger spat. The older leaned to run his fingers in the trickle to see who it belonged to.

A pickup rolled by on the road above. It went slow and they did not look up but they knew who it was. After it had passed the younger said You took him down here already, didnt you.

I did.

You going to sell.

I already said what I aim to do.

What did he say.

He said the city will stretch its hand and take us anyway. He said I could do worse than sell while there was still a thing called a market. He said his boss is good to do business with. He said a lot. He had a gold watch and a shine on his shoes like he was fixin to be buried that way.

The younger leaned both hands on the headgate concrete and the grit went into his palms and he looked down into the run of water and watched it. He watched and something came over his face such that if his father had walked up right then he would have known that face for one he had known when the boy was not a boy and the man neither yet a man. The older looked away.

We could put in subsurface drip, the younger said. We could pull the older sets and just run five rows and keep the five that throw. We could pull the end trees and sell the wood and get enough to pay Ginny and the electric and the ditch and go to next spring.

We could, the older said. And when the river is a chalkline on a blackboard what then.

It aint goin to be that.

A crow croaked from the cottonwoods and the sound went over them and through them like it belonged and had made them what they were. The headgate leaked in a small regular way. The swallows spoke like coins in a tin. The motor at the pump house had a rhythm in it not unlike a heart and not unlike a thing too old to still be running. They went back.

When they came to the break there was a little jet the size of a wire singing in the heat and the younger set his thumb on it and it cut his skin and he moved his thumb and the jet sang again. He looked at the older and the older had a piece of cloth pulled from his pocket like a white flag and he smiled once with half his mouth and then did not smile anymore.

Theres always another leak, he said. He shut the pump and it died like a thing done wrong.

They worked again. The sun had climbed to that place where there was nothing beneath it that did not cower. Their shirts clung like wet paper. The glue made its little web and it had dried on the older’s fingers and his fingerprint now was a new thing no one could read. They cut and glued and held and they set back the earth and tamped with the blades of their hands and stood and removed their hats and wiped their hair with the backs of their arms and put their hats back on.

You want to go to town and get the ninety, the older said. I aint got another ninety. I got four half couplers and a forty five and a cap.

Ninety what.

Ninety elbow, the older said. He squinted. He made with his two hands a right angle so the younger would not think he meant some other number. Go to Shelbys. Tell him to put it on me.

We can do it with two forty fives, the younger said.

We aint got but one.

Then I’ll go. I was goin anyhow to get some more clamps.

He started off and the older called and he came back. You want the truck.

I’ll walk. It aint but down and up. You look like you ought to have the truck.

The older nodded. He studied the ground. When the younger went he went along between the rows as if the rows made roads and there was not to be any other road. The hawk had gone. The heat swelled and made a sheen over the ground like looking through tears. The older returned to the pump and opened the bleed and set the wrench to a bolt and fought it like it had set itself to be forever as and not to ever be undone. He gave to cursing that had no heat in it for the heat had took it.

The younger came to the highway and stood there with his hat in his hand and he looked down both ways and no one was coming and he walked across and climbed the bank and the gravel under his boots was that kind of gravel that rolls when you set on it and takes you to ground if you let it and he kept to his feet. There was a cotton picker out in the old Rudd field where there was nothing to pick and he was not working it but had come to look as if to say goodbye and see what he had not done. The younger passed the goats at the Gonzales place. They were all crowded under the one oak that had not done well and there was not shade enough for them all. Their eyes were the yellow of old beer. He waved out of habit to the old man there and the old man raised a hand like it weighed something. The younger went on. A little wind ran up the road and fell. The heat did not.

In town there was a cat asleep in the doorway of the cafe and the bank sign clicked off and on with no message. Shelby was leaned against his own counter with a magazine he never turned and the doorbell rang and he said Mornin without lookin up.

Mornin, the younger said.

Well dont you got better sense. He turned a page and set the magazine down. What you need.

Ninety. Three inch. And some clamps if you got them small little ones with a screw. Not the wormdrive but them little ones. I’ll know em if you show em.

Shelby looked at him. His face like old fruit leathery and not much left to it but to be what it was. He said I aint seen you in here in near a month.

Been busy.

Busy is good.

You goin to sell, Shelby said.

Who told you that.

Shelby lifted one shoulder. The way of the world is to talk. He stepped off behind the counter and went in the back and came out with one ninety thrown in with a few fittings like a man brought out biscuits and said take what you like. I’ll give you some glue. You want primer.

I got.

You dont want the purple, Shelby said. Folks come through be knowin you patched and that you aint good. He smiled.

We’re past caring if folks know we patched, the younger said.

Shelby nodded as if agreeing to something larger than had been said. I can put it on your brother. He come in Tuesday and got a check; you know that.

I know it.

You got any idea where you’re goin to be once they scrape that off out there.

The younger took the elbow and the clamps and put them in a paper sack. He looked at the floor and he looked at Shelby. Where was you when they scraped yours, he said.

Shelby made a little snort that might have been a laugh but it was not. Wherever the ground is good for men they will be and wherever it aint good for them they will be, he said. You learned that yet.

Not like I’m goin to.

Tell him to bring back my monkeywrench I lent him last year. I know the wrench by the way it sits on a nail.

I’ll tell him.

He paid and left and the bell bit the air as the door shut. He went slow past the barber where no heads were being cut and past the feed where the shelves had picked over spots like a man’s head and nothing grew there. He walked back in the heat and the road seemed longer going than it had coming and he saw the hawk again now riding a bubble of air over a cottonwood and then slipping away all casual like it had places to be and when he topped the bank he could see down into the orchard and could see the truck and the older there by the pump and he could see the shimmer of the sun enter all things and hide in them and there was something else he saw and he slowed to see what it was. In the lane by the orchard gate there sat a white car with the men in shirtsleeves and they were talking to his brother and when the younger got closer he saw the laughing way one of them threw up his hands like he was explaining to God a plan he knew was true and right and foregone and the older had his hat off in his hand like he was standing in a church and the younger stopped.

If youre goin to stand stand, one of the men said.

The younger walked down. He did not look the man in the eye. He looked at the dirt and at the calluses in his hands and at the water around the pump house making a sheen and then he looked up. What.

We were just sayin hello on our way back to the office, the man said. He had a shirt with thin blue stripes and a pocket full of pencils and a gold chain that had hair in it and the other one had a moustache and his car was not dusty.

We dont need to say much of anything, the younger said.

We’ll be back by anyhow, the man said. He put his hand to his mouth and pried something out of a tooth with his fingernail and flicked it on the ground as if he were planting something sacred. He smiled. He said You be good, to the older, like a man to a dog, and the older said nothing and watched the white car take up the road and go away in a fog of its own dust.

What did I tell you, the younger said.

He did not say it like a question and the older did not take it like one. He set his hat back on and turned and bent to open the pumphouse again and run his hand down the side of the warm motor like that might calm it and bring it round to a right mind. He said We aint obliged to do nothing to please them today. You got my elbow in that sack.

I got it. He handed it. You set with them Tuesday.

The older glanced at him and then went back to pulling the cap and daubing the threads with dope and resetting and he looked up again. You want to throw it, throw it, he said. I aint got time to throw it myself.

The younger took his hat off and looked into it like there was an answer in there and there was not. He put his hat on and he said You could have told me.

I could of, the older said. He worked the wrench and the crack of metal on metal was a sound that belonged to a world older than either of them and would be here when they were gone. He leaned back and he said We aint in business to surprise one another. You know that. If you knew what I knew you woulda done the same damned thing. He had a half laugh in his mouth and he swallowed it. He said We lost the top quarter to the frost three springs ago and that’s money we dont get back. We lost the bottom with the salts. The ditch come down twenty percent two years runnin. The tree borer is in it. I set traps and it dont matter. The man from the county with the little shiny badge he aint bring us no water and he aint bring us no money and he aint even bring us a story.

You aint got to sell the whole of it.

He set the wrench down very gentle and looked at the younger with a look that was an apology and it was not. The buyer dont buy in parts. They buy a shape out of the world and if it’s not the shape they want they go away and wait. And we go on and we go on and you come to me when you need fifty and then a hundred and then I go get it and it’s on me and then it aint on me it’s on our father’s name and my name and your name and it aint that I cant do it. It’s that I wont.

Then you wont, the younger said. He fired the pump. The younger walked out and onto the row and set his hand on an old peach limb leaned down to the earth from too much bearing other years and he leaned on it and the tree leaned under his weight and there was a sound like the sound that comes out a door that has stayed shut too long and the tree cracked and he let go as if it had bit him and stood there and said nothing. He stood with his hands open like a man just robbed. The older watched and did not go and he did not speak. The pump sang and there came a tremor and then under their boots the earth had a small speech to it as the water took to the pipe like a thing persuaded.

It came out of the repair as a sweat and then as a bead and then as a little so and they both watched and it did not go to a jet and the older let his breath go and in that same breath the younger said Lord, he said, in a small way like he was not sure the Lord had done anything yet but he wanted to make mention should He happen to be standing by.

They walked and looked and they spoke little. A few peaches hung like a drunk’s ear was hanging and you would not pay for them. The leaves of the trees rattled very softly of their own weight. The orchard floor run with ants. One of the trees had died by inches and what was left was a green rag hanging on a dead cross. Down the row the water had begun to show itself as a wet dark moving through the earth like a fugitive. The younger knelt and touched the earth and brought his fingers to his tongue. The taste was like pennies. He spat.

What are you going to do, the older said.

I am goin to go on and set siphons down on the west block. We got a hot day and a hot wind and they’ll be hurtin. I am goin to go on and do what needs done like always.

And when the papers come.

They wont come.

Dont fall to wishin when you could be figurin.

He said I am figurin. He stood. I am figurin that if the Lord set a river in this world then He did not set it for a man with a pencil to say it was his. That’s my figurin.

The older smiled without part of his eyes. He shook his head. He did not tell him about the watermaster that had come two summers back and locked the headgate with a bolt as bright as a tooth. He did not tell him how he had taken a saw and cut it and caught himself with his face in the ditch water after as if that water had done it. He did not tell him what he had done with the bolt and where it was.

They went to the west. The ditch there was an old earth ditch lined with cottontop and the sides caved where a boy had six times slid a bicycle down the bank and six times come up muddy and been whipped and six times done it again. The sideroller rig was parted in the grass and rusted and would never roll again. There were thirty aluminum pipes stacked behind the shed like a set of bone flutes no one could play. The younger took three and set them to his shoulder and the older took three also and they went and set them in the ditch and pulled them up and let the water run into the block and the smell of wet dirt rose and they both stood there a moment and they both breathed it as if it were something they had been commissioned for and then they moved on. The younger bent and drew water into the white siphon with his mouth and then lifted and set it over the bank and he could feel the water slide in it and the pull of it was a very old thing and good. He spat the grit and the black of ditch scum. He licked his lips and moved on. They set in ten. They watched the water lay in lines down through the trees and they moved through the rows like doctors in a ward of the ill where the ill are trees. The sun threw their shadows thin and sharp.

That afternoon the sky began to stack up to the west in towers smoky blue at the base and white like bone at the top and the high parts were lit by a light you could not see from where you stood. They could hear thunder in that pure high country way where it seems so far as to be another world being told of by a child. The leaves of the cottonwoods turned up their pale undersides and the world took on the smell of iron and desert and a remote green and there was a rising in their own skins like answering.

If it comes it will hail. The older said.

I’ll take it.

You take what you get.

They went back to the pump. They both were dusted and wet as if they had been pulled from the earth themselves and left someplace to dry. They stood with their hands on the hipbones that had outlasted boyhood. The younger looked at the older and said When did you tell them.

Last week.

You would of told me when.

When there was to be told.

He picked up a little stone and studied it and set it down. If you think I was wrong, the older said. Say it.

You were wrong.

Allright.

The younger took off up the row and he stopped and then went again and his walk went heavier and then let off and then heavier again. He came to the far end and there the ditch angled into the neighbor and he set his boot and turned with the whole of him and came back and when he reached his brother he swung at him with his open hand and it cracked like small thunder in that hot air and the older stood with his hands still at his sides and he did not raise them. A little blood come at once from the older’s lip and it hung and the older spit and the spit was red and he turned his head and looked out into the orchard and he looked back and there was not any hate in him that could be found and that was a terrible thing.

Are you done, he said.

No, the younger said, and he moved and the older stepped into him and took him as you take a man from a ledge and they fell together in the dust and rolled and the dust made a cloud around them no different than those that come up when cattle run and then it was over. They lay apart on their backs and looked into the high late sky and it was vast and empty and there were tears in the younger’s eyes and the older’s blood ran down into his ear and he brushed at it and the blood made his finger as red as if with paint. Neither of them said that they were sorry. It would have been a foolish thing like telling the river you wished it to be a road.

You remember when he set them fish, the older said. He had his hat off and his hair was black under the dust. The fish he made of old tin and he put em in the acequia and let em run.

I remember. The younger had his eyes closed. The sound of the pump was steady and the wind had come a little and he could feel it pass across his face and it was like a hand of something that wished them well and could not do for them what they could not do for themselves. He said He told us then. He said keep the ditch and the trees and the water and your own counsel. He told us before and after and it dont make no difference and here we are.

Here we are.

They rose and dusted and the dust made on them another layer that no one would ever remove. They went to the pump to listen and to adjust and to keep it right because there ain’t nothing more to be done when there is to be done something and you do it because it needs done. They ran a hand over the casing as if the pump had a body and a soul and wanted to be acknowledged of it. The smell that came with the first wind was of creosote and of wet rock and of that wild desert rain smell that can set any man to hope even if he had set his hopes down behind him and told himself to walk on.

The first drops came like small pebbles thrown by a child from far off and then there were many and they raised a dapple on the dust and made it dark and then the dapple became a sheet and the sheet a hard white and they stood and let it soak through their shirts and their hats and down behind their collars into the place on their backs where sweat had carved salt there and the rain cut that salt and it stung and they both laughed without knowing they had and then the hail came as the older had said and it came small first and then like marbles and then like dove’s eggs and it lay bouncing in the furrow and lay in the crotch of the trees and the leaves of the orchard were machinegunned and laid down and above them the towers above you could not even see for the white. They ran for the lean-to.

They went under the lean-to at the back of the pump shed and sat on a block. The saint in her paper blue cloak was wet and the water ran down the nail and fell from her like tears and fell on the bench there and made a dark place there and the whole of the world sounded like some drummer boy that had been told to beat for a war that had never ended. The younger turned his hat and poured the water out of it and set it back on. The older pressed his lip and wiped again at the blood and the blood now went pink and then clear. He did not look at the younger when he spoke. He said They’re askin this week. They have a date. I told them I’d tell them this week.

What if you dont.

Then they will find another shape. They will leave us in this one. We will be what we have been. We will go get money where there is no money. We will set the pump when we cant pay the light and we will pick what there is to pick. We will have that.

The younger leaned, elbows on his knees, hands together like praying and not praying. The hail was slackening and the hail that had fell lay about like some lost crop from a tree that had took to bearing ice. He watched a single hailstone melt in the palm of his hand and watched the water make a path down his lifeline and not give a damn for it. He closed his hand and it ran between his fingers and was gone. He said You got a pen.

The older laughed once and it was not a happy sound. He reached into his shirt pocket and brought out a pen gone soft and he handed it over.

What is it you reckon to sign.

You got paper.

I aint writin em here in the rain. You go on with your jest.

It aint no jest, the younger said. He bit the end of the pen and it left blue on his lips and he spat the blue and the rain took it into the earth. He looked at the saint and he looked at the motor and he looked at the rows. He said You give me a year. Not to buy the whole of it. I aint sayin I can perform miracles such as that. But to turn it. Five rows. Ten with mercy. To prove there’s a seed yet. Give me to next June. Then if it dont bear and it dont show, then you do what you got to. You wont hear another word from me.

The older watched the hail draw down into nothing and he watched the water run off the eaves in curtains and he watched the straps on the pump quiver with the running and he looked at the rows whose small green showed from beneath the wet and looked cleaner than any leaf could look and he did not think of the men in the white car and he did not think of Shelby nor the bank nor the board at the ditch nor the badge that had been at their gate. He thought of a boy and a second boy downstream of him and he thought of a man who had not returned once and now was returned only in the way of his sons. He pinched the bridge of his nose. He said A year dont make a man rich and it dont break him. You get your year. You get it all of it. And you dont take it as a mercy to be repaid. You take it as what we give each other. And you dont take to prayin that it dont rain hail because it will. You do your fox tricks and your hawk tricks and any dog tricks you got and we will see.

The younger lifted his face as if to let the rain have him. Thank you.

Dont thank me.

They sat yet some minutes while the weather did its will and the will slackened and then fell away and the storm gone east like most things that come to those parts and leave, and the sun came a little through the open place and it made a light on the edge of the block where the older had set his boot and it made a light on the saint’s paper cloak and it made a light upon the just-repaired line where the glue had filmed up and taken dust and was now itself a thing like bone in the ground.

They came out from under the shed and the orchard looked like a thing dowsed and woken. The hail had stripped the leaves some but not many and the peaches lay in little bruised drifts and they would not be sold but a man could stew them and he would if a man was what he ought. The air had gone cold and then was going warm again in the way of that place. The birds came down to take of whatever they take and to keep count of men that held to one square of the earth for longer than was normal or wise. They walked the rows with their heads down as if to miss a thing underfoot was a sin yea and their sin great, and the pump droned like a thing contented of its work and the west wind was mild and grief was far and pegged out for them in that hour like a tent not under their hands.

The older turned the wheel on the bleed and listened and closed it and took his hand off and looked at his hand as if to see if it had taken anything from the world that were not his. He said I told em Friday. I’ll tell em different.

Tell em what you told me.

I will.

They worked again until the light went low and gold and the shadows took their own substance and turned black and went from men to trees as if looking for company. They tied up a limb with baler twine and the knot the older made was one would last until a man cut it and no wind would ever come into that knot and say I’m here. The younger righted a pipe in the ditch and it took and he smiled. He gathered peaches in his shirt hem like some boy steals eggs and he carried them to the truck and poured them on the seat and they rolled there and one fell to the floor and lay and he laughed. The older took one and bit it and the hail had bruised it and the juice went down his chin and into his beard and into the little cut into his lip and he inhaled sharp but he did not cry out. The taste was sun and water and earth and work and the tree and a little of God and they ate standing there with the truck door open and them in the door like men in a theater but the show was theirs.

When it was near dark they shut down the pump. The sound of the world filled in the place the pump had been. Crickets and frogs where there was water enough for frogs to consider their lives and the far cars on the highway made a long iron herdsong and the owls spoke and were not answered. They got into the truck and sat but did not go. The older turned off the lights and they sat in the near heat and the smells of peach and dust and glue and gasoline and wet iron and one another and their father hung about them and would not go and would not be driven.

You come in the house, the older said.

The younger said I got to go up to the west and raise the pipes. They’ll siphon empty and suck. I’ll come after.

I’ll leave the porch on.

You do that.

The older studied the wheel and then he put his hand on the younger’s shoulder and he did not grip it nor squeeze it. He put it there and left it and then he took it away. The younger stepped down and shut the door and the truck moved slightly under him and then settled like a horse will. He looked back. The older lit a cigarette and the tip burned like a thing that does not belong in the dark but believes in it all the same. He backed down slow and turned and the dust took the back of the orchard and bowed itself about the truck and then thinned and then there were only the tail lights disappearing like a pair of embers carried away on some wind no one can call back.

The younger went on. He went along the row, he went where the water ran and made little tarried tongues under the leaves, he righted what was down and he set what was to be set and he bent beneath a limb and felt the leaves brush his neck like the hand of some living thing that had pardoned him. He stood a while. He stood and looked at the mountains with no names he knew for them, mountains that had been there when all the water in all the rivers had taken their first lesson in going, and he watched the lightning flicker behind the last of the storm and he thought that a man lives by the names he gives to things and that when he is left without names he is left with nothing he can speak and therefore nothing he can keep. He said the names under his breath like a prayer or a curse. Peach and pipe and pump and ditch and stone and gate. Brother. Father. Water.

When he went down to the west block and put his hand to the first pipe to raise it out and pull it back up on the bank he could feel the pull of the water like a thing alive. He leaned and fought it and it came and he laid it and then he took the next and the next and the next and the world had grown dark enough that his hands he could no longer see and he worked by how the pipe felt in his fingers and by the sound of the water in the ditch and by the scent of the trees and the dust and of the hail melting to earth. Above him the sky turned over like some great beast in sleep. He worked on until he had done what he had said and then he straightened and his back spoke to him in the old tongue and he answered and took it and then he started up the road toward the house where a porch light had come on as it had nights before and would nights after and whether it was there or not a man could see his way because men have lit such lights for one another and will when all other lights have been taken from them.

At the gate he stopped and looked back at what the day had done to them. The orchard lay cool and black and wet and the rows went up dark into the dark and he thought he heard a little trickling yet in some piece of pipe which had not bled clear and he thought he could catch the shape of his father walking there with his hands behind his back and his head down counting trees or anything but he knew that to see a man who is gone is to put something in the world that is not there and he did not wish to be that man though he would be that man and more before he was done. The dog made some small noise at his coming and he put his hand down and the dog put his head into it and that was all they said of it and he went on up.

The older was at the sink and had his hands under the tap and the water ran clear until it ran color and made a little muddy cloud in the steel like a storm in some other state and it went away and the water ran clear again and he turned it off and dried his hands on a towel that had been a shirt and he looked up when the younger came in and his mouth made a line and he said We’ll get up and do that again tomorra.

The younger nodded. He took the chair with the slat broken out of its back and sat and the old floor of the house creaked beneath him as if it were alive and would have spoke if it had language and he looked out past his brother’s shoulder at the square of the dark where the porch light made its small haven and the dark beyond like that animal that waits where men go when they are done being boys and he said nothing because there was not to be said any thing that would make it more right than it was and maybe some quiet is better than some words anyway, and the night lay about them and the orchard lay and the river far off flowed such as it could and the world was asked to go on and it did.
