It begins, most nights, with a sound the city sleeps through. Not the siren that threads its way, not the train that throws its low muscle under the river, but the hush and clink in the switchboard room when all the cords are hung and we are waiting like birds on a line, backs sloped, headsets pinning us to our places. There is a lapping at the edge of your hearing you learn to call current. There is the mutter of keys from the girls two positions down, the sigh of the supervisor’s nylons crossing and uncrossing, the thin radio at the far end where someone has put on a station that plays only strings and woodwinds and commercials for cigarettes with names that sound like air.

Light is its own town up here. The little glass lamps in neat rows, each one a house that goes warm all at once as a call comes in. You only see so much of a life through a window. You get the breath and the hesitation and the money shuffled into this thing you are helping to happen. We were taught to look at the board like a battlefield—don’t laugh, Miss Hennessey really called it that, pointing with her pencil. “This is the line of attack. If your left cord is already engaged, you shift your focus to the trunk. Never let a lamp glow more than three rings. Listen. Anticipate. You are the bridge.”

I liked the idea of it, at the beginning. Bridge. It made a quality of mercy out of doing a job. And you needed to put something bright down in your mind, because the dull part of it, the ache where the band of the headset sat along your hairline, the way your ears would go hot by midnight, the hours spent saying the same decencies—Number, please. Long Distance. Person-to-person? Yes, sir, hold while I try—could make you forget the nerve of the thing you were living inside: voices harnessed and sent, dear words running under streets and up poles and across creeks, distance spooled in your hands.

My position was the third from the window. From here I could see the backs of people on the sidewalk when I worked an early shift and the picture of a man’s head in the barber shop right across from the exchange, stopped mid-snip by the plate glass. Mostly, though, things were just sound and light. In '68, Long Distance was a room on the top floor. By then we were told we were only a few years off from everything being done on dials and machines, and some of the older ladies would purse their mouths and say, I’ll be dead before I’m obsolete, and other nights they’d say nothing at all. There were machines already, some of the work handing itself off. I tried not to think about it. I liked the cords and the plugs and the little quick bloom a lamp made when it asked for you. It kept your hands in it. It was something real in a sea of voice.

I measure the year by the headlines that came like pigeons to the lunch table downstairs. In April, it was Dr. King, and the way the other girls’ voices lined up and fell out like dominos—oh my God, no, oh no, someone get me a cigarette. One of the night girls, Leta, flat-out cried with her whole body, so that her green plastic fork fell to the floor and we all watched it hit and spring once, stupidly bright. In June there was Bobby. In August, the convention on a television I didn’t own and didn’t need to, because the voices reached us anyway in their ways: men calling men to ask if they’d heard, if the march was still on, if the bus was full. A boy to his mother: “Ma, don’t you watch, okay? Don’t have the TV on. We’re fine.” And the little stillness afterward, while she said back the handful of things mothers say when their sons walk into a crowd. I could pull the cords and drop them and write down the time and charges and know exactly what I was doing and still feel that I was balancing their speech on my palms like water.

Tuesdays at nine were the ones I looked for without admitting that’s what I was doing. People are patterns if you do this work long enough. Nurses get twenty minutes off their feet and the world goes into them the way soup goes into a body. Miners call on a Sunday if the shift rotation has been merciful. Boys about to be drafted call at bad hours with a practiced lightness and words they mean to be brave, salty little jokes that wear the length of your day out with their seams. A man in Wisconsin places person-to-person to “Mr. Christ.” A woman in Maine orders a cake over the line, three layers, no coconut, yes, we’ll be up Saturday for Aunt Hattie’s. But Tuesdays at nine I came to expect a certain pair as you do the sound of the pest control man’s truck on a Thursday afternoon or the scrape of the postman’s bag.

He’d be the one to ring in—sometimes from a pay phone, if there was wind in the speaking of his voice, or in a hallway with tile that echoed like a train station. “Long Distance,” I’d say. “Operator,” he’d reply, always in a voice like when you tilt a pitcher and the milk doesn’t quite leave it yet. Hold in it, a kind of lean. “I’d like to place a person-to-person call to Miss Marian Lowry, Chicago, Illinois. Michigan 4-9403.” The first time, he had taken care saying it, and I’d repeated it back to him. “MARy-an?” I said, because names are a place you either step wrong or step into. “Mare,” he corrected, in that way it is to correct a stranger while holding something careful. “We call her Mare. Marian Lowry.”

“And your name?” I asked, turning his number over into place with the end of my pencil, listening to the click and thrum of the line I was drawing toward hers.

“Jack,” he said, and if I have ever liked the way a name sounded, I liked that one. Solid as porch wood when said by him. “Jack Ross.”

They were not my mother’s age and not my age either, not the young ones with their urgent swiftness who ring in for a practical and fling off again as if they’ve taken a coat, not the steady middle ones who fall into talk like a black coffee and stay until the operator checks in with time and charges and they recollect themselves with a laugh. They had a way of making their talking a room, and they entered it the same way, week after week. “Hi, you,” Marian said when I had her on the line that first time. “Hi there,” Jack said, a gladness he didn’t bother hiding. You can hear a smile the way you can smell rain before it falls.

The first Tuesday was early March, and by the time I had picked them out from the other voices, we already knew the snow would not be leaving this year the way it was respecting the rules of past years. There were piles of it still black at the curb, little icebergs where someone had backed through and left the shape of their bumper. The telephone poles wore it in crusts, and sometimes at lunch I would sit in the window because it was warm there and watch it fall off with a thunk. “There’s still a drift higher than my boots,” Marian said to Jack that first night, and I told myself I had to be on the line anyway. I had to listen for the “There you are, sir” moment that tells your pencil when to come down and start noting time and charges. She works on the seventh floor, I thought, with sensible shoes, if her drifts are that bad. “I’ve got salt marks up to my knee.” Jack laughed, surprised and warm. “You walk a lot?” he said. “What a thing to ask,” she replied, even and bright. “I don’t know what I do. I get from one thing to the next like the little man in an animation with stairs. I look up and it’s a week again.”

“Then I am the next stair,” he said, and I watched my own hand write down the time like I wasn’t the one making it.

Some weeks were steady like that. There’s a charm to their kind of call, person-to-person, because you are an usher. You go in, you go up an aisle, you find the reservation. “Mr. Ross calling for Miss Lowry.” People rarely call one another by full names unless they are in trouble or in love. They called each other both. “You are in trouble,” she would say some weeks. “You didn’t call me back on Sunday when you said you would, and I held my dinner and then ate it cold.” “I’m sorry, Mare,” he would say. You can practically hear a man remove his cap, no matter if he’s wearing one. “First shift went long and then I meant to only put my head down for five minutes.” Other weeks he’d be the one with the tartness, which suits men if they’ve a sweetness, too. “You said you’d take your lunch in the sun and you better have. Did you?” She would say if she did. She didn’t lie for sport. Once I heard her say, “I forgot I had a body,” in that Oh no way women sometimes tell on themselves because they hope it makes it better. “It seemed like it belonged to somebody else,” she said, and to be honest, that was maybe the first time I thought of her in a way that wasn’t just a line connecting. You sit in one of these positions long enough and the currents make little patterns in the air, you will swear to it. Sometimes, if you rest the heel of your hand just above the wood, you can feel where the warmth ends, the way a cat finds where the sun was.

There are rules about listening on lines. There are reasons you must. Supervision, they call it. We were trained to connect and drop out, then to drop in at intervals to know when to end the time and read out the cost. You cannot hear much this way—snatches. The flap as if a hand had shooed at a fly. Laughter, brief, without its preamble and reply. “Hold on—no, that one was for the egg man. I’ll be right back.” And the hymn of the house—or the factory floor, or the diner, or the barracks—underneath it. Still, there are some voices that sound like they live in a certain kind of air, and Jack’s was a room with a lathe in it. Even when he called from a booth with glass walls I could hear the factory on him somehow, which is maybe not the same thing as hearing it on the line. There was a clean grease to him. Marian, on the other hand, had a window nearby and talked in front of it. I’d have told you her light changed. Not every week, but season to season. “The trees are making me sneeze,” she complained in May. “Bless you,” he said, quick and instinctive. “What kind are they?” She asked someone else—I heard it, faint, another woman in her life you want to be her sister if you like the sound of her enough, “Maeve, what are these trees?” and in the background someone answered, “Honey locust,” so quiet you could have thought it part of the draft in the room. Or maybe I’m the one who made it a tree and not the sharpening of a pencil. Maybe I am the one who names a woman’s laugh and makes it belong to a person whose collar I can see from my chair on the top floor. I told myself that sometimes, to be decent to my own mind. “You are making a life out of traces,” I’d say, taking a cigarette even though I’d quit in January and fallen off the wagon three times since then. “Clara, for God’s sake, you are not their aunt.”

I wanted that. To be an aunt in an old Greek play with a chorus. I think you could spend your whole life wanting that, and then you look up and it’s half over. I was thirty-four that year and I lived with Mrs. Keller in a woman’s house near the Exchange. I rented a small room under the dormer and had a dresser with a mirror in which I looked like a person in a film when I brushed my hair. That is to say: a bit set off, a bit obscure, with the weight of the glass between me and my own sight. The house was kept good. Mrs. Keller turned her mattress every even month and made us leave our tennis shoes in the back hall and at Easter she put out an egg she had glued pearls on. She grew geraniums in coffee cans. Sometimes I felt as if I were renting decency from her by the week, as plain and regular as a loaf.

My parents had left me the town and their quiet. My father took his hearing with him when he went; he’d been a foreman at the railyard and told me, whenever he got grand about his own pain, that it was the great squeal of steel which had set him apart for good. “When a man’s ear gives up,” he said, shameless, half pleased, half sorry, “he is convinced you are not saying anything worth hearing anyway.” I tried this idea on the board, sometimes. I wondered if the machine would be glad to do away with us or if it would listen in the way a dog does, with its whole head. I did not see what would become of girls like me when the board went, what job would keep our good hands busy and our mouths polite.

I did not put those thoughts in Jack and Marian’s possession. They had their own things. He worked a job I came to think of as the plant even if I never heard him say it. There was a rhythm to his voice like the open and shut of a press. He would come on Tuesdays smelling of metal I could not actually smell, and I had to stop myself from wiping my hand against my blouse after I unplugged, as if I had touched rust. He rented a rooming house with a woman whose name was Elsie or Alma—no, not me; I am not in their room—and I only knew it because once he apologized for the sound of a radio in the background and I held myself in time to write down “Tin Pan” when one of the other women on the floor waved me for a cigarette and said, “You’re floating, Red. Are you pacing? Are you in a meadow?” I did not think of him as handsome. I thought of him as someone whose winter hat made his hair sweet. I suspected he mowed someone’s small yard.

Marian belonged to a file folder and a rubber stamp, the thick groove in a desk where a right hand goes again and again, the life that makes a woman decide how low she can wear her heels and still arrive home with some feeling in her feet. Once she told Jack about a girl who was training under her—“She will not stop chewing on her pen,” she said, and the way she said it made me think of all the women I have ever had to tolerate without slapping their mouths for small things, all the ways we do not harm one another for the sake of the machine. “She thinks if she chews, the thought will come. It’s like having a calf at my desk.” Jack laughed so she could feel it. “Maybe she’s in love,” he said, which is what men say when a woman is impractical. “Then God help the poor boy,” Marian replied; I could hear the woman chewing on the pen whether she was in the room or not, and I felt kindly toward her anyway.

For a while it felt like they were armor against the headlines. A thing the line would hand me that I could bear, two cups at the ends of a string, and the string running through my fingers. It embarrasses me to say it. I have handled calls from boys who wanted to call their fathers and had drunk too much to dial the number. Men whose breath came in with tobacco and the fear of being sent. “I just think,” one of them said to me once as if it were a new thought, as if thinking had only just been invented and he had got the first of it, “that if a man doesn’t want to go, he shouldn’t have to.” What am I supposed to do with that? I am a woman whose job makes a geography of other people’s sadness and asks me to write it in little figures to turn over in a drawer. You develop small devotions. You like the crane operator who calls on his break with his palm against his cheek. You like the way the woman in Tucson answers with “Hello, desert.” You like the Tuesday people.

He said he had known her “since paper lunch sacks.” I do not know what that means. I wrote it down on a slip and found it later the way you might find a pebble in your pocket and not recall where you bent to pick it up. “Since paper lunch sacks and string around our calves in the spring when the stickers were bad.” He had changed in her mouth. I heard him gather himself on one of the weeks after King’s murder; there was a weariness to the voices that called in that month the way there is blue to a bruise. It made people slow and hackled and polite to death. He said, “You saw in the paper,” and she said, “Yes,” and he said, “I told my mother there are a hundred ways for a country to break a man and none of them are original, and she said don’t be smart.” Marian said, “Well, don’t be,” and he laughed, but it was one of those laughs you give a person you love when you could knock their head for them and then kiss the place above their eyebrow. I had not known I could hear that through a copper line. Sometimes, when people talk, you forget how it gets to you. My God, there are wires in the sea. When I was a girl, my father told me that and I did not believe him, and then I worked long enough at a position and saw pictures of it—cables under water like the bones of an animal—and thought, What we will do to not be alone.

I do not know what their history was. Once, when I dropped down into the call after a mess of clicks from the far end where Chicago had hardly been buckled to the board, he was telling her about the time he saw her at a football game, which might have been the first time, or the time he thought of as the moment his life snapped into its line. “You had on that green coat with the buttons that didn’t match,” he said, pleased with the person he had been in the story, the young man who’d had the presence to notice a thing about an unsteady coat. “I thought you were a tree then,” he added. “I thought you were a little tree walking up the bleachers.” “I would have hated that,” she said, and her voice went soft under it. “I didn’t hate it,” he said, and the way he said it made me set my pencil down for a second. Something in that one line gave me more about a life than I can bear to know, because what business is it of mine, for example, to know whether they had ever kissed at the gymnasium door? Whether she had married the boy she thought she loved when she was nineteen and then had to turn into a woman with a life that would suddenly be either a good one or a bad one in a way that would seem to her like the work of God. Whether he had gone into the service and come back to a town that did not line up with itself anymore, the river moved six inches to the left and all the people with it.

I did not ask. We do not have a name for the person I am in their story. I am something like the woman at the counter who suggests a fresh cup of coffee when a person looks like he has been knocked down by news and is a little bloody under it. I sit no inches from the room. On the worst nights, I am a finger in the seam of the door, holding it, the breath of air that comes under. It made me greedy for them. I would look up at the clock on Tuesdays and move my break as if I were rearranging the furniture in a rented room, anticipating where I would stand to get the most light, the least glare. I did not admit that this was what I was doing even to myself. I would tell myself, Your legs are tired; have your coffee early, and then I would be in place with a fresh notepad when his light came in and I would arrange the board to make them room.

You cannot know a week has a break in it until you hit it. There are no omens. The week in which the call did not go on the way it had goes on until it doesn’t any longer, and then your hand stops halfway to the trunk and you think there must have been a click you missed, a small slick of static that made you drop the wrong cord into the wrong place. There had been a man at the pay phone down the street from my boardinghouse the night before, talking and talk-talking with his whole head thrown back as if the phone were his sweetheart’s throat. He had said the word “baby” in such a way that it did not mean anything like a person who can’t walk. I even stopped to button my coat to listen, if I am confessing. When I got to my shift Tuesday I was pleased with the feeling I had that voices were animal and close and full of breath. It was nine before nine, the way it is when you are holding a place in your mind for how the minutes will be.

I could have drawn the line on the wood where his lamp would be. It lit right there. “Long Distance,” I said. “Operator,” he said, quiet. It was the quiet that put me off. Even in a booth men have a way of throwing their voice as if it has dimensions they might fill. He did not. He spoke like someone who had been put in a room with walls close to him and was careful not to touch them. You must understand I have answered men who have been told something and have tried not to know it. I have heard the difference in a thousand “Hello?”s. It isn’t always death. News breaks us in dull ways more often than it kills us. But I tightened my hand in my lap and put my pencil down and turned to the place I would connect him and said, “How may I help you?”

He gave the number the same as he always did. He did not say her name.

I called it. If you are lucky you never know the sound of a phone ringing without an answer that someone on one end of the line is trying to make into a pattern they can live with. You tell yourself, two rings is fine—people take time. Four rings is not an insult. Seven rings and you are the person with your hand on a door and a little voice asking if maybe you got off on the wrong floor. I counted. On six I glanced at Mrs. Hennessey at her desk for no reason other than the way you look around a room when the thing you are hearing is happening inside you and you want to make sure your body is still real and visible to other people. On eight I broke. “I’m not getting an answer,” I said, plain. There is a rule that you say it like that. You don’t say she isn’t answering. You do not put the verb on the person. “I’ll let it go a few more if you like.” There are reasons we cannot let it ring for an hour. He said, “Please,” in a voice that went through my headset into my inner ear like a pipe has burst. If you have stood up from a chair and the muscle in the back of your thigh lets go in a way it isn’t supposed to, you will know something of the physiology of such a sound. I don’t want to be fancy. That was just how it was. The sixth through the twelfth rings, if anyone cares, are for a person you love. The thirteenth through the eighteenth are for the emptiness that starts to open up like a sinkhole in the middle of the street. On the twentieth my supervisor would have come to knock me on the back of the head if she touched bodies at all. I told myself to move my hand. I pulled my cord. “I can try the number again,” I said. “I can try later.” I wanted to say her name so that we could both hear it. “Miss Lowry,” I told him, “is not at her telephone. I can try to ring the line every ten minutes for half an hour, and if she answers I can connect you.”

“No,” he said, and hesitated. “No. Thank you, Operator. No.” He did not hang up. The line stayed; there is a way a line lives in a man’s chest like it has grafted itself on. It keeps, I don’t know, a hum, a small pulse. I said, “Is there something else I can try? I have the number as—” and read it back so that he could catch me if I had made it wrong the way a man will sometimes let you mispronounce his name ten times and then finally be too tired to accept the eleventh, as if it were now an injury. “No,” he said again. “It’s right.”

There are things I am not supposed to say. The weight of them could drop the building. I said one of them. “Are you all right?” It came out like a cough that is also a word. I have written the codes for how much time and how much money a person pours into the wire. There is no code for “You are a woman whose hand is on a piece of someone else’s breath and you feel the hinge in it break.” He took so long to answer me that I thought he had hung up without the courtesy of a click. “No,” he said at last. “No, Miss. My mother would say that I am not all right. My mother is dead. I don’t know whose mother that makes me.”

I did not understand that sentence for a second, and it felt like I was falling into a place under the words, like when your foot goes to the next stair and your body keeps going: the way he had used the word mother twice, and the way he had extended its shape to try to shelter himself under it. I do not know why I asked what I did except that it was the only question you can ask a person when it is nine at night and you are a voice on an internal line. “Do you have someone,” I said, “physically with you?” I wanted to say, “Is someone breathing in your room?” I learned that in this job. Sometimes a person needs another person’s breath.

“No,” he said. “No.” He sounded at once very far away and very much in my ear. The trick of the thing: how your mind insists on making a topography out of a voice when what you have is conduction. “Don’t be worried,” he added, as if he were making conversation over coffee. “I won’t do anything to myself.” The hum went all the way through my head and out the other side. The lost thing in the line made a noise you can only hear if you have sat with your hand cupping a plug for five years, and I could hear it. I did not know where she was, his Tuesday. It is arrogant to assume one person can bear the other’s weekly talk and then stand you up and you are owed an answer. Maybe she was in a cab. She could have been behind a door from which she would not come that night. Or maybe the room she worked in had stopped being a room and turned into a tunnel. People that winter and spring turned into and out of citizens and ghosts. If you have ever sat on a bus full of women at ten in the evening you have heard the sound of missing made over into a story. You tell yourself the story repeats in a way you can live with. He had come to tell her something, I realized. The way his voice had come out with the ticket on it. The way a person looks in the lobby of the hospital just before the orderly wheels out the bed.

“Is there a neighbor with a phone?” I asked, and I could hear Mrs. Hennessey in the way I said it, practical in the face of the cloud you were trying to push, her short nails applying themselves to a problem like a paperclip. “We can try next door. We can try the office if you have it. The doorman.”

“There’s no doorman,” he said. He’d only said it once, I think, in any one night, but in my head the way he said “No” had started to pick up a weight. One of the girls had brought in a bag of oranges that week, her aunt had sent them from Florida—they were pale and the skin stuck to your fingers and left you with a smell you could not wash off even with scalding water at the sink in the ladies’—and I had cut one in half and left it sitting open on the shelf below the board so that the shards of acid would keep me awake. I wanted to pick it up and hold it under my nose and not say any more dangerous sentences. I wanted to touch my own head. He must have heard me draw a breath. He said, in that formal way people on the edge can pull together when they think of their better natures, “Thank you for your help, Operator,” which surprised me. Here is a thing I will always know and never say to anyone: kindness wants a folder, too. You want to tuck it somewhere you can find it, and only we have the folders. “Sir,” I said. I meant a lot of things. I meant, I am sorry. I meant, Please call another number. I meant, It is a thing I have liked to listen to you be alive on a Tuesday. He hung up before I made any of those meanings into a word.

If you stop working on a line you miss five calls you will call back later. You are not allowed to mourn a stranger in real time. I turned to the next lamp, which had boiled merrily at my elbow and gone dull and boiled again while I had been in there with Jack Ross and all the things I had no place to put. “Long Distance,” I said, and if the man on the other end had asked me if I were all right, I could have told him the truth and been fired inside of a minute. As it was, he wanted to place a call to a hotel in Reno where he believed his wife to be staying, and that was ordinary enough to let me get my body back into its shape. “Ma’am,” came the voice from the other end when I rang the hotel, “if he’s got something to say to her he can say it into my ear. She is in this room with me,” and there is a tiny stage to those. I have developed a credo about them. You will not rest easier for having taken sides from your unfortunate chair.

Marian did not call in that night. It would be clean to say she had. It would be easy to make her the person who called at nine-thirty with her voice like a little packet tied with string, saying, “Jack?” and the quiet where he did not answer, and me in the chair with the little clatter and hush my position makes when I push the plug in, holding that quiet for a moment before I find a way to let her down. All right then, I am not the person who makes stories neat. She did not ring into my board. I could not go home with that and keep it off my pillow like hairpins.

There are ways to make a call that are not strictly policy. A woman who is a bridge, who has spent years with weight going through her, learns where the give is in the cables and how to lie down on them. I had her number. It had lived at the edge of my hands since March. I had never imagined calling it. The line can make people into a place and I had always left myself the space of the money between us. It was not nine in Chicago when you took the hour into account. Everyone’s eight is someone else’s seven; the world is shocking like that when you first let it sink in, how your breakfast is another woman’s lunch.

It was eleven-thirty here and my floor was mostly emptier than it had the right to be and the shift had spun down to the men who had not found a bed for the night and the women who worked sour hours because someone else’s money was in their hands. I waited until Mrs. Hennessey left her desk for the ladies’ and then I did not think. I left my cord resting like a piece of hair and I drew the other cord out as if my fingers had done it for me and I pulled that place through. It rang. The ring of a phone you’re not supposed to call somebody on is a different sound even if the pitch is the same. Years from now, if I am lucky enough to be a woman who says stupid things at a barbecue to the children her friends have had, it will still be inside my ear.

One. Two. Three. I practiced saying, “I am an operator with—” and then stopping. What could follow that that would not undo my whole life. It does something to a person to live by a rule for a long time. The fourth ring reached into a room in another city where there were probably a glass with lipstick on it and a shut-up typewriter and a coat thrown onto a chair. On five, someone lifted.

She said hello like a person who had not been there waiting; like a person whose life had happened at its usual speed and who was making a shape with her mouth to put it all into the thing we send down a line. It is a skill. She said it like a door opening in a house you have never been in and still imagined. “Hello?” she said, and I did not know until the sound hit me how much I had put into knowing that hello. All the things I had conjured—she wore green dresses only on Fridays, she never left a dish in the sink—and there it was, the main thing, so straightforward I felt foolish for feeling my heart move. “Hello, Miss Lowry,” I said, and my own voice had become my mother’s hands making a precise shape with pastry. “The operator?” she said. She is not an unkind woman. Even so, the way she said it took me into a little cold place in myself because I knew at once what it would be like to be a strange woman to her, uninvited in her room. “Yes,” I said. “I am very sorry to intrude at this hour.” I had gotten as far as I could go by rote. Now we were in the snow, the place with no ruts, and I was the one who had to put my foot down. “Mr. Ross—” I said, and the way she took his name from the air and put it to her mouth made me think of the way some people say the blessing out loud before they eat and some in their own heads. I stopped. The words were making me. “He called,” I said. “He tried to reach you. I thought—” The lies lined up like goats to be milked. What could I do with them. “I thought you would want to know,” I said. The only true thing I could have told her.

She did not say anything for a medium-long time. The hesitations are all you have, in this. They are better than faces. “Thank you, Operator,” she said at last. “I was here. I fell asleep.” She said it like an apology to a person she had promised a life to. That was when I understood that sleep is part of what happens to you and not something you do. The room I was in—the humming one, with other women’s backs to me, all of us hemmed by cord—blurred outward. It is a dreadful thing to feel your job run out on you because the work at hand is human. “I will try his number now,” she said. And then, as if that were not enough, as if there had to be a kind of grace in the system, a thing your whole life might be for even if no one would ever be able to prove it—the line clicked in a way that says I am still here and not here, and her breath came into mine, just briefly, and she said as if to a friend, “Do you have to sit there all night, dear?” I had been a dear to a thousand women, in passing, in a shop, in a church basement. Her dear found a soft inside place in me and set it down in there, unafraid it would be broken. “Until midnight,” I said, very carefully. In front of me, Mrs. Hennessey came back and eased herself into her chair and I could have been a pile of wire. “Then go home,” Marian said. “Do not talk to the first man who sits too close to you on the bus,” and she hung up with a cleanness that could cut cloth. I believe she dialed him at once. I like to picture that even though I know that life is not generous with the things we want.

When midnight came, I stood too quickly and the world did its little jump around me and then settled like it does when you take a train for the first time and get used to its sway. The other girls swore and told jokes and fluffed hair and did the things women do when they are leaving a place where they have behaved very well. Out on the street the dark put its coat over the buildings and the cats had come out to do their rounds. I walked home by the long way, as if time might be wide enough to change what had happened if I gave it ten extra minutes. The pay phone near Mrs. Keller’s was empty. The metal door of the barber shop had graffiti on it I had noticed every night of my life since I had moved in there, and this was the first time I thought to think of the hand that had made it, what it had felt like to go a few inches up and draw a line through the M so it turned into another letter entirely.

I put my head on the pillow and did not think about anything. When I woke it was grey and I knew I would be asked to go back and do all of the same duties with my hands and I would do them and the day after that and the week and the year, if they asked me, because I had not yet thought of any way to be that was not this. At lunch I would open the paper and there would be a man with his hands over his eyes, and a boy with a bandage on his head, and a woman behind a counter counting over what looked like oranges from a distance and could have been grenades for all the picture was clear, and I would fold it in half and put it under my tray to keep the egg salad from slipping.

The next Tuesday, Jack called. He did not call on time. He called at seven. He sounded as if he had taken all the bones out of his words and set them in a line on a counter to wash. “Operator,” he said, and I heard in the way he pressed the P that he had not eaten, that he had sat his body in a chair and then stood it up and then sat it down again because otherwise it would know too much about itself. “I would like to place a long-distance call,” he said, formality meeting itself in a mirror, as if the part of his mind that had been built for politeness was still holding him up the way a string will sometimes make a kite seem steadfast though there is nothing but air and a small boy who wants very much to hold on. I set the cord in and said nothing and waited. “Person-to-person,” he said, and stopped. “No. Station-to-station.” The sound went sideways and then forward, like a car whose clutch is nearly done. His breath came in and went out again in a line I could almost mark my watch by. He gave me Marian’s number and I tried it. There was an answer after two rings this time. I did not drop out right away. This is the other thing I will never put in a folder where anyone might find it and say, Ah, here is a rule broken on June such-and-such, 1968, by an operator whose handwriting is distinct enough we can fire her with a clear conscience. I stayed a half-second longer than my job asked me to. She said “Hello?” with a question on it like a string tied around her finger to remind her of a thing the day might not let her remember. When he said her name, not in a question but in some other way, a way that was both a naming and a reaching for a thing you cannot speak into a room, she did not do the thing people put in pictures, which is gasp or cover her mouth. She said “I’m here,” which is the right thing to say. “I’m here.” There are many things in the world I wish to be, and more and more of them are useless: clever, prepared, in possession of every shoe I might need for any situation. There are a few things I think might be useful to be. “I’m here,” is one. They talked. I do not know what they said. I know the feeling of their words because the line took it in and out of my arms like breathing for seven minutes and twenty-three seconds. I waited to be the time. I waited to be the end. I waited and then I was. When I came in to them with the line the way the rules say—“I have your time and charges if you’d like them”—they did not change their voices the way people sometimes do when a cost enters the room. Marian said, “Yes, thank you,” like a woman in a grocery store taking a receipt. I could feel her sitting up a small bit straighter. I told them what I had written down. “Seven minutes and twenty-three seconds. Two dollars and eighty-three cents plus tax,” and someone somewhere has made an exactness of that, enough men with pencils that the wires under the ocean have a budget and the building I am sitting in has a stairwell supported by good bolts. When I had said it and it had fallen into their room there was the small hush that might make you think the line had gone dead. They had not hung up. A person will not always make a sound when the cost of a thing lands. Sometimes you can hear them taking the coins out of their pocket. Sometimes all that changes is the breath. In that moment, it is my job to anticipate whether someone will want to add time, to risk another quarter and what it might buy them. Jack said, faintly, “Thank you,” and the world’s most ordinary thing left us for a minute—the click you get when a man lifts his hand and lets it fall—and I sat looking at the black little face of the lamp as it went out.

That night I went home the short way. I did not pass the pay phone. Mrs. Keller had put out molasses cookies and a blue bowl of milk for a stray cat she insisted was stalking the house and never showed his face. I sat in the back hall and ate a cookie and picked the molasses out of my teeth and stared at the stripe of someone’s shadow on the linoleum and thought about two voices that had come in to me from places I will not see and gone out again unchanged but not unfelt. Maybe that is the work. I am not a nurse. We are not bled onto. We are not thanked. We are not necessary the way a neighbor is when your basement floods and your hands need another pair of hands to scratch at the wet paper bags and carry them into sunlight. We are a thing you think you can get rid of until you become grateful for the warm person in the chair who says, “Hold while I try your party.” In the winter of ’68, I had come to warm myself on them the way a person stops in front of a bakery on a cold day and stands on the sidewalk without a dime.

Tuesday again, and again. You think the world will learn a different day. It doesn’t. He called a few more times that I know of. He tried not to be grim. He spoke to me once as if we were people who had seen each other on a streetcar every day for years. “Operator,” he said one of the following weeks, “are you well this evening?” and I did not want to answer that because of the reasons there are for a person to ask you in a public place how you are and mean it. “I am, Mr. Ross,” I said because I would have used his name if I had ever been allowed to say it, because sometimes I felt as if I were running into him in a grocery store and we were both looking through a bin of green apples that had spots. “I am very well. Thank you for asking.” He might have smiled; I do not know. He placed the call. She answered on the fifth ring and when she did, her breath—no matter what you tell me about physics, about lines and current and the cost of a thing—her breath surprised him and also did not. “It’s me,” he said. “I know,” she said.

I began to keep a small book in my purse. You are not supposed to. It is a superstition, as much as a sin. I wrote down four letters, one for each of their first names, and then a time. The only other thing on those notations was the word “here.” It was not for them. If I had been hit by a bus carrying chain-link fence into a yard, no one would have found it and made a story of it. It was for me, in case time melted and I wanted to be able to say, On such-and-such a day, two people were at the ends of the wire and breathed at the same time. I am a woman who has put her head against the window of the trolley in January and watched the breath go in and out of her own mouth just to make sure the glass will frost. Some days you need proof that people are not alone without having to test it yourself.

There was not an ending. Not that month, not the next. If there was, it did not show itself to me, who had suddenly made rules where there hadn’t been any. I did not call her number again. I did not use the magic perversity of my chair to get in where I did not belong, not because I didn’t want to but because I had been allowed a second by accident and felt my own good fortune so purely that anything after that would have been gluttony. Once, he called at a time that was not hours—two in the afternoon when you would expect a man to have his back bent over a lathe or a steering wheel. I thought of that as the week he lost his job or his nerve. He placed the call anyway, and she picked up, and I left them to it like a woman standing on a street outside a closed door thinking, All right then, you rest and I’ll carry the milk home myself.

The men with the tools for the sea came and ate their sandwiches sitting on the curb outside my building that summer. I watched them from my window at lunch. If I had leaned out and asked them what happened to a voice if the wire broke, they would have laughed and told me nothing happens to a voice. It is not like a train or a person whose breath you can feel cooling even in a room with poor circulation. It is not like a thing dropped into a glass that splashes and you have to fetch a towel. It is a thing you do and then you do not do. It has no body. But tell that to a woman who has had a man say “Are you well this evening” in a tone like a slender creature stepping over a fence and not catching its stomach, coming clean over into a field where it was not supposed to be. Tell it to me. She would have called back, if I had given her the time and charges for the call she made at eleven-thirty the week before and she had thought to ask me how many minutes I had. It would have been a terrible conversation. She would have had to make me into a person and then we would be even, and I am not built for evenness. Some mornings I stand in front of my sink with a tooth powder I cannot bring myself to get used to and I wish for something that would make me answer a question honestly and keep my job, too. The only way I get to make an honest thing of any of it is to put it on the paper as if I were making a system of proof, write down “here” and “here” and wait for the sentence that says, in other words, that two voices were not drowned.

Another Tuesday would have found me at my position, the way my father would have found his bench the day after one man lost his finger and another was fired for crying. It is not that we don’t notice. It is not that people get less. It is a different scale, how we measure it. The days lay themselves down, thick as cloth. There are weeks that do not have the mercy of a Tuesday with people I could not touch and loved a little, and there are nights that do, and there is a woman who knows, and a man who knows, and a wire underwater that a fish considered for one fine second and decided not to bite. I am not required to be wise. I am required to be exact. When a voice lifts, I lift a plug. When a light goes dark, I rest my hands. The city makes a sound like a mouth closing over a word it will not say out loud again. I go home and feel the crooked places of my skull where the headset has been and I sleep on one side to give that bone a break, and in the morning I wake up and I make a thing to eat and I put on my shoes and I walk down a street with cracks in it, and there are wires over my head. I have told myself, as I pass under them some afternoons, that if I am quiet I can hear both their names pronounced in the city’s throat. If I am right, I have not proved it. If I am wrong, I have not ruined anything by believing it. I will go up the stairs, chalk-smelling, two at a time or one by one depending on the ache in my foot. I will sit where I sit. I will lower my head. A lamp will light. I will say, Long Distance, and accept that what I am attached to is mostly air that has learned to carry voice. And if at nine on a Tuesday I feel a small bright place of attention form in myself and not be embarrassed to have made a ritual out of breath, well, there are worse things a person can do with her hands than tend to a line between two rooms. I will always hope for them on a Tuesday. I will think of paper lunch sacks and a green coat with bad buttons and the word Mother said twice by a man trying to stay in his body. I will think of a woman saying “I’m here,” and the way those words make a place, and the miracle that anything has made a place long enough for any of us to find it.
