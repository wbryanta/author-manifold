Beneath the new awning, under the glossy black letters of a name that had not existed when I last walked under this portico, the valets looked the way valets are trained to look: as if they were sprung from somewhere clean and polite, a place with endless lint rollers. Their jackets had black piping and their necks shone. The street had been widened since the fire, the tree boxes squared off, the facade pulled forward into glass. The same sky, the same river two blocks over, the same gap that wind took through this corner, making everything shiver a little. Owen touched my elbow the way he does when he means to be solicitous without crowding me. His palm was warm in the thin cold of November.

“It's nice,” he said, getting out of the rideshare. “They rebuilt it nice.”

I said nothing because my mouth had filled with the taste of smoke that memory likes to imagine as sweet when you first encounter it: saccharine and gray like burned sugar on a pan that's only starting to go black.

A bellman took Owen's garment bag, balancing it over one shoulder as if it weighed more than a pair of trousers and an off-the-rack suit could possibly weigh. He smiled with half his face at me. His hat rode low. I'd worn a hat, once, too, as part of a uniform I hated because it made me look like a bell. My mind tried to slide out of my body, up into the chandelier that was not a chandelier back then, only a ring with bulbs like grapes. Now the lobby opened like a pool of light and orchids. Now the stone floor was polished gray. Now a smell like elevators and new carpet and lemon came up, and one thread of old ash, but that thread lived only somewhere between my nose and brain, retracing its own winding path down.

Behind the front desk was a woman with a part so straight you could sharp a pencil on it. She said welcome like a wish. The check-in counter had a brass rail along the bottom, in case you'd brought your dog. In the mirrored panel under the counter, I saw my shoes and remembered kneeling, all those years, making beds, wiping along baseboards for dust where no guest looked but God and the head of housekeeping did. We never stood at these counters unless we were told to pick up our pay. If we were called up here, we were being listened to by someone who took in the world sideways—someone's lost cuff link, a complaint that had to be oil-soothed with phrases like of course and my pleasure.

“Two nights,” the lobby woman said, looking past our faces to the screen in front of her. “Wedding block. Are you with the Campos-Wright party?”

Owen laughed before saying he was “Owen Wright, brother of the groom,” and then opened his arms in a little self-mocking jazz-hands gesture at me. “And this is Lila,” he said. “Plus-one extraordinaire.”

I signed my name on a lit rectangle like a small pond. The screen glowed blue under my hand. For a half-second my reflection on the glass was the pale oval it had been in the tiny mirror taped with band-aids inside my locker, the one that had been half-popped out of its frame and always threatened to drop and shatter and slash some girl opening the door too fast. I turned my hand and the bellman clipped a plastic rectangle back into the little leather wallet for keycards and handed it to me as if I might be the sort of person who preferred to keep such things in order. I have always been the sort of person who preferred to keep such things in order, but never before had anyone assumed it of me.

“You've been here before?” Owen murmured while we followed the bellman to the elevators. He asked because I had lagged, because my eyes had snagged on a plaque at the far end of the lobby wall, its brass face brushed in a way that catches the light only if you're moving. Names there. Names. The way you have to move to read something like that is a kind of permission and a rebuke.

“Yes,” I said, and then coughed on the lie. “I mean, I worked at the old one.” The words had waited in my chest for his family to choose this venue for their ceremony; they'd waited in my stomach as I ordered dresses online and returned them, tried them on and returned them again like something that wouldn't stop arriving in the mail. In the car I'd thought maybe I could tell him, in the forty-two minutes between our street and the hotel's, with him looking out at the river and nodding as if I were telling him a story about someone else, long ago. But the city came closer in its own glass, and once you see the building you can't pretend you didn't come to see the building.

Owen said “Oh,” and I heard both shapes of that syllable in his voice: the one that says I didn't know that; the one that says why didn't you ever say. He put his hand more firmly on my back like he was moving me toward warmth. The bellman's eye flicked in the elevator's mirror and away again. The door was heavy as a mouth; the carpet inside was brand new. There is a smell to wool mixed with adhesive when it is just down on concrete. I didn't know that smell until I took my first job in a hotel and learned to go home with it on my clothes. You learn a lot when you spend your days trying to erase other people's hours.

We rode. The new elevators were fast and smooth and didn't make the lurch they used to make, where your guts would do a small flip and you learned to press your knees so you didn't look as if you'd skipped a stair. The numbers slid in a line, clean red. Nine was where the fire had been worst, because someone had propped the door to the service hall with the corner of a hamper to let in air while she stripped bed after bed after bed. The hamper had burned like a bell. The service stairs were on the nine. Somewhere between eight and ten my heart tripped.

Our room was on the seventh floor. The bellman swung the garment bag to the closet's hanger bar with a professional thump and made a pun about hanging your hang-ups that I barely heard. He put the room service menu on the desk corner like a gift. When he left, Owen tipped him heavily because he has learned that's one way to demonstrate decency without having to narrate it. I pressed my palms to the bed, to see the topper give and come back. It was a duvet in a cover. It was bright white, too white to be real. In the linen closet back then, in the hot thin air under a buzzing fluorescent, we had sorted pillowcases by wear: the ones with grayish seams went in the mid-week pile; the ones with laddered threads, to be caught and mended. You can see the color called white turn into ten thousand versions of itself if you look long enough. Never, not once, had we turned a room with everything this white unless there was a photographer coming, or a top-floor guest with an onyx watch and a woman in something like a nightgown at three in the afternoon.

“This is...nice,” Owen said again. He turned a slow circle with his hands in his pockets. He is thirty-seven but sometimes he turns like a boy who has never stayed in a hotel with a desk that looks at a window that looks at a skyline that has three cranes doing three slow, separate dances. He has stayed in plenty but he also spent years not staying anywhere, and those years put a shine on things. I like that about him. He makes oatmeal taste like a trip to Paris six days out of seven just by the way he says “Look at this” and hands me a spoonful.

“The stitching on the pillow shams,” I said, because my tongue had burned and I needed a thing to do with it. I picked one up. They had used a taupe binding. The sham had a flange. “Nice,” I repeated, and in that second the word made me hate myself more than any other word could: it was what the bride would be saying downstairs to her mother as they looked at the napkins, at the cake. Nice is what you say when you don't need matter to be anything larger to you than the thing it seems to be.

“You okay?” Owen asked. He said it low, which is one of his gifts, the way he doesn't require you to perform in front of the whole room.

“I will be,” I said. I went into the bathroom and touched the soft close lid of the toilet. I opened and shut the heavy glass of the shower. I pulled back the shower curtain because that is what you do when you are a maid: you look for mold. I set the complimentary soap on the marble, then picked it back up, because if I left a wet ring I would feel bad for the girl who would clean it later and wonder what I'd been like. My hands itched for a rag. The old muscle memory flared so strongly that—if the role required it—I might have knelt and lifted the edge of the vanity to look for hair. I didn't. I drank a glass of water instead and felt it hit my stomach as if my stomach were the deep part of a lake.

We went down for the welcome cocktail hour because there are things families expect, and the easiest way to be part of Owen's family has always been to be the person who understands a schedule. The second-tier bar had been set up to the left of the new staircase, two bartenders in bow ties nudging conversation with small smiles under the clink of stemware. Furniture in the lobby had been rearranged so the risers could hold candles. The candles bothered me. I couldn't tell if that was because the fire had not been my friend or because the candles were overpriced and someone was about to bring a bill to someone else. In me was both chambermaid and person who, after the fire, could not afford a better job for a while than tying supermarket balloons to rails on weekends. Both of those women had become a woman in a black dress now, with a wrap in case of chill, with a pair of low-heeled shoes chosen for dancing. There were two meanings to the phrase “we clean up nice.”

The bride and groom were not yet present. Owen's mother—hair sprayed into a ceremony all its own—stood in a small pool of people like a dandelion with a champagne flute. She hugged me with the insistence of someone who was trying to make sure you felt the same warmth she had not gotten when she entered rooms like this once, in a different life, as a caterer's daughter. I like her too, even in her fizziness, because she tries, and trying counts for me as almost everything. The father, Richard, kissed my cheek like a television father. Owen took two glasses, put one in my hand. I held mine at my waist.

“It's good they rebuilt this,” Richard said, to no one in particular, as he looked around. He had the look people wear when they enter a famous room for the first time, a little borrowed pride. “Gorgeous. Phoenix from the ashes and all that. I read there’s even a memorial.”

“Dad,” Owen said quietly. But he could not fix what his father had said out of a desire to say anything at all.

“It was a...tragedy,” Richard added, and put his mouth around the word the way you put your mouth around a pitted olive, trying not to get the oil on your tongue. “But look.” He flung his hands up. The ceiling looked back with ten thousand auras of light.

I excused myself before the speeches started. There were already men with clipboards drifting into corners; there was already a microphone on a stand by the staircase, there because someone needed a place to stand and lift their face into it and talk about love and fortune. I walked to the far wall as if I were going to admire the art. The plaque had been brushed bronze, as I had thought, and it sat on the stone like a thing trying to be ordinary. That is how you display memory in a luxury hotel. You tuck it where the line for the restroom isn't. You make it visible if you are someone who looks sideways, if you are someone who reads the third line of a menu before the first. There were names etched into the metal. They were the names of guests that night, and the names of two firefighters, and there were four names pressed a little closer together in the order of housekeeping shifts. I saw the name I had said to myself a million times since. I said it again. Nati. The night she died she had taught me a song in Spanish about a bird. She had been four years older and laughed like the white bedspread had just leapt onto the bed at your command.

I touched the metal. It was cool, and warmer where other fingers had rested. Some had wiped their tears there, I suppose, and you do not think to bring a tissue when you are reading a plaque.

My only regret about the fire had not been what you might imagine from the way I sat up in the middle of the night for three years and put my hand to my throat and drank from the cup on the nightstand as if it were the only water. It had not been that I had lived. I had never felt obligated to die with the poor in order to prove some loyalty to them. My regret had been that I had stood for thirty seconds that were the size of a stadium, frozen, with a hamper handle in my hand and my eyes full of gray. Thirty seconds is time enough to save a person if you know that that is what you have to do. It is not time enough if your ears are ringing and all you have is all the blood in your hands and what you can carry. And yet I had lived and Nati had not and that is the calculus you do when you decide, unconscious as an animal, to put your body through the door you can find.

A man in a suit with a small pin on his lapel that told the wore whoever's initials you needed to report to if there was a spill approached. He looked at me with a face he had been taught to hold: soft, professional stone.

“Do you need anything?” he asked, and the question floated in the air like dust in sunlight. He meant water. He meant a chair.

“I used to work here,” I said.

He glanced at the plaque, and back at me. His smile—a piece of trained property—never faltered, but something in his posture clicked back half an inch, as if he hadn't expected the living to talk back to the dead. “In the old building,” I added. “Years ago.”

“We are honored to have you with us,” he said, seeing my dress now, mapping the status of a guest to his phrase book. Then he nodded once and left as if he'd been called. He probably had been. There is always a headset in some ear, behind a hairdo like that, an instruction murmured through plastic and wire.

I went outside. November air will pinch you into yourself sometimes, remind you of edges. There was a bicycle rack that hadn't been there before. The smokers had been moved to the side gate, a little desired and a little punished. Two women in navy jackets with the hotel's initials pressed in the shoulder stood there with paper cups. One of them had a pink scrunchie eating the back of her ponytail; the other had a gold tooth that flashed when she laughed.

“Light?” the gold tooth woman asked. Then saw I didn't have a cigarette. “Sorry.”

“I used to smoke,” I said. “Ages ago.” I wanted them to know I understood them. I wanted them to know I wasn't one of the people who wrote notes about ash on the patio table and circled a nonexistent fleck in a photograph of my own disgust. But everything you say when you want to be understood becomes a little false in the saying, like a song sung too close to a microphone.

They made the small talk you always make on a break: cold enough for you. The girl with the scrunchie told the other she had a neighbor who kept her thermostat at sixty-two. You keep what you can pay.

“The new building,” I said, because I could not not ask. “How is it?”

“Better,” the girl with the scrunchie said. “New. I haven't been here long, though, just a year.” She looked at me more closely when I didn't answer. “You worked here before? Older one?”

“I did,” I said.

“Back of house is nice now,” said gold tooth. “No leaks. We got lockers with codes.” She held up her wrist as if to show me a thing I couldn't see from here: the small white scars bleach will make if you let it dry on your skin; the splashes where it freckles you worse than the sun. “They got sprinklers everywhere. Drills. They love their drills.”

In the air I heard the echo of the last alarm we'd all ignored; the bell like a bird with a tin beak in the ceiling. People ignore alarms because they live in buildings where the alarm goes off weekly, and because dressed strangers knock on their door and tell them fifteen minutes is all they have for a thing they had thought would be three hours. You make time by cutting seconds off the edge of it; you cut the wrong seconds and the day changes shape. Maybe the sprinkler detail gave her the feeling of adult oversight, of someone older in the room, someone useful.

“How'd you get out?” gold tooth asked, low, like maybe she shouldn't ask but she would hate herself if she did not.

“Stairs,” I said. I said it quickly. I did not tell her the lunge I had made sideways from the room with the hamper at its door into the other room, over the balcony we never went out on because the bird poop out there would have needed a different cleaning protocol. I didn't tell her how you remember the feel of a railing under your envelopes of knuckle and the way a knee scrapes like chalk when it hits concrete. I did not tell her about Nati yelling through the tamarind smoke to get out, get out, that she was on the phone, that she'd called and the line had been dead. I had not even told Owen.

We stood in the cold until the skin over my collarbone felt like paper. Then I went back in. My lungs were my lungs again and I left them alone for a while.

The bride came sashaying through the lobby with her mother and three friends like a school of rare fish. Her hair was in curlers as big as fists. She wore a T-shirt that said Bride in rhinestones like the pasties of some patient stripper. Someone had forgotten bobby pins; someone had remembered a checklist and was reading it all aloud in a laugh. I felt a surge of affection for their focus. Weddings are nothing if not the theater of lists. Owen went to his brother; the men crashed into a half-hug half-fight they have done since they were small. The father pointed at the cake, which had not yet been wheeled in.

At the rehearsal dinner the GM stood up and said a sentence about the history of the hotel. He said the word resilience and he said safety and he said community and the sixty people in the room who had not spent a day stripping a bed did the sound people do when they want to go on to the next course. Then the wedding planner, in a dress with a zipper like a spine, took the microphone and got laughter by asking everyone not to drink too much before the vows. Her hands were small and tanned as she waved them. Two servers slid plates down in front of us with choreography you only learn by once dropping a thing in a room like this and being told, later, that you cannot ever do that again. One of them was a girl with hair the color of the inside of bread. She had a burn scar on her wrist you could see when she pushed back a strand with the back of her hand. She put down a plate and I made eye contact with her because you are taught not to. She looked away and then looked back as if to see if I was testing her.

After dinner Owen found me by the toilets and said nothing, put his face to my neck until the breath there came back home. We went up. I lay awake. You might think sleeping in a hotel where you used to clean a hotel is like staying overnight in your own office, but it is not like that. It is like sleeping in the body of a person you know the insides of too well and you hated them and you loved them and you cannot forgive their failures and yet you know, intimately, what they suffered to do simply what they did. Every hotel is a person with two faces. The night face stopped me from sleeping because the staff hall on this floor ran under the carpet beneath our bed and I could hear things like whispers in there: wheel, wheel, wheel; lock, click, click. At midnight a door closed somewhere down the hall and I thought, as I always thought, of the person on the other side of it needing—from me, from someone like me—a tissue folded into a triangle and slid into a metal box as if it were a secret.

At one in the morning I got up and went into the hall and walked to the exit sign past the ice machine. Owen snored in the bed behind me as a signal that he had finally let the day go. The exit door was propped with a plastic wedge because someone had brought in carts. The green man in the sign walked forever down his little stairs. I slid my hand along the cold handrail and stood in the service hall that ran behind the guest doors like a vein. It smelled like the linen closet I still dream about: hot and damp and good, like a hundred steam irons have been fired five minutes ago and set back upright. There were voices. A radio played oldies in Spanish; a pot clanged. To my right, a room full of towels sat like a forest you could get lost in. On the table by the Coke crate was a stack of amenities, their plastic wrappers tight as skin. And the hamper, oh God, the hamper, like a big wheeled mouth with rim rubbered for safety. The same, even now. The plastic had been replaced since, and the wheels shone.

“Ma’am?” a voice said down the hall, and I turned as if I'd been caught at a crime. He was a security guy, young enough to be my cousin. His face had three pimples trying to be brave on his right cheek. He looked like a child in his tie. He said it again, kinder this time, ma’am? “Can I help you find something?”

“No,” I said. “I just...was looking for ice.”

“It's that way,” he said, not unkindly, and pointed toward the guest hall as if to remind me where I belonged.

In the morning, hair-and-makeup showed up in the bride's room on the ninth floor like a brigade. I had not planned to go. I had planned to keep my heart in my mouth until it dissolved there and then to put lipstick on and smile like a guest in the clutch of a napkin. But Owen's mother knocked on our door and said “Will you come? They need an extra pair of hands,” and the logic in me—the habit of work I am proud of—made me say of course. If a woman tells you a room on the ninth floor needs hands, you give them.

The elevator opened like a soft mouth onto old space baptized new. I stepped out and saw everything doubled: the carpet like sea grass, and the carpet then: singed in a strip where the hamper had burned; the air now, fresh and chemical; the air then, boiled and dirty. There was a big window on the ninth that hadn't existed before, and the light through it tasted like the thing you get when you have been in a fever too long and someone pushes a glass of something cold into your palm and you do not even say thank you because what is breath to someone who thought she had none left. In the bride's suite there were six women and a man with hair excessively cautious in both direction and intent. The counter had become a forest of spray bottles. By the sink—my eyes went first where I always look—there were towels, bright, folded like new words.

“Oh thank God,” the wedding planner said when she saw me. She pressed a safety pin into my palm and said, “We lost the hook and eye on this romper, can you find it?” The bride raised a shoulder and winked. She did not need comfort. She needed the small repairs of a day that had been too carefully planned to allow for them.

I found the hook. I took the safety pin and a needle from the sewing kit that lives in the bride's suite because women in rooms need sewing like fish need gills. I put a finger under the hook to keep from pricking the bride, and my hands remembered. There are chimeras in the body called memory that live in the fingers and the neck, that sit quietly and twitch and wait. The bride looked at me with a weird mixture of gratitude and surprise. “How do you do that without looking?”

“Practice,” I said, and the women in the chairs said mmm the way women do when they know what practice is. The hairstylist sprayed. The sun came through the new window and made a square pattern on the carpet where we stood. I looked out and saw the river like a blade in the distance. I put the needle down and walked to the window and thought: this is where I took the other window when the other window wouldn't open and I needed air.

“Lila?” Owen's mother said quietly, as if she were calling a finch.

“I'm here,” I said. I put my hand on the sill and left no print.

No one cried during the vows because everything they said felt—somehow—earned and also like something you say in this kind of dress. The officiant was a friend who didn't believe in God but believed in afternoons. When he said the word witness I felt as if he had pushed me back three inches in my seat and then set me forward gently like a child on a swing. Owen’s hand was on my knee and he didn't move it. I watched the sprinkler heads arranged along the ceiling like small mouths, silent and vigilant. There were also candles in glass hurricanes because the bride liked the way fire looks when you can control it. In my head I counted exits the way some people count calories. It is a habit I still haven't broken. Maybe that makes me the wrong person for rooms like this. Maybe it makes me the right one.

The clink of forks on glasses drove the new couple to kiss. It was sweet and competent and almost old-fashioned. The band struck up something with a horn that made middle-aged men fling their necks around like boys. Owen pulled me to the dance floor once, twice. He is long and moves awkwardly until he doesn't. He has always been strong in the ways that do not frighten. When the small sparklers got lit for their first dance, I told myself my lungs were my lungs and my skin my skin and the air just air. Then the DJ—God bless him, God keep him—from somewhere beneath his console sent up a slim flare of fog, a frail offering to a dance tradition that wanted its bride and groom to float. It was nothing. It was barely the suggestion of fog. It was a man squeezing a trigger on a bottle to make a room think it was a better painting. The fog slid over the floor like a lick of sea. And I was suddenly back. Not just back. I was in the room with my hands on the hamper handle, with my mouth open and no air in it, with Nati laughing ten minutes before saying you see, this city is the one where we will make something, with my own breath tasting like toast. It didn't matter that the fog was white and clean as chalk. The bell in my ear split open.

I walked off the dance floor like a person who has to pee and is too polite to say it aloud. I walked, and then I walked faster, and then the hallway came down and met me like a hand, and I put my hands on the banister because the banister is always still, and I went into the service stair because it knows my feet.

In the rectangle of the stairwell everything clicked into place around the sound of my own lungs. Concrete. The metal tasting like pennies on the fingers if you lick them. The echo-hum of the building, like a bass string played slow. I stood on a landing. I put the side of my face to the wall, the way you lay your face to a sleeping child's neck to check whether they are fevered. I don't know how long I stood. Seconds, surely. A minute. Owen found me because he can hear a thing drop in the next room like a conscience.

He touched my shoulder with the care you use to lift glass. “Hey,” he said. “Hey.”

“I worked here,” I said. My voice came down the stairs like pennies falling into a fountain. “In this—” I gestured at the stairwell and laughed, which did not feel like mine. “The old one. This is where—” I stopped because in the act of saying it, I felt as if I might empty my pockets and my mouth of something I would not be able to pick up.

He didn't say anything. We listened to somebody down one floor dragging a bag of bottles. The clank was weirdly soothing.

“I know you worked in a hotel,” he said. “I didn't know it was this one. I didn't know...” He trailed off, the way people trail off when they realize that their map of you left off a country the size that you would have to put on a globe to see it.

“I left,” I said. “After. I left the city. I took a bus. I didn't go to the memorial.” The words were all gravel. “I sent money to Nati's mother for a while. Through her cousin. Then she moved and I lost the address.” I could see the envelope in my hands, the way I'd written the name wrong at first and had to write it again.

He put his hand on the rail next to mine. The metal was cool. “It's okay,” he said. It was and it wasn't. When someone tells you your past is okay, what they mean is your present is here and that is also true. He might have been about to say more but he stopped, which is a skill I wish I could put in a bottle and sell to men.

“I think—” I said, and then my hand found his hand and put it back on my shoulder because I wanted to anchor my flesh. “I think I was afraid if I went to the memorial it would make it like a line, like there was before and after, and my body didn't want a line because the line would also be a wall and if there was a wall I would never be able to go back to any of the days before that day and get what I left. I didn't even own enough to leave anything. I don't know what I'm saying.” I started to laugh. It was not the right sound. It was the piece of laughter that lives in you like fungus after a damp season.

“You're saying something I've said to myself on different subjects,” he said. “You're saying sometimes you don't touch the thing because if you touch it it will become a statue and then it will be too heavy to move.” He said it like he was reading a manual. He is a designer; he thinks in functional shapes.

“Yes,” I said. “Yes.”

“Do you want to go look at the plaque together?” he asked. He did not say: do you want to go read the names. He did not say: do you want to lay a flower. He said the smaller version of the thing that leaves you room to mean something bigger if you want. That is the same way you move a bed around a corner with a partner who knows which corner to lift.

“After the cake,” I said, suddenly as if I were joking. His eyes searched. “No,” I said. “Now. After now.”

We went down. In the lobby the bar had been pushed temporarily to one side, to make room for the inevitable drunk cousin's dance. The plaque sat on its wall like a patient woman. We stood in front of it and read the names like you read a liturgy. People gave us space without knowing why they gave us space. There is a force around grief that the polite can sense, the way you can sense someone has been in a fight even if you don't see the bruise. I found Nati's name with my finger again, but I did not touch the cold this time. Touching would have made a cold print and I wanted the letters to remain as warm as they had become.

“She liked birds,” I said. “She had one when she was a girl. She taught me a song.”

“Tell me the song,” he said.

I did. I sang two lines of it in a voice I have used only for babies and people whose faces I could not see. He listened the way he's learned to listen at the office to clients who cannot find adjectives for the thing they very clearly know they want.

“I'm sorry,” he said, and he meant: I am standing here with you on a day about happiness and there is also this. “What do you want?” he asked—slightly formally, as if I were an adult ordering a thing at a counter from a patient clerk. It is a good question. It is better than I'm sorry, most days.

I wanted a thing that does not exist in the world and if it did we would have no need of jokes. I wanted to go upstairs to the ninth floor and open a door that would open and not be hot and see Nati sitting on a bed counting pillowcases, and I wanted to tell her I had been in love more than once, that it is not a sin to forgive the first person you loved for not being all of the people you would love. I wanted to tell her I had learned to make roast chicken by myself, the kind with lemon you push in under the skin, and that I'd baked for the people I loved until their bellies went soft. I wanted to ask her what her daughter's hair smelled like now. I wanted to return to the hallway with a bag of bread and feed it to all the birds on the sills we had been told never to open. I wanted to tell her I had not walked a single block without her voice along the inside of my skull for five years.

“Let's sit,” I said, because the body needed a thing the soul didn't think of.

We sat on a couch in the lobby that had been designed to look like someone could fall asleep on it even though no one would be allowed to. A server with eyebrows like parentheses put two waters down as if she had been told there were waters to be put down, here, every hour. We drank. The mouth becomes real again like a plant you remember to water after three dry days. In my peripheral vision a bag of ice shimmered where a busser had set it, and in my head that and the fog and the steam and the river flickered like a timeline you didn't get to hold all at once very often without going numb.

“You could tell your mother-in-law,” I said to no one.

“I have no mother-in-law,” he said, and laughed. “You could tell Laura,” he added, meaning his mother, because he says her name now that we are almost married in the way that only our cynicism stops us from making legal. “You could. If you want.”

“Later,” I said. “When my face has settled back on my skull.”

He kissed the corner of my eye, which is weird comfort and perfect. We sat and watched a man in a suit rehearse a speech under his breath while he walked in a square that would one day be a dance. We watched a kid in patent leather shoes detour from his mother to slide across the marble and fall down, and looked, and saw the exact same sequence run twice, because children and drunks always do favors for the people watching them if the people are paying attention. After a while we went back upstairs, and the cake had been cut, and there was a piece for me with some raspberry embedded in the icing, and I ate it like a person who eats cake.

By the time they rolled in coffee, I could breathe beside the sparklers and the smoke machine had been retired by the solicitous DJ as if it had tired itself out with the foreplay it offered. I danced with Owen’s aunt who had a knee brace that flashed like a sign under her pant leg. I danced with a cousin who could not find the rhythm if you pinned it to his shirt. I danced with a girl from the smoking corner—she had come in for the last hour, her break extended by management because the new idea is to make people feel like family rather than fired.

“You’re good,” she said, with the curiosity of a person who has not seen you all day except smoking and reading a plaque.

“Practice,” I said again, and did not say from nights when you have to spin the room to leave it. Did not say from a childhood where we danced in kitchens because the floor was the only thing big enough we owned. Did not say from weddings like this where the dancing is the only thing we all agree on except the cake.

At midnight the lights rose a notch and the band moved to more percussion. Someone shouted along. The mother of the groom kicked off her shoes and did a small thing with her hips that made us all cheer. The father of the groom tried and failed and made us clap for the failure. Owen took my hand like he had found it in a coat pocket he’d forgotten his own fingers could use. We sweat. The smell of other people's perfume and work moved around us like water, like a river you are just deep enough in to feel it push your knees.

When we went upstairs, the hall was a different color. The carpets darken a shade when they absorb enough of the day; it's like watching an animal's fur change in winter. Inside, I took off my shoes with the sound shoes make when they have worked enough to be allowed to rest. I went into the bathroom and washed my face with the small bar of soap the hotel had given me for my own face; I thought of the hands that would touch the sink tomorrow and the next day and how many were mine, indirectly. In the mirror I saw nothing but light around and the oval of my face in it, the small fatigue, the black comma of a mascara smudge under one eye that looks like dirt if you don't love yourself. I wiped it away. I left the towel on the bar because I have learned, finally, to accept that I can be the person who lets another person do the small parts of their job; that this is not a crime. I put the do-not-disturb on the knob because we had the morning for ourselves and because I wanted to give a small gift to the girl in my head who was pushing a cart and hated the sound of taps on a door when no one answered.

In the morning I woke before Owen, because I always do; and the sun had come up with its hands open, offering November to the seventh-floor windows like a thing you can touch but not keep. I stood and put my hand on the pane and felt the border between inside and out like a doctor. It was cold on the outside. It was cool on the in; the early hours still hold someone else's chill. We showered. We dressed. We went down and I stopped in that small shadowed section of the lobby on the way to the elevators, the one where there used to be an ATM. The plaque was there, as it will be long after these carpets have grayed and been torn up and another style decided upon by a new set of owners. I took a bobby pin from my hair. It had held everything in place last night while I did the thing my body had done and come undone and that had been a kindness to both acts. I slid the bobby pin, quietly, between frame and wall, not so anyone would see it and tidy it away quickly, but so that the building would know I had left a thing in it, finally. I am not the kind of woman who believes buildings have souls. I am the kind of woman who knows bodies keep count.

On our way out a housekeeper in a navy dress with a thin white band of piping pushed a cart past us. She had a bottle of blue cleaner tucked into the top corner so it wouldn't tip. She had a pen stuck into her bun. She glanced at me and then away because she had work to do. I nodded, nothing more. Outside, the day had already been lived by the men who sweep in front of the storefronts, by the women on their way to serve eggs, by the kid on the step with a skateboard and a face like a page at the start of something written by a boy who will be happy for a while and doesn't know it. Owen slid his arm around me in that way that makes me think of latches: you don't know until they click that they are doing their job. I leaned into him, not because I needed support to walk but because there was room in the lean to believe the morning had been earned, that the long line of hours behind me led also to these hours in front.

We stepped to the curb to wait for a car because cars and hotels and weddings conspire to make you still when you want to walk. Across the street the laundromat's door—blue paint nicked down to the old yellow underneath—opened and a girl came out with a basket on one hip like fruit. The day was shining in that particularly American way: all the edges polished until they bite. The hotel's letters cut their name out of the light. On a day fifteen years earlier I had run down this same block with my lungs on fire, cheeks wet and part of my arm scorched where I'd grabbed the hamper rail too hard. I had not thought there would be a morning like this where the letters would be new and I would be clean and the man at my side would be the one whose spoon of oatmeal tastes like Paris. I had not thought I'd be someone else's guest ever again.

The car came. The door opened. A wind lifted one corner of my scarf as if to wave to someone I didn't see. On the glass behind us a set of fingerprints showed for an instant where someone had pressed their hands, and then the sun flattened the light and the prints disappeared. I put my hand there, not to keep them from going but to feel the same patch of warmth. Then I got in, and we drove, and the farther we got from the corner the more the hotel seemed like just a building, with elevators and towels and a woman with a key ring counting her day in quarters of an hour. The sign got small. The person I had been—hands raw, mouth black with smoke—waved once from a window you couldn't open then, and I waved back as if we had all the time in the world.
