By noon the sign I chalked this morning has begun to drip around the edges from the humidity, ESTATE SALE TODAY, with an arrow pointed toward the front porch that sags the way my mother’s cheeks sagged in her last photograph. I stand on the lid of the cooler to fish a pebble out of the gutter where rain from nights ago has left a shallow, slick riverbed. A blackbird drops onto the telephone line and clucks its opinion. Across the street, Pete Peterson rolls his trash can back into the garage and gives me a thumbs-up in lieu of stopping to talk. Nettleton’s main road two blocks over sends a ribbon of cars past the end of Maple with their windows up, their radios low. They’ve seen the Facebook listing—Estate Sale: Entire Contents, Priced to Sell, Cash Preferred—and they are arriving already, ten minutes before I wanted them to.

“Mom,” I say in the direction of the crabapple tree. “You’d hate this.” The tree’s leaves are rusted with spots, the way they always are at the end of July, surviving anyway. The city lopped a branch off three summers back because it interfered with the line, an ugly scar sawed on one side. My mother had insisted on standing at the edge of the yard then, her little hips pitched forward in indignation, telling the foreman they shouldn’t be cutting in the heat. The men had smiled and steadied the ladder and cut anyway, glad to be noticed. She made them iced tea with extra lemons. Today the lemons in the fridge are hard little fists.

The first car to pull into the gravel crunch of our drive is a sunburned Silverado with a wooden pallet in its bed, dust printed on the tailgate in a ghostly hand. Larry Eaton from the hardware store climbs out in his ball cap and wipes his brow with a folded bandana, as if to let me know he works for his bargains. He is not a friend exactly, but he knows me, knows whose girl I am, whose house this is; he knew Daddy when Daddy smiled sometimes and smelled like machine oil. Next is a scissory pair of women in capri pants and sandals, the kind of women who go in pairs, who cackle and bless hearts and carry tote bags they bought at library sales. One of them is named Brenda—I know her ranch house behind the school—though I can’t remember if she has a granddaughter or a little dog; they blur together in my head. They smile sympathetic and say, “Bless you, Ruth,” and I am not sure if they mean my mother is flying through the pearly gates or that it is a pity I haven’t priced the quilts higher.

The screen door whines when I tug it open and the hot inside air meets the hotter outside. I have a cash box with singles and fives, a roll of quarters, and an apron with deep pockets that was my mother’s—stiff and clean, ironed this morning with lines like pressed leaves. My phone sits on the edge of the hall table where last night I slept an hour on the sofa and woke with the television whispering to itself when the luminance dropped. I fixed little circles of blue painter’s tape to things with amounts written in Sharpie I thought would hurt the least.

“Just a second,” I say as Larry touches the storm door with his big paw. “Let me just—” I straighten the plate of brownies on the kitchen counter that Bette from next door brought over and insisted I take. I pushed toothpicks into them like they were a thing you sample on an airplane, rubbery with Cool Whip. I set out hand sanitizer because that is what we do now, though my mother would laugh at it and say to use soap like a normal person. The house smells like us, still, like lemon oil and the smell that lives in old wood regardless, sweet and a little sad, and under that the faint ghost of Vicks and Pond’s cold cream that I cannot get rid of because the scent has lodged itself in the paint.

“Come on in,” I say and step aside, and for a second I am tall with the small power of deciding and then it all washes away because this is not power; this is release.

They step into the living room that has become an exhibit: index cards with geography written on them. I think, Did you see the way she polished that? Did you notice the dent in the coffee table that my brother Mark made with his scout knife and lied about for a solid year? Did you appreciate, did you understand. Larry drifts toward the bookshelf because he is the kind of man who reads, not novels but histories and books with maps that fold out like linen handkerchiefs in a gentleman’s pocket. His schooling ended junior year when his father got sick, but he likes to say he goes to the library like it is his bar. He runs his fingers along my father’s World War II paperbacks and the service manual on a Buick we never owned.

The women make a beeline for the dining room and the china cabinet that I dread, a big-bellied thing my mother had as a girl and brought here in 1971 when he got the job at Union Carbide and she chose the house with the porch and the shade and the slope of yard, a spread of a garden she said would keep us out of trouble. It’s not worth as much as Mother believed. Everything is less valuable when it’s arrayed like teeth in a skull, stacked to be rid of. Everything shines for a new reason: departure. They want the Pyrex, especially, the set of Cinderella nesting bowls with the blue cornflowers that sat in the cabinet my entire life, that she made Jell-O salads in for funerals and baptisms and the potluck when Coach Pritchard retired. I have priced it too low; even I know it as the women ooh over the pattern.

“Twenty for the set?” the not-Brenda says, already palming the largest like a softball. Her nails are short and she’s put a constellation of bobby pins into the top of her hair as if any moment she could switch from shopping to building a pergola. I look at the twenty taped there and I remember the cold slurp of Jell-O down a paper plate when I was eight, the crunch of the celery, the lettuce leaf underneath because for a long time women thought lettuce under anything made it a salad. I hear my mother telling me not to scrape the spoon against the enamel bowl because she would hear it as a chalk scrawl on the nerves.

“You know,” I say, and then stop. There is no you know; there is this price and my wanting both to keep and to be done. “Twenty.”

Brenda looks at the blue and white and gulps, not swallowing. “I had this one. I had this exact. My daughter put it in the dishwasher and now the little flowers are gone and I will never forgive her.” She laughs, and the laugh rises and pops like gum. She adds the breadbox to her armful and a little cruet with a cork that always smelled like Italian dressing no matter how many times we scrubbed it. People pass like bees through the rooms, as if my mother has grown flowers in every corner, which she did, in a way. They are drawn to the bowl of buttons in the sewing room, to the tiny vases she kept for the thin-necked violets that were brave in March, to the stack of afghans with zigzags in the caramel colors of the seventies and then later a gaudy one that a church woman made for her when she got back trouble and needed a cheerful thing. I picture that woman crocheting and praying into it and laugh in my throat because neither of my parents attended church except weddings and funerals, yet the church ladies assumed them.

On the mantel is the clock that never entirely worked but which Daddy wound anyway, a bulge of brown that tried to speak and then held its breath all afternoon. I have priced it high because it is safe there. I could price it at a million, and someone would still ask, “Would you take ten?”

Larry holds up the vise from the garage like he has won a competition, which he has; people sprinted past me to the garage as if that was where the holy relics lived. It is where the smell lives, of oil and old sugar—that is, sawdust—and metal that sheds a bright dry scent when you file it. My father with his particular body of work at that bench: fixing, creating a clip for something that did not have a clip. When I was ten he made my Barbie a tiny swing out of a paint stirrer and fishing line and a nail in the frame of the basement door. Barbie swang crooked and fearless into the laundry room and back. If you stand in the garage and look left, you can still see where he wrote measurements and sometimes phone numbers on the framing wood with mechanical pencil, notes to his later self who would arrive and be grateful.

“How much you taking for this?”

“Twenty-five,” I say because a young man in a T-shirt with jagged mountains on it offered me twenty earlier and I said, “Let me think,” and then he disappeared back into the swarm. Larry grimaces the way men in our town like to do because haggling is the closest thing to flirting they allow themselves. “It’s a good one,” I add, and it is; it opens like a loyal jaw; it stands; it would clamp a thing and hold it through an earthquake.

“Twenty and I carry that thing for you,” he says, gesturing toward the upright piano like a man who wants to wrestle a hippopotamus.

“We’re not moving the piano.” I lean back into the doorway of the garage and wipe the back of my neck with my hand like I am throwing away an emotion. “Or we are, but not today. Someone’s coming.” This is a lie. I have listed the piano for free if you can carry it and no one has yet, and Mark texted me too late, last night, to say, “Don’t give away the piano; I want to think about it,” as if the piano is a loaf of bread he might want a sandwich from and not a friend we hurt by not touching. The bench is full of sheet music, some of it bound with purple rubber bands that have gone brittle and slack. The books are full of her careful, high, slanting pencil, the fingers she scolded herself for not curving, “You will never get it, Ruthie, if you do not curve your hands like water spiders,” lovingly critical, the sort of sentence that both scars and teaches.

“You sure?” Larry muses. “It’ll take—what, three men? Four?”

“Four men and particular geometry,” I say. “And there’s a step at the entry that is exactly the wrong height.” I picture the way we moved it in, a long morning in 1973 that ended with lemonade and a throwaway line from my father—“We oughta just build the house around it next time”—which the men grunted agreement to like it was an original thought. My mother sat on the bench and ran a scale with the lid up; the sound was both clamped and immediate, as if a voice had been trapped in a wooden throat for too long.

He takes the vise for twenty-two because he is old enough to be generous and because he knows something I do not: that this thing, once a grip on my father’s world, will hold something else in his. He counts carefully, a man who remembers when you always counted because people pretended to be good to your face and then wasn’t. He pockets it and promises to come back at one with a dolly to see what else he can’t live without, and then he is gone and a new wave is in, like a tide I cannot predict.

In the bedroom there is a man about my age with his hands heavy on a dresser drawer full of stockings, their pale lengths like onion skin, like the molted skins of snakes. He looks a little guilty and then points to a scarf that I used to tie onto my braids when I was ten to feel like a horse’s mane, teal and white with navy polka dots. “How much for scarves?” His voice is timid, as if I am going to require him to justify his desire. I imagine his wife telling him to look for linens and him hearing limits, the small twinge in him that is happier alone in strange bedrooms than in his own bathroom, where the mirror knows him too well.

“Two dollars,” I say, and he stands taller, relieved he can afford some bright memory he has never lived. He asks about pillowcases, and I tell him the price for the pillowcases embroidered with little roses that my mother did in the year after Daddy died when her hands needed a job that was not sitting. He carries the scarf and I fold the pillowcases into a bag and he is nice to me on the way down the hall, stops and says, “I’m sorry about your mom,” and he may mean it, or he may mean it the way we mean it at work in passing when someone’s mother has become a line in the paper.

The quilter’s guild lady from church shows up and makes a fuss over the nine-patches, identifies with her fingers the way you read Braille who made what, how fine the seams, whose corners match. She has the kind of kindly superiority that cannot keep its mouth from teaching. “Oh, Louise,” she says like an ache, like a prayer. “She was so exact. See here. She did not pin.” I do not know if not pinning is a brag or a sin, and I nod. The quilts are priced lower than they ought to be; I know it and I do not care because I do not have room and I have one in the car already, one she made me when I moved to Chicago and I was sure I would never come back.

In the dining room, a woman I do not know holds my mother’s wedding china, the plates white with a band of silver, a pattern outdated even by the time I married for the first time. We never used them; my mother said we would when something extraordinary happened. It seems dangerous to say nothing extraordinary ever did. It seems ungrateful to admit that she and I measured extraordinary differently, and telling her mine would be like telling a child you must not swallow a star.

“How much for the set?” the woman asks, and the word set does something prickly to my forearm, because it implies completeness and I know even new you could never buy full; there would always be a dessert plate missing, a sugar bowl lid broken in 1982, a cup that fell and died when Aunt June gestured with her hands and I reached too late, fifteen, clumsy, breathless from the kitchen, a pot of coffee sloshing because I wanted to be helpful, because I wanted to be the kind of person who saw what people needed before they asked. My mother made that face then that was not quite angry, the face she made when any dish was chipped. She pinched her lips and said “No use crying,” and then later when we were alone she pinched the bridge of her nose and closed her eyes the way she did when she felt something deep.

“Fifty,” I say before I can measure if it is fair, and in the additional second I am wishing I had said more or less, the woman says, “Forty?” and I see the flash of my mother’s eyebrows. The woman is a teacher, probably, based on the safe sandals and the good cotton dress with pockets. She has a warm smile and a sweat mustache and she holds the box gently, waiting.

“Okay,” I say, and she adds a serving spoon with bakelite handle to her total like a joke, and I do not mind. She says, “I’ll think of your mama,” but of course she will not, not after the first two uses when her sister tells her the sugar bowl is too fussy and they drink coffee out of mugs with chippearies. She will remember who she and her sister are when they are kind and when they are not. We are all the only people who can carry our own dead long enough to give them their right size.

By one-thirty the rooms are threadbare and I am down to giving deep discounts for boxes of canning jars. Twice, women ask if they come with rings and lids, and no, of course they don’t; nothing ever comes with the part that keeps it closed. In the kitchen the stove is cold and looks suddenly older than I remember. The window over the sink does not open clean anymore because the frame swells in the damp; my father never fixed it because there was always so many else to do and the house, like a child you love too much to discipline, got away with being itself.

On the refrigerator with its clean door are three magnets I found down the side and washed: a cardinal, the number of Peaslee’s Plumbing, a calendar from Moser’s Feed with a Holstein. I stick them on so the white is not naked, so the room is not a hospital, so when the stranger reaches for a Diet Coke here today he sees our bird and our cow and knows we believed in both.

At two, Mrs. Stonelake from next door stops with a carton of bottled waters clamped in one crook of her elbow and says, “Oh, honey,” which I accept without groaning because there is no reason to pretend anymore I am not in need of something; I am. She has places she remembers me at: on the swing with my legs splayed like a compass pointing to the neighborhood, at the curb drawing a hopscotch grid with chalk while boys on banana-seat bikes cut through and smudged it and I did not care, my mother insisting I wear a shirt even when I wanted to go like a boy in just shorts and scraped knees. She tells me again that my mother brought her a pot of chicken soup when her husband had his gallbladder taken out and that really he got more pity than he deserved, weak as he was, because afterward he sat in the recliner for a week and rang a bell that no one answered except the dog. “I’m sorry, honey,” she says again and breaks open the plastic with her thumbs the way you break a seal, as if today has a holy thing inside.

People from my childhood peer at me like naturalists at an animal that was once common and is now hardly ever spotted: Ruth Clements come home and thicker through the middle and with gray like a good idea in her hair. I make small talk. I keep count. I take a lunch of peanut butter crackers eaten over the sink. I drink water from a Solo cup as if I am hiding the fact that it is only water. The ice from last night is gone, the cooler a little creek at the bottom where the bag sits like a beached jellyfish. I fish out wet bills and lay them in a line on the counter near the toaster to dry in a pathetic little parade. I am watchful and permissive and firm and forgiving in turn, the way my mother was at Kmart when she slept a stockboy wrong. When a woman’s boy wobbles too near the curio shelf with his sticky hands and knocks a porcelain hummingbird from its perch, snapping its beak, I am surprising to myself. I hear a click in my ribs and take a breath and the boy is bracing his hands on his knees and the mother is raising her hand halfway to smack and then stopping, praise be, and I say, “No worries,” calmly, which is what people say now. I pick up the tiny beak and lay it in the saucer as if we could glue this bird back together and it would again hover in place, eternal, fragile, a thing that pretends to flight.

At three, a small storm builds the way they do on hot Ohio afternoons, the kind that arrive like a dropped curtain and leave as quickly, the smell of rain rising before the drops, the street darkening like a rumor. The wind blows through the house and bumps doors so I have to wedge a phonebook, the last phonebook on earth, into the crack to keep the bedroom door open. There are people in my father’s closet, two men muttering over a hunting knife and a belt sander and one of them squints hard and says, “Ain’t no Craftsman,” and the other says, “Don’t make no difference,” and I think a lot of things are true and nothing is, all at once. The electricity blinks and comes back. A small girl with cornrows pulls at the hem of her mother’s dress and the mother, a stranger to me who will buy the Tupperware colander with three legs like a little cow, picks her up without looking like she’s done it every day of her girl’s life, which I suppose she has. The girl puts her wet cheek on her mother’s shoulder and watches me, cautiously, as if I might be a person in a story who wants to steal secrets.

I go to my parents’ bedroom under pretense to get more bags. Truth is I want to stand once in there without anyone. There is the imprint of her on the mattress that has survived even now, two months after we found the hospice nurse had folded her hands and put a dab of balm on her lips and closed the curtains and turned off the soft light that the hospice nurse calls a “comfort,” which it is, but which is also a lie because nothing is comfortable, nothing is merciful, in the room my parents slept their whole married length in. The bureau top is polished bone. Her hairbrush still holds some hair, which I have decided not to keep because I know myself; I will keep it and then I will one day throw it away and feel a murder in me. I pick up the little bottle of Evening in Paris perfume that is older than me and uncap it and sniff and for a second I am four, pressed into her coat, smelling everything at once, her armpit and the coldness of winter and cigarettes she pretended not to smoke. God, we are always someone’s child even when no one needs us to be.

I pull the bottom drawer to find the bags and there is an envelope fallen flat beneath sweaters, cornering my hand. It is cheap paper, the kind that used to come in a pack with matching stationery, blue like a sky drawn wrong, never mind; people wrote their lives on this. My name is not on it. I am not meant for it and yet it is here. I slide the envelope along my palm and sit on the edge of the bed without thinking to wipe my damp legs first, my thigh leaving a dark comet on the percale. Daddy’s writing on it in pencil, my father’s before I knew it was a match for mine: Louise, open when you are old and we aren’t anymore. The pencil is both careful and crooked, the way you write when you are hurrying to hide and wanting someone to find you.

I am old and I am not. She is not anything now except inside me and inside Mark and inside Bev and asleep in the ground up at Saint Mary’s, a place she would have made fun of living all her life. I slide my finger under the flap like a thief and there is a single sheet with writing on both sides. He wrote when he was at Camp Lejeune, or he wrote at the plant on a lunch break, or he wrote a week after I was born and he looked at her asleep and the way the light held her and he had to say. He says it plain. He says I thought the world ended as a boy when I saw the first dead thing and then I saw you and it began again. He says the exact day he knew she would say yes was the day she didn’t laugh when he spilled hot coffee into his lap in public; the day she tied his handkerchief around his thumb when he cut himself and went on slicing tomatoes for her mother like it was nothing; the day she put her hair up in the back of the Chevy and stuck half the bobby pins in his coat pocket and he felt them jingling all winter and liked the secret of them. He says they should go west someday, Arizona or at least St. Louis, to see the river as it fattens, and she has said it’s impossible, and he has said not everything is and they have argued sweet because arguing sweet is the only way to hear one another when you are young enough to think you could lose. He says I promise you I will mean yes to you for as long as I can help it. He says Forgive me for what I will not be brave enough to do. He says When you are old and the kitchen is finally how you wanted it and I am not putting my tools there on the table I want you to go to sleep early sometimes and let someone else turn out the lights. He says Tell our kids the time I wrestled a goat and lost. He says You smell like apples. He says the dog looks at him with more god in his eyes than any man and he takes it as a sign you should get another one when this one dies. He says All my life I will carry the first sight of you down to the creek in that yellow dress you hated because it wrinkled in stripes. He puts a little drawing of the creek and the dress and the sky. He says I am yours, I am. His will is not to break.

I fold the paper back into the blue, wanting to read it loud to her ghost, wanting to knock on my own chest and ask what else is in there I have not found because I was busy being practical. I tuck it into the back pocket of my cutoffs with a sudden private greed I have rarely indulged my whole life—this is mine now, mine and no one will ask me to price it. There are rolled socks still in the drawer, thin ribbons, one bra that did not try much and did well anyway. The phonebook wedging the door drags a little when I shove it back to keep out the storm with its rebellious air, and a damp string of hair sticks to my neck again. I take two more bags with handles that cut your fingers and return to the living like a swimmer through cold.

“Ruth?” someone calls from somewhere as if we are in a park.

“I’m here,” I say, not meaning the kitchen or the world. A tall man in a shirt with breeding labs on it, glossy and ideal, stands by the piano running one key with one finger, like a boy testing trouble. He does not look like Nettleton; he looks like the highway and Columbus and clean gym floors. His right forearm is crowded with a tattoo of feathers or ferns. He says, “You the one in charge?” and I nod and I am not.

“I’ll give you a hundred to get this beauty out of your life,” he says toward the piano.

“You will not,” I say, not even knowing if I mean the price or the word give.

“I’ve got a trailer,” he says. “I know what I’m doing.” He doesn’t, but not in the way I think; he is the sort who thinks anything can be done with will.

“Two hundred and you have to bring four men,” I say stupidly because there he is the one. He looks at me like something sharp; he studies me, and I am suddenly embarrassed of my shorts and my legs with veins cracked like maps, of the sweat pooling between my breasts, of the hair I cut myself in my bathroom three months ago when it became too much in my eyes.

“I can do it,” he says and we settle at one-fifty and his brother arrives in another truck with two men, both immediately helpful, one with the gentle movements of someone who has broken too many things before. They start measuring and thinking and laying down two-by-fours I do not own. They rock the piano a hair. For a while the house vibrates with small grunts and plans and the smell of sudden effort, and then the piano crosses the sill like a ship, lists, rights itself, descends, a summer storm dark behind, a laugh from one of the men when they do not die. They are not careless, and I can bear it. The bench goes too. I feel a want rise in my throat to say, Wait, the little book with Claire de Lune, but it has been years since any of us touched the keys except when she dusted under the lid. I think maybe Mark at sixteen pressed a chopstick there to make a trick noise, maybe Bev at nine sat and ran her finger left to right just to see the action. It was not Beethoven who lived here; it was children and my mother’s patience which was both forever and never.

As they carry the piano down, the UPS truck rolls up like a bright idea and stops because he cannot get through. The driver leans out and says it will be five minutes, and I want to say, Yes, and your life will be five minutes, and the hard part will be five minutes, and love will be five minutes and the longest thing you ever know. But a woman behind him in a minivan flips her hand in the general direction of God and says loudly, “We all need to get somewhere, Todd,” and Todd calls, “We do, we do,” as if the thought is fresh.

I go back into the kitchen where sweat has beaded the melon I cut earlier and where the knife I used to cut it lies on the board, seeds sticking to its blade like punctuation. I put three pieces into my mouth one after the other and lean forward because the juice runs down my chin, just like you would not do in polite company, just like my mother would have told me. I wipe my mouth with my wrist and it leaves a tacky sweetness there that will catch dust all afternoon.

This is the way the day goes: a slow tide and then a rush, a morality tale with money and objects and me playing a detective who can identify who needs mercy and who needs a price and who when they say, “Your mother was beautiful,” really means, “I miss the women I knew when I was small,” and who when they say, “You ready to be rid of all this?” really means, “I am afraid of my own children’s faces when I will ask for help.” I take ten dollars for the toaster because the woman buying it is moving into an apartment that will smell like someone else for a month and I want her to have toast the next morning and not be lonely. I say no to the man who wants the photo albums and I am sharper with him than I meant to be. “These are not for sale,” I say, voice cold, and he says, “Of course,” quickly like you would to a person with a temper you did not know, and I am sorry then but also not sorry at all. The albums are heavy red, the kind with the sticky cellophane that ages everything, and inside are the faces that made me, that made the people who made me; inside are infant strangers in bibs with lace that seem every blink like they could be me.

Someone laughs in the front yard, a young laugh, and I turn because for a second I think I know it from 1980. Michael Mitchell—Mitch to most but Michael to me because I liked the length his name took in my mouth—stands on the grass holding Daddy’s old record player like a trophy. He is deeper in the chest, the white at his temples interesting, the boy in his cheekbones still. His wife is gone two years now and he has that look men have then, that look like a gap in a fence. He grins and then it drops to something like a question.

“Hey there, Ruthie.” Nobody calls me that but select few who can. The way he says it is a reversal: hey there, time.

“You want that old thing?” I point to the record player because I cannot point to the space between us.

He grins again like a dare. “Oh, I’ve been wanting that thing since 1979,” he says, and we both remember the afternoon we were in this living room while our parents were in the backyard and we danced slow to a record of The Association, both of us a shade too embarrassed to look at each other and the smell of grass and the way my thighs shook in time with the hum so small a dog could not hear it. The record skipped in “Never My Love” and we stood planted while our feet said a small circle on the carpet. He looked at my mouth and looked away. We did not kiss in the way that makes a story, but we did when we were twenty-two and drunk on Christmas Eve behind Schmidt’s Bakery, and then we did not again. He married Laurie and I went to Chicago and married Ray and we did not write like a novel any of it down.

“It’s yours,” I say, too fast, like I am giving him the past free. He gives me forty and I say twenty and then he puts twenty back and tells me that is not how this ends. He says, “Your mama scared me,” and I say, “She scared me, too,” and we both laugh the way you laugh at a funeral when your body is confused. He puts the player gently onto the passenger seat of his truck and calls, “I’ll bring it back to you if it breaks,” and I say, “You won’t,” and wave my hand.

It is five by the time the last of the big things leave. The recliner goes on a tall man’s head like a ridiculous turban. The dining table, leaf slipped in, edges scarred by hundreds of homework scribbles and one ugly cigarette burn from Aunt June that my mother laid a doily over and pretended did not exist, rides out to a minivan with its hatch ratcheted down with a bungee like a bargain. The bookcase goes and a woman runs her finger over the dusty rectangle where Daddy’s copy of For Whom the Bell Tolls sat for fifty years and says, “I love that book,” and I nod and do not tell her it was the one he threw at the wall when he was angry the summer he was out of work. The iron bed from the spare room goes to a college girl whose father is the man who cut my hair in fifth grade and made me look like a boy. He says, “This will be in her first apartment.” It makes me want to cry, the idea of the bed breathing in a room with a new ceiling.

When the storm passes it leaves a varnish on everything. The men who have moved the piano lean on their trucks like colts after a race, foaming a little. They are good to each other in that wordless way of men, and I love them for it and hate them for it because it seems I never was granted that beautifully simple grammar. A robin stands in the wet like a coin. A train throws its voice from the tiny triangle of blue in the south where the tracks cut right. The highway is further away, humming. The UPS truck is gone and the mailbox holds a glossy flyer for mattresses, which seems unkind.

In the near-empty living room, I sit on the floor with my back to the wall and my legs long out and count the cash and do not count the cost. There is a fine stripe of dust along the baseboard where the couch lived so long I cannot remember the wall behind it as a color instead of an absence. The house feels echoy fat. My voice, when I say, “I am here, Mom,” bounces back to me not in words but in what a child hears in the womb, a boom, a bassy comfort that means not language but heartbeat. I am no one’s child just now as far as the paperwork goes. I am forty-six and I think, no, fifty-six, dear God, like a riddle you get wrong every time until suddenly one day you cannot make the answer come out another way.

In the kitchen the recipe box sits where I put it out of sight, pushed behind the mixer my sister might want but has not asked for. The box is red wood with a hinged lid with a horseshoe stamped into it. Inside are the stacks and stacks of index cards, my mother’s and other women’s, the fudge and the pickles, the Italian meringue copy from a magazine she cut and glued to a card and never made because it was fussy and flopped in humidity like ours. Her handwriting is a map of will: the long loops, the refusal to waste space, the little drawing of a chicken scratched to indicate you could substitute chicken for tuna in the casserole if you had it. There is the recipe for apple cake that is just apples and flour and sugar and miracle. There are Burns, Dot, Marnie in other scrawls, neighbors who are not here anymore, neighbors gone to Florida or the next town or the next world. I take the box and put it next to the envelope with my father’s letter in my bag, which is more honest a purse than any purse ever was. I will keep this, I tell the air, I will not give this price. Tell me, Mom, I say, tell me how you wrote this sentence about large eggs and they will become small one day, tell me how you wrote about the temperature on a card when you kept everything else in your head, tell me why you put a star next to the molasses cookies. Who did you love when you made those? Who was here? Which afternoons did that smell pull someone home on purpose from the street?

By six, the last person lingers. She is twenty maybe, a little unsure, a fine line of sweat on her upper lip like a boy’s mustache, shoulder blades sharp under her T-shirt. She holds a jar of buttons as if she has been waiting all day for this and says, “Will you take five?” There are five thousand buttons in that jar, a life of clothes. I can see the square black one that died on my winter coat in twelfth grade and I sewed it back on with thread I licked to get through the needle; I can see the pearl that fell from the cuff on my confirmation dress before we had our picture. Five is nothing. Five is a miracle. I take her five and close my hand around it as if it were the warmest party favor I have ever been given and she says, “I make things,” and I say, “I hope you do.” She hugs the jar to her chest. She leaves and the house goes quiet in that very particular way of houses when they realize finally it is only you.

I walk through the rooms, a ritual I have given myself permission for. The front bedroom, which was mine and then Bev’s and then the sewing room when we were all gone, is bare except the marks on the wall where a dresser leaned, the holes where the curtain rod was, the faint outline where a mirror hung and kept the paint from fading around it. In the closet are the hangers—thirty metal, a few padded with blue satin that now looks obscene in its wrongness, two with paper like church bulletin that crackles. I run my hand along them and they sing a very thin music. In the bathroom the mirror is spattered but I can only see myself in slices, my mouth, my ear, the line from my nose to my lip. I switch on the light, the same light my mother had, a fixture with glass like the tops of creamers, and there she is in that slice, my mother in me, old and young at once, wearing my sweat and my father’s eyes.

On the back stoop I stand because it is what we did our whole lives, stand there and look at the square of yard that is a kingdom if you are a child and a prison if you are not, the fence to the Petersons’, the neighbor’s rhubarb turning monstrous, the line of hostas that thrive on apparently nothing, the marigolds stubby and upright, enough. The pegs from the clothesline are in their little bag with a drawstring, and I could cry from the sweetness of their little bag, the way my mother would not let them be rattled in a coffee can like the rest of the world did. “Those go missing,” she said and she was right; everything does. I leave the bag hanging on the hook, because I am not Jesus and I cannot carry away all things for all people.

A last car slows and a man leans out and says, “You closing?” and I wave and say yes, and he sighs like a story that has ended before it began. It has started to be the hour of supper even for people whose mothers do not live to call them in. Pete’s back light pops on with a little sound that is pretend thunder. The sky has turned into something gentle and mowed; it is like someone pushed the world through a colander and it came out homogenous and fine. The air smells like black dirt and like ozone and like fried onions from somewhere far. A dog is making circles at the end of its lead. It is all right and it isn’t and it is.

In the living room with its floor worn smooth where the couch sat, I lie down on the old rug that will stay because no one wanted it and because it is hair-thin and because I want to lie on the house like I could be the thing that keeps it from floating off. I can feel the boards under the weave of flowers gone colorless from sun. I press my ear to it because somebody told me once that you can hear the house when it settles. What I hear is my own blood. What I hear are cars on 224 at the edge of town and a crow with a grievance going to bed in the maple and two girls out the front window laughing low and the little creak of the hall if anyone, if anyone, were to walk there. The front door is open to the screen and through it I can see the top of the crabapple with the scar and the slice of sky a color I cannot name without sounding like a person who ruins things by naming them. Bugs make a high fizz that does not belong to the front of the day and now does. The light we get only the one way, the long lay of it through the west window, makes a bar across the rug, and my arm in it looks young where the light hits and old where it doesn’t.

I could say goodbye. I could perform it like a citizenship test, showing what I know of our history. Instead I swallow and it goes nowhere but inside. I go to the front door finally because mosquitoes are coming with their persistence, because the day is not mine to prolong. I stand there a second more, just long enough to believe that on another day in another year on this clockless porch my mother might growl, “Oh for mercy’s sake,” and turn off the light and shoo me in. I draw the bolt because it is habit and then unbolt it because in the morning I will come back with the trash bags and the soft cloth and the box for the things left that I think Bev will want even if she tells me honestly she will not. I turn the kitchen light off so the room is blue. I pick up the one earring I find by the baseboard, a tiny gold teardrop no one in the family wore that I remember, which means my mother bought it for two dollars at a rummage and put it in her ear one afternoon and did not mind that its partner had gone. I put it in my pocket with the letter and, because her body taught mine how to carry, I am smaller and larger at once.

I step outside and the heat has broken and the sky is a pearl thing. The yard hums like it is saying its prayers. From a block away someone says a single name that is a warning and a love; a child runs; a screen door slaps and out there a mother or a father says a thing ordinary and, today, that matters. The strangers who were my people for the day have all carried away their adopted history and will be telling each other tonight, “I got this at a little sale,” and that will be true, and this is the truth too: that here everything has happened to someone. I get into my car and I sit. I do not start it. I put my forehead on the steering wheel and it presses a little crescent into me, a memory. Then I start it and the car says okay. I go to the end of Maple and look left and look right as if there are two directions and they might be equal. The light down 224 is green. The other way is red. I put on my signal and for a second I cannot remember which one I am meant to be, which way backs me up and which way carries me forward. I breathe. The arrow blinks on the dash. The sky will darken and then it won’t and then it will again. I pull out, and the house behind me holds nothing and everything, and if I turn I will not be surprised to see my mother at the window, not waving, reasonable, as if she knew all along that every good door swings both ways.
