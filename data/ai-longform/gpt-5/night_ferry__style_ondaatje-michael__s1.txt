The ferry had a name that was a sentence to itself, a soft brag about endurance, but no one in the line used it. They said simply the boat, and the booth attendant in her red hat wrote my ticket by hand and slid it under the glass without looking up. Behind her were a calendar of caribou in early light and a photograph of a rusted anchor hauled up from some shoal, its flukes welded with barnacle and mussel. I could hear, above the conversations and rattling thermoses and the stridulations of a dog that had been left briefly in a cab, the slow firing of the ship’s engines as if the belly of it were practicing a heavy breath.

You learn a tone of waiting in these northern places, a scale of slownesses, a way to rest with the luggage of time. The building had an uneven temperature, cold fingers, warm ankles, and when someone opened the outer doors the fog swept in with a taste you could almost salt. Men with belts that had more function than I possessed in my whole luggage moved from vehicle to vehicle, chalking a letter on windshields, raising a forefinger like a conductor when it was time to move. I took a tea that had come already sweetened from a girl who had no more than a glance for my idea of a joke. On the wall, beside a secondhand vending machine for postcards of snowstorms and a lighthouse in a storm that had long ago been dismantled, there was the familiar display board of what it called Community Concerns—a cluster of paper faces in their disappearing.

You don’t usually look too long. The posters keep their own private weather. But there was a boy of perhaps nineteen, his hair shaved to the point where his head resembled a plum, and a woman who wore a necklace of orange glass and the sort of hard smile you have when you are staring into the pale sun of a camera. And there was a paper no larger than an envelope that had slipped askew and been pinned again, a photocopy of a photograph two decades old. The face in it was my brother’s, except that in those days his face had still been unfinal. He wore my jean jacket, our father’s old scarf with the blue flecks, and his mouth was half open, as if he would say something if he were certain he wouldn’t be heard.

All of this was only the outer work. In my pocket I had the other script: a printout from a constable whose name, when spoken, had the scraped roughness of a wet match. Your contact is me, it said, and the name of the island clinic was a clear word as stone. He had attached a map from the internet and then drawn a rectangle on it in ink, an older map pinning the newer. Within the rectangle he had written The Morgue—a room marked the way a child marks a tree that has a bee’s nest in it.

I waited with the other bodies and machines in the long barn. It was a winter month with the previous season crowding the sky. That was what the weather did here. Everything else was layered, too. I had not seen my brother in eight years. There had been a letter four years before that, a bright page that said I’m fine don’t be always thinking, but with his left-handed loops and small jokes about people who had never existed. I folded it and put it into the book it had come with, which was about the ships that the wooden men used on the lakes. And later, when I moved, I forgot the book. But at every crossing after that I found myself counting the men on deck or the hands on the rails in the cold, that way we count because we have lost something that hasn’t yet agreed to go.

They hired us on in our youth for roofing, for painting water towers, for anything that required a heartless respect for height, and my brother saved every cheque in a biscuit tin because he was, like our father, leaning towards an exactness of thrift. He would say we need only a table and two chairs and three good pans, and then he would buy a knife with a blade like a line of coast.

When the clerk called my car’s letter, I walked without hurry to the ramp. The world leaned to accommodate the difference in levels and I faced, as we do at every threshold, the old fantasies of turning around, of walking away from the building that has put its hand to your back. The steel slope flexed a degree under my weight. I stepped onto the deck, thinly painted green so the salt stains could pretend to be on purpose.

On the upper levels windows held cigarette ghosts. The solarium, with its heat lamps and spindly potted plant that was some kind of desperate fern, was roped off. I found the stair that would take me to the soft center of the ship, and the smell of diesel and eggs and cheap coffee pointed like a compass. On a good crossing you can look down into that open, rowdy cafeteria and see on the flagstones of linoleum a map larger and less convincing than any you have on your phone. Years ago, when we made this trip, my brother and I tried to rename the bays we passed without seeing them. He called them after animals we had never seen. I called them after stories we had read when we were supposed to sleep.

I had booked one of the rooms that had no porthole and no view, as if practice for the morgue. I did not sleep in such places; sleep did not arrange itself as a gift. But there was a quietness in a room that had only its own breath. The keycard made that small consenting sound and the brown door opened to a world of curved edges. A bed with its corners tucked as if to take a rap on the knuckles, a shelf barely sufficient for the one book I had brought. There were two metal hooks that had been painted white in some other decade and were now trying to shrug off the white paint the way a halo wants to shrug off a saint. The shower stall had a plastic curtain with bubbles where you could look and see no distortion.

To summon a lighthouse out of a bell takes a bell ringer, and on ships the bell is a hum instead. I sat, I stood, I stepped into and out of the small space, I lay down and placed my hand under the pillow where there was nothing but the idea of old hair. I counted the number of times the hull shifted its diction and found a number too large to hold. Above me a child ran in a frantic or joyous path that was its own logic. I was carrying a suit jacket that still smelled of a meeting room. I took it out and spread it on the spare bunk, and I put the printout in the pocket, a permission I did not require. Then I went out again to the deck.

It would be wrong to say night had come; this far north there is simply less day. But on water that is an advantage; you can see the lit points on the opposite shore, the place that you are going to give your body over to, the small and distantly stitched communities that have a wheezing truck on every street and a way of arranging barrels that is a code. The ferry’s horn lifted once and with that the last of those still ashore turned around. I could make out, on the metal curve outside the passenger lounge, the line of faces like a run of fish beneath a weir. I walked toward the stern and touched with the tips of my fingers those green ropes that were thicker than any wrist I had loved. You could not move them alone; they demanded a chorus.

There was a man standing near the lifeboat with his gloves off. He was inspecting his hands the way men look at their boots in winter to see if the laces are fraying. He had the calm of someone who had done a thing too long; on such a night the ferry itself will take care of you. He nodded to me. I nodded. We stood like two beings who have found the same page in the same long book. The ship gave its first proper shudder and the lines were loosed. There is always that obedient, theatrical slow backing away from the concrete, the slim edgeless water that opens as if a zipper has been undone. After that the river of it begins, the strait narrows or widens and the weather’s new plan is proposed.

I went inside and found, at one of those tables lacquered to an eternal cleanliness, a small woman in a fur hat examining a carton of maps the way a conductor compares a complicated score to a remembered version of it. The stripes on the maps were the lined guts of the land. Maybe she was going to a place where she would finally be blessed by coordinates. Beside the salt shaker was a packet of seeds. No one reads properly on a moving ship. You read the other people instead, as they try to care for their children or their shyness, as they try to hold a cup steady as the floor now and then lets go of its idea of being the floor. There was a hockey game on a mounted television, the sound off. The men at the counter were yelling and laughing in a dialect that had its own tides.

I had in my head, miles away, a quiet room with its white tile and its trays that had a clanging in them even now. The plan was this, though I could not tell if I had fashioned it or it had been given to me: in the morning, or what in winter is morning, there would be a small municipal car with orange lights. There would be a man with a hat that could also have been worn three countries ago. There would be, when we reached the clinic, a form to fill and a form to sign. He would ask Did you bring anything? and I would say Only the idea of him, and we would smile in the partial light of not fully lying.

When my brother and I were boys and then men who were still boys in certain key aspects, we lived in a house in the bay with more staircases than necessary. That was our mother’s doing. She believed that you should always be able to go up, and always be able to come down by a different route. If I run a hand along a banister in any hotel in any place I could travel to, I am back on the fourth riser where there was a dent shaped like a heel. We would descend at night as if we were thieves; we would ascend during the day as if we were kings returning from a hunt, though there were no things to hunt there but each other’s retreating backs.

Our father had a teacher’s hands and, in summer, work that was raw and hard. He studied water; he was a technician of currents. He would take us out in a dinghy with a spool of line like a spool of a century and draw with it the hidden road of the tide. Your body will float here, he would say, if you put it in right there. He had the gift of making the sentence awful and entirely practical at once. He showed us that if you dropped a piece of bread just here, it would be in our neighbor’s harbor before the gulls had decided. He taught us that the ice in the river is a second city and that you never step on it without some obligation to the other citizens.

He was polite to our mother in the way that the serious are when they love the frivolous. She kept, behind the basement fridge, a crate filled with pale pearls that were not pearls at all. Glass, she said, or white shells brought inland because someone loved the shape. Every second weekend we were sent with a jar to fill in the marsh and we would come back with water that was older than us. My brother was the one who made a stove out of a tin box and a coil and cooked eggs that had the taste of an electrical accident. He was the one who would walk too far into a storm because storms then were to him sentences that might have his name in them.

He had that small white scar on his upper lip where, when we were eleven and ten, he put his mouth to the radiator because he wanted to understand heat. He had the moon of a burn on his shoulder from a welding rod when we were eighteen. And later, when a piece of steel fell on his foot because the other men that day were hungover and because he would take on what was not his to take on, he lost the tip of the second toe. These are the tidemarks on a body that will, later, have to show itself. You think and think and parade these signs in your head when sleep can’t come, so that if someone were to ask you on a ship in the dark What would make you fall to your knees you have the inventory ready. He had the tattoo that was supposed to be a swallow and looked, even drunk, like a smudge. He had, if he had kept it, a small chipped tooth, a darkened half moon in the left eye from a fight with a boy named Allan, and the habit of turning his ring twice on the ring finger of his right hand because he refused that ring to that other finger as if that were a defense that would stand up in court.

We were not good with the law. We were decent with gentleness and we did not, either of us, make a sport of cruelty. We fished without hooks sometimes because we could not bear the idea that the hooked and living were ours. But we had that unasked-for belligerence that comes when your father leaves and then returns, leaves and then returns. Only once we saw him cry. That was the year the marsh filled with ice in the shape of old window panes, and my brother pushed his bike onto it and then was in the black of it and came up with his mouth making an O as if the world were a trumpet. We were foolish and brave in turns. The priest said they were the same. When my brother was twenty-seven he called a woman he had once loved and made a speech that I have never heard. She came and took his books into her car in the way of someone who can be a friend to your need but not to you.

Some of this I tell in the way we tell these things when we are on a moving craft and our voice must adjust the way our feet do. The ferry had, by now, caught the rhythm of the sea—or is it the sea that takes the ferry’s gait? In any case the tables and everyone at them had developed a small preemptive sway that meant no spilt drinks for a while. I walked down the hall that had the posters for places the ferry did not go. The carpet was stained with a name that had been changed. On these linbrinked walls there are photographs of the ship when it was younger; there are photographs of women and men in navy jackets leaning into an old wind. You think you see in their faces the thing that has just happened to you, but you see only your own reflection.

I went to the stern again. The cold was more definite, the way the veteran is more exact than the recruit. The paint on the rail had bubbled back and re-cohered so often that it was a palimpsest. There was that sound of a thin chain moving slowly, and now and then the suggestion—impossible, but you feel it in your soup—that the ship was not going forward at all but had stalled against something that was invisible and definite. I stood with my hands in my jacket. I thought of a night with my brother when we swam in the quarry pool and climbed naked onto the raft and lay there looking up at the cathedral of factory that had made once a brave steam before it died. It was not bravery to be naked there; it was a way of telling the walls that they had no power. A man came down to the water and sat and smoked and watched us and we felt his eyes like a legislative thing so we turned on our stomachs and lay that way until he left. Back in the truck we did not speak. He drove with the window open and his hand making the small circle that keeps old cars paid attention to.

Estranged is the word people use when they do not wish to know the story or are too kind to want to hear a violent noun. My brother left because there was no longer a way for us to be in a room without the old scaffolds of our lives wanting to be carried in. He left because I wrote to our mother and suggested the sale of the house while he was living in it. He left because I did the thing I had, in my youth, promised myself not to become—someone who writes in careful, undamaging sentences what is likely to hurt. He left because his temper answered my temper and neither would stand and watch the other pretend to extemporize. He left because men of a certain tenderness can be terrible in competition. He left finally because he found, in the north, a job that required quiet hands and the following of a line through snow, and this appealed to the honest core of him. There were rumors that he worked in a fish plant, that he worked as a runner for men that needed things that cannot be found on a shelf, that he worked on the power line, four weeks on and two off, and that once, at two in the morning, in a bar with chairs upturned and a floor meant to be mopped at dawn, he lay down under the pool table and slept, and no one, in that place, thought that unusual.

It is possible that the body was not a body but a misunderstanding. It is possible that the body was a man whose face had been changed by water, and that there was, in him, no brother of mine at all. It is possible that, by morning, there would be at the terminal a dog that found his owner at last and made the noises that we know to be love. It is possible that the man in the fur hat was, in daylight, an old woman who would speak to me and tell me a number called an isobath. I stood at the rail and tasted the metal of it the way a child does, putting my mouth to the cold until my lips stuck. The night was not yet midnight by a clock but was midnight by agreement.

I have said that I brought one book. It was an old book about the river I grew up near, written by a minister who put on a raincoat and walked everywhere in it. In his photographs the boys wear hats with ears and the women wear boots. He is resolutely plain. He makes a joke about the ways the river’s name is mispronounced. He lists, with justice and kindness, the occasions when a body went in and did not come back and then, without malice, the occasions when a body did return. When my brother went off to work with men who carried the shape of a line from river to mountain, he left behind the book that had arrived with his four-year letter. I took what was mine and then I took a book that wasn’t. It is like with siblings: you keep them without a theory of property until the world demands a receipt.

In the cafeteria I bought a sandwich that had no history. There was a man, different from the one with the lifeboat hands, reading aloud to his daughter from a brochure about whales, making the whale noises with an admirable lack of shyness. In the corners of the ceiling old bunting had gone pale. I sat at the window that had salt on it in a geometry the way frost designs itself and then forgets later what it has designed. I would have telephoned if there had been a person to bear that. There was no one. The woman I had, year by year, allowed into the space of me that I had kept as foreign territory had gone with a politeness that did not quarrel with me. She took the lamp we had bought in the Montreal market, the one with the tassel, and the three bowls from her grandmother, and the photograph in which I am trying to kiss her and she is looking at me with something like laughter and sorrow at the same time. My brother called her once a songbird and a reliable liar, which he intended as a compliment. He wrote to me on a postcard he bought in a gas station, a photograph of a moose and a quip about the moose’s opinion of our capacity for delight.

You do not, in my place, sleep. Sleep is given to men who have done their day’s work or have managed to forgive themselves for not doing it. I went to the room. I lay down. I got up and went again to the deck. I put my hand on the steel, which was cold then, and in the morning would be warmer than the air. The stars had withdrawn. There was the domestic breath of the ship and, far away, the possible red blink of some other moving thing. In the corner of the deck a man and a woman were standing too close and leaning too far apart and I removed myself because you do not want to interrupt vows that have not yet been formulated.

In the sleep that did come, the brief sleep of a thief, I was in the marsh at night and my brother was folding a line over his arm and standing with his back to me, the way when he wanted to refuse he would turn without turning. He wore a hat that had been our father’s. He looked like our father not in the face but in the arranged mildness of his stance. He had a thing in his left hand that might have been a bag or a dead bird, and he put it down in the lid of a barrel. Then the barrel was a small boat and he was pushing it into water and saying to it the things you say to a boat when there is no one to satisfy with your words. He stepped into the boat and was startled to find the boat obliged. He moved down the creek and out into the larger water. Everything was a shade I had no name for. The reeds were the kind of black that your eye takes an hour to find any goodness in.

I woke when the ship changed its voice, when it lowered itself a fraction and then rose as if to test a binding. On the wall there was the notice laminated to paper that detailed what one should do if one saw a fire, in case, like the God that used to be everywhere, fire should present itself in the metal world. I was in my clothes, without shoes. The digital clock on the wall was an arrangement of red lines that had drawn themselves into a false mathematics.

I put on my shoes and the coat. The corridor had a sound like the emptying of a church after some news had broken over it. No matter what the salt and wind do each crossing to our human surfaces, there is always the gift of seeing other people asleep in plastic chairs, the boots splayed, the head at an angle that would, in any other setting, be an emergency. I stood at the door with the rubber edge and looked out. The lights of the island were drawn like the stitches on a wound that someone had made an effort with. The island was less an island than a promise on a postcard. I stood there a moment too long and a young hand heaved the door and I stepped back and he went through and glanced at me as if I were the door and my job was to stay open or get out of the way.

Later, after the docking, after the cars exhaled and the ferry emptied, I moved down the ramp with the small line of foot passengers. No one spoke except the woman in the fur hat, whose companion said I told you it was this cold and she said But look how beautiful which can be a safety device. There was a man holding a sign with a hospital symbol and the constable’s name in a bold I recognized. It is odd and then less odd than you expect to see your own name drawn by another’s hand. He stood like men who have learned to stand in the cold with the same patience they bring to standing in a hallway while a door is opened slower than any decent man would open it. He saw me looking and raised the sign a margin and I nodded. He had a car that had the ghost of a siren and he drove with two hands and country between them on the wheel.

I sat in the front because I have never seen the advantage in having a stranger in charge of me and not asking, at least, for the small comfort of a human face. He said, with the old courtesy and some relief as if he were glad to be around wind that did not argue, You came a long way. I gave him a distance in hours. He was younger than I had expected. He had that thin moustache the police used to fancy in places that have a fondness for old television. His hair was winter hair. I had learned in my life to admire men who had found work to do in their own community without either condescension to or desperation from it. He had the way of opening his mouth to speak and then letting the sentence move ahead of him a pace.

He said, When we get there, we don’t have to go right in. And I thought, You do this more than you wish to. We passed a building made of those trained concrete blocks. We passed a store whose sign was older than itself. We passed a fish plant that looked like a man sleeping as he has slept every afternoon of his life. And there was the metal shape of the clinic and there was the slant of snow in the parking lot and the way that wind in such places moves not as a weather but as a local citizen that knows what car to open and what door to close.

He parked and left the car running with one courtesy light burning and then turned it off so as not to be a liar. He said, It will only take a moment. Inside there was the woman who had been the nurse in that place since forever, or so the clerk in the ferry terminal would have said. She was tall and had the air of someone who could, in an emergency, laugh. She took my coat and I let her take it as if we were in a museum. She did not ask me to sit. She said, in the voice you use to a creature bent with making of every phrase a question, Would you like a tea? and I said Yes as if the world had offered itself up to me in a way I could not refuse.

The constable went into the other room with the door that had a metal plate for the shoes to slide against. He said a name to someone. Someone else, off to my other side, coughed. In these rooms there are always men with coughs that are not the reason they are here. He returned with the doctor, a man in that good brown sweater that suggested a woman with care at home. He had the cardiganing of an academic, and hands that had touched more than paper. He said my first name as if to make of it a grace. I thanked him with an old nod.

We drank our tea. The nurse asked me if I would like sugar, and I said No and then later, after I had tasted, I said Yes and she smiled without thinking of it because here men learn, belated, to ask for the sweetness they cannot manufacture. The constable was watching the window in case an event should pass. The doctor asked me whether I had traveled with a coat, as if that mattered, and I told him my small story of the woman in the ferry terminal who did not keep her laughter safely hidden. He made that small sound the sympathetic make and put his cup down softly as if not to wake a child in an upstairs room.

There is the walk when it comes. It is a thin corridor with doors that will never be opened in our presence. The only light is a friendly white. The breath in such places holds itself as if even the exhaled must be respectful. It is cold in the room where the table is, but less cold than the earth is when you have to pull potatoes out of it in September. There is a blue sheet. There is a stainless steel that has made its peace with hands. And there is a window that no one remembers in summer, when we are adventurers and leave the town for weeks, but in winter becomes the only idea of outside for the lonely public of patients who cannot walk to the door and insist on looking out, and for the doctor, whose face remains in a register that is not indifferent but not amplifying either, like a streetlamp that does not flicker even when a fox passes.

You will be asked if you are ready. It is an old sentence and it asks you to consent to the thing you have refused you would ever have to do. The doctor will not hurry. The nurse will fold her hands in front of the spare slope of her. The constable will be already in the future, writing down a thing that needs to be written down. You will notice first the parts of the human you had forgotten. You will notice the ear, how like a sea creature it is, how original and never again it is. You will notice that the fingers are longer than you had planned them to be, and that the nails bear the marks of a day on a different boat maybe, how a nail has been blackened by an accident you told no one about. You will notice, too, that the world of hair is a world of its own, that it makes patterns you did not give it permission to make, that it holds the scent of the last place the head was happy.

The sheet is pulled back with the expertise that comes when doing something a hundred times is what saves you. And then the face. You would think that the face would present the answer and let you go home. But faces, in these circumstances, are cousins to themselves. We make of the people we love an image that is several images in conversation. We add to their mouth a laughter they do not possess; we add to their eyes a history they cannot be properly carrying. We make of them a man we can keep loving. And then when a body is returned the body does not know this myth and has done, in water, what water does, which is to rub out some of the punctuation and leave the sentence with too much breath and then too little.

I have a quiet, deliberately unhelpful habit at crisis: I look for the small known fact. I stepped, with the doctor watching me and the nurse, I could feel, making ready to catch me if my knees were to display their intention to break. I stood where no one else had stood. The mouth was not his, and then it was his. The left eyelid had that mistake of a freckle. The hair at the temple had turned that grey our father had had at the same age, and that felt like a decision, though another man could have had that head at that angle and you would not think it an omen. I lifted, without permission, the sheet until I could see the shoulder with its arc of white. The burn had that precise, patient curve. The toe on the right foot had its missing tip. And the small swallow on the left forearm, that we so often made fun of in our young cruelty and our later compassion—there it was, flown nowhere at all.

You would say this had made the thing certain. But doubt is a muscle, and when you exercise it for years because you are trying not to hate yourself for what you have done, it grows and keeps the door from closing with its brief, strong arm. There was, at the base of the throat, a scar I did not recognize. It could have been made by that white hope men have of taking the hair away for luck. It could have been made by a life somewhere else. There was, in the pocket of the blue jacket that was not his—a jacket that had a brand name it was his humor to never have worn—the ticket stub from a movie I had not seen but which, when I later read about it, had in it a scene of a man held under water by men he had not hurt. There was a button missing from the sleeve, and my brother, careful to a fault, never lost a thing unless it had been taken from him.

I do not believe I wept then. That came after, later, in a rented room with sheets that had no iron in them. I was too busy, in that sacred, hideous moment, offering to the little gods of rational evidence the things I had stored for their use. I nodded to the doctor. He placed the sheet back over the head. The nurse placed, like an apology, her hand on my sleeve and then let it be. The constable folded the paper he had been holding and put it into the pocket inside his coat and then took it out again because he would need to give it to me.

We sat in the small room with its winter plants and its lamp that had been made out of a section of a boat oar. The nurse brought the clipboard. You will sign where men have signed, where women have signed, where sons and sisters and the occasional neighbor have made their small little marks. You will write the name in the careful letter you learned from an old woman over a table of slate, and you will think how you have seen that name written in pencil on the inside of a book cover and in the curve of a drift in June and in the frost at the corner of a window when you were small. You sign and you are something in the world again that you did not want to be.

The doctor, with a voice he has honed to this, will tell you things you will not remember. There will have been some time in the water, he will say, and there will have been a strike to the head at some moment. He will say, It is often difficult to know. And you will want to say, That is our entire life. He will speak of men who saw a shape in the water off the breakwater, who went out without ceremony and brought it in, who did the new world’s version of old rituals that used to belong to the old world and still do when no one is watching. He will tell you the weather for the morning. He will tell you he is sorry as if the word, given often, might acquire weight and be placed on a limb that is bleeding.

I left the other rooms, and the two men who were reading the paper at the end of the corridor, and we went back to the parking lot. The car had a thin creating of frost on its rear window and he used the edge of his glove to write the first letter of some word and then rubbed it away. He drove as if the road were a drawer that had not been opened in years. In such a town you know every house even if you have never lived there. He pointed, without comment, at the school where the wind was playing with the tetherball as if it were an old pilgrim. He turned off the argument of the wipers. The lights from the ferry were still standing in a line out there and moving, like the first notation of a piece written by a young composer, small and precise.

Do you have someone, he asked, to be with? He did not ask in the way some men do what they have no business with. He asked in the way of someone who knows that people go back to rooms with a kettle and a bed that still belongs to someone else. I told him No, and he nodded, and he said, If you’d like, there is a place, and he named it. The Bell and Compass. It had a sign in the shape of an oysterman’s knife. It was too early to drink. It is always the right time to be in a room with the innocents who work in kitchens when there is nothing else to do.

I did not go. He took me to the small inn beside the co-op where the woman at the desk had hair that had decided to be in its own weather. She gave me the room key as if it would answer me back if I spoke to it. She said the water would run rusty and then clear. She said the heater has its own mind. She said, if there’s anything. The constable said his name again and told me not to hesitate. He gave me his card because little white rectangles are the boats we push into the water between us and what is asked. He shook my hand. It was the shake of someone who believes you can leave pain briefly and then come back to it fresher.

In the room I turned on the heater and stood with my back to it like a pilgrim to a hearth. I set the kettle on its base and stared in an honest way at its unrendered silver. I looked at the chair and I looked at the bed. Out the window there was the wide plain of the bay like a field that had decided to try at being a mirror. In the kitchen through the wall a radio was listing the prices of potatoes in the language of the river. I took my book and could not open it because the spine would not agree with my hands in different weather. I sat. Later, without intention, I lay on the bed and closed my eyes and saw the green rope swing we tied to the spindly pine that year and the way my brother went out on it over the marsh as if across a vowel of a colossal word. I saw the night we tried to set the record for being under water. I saw him put his head into the crook of my elbow and laugh at the thing we had not managed to say.

Grief is the wildest proof that we have been men of some engagement. It is also the proof of our patience. It arrives like a cat you did not invite and refuses to leave and then one day is gone and you find, in a room that has kept its clean heat, a small grey hair on the black of your jacket and you know you will still find them later on mornings you thought were meant to be something else. I lay on the bed and kept my shoes on.

He did say from time to time that he would rather have been an inventor of small machines. He would build a device that would make the pullover of a skiff against a wharf silent. He would build a contraption to lower and raise the casket straps so that no man had to put his hand into the verge between this world and that in a clumsy way. And once he said, Fences should be listened to. And from then on he carried, on the hillside in summer, a small roll of wire and a pair of pliers and determined that he would tune fence lines to a harmony. I went with him once, the way friends go with the man they love in order to throw him a rope. He plucked them. He tightened them. He loosened them. He found the one that sang. What shall we call this, I asked. A E string, he said, and laughed. Somewhere in that theory was the idea that we could go through the world and not bruise against it so hard.

I slept into midday, my body taking at last the chance it had been holding not as greed but as salvaging. When I woke it was to the radiator’s dry clatter, not ungrateful, and the radio now was the story about the man whose barn had burned down, who said I was going to do something with that wood anyhow. I went down and touched the stamp of the floor. The woman at the desk had a bowl with wrapped mints. She asked how things had gone and I said as if we were two thimble brains in a school play, He had a good name, and she said, That’s everything, and we both knew we were speaking nonsense that had its own beauty, like a courtesy in winter.

At the co-op I bought the sort of syrup-soaked pastry my brother had loved and then refused because he believed we should be thinner than our fathers. I bought a pair of thick socks I did not require. At the back there was a rack with local notices. There was a poster for a dance next week and one for a band that had the name of a weather system and one for a man who would fix your boat motor and one for a dog with a face that wanted to be anyone. There was, too, a notice typed on a typewriter, that old machine that is still better than us at not forgetting. Needed Immediately: men for ice. You stand on the ice with a pole and your life and you watch. You go out in the morning and you watch and you mark the places where it will break and you come home in the afternoon. You are paid cash.

It must have been like that in him, I thought. He had stood somewhere with his pole and he had watched, and he had made his marks on some clipboard, and he had seen a thing in the distance and he had not wanted it to come and he had not wanted it not to come. He had probably turned ring on finger. He had probably the taste of Marseilles soap from the morning when he had set the bar at the back of the enamel tub. He had likely smelled the iron in the air the way our father had taught us you can when you mean to. And then he had fallen, or been pulled, or had stepped as if it were the step onto the raft at the quarry. The only smart thing to do, our father had said once, is to step where you know. And the only brave thing is to step where you don’t.

It would be a lie to say that, later that afternoon, I did not go to the Bell and Compass. I went. It had the low ceiling of rooms built to make warmth stay. There were six men and a woman and a boy with a lemonade. The hockey game was back though it was another game. The bartender wore a knitted hat as if the hat had forgotten it was wearing a head. He poured me something I did not know I was ordering. I said to him, in that way grief has of asking an irrelevant stranger to stand in for a person you have lost, Do you know a man named— and I said my brother’s name. He said, without looking at me, He came in sometimes, a man who called himself by that, and I thought it was a good joke and then I saw it was not. He said, He paid his bill. He said, He told good stories. I said, like what. He said, like how the only cigarettes that worked in a storm were the ones you lit in a man's cupped hands, like how the geese in the marsh had their own surnames, like how if you stand in a place too long it stands back at you and one of you has to move.

What else did he say, I asked. He was careful, the man with the drink. He was careful for me the way you are careful with a boiled egg, so it will not split. He said, He never said where the friends were. I didn't need him to. And then, after a while that was a while to me and a blinking to him, he said, He said you were the one who knew the names of the months. He said you would know the right time. He said there is always a brother who keeps their mother’s calendar in his head. I held the glass and then I put it down and then I thought how strange it is that a man’s life can be partially reflected in a line of clean glasses turned upside down above a bar.

When I left and the air was again the air I had breathed at midnight on the upper deck, the sun was at that angle that makes small things look like statements. The snow plow at the edge of the parking lot. The black boot someone had dropped in September. The lifebuoy nailed to the post though there was no water for a mile. I stood there like an actor who has lost his line and waits for the prompter. There was no prompter. There was, up the street, a church, and its door was open, and a man with a hammer was taking down the board that had had on it the times for weddings. I went inside as one does not necessarily because one has anything to say to the god but because the bench there knows how to take your weight.

It was warmer in that old room than it was outside. There were three women arranging paper flowers at the front. There was a tape recorder on the table with four cassettes beside it with the names of hymns on their sides written in a hand that could have been anyone’s grandmother’s. I sat and was immediate in my sitting. I was the sum of my living and my grief and my brother’s elbow against my arm at the movies and my father’s hand on the back of my neck and my mother’s belief that every lost thing is a blessing because it reminds us there are things. There was the noise a heater makes that is like the sound of men in a small boat with a short sea, the slap of the hull when it does not want to be slapped. A woman in a coat with her hat in her hand came past me and did not look but touched, just lightly, the rail in front of me as she went and that was a blessing as old as any blessing.

In the morning, which would be the other morning, I would go to the funeral home where they have the skill of making what is not a place for beauty a place where the possibility of it is held in the corner of the room where the good chair is. I would speak to a man who had been a boy longer than he wanted and now counted his days in caskets moved. I would make a plan and it would be a plan that included my brother’s name as if it were the name of a traveler who would need his ticket stamped in order to be allowed to go further. I would call the woman who had been his wife and not his wife and not say the thing we had both tried not to say all these years, and I would call the mother we had not called in two years because there are kinds of cowardice we have, some we have to honor for a while until we can stand up again in their sight. I would call and then I would call again if I could not speak, and I would write and I would sign again, this time a different form. This is what there is in such travel: the moving and then the stopping and then the moving again.

But for the moment I was in that ship still, just a man at a rail, and my brother was at the place where you are both weight and light, and the world was a line on the water. The ferry would take me back or would go without me and I would not mind either. I would be on it in any case, even if I were in a small bed in the inn and had put the book face down and had turned off the lamp to a half angle. The ship was in me now as those old songs are in you forever that you did not ask for. My brother’s body lay where the doctor had closed his door over it, and meanwhile the harbor froze and un-froze and froze, and our father, who had invented a logic for the movement of bread in a tide, was in no position now to correct us. He was in the field of me, as the marsh was in me, as the night deck of the ferry and its paint and its rope were in me.

So I will say it here, because there is no other right place, as if saying it put it in a pocket where it would not be ashamed: I looked at the body and saw my brother and I saw not my brother. I signed and I did not sign. The hand that made his name was mine but had been taught by my mother and steadied by my father and watched by my brother when we were eleven and I was at the kitchen table and he said, Make a B like a boat that sits low because it is filled with fish. I did as he had asked and there it was, a soggy B, a B ready for pulling. And so all these years later, on a ship with the hum of a bell and a swarm of snow and the old unanswerable things beating at the window, I made that letter again, and the word, and while I did, and while I did not, the ferry settled into the second hour of its crossing. And then the third. And then the last. And the horn, as if to keep faith, sounded once into a morning you would have to call day because there was work to do.
