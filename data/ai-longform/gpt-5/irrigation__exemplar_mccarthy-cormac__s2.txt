They come into the orchard before the sun is up and the trees are just black against the paling sky and there is a blue smell in the dust. The path from the house to the lower ditch goes through grass gone to fox tails and burrs and there are rabbit prints on the powder of the path and a place where a coyote has taken a bird and left only the ghosted arcs of wingfeathers. In the east the light is a thin wound. The brothers do not speak. They carry lengths of pipe on their shoulders and a sack of couplings with the purple primer slopping in the bottom like ink in a squid. Mason carries a pipe wrench and Levi carries the shovel and a water key and they both wear hats turned down and old boots that leak. They have a toolbox that has belonged to their father and before that to their father's father and the hinges are wired on and the black paint is gone from the handle. They put these things down by the ditch and listen.

There is a sound of water like a beast breathing. When they lean out over the culvert they can see the water in the darkness scudding with bits of grass and foam. The banks are caved in and the concrete spalled and in the far side something that looks like a dead dog is just roots with the hair long rotted off. It is watch the water and take off your hat and throw and see where the wind is and listen to the pump and say it is running rough and know you are right. It is know a tree looks gray before it dies. It is know a storm dont mean no good to you. It is know that a thing can be hard and right and no man will come to shake your hand for it. It is know that when they burn the little weeds under the trees they got to burn quick and not so hot that the bark will split because that cold come in January will make a crack of it. It aint nothing you can run a pencil through.

They stand until the frogs seem to have grown louder and until something like a grace comes. The stars are very many. There is small wind and it makes a sound in the dead leaves like paper whispered over in a jail. The dog has found something dead and is chewing and cracking little bones. They do not call him off. This is his day as much as any man's.

They go up to the house. The house is old. In the kitchen their mother had stood one winter with the oven open and the gas on and the heat on her shins and the look on her face both content and sad as if to say this is what our God has laid upon us and it aint so bad considering. There is a skillet hanging by the door and a hat and a calendar from three years past with a picture of a calf in the snow. There is a smell of mouse. They go through and sit on the steps on the back and watch the dark.

Mason says I will go see Joe Padilla tomorrow.

Levi listens. He says You go. He says Take me with you.

I will.

He says But we aint going to sell.

We wont sell tomorrow. Maybe we sell in a month. Maybe we sell in a year. Maybe we wont. There is no virtue in marking future things. If we had a mark we would only mislay it.

Levi says I know.

The frogs go and the water goes and something in the sycamore moves like the turning of a wheel and then stops. Mason thinks of his father. He thinks of sitting on the tailgate and of the old man telling them a story about some man who had come upon a group of wild horses and had followed them for two days and then had gone home with nothing and when his wife said Where are the horses he said There are some things you follow because it pleases God to see it done though it turn you out empty. He remembers the way the old man's teeth shone white in the lamplight and the scars on his hands and he remembers him saying in a different time God never promised you fairness son. He only offered you mercy.

In the night the pump coughs once like a sleeper and then is still and neither of them moves for they have shuttered it and it will lay till morning. The sky is deep and the earth is a thing afloat in it and the little orchard is a toy scratched on it. A car makes a white hack across the dark far off and then is gone. The dog comes and lays down on the step. The wind turns and you can smell some dead thing in the lane and you can smell alfalfa and something metallic and deep like the breath of an old well. The river speaks in a small voice that could be mistaken for the wind.

Levi says We have done enough for a day.

Mason says We have.

They sit another full half hour. Then they stand and go in and the screen makes its spring speech and claps behind them and there is the shape of them on the wall in the light and then there is no shape and the night takes the place back entire and the frogs go on and the water goes on and sometimes you would say that things really do keep, to their own poor way, the vigil toward which men were made.
