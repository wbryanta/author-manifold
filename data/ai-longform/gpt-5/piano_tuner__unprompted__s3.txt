He woke before the frost could take shape on the windshield, poured coffee into the thermos while the kettle still gossiped steam, and stood in the kitchen with the light off and his hand braced on the counter, feeling the day press itself into him like the weight of an instrument lifting from a dolly. The house held its own music: the tick of the old regulator clock in the parlor, the murmur of the refrigerator’s cycling, a mouse negotiating baseboards. He took the brown leather roll from its hook by the back door and let it unfurl across the table to make sure each thing was in its place—hammer, long and scarred and smooth to his touch; mutes like little green fish; temperament strip; rubber wedges in three sizes; tuning fork, though these days even he would slide a clip-on listener to the plate for a starting A before trusting his ear to stretch the octaves. In the knife slot he kept a stub of pencil and a folding rule. He rolled the felt careful the way you might bandage a wound.

His fingers were stiff. The first knuckles had started to seem like foreign stones that had worked up from his hand’s soil. He flexed until he felt pain like a thin light bloom and then ebb. He had gummed a note to himself to the side of the fridge two weeks ago when he sent the letters to the last of the names in the black book: last run end of the month. Call to confirm. The gummed note had fallen off and migrated to the floor twice, like it also couldn’t stand to watch that fact sit in the open. He had thought about writing to nobody, then just not making the trip one day, letting the route end the way some pianos end, unthanked and passed over until a son-in-law says can we move this to the garage. But he had tuned instruments for most of these people since their hair was a different color, their houses smelt like different dinners. If he was to stop he wanted to stop making his way through the places in a known order and then end where someone had first asked him to listen harder.

He set the thermos next to the roll and swallowed the little white pill the doctor told him to take in the morning for his blood pressure and the bigger one for his joints that did more to remind him he had joints than take any ache out of them. He wrote a note on the pad by the telephone for Mae even though there was no point leaving notes anymore, her days circled like fish in a bowl and then darting under the plastic plant. Out for route, back before supper, church last. He left it anyway. He folded his cap down over his ears and went out.

The van compounded its cough and then steadied. He had kept the van so long that its seats knew the shape of him in a way no chair in his house did. There were a few stray damsper felts in the cup holder from some years ago and he had never thought to throw them away. He flicked the wipers though there was nothing to wipe and steered down the gravel drive. On the hill past the mailbox the first field was washed a soft pewter in the half-light. Geese went overhead, tearing a V in the sleepy sound. He had to stop himself from saying out loud the route, the string of names and places that took shape like a set of intervals in his mind: Pipher farm on Rowe Road, the old schoolhouse beyond the creek, Jensen place with the looked-after grand on Trew, the Horvath trailer, county home, and then the church on Maple and Third. He did say each name inside himself as he moved, the way, tuning, he would say C and let the C bloom even before he’d touched it. Everyone who did the same thing long enough developed this double track, the out here where his hands fumbled for his thermos cap and he rolled to the stop sign at the end of his road, and the in there where the fingers were better and the road held every wheel before it had to be taken.

He had been listening for a living since he was eighteen. He could tell you what a series of bad winters had done to an instrument the way a veterinarian might read a dog’s coat. He could tell if a certain family was a people who put glasses on the lid without coasters. He could tell, impossibly sometimes, if love had lately moved into a room or out of it. People liked to say that the piano was a furniture that sang, and he could not correct them even though at a certain age you want to place language precision under things. It was furniture, it was machine, it was an ark and a coffin. It was kept in rooms for years for nobody’s hands at all, it was played once by a child in a white shirt because the teacher made them learn a Clementi and the parents clapped and someone put cake in a paper plate and then both instrument and lesson went quiet. He would tune them just the same. This was his pride and his, maybe, fault. He had gotten used to setting the house right for those who had given up, maybe in the hope that setting a house right made it endure a little longer.

At the Pipher farm a truck the color of mustard sat in the gravel with one tire flat to the rim. The porch boards had a tilt he could read like a face about to tell the truth. He parked behind the truck and sat for a second looking at the blank windows that once belonged to Ruth Pipher’s eyes. Ruth had had patience with metronomes and impatience with parents, which had suited Harlan. When she had phoned first, decades ago, to ask him to come tend the upright in the front room because the twos and threes were beating each other up, he drove out on a January day with the chain on his tires and she made him cocoa with the marshmallows she claimed tasted like chalk and she had him sit and listen to six different girls play the same song one after the other so he could tell her which of them might be hearing the middle of it instead of the outside beat. He’d said the one who kept glancing to the window was the one. Ruth had laughed and slapped his forearm. You’re as bad as me, she’d said. Now the porch door moaned when he thumbed the latch and the house smell came around him, a draft full of dust that had spells of apples, old varnish, a mouse that had made its life alongside the felt hammers. He knocked the jamb with his knuckles and called. A voice came from the kitchen, a man’s, the word just this way: back here.

He found him—Tommy, who had been a boy with a cowlick and a hatred of his scales—standing with his hands in his pockets looking at a table piled to its own corners. Hi, Mr. Creel, Tommy said, and then seemed to catch himself. He’d stopped growing into his name; you could see in middle age where some men had been made to stop. He touched his ear with a forefinger when Harlan said his own hello, and said, I’m sorry about calling you out here when, you know. He let the you know hang a second. The auctioneer’s coming by next week. Man said some folks’ll call about the piano without seeing the place. I thought it’d be better, I guess, if it sounded like something.

Harlan said, That’s what I’m for. He told Tommy it wouldn’t be perfect with one visit. He did not say I am out to say goodbye to these places, though he had written the letter. He asked where the instrument had migrated in these years. They went down the hall to the parlor that had always felt too cold. The upright stood in the corner with a cotton pad on top of it where a fish bowl had once sweated. He put the leather roll on the floor and slid the keyboard cover up and rapped middle G the way a man will nudge a shoulder to wake a sleeper he doesn’t want to startle.

It was gone some, down nearly a quarter-tone in the bass and wandering in the high, but he had brought back worse. He pried off the fallboard and front panel. Mouse droppings in the bottom felt. He sighed and leaned over and blew once out of his lungs and they scattered like pepper. Ruth would have scolded him for not bringing a vacuum. He stuffed a small piece of felt between two doubles and set the hammer on the first rusted pin and nudged, listened, nudged back, waited for the beat between the two strings to slow and then pause and then, with a long patience he had taught himself out of his haste when he was young, settle. He set the temperament in the middle so his octaves could stretch a shade sharp to suit an old ear. He was thinking about Ruth showing a six-year-old how to relax a thumb when Tommy said from the doorway, Nobody plays it. I guess you know that.

He said, I figured. There was a time when Tommy’s sister, Sally, had banged out Chopsticks with a kind of deliberately squalid humor at the end of a lesson while Ruth pretended to scold, and Harlan had clucked and put a felt on the A above A just to silence the worst beating and bought himself one more second to be around the both of them. Now a gray fish scale of daylight wobbled against the wall. He tuned. He had learned in his life how not to let his hands go faster than his hearing; this was partly why he liked his work. Some men, in their last years, sped up as if they could bully time by moving more quickly through it. He was not slowed enough to pretend he could outrun anything; he let himself bring each note back until he could live with it, not hold it too long. When he finished the midrange he went out to the truck for the tall sticks and lifted the front board higher to get at the top and knocked something hidden by the keys onto the floor. It was a small clipping from the county paper, yellow as a daffodil petal at the end of its weeks, trimmed with a scissor that had not been careful. In the picture a row of children stood side by side in the church sanctuary in front of the piano and behind a bald minister holding a pose like he’d just talked them into it. One of the children looked down at his own hand. He recognized, with the ache of recognition that takes you sideways, his son.

He did not bend to pick it up. He tuned. He heard that one of the steel strings up in the last octave had a flaw in it that gave a faint sourness on the attack and he placed the last cents not to make the sourness louder. Something like this would be an argument on a brand-new piano, he could hear the outraged young hand scoffing at him for building a compromise into his day, but he was long done believing that people wanted or could handle a world without the little arrangement made here and there. When he brought the cover back down he let himself run three notes and then a fourth to feel if they hung a line together and the note he struck sympathy sounded a little like someone behind him clearing a throat.

Tommy said, How much.

He told him and he saw in the other man’s face both the shame of valuing a thing in dollars and the sting at paying for a thing you think you should be able to get rid of and he hid his own shame where he always had, down in the place where he kept the old tears too. Tommy counted from a knotted handkerchief. When he took the money Harlan lifted the clipping from the floor and put it on the mantel without remark because there was a frame up there large enough for it and he could not bear to put his own boy in a pocket with lint. Ruth knew the day of that picture she was moving away into her last stretch. She had sent Harlan a loaf of lemon bread that week. He should not have remembered the scent of the lemon like a note.

He stood on the porch with Tommy a minute, the two of them looking at the field that looked like all fields when it’s a morning you can’t quite own. Tommy said, You still doing the church, then. Harlan nodded. Tommy said, I used to think I might play again one day. Then he laughed and touched the back of his head and said, Listen to me.

The old schoolhouse sat a mile or more past the bent bridge on the way into what used to be called the hollow. The creek was low enough that stones showed their slick black backs and gulls had found, somehow, between the sweetgum and the pine, a place to settle a while. He parked by the basketball pole with its hoop hung down like a tired eye. The key to the schoolhouse was still kept on a nail inside the broken little clapboard of the well house. He opened the door and air touched him that had been living in there a long time, flat and cold and unambitious. He moved through the front hall where in the old days the glass cases had held photographs of winning teams and crocheted potholders because someone’s mother had taught the art class. In the main room the light came through nine tall panes and set itself down on dust as if dust were the thing it had wanted to find all along, which he felt sometimes in a church, too. The piano was back-facing the teacher’s old stage as though ashamed. The oak around its fallboard had taken a drink from the humidity many times in summer and then had had it wrung back out in winter and now was neither quite open nor shut. He slid the upper panel out of its pegs when it refused to budge and leaned it against the blackboard. A mouse, less polite than the mouse at Ruth’s, fled. Someone had scrawled in chalk months ago: you are here. Someone else had drawn an arrow and in a different chalk hand someone had written: liar.

He gave himself the liberty, since nobody was waiting and the room had that way of time being stacked in it, to sit a bit before doing anything. He had eaten half a sandwich in the van, the bread a little hard, and had not tasted the mustard and thought how in his life there had been certain condiments you only tasted when you were young. He thought of dances here long before his time, before the county had consolidated everything out of walking distance and into bus distance, nights where men and women moved at each other and through each other in ways that would have made Ruth purse her mouth, which she did not do often, and then in the moonlight someone had played Red Wing wrong half a dozen times until it was right.

When he touched the keys here the sound leaped and then shrank as if the instrument were a racehorse turned to ride in the ring for children. The soundboard had two visible fissures and at least one he could hear angling out of sight. He decided to start high to get the worst of the fear over with. He put the long felt strip across six unisons and teased the middle string of each to where in his mind the colors lined. He had never had synesthesia; he could not tell you a taste for a note. But he did feel a line, almost a weight, when things were right, as if the wall he leaned against had stopped stuttering and admitted its own substance to him. The D was wild until it wasn’t. He set a narrow octave between F’s and then walked his way out. Three times in the tuning of many instruments his heart had done a flutter that startled him but made him feel large and necessary, one of those flutters happening now and the thing that set it wasn’t a note but the sight of a little paper in the tray at the bottom of the chalkboard where someone had left a list:

things i’ll do
1. go to the ocean
2. ride a train further than the next town
3. sing somewhere without anybody telling me to quit

He touched his middle pocket where he kept his cells phone and felt foolish doing it. When he was young he would have called someone over to stand with him and he would have pointed at the paper and they would have laughed at themselves for how much they felt about it. He was beyond the age of pulling anybody else into anything he was willing to be surprised by. He played the list’s tiny hope for a kind of prank by feeling if the G two above middle would come with him when he asked, and it did, sweetly. In the corner a chair with no purpose sat, a planked seat with a shine of rubbed-down bodies in the seat. He remembered unbidden the afternoon his boy had sat in a folding chair next to him at a tuning in the rectory thirty years ago and asked if you could hear the ocean in a piano if you lay your head on the wood, and he had said, Not the ocean like out there but the ocean of a certain thing that resists you and still makes your hands interested in approaching it. He had used too many words, even then. David had laid his face against the case anyway and said, I hear it. He had been in the phase then of wanting to call the tuning fork a pitch-pig and the hammer a lever and the unisons friends because Harlan had let him. He did not let him anymore by the time the boy was a teen because it is hard to be the father to a son who is growing out at the same time he is growing in. He did not often think about the sentence but it was with him when he laid many sounds into place. The ocean is always out there being itself regardless of whether you can carry its bit or not.

When he tried a progression for his own private ear at the schoolhouse, a damp came out of the bore where the low C lived and entered his lungs and he had to cough and that hurt in his ribs. The top lid of the piano had a banner of cobweb at its side. He told himself he would leave the piano a place where the next person to settle their hands there would not be offended by the instrument lying to them about where the notes were. He did not want to make any promises he could not keep for the place. He closed the panel. Outside a diesel engine moved. A man’s voice had its day. Harlan rolled the felt strip into its circle and packed his hammer and moved with the care he had begun lately to apply to every common thing—not for fear that the thing would break, but for knowledge of how many little things got to be last things in any given month. He rehung the key under the nail in the well house without making a ceremony of it, with only the regard you give a nail that has done more than be a nail.

At the Jensen place on Trew Street he parked at the bottom of the steep drive because he had no interest in creeping his old van up and then rolling it back like a thought you could not complete. The house had had painters two summers ago and the white was still white and the grass had that beige tidiness of someone having made an arrangement with a mower twenty days out of thirty. He let himself in with the key the realtor had given him, an envelope with the cursive J in a circle pressed into the corner, and stood a minute, because his breath had asked him to, in the foyer where the old Victorian stairs lifted themselves in an impressive flourish you would not want to climb with swollen knuckles. He loved the feeling in such a house of having been trusted by people who had only heard his name. It gave him a feeling like the inside middle of a long chord holds itself cheerfully while the outside notes argue.

The Steinway baby grand had sat with its cloth cover on too much. The wood had dried and then had been fed oil and then had dried again and now looked like one of those old men in town who decide one winter to wear a leather jacket and then the jacket does not take the new man into account. But the action felt good when he moved it. He had a way to work with grands that was like he’d learned to greet someone he had loved who had now become a little hard to see at a distance; you did not wave like a boy and you did not nod like a general; you put your palm just there in the air and you let the other being notice you noticing it. He opened the lid to the short stick and then to the long because the room could hold it. A draft moved up the stairwell and teased the sheet draped on the empty dining room table. He worked low to high because the bass on a Steinway will lie to you most if you let the gossip of the treble get in your head first. He set the intervals that his own teacher had set in his hands: fourths and fifths, a narrow third, a wide one, a check back to see if the wolf had sneaked out of the woods between D and A. He was relieved and surprised at how obedient the instrument wanted to be. Once, early on, he had thought of pianos as to be tamed. When he got older he learned the word wanted did most of the work in such a sentence. He did not imagine himself bending a nature until it submitted. He invited a nature that spent most of its day ignoring humans to notice he could be used by it in a certain way.

It made him wish to play. He did not let himself. There were days when his hands still made him proud, days he could run a simple figure without his joints seizing and his fingers forming awkward squares. But the wrong day to try to shake some Beethoven out of a thing would put a stain on him. He allowed one arpeggio in each hand and the tremble-nerved joy of it ran through him, opening little doors in old rooms where there were pictures on the wall he had not looked in the eye. Mae had once sat on a bench in the dark beside him at church after an Advent service until all the candles had been pinned to stubs and there had been a smell of wax like bees in spring. She once had had a voice that made people weep without knowing why. It was a gift, he had told her once, and she had looked at him the way she looks at small children who hold something fragile too hard in their mouths.

He replaced the lid and left the little note under the vase that said Paid in Full with a check because the realtor wanted the place to smell like fresh wood and sound like money. He did not take it personally. On his way out he touched the banister with the kind of courtesy you give a hand that has touched a century of other hands. In the bright yard he ate the apple he had stuck in his coat pocket and flicked the seeds in an arc. He put his hand on the hood of the van where the sun had found it and liked the way heat felt on his palm as if he could read that too.

By noon the road shimmered in places even though it was October, the kind of fall day that spends its morning trying on summer again. He passed what used to be the feed store with its scribbles of old ads for hay and kittens and rooms for rent and now was a hair place with a name like Zone. He thought about David at eight with his cowlick down and a proud frown on his face because he had learned to clap a two against a three. He thought about David at nineteen not answering his phone and then at twenty-two looking at him with a look Harlan had given his own father once, the look that is mostly do not jail me inside a kindness of yours you have come to like too much. If you do not let a mind go where it’s trying to go, he had said like a man making a pledge that might be too late, the mind will go mean. David had gone. First to Portland to chase a girl who had eyes and a car. Then out west where the boy put on a coat that was not cut for him and had to shed it on the side of some road. These are stories fathers tell themselves when nobody else can contradict. He did not think of the morning he had reached under the seat of David’s car for the registration to show the trooper and had found not a map, or the wrappers for food too often eaten one-handed, but a little bag of little things and he had looked at his son and said no word at all for too long, then too many words after. He did not think of the day five Junes ago when the phone had rung at supper and his spoon had hung midair. He was good at stopping that thought before it made the lit tunnel. He would go there later because the day itself asked for it and he would not put off what the day had asked.

The Horvath trailer smelled of fry oil and lemon cleanser. A wheeze of the oxygen machine like a cricket that hadn’t found the outside. Mr. Horvath sat in a recliner watching the kind of show that can’t be heard when the room is loud, the kind filled with faces talking in living rooms. Harlan said hello and the man raised his hand a little and then put his thumb to the machine and flicked it off so the quiet emptied around them and said, Don’t say I don’t know how to greet a man. The spinet was an old Story & Clark with a plastic smile. One leg had been braced by a block of wood. When he opened the lid, the weak smell of a winter’s damp living came at him out of the felt like breath out of a scarf. There were three magnet letters on the music holder that once spelled cat. They spelled act. Mr. Horvath said, It’s for my granddaughter to mess with when she comes. She’s not come in months. You still got kids doing scales these days?

Harlan said, Less than used to. He put the hammer on a pin and went to work while Mr. Horvath watched him with that intense courtesy of lonely men, the courtesy that can feel like a stare if you don’t know what it is. He asked for the television down and the man obliged and then the room seemed full of the music of the tuning itself, a tap, a slide of wood against wood, the way his breath caught when a stubborn pin gave in and then settled. Outside, a boy on a bike rode to the end of the dirt lane and then rode it again. When he finished Mr. Horvath had dozed and his mouth had gone slack the way mouths do amid oxygen machines. Harlan set the lid down without letting it fall an inch and went to the kitchen counter and wrote on a sheet from a pad: tuned; minor pitch raise; second string E4 near break, replaced at some point; if you hear buzz, call. He left the man’s cash on the arm of the couch with the receipt under. He stood there in the lemon smell and remembered the afternoon Mr. Horvath’s wife had stepped out of the kitchen drying hands on a towel, said thank you for making our noise kind, and he had gone away with his ears full of their talk.

By then it was almost three and the county home would want him in the four hour so he could be out before their dinner. The matron with the sugar voice and the plucked eyebrows always wanted to be sure she was not complicating anyone’s supper and always did anyway. He did not mind. He tuned among the interrupted, that was part of his work. The county home’s rec room had mauve chairs and a fake plant that had tricked nobody since the day it came out of its box. The piano was a Yamaha console the county had bought twenty years ago with a grant. It was serviceable as most things somebody had to write a report about become serviceable. He had always loved it for not pretending to be more. When he slid his hammer on the first pin a woman in a green sweater wheeled herself into the doorway, smiled at him, and said, Hey Gabe. I thought you died.

He stood and said, I am still Harlan, and she thought, dizzily, he could see in her eyes, and said, Harlan Creel! and then a little later she said, You’re my brother. He gave her a chair with its rubber stoppers and let her sit near while he did the unisons within the temperament and she hummed a thing that did not approach the temper at all but was the hum of her day. A nurse’s aide came by and put a half a finger to her lips in apology and tugged the chair a foot away and Harlan shook his head and said she can stay, let her stay and the aide smiled because she had needed someone to be kind to her, too. When a janitor with a vacuum inched his way into the doorway like a novel you might want to read but that isn’t time yet, Harlan asked him if he could give him fifteen. The man nodded and went away with a mercy that touched Harlan like the sight of a fingertip against a pane of frost.

He thought, while he worked, about the day David had stood in this room for some church charity thing and had eaten a muffin and had not wanted to be there. Harlan had played Autumn Leaves without being asked in a key he knew too well and the room had given him two kind looks and one woman had gone to sleep also kindly with her head pointed at the piano like a sunflower tries to find the sun. David had gone outside and later he had leaned against the van, looked down, and said, I can’t do the slow you do. Harlan had not known yet then that slow was not his son’s pace; he had not known that for his son slow was a bruise he did not want touched. He wondered, at the home, if there were men in there who had come to music late. He wondered when his own ears would go from being what he lived through to being what he tried to live around.

By the time he parked in the shadow of the church, the maples were already getting ready to let go. The trees had gone red early this year as if they had a train to catch. He sat in the van and let the engine idle half a minute and turn itself off and then he took the key out of the visor and held it in his hand as if to see what it weighed. The church had a door that could be held open with a horse brass at the foot. The brass had somehow never been stolen when other little things had migrated away. He put his palm against the cool wood and went in the side entrance with its pegboard of nursery notices and lost gloves. There was the bulletin board with pictures of kids and adults standing in hunger lines holding trays full of paper bowls of something brown and white. Near the coat tree, a sign asking for volunteers to rake. He could see through to the sanctuary, its pews with the green cushions somebody had picked to make it seem less plain in there. The instrument was right where it had been, at the crook of the transept, a Kawai grand that had been bought by a donor in memory of a man who had taught Sunday school and had died with his hands in the earth.

He let the lid up. He did not like the light in the sanctuary—they had put can lights in twelve years ago that made the ceiling feel too near. He wished they had trusted the old windows. He had to stand for a minute with his hands on the edge of the instrument, not an embrace, a steadiness. He could feel the sanctuary remembering all the mornings like this, him working alone while somewhere in the kitchen a woman moved a coffee tin and in the office the secretary bought stamps online. The ghost of the old organ blower held still over the choir loft. His hands found the lever and the mute on their own. He always began here by finding A not with his fork but by striking it and hearing the room hold it the way a throat holds a vowel, then he would nod to his own decades and clip the small device on anyway and see what it said to him in red and green and then he’d toss it back in the box. When the note settled the way one old friend settles when you see them and you don’t have to say anything first, he set the circle. He felt the quiet around his own breath. He could hear, below everything, the small hum of the exit light over the choir door. Outside, a man spoke to a child. The sound of a truck gate.

He tuned, and while he tuned he remembered the night of the recital. It had been August, a heat that came up through the floor like an idea. A row of fans lifted their hands and set them down again all through the long prayers. He had tuned in the morning and then had stayed because the piano had gone down even in the two hours between. The teacher, not Ruth this time—Ruth had three years by then in the ground—had given him a look halfway through the program that asked if he would touch things when it was over. He had; he had put his hand inside the instrument like a surgeon and righted the two or three worst false pitches and retreated. David had stood with his shirt stuck to his chest while he read a program that listed a sonatina and a little Debussy no one had any business making a child play in August. He had wanted to go up and put his palm to the boy’s back like when he had fevers as a kid and speak a sentence that would make perfect sense and yet not ask anything of him, something like, You will just go and make the sound you make and we will all sit inside it with you. He did not. He had sat with Mae and looked at her mouth and watched it tremble. She had not wanted him to push the boy any more than he had. He had pushed, you could be honest about that, you have to if you want a person not to fall into the old ditch. When David had played his left hand had, a few times, struck earlier than his head had told it to, and his right had looked like a bird trying not to be caught. He had finished the sonatina and stood and bowed awkward and only then had a big tear rolled down his left cheek as if he had not had time to find that feeling before. Afterwards in the church lot a little girl named April had offered him a slice of sheet cake with roses that stained people’s tongues and he had taken it and smiled and then set the plate aside out of a habit that meant something like he wanted to please. He had then said, too late to take it back, I don’t want to do that again, Dad, and Harlan, too slow for his own good at that kind of moment, had said something like we at least finished what we started, and the boy had looked at him with a sudden flatness and said, Not everything has to be finished to the end for it to have been worth doing, and Mae had reached out to touch Harlan’s sleeve and could not find his arm in a way that had made him cold for an hour after night had olded and the fans stopped.

He tuned through all that, because he knew how to be inside your work and think of a thousand other things. When Mr. Blume, the custodian, came in with his hat in his hands and the way of walking that says I do not want to creep up on a man with a task, Harlan was finishing the low resonance check. Mr. Blume said, There he is, and then, I heard you were making your last loop. Friend of mine called to gloat he’d gotten on your list but only once more. He grinned, which in Blume meant he let the corners of his mouth say something his eyes already had. Harlan nodded and said, I should have done it two years ago. Blume said, Nonsense. He touched the piano where Harlan was not touching it, laid his hand on the lid as if it were a sleeping fox. He said, How’s Mae. Harlan said, She’s all right. Days. Some days. There was a pause. Blume said, You tell him about your boy. It was a stupid, clumsy thing to ask that way and Harlan saw the instant Blume said it that he wished the words had climbed back up his throat. What Blume had meant was: Do you tell people. What Blume had made instead was a jolt. Harlan said, No. I don’t tell. Blume folded his hat and unfolded it and went to the door and stood a second on the threshold. He looked back. He said, I remember the look on your wife’s face that night. That’s all. Take your time, Harlan. I’ll be in the basement fighting the ancient boiler dragon. He was gone.

Harlan stood over the instrument and realized the tremor in his right hand was more than the tremor he used now and again to make micro-corrections with, to let his touch on the pin be lively and not rigid. He sat at the bench and set the hammer down and put his palms on his legs and breathed. The sanctuary watched you breathe the way no other space did. He had never settled in his mind whether it was better to have God watch or to not. He lifted his hands and played, before his body could prevent him, the first four bars of the Beethoven sonatina his son had played. He played them in the most unshowy way he could. He pretended he was telling a person in the dark which way to go to find a door. On the fourth bar, not because it was the fourth bar but because he had to, he stopped. He breathed once, carefully. The stop was not a failure. He had learned that in the last years too. It was movement being faithful to itself.

He let his left hand delay a toad’s-length over the low C and then brought it down soft which is how David had been able, at home, to get a cheap piano to sound like it had dignity, you set the low note into the wood with courtesy and it hums and tells the room who we are. He had been that kind of boy, respectful and then cutting in his sentences later because he had a tongue that did not attach itself to his mother’s strictness. He had died on the side of a county road two hundred miles from the ocean, which still made Harlan furious at an element even as he was lit by memory of David at eight lying along the piano and hearing water. He had not died of the thing in the packet under the seat, he had died in the idiot way most boys die, which is to say a truck and a phone and a rain and suddenly there is the absence of your boy. He had spent the phrase he had to spend on this many times with many men in dark rooms in the three and a half years since who came to him not because he spoke but because he could bear to stand next to a sound for a long time and not ask it to be another sound. He had not come publicly. He had gone among the unisons and he had set them right for other people.

He reached into the case and touched one string with the pad of his index finger and it sang a whisper up into his ear. He had learned things over the years about touch he could never explain. He touched again, a little harder, and the whisper raised its head. He was aware of how easily he could make this into something, but he let the sound relent. He sat hard in the bench for a long minute, longer than that, then rose and gave his back a question and his back answered no and he folded the front board down and laid the lid over and stood with his hand where he had been working until the impression of his own hand warmed his palm with the returned warmth of the wood. That was one of the reasons he had done this. That small returned heat had often been enough when he had not had any other thing that was going to keep him.

He pulled the little invoice pad from his pocket, wrote paid for with a flourish that did not feel like a decision. He set a trap for a clumsy boy in his head and sprung it and saw him land and then sprung it again until the best part of him stood on his own. He tucked the hammer into the roll the way you bed a child you do not want to wake. He found in the side pocket the little photo that had fallen in the first house—he did not remember putting it there but he must have, some version of him must have—and he set it under the plant on the piano, tucked where a cleaning woman could find it and think for one little second: this is a picture of boys in a church. He did not want to hold it on the drive home where the sun would cut in at the wrong angle and would ask him to look at it and to compare each face to a later one he had known. He wanted to leave his son in rooms where the stops still did their work and the notes knew the names they had been given. He did not stay and pray though he could have. He had been worn away enough by the last three years to know that there is no advantage given to you if you speak in sanctuaries; the advantage given is what you do when a thing needs doing.

He walked the aisle where the weddings had made path before, where his own feet had walked up and then together with Mae and then had walked down between cheers. He touched the pew back with his palm and liked the sound of his skin whisper there. He closed the big door behind him so it shuddered its wooden sigh back into itself. On the steps he stood because the light was arranged in such a way that the world seemed to line itself along the edges of his day. A girl across the street rode a scooter in little half-circles that looked unpromising from far away and then pleasing when she came close. A woman with a bag walked a dog with the gait of an old man reading a book he likes. A pair of crows told each other what they’d seen in the yard. He wanted to sit but he stayed up, holding his roll and his thermos. It had the hot on his hip in a way he had known for most of his life. He thought for some reason then of putting his head to the wood and listening for water and he barked a laugh at himself, unpretty and out loud, and a person walking by gave him a glance and a nod. He nodded back. The van coughed and settled. He was the last person in the maples for a minute and then a wind moved through them and let a thousand notes go with a clatter like the least part of a symphony.

He did not look back when he put the van in drive and eased from the curb; there is no rule that says you must look back. The route, for that day, had been played. He held the wheel the way you hold a hand that you will not be able to hold much longer. On the radio, a preacher finished saying something and a fiddler came in with a tune whose title he could not place and then the road came up to meet the van where the shadow at the dip had always been. He drove, and in the instrument under the church lid near where the choir would sit tomorrow, the notes settled where he had set them, little, righted ships in their slips, waiting, as everything in their own gardens waits, to be touched by hands or air, making a sound then that made no claim on any ear at all and still was itself entirely. He liked knowing that. He let it be part of the noise in his van. He let it be what it was. He said the names to himself one last time, because you are required to be ceremonious in a small way, if only to yourself, when you have finished a thing: Pipher, schoolhouse, Jensen, Horvath, county home, Maple Street. He said his son’s name inside the sequence because it was anyway. He said his own, as if by calling himself in he might know where he was, and when he turned at his road the van’s dash ticked like the little gears in the old regulator in his house, and the fall around him kept being a thing that believed in dying only because it had seen how it made a color out of green that green could never have made alone. He drove into it. He held the wheel. He listened until there was nothing left to tune in that day but what he could lay gently back down. He went home.
