By the time the ticket clerk slid the printout under the glass with two fingers and a kind of rote kindness, the sky over the terminal had already turned the color of wet slate. The big sodium lamps struggled against it, buzzing as if each wordless yellow halo were a private, industrial moon. Wind came in lashes off the water. Inside the terminal was a long mural of whales and fishing boats and men with beards you could stand your hand in. The vending machines blinked like small casinos. She said to me, You’ll board on C, follow the blue arrows. She tried a smile that didn’t make it to her eyes, and I kept thinking of the voice that had pulled me out of the quiet Tuesday morning in my kitchen, the voice that introduced himself as Constable McKay in a manner apologetic and firm all at once, as if the apology would forever stand under, and be required by, the firmness.

You’re listed as a contact, he’d said, and hesitate, then add, for a… for a Simon, is that right?

He said it like he was asking me an unrelated question, the same tone people use when they ask whether I’ve seen this weather before, how remarkable that it is mid-October and the leaves have held. It occurred to me to say No, wrong number or Who’s this? I could have lied in that clearing of deep dread that followed his sentence and the way he lifted up the name of my brother like a raw thing out of cold water. There are different forms of cowardice and kindness, and sometimes they look the same inside the length of a single second. Instead, I heard myself say yes, and yes again. He explained with phrases I only half heard—a body found on the north side of the bay, no identification yet, the wallet gone, we need someone who can confirm, if at all possible and I’m sorry to have to ask. He used the words, If it helps, and it didn’t.

The woman at the ticket counter slid a small plastic token toward me. Cabins are extra, she said, and gave me a look that asked whether I looked like the kind of man who would want to lie down alone in a rectangle with a vent and a sheet of white paper called a pillow. I must have looked like I could survive a seat. I slid the ticket into the pocket of my coat where the seam had begun to go. I had nothing but a backpack that held an extra shirt, a toothbrush, the papers I would need to use to prove I was the kind of person who could identify another person, and a single photograph that was twenty years old and damaged between my thumb and first finger from the way I’d held it this afternoon, looking for some sort of answer in the shadow grain of a thing that never agreed with the present.

The lengths of blue arrows on the terminal floor led up to a long glass tunnel that vibrated with the tractor movement of the ship. The ferry was wedged into the long concrete mouth of the dock, its lit windows in staggered rectangles like apartment buildings you see through the emptiness of cities at night. The wind found the gaps in my coat and worked them. Other passengers pulled their bodies through the tunnel, their luggage whispering on plastic wheels, a little parade in which, for once, no one looked like they were heading on holiday. A woman in a floral scarf had a small boy with a backpack shaped like a penguin who kept turning to look back, solemn-eyed, as if he had already noticed that all departures are really many different kinds of leaving at once.

On board the light went from the gray of the afternoon to an insistent, clean brilliance, almost blue. There were signs guiding foot passengers up metal stairs and across carpeted concourse. The ferry had the faint smell of old vomit and new coffee, oil and air freshener, fryers and what my father used to call ship. I pressed my forearm against the steel of the stair rail and it was colder than bone. The huge space of the car deck hummed beneath, a seabed engine, while people found their way to lounges with names like Aurora and Horizon. A woman with dyed black hair and a nose that looked as if it had been broken twice was leaning by a vending machine, pushing the toe of her boot against a line between tiles as if it were a dangerous border. She smoked in a place where the signs said not to smoke, exhaling into her jacket and peering into the bright little bag of potato chips like it might answer something if she just categorized each of its folded little yellow ridges.

Underneath a long bank of windows - black glass for now, reflecting back everyone’s face as if the sea were all of us tonight - sat people who lived by the strait, who crossed it for work, who crossed it to be with someone else when it couldn’t be delayed. Their faces had a rehearsed blankness, a kind of stage that knew by now how to hold up the play of weather and the movements of ships leaving and arriving. I found a seat near the window. When I leaned forward, my breath made a fog on the glass just for a second, and my face in it looked the age I was, which was younger than I felt in that moment and older than I was supposed to be when I used to picture this part of my life, all those years ago when I imagined a straightforward line.

When we pulled away—so much smoother than it seems from land—you could feel it in your inner ear first, a suggestion rather than a change. The horn let out a sound that is the same from childhood, calling down the winter gulls and the hidden trawlers and my father’s old laugh from behind his teeth when he’d say, Listen. The port lights fell behind like a small city that didn’t mind who left. The town that faced the water sent its yellow beads across us, and then we were already farther than I’d been in a year.

I thought I would go to the bar and order something hot and sweet to put into my shaking body, but when I got there, a queue had formed and then dissipated, as if they’d all arrived at the same sudden thought at once and moved away from it. I stood anyway and read the little menu that promised chowder, which would be microwave-chowder, which means a taste of fish and a taste of damp paper. I ordered tea. When it came, the tea bag tag showed a ship like this one, drawn a little too friendly and too proud of its red funnel. I took it back to my seat and let it sting my lip. On the other side of the aisle two men whom I would have called truckers if I allowed myself clichés—and I did—sat in high-back seats with their boots spread under the table. They talked in that way men talk to each other about work, looping back to the same admission in different phrasing until it sounds like conclusion: wind picking up, they’d seen worse, but still, you never know, no? No. One of them had skin that had tanned but only the parts exposed around a baseball cap and the shape of it was there like a drawn-on outline. They said the word ferry the way I’ve said the word brother in the past five years, a word that fills your mouth and yet carries nothing of what it is. 

I took my phone out. The officer had texted the time, morning intake hours, what door to use. It looked like something you’d get from a dentist, routine, except that if the thing were routine in the way of teeth, I’d have had an appointment card resting under a magnet shaped like a carrot on my refrigerator door. There was a message from Zoe that only said, Are you okay, and I held my thumb over the letters of her name and thought of the last meal we had together, a tiny plate of bitter greens and a sliver of cheese, and the moment she’d asked me if I’d thought of calling Simon, just to see how he was, and how, instead of answering her question, I’d picked up a bread knife and started shaving too-thin pieces off a loaf as if it were a problem that required geometry. Strange the way you can sculpt a life to avoid a call.

I texted back, I’m on a ferry, and watched the three gray dots appear and then dissolve, a tiny weather system indecisive over the ocean of a screen. Finally, from her: Do you want to talk? And then, two beats later, Take care of yourself. I sent a thumb-up like a man under a glove. I didn’t want to talk. I wanted to sit in the blue light with the engine under me and remember my brother as he was without it feeling like the rehearsal of a eulogy. But then my mind supplied the last time I saw him, his cheekbone sharp as a drawer’s edge, a new scar under the chin I had never asked about, his winter jacket in June because he’d pawned anything else that could be pawned. He asked me for money and I neither gave it nor refused it, which is a way of making someone wait in a door you won’t open. I said I wanted him to tell me how he was and he said, Well, I’m here, aren’t I, like a cold joke we couldn’t press our hands around. He was angrier that day than any days before it. Or maybe we were simply the age we were, and the anger that had been smooth and promised in us had become completed, come into its inheritance. He said something about how I had gone south and become too good, as if virtue were geography and only the wind could choose its direction. He had always been the one to leave: the house, the room, the breakfast—you could count on his body to make a line outward. It was as if he had been born oriented somewhere that no one could name and had been trying to line up with it since childhood, like a compass with a piece of metal hidden under it.

A small boy was crying two rows up in that whaling way that’s a doctrine, and his mother held him and said nothing. A man with a hearing aid took it out and put it in his breast pocket delicately like a bird. The tea had gone tepid. I cradled it anyway to stop my hands. There is an agitation to anticipation that feels like your muscles rehearsing an action you will never get to take. I found a pen at the bottom of my bag and tried to write down what I would tell the constable in the morning to help him gauge whether the body on the metal table and the boy whom I used to cover with an old cardigan when he’d fallen asleep in front of the television were the same person. I wrote: scar under chin, on right? then scratched it out and replaced it with left? and put a question mark after that, which is a strange thing to do with a scar. I wrote: chipped incisor, not front, one off, left. I wrote: two moles on the upper back, one like a faint smear and then thought, Who would show me his back in the last decade? I wrote: his ear lobes free not attached, and I couldn’t remember if that was true or a thing I had once seen on an invented checklist of earlobes. I wanted to write: he had a way of laughing behind his hand when he wanted to wound you and make you believe he was the one wounded, he had a talent for promises that tasted true until you swallowed them, he made small boats out of all the paper he could find and sent them down the drain after the rain and loved them while they lasted and loved them when they clogged the grate and the water backed up and flooded the back step. He could not take care of a dog, and he could not keep a secret, and his kindness made you fearful because you could never tell if it had somewhere to go. I didn’t write any of that.

On deck, the wind took what little there was of me and tried to fold it back into its own body. They had laid down grit near the rail against the small ice that appears like a prophecy. You could see nothing but the kind of darkness that is made of all the things that have been put into it: ships, whales long gone, dead men on the renumbered days of their lives, words given and broken, new words just now being formed in the throats of people who will never meet. Far out to starboard a smear of something like phosphorescence or the blinking of a navigation buoy, which might as well be a man’s eye opening and then closing trying to remember the thing he had meant to say. I swallowed the wind and into that swallowing came the old nights when Simon and I would play at being sailors on the frame of our bunk beds, the way his hand would slap the mattress like it was going to take off. He was good at doing the voices of men we’d only barely met—the cousin of the neighbor who worked the loch and had a gull tattooed on the striation of muscle, the man who sold us bait and counted the coins slowly like he was anchoring us. He could make the sound of the school register being called and would say our names in different versions of the teacher’s voice, and we’d answer until our voices became the voices of our parents and then change those, too, for the fun and terror of it.

A woman had come up to smoke and we were the only two on the leeward side. She cupped the ash in her hand like wind might be a person trying to hear. She had that face you couldn’t guess at the age of—anything from thirty to fifty—lined in the middle, well kept at the ends. She nodded and I nodded back, both of us in the fraternity of those who step outside to be somewhere colder because inside is a room with too much breathing. She said, You go across often? and I lied and said, Sometimes, because when you answer a question like that truly to a stranger you suddenly have to stand inside the answer, and I didn’t want to live in any room where I had to say, No, not since the thing with my brother. Where are you headed? she asked, and I said, The first stop, because I couldn’t remember the name for a second. She said, My mother’s got the winter cough and thinks she’s old because of it, and the way she flicked the ash away with the sudden attention of the wrist made me think of Simon doing that with anything—the ball of tinfoil from the sandwich, the sprinkled salt on the back step to melt the morning ice. He had used his wrists to seize the world and then to give it back when giving it back made other people panic.

We stood a while without talking. There is an exotically precise way to be silent with a stranger in a place like that: you behave as if silence had been their idea. The smoke drifted starboard and was taken apart molecule by molecule, and for a second I pictured that as a kindness, something the world does to the things we put into it: takes them, distributes them, says, Enough back. She said, I don’t like them at night, the ferries. You can’t see a thing. I said that in the daytime I always wished it were night, and when it’s night I want to see. She laughed and coughed into that laugh. She said, They’re saying the weather will change tomorrow, and I thought of the phrase we’d grown up with, weather coming, as if weather were a man you could see down the street, his collar up.

Inside, the seat had achieved that role of a cheap lover, warming the small of your back and then pulling away when you moved. The truckers had gone somewhere to smoke or to spread themselves on the floor. A young man in a puffer jacket, Oxford shoes, and a haircut that had something to prove had taken off his shoes and was sleeping with his socked feet on his backpack. His hands were small, like an old man’s, and I had the sudden, aching thought of Simon’s hands when we were boys—uncertain inwarding of palm, weakness in the joint of the thumb from a break falling off the wall we weren’t supposed to climb, a whiteness in the scars that look like slugs on the top of the hand, small white animals that live there. I must have slept, then, because there was a time missing, and when it returned, a woman in the aisle was speaking in a low, patient voice to a child, and you could feel people deciding whether to be compassionate or angry in the way their heads tilted, the gentle invention of mercy.

At some point I went to the toilet and stood looking at myself in the mirror that made everyone forward and long, a funhouse that had never meant to be funny. The soap was a pale, insufficient blue. My face looked like my father’s when he was tired in the year after our mother left. People keep using the word left as if it has to do with hands, with direction. It is a word that should be wind but is used for walking away. He had stood in the doorway looking at the way the light didn’t enter our sitting room on winter afternoons and said that summer would be better, and it was, in that it was lighter. He took us out on the water in the skiff after that, religiously, as if the winter we had lived through had made him decide we should know how wood feels when it’s wet, how rope alters a hand, how fish eyes don’t ever shut. Simon and I baited hooks with thighs braced against the gunwale, him always daring the day harder, his hands inside the tin of bait, up to the wrist, as if something in there might absolve him. The one time we capsized—late, later than we should have been out—he laughed in the water and coughed and laughed more and said, Well, that’s us sorted, now we know what it is, and my father just looked at him with a kind of extra respect and terror that I knew even then meant here is the child I will lose to this world.

Back in the lounge, the television that chattered silently during the day had images of a fire—some warehouse, maybe, too many men with high-vis and their arms folded, nothing happening while everything had already happened and smoke being the thing left. The captions ran under the screen: words like suspicious, like damage estimated. Next to me on the seat, a pamphlet reminded me that this was a ferry you could later recall in a story that was supposed to be easy—puffins, whale-watching cruises, heritage knitwear. On the inside: polaroids of a coastline I knew the shape of by heart and yet couldn’t draw with a pencil like a child would draw the outline of home. Under one picture was a small anchor in brass that made my throat go raw again. The constable had said, There was a key ring, we think it’s a coin from a tourist place, with an anchor on it, and had been quiet then, as if that image might arrive into my eye with force. We had once had a key ring like that. I had used it for the shed. Simon had taken it once for a few weeks and then returned it with an extra nick near the shank that I’d never asked him about.

The ferry rolled a little, the sort of roll that prompts everyone to turn their heads like cattle at the side of a field watching a single car go by. My stomach caught at the movement and then let go, embarrassed. An old man with a cap that had the name of a farm on it handed me a mint without looking at me and said, Suck, in a way my grandfather would have said it, and I did. He said, They’ve done worse in their day, these waters, and I nodded because that was the kind of truth that you cannot receive in full. He said, I lost my brother in a storm forty years ago, and I looked at his cap and his jacket and his hands, and I wanted him to say more, but he’d used all the words he was going to give to a stranger and got up and went to the bar. I put the mint inside my cheek and told my jaw to behave.

Later, a crew member with a face you wouldn’t remember put a mop in a yellow bucket near the bar and then rolled the mop over the same patch of floor three times, the way the body likes the idea of an action more than the action sometimes. When he pushed open the door behind the bar, the light from the back room was a different country: warmer, slower, a sloppiness to it that was almost erotic. The door fell shut again and the world went back to the blue and the hum. I must have dozed and woke and dozed like a bad clock. In one of the dozes I was back in the kitchen looking at the constable’s number and deciding to call him back because I wanted to get ahead of something you cannot get ahead of. He had said, Eight-thirty, and when I heard my voice ask, Will he look like himself, will I be able to tell, the constable was silent for long enough that I heard the room behind him, a radio with the volume nearly off, a man or a chair moving, and then he said, He’s been in the water a bit. These things can surprise you either way, he said, and I said, Okay, okay, as if okay were a word that could learn something new on a Tuesday.

By three, the ship felt like it belonged to the night and not the other way round. A young couple sat with their heads together, and you could feel the small heat under their hair. A man cursed when he tripped over a bag in a tightness of sound that made everyone decide whether to look up, and then didn’t. The boy with the penguin had fallen asleep with his mouth open, and his mother held her jaw in her palm like she had a headache you could cradle.

I went out on deck again because the inside of me made a noise I didn’t know how to answer. The cold went through the fabric as if the fabric had never been claimed for warmth. This time I walked to the stern to see the water open itself to us and be left. There is a beautiful, cruel honesty in the way a ship makes a white caul of the sea and then it comes back together behind you, the world undoing what you have done as you do it. I thought of Simon sitting on a quay in a town far to the north where he’d gone to work on boats because you could make money up there for a while and then it stopped the way anything stops that a town can’t afford to pretend isn’t stopping. He’d called me from a payphone once, the sound of it so loud that at first I put the phone away from my ear and then put it back, and the first thing he did was to laugh a little the way he always started when things were bad, like he was telling you a story about someone else, and then he’d said, Guess what, and then talked for eight minutes and told me nothing that could help me guess anything. Later in that call he’d used the phrase You wouldn’t believe what it’s like and I had not liked that, because it is a phrase that already includes within it the failure of the other person to have been anywhere with you. He never told me where he slept that winter. He said work as a word like a ship’s name—without article, with dread, like a thing that is not to be described but to be survived.

I found the tiny prayer of a cigarette butt at my feet and ground it under my shoe as if I had something to do with the extinguishing of small things. The black water had a question in it you couldn’t answer without not being a person any more. I went back inside and the watch said four, then five, then six, and the soup in the bar smelled like soup, and the fryers were back to building their clouds, and the staff were rubbing their faces with the heels of their hands in a way that made you know they’d started their shift when we were still a day away in our minds.

Around seven, the dark lifted not by lightening but by thinning, and the glass went from mirror to film and then genuinely to see-through. The window gave the first faintness of coast like a line left by a pencil that wanted to run out. People started the small rummages we do when we pretend to have been ready the whole time. The boy with the penguin waited until he could be allowed to be excited again and then was. The truckers put back their boots in their boots. There was a horn, shorter, and then the world made its first shapes: the cranes, gulls, the low houses near the quay that say, We know what wind is. We pulled into the slot between concrete and concrete, the rope came off the ferry in muscles, the men in high-vis shouted in a code that looked like family. The gangway moved into us and we became a place again with doors.

A man stood at the bottom with a clipboard and that particular face of official kindness. Are you Mr. Law? he asked as if he’d asked the same thing of too many people. I said yes. He said, I’m Constable McKay. He was younger than his voice. His hair had made a kind of decision too early and left his head in preparation for the winter you must imagine. He said, If you’re ready, and I thought, No, not now and not ever, which is a form of blasphemy if you’re a person who believes in there being a time for anything. I followed him out into the air that had already been used that morning by gulls and men hauling nets. The smell of fish was both exactly what it is and something too much like sex to mention. The car was small, the inside litter-free, the air crisp in the way of a car that is owned by a man who believes the inside of his things should be less dirty than the outside of him. He drove well, hands at ten and two, not talking immediately like the kind of kindness you can’t buy.

We passed low houses with washing lines hung with no washing, a man in a flat cap smoking with his eyes closed like he had once let his eyes be the person responsible for deciding things, a dog on a short lead peeing in segments on three consecutive poles, a school with words like welcome in languages we do not expect in towns like this and yet that have been here maybe the longest. He said, Did you get in okay? and I heard myself say, Yes, as if it mattered now that I had arrived at the place where something would be announced to me in a form that didn’t rely on me at all. He said, Look, we’ll go straight there if that’s okay, and if you want after that, we’ve got a room if you want to sit a bit, and there’s a cafe, and I’ll walk you there. He said and a lot, like many soft nets thrown around hard fish.

The hospital was not really a hospital but the part of a hospital that holds the day for a while. Inside smelled of the things they use to make us believe that death is not a kind of returning to dirt. On a chair near the door a woman with hair flattened by a night that had not given her what she wanted sat with her hands cupped in her lap like she held a small precious bird, and I wanted to kneel and look into the empty of her hands and say, It will pass, this hour, this hour will pass. The constable held doors open for me like he had a code in which allowing someone's back to remain undefended was the sort of crime you didn’t need a uniform to notice. He introduced me to a woman with silver hair and a badge with letters too thin for my eyes, and she said my name and the syllables in it felt heavier than when I had given it to Customs on summer holidays, heavier than when Zoe used it in bed and took a piece out of it for herself. She said, If you're ready, and I nodded as if I were part of a choreography we had rehearsed.

The room was cold in a way that sits behind surfaces. There is a kind of cold where you can say to your hand, warm this, and it listens. This was not that cold. A metal table with wheels. The sheet a version of practical that does not present itself as poetry. The woman waited for me to be the one to say something, and when I did not, she did not punish me for it. She said, We’ll just show you the face, for now, and we’ll give you any time you need. There is a phrase someone had carved into such sentences: any time you need, any time you need, and they will stand in the doorway and look nowhere. She drew the sheet back.

There is a way in which water changes a person who has been itself for some number of days. It takes the sentences first, the inner words you think make the person you know. Then it rounds. Then it bluish-fades. The eyes maintain a blank you could write one very bad poem about and no more. I had rehearsed that I would find the incisor and the hole in the ear and some child thing that only I would know and then would say yes or would say no and that would be the end of it. But what presented itself to me was the topography of a place I had loved when I was young and had not seen in so long that I could not be sure the river hadn’t moved. The brow was his or it was like his. The swell of the cheek had become something else. The mouth—there was a cut down one side that ruined the words I had for it. I looked for the scar under the chin, which would be no longer the way it was, and I couldn’t tell. The ear lobe looked unattached and then the other one didn’t. I thought, This is a person who is not my brother. Then, in the same second, I thought, This is a person who is.

I stood with my hands at my sides like the men in paintings who have been told to be good. The woman waited. The constable took a breath in and let it out like you do when you are thinking of someone else’s child. I thought of the anchor key ring and wondered if it had been his, and if it had been his once, once had been many years ago. I thought of how inadequate face is for someone you have known inside an entire childhood, how the shape of knowing him had moved from face to everything and then had narrowed back to one thing on a table. I thought of Zoe and how she had watched me not call. I thought of my father in the long year and the way he had made eggs ten different ways as if there would be one that could contain what had gone.

I surprised myself. I said, Can I see his hands. The woman looked at the constable and the constable nodded like he had been thinking something similar and had not wanted to be the one to say it. She drew back the sheet farther, and there they were, open-palmed, obedient in a way Simon’s hands had not often been. The thumb on the left, the small, healed line near the joint that he’d got falling off the wall. The curl of scars gone white at the knuckles from the summer of the crate of bottles when we were thirteen, stolen and released on the back step in a moment of theft we had called a raid. Between the second and third finger of the right hand, a small crescent where he had once pulled a hook out of the belly of a fish and failed to be careful with it. None of these are unique to a man. All of them made me bend my head as if I were going to be sick and then not be sick.

The woman said something and I did not hear it. I leaned forward in a form of bowing before a body that had nothing now to do with prayer. Out of some part of me I had not been inside in years I felt the urge to touch the hand, not even to be sure, but to complete something started. I didn’t. I had the sense that if I did, some geometry would collapse in the room and I would find myself on the floor with the sheet and the body and the day would never advance past this light. Instead, I put my own hands behind my back, the way schoolboys do when they have nothing to say.

He had, as a boy, taught himself to write with his left hand because of a teacher who believed children should be able to do both, a small cruelty that we had both then loved the way you learn to love the discipline that leaves its first marks on you. He had written me a note once when he was eight with his left hand and it looked like ogham, like not for this language, and I had kept it, and when I thought of it now, I knew which drawer it was in: the bottom, under the socks that do not match. There is such a grief in the details. They become the only yard you mow again and again, long after anyone comes down to check on the lawn.

I found my voice in the throat given to younger men. I said, I think, and then the word was a full minute away, behind the glass of the day the constable had promised me. I said, I think it’s him. The woman put a small X beside something on the paper she held, a line, a box. She had the air of people who are good at the world even when the world has not been good to them. The constable put his hand on the door frame and let his head bow for a second, then lifted it. I heard the small catch she’d made with the sheet as she smoothed it back.

He said, We’ll need you to look over the effects, and I made a sound that I hoped sounded like a yes but might have been a cough. He led me down another hall to a small room that was a room the family is always led to in the films, the place that has one canted window and two fine cracks in the plaster. There on a tray was what remained: a belt, swollen with salt, the buckle clean because of how metals work even when hearts don’t; a coin, three keys, the salt-shrunken leather that had been the ring he kept the keys on, and there, its little keloid of brass, the anchor. It was like the one in the magazine but it was older, scratched where the stem joined the arms. The constable said, Do you recognize any of this, and I touched the key ring with my finger and saw the grain of my skin in the tarnish. I said, Yes, I said, We had one like that, I said, I think.

When I stood up too quickly, the room went narrow and then came back. He put his hand out then, in that way, to be there and not there at the same time. He said, Take your time, and I thought of all the time we had not taken, all the time Simon and I had traded to strangers, to weather, to work, to the old lies that stand up on their back legs and call themselves necessity. He said, There’ll be papers. If you like I can make a cup of tea. I sat back down like an obedient person whose job it is to do what you can on the day you can, because that is all it turns out anyone asks of you and also more than that.

Across the narrow window the light shifted and sat itself on a van across the way and didn’t move again. I pressed my fingers into my eyes until I saw stars that were mine and not part of any sky. In the sense that writing is not a thing you can do with your body, I wished for the photograph in my bag and did not reach for it. It is a strange trick what we carry into rooms, the objects that participate in us. I took my hands down and looked straight at the tray again, and this time, with a clean, dreadful certainty that does not allow embellishment, I could see that the leather of the key ring had been relooped with a strip that wasn’t there when I had last held it: a narrow brown leather cut from the edge of a belt that wasn’t this belt, rough and lopsided, the work of Simon’s hand, the particular way he had always made do and then undone what he made do.

I said, It’s him. I did not add, and we were already the people who could say that. He nodded and his mouth made the movement it makes before you put the right phrase on it, a thing you can give a man and he will put it in his pocket and touch it now and then to see if it’s still there.

He said, Would you like to see him once more, and I said no, and then I said yes, and then I said, Could I have a minute, and he said, Of course, and left me in a small room with what wasn’t alive, with an object that had begun its service to one boy under an older sky. If I had been someone else I might have knelt and prayed. Instead I touched the key ring with my thumb hard once, in a gesture that had nothing to do with anything, and then put my thumb to my mouth like a child and tasted the old bitter of metal.

On the ferry, in the night, I had thought: I will arrive and I will be the person who does this, and afterwards there will be hours that continue like a belt through its day, hole by hole, until no more holes. Now, in the light that declined to rise into morning beyond mere sufficiency, I knew the day would need to be lived in its small segments instead, in the tiny rituals of the strange: the phone call to tell a person who could spell my last name with one hand, the call to the man who had always deferred to me when it came to the difficult moments, the texts from the handful of people who had continued to carry a low-res image of me in their pockets. The tea that would sit and go cold. The winter cough in a mother a few streets away that would give her the belief she was old and then not, the ferry ready to industrial-announce its departure again, the water drawing its white line and correcting it.

When I stood it was as if the floor had come a small way toward me. The constable was in the hall, not looking at his phone, a courtesy I noticed with full and unwarranted gratitude. He said, If you’d like, I can show you where to get something to eat, and I nodded though I had no idea how you would put food into such a body. He said, We’ll take care of him, and the phrase sat a while and then moved a little into the part of me that needed to know there were people in the world who used these pronouns with accuracy. On the way down the corridor a porter wheeled a cart of linen and the smell of the metal shelves was the smell of childhood and of boats and of everything the skin learns to expect when hands have been asked to hold their shape for too long.

Outside, the air had warmed a degree because the sun had made up its mind to pretend. The gulls had opinions and voiced them over a skip near the back of the building, the fish-sour of their day already on their beaks. The town lay itself out like a man who had the right to his mess. People were making their ways to jobs, to school, to the corner where the new bakery had decided to be brave and bake something no one had asked for. I thought of the ferry across the water and the women with their cigarettes and the boys who slept on their bags and the men who managed to not make room and then did.

I turned my phone on and off once, like the person I am when I hope that the world will have changed in the second that separates on from off. There was a message from Zoe that said, Here when you’re back, and an old one from a number I do not have saved that read years ago: You’d laugh if you saw this sky, S. It had been sent three winters back, in the month when I'd believed in the long, flat patience of the year ahead. Whether the man in the room in there had sent it was now, briefly and fully, a question with an answer I could carry in my pocket or not, a question that would not change what there was on the table in the room without windows. I moved the phone into another pocket as if it were dangerous near my heart.

We walked toward the car, the constable a half-step ahead, and I tried not to hear in that rhythm anything like the way we step on boats, the way we step into mornings we have not consented to. I thought of the skiff and my father’s instruction on knots: the way you need to know which kind of rope you are dealing with before you decide how to bind it, that different fibres understand different forms of commitment. I thought, too, of the way Simon could turn a knot into a story and then not untie it when he was meant to and carry it around fashioned to sit there until such time as it damaged another thing, the old art of the unintended. I did not cry because there is a time for that and it had not granted me its permission yet.

We came out under the little bit of sky the hospital keeps for itself and the gulls scissored it and the wind took me a small way and I let it. I put my hands in my pockets and felt the ghost shape of my brother's key ring under my thumb, felt the round of the brass and the small sharpness where the arm joined. I could feel the ghost of his laugh back in my throat, and it wasn’t cruel now, not the way it had been once, it was simply my body doing what it had been taught to do with absence. The constable unlocked the car. Somewhere a ship sounded its long single note into a day that would, as always, accept it and then forget, and then there would be lunch, and then somebody else would cross the water and become the person they were required to be, for a set of hours, and then again after that they would try to be the other thing, the private citizen of themselves. I stood there and inhaled and thought of the sheet and the hand beneath it and of the old boy we had been with his tricks with paper boats and his belief that making a thing set sail is the same as promising it an arrival. The engine started. The air offered no change and then a small one. We got in.
