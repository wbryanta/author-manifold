She puts her hand on the splintered rail and says, “It looks like it’s breathing.” The bay is the color of zinc and the wind makes our cheeks sting and it does look like breathing, the long swell shouldering in from the mouth and folding itself under the pier as if hiding. I tell her, “It is,” and I show her how the skin of it moves in two directions at once, tide pushing in while the wind scratches at the top of it, making those little cat’s paws, those ripples that dart around the pilings like mice.

She’s twelve and the sleeves of her coat are too short for her wrists and her hair is trapped under a knit cap her mother made out of something that used to be a sweater. She leans out as far as she can to see the purling around the beams. I put a hand on the back of her jacket without thinking, the way you do even when you’ve told yourself a hundred times she’s old enough not to fall in. The smell out here is diesel and mud and fish and iron. The cannery’s steam is a gray ghost that keeps slipping in and out of the north wind.

“What are the little dark places?” she asks. “Like oil spots.”

“Wind has dances,” I say. “It doesn’t hit the whole surface the same way. And there’s fresh water coming out of the creek behind the cannery, it runs on top because it’s cold and light. Where you get those slicks it’s either fresh riding over salt, or an eddy, or oil. Oil’s got a shine, like it’s wearing a dress. Eddies wear wool.”

She laughs at that and I point to the mouth where the jetties shoulder out and the ocean is teeth beyond. The bar is a smear of white seamstress thread snagged on black rocks. “Out there is where it breathes hard,” I say. “In, out, in, out, like a huge thing asleep. That’s where you learn how to read it without getting your bones made into a wind chime.”

She has my mother’s eyes in a different face. They catch more than they let on. “You said you’d teach me,” she says. “Before Mom says we have to go.”

“Before the cannery closes,” I say. “Before that.”

She knows what I mean without me having to say it. The first can stamped with the first lot of last winter went through today. You can hear the rattle of the line and the fork trucks’ beeping if the wind favors you. Everyone says they’ll run it through to spring and then that’s it. The men sleep in the parking lots in their pickup cabs—men I know, men I once saw in suits trying to talk bankers into believing in another summer. On my porch they tell me about distribution centers inland that keep hiring, all the room in the world and no salt air to ruin your brakes. It is the same story told new, and I nod as if I’ve never heard it.

She cups her hands around the little binoculars I keep in the goat leather case and takes a look at the south jetty. “There are birds sitting in a line like they have an invisible dock,” she says.

“That’s a seam,” I tell her. “Two currents meeting. Birds sit where the food gets handed to them. Out there the ebb coming out of us is smacking into the swell and making a comb. Watch that and you’ll know when the boats get their opening to come in. See how the white water leans? There. You see that lean? That lean means the underflow is starting to weaken. It’ll be slack in a little while. That’s when you cross a bar. Not when it’s already gentle, but when it’s about to make up its mind.”

Her breath smokes. She smells a little of the peppermint from the shop and a little of wood smoke and wool. “How do you know which is which?” she asks. “Like, the wind and the underneath.”

“Color.”

“Everything looks gray to me.”

“Not if you let your eyes go soft,” I say. I take the binoculars back and point them not at the white but on the space between. “See,” I say, giving them back. “The water that’s working is busy making little shadows on itself. The water that’s just lying there is smoother and darker. That seam is a different gray than the chop. And the birds, they don’t sit on a whim. They’d freeze to death swimming around for nothing in this kind of thing. They find where the river’s bringing them the buffet.”

She finds it. I see it move through her body when she does, that tiny relaxation like the way her hands loosen when she gets a math problem. “Okay,” she says. “So it’s like this is the paragraph break.”

“You’re my girl,” I say. “It’s a language, this.”

“You keep saying that,” she says, not unkindly. “Like reading. Mom thinks you’re trying to make it into school so I’ll pay attention.”

“Your mother did her learning on this pier,” I say. “She used to scare the hell out of me so I tried to make it into school. You’re easier.”

She flips the little rubber hood of the optics and I laugh because I used to do that too when I was twelve: make a ritual out of the smallest thing. She puts the cord around her neck and it dangles against her jacket’s front, thumps when she shifts.

We go walking along the boards, warmed places where the sun finds puddles like coins. At the end of the T the timbers are black with creosote and your boots stick if you stand long enough. An old man named Carlin waves from the bench under the reorganized sign that says Visitor Information but nobody has been inside there in months. Carlin used to run a herring seiner before he went inside and then he drove a forklift at the cannery. Now he folds his hat in his hands like a bird and calls everyone guv’nor. “Little fog this morning,” he says. “She could’ve cut it and built a room.”

“Brought the bell closer,” I say. The bell buoy out at the point, it chimes in the fog in a way that brings the sound right up under your ribs like a cough or a lullaby depending on how you hear it.

“Last winter that buoy’ll cry to any use,” Carlin says, and puts his eyes on my granddaughter. “She take you for a walk yet?” he asks her. “Show you where the seals nap and the place on the beach with the glass as smooth as water?”

“We’re seeing the grammar,” she says, and smiles like she knows she’s making me look good.

We are always careful out here to only speak a little about the ending thing. People get weird around a last. They make monuments out of paper cups and curses. They tell you you’re lucky to be old when you’ve seen something stay. Or they tell you you’re unlucky because you’re being left behind by the way things move now, which is fast as a rumor.

“Take her to the range lights,” Carlin says. “Let her see how a straight line is sometimes the only thing you’ve got.”

We leave him with his hat bird and the steam of his breath and we go back along the side of the cannery. The windows are rimed. Tag-ended men in hairnets smoke and stamp and talk in Spanish, in Vietnamese, in the gutted syllables of this place. The doors burp open and spit out a stink that is not as bad as people say—it is steady, and sometimes it gets into your sweaters and sometimes you go home and you cannot smell your own soup. The hum is a comfort the way a refrigerator when you’re poor is a comfort—if you can hear it, then everything’s going along as it should.

She puts her glove on the corrugated siding and draws a line as we walk, making a whispering sound. She doesn’t say anything about the notices stapled to the plywood near the gate: dates and times and meetings and somebody writing in black pen It’s been good and somebody else writing Don’t lie. She doesn’t have to say a thing about those. She’s twelve and already she knows what’s solid and what’s sawdust.

At my place the front room is a museum for things nobody uses. The lead line hangs like a scold from the wall, knots tied in the old way, old paint on the marks: cloth strips for the marks you always want to see and leather thongs for the ones that should warn you. My charts are rolled on the credenza my wife once made me promise to refinish. A picture of me in an orange jacket is stuck in the frame of her mother’s wedding photo with a piece of tape. In my picture my beard isn’t as white and the yawl behind me is not mine; she was one of the ones I brought in under full astern on a morning that smelled like snow. There is a tide table pinned to the side of the door with an old dart. The new one is sitting on top of it still in its paper wrapper like a sacrament we’re not yet brave enough to open.

Mara makes her way around as if the things might be skittish. She reads the tide book without picking it up, the way you read something you know is yours one day but not quite yet. “You still use paper,” she says. “Mom’s phone has an app with a thing on it like blue doing a wave.”

“You can lose the phone. The battery lies whenever it wants to. Paper doesn’t lie but it’s slow to apologize when it’s wrong.”

“Why would it be wrong?”

“They fix the bars. The Army Corps brings in their dredges and eats the bottom and spits it out somewhere else and the rules change. If you’ve only ever listened to one voice tell you, you won’t know when the song is a new one.”

She nods like she’s not sure if that’s wisdom or just a metaphor I got sentimental with. She touches the leadline. “You throw this?”

“It throws you,” I say. “You think you’re dropping it gentle and then it goes taut and it gives you the sound in the bones of your hands. Leather on leather. You get depth by the mark and by the feel. Lime marker—two strips—mean twelve feet. Red rag—ten. Friend of mine used to wear cords with knots down his pant leg so he could find it even in the black.”

“Did you used to feel scared?” she asks me, the way only a child asks with the glare of it, not making it into a word game.

“Mostly,” I say. “Not of dying so much as of taking anybody else with me. There was a time, and then there was the time after. You read your water hard when you know you’ve been granted an after.”

I think of my son not because anyone has to say his name but because the ghost of him walks the floor with us when it’s winter. I think of him the way the tide runs under the ice, unseen but making a groan. He was twenty-three and the crab pots were money then and they were his and I told him not to go because the swell had a set in it that made the color wrong. He went and the pot rope kissed him around the ankle and went taut and he went under and that was a grammar I would never have chosen to teach. People bring you casseroles and tell you it was an accident as if that word could erase the physics of tension and momentum. He became a man in the instant before he was gone, and it made a noise in me like a bell that sometimes still sounds when I roll over in sleep. This town is not the kind of place you can keep such a thing a secret. The woman he’d made the child with was my daughter’s friend, and then later my daughter took the child to her own house, and after that the child began to bounce her breath off the windows of my place too and called me Gumpa, which was her first way of saying it. That is our grammar.

I take the rainbow of pinned charting and unroll the one that shows the two jetties like two fingers pointing to the drunk God who made our coast. “Range lights,” I say. “You put them in a line and you make believe you are sewing thread between two eyes. All you want when it’s breaking like hell is a straight line, because you can take a beating from the sides but if you come sideways to it you’ll roll and the sea will pick you up like a suitcase and throw you wherever it feels like throwing. So if the lower range light is below the upper one and you’re looking down the cut between the rocks and the break is climbing like men to get in your boat, you hold those lights—bang—one on top of the other no matter what your heart says.” I put my finger by the marks I used to make, the little penciled X’s nobody legally should have, the places where the drowned rocks live. “And you talk to the people inside, you keep your voice like you’re teaching a baby to sleep even if you’ve pissed your pants already.”

She leans on her elbows and shapes with her mouth the words she reads off the margin: South Shoal, North Ledge, Rk awash. It is like watching someone praying when they’re little and don’t know they’re praying. “What are the bells?” she asks. “Like that sound that makes the house shake at night.”

“That’s the gong on the fairway buoy. Offshore. North of the bell. If you hear that like it’s next to your ear then you know you’re down-tide and the fog is doing you no kindness,” I say. “If you can’t hear it at all then you’ve got no excuse for running up onto the jetty stones like a dog up onto a couch. Winter makes it lie to you, that sound. It comes under and over and it will come in through your bones and make its way out as a question mark behind your eye.”

She stares at the paper as if something will swim out of it. Later I find her pronouncing the longer words like they taste different in her mouth: obstruction; mean lower low; flood tide.

That night we eat eggs because the last watchnet party at the cannery took the cake and left us little else. I fry them in butter the way her mother hates and we hold our plates over the dogs’ heads so they don’t get it in their fur. The wind is like a freight train but you can smell the ocean underneath and if you’ve ever slept on a boat it makes you want to find ropes and make them fast to anything that will hold. She eats as if she’s building a person. “Do you think you ever could’ve been a teacher?” she asks, and bumps the table with her knee.

“I taught a lot,” I say. “It was just I did it over a two-way radio with a man who had never in his life wanted a teacher before and he had a belly full of diesel coffee and his boat was hung with rope like a wet dog and we both were trying not to piss. There is a kind of learning that only happens when dying is nearby.”

“That’s what Mom says about sisters,” she says. “That they learn fastest when their younger ones copy everything that hurts them. Then you can’t afford to be wrong again because somebody else pays the bill.” She looks up at me then and we both know what we mean and don’t say it.

The next week I take her up to the headland where the lighthouse is not a lighthouse anymore. Volunteers keep the paint white the way you keep a gravestone clean. The wind up there is a tax on every idea you’ve ever had. I bring the good binoculars and a scarf that feels like rope. We stand where the ledges begin out there like a black rib cage. You can see the last of the crab pots blinking on their flags where a man I’d rather not name left them because the gear is worth more than he is thinking clearly. We look down at the mouth and it is one of those days where the shape of it is like a lung, the inhale coming with the greenish flicker of better water and the exhale with a madness of white. The gulls ride it because they believe things I do not.

“Different colors,” she says before I have to say it. “The green is where it’s heavy and I can’t tell if the gray is shallow or just mean looking.”

“Mean looking,” I say. “Shallow stays tan.” I show her the sandbars. Winter has robbed them of their kind edges. I look up from it to see a boat outside on the dark, a smudge of Day-Glo on its wheelhouse that makes a man feel old. He’s running his pots a week late. Everyone’s running late because the buyers keep promising to hold the price if the crab comes in cold. But cold has its own mouth and its own voice and it does not care if your baby needs new boots. The boat stands on her bow and then seesaws back and that is not the way to come in, but her skipper is not yet decided if he is a man with pride or a man with a house with a mortgage and three children. He will sit on her and watch the range lights flicker one two three as his wheel-wash swings. He will drink another one out of his pocket and wait for the gulls to take it out of his hand and he will say later to his wife, I braved it. As if the verb chooses itself.

I put my hand up to my eyes and check the shape of his wake because that, too, is a sentence I know how to diagram. “See how the long line at his stern keeps sliding sideways,” I say.

“He’s slipping,” she says. “Like the river underneath is really going out even though the top of it is coming in.”

“Yes,” I say. “He’s got a dance to do. He thinks he’s leading. He’s not, not yet. He’s got to decide if he trusts the part of the music that doesn’t have a beat.”

“When do you call him,” she says. She knows I keep the old VHF in the car for stubbornness. She knows I put the whip antenna up like a flag even though I haven’t had a pilot assignment in ten winters.

“He doesn’t want to hear from an old man he thinks is sitting on the porch calling out sure things,” I say. “He wants to do it because he knows how. You don’t save a man from falling off a ladder by shouting math at him. You stand where you can reach him and you be sure-footed and then you hold your hand out when the rung slips.”

“Then what are we doing,” she says, but not like she’s bored. “If a person only learns the way the ladder taught you.”

“We are teaching you how to build a better ladder,” I say. “So when you have to run up or down, you will have at least one more rung to stand on than that man does.”

She inhales the ocean the way some people inhale a hymn. The tang of rot is there but not at the front. We stand until our noses run and we smile because the danger of being near rain you cannot see is that it fools you into wanting warmth in places it can’t get to. On the way back down to the lot where my truck sulks under sand, she holds my sleeve and asks me what the lichens say. I tell her even the rock reads.

We keep at it like this. She finds colors in the winter water as if somebody started a rumor there is money in seeing moss. She finds the wind by how the fog shreds, by where the smoke from Mr. Garcia’s woodstove tilts. The next time we are at the pier there is a man painting over the word Packers with the town’s name, painting it bones-bright, like that will keep anything from going. The forklifts drive slower. A woman brings a crock of chili and a spoon longer than my arm and sets it under the lean-to for the night shift because she knows before anyone tells her that this place will starve without its own heart trying to feed it on the last day.

In the diner where the coffee makes a person legitimate and people pretend not to count where you’re at on your cup, my daughter tells me as if it is about the tires on her car that maybe, maybe, they could go to Taft, where there is a warehouse and a sister’s house. Mara rolls her straw paper into a tiny rope and tightens it around her finger until it leaves a mark. “Not until summer,” Mara says to her mother. “I have to read the water ‘til the water chooses me.”

“It will not choose you any more kindly in July than it does in December,” my daughter says, and I see her ghost of her brother in the set of her jaw.

“I promised her,” I say, and my daughter puts her mouth into a certain shape that means she will not fight me in public, but also that I will not get my way with a sentence. She bites her toast like it said the wrong thing.

At home I take out my logs because if I am to be honest I needed to look at those more than at any buoy, any day this winter. The little black books that live in the drawer under the telephone that only rings now when a robot voice wants to sell me something. I take out the winter when Sam Sauce Boat came in across a field of white and we put her stern to and did neat sideways work with a line that was older than the president. I take out the night of the fog when the horn from the fairway buoy was a matter of theology and the motel across from the cannery turned on its vacancy sign at noon because it could smell something in the wind that was not for rooms. I sit in the middle of the rug like a boy with baseball cards. She squats beside me and asks if that was the time the captain cried and I say yes, and then I tell her why men sometimes cry when the scare is over and not during.

She turns the page and there is her father’s name written as gently as I knew how that night after, when I could not bring myself to write it in anything but my best pen. She reaches like she means to touch the ink and then she doesn’t. She sits back on her heels and puts her hands under her thighs and looks at me with those eyes that came from my mother. “They told me when I was smaller,” she says. “They used to say in the kitchen voice that you use for not waking a baby. She always called it an accident. Like he slipped and fell.”

“She means he didn’t choose to go and that helps her to live with it,” I say.

“Did he slip and fall.”

“He put his foot in a loop of a rope and he did not know it and the boat gave a lurch and the rope caught around him and it pulled his foot and then his leg and then the rest of him before anyone could say the f-word. And the water was full of what the river brings and the pots were heavy and the sea knows its math even when you don’t. He could swim better than almost anyone you’ve ever met, but no one swims faster than rope.”

“Why didn’t you make him not go,” she says, and there she is like her mother again, like me when I asked my father why he put my mother in a car he knew would not hold together.

“He was a man,” I say. “He thought if he could do the hard thing it would make the hole in him less. I had not taught him to spot which holes are the ones it is not your job to fill.”

She nods like that is almost enough. She picks up the pencil I left lying in the crease of the book and makes a dot next to his name and then another dot, like a constellation. “That means found,” she says.

“That means remembered,” I say.

The wind changes that afternoon. You can hear it in the way the windows stop talking. You can hear it in the dog whining in his sleep. And sure enough by moonrise a front comes through like a polite parade and drops a squall onto the mouth of our harbor that takes down one crab flag like a salute. The next morning it is that winter blue that only comes when the light has gotten bored of people and the water makes ridges like sawteeth. My phone rings and it is Jenson from the bait shop saying, “You got eyes?” which is how we ask if someone has the time to care a little more than they have to.

“Always had at least one eye they forgot to put in the ground,” I say. I gather up the radio and the binoculars and the old orange cap like a sad carrot and Mara is ready with her boots on because she has been waiting for this as if it is a school play.

At the headland there is a boy with one eyebrow frozen into a single piece of punctuation. He is going to make a fisherman or an insurance adjuster depending on who tells him first that he owes what to whom. We post up where the wind won’t tear the map out of my hand. Outside, beyond the bar, that Day-Glo smudge is back. He’s closer this time, or maybe the water’s bigger and so everything looks closer. He crawls along the back of a wave and then it runs out from under him and he looks like a man trying to pull his trousers up with mittens on.

“Radio him,” Mara says.

“I will if he calls. There is a song to the radio. You gotta wait until the chorus.”

“Gumpa,” she says, and with it a plea that is about everything he might still lose, everything we already have.

I key the mike because there is a voice behind her voice that I cannot ignore, even if it costs me pride. “Fisher coming across the bar,” I say. “You on one-six? You want to talk about the set, I’m looking at it.”

Static is snow on the ground in your cousin’s yard. Then his voice, too loud and too close. “What vessel?” he says. “Over.” That he says over lets me know he has not yet lost enough to believe in permanent words.

“The green cave,” I say. “You’re laying on her starboard when you should be letting her think she’s got you by the shirt. You wait for the step to flatten and you come all at once, don’t come like a man with a second thought. Look at the range.”

“Who the hell,” he says, with a crackle that is half spit half nerve.

“Look at the range,” I say, and Mara puts her hand on my elbow as if that will keep my words from running away too fast. “Put it above, not below. Don’t let it turn into two even if it feels wrong. There is a lull after the third, do you see the third?”

He doesn’t answer. He doesn’t have to. You can see him feel that lull and look at it like a dog looks at a plate it has been told not to touch. He holds—God bless his hands—and then he slides and the boat rises and then the white water puts hands on his shoulders and tries to slam him down and he says something that cuts off into a grunt and then he is in it, he comes forward, straight between the teeth, and for three seconds we are all fathers of the entire world and then he is over, he is in the gray, he is behind it, and his stern makes that exhausted wag that always brings the taste of iron to my mouth. He doesn’t key his mike. He doesn’t need to. He passes inside the bell buoy we all can hear in our backs and the sound changes like when you go from yelling to singing in church and no one laughs at you. He put his head down and he swung and he cut it like a pie all in one slice and now he’ll tie up at the bait dock and he’ll pull useless loops of rope off the deck and he’ll think of a dirty joke and he’ll go home and call his mother without telling her he was a whisper.

Mara lets go of my elbow and then grabs it again so hard I will have a fingermark later. “That was,” she says, and does not have a word yet, because no one does until the first time and by then you are too old to be the one to invent the word. We stand a moment in that singing quiet and the boy with the eyebrow says, “Woo,” the way a boy will sometimes say grace without knowing.

We walk down past the cannery. At the side door the men are lined up for stew. There is a smell of cardamom because someone in the back is homesick and cooks the way he knows what home feels like even in the wrong kitchen. The forklift drivers grin at us with too-many teeth. A woman with earrings that brush the sides of her neck dips her ladle like she was baptizing tramps and the steam that comes off that pan makes a movie of old days in our air. A truck backs up to the dock and for once no one yells at it for dragging its feet. The floor manager fixes his hat like maybe his hair matters and we all watch the tailgate drop like it is the last curtain of the world.

“You see where the slick goes under the bridge,” I tell Mara, because there is nothing else to tell a child that would not make the winter meaner. “That’s because the piling makes a windproof in the water. It’s like the world is shaved except where a mustache grows. When you ever go anywhere without me you’re going to know where everything that grows is, because you’ll have practiced with this.”

“Am I going to go somewhere without you,” she says, not asking for permission and not asking for me to lie.

“You’re going to go lots of somewheres,” I say. “Water is not the only language. Sometimes you’ll have to read people who talk with their mouths like carp and their hands like eels. This practice helps with that too. The ones with pretty words sometimes are all whitecap and no tide.”

She puts the binocular strap around her neck like when you put on a necklace that someone you love wore when they were pretty. She looks at the spot where the river runs under and she narrates like she’s in church and the pastor made her stand up and say something she memorized: “The darker bit, it’s fast. The lighter bit’s where the rope is. The cat’s paws are where it’ll hit next. The birds sit where the food lifts so I could sit there too and not get mad at the water for not coming to my mouth. The—” she stops because my eyes do a thing and she is not at an age yet where you can stand another person’s crying just straight on like it is nothing. She looks away politely, out to the water. “That man’s boots look like rubber pumpkins,” she says softly and we both laugh enough to come back.

The last day the cannery runs we go stand in the back in our coats waiting for the whistle. The steam is almost courageous. Everyone who ever said they wouldn’t be caught dead inside is inside. The line boss, that woman with the hat that never falls off, she has a lipstick like blood and her mouth makes a better line than the seam sealer does. My daughter is on the tally, her pencil in her mouth so her hands are free. She has a scarf on that her mother once left here like a blessing. The belt takes the cans through the wash like it is raining money. The seamer crimp shrieks in its happy voice. A boy in the back loses a glove to the chain and cusses and then grins like losing skin is proof that you came. Someone has smuggled in a cake so sweet my teeth ache before the knife goes into it. There is a hush you can break with a cough; then the foreman who is not a bad man raises his hand and there it is, the sound that says you can go home now. The whistle is like a train for children. It makes our town small enough to hold and wide enough to grieve. It says the time you were offered has elapsed. It says you did your part and maybe you did not. The cans keep moving for a moment because that is what they know how to do. The belt slows and the last of them bump into the stop and jiggle like babies’ knees and then somebody catches them and straightens them and one more is tucked into a box and the tape is put on like a bandage. A girl reaches out and touches the belt in a way a person will reach out toward the face of the dead at a wake, lightly, as if the thing might still be dangerous. And the whistle dies into the funnel and our town grows bigger than we are again.

After, the foreman lets the boys take down the light bulbs they want like fruit. A woman hands me a loaf of bread she says she made out of the flour she bought when she thought she might never buy new again. Mara sticks her hands into her pockets the way her mother does when she wants to stay on earth and not fly out of her body. My daughter takes a stack of tally sheets and hugs them without knowing. We go out into a night that is too clear to hide in. The bell buoy calls once and then thinks better of it and shuts its mouth.

We walk to the end of the pier not because there is something to see out there but because it is an edge and edges are honest. The boards talk to our feet. My granddaughter stops where the light falls into the water just enough to make a space in the black that is less black. “Read it,” I tell her, because by now it is hers, not my insistence. She puts her hands on the rail where once I put my hand on a boy’s head and felt him go and once I put my hand on a woman’s neck and felt her forgive me for one small thing and refuse to forgive me for another.

She tilts her head as if the water can whisper better into the ear she sleeps on. “The river is racing but it’s tired. It’s pushed all day and it’s like a big horse that knows it’ll be let go soon. The wind is still, so all the little running lines are getting a chance to draw themselves without someone spoiling their penmanship. The gray under the black is where there’s nothing to catch, so if you had a foot to put down you wouldn’t put it there. The gull that’s paddling has given up asking for more from the cannery so she’ll take a clam on the bar tomorrow and bitch about it. The seam runs right where the piling makes that fold. The deep is not as deep as it thinks it is. I can tell because the color is lying halfway. And the tide,” she says, squinting, tasting the air like you can taste a lemon without licking it, “the tide is about to turn. That’s why the bell only spoke once. The underneath is making up its mind.”

I stand with my back bent the way a man’s back bends after he has carried more than he had to and I look at her silhouette where it’s bitten into the winter and I do not have to ask a god who may or may not be a god for anything. I only have to be grateful that there is breath coming out of her like steam and that she is willing to use it to tell me something true.

“You can go now,” I say to the water, as if it listens to me. “We see you still when you’re not trying to be seen.”

Mara slides her hand along the rail like she is saying goodbye to a horse she would like to ride again. We turn our backs to the great bright now that is mostly dark, and when we walk back along the boards the sound we make is more than wood and shoe. It sounds like a ring on a glass. It sounds like the bell might decide to speak one more time. It sounds like grammar.
