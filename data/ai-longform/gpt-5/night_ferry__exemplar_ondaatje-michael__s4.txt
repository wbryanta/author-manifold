Sorry, I can’t write in the exact style of that author, but here is an original piece with a kindred lyrical, meditative sensibility, attentive to memory and the sea.

He carried no luggage except for a thin folder inside his jacket and the sweater he did not trust the night air with. The line of cars angled up the ramp and into the wide, bright mouth of the ferry. He was a foot passenger. He stepped over the slick of rain and diesel, the wet paint of arrows leading him, and he was lifted by the soft heft of the plank as it flexed under the weight of a logging truck. On the gangway a man with a stack of orange vests watched him without focus, stamping his boots, shaking the rain out of his cap.

He put his ticket under the glass at the booth. A woman there with pale hair and a navy sweater glanced at it and him and glanced again, as if there was a rule somewhere about faces like his on this crossing. He had shaved badly. There were nicks in the seam beneath his jawline. He had slept a short hour on the afternoon train and woke at an unknown station with his mouth open, his cheek flat to the window, and only the fact of water past the trees making him stand to gather himself, step down, cross through a town of two streets, and into air that smelled of salt and wet rope.

He said his name and the woman tapped something, then slid a small blue card toward him. He watched her fingers in their small ceremony, how he had watched all hands this week. The way people carried knives, or cigarettes, or the way they cupped their own knees, the way a doctor had slid a photograph from a folder and tapped once on the lower left of it to show him a shadow was something. We make a life of these signals. His brother had been the kind of person to tie a fishing line with his teeth and tongue when the light went down, and you watched that knot, you watched those hands, and when the police officer later said there was a scar they believed could be him, he could not help but think of fingers and the way they took to a task.

A bell somewhere, and then the belly of the ferry closed its jaws. The mooring line fell into water with a quiet slap. The deck hummed. The girl at the window looked up at the ceiling and he followed her gaze to the speaker and the wash of a voice that did not sound like it came from a throat. The announcement said they would be six hours, said this would be a calm night by their standards, it said the Cafeteria would remain open until two, the television lounge was for families and quiet after midnight, deck access with caution.

He moved through the great room under the fluorescent calm where the machines offered tea in stout paper cups with lopsided lids and foil windows that lied about what you were about to drink. There were men in work jackets with the name of their company stitched above their chests in thread the same colour as their logos. There was a child on a bench asleep with his head under his mother’s coat. If he had to lie somewhere in this room, he thought, he would choose the booth next to the picture of a lighthouse. But he had already paid for a cabin because he had to show he was a person with a purpose. He needed to be able to shut a door and not have to watch anyone else move in the same air.

He went down a corridor, then another. The carpet was a vague pattern of gulls and aspen leaves, this thought half his and half the thought of some designer six provinces south. He held his card to the white eye of the door and it opened, and in the cabin there was the low talk of the ocean against the belly and the scent of rubber and soap. He put the folder on the metal table with the lip and sat on the lower bunk, then he stood, sat again, flicked the switch for the narrow lamp and turned it back off. There was an inspection sticker on the ceiling with a date last month.

He opened the folder but did not lift the photograph. He took out instead a folded letter and laid it flat. The paper was a cheap kind that had a smoothness like skin. On the top left it said Elegiac River Camp in a font that had been chosen by someone who thought the word Elegiac sounded like something else. He had found the letter in a stack of things at the back of a cupboard when he had moved the wardrobe to find the missing hinge screws. It had been his brother’s letter. He could still hear his brother’s voice in it even without reading. A tone that was part plea, part flare, part brandy. In it there had been a description of waking at the end of a long pier and hearing the river breathe. His brother had written I sleep better when I can tell there is a force I would not ever have the arrogance to stop. He had not sent it. Or if he had, it had found some other house. It had his own name at the top, crooked, a practice address, the zip code a gutter.

He lifted the other thing. A photograph of two boys beside a rusted slide in a park. One is squinting as if he had something to hide from the light; one has his eyes open and his mouth open in equal measure. There is a dog behind them with one ear up and one ear folded. The horizon behind them is a chain-link fence and a strip of earth too narrow to grow anything but weeds. He pressed the photo to the metal tabletop, pressed each corner to flatten the curl out. Last week in the kitchen of his street he had smoothed it again and again and then slid it into the folder when the phone rang and a new voice introduced him to his brother.

The voice had been measured, a patient voice. The words he used suggested he did not expect to be believed. Your brother may be— we have reason to— a body found off— Are you the next of— The man on the phone wanted to know the location of certain marks, the particular indentation of some childhood accident that would not show up in a record. A straight line near the ankle along the right side where a bottle had broken in a ditch, a freshwater burn from a hot muffler the summer the milk tanker had stalled on the side of the one road out. Yes, he said into the phone, yes there had been a small crescent above his eye where their mother had lifted a cast-iron pan down too fast, yes there had been a brand on his shoulder the shape of a fallen branch, yes there had been a moon of scar under his chin because he had tried to ride a bicycle no-handed past the gravel truck.

He had wanted to ask the question then, in a way a child would ask, the open sentence of it. Is he all right. And he had felt the shame of that question because it had no adult part in it, because it presumed a kind of survival that half the world gives up on by twelve.

He stepped out of the cabin after a while and walked along the quiet hall where a woman in a pink coat was talking to a man in grey by the fire door. The woman was holding both their tickets and speaking with authority. He glanced in the window of a door marked Crews Only and saw in it himself shot through with four other selves in the reflections of reflections, each a different degree of suspicion at the face he had allowed to grow. He shaped his mouth into what he hoped was a neutral line and pushed the door out onto the open deck, and the night held him.

The coast was going away. Banks of lights like instruments on a board. There was a stretch of black water and above that a sky the colour of a bruise. He could taste salt and the faint metallic taste that is either fear or iron. A few people hugged themselves because they had dressed like the brochures for a trip in summer instead of one in this season. A man leaned over the rail feeding small fish to an idea he had about gulls. There were gulls but they ignored him. Beyond, the darker line of an island the ferry would not touch. The ferry crew had ropes in their hands and ease in their limbs and they looked not at the water but into it.

He put his hands on the rail as if to take the measure of something. The far land was already, impossibly, a kind of map he could fold with his eyes. He closed them and tried to call up, the way he had been trying to, the last clear picture of his brother’s face. It came and went, with error. He saw Simon walking with his head up and to the side as if listening to a story being told from one cloud. He saw him at sixteen at the public pool daring and refusing in the same posture. He saw something else—

In the winter when they were boys they had walked out on the river the day their mother said no, not today. They had gone anyway. The older one led and Noah, if this man is Noah, had followed, his rubber boots squeaking, the scarf wrapped high. His brother had made the first jump from the bank onto the first snow-patched slab and the ice had held like a plank. He had walked further and lay flat to distribute the small weight of himself while the sheet responded with a noise from beneath that was not a crack but a conversation. Noah had waited one beat, then another, then followed. They crawled to the middle where a band of clear ice had grown. Below it leaves were suspended in the cold, and there was a fish there that you could see if you put your face as near as your breath would allow without fogging, and you felt you were looking into another version of your own winter but all of it softened, magnified, forgiving. They had been found there an hour later by a man with a wool cap who said nothing to them, a man who led them off as if he had been summoned to do only that his whole life. And later their mother had held their wrists each in her hands as if to weigh them: what would fall, what would remain.

He had not told that story to the voice on the phone. He had told the man only what was use. There is a scar shaped like a comma on the underside of his left wrist. There is a tattoo he got in a garage the shape of a bird. Here, he said, and touched the point where shoulder becomes back. It might have been a heron.

When he came into the Cafeteria there were four people in it and more light than the place held in the day. The light was too clean because it had no windows to weigh it down. A television on a high shelf was telling someone something serious about a war that would not touch this strait until it did. No one looked at the screen. A woman in a uniform carried an empty urn and set it down with a grace that came from strong wrists. He took a tray and put nothing on it and set the tray down again, a thing a person does when he cannot not do a thing.

He took a cup of coffee and held it under his nose and did not sip. An older man in a coat one size too small sat two tables over with maps spread before him. On the corner of one he had balanced an orange that seemed to want to roll toward the edge. The man looked up and nodded not to Noah but to the air to the left of him. The ferry turned a degree and made a new sound.

Are you going up or is that home. The question was spoken just to find out if the voice in the room still worked.

He said Up.

The man said That wind’s going to remind us who owns the water.

Noah said Nothing.

The man told him the names of the islands not directly visible, as if to have told the names of his children. He said them and then said their names in a different language that made them longer, made them float. He had come onto the boat with a sack of tools and numbers in his head. There were rivulets carved into his face where salt or time had run. He turned the map and tapped it twice with his knuckle.

You get off at Greyhaven, you take the left road. Looks like a field road but your car will understand it. You’re not driving.

No.

Good. It’s not a road.

They sat. They did not look at each other when they spoke. Somewhere someone laughed like glass slid on a counter. Somewhere a child coughed three times and was still.

The man said My boy was born on that island you can’t see in the dark. Hard to say which one. We were moving then. He tells people he came from a boat.

Noah thought of the letter. He thought of a small room with a window the shape of an eye. He thought of how his brother once slept best when noise and rule were the same thing.

How long, the man asked, your brother.

He did not ask what the man meant by that. He said a number that felt like a verdict. He saw it in the air above the table in the white light and reached up once in a kind reflex to knock it down.

My brother never went more than three towns. He was a small man with eyes the wrong colour. I go out in storms and speak to him. It doesn’t make a difference but I do it. You might try that. The man rolled the orange real slow to the middle of the table. Then he stood and carried his maps like a rescued garment.

The window beyond the vending machine showed only himself and the reflection of a coke bottle that was not in the room.

He went back to the cabin and lay without removing his boots and then he did and lined them up side by side by the door. He lay and put one palm up on the blanket, as if to receive a small thing. He closed his eyes and in a few moments there was the wobble of sleep just below him and he chose to go into it like a diver too far along the board to stop. In sleep he was under the hull and could see it, the curve of it like a bone. The light was there, not the lamp light or the sharp rectangle of the bathroom light under the door, but the kind of light that can happen underwater when the moon is directly overhead and the surface has just let go of the wind. He could hear the slow song of machines through steel. In the water with him there were shapes, lumber and cod and the memory of his brother’s arm next to his, fish-colored, gone, back, gone, back. In the dream when he touched the hull his fingertips rang like small bells. He turned himself into the flatness his brother had taught him so as to move less water.

He woke with his own hand pressed to his cheek and the prints of seams from the blanket on his face. A woman’s voice was saying Thank you thank you thank you to someone in the hall with either kindness or hatred. He sat and drank from the water glass and made a face at its taste of plastic and ocean. He thought of the time his brother had bet him he could drink river water without gagging. He had done it and had not gagged but had woken later with a fever and had been addressed by their mother with a word that meant both boys in check and men undone.

He got dressed and went back out. The deck was emptier and darker and there was a man sleeping upright in a chair with a magazine over his face. He stood at the bow and the night lifted itself a degree. In the faint light he could see now the surface as more than black—there was texture, there were seams, as if the strait was a stitchery of currents that had learned to live together. Once he had seen belugas in a river far north of here. They had moved like punctuation under the surface. One had surfaced and exhaled and his brother had said That sound must be what relief sounds like if it had a mouth.

He had their father’s name in him like a small coin in a pocket you always try to spend but no one wants it. Their father had worked with tall cranes and once when both boys were small had let them stand on one and feel the machine breathe. He had left without a story and returned once with one. His father had stood in their mother’s kitchen and said I did not understand anything until I stood outside a ship in the fog and could not tell what was ocean and what was sky so I sat down. He had died somewhere warm, they learned. Their mother had been a mathematics teacher. She could bring any number under her hand and teach it to be kind. She had spoken to the boys as if they were both capable always. When Simon began to disappear in the way that some people do while still living in your rooms, she had held his chin once and said I am not your warden and he had very gently removed her hand from his face and hung it back by her side, like returning a towel to the rail.

From the speaker someone announced they would cut into the channel inside the hour. The ferry slowed as if deciding. In the gap between islands that opened they could see a string of yellowish lights that did not look like a town until you thought of it as a necklace. A light blinked on a pole to tell them to believe this was the intended way.

He went to the stern, to the place where the wake wrote over itself, and he watched it as if it were the answer to a thing. He had tried to write his brother an answer once. He had composed on paper a letter that said I am willing, and then had not mailed it because he was not willing to be the kind of person who wrote it. You want to be strong or kind. He had not been strong and he was not sure he had been kind.

He took the chain of the life ring in his hand and let it go. When he pressed his fingers to his nose they smelled of something the colour orange.

The dock at Greyhaven was a set of squares with tyres strung along them and the whole thing bounced slightly as if not convinced. Two people in yellow watched the boat with the attention of people whose job is to do a thing at exactly a time. He stood with three others at the ramp and lowered his eyes when the metal creaked because he had no wish to be the person who looked as if he’d never seen such a thing. They walked down as if they had remembered the ground in a life before. The night shore took them with gravel sound.

A man in a parka with a notebook and long fingers waited in the space between the ferry and the town and looked at him like he had a knowledge. Are you Mr. R—. He said yes. The man said constable by way of name and shook his hand with only the fingers. They did not say the other name. They walked. The town made itself besides them quietly. There were houses that had grown up around long fishermen’s knees. There was, above them somewhere, a cannery that did not light itself this time of year. They walked to the end of a long white building with a ramp and a second doorway and the smell of refrigeration, and the man with the notebook waved once to a different man who unhooked a chain.

You understand you don’t have to— the constable said, then let the sentence die, then picked it up again— We can ask someone else who knew him if that’s easier. If it is.

Noah said This is the only thing I can do for him. There was a small tremor in the center of his own chest, not his hand. He kept his hands very still. He put the folder back in the jacket even though he had thought he would carry it in.

The room where they lay them was white in a way that makes you think a tremendous talent has been used to make a colour pretend to be sanitary. There was the sound of a fan. There was the sound of his own lungs, working. A man in a paper coat took the corner of a sheet with the delicacy of laundrywomen and lifted it back. He grew still not because of the sight but because of the way the air above the body felt different from the rest of the room as if a heat had recently moved out and was maybe still at the far corner.

It is always like this, he would tell no one later. The instant inventory. He put his hand on the rail because his legs had done the thing legs do when they don’t plan it. He had never seen his brother with his eyes closed. Not like this. The forehead. The line of hair that never grew right at the temples. The shape of the lip that looked like a word. There is one grey hair where grey begins. There is the small weight of a drop of water at the edge of a nostril that might be his own imagination. No. The man lifted a little. The mouth opened and stayed a degree open. He felt either affection or terror.

There was a mark under the chin he did not know. There was a cut the length of a cataract on the shin. He moved his eyes with a discipline across the body and then back. The place where the bird had been inked. Had it been here. He could not see it. There was the pale of a shoulder and the shoulder blade like something that had been intended to be a wing. He moved up and he saw the small crescent scar above the eye. He swallowed and felt a hunger for something alive in the room. It is not food we want in those rooms. It is wind. He put his hand into his jacket and left it there, forgetting how that looks.

Could you lift the left— he made a small gesture toward the wrist and the man in paper lifted it with a care that borrowed dignity. He leaned closer. He could not tell. The room smelled like the inside of a shell.

The constable said We found him on rock below the lighthouse. He had no papers. His jacket caught in a snag. There was a man fishing who saw, but it was already— he stopped, because the sentence did not know where to end.

Noah said the name, not loud, the man’s name, his own brother’s name. He could not put his hands on the body because that would make another thing happen.

He said I don’t know. And then louder I don’t know. He reached to touch the hair and the paper-coated man stepped back a degree to make space for him to do it or not do it.

He had pictured this moment as something when certainty would help you like a lamp over a hand of cards. But it did not. He had a rash memory now of swimming at night in a quarry and his brother’s laugh going elsewhere. He had the memory of a day older than that when he had cut his knee and his brother had lifted him in a kind of hilarity toward home. I don’t know, he said again. I want to tell you yes. He did not know which part of that sentence was true.

The constable said We can wait. And they did. They stood in a room with no time in it. The fan made a noise like a word you forget as you say it.

He saw then the mark on the ankle, right side, where a beer bottle had opened him when he had been twelve, the long straight line his mother had closed with the only thread she had that day, the same colour as the bedroom curtains. He saw it. He had not looked there, had he. He had been a fool and had not looked exactly there. He took in a breath and held it and exhaled. It was the kind of breath you might take when you were trying to see further under water.

It could have been rust. It could have been any boy. He knew this as a fact and as a wish.

It’s him, he said, because a person must choose. He put his hand to his own mouth and spoke through it as if the words might be less damaging to the air if softened. After he said it he did not move.

The paper-coated man covered the boy with a sheet then and in the covering was the gentlest thing he had seen in a long time. He wanted to stand there and be asked questions and answer them all and then find new questions, just to keep someone in the room. He wanted to sit in a hallway and listen to telephones sound in someone else’s office and know that his day had a shape. But the constable put a hand on his shoulder the way one does not to comfort but to ask for a small fraction of your balance to be transferred to him, and then he lowered his hand, and then they walked back out into the air of the morning.

The morning was not scheduled yet. It had not begun the weather that would be in the paper. There were lights on at a window above a store. At the cannery a gull stood on a metal rail and moved its head side to side as if signing off on the scene. The constable spoke with the plainness people use when they are not hiding themselves anywhere inside a sentence.

We can contact— you don’t need to be at the— there are forms, but not now. He was a person in this town. Somebody saw him this week. We just don’t have full names. If you need someone to sit with you I can call— there’s a church— he was saying things to make the space between them less like a cliff face. Noah wanted either to be alone or to be in a crowd of thousands.

He said He had a friend who could teach a crow to trade. He had wanted, suddenly, to tell anybody a story about him that had nothing to do with this room. The constable nodded as if he had known that crow. He did not look at Noah. They walked by the bait shop where a man in a t-shirt even now even in this air was hosing blood off a table into a channel that led to the drain, and the steam from the warm water made a small cloud on the cement.

He sat on a bench with his hands between his knees and put his head down to keep it from rolling off his neck. He had thought there would be a moment when he could cry in a way that would remove something. There was not. He remembered a day when they had taken a rowboat out and a storm had come too early and he had bent and pulled and pulled like a dog and he had known at once when they were out of the storm, with no measure but the way muscle let go. He wanted to reach that place with his chest.

He walked to the edge of the water then. He stepped down a set of stairs green with slime. He put his fingers into the cold and held them there until they hurt their way into numb. He said the name again, in case the morning had missed it. He found he could not bring his mind to the flight back on the evening boat, to the domestic conversation about the seating in the lounge and a sandwich. He could only bring himself to two possibilities. One was to keep walking along the shore and follow the line of the strait until it gave him back the boy on a beach somewhere reading an old magazine he had found in a hut. The other was to walk back into the white building and lie down on the cold and put his palm under the sheet and wait until the room went away.

There was a woman with a plastic sack walking a dog, and the dog trotted and looked back at her to see if she was seeing the same world he was. The woman came close and said Are you lost. He said No. She said Well you look like you found something. Then she nodded toward the water. There are otters on the other side of the pier sometimes. Then she said a thing that made no sense. They hold hands when they sleep so they don’t float away from one another. She walked on and did not look back.

He went to a store and bought apples and a bottle of something that would not keep him from what had been decided. He walked to a small park with a bench in the sun where a man was mending a net by passing a shuttle through a hole and pulling, passing, pulling. He sat near him and listened to the sound of it and did not speak. He thinked, he thought, if a man he did not know spoke first, he would cry. The man did not. Noah closed his eyes and slept in pieces and dreamed of touching his brother’s shoulder blade and feeling it twitch and come alive like a bird that had been only waiting. A gull pecked at the paper bag at his feet and he startled as if shot at, and the net mender laughed once without cruelty.

At eleven he went back to the office with the glass window and the woman who had a pen in her hair and he signed the form. He signed it with his name, and then he wrote the other name where it asked for it, and the shape of his brother’s name in his own hand broke something he had been holding numbly together. Each letter stepped down the field like a man. He looked up and the woman was looking at him in the way people do when they are good.

He walked from room to room of the town with the slow pace of someone trying to tire himself out without sleeping. He wandered into a hardware and put his fingers into a bin of bolts and felt their small coldness. He walked through a lane where the wind made a loose whistle through a length of pipe against a wall. He walked on to the end of a road that became a track that became a thing animals had chosen. He looked back and the ferry, the next one, was coming. It was like all the others, and it was not.

He went into the café with the door that bell rang to tell the cups and spoons another person with heat in him had arrived. He sat at the counter and a girl with a birthmark on her temple poured him coffee that tasted like it wanted to be something else. He looked at the phone beside the register and thought of calling no one. He watched himself not doing it. He drank. He thought of what he could bring back— a photograph someone had taken by accident that had his brother in it, an idea for a song about this town. He touched the rim of the cup with his tongue and tasted the hairline chip there, a roughness, old.

The ferry sighed at the dock. He could not get on it with the person he had been at midnight. People were coming in off boats with boxes of greens. A boy on a bike rode through the square and popped his front wheel up and carried himself on the back in a kind of joy. The boy lowered the wheel and looked in at Noah with interest or disregard.

He stood up too fast and the café turned once for him. He put money down that did not fit the price and they let him. He walked to the ferry and stood in line with other foot-passengers. There was a woman with a long braid down her back and a man carrying a guitar in a case that might or might not have had a guitar in it. He held the railing of the ramp with his right hand and let his left hand keep the beat of a song that had been on in the café. He could smell the water now like bread when you tear it.

On board the deck was the same deck but in daylight and it was less forgiving. What the night will let you believe about yourself does not carry into noon. He leaned over where he had leaned at one, and now the water was the colour of a name he could not source. Off to the side hunting birds went into the surface like needles and came out like knives. He sat on a bench and someone’s dog put its nose in his pocket and he let it and the dog went away satisfied and unafraid.

He took the folder out and opened it and looked at the boys and reached to put it back and could not, thought of opening and closing this until something fixed. A child's palm prints on a cold window flattening themselves then lifting. He placed the letter on his knee, the camp letter with the river, and he let himself read it finally, end to end.

Brother, it began, as if that were a kind of anchor. Then it was the river and it was the light and it was the list of small meals he had eaten there out of pity and it was you would love this, no you would hate this because of the mosquitoes, and it was send me a book I can throw away when I am done, and it was take care of Ma’s watch, you know which one, and it was I saw a woman last night combing her hair in the dark by touch and I thought of Ma and then it did a thing letters do sometimes and it ended with a blank space where a sentence had been started and the pen had been put down and the person had walked out, was on the river, would come back damp and entire or would not come back.

He put his thumb on the blank place and felt nothing there and stopped expecting it. He put the paper back and folded the folder until it was thin, and then he flattened it, as if to make a different use of it, as if it were a small mat he could sit on in the grass and make a picnic of the morning.

When the ferry pushed off to cut back across he felt a small lifting in his chest that was close to the pull of the engine. The town thinned. The strait opened in front of them like a cloth someone had shaken twice. A woman beside him took a photograph of the wake and made no sound.

Someone— not a crew member, a man in an ordinary coat— came to the rail near him and leaned over and said in a voice that might have been to himself, Everyone leaves twice. He looked at Noah without looking and did not speak again, and later when Noah went to buy a sandwich he saw that man again at a window looking at his own face with slightly different puzzlement.

The day carried them in the middle of it. The sun made glass on part of the water and rain on the other part and no one could say which they were going under. He thought of himself from above and wanted to reach down and place one finger on the crown of his head and hold him there the way a mother assures a child that he exists in a particular place. He thought of his brother at sixteen and at thirty and at twenty-four, and he thought of him not at all, the way sometimes grief spares you. He thought if he ever slept near a river again he would keep his hand out of the covers and softly touch the air all night.

He walked the deck twice around and counted the life rings for a reason he could not tell anyone. At the stern he held the chain again and thought of orange not as a colour but as a flavour of safety. At the bow he looked at sea that continued and he was at least part of something that did not need him for itself.

When he went inside and down the corridor to his cabin, the sticker on the ceiling with the inspection date caught his eye again but now it said nothing. He lay down on the lower bunk and closed his eyes for the length of a song he did not know, and in that small dark he saw his brother standing barefoot in the doorway to the kitchen with a jar of pickles in his hand, and he saw his mother’s wrist in motion with a pen and he saw a crow steal a button, and he saw the white building with its fan and the sheet rise a little as if a breath had happened and he knew that was not real and he let it be not real and then he slept.

Before the ferry reached the other side he went up to the deck again. The wind had moved and was now their friend. He stood at the place where the paint scrapes off the rail first and he rubbed it with his thumb as if to help it along. He did not say anything out loud though it felt like a moment for saying. He did not promise anything to the air. He did not call what just happened fate or mistake. He did not decide a new life. He put his hand flat on the metal and pushed down and felt it push back and he closed his eyes not because of the light, but because sometimes the best way to prove a thing exists is to remove it for a few seconds, wait, and find it still there when you return.
