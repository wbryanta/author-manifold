By the time I turn off the highway and follow the slow curl of the frontage road, the sea is on my right, less a body than a suggestion of light stretched flat, and the hotel—a shape I’d held inside my cheek all week like a worry bead—rises out of the low thicket of ornamental grasses and new plantings. The first thing that shocks me is its shininess. The old Larkspur had always worn a film of its labor: a patina of salt and fine dust, the film of breath and skin. Even the brass we polished until our fingers ached still seemed tired by evening. This one, the Lark, gleams. It has more glass than I remember, a modish bank of windows running along the lobby like an aquarium. The signage is a laugh: a serifed L cut from mirrored steel, a stylized flower whose petal points are flame-shaped if you are of a mind to see that. A tiny fountain murmurs where once there had been a circle of pebble-studded asphalt where the vans idled.

I pull into a parking spot farther than necessary, use the extra steps to decide if I am going to touch the building when I reach it. I try out different versions of my face reflected in the cars I pass. There is a dress folded like a paper thought on the backseat, a pair of rose-colored shoes I will probably regret having to wear after an hour. My sister’s text from this morning blinks on my phone—Mara, are you almost here? please answer please—and I let the screen go black instead of answering. I have learned that small withholdings are the only versions of control I can stomach.

The air near the hotel has the smell I remember but wrong. The sea’s iron and algae tang, the sweet chemical of pools, the diesel-steamed undernote of kitchen deliveries—it’s all here. But overlaying it is new cedar, expensive mulch, some resinous lemon cleaning product atomized into the lobby through a duct. When I step out from under the portico—no doorman, just sliding glass and the hush of good dollars—the conditioned air hits my arms, and my skin does that late-August raising of faint bumps that used to tell me it was time to go scrub the ice machines, time to empty the slush of cigarette ends and wayward shrimp tails from the ballroom ashtrays back when people were still allowed to make slow fires at their tables.

I never thought I would walk into this building as a guest. I imagined, when I imagined it at all, that I would be called for a deposition, or that I would turn up in a rainstorm with my shoes wet through to see if I had really made it all up. But the thing is, you can be pulled back into places by other people’s happiness just as easily as by your own nightmares. My sister is five years younger and has always been braver in certain ways. She chose this hotel for the romance of it: the old tales, the promise that this one would never again be undone by smoke. She is marrying a man who says he wants to put roots down, which is a funny thing to say to someone like me who has never felt anything other than movable.

The front desk is an elegant slab of pale stone, thin as a promise. The woman behind it wears a navy suit the precise shade of the old pool tiles. I used to dream that color. She smiles at me with the fixed generosity of someone whose job it is to be seen being generous.

“Welcome to the Lark,” she says, singing the r a little as though it’s a ribbon she can pull to unwrap the place. “May I have your last name?”

“Marin,” I tell her. “Mara Marin. I’m with the Sullivan-Huang wedding.”

“We’re so excited to have you,” she says, moving her fingers over the keys not like a typewriter but like a piano. Her name tag says DANI and beneath it, in smaller font, Guest Experience. “We have you for two nights in a Coastal King. Ocean view.” As if the ocean could be domesticated into a view, as if it lived its whole life peering into panes.

“Yes,” I say, although I booked the cheapest block rate Nate’s email linked me to, and this was what they handed out. “Second floor if you have it.”

She lifts her eyebrows in that professional way, not too surprised, not prying, and makes a notation. “Second floor we can do. Would you prefer elevator at this end or near the back stair?”

“Back stair,” I say, and she looks up properly now, and there it is: the flash of the human, the curiosity that lifts a face out of its script. I could tell her I prefer stairs because I like to move, because I get dizzy in boxes, because the elevator in the old hotel had once stuttered and then died between floors with a bus of bridesmaids inside it, their clutches like birds they strangled while they waited. But I don’t say anything. She slides two keys across to me, slick rectangles with the flower-flame logo.

“For the welcome reception,” she says, “there’s lemonade and infused water in the atrium, and the wedding party will be gathering at four in the Juniper Bar. The ceremony tomorrow is on the bluff.” She is doing exactly what I used to be told to do: lift, place, gesture, don’t give more information than requested, don’t stick to emptiness, always add a name to a direction so the guest feels tended.

I carry my bags like someone who has been taught how to carry the right thing in each hand. Near the elevators, a lit glass box contains a curated arrangement of the hotel’s old artifacts: a char-streaked brass room number plate, a key with a melted head, three spoon handles that have been blackened and curled back onto themselves like the skeleton feet of small birds. A little card explains, in elegant serifed type, that in 2009 a fire damaged the property and the Lark was rebuilt with state-of-the-art safety systems and a renewed commitment to community. No one calls it what it was: a night that wouldn’t stop.

The corridor to the back stair has carpeting with a pattern that looks like a decision: leaves oriented east-west, arrows pointing you one way or the other. I open the door to the stairwell, and obedient, the hotel gives back to me exactly what I expect: the cool block walls painted a color that is institutional but with taste, the rubber tread on the steps a gray pocked skin that will wrinkle and peel in a decade. The smell of it, the stair smell, is a truth. For all the newness you can throw at a building, service spaces have their own ancient collusions. This stair smells like the old stair: dust that’s been stirred, the faint lingering of bleach, the echo of a thousand feet.

On the second floor landing, there is a door marked STAFF ONLY, but it has no lock. I put my palm against it and stand there for a second, feeling the low vibration of unseen machinery. When I let myself in—just far enough—I can see the edge of the maids’ locker room. They don’t call us maids anymore. They call us housekeepers and attendants and engineers. The vending machine in the corner is the kind that takes bills and coins and has live little prices that adjust. A cart, two towels folded in their triple-sheath style at the top like hats. A set of soaps in their paper wraps, the way we always stacked them before we learned they would seep their scent up into the pillowcases and make guests cough.

I take a breath so deep I feel it in the base of my spine and exit before anyone sees me. I am not going to do the thing this weekend of turning into a ghost who clings to the arm of the living. I am here because I promised my sister I would be here. I am here because I said I could sleep in a bed in a room and deal with there being a door between me and the hallway. I pull the door to my room shut behind me and let the hush slide over me like good water.

Everything is crisp and considered: a white bed like a thought with no qualification, a low chair in slate-colored velvet, a rug in a pattern that repeats too perfectly to be real. The little slip of paper with my name printed in a font that aspires to personal but is resolutely not. I lift the corner of the bedspread, because I can’t not, the way a mechanic can’t not listen to a strange engine. The corners have the same old hospital tuck. The sheets smell less of bleach than of whatever the scent supplier is hawking this month to everyone with a procurement account: cucumber, linen, a molecule invented to suggest safety. I lie down on my back and spread my arms out so my hands don’t touch anything. The ceiling has a shadow line running around its perimeter where the dropped inset meets the architectural gesture. The fire sprinkler is a small metal flower. I look at it long enough that my jaw loosens and I feel the old flavor pool at the back of my tongue: copper, charred sugar, something animal.

The first year after, whenever someone struck a match, my fingers clenched around whatever I was holding—a spoon, a glass, once my nephew’s small ankle—and I had to put the thing down and leave the room and come back when I knew my face had smoothed. The news printed my name wrong so maybe I am not the person that happened to. I have written out the story as truthfully as I can on the few occasions someone asked for it—including a man with a tape recorder and a stipend and an earnest tilt to his head, including my mother when she still had enough clarity to grip the line of me—and in every telling there is a place where the floor buckles and I slide sideways and I am saying the words but the taste in my mouth isn’t my taste.

At four, having washed my face and surrendered to the dress, I walk to the bar where the non-bridal parties are gathering. If you duck your head under the sweep of the atrium and turn right, you can cut the corner by the photo of the original Larkspur’s opening, all the men’s hats pointed at their chins. An invisible pull draws me to that end of the cut, to stare at the women from 1951, their lips in stiff smiles, their eyes skeptical. I wonder if the attendants had been hired then or if the owner’s wife had flitted through each room smoothing beds with her fingernails until she felt she had touched enough lives to count as a good deed.

The Juniper Bar has wood slats on the ceiling that make you think of hulls and ribs and something that could carry you if it had to. The bartender at the far end polishes glasses like one of those soothing loops you can watch on a screen when you can’t sleep. There is a table with tilted platters of little foods whose taste is mostly textural: salted asparagus tips rolled in prosciutto, tiny biscuits, shrimp you can fit whole into your mouth if your mouth is practiced at that kind of excess. A group of people I recognize only from dress fittings and social media shrieks at my arrival, and my sister turns and her face opens and she is twenty-nine and wearing a white dress that is not her wedding dress but which will serve as an overture to it. She is lit from within in a way I have never been.

“You came,” she says into my ear as she squeezes me, as if she doubted that the texts I didn’t answer meant anything other than I had fled. “I was going to come and get you if you didn’t.”

“You are not allowed to come and get me,” I say. “You have to be received.”

“It’s all pretend anyway,” she whispers, and that’s one of the reasons I like her. She can hold the dream and its falseness at once and not find the balance nauseating. “Nate’s parents are already here. He will be late because he went to get the thing from the thing—”

“Say no more,” I say, and she laughs because she knows the way her words can tangle when her brain is three steps ahead of her mouth.

I let myself be handed a glass. I stand in one of those small conversational circles that form and unform as though commanded by body math, and I am so carefully quiet that no one asks me a thing. It is a magic trick learned from years in corridors: if you tilt your attention outward and make it a mirror for other people’s faces, they see themselves, and that is enough. There are relatives here I have not seen in five years. There is a girl grown into a woman who used to sit in my kitchen and draw horses with her tongue sticking out of the corner of her mouth. There is a man who has married a second time and brought with him three new children who behave like emissaries from a friendly nation. The bartender sets down a dish of candied something near my elbow, and for a moment the sugared perfume is so exactly like the smell of burning mints that my mouth waters with a sickness that is not about candy. The woman next to me—Nate’s aunt, I think, in a dress the red of a ripe beet—has been telling a story about a flight delay in which the pilot came out and explained, in a lovely Irish accent, that birds had plugged the engine.

“It’s always birds,” she says, and she laughs and touches my wrist. “You’re Mara, right? The sister who—who came up from the city? I’ve heard so much. You two look so different.”

“I used to think I was the rough draft,” I say, and she laughs again, this time startled, and I watch the calculation cross her face: am I allowed to laugh at this? There are rules at weddings that supersede other rules. One of them is that if the woman in question laughs, you can, too.

Later—how much later I cannot tell you because my head has a trick it plays at gatherings of giving every minute equal weight—I find myself outside on the low terrace beyond the bar with Nate, who has not, as it turns out, managed to retrieve the thing from the thing. He is sweet, and I say that in the old way meant to be a compliment. He looks at you when you speak, which is rarer than it should be. He’s wearing a jacket that is one shade too warm for the day, and his hair does the thing it does in humidity that makes him look like a friendly poplar tree.

“I’ve never been in a hotel this nice,” he confesses to me, not in a way that asks me to say I haven’t either, because he is smart enough to know better than to solicit that kind of lie.

“I have,” I say. “It smelled worse.”

He laughs. He knows a little. I have told them just enough. I have never gotten the hang of knowing how much to hand over to people who have not yet dropped something on the floor and broken it forever.

“Your sister says you won’t ride elevators,” he says, immediately contrite, as if he is telling me a secret I don’t already keep.

“It makes me feel like I’m being delivered to someone else’s idea of a room,” I say, then add, because it is my job this evening to be human-shaped, “You two picked well. This place holds a view in its hands like it is trying not to spill it.”

He leans on the railing and breathes in and out as though practicing. “I keep thinking of all the people who have gotten married in this building. Even with what—shit—even with what happened, it’s like the walls remind you it’s possible to say yes and then keep saying it. Does that make sense?”

He is the kind of person who apologizes to the air for the limits of what he can make happen with language. I say, “It does,” and in truth it does, the way your body’s decision to get up again makes sense even when it wanted to lie down forever.

When I return to my room, I find I have been marked by the day in small ways: a smear of balsamic near my thumb, a scent of someone else’s perfume caught in the fold of my hair, a corner of napkin in my clutch with a phone number written on it by a cousin who wants me to adopt a plant that has outgrown her light. I sit on the floor and pull off the rose-colored shoes and massage the dents they carved into my skin. I like the honest ugliness of your feet at the end of a day. They do not lie about what they have carried.

The night is a long gentle barge that takes me past the different versions of sleep. At two, I wake and lie there in the stillness and can name all the noises: the deep hum of the rooftop units, the small click of the mini-fridge making decisions, the faint hiss of the highway behind the building where it cuts through the last marsh the developers haven’t yet eaten. I get up and go to the window and put my hand against the glass. It is cool. There are few lights on in the building opposite and one of them is the unkind, unflattering rectangle of an office, where a chair has been turned deliberately askew for the next day’s body. I think of all the rooms, all the slow mechanical beats of them, all the invisible kinds of safety that woke after we slept. Do sprinkles dream? Does steel remember? It is a comfort when my mind turns to those jokes of personification I used to hoard like pennies, the way you give a machine a feeling so you can have someone to talk to.

In the morning, the sky is a blue so complete it feels like an afterlife. The bridal suite is somewhere upstairs where the windows are wider. I drink the coffee I make with the device on the credenza. It tastes like distant rain. The breakfast room is full of small jubilations: home fries that are better than they ought to be, toddlers gripping bagels as big as their combined fists, an old man in a hat reading the ceiling as though it contains instructions from a youth he is able to return to for only two minutes an hour. I carry a plate to a table by the wall. When the servers—young, alert, faces already trained in the art of smoothing—approach, I can see the flicker of their eyes as they take in the evidence I leave for them to see: a tidiness where others have scattered, a fork returned to its napkin the way you’d lay down a tool you respect, a bill tucked under a saucer with a folded dollar on top. People will tell you this kind of notice is condescension but I do not believe them. A person who sees labor is a person with whom you can have a full conversation.

And what I do, after breakfast, without planning to and so perhaps it was always the plan, is go find the laundry.

You will not see the laundry unless you know where to look. Hotels put it on maps only when they have to. It is the repository of the hotel’s private fluids. In the old Larkspur, you could find it by following the hum that increased in pitch as you entered its sphere, the whine of belts and the roar of dryers that you felt in your teeth. In the Lark, everything is hushed and calmed and hidden. But all buildings reveal themselves if you go to where the rugs don’t run. I trace the regular patterns of backs of houses: service doors, exit signs green as tropical leaves, a scuff on a baseboard at the height of a cart’s second shelf.

There is a woman at the desk in the back hall whose nameplate—silver, scratched—has been flipped over so the blank side faces me. She is eating yogurt from the container with a spoon she has taken from a drawer. Her hair is braided against her head in a pattern I would like to put my fingers on just to feel what it is to hold such neatness. Her eyes move over my body, down to my shoes, to the lanyard that still swings slightly from my neck with the keycard in it, and then back to my face. She could call the front desk and have me escorted into the brightness by a security man whose pants never sit right because no one makes uniforms for the particular width of him. Instead she waits for me to speak.

“I used to work here,” I say, then correct myself. “Not here. In the Larkspur. Room attendant. Nights. Years ago. I wondered if you’d show me the—if I could—if that would be okay—” I have not stuttered like this for years. I hardly recognize the sound of myself.

“Why?” she asks, not unkindly. Curiosity saved more rooms than vengeance.

“Because I ran toward the old dryers once,” I say before I can stop. “And I need to see that there is a world in which I could walk this hallway and not break.”

She pauses a second that is not a second but a decision. “You a guest now?”

“Yes.”

“You with the wedding?”

“I’m the sister who used to smell like bleach.”

That is too much and she laughs, and I realize with relief that I have correctly guessed her appetite for the embarrassing and nervy. She gets up, tucks the yogurt lid under the container like a neat soul with its self, and moves toward the door with the push bar. “Ten minutes,” she says. “I got a delivery coming.”

We step into the realm of heat and industry, and it is instantly another country. The washers are enormous chrome mouths, their glass doors like portholes, and as we pass one, a sheet slaps wetly and gives me back a sound I must have been listening for in the shift between breaths. The machines are not what they were. They are better. We build smarter. There are signs laminated in bright colors that instruct you in five languages about what to do if this, what to do if that, and below the machines mounted metal boxes with pull-down handles like the old sprinklers used to have, that would drown the air with wet when triggered. The woman—her name, it turns out, is Chantal; she tells me without telling—says, “Back then? We had a dryer that had been making a noise for a week.”

“Two,” I correct before I can stop myself. “We had two making the old-bones noise. The left one and the second from the end.”

She nods and I am embarrassed by how pleased I am to be right about machines that nearly killed me. She says, “Owner came by yesterday with a cousin from Pittsburgh. Wanted to see if we could stage a towel-fold video for her feed. I told her she could have my resignation letter pre-folded into a swan.”

She pushes open another door and we are in the zone where the clean things go before they are redistributed to the world: carts like small steel cities; stacks of pillowcases in bricks; the smell that is everything at once: steam, lint, soap, woman, ambition. In the corner by the industrial ironing roller—called, I learned then, a mangle; you lift the sheets and feed them in and say a prayer to gods you do not believe in that you will not feed your hand by mistake—there hangs a photograph, small, taped, of five women in aprons, their faces unserious. A palm tree is painted on the cinderblock wall behind them. I cannot stop myself.

“Do you know a woman named Lila Hernandez?” I say. I hear my voice bend in the middle.

Chantal turns to follow my gaze. “From before my time,” she says. “There’s a placard outside the staff lounge. Names of the people the old place lost. Lila is on it. Lupe reads her name out loud on Fridays.”

“Thank you,” I say. In my mind, Lila has not held still for twelve years. She looks exactly like she did the day I met her. She wears her hair in a messy knot that she used the handle of a spoon to twist because she never had a hair tie when she needed one. She is telling me how her little boy eats all the mango only when it is in other people’s houses. I did not get her out. I got out with the eloquence of a person who has resolved to memorize everything that will punish her later. Every room I ran to and then away from is a hallway I still walk three times a week in the two minutes after the tea kettle clicks off.

It was in October. The air outside was the kind that seems to have been ironed flat by the sky. We were short because we were always short; the hotel was having a conference of people whose name tags said things like VP of Learning. On the ninth floor, the trash in a room smelled like bourbon, and the man in it was sleeping the hard sleep of the gentle murderer. My shift had a float to it that day, an almost-laziness born of all the ways I counted my steps: ten to room 917, twelve to the ice machine, eight to the linen closet where I should have gone to fetch the pillow protectors I forgot, except I bent to tie my shoe and ran my thumbnail under the rubber edge of it the way I did when I was thinking and when I stood up the smoke alarm started its cricket chirp of prelude. Not the shriek. The polite trill. The way the heater knocks in an old house to announce its watery intentions.

I went to the closet and pulled out the cart already packed with bleach. I told the lifts to go where good lifts go, and they did. On the fourth floor, Chantal’s predecessors were reminding a man in a suit that he could not smoke at the sink and he was saying, “Who am I killing?” and in that space a gust of smoke came down the hall like a promise kept. The alarms found their voices. The doors slammed—some do, on magnet release—and the light changed in the way it does when a room loses a difference and becomes a thing with the hall. It is a sound map as much as it is a scent. Heat speaks. Air moves. I remember the shape of the air on my skin—the way it leaned—more than I remember faces, which I redecorated later with what I thought they ought to have been. Lila had gone down to put a finish on the last load because if she did not, someone would have scolded her. We run even into fires with the obedience the order knows we’ve been trained to have. I thudded my palm three, four, five times against the door of a couple whose arguing had become such a worn groove they could not hear. I moved a woman who had determined to pack the fish she had bought at the market into her roll-aboard toward the stair with a force I would have never let myself use on any other day; she swore at me in the soft curse words that are meant to bruise without breaking. I went past the window that looks down into the atrium and saw, through smoke that was starting to fit itself into the spaces meant for air, that the bar’s bottles were lit like lures under an aquarium sun, and I envied the people I imagined drinking there, who would be told later that they had almost been a part of a thing and would carry it forward like a medal no one else could see.

Somewhere between the third and the second floor the tempers of the smoke and the men with clipboards and the insane bravery of the dishwasher with the burn on his forearm and the sound of my own breath fell into the same rhythm. I was carrying the thin weight of a boy who had finally, finally gone limp in my arms after struggling against me as if he had a pact with the carpet, and I was thinking—not words, but something you can translate later into words—that if I put one foot in front of the other nine more times, there would be a corner, and that corners meant different air. That there are always corners.

After, I coughed till my ribs bruised, and I was made to sit on a curb and drink a tasteful bottle of water that someone delivered to me with a label practiced to look like help. A nurse touched my wrist with two fingers and said, “Your pulse is like a songbird’s,” and I said, “Well then,” and she laughed as if we knew together what that meant. There are things it is impossible to say without sounding like you are borrowing someone else’s life, so I rarely say them. I do not tell people about the night terrors that had terrors, or the way the smell of ironing water turns my knees to salt. I do not tell them that I do not forgive Lila for going down to see to the laundry, as though layer by layer, the hotel could be tamed if we hugged it close enough to bleach it. I do not tell them that I misplaced a man in a bathrobe in that smoke so thick my hands could not find my face. I tell them the boy I carried was fine, because that is a story someone else can walk away with and put on a shelf.

In the corner by the mangle, there is a small dent in the smooth floor where, someone tells me later, an old standpipe used to go down. They cut everything to the bone and started again. But the ghosts keep their feet anyway.

When I come back out into the service corridor, I feel like I have sunk a foot inside my body and can stand there without wobbling. Chantal says, “You want to see the staff lounge?” and I say yes, and we turn into a room painted with a mural of lilies so insistently cheerful you want to hush them. On the wall is the placard she mentioned: a list of names in block letters, laminated, a border of flowers and a tiny crossed broom and faucet cartoon in the corner. There is a weekly sign-in sheet below it for people who want to take overtime. There is a sign that says, Remember: Nobody knows the work you do as well as you do. It is meant as encouragement but reads as a sentence.

I touch my finger to the laminated sheet where Lila’s name is. It glides easily. My hand is not worthy to stick to her. I press until a bubble of air and something like laughter moves under my nail. Then I feel ashamed and remove my finger and put it in my pocket as if I have taken something and can carry it away.

The ceremony is on the bluff behind the hotel, where the grass is clipped and the ocean is blue as a storybook and the party has consented, en masse, to be charmed. There are chairs draped in white as if they are people in a different heat. There is a fisher’s breezy flag marking some stake in the world I can’t see. The arch is wrapped in flowers so wide they look like they would bruise if you put your face in them long enough. Someone thought about the color of the ribbon on the programs longer than they thought about the concept of mortality, and that’s as it should be. War, plague, fire—we step out of them and put on our soft shoes and we say yes to each other because we have to. We do it in hotels because hotels are places made to seem like whoever walks into them is the first person the space imagined when it was built.

There are candles. My sister always wanted candles. The kind in hurricanes because we learned when we were girls not to leave live things alone with the wind. The officiant—a woman with a voice so easy it slides into you like a boat made of felt—says we are gathered, and I almost laugh because that is the phrase all my grief groups use, as if we need to be convinced we are collected. Nate looks like he is about to make a series of promises he can only barely comprehend and that is exactly what marriage is: promise beyond comprehension. My sister stumbles over him being her home, and someone coughs deliberately, and she says home with deliberation. The ocean makes a sound like an audience. A gull cackles. I watch a little girl in the second row set her program down on the ground, and the small breeze tug it forward like a shy animal.

When the unity candle is lit—my aunt’s hands, then mine, then my sister’s—it is true that my stomach drops. But I have learned to ride my stomach back up. My hand shakes when I hold the taper out, and my sister laughs nervously, and the flame mouths and pulls away and then finds itself as a new fire, and no one thinks of the kind of fire that eats buildings. They think of hearths. They think of bread. They think of the ancient stories we have always told ourselves about the people who carry embers from one dwelling to another in the hems of their skirts.

Someone else cries; I don’t. The cameras click, and if you didn’t know better you’d think it was rain on the roof. Just before the kiss, a child shouts, “Now!” and the room of the lawn laughs and the someone who seemed to be holding their breath releases it. I give my lungful back to the sky and it doesn’t need it.

The heat tilts in the late afternoon and the party tips into its next dance like a lady sliding through two doors at once. The ballroom—called, now, not a ballroom but an Event Space—is a museum of the building’s newness: recessed lighting made to seem like it comes from nowhere, a floor that may give you splinters only in your dreams, candles in glass that make no hazard, a wall of windows that turn everything outside into a person’s reflection if you lean too close. At some point between the salmon and the cake, my sister looks to me and lifts her chin: now. I know what it means, because she called me on Tuesday and said, “Will you do it? Will you stand up and say something? I don’t want it to be all about parenthood and money and how babies will look when they’re mixed like batter.” I said yes without thinking because that is how I sometimes end up doing brave things.

I do not drink the extra drink before I go stand behind the pair of microphones stuck into their foam cubes like strange flowers. The sitting people do that turn a room can do, each body a petal rotating toward the center of the bloom. You know that feeling of being seen by three hundred eyes but not actually perceived? For a second, that old fear, the out-of-box elevator, the stuckness. Then I think of the woman who made the beds this morning and tucked the corners so with care that I wanted to ask for her hands. I think of the sheets hot in my grip as I fed them into the mouth of the machine. I think of the breath that is breath and the breath that is sound and how both saved me. And I say what I have been working on in the car on the way down, in my mind when I pretended to nap. It is not a poem and it is not a confession. I talk about making beds for strangers and about how the thing you learn is not just that people are messy, or that you can tell who they are by their socks, or that grief travels with us in the way we leave the cups we drink from. It is that someone else will come behind you and make the same bed, and someone else will lie down in it, and the person who does the making never gets in it but will tell you anyway, if you ask, that it is soft.

“I wish for you what we all want,” I say, “a soft bed and a door you can shut and a key that opens it from both sides. I wish that when you argue, you leave the trash by the door so you remember to take it out in the morning, because those little acts of removal are as real as the vows. I wish that your home has a good stair with a landing big enough to sit on when you need to sit on stairs and not be all the way up or all the way down.”

When I say landing, something moves through the room that feels so like laughter and like relief that my eyes blur. I do not say the name of the building. I do not say my life burned and then it didn’t. I do not say I forgave the part of myself that wanted to go back up. I say, “May your fires be in fireplaces,” and everybody laughs, and there it is: the way we tell the truth by making it safe and the way sometimes that is the bravest thing we can offer. My sister squeezes Nate’s hand so hard his knuckles go shiny. When I sit down, the beet-colored aunt squeezes my shoulder as if I have returned a portion of the check paid to the universe.

Later, after the dancing begins and I have allowed a small boy to drive his Matchbox car over my foot ten times in exchange for not crashing it into the cake, after I have hugged an uncle who is drinking ginger ale like it is whiskey, after I have stood next to the open bar and watched the woman pouring count to three in her head for every glass so that no one can accuse her of favoritism—after all that, I slip away.

It is easier at night to find the places that are not for you. Glass reflects more honestly when there are fewer watching it. The service corridors are emptied of purposeful hands. The track lights flicker in a polite way when I cross them, the motion sensor waking as though embarrassed. I walk to where the old building would have had its heart. I stand in the square of space that is the new building’s answer to the old building’s flaw. If you wait and listen long enough, all buildings speak. You may think I am being fanciful, but it is true. The air moves because it knows where it is being asked to go. There are rules of flow. There are corners.

At the end of the hall, where there is a door that opens now onto a loading dock that was once a small ill-kept courtyard with cans and cigarette ends and the kind of lonely trash that finds its own corner to hide in, a woman stands smoking. The nosmoking signs share space with the todo list. The woman smokes anyway. Her hand is cupping the coal because the wind from the sea is, as ever, a creature that wants to take what’s lit and make it somebody else’s. She looks at me and I lift my hand and I don’t say anything about her breaking a rule. She doesn’t hide the cigarette like you do if you fear reprisal. She exhales into the night as if it has asked her for this.

“You work here?” she asks.

“I worked here,” I say, and realize my verb has decided.

“You one of the ones from before.”

“Yes,” I say.

She nods and flicks ash into the dark where it leaves a trail like a thought. “My cousin was a dishwasher here back then,” she says. “Burn on his arm never lost its shine. He decided it was lucky. Changed the way he told time. He’d be like, it was before my arm or after.” She laughs. “We all tell time in our own ways.”

“Do you like it?” I ask, because this is a forward question between workers and because she has the air of someone who will accept the forward.

“It’s a job,” she says. “It’s a good one. It gets in your hands. I like the laundry better than the rooms. The rooms make me feel like I’m in people’s lives and I don’t want to be. Laundry is one big true thing.”

“I liked the rooms,” I say. “They made me feel like I could take care of people without them having to know what I was taking from them. The stains. The way you go to sleep in a mess and wake in a square.”

She laughs again, and I feel the odd lift that comes when you say a thing like a spell and the person listening nods. “Look,” she says suddenly, and we do: out at the dark. The ocean is a crease of sound tonight. A late delivery truck pulls up and idles. The driver hops down and pulls his shirt down over his stomach without shame. The building behind us breathes its deep measured breath. Between its waves of air, I hear faint music from the ballroom. A song my sister and I danced to when we were ten in a living room with a stain on the rug that never quite confessed to what had made it.

On my way back in, just under the lip of the fire door, I kneel briefly and slide something small into the space between the wall and the floor. It is a key. Old, bent, slick with the handles of people who have carried it in their pockets for a decade. The card in the lobby claimed a melted key had been recovered from the old site the week after the fire, an artifact for curated grief. This is not that key. Mine is ordinary. I kept it because when I went home the morning after, the stars were still up, and I reached into my pocket for my last tissue and found it. I have moved it from drawer to drawer as if it were alive. The corners of its plastic head are smoothed with all that moving. I have not been able to throw it away because I do not throw away the thing that opens the door to the room where you became the person you became. But now, in this place, I think it should rest. It is not the old building’s bone; it is mine.

When I return to the party, my sister is barefoot, her hair escaping in tendrils I remember from when she was five and imploring me to braid it tighter. Nate has his tie slung over his shoulder like a sailor. The two of them are in a spinning clump of bodies, their skin shining. I press my hand to my own sternum as if there were any chance the music could be coaxed to play inside me instead of out of me. There is a moment in the center of dancing when everyone’s arms go up at once in a motion no one taught us, as if to show the ceiling we are here. I watch that happen and do not take notes; I am in the taking.

At the end of the night—if there is such a thing as an end—I carry a bag of gifts up to my room and take my shoes off and sit on the carpet and eat a second slice of cake with my fingers while my knees rise and fall as if they were waves. I wash my hands and let the water run over the red marks the straps have made. I climb into bed, and this time I do not lie flat like a body in training for stone. I curl on my side and put my face into the pillow and breathe through the scent that is not entirely fake. I sleep and do not dream of smoke. In the morning, I wake before the light fully agrees to be day, and I lie there and listen to the room. The fridge clicks. A soft thud in the hallway is a paper slid under my door. I get up and retrieve it and unfold the world’s latest news the way you would open a letter from an old friend whose life is always too much to fit on pages.

Before I leave, I go downstairs and stand in the lobby where the light is new and forgiving. Dani is there, with a thermos that has been instructed to keep coffee very well. She smiles but not in the performance way, in the way you do when you see someone who has passed through a small trial you do not need to name. There is a toddler near the elevator pressing the button, then the other button, then both, and his mother laughs and says, “That’s power, sweetie,” and I want to tell her that here power looks like a button and sounds like a satisfying chime and smells like whatever scent we buy to suggest clean. I do not say it. I stand in front of the glass case containing the objects the hotel has decided are its history. The melted key, the curled spoons, a placard that says only: We Remember. It is all a version of sincerity you would mock if you had not once sat on a curb with your chest bit open like a bag and wept into a bland towel you were meant to fold later.

Outside, the fountain is catching the new light and throwing it up as if it is tired of holding it. The landscapers move along the strip with a speed that says they have a schedule that will always own them. The ocean has become, with the angled sun, a sequined field. I could stay another night. I could leave this minute and not think of the building again. I could write a review that mentions the softness of the towels and the attentiveness of the staff and not include the part where the walls remembered me, and I remembered them, and we both decided we could keep the peace.

I get in my car. The air is already warmer than it should be. The radio gives me a song I used to put on when I cleaned the bathrooms in the bungalows near Marsh Road, and the chorus lifts me an inch. I follow the line of the road out and down, and in my rearview mirror the Lark is smaller, then nothing, then someone else’s appointment at four. There is a small weight in the bottom of my bag: a napkin with a number and a promise about a plant, a plastic bag of almonds someone pressed into my hand during a toast, a single heel plate I took from the shoe repair as if I might learn how to mend something. The world is out there being its extravagant, treacherous, mended self.

At the next stoplight, I laugh out loud. It is not a hysterical laugh. It is the kind that makes you shake your head at your head. Then I cry a little, because the line between the two is a ribbon you can tie and untie with one hand. The hotel sits behind me rebuilt and I sit ahead of it, not rebuilt, exactly, but remade and remaking. My sister texts me a picture of her feet and Nate’s, bare, next to each other on the suite’s rug, with the caption: we did it. I text back an emoji that looks like a spark because even in my complicated heart I believe in sparks, and I press the gas and I go.
