The column rose clean and white at first, a plume like someone had breathed on cold glass. It unfurled from behind the black scab of Carrion Ridge and then leaned, wind taking it east. I put my coffee cup down and went to the middle of the cab, where the old Osborne fire finder still stood like a stubby altar, map clamped under the brass ring. It hadn’t been used by anyone official in years. The Forest had decommissioned this tower two summers ago. The last paid lookout had signed his name in pencil on the inside of a kitchen cabinet and left a coffee tin full of screws. I had asked to stay anyway. Final summer, I told the district ranger, then I’ll go. He’d shrugged in that government way that isn’t a yes and isn’t a no and said not to call when I saw smoke because they’d pulled the repeater from the north face and the radio was unreliable. I told him I had a sat phone. I lied. I had a box with a cracked antenna, some wire, and faith. That had seemed then as real as the trees.

I put my eye to the alidade’s sight hole. The glass was scratched but serviceable. It painted a crosshair on the far ridge and beyond, where the light dulled and the smoke was already shifting colors, white to dirty gray as it found something more than grass to eat. I nudged the sight north a hair, read the bearing, and whispered it once to remember. One-six-eight. I squinted at the pooled topography, little wrinkles of creek cuts, each one named in tiny typed letters I’d memorized years ago the way other people memorize lovers’ middle names and the birthdays of dogs. Carrion Ridge. Spider Gulch. Deerlick drainage. Deerlick was where June kept her place, a cabin the color of dead moss, shoveled up on an old claim flat by the bend. She’d moved there five years ago with half a dozen goats and a blue pickup that had to be sweet-talked on cold mornings. Estranged is the word you’d put on a form. It feels plainer than it is. We had said things to each other in the last conversation that should have cracked our molars. Then we had not said anything at all.

I set the bearing with my finger pressed to the rim, came over to the window that looked south. The glass was warm. The smoke column fattened as I watched, climbed and spread a little paved road of itself in the high air. The lookouts in the neighboring districts would be calling it in, triangulating with their healthy radios, scribbling bearings in their tidy logs. Someone would be sending an engine, then a dozer if they could, helos if the wind went ugly. They’d look at the resources map and see the places people had put themselves down with cabins and canopies and trampolines. Deerlick’s headwaters and drainage: structures minimal, occupancy scattered, no power, one road. They’d type this and that, do their arithmetic, and then some young planner would make a choice. Or, the wind would make it for him.

The radio on my shelf had been dead all morning, a pet that had stopped breathing. I had climbed the ladder to the trapdoor and gotten up on the belfry to stand with it in my hand and the whip antenna lashed to the railing, in case the extra height might blush it back to life. Static, a wash of it, like someone in another tower was dragging aluminum screen against a door. I had a roll of foil I’d cut strips from and wrapped around the base of the antenna to fake a ground plane. The clouds had passed all morning without a sound.

The tower creaked, a slow old boat sound as the wood shifted in the warmth. There were pencil marks at calf-height, original names of men from 1963, the summer of the big lightning bust when the ridge had cooked for a week. Tallies. For a while, early in my first summer up here, I had erased the little pencil notches as if polishing away ghosts might clean the air. It hadn’t. The ghosts stayed. They joined me for coffee and watched me sleep and lay down beside me on nights when the thunder rolled in from Idaho. I had made peace with them as with everything else that was no longer mine to fix.

The bearing I’d read once leaped again at me, letters going slaty in the mind. Deerlick. There was a slumping in my gut like a porch with termites. June had put that cabin there to prove she could stay where I couldn’t. Everything about it had been built out of that proof: the solar panel she’d wired herself, the graywater system she’d designed out of old gutters and a hand-dug trench. I had visited only once, a year after she moved in, and had stood on the stump that served as a front step while her goats nuzzled my thighs through my jeans. The place had felt like an intention, and I had looked at the way the slope cupped the meadow and the fuels laddered in the trees and I had thought, not with jealousy, but with the weatherman’s shrug of inevitability, one day the wind will find you.

The smoke budded a puff of brown from the column’s middle and I caught my breath. That particular brown, the almost orange of it, meant dirt, means a landslide or a dozer licking at the edge. Then the wind pressed the plume sideways and I saw that it was only swirl, not earth, not help.

A jay landed on the window’s edge and cocked its head at me. It opened its beak and produced the sound of a telephone that hasn’t rung in twenty years. It made me laugh and then want to cry.

I went to the radio and picked it up again. The battery needle lay where it had lain all morning, opinionated as an unhelpful aunt. I spoke anyway. Severe Ridge, calling Palmer dispatch. Severe Ridge to anyone. I listened to my voice return to me in tin, a sound like a coin bouncing and then falling between boards. Nothing useful came back. I tried the sat phone I did not have by setting the radio in the kitchen window where I’d had bars five summers ago when someone else’s satellite footprint had wandered here. Nothing.

I swept one arm across the table, pushed the crossword book, the coffee mug, the little plate of almond shells aside. The plate had been my mother’s, painted with lemons. June and I had fought over that plate in a pile of boxes on the day we’d emptied the last of our parents’ house. We had flung lines at each other like grapnels. She wanted it because she was the one who had been there at the end. I wanted it because I had been the one who wrote the letter that made our mother cry in the clinic waiting room. We pushed and pulled and then the plate slid, caught, and broke. We had both stared at the two halves, at the lemon suddenly a question on a fault line. It is not forgiveness to glue a plate back together and put crackers on it for the occasional day you think of her, but forgiveness comes in the shape it likes.

I kept the broken plate because it made an argument in my kitchen with a tidy line. June had kept the house. Then, after a winter of pipes that clacked and mice the size of hands in the oven, she’d sold it for a number that made her mouth tight and rolled the new money into the blue truck, into the goats, into a shed full of buckets and all things a person could do with milk.

I sidled up to the Osborne again, set the sight, checked the bearing against the dot of smoke now shading brown at its belly. If I walked the line off this ridge, if I dropped through the fir and the talus and the switchbacked slope, I could be in the bottom of the east draw by three, and if I kept the creek close and boulder-hopped where it pinched, I could be at Deerlick’s crossing by five. The road was eight miles of honest dirt down to the highway and then cell service and then sirens a lifetime after that. Or, as the smoke was telling me, other folks in other towers had already called it and the contracted helicopter I had heard duvet-water in my ears for years would come slapping up the draw with a red bag. They would not be flying for June, of course. They would be flying to save trees they had labeled “resource” in a spreadsheet. In saving them, they might incidentally wet her meadow, lay a ribbon on her tin roof. Or they might not.

There is a way you can live for so long with certain distances that you forget their slope. My tower to her cabin was a line like a scar. I could have drawn it blind with my finger in the dust on my desk. Some scars throbbed when it rained. This one throbbed under a different weather.

The wind freshened and the pines murmured in their old sussuration. The windows on the west side flashed my face at me in rectangles, each angled to a new truth. I had put off leaving this tower because planning to leave a place that had held you quiet feels like plotting a betrayal. I had made lists: what I would take in the last descending, what I would leave nailed to the walls to become the next ghost’s inheritance. The enamel basin. The half-can of spackle. The tiny music box with the ballerina whose head had snapped off in 1979 and been glued back with rage and Elmer’s. The radio, of course. The Osborne, big and dumb and too lovely to throw into a landfill. I had laughed about it with the ranger over the phone. He’d said I could sell the brass by the pound. I had said I’d leave it to whatever weather had more patience than men.

Now, I took my pack from under the cot. I counted out water in the way that looks like prayer. Two liters in tucked bladders, one in a dented Nalgene the color of a bruise. A length of rope. A coil of paracord. The first aid kit that assumes the person you are going to fix is an idiot who cut themselves badly with a brand-new knife. I took the Pulaski from under the stairs and ran my thumb along the blade. I had sharpened it in half-sleep on evenings when the mosquitoes chewed at my ankles and there were too many thoughts to swat.

The radio hissed again without voice. I renewed my love affair with the idea of battery terminals and wiggled them with my fingers. I lifted the radio until they stung me. A drop of sweat leaned its longness down my back. I set the radio on its shelf, turned it off. The tower seemed to hush at the click, a church when the catechism ends.

I wrote a note on the log as if the log were not wholly for ghosts and future archaeologists. Severe Ridge Lookout descended from tower at 1305 hrs. Smoke observed one-six-eight. Proceeding to Deerlick drainage. Radio inoperable. Laid the stub of the pencil on the line, blew dust from the paper. I set the note under a mug so it wouldn’t go wandering on the drafts from the trapdoor. I picked up the Pulaski and my pack and, after a moment’s silly pull of longing, the lemon plate half with the two intact yellow ovals and slid that too into the pack. You do not carry what you know will save your life. You carry what, if you live, you can set on a shelf and say, I did not abandon you.

On the landing outside, the smell of resin made a fog in the back of my head. The stairs rang under my boots one, then two, then a run of more than could be counted as I went down with my palm on the rail. The tower had leaned since it was built. It surprised me anew every time and then I was not surprised, only relieved that a thing could lean so long and still be.

Grasshoppers sprang like thumbnails in the grass. The sun found the center of my head and pressed. I locked the door at the bottom knowing that to do so was to tell no one not to come in. The berry bushes along the side had already flung out their seed. A lizard zipped over my boot and under the concrete pad like thought into a skull.

The trail off the ridge falls hard at first and then settles into switchbacks that seem too moderate because your legs begin to shake. Fir pollen stuck to the sweat on my forearms and made a dusted sweetness of me that would have made a bear sneer and pass. I had long ago learned to let the mountain’s spine tell me which way to move my feet. There is a trick to it, a listening that has nothing to do with ears. You hear it in the tendon behind your left knee. You hear it in the way your breath tightens on the second syllable of a song you hadn’t realized you’d started to hum. The Pulaski rode on my right shoulder and my pad strap creaked against the haft and the sound nearly made me weep again, not because it was melancholy but because it was the sound of work. I had not made anything in months but eggs and lists.

When my father had left the service, not in any romantic blaze but in the small repentance of age, he had gone south and found a shack by a river and taught himself to carve tiny birds, each with a particular intelligence at the beak. He had given me a wren the size of my thumbnail and said it was mine whether I came back or stayed, and I had smiled and put it on the cheap bookcase in the rental apartment that always smelled like rain in a way rain should not smell. The wren had been burned in the kitchen fire I had then and I had not told him because the day I would have told him was the day he died in the dentist’s chair. The chair had been my aunt’s description; she said it like a flag planted where he had been.

On the third switchback, I saw a snake in the trail, mottled and loose as a fallen rope. I stopped and we looked at each other in the only negotiation the mountain permits. Its skin moved in a noise like rain on a very old tent. I knew it by the head, by the kindness of the shape, that it wouldn’t kill me if I were not injured and foolish. It lifted its frontmost footless inch and drew its tongue through the air between us and then slid into the duff and was gone. The moment after an animal leaves you is the closest thing to prayer I have had in the last two years. There is a way your body remains made of its awareness, as if your skin has new seams.

I hit the ridge bench and my legs steadied. I could see the tower above me now, the little wood cabin box on braces, already small in a way it did not deserve. If I turned, I could see the smoke. I did not turn. There is a way you can spend yourself looking back. There is a way that kills you.

Down again into the alder at the creek’s upper run there are beaver dams, some barely begun, some collapsed by the last big spring run, their sticks exposed and grayed. The water there slid over stone with a sound so thin it might have been wind. I bent and wet my bandana and tied it over my neck. A dragonfly came to see if I was a flower, and as it hovered near my temple, it clicked, an electric little note that made me grin. People love birds for their organized song. They fail to love insects because the music is not theirs.

The smoke had leaned full on the ridge now, a scarf flung at the angle of a girl wanting to be seen. I could smell it when I climbed from the creek again, something sweet under the char, perhaps the loss of sap in cedar. They say you can taste what is cooking, that you can identify someone’s couch and someone’s baby pictures in the air. It was green duff now and needles and the ash of a barstool somewhere in the map. I let the taste be what it was. I let it pass over my tongue without making it into a memory I could later throw at June for choosing the wrong woods to love.

When we had been small and had taken to the gully behind the school with our crew of kids who were all elbows and bandanna knees, we had lit a fire in a coffee can with pilfered matches and fed it pine cones and pages from our math homework. June had crouched with her braids down the front of her shirt and had extended her hand like a saint to feel the heat of her own power. She’d burned her knuckles purple and then white and had wept not with pain but with the astonishment of her body telling her no. That had been my first lesson in her. I had been the one to pour water from the bottle and scold and blow and blow again. Later, when our mother had chastised us both and set the kitchen chair between us, I had been the one to say, It was mine. I had not been covering her. I had believed it. I had made the match the same as she had.

There was a long run along the north-facing slope where the duff was deeper and the bare roots reached out to grip your ankles like hands in the dark. I could hear the creek rising with the fall. The smoke showed in fits through the branches and it had a new color in it now, a charcoal thickness that meant bark, perhaps oak trying to barter its turned plates for time. Then, as I came down out of the fir and into the first stuttering of ponderosa, I felt the wind change. It’s not a poetic thing, the way the air shifts. It is not a throat. It’s a knee. It bumps you. It had been on my right cheek, and then it was at the back of my neck. I stopped and looked up to the east, to where the ridge had shaped the valley like a barn roof. The smoke smeared differently, fanned in a line toward me that made a kind of map over the place where Deerlick’s mouth would be. The difference between difficult and too late is the time you spend naming it.

My pack straps bit. The Pulaski had found its own shape against my shoulder and I would have sworn it hummed. I told myself that if I came upon a truck, any truck, I would take it and leave an envelope of guilt later. I told myself I was thirty-seven and no longer in the mood to practice righteousness at the expense of usefulness. Then I laughed, because I had been righteous when it came to June any number of times and it had never saved a thing.

I dropped into Deerlick at four-thirty by my ridiculous watch, the cheap one I had bought at a gas station, its face postered with a mountain it thought I might want but that did not exist. The creek there widened into a kind of meadow that had been tamped down by hooves. There were her prints in the mud, the two halves of her boot sole like quotation marks. There were prints of small hooves and of dogs. Chickens do not leave much of themselves, but I saw their little cursive whorls, the scribbles of their happy minds. The air was warmer here, cupped. The smoke had diminished the sun the way dirty glass takes daylight from a place and gives nothing back. On the left-hand bank, someone had set a line of rocks and the one in the middle had split along geologic argument. In the crook a tuft of tiny white flowers insisted. I bent and put my hand in the cold water and held it there long enough that my skin looked flayed. Long enough that the heat walking under my shirt like a cat went elsewhere.

The trail to the cabin is no trail, just a place where a man once carried shingles and then June dragged woven wire, and the mountain always knows those stories. It holds a scent the way a shirt holds grief. When I stepped into the stand of young alder that had styled itself into a little hall, I heard the goat bells first, a far clunk like a spoon dropped in a bathtub. Then I heard her voice. It wasn’t calling. It was talking. It was lower than I remembered and it was full of a patience I had mocked. I slid the Pulaski down into my hand, ready to be too dramatic, and then laughed again at myself because there was nothing here to fight but an animal and that animal could not hear me.

She was behind the house, at the pump’s handle, her hand on the worn-down spot where the metal had become satin. A length of green hose snaked over the ground, water running from its end in a glossy braid that darkened the boiled straw color of the grass. There was a pile of cut brush under the pine closest to the cabin and another pile on the other side. The natty little fence she’d built from pallet boards lay on its side like a ladder sleeping. Her hair, which she had always kept shorter than mine because she’d said she couldn’t bear the heat on her neck, was pinned back with two twist ties, the green of choked cucumbers. The goats, less well-behaved than children, wandered where the water pooled. One got its mouth over the gusher and came away triumphant and choking.

“June,” I said, and then my voice left like breath from a tight animal.

She turned and saw me and a dozen things crossed her face, each one as complete as a season. She said my name not like a gasp, but not like any of the Sunday afternoons either. As if she had been taught to say it by a stranger.

“You don’t have a radio,” she said stupidly. Then she grinned, a crack of wicked humor that took ten years off and gave three back. “No,” she said to her own mistake. “Of course not. The Federal Government has not deigned to bless you today.”

“The wind shifted,” I said, which was to say I had come anyway. “You’ve got an hour, if that. Less if the draw below crowns.”

“I know,” she said, and she was matter-of-fact in a way that both reassured me and made me want to slap her. “Help me with the woodpile, will you? Move it to the far side of the meadow. The goats think it’s a castle. I tell them it is an extravagance, but they love an extravagance most of all.”

“You’re not evacuating,” I said.

“I am in the very definition of evacuating,” she said. “I’m removing every possible fuel from thirty feet. I am not a fool.”

“Thirty feet,” I said. “Is a verse from a city regulation never intended for a canyon.”

“Forty, then,” she said. “Fifty. Help me, or go back to your spire and draw pictures of sad clouds.”

There was the swat of her tongue. There was recognition in her cruelty. It restored me even as it burned. I picked up one end of the pallet wood that was that extravagant castle and we walked it together, the boards raking our jeans. We dropped it beyond the oat stand where she had once failed to grow beans. I turned and grabbed a bale of hay and flung it too far and it burst and seeded the air with dust that was ancient in its disagreement with lungs. June coughed and spat and then laughed and it was a spate of sound that took my architecture and softened it like heat under a vinyl tablecloth.

We moved for ten minutes as no time at all and then she stopped and put her hand on my arm in a way that old strangers do. “Did you walk,” she said. “How long did you—”

“I left at one,” I said. The goats came and put their noses into my pack as if I were family they had a right to inventory. The chickens scratched under the edge of an overturned wash tub like a brigade. There was an unearthed bicycle wheel leaning against the cedar and it looked at me like a moon caged on its edge.

“Asshole,” June said, and then: “Thank you,” she said. The pump squeaked again like a child traveling under bedding. She turned it, and water came, the indifferent kindness that makes people sing.

We cleared the piles and moved her burn barrel and dragged the trash can to the creek bank. I pulled the blue tarp from the wood crib and half-carried, half-dragged that too to the clear. There was an orange propane cylinder under the porch and I had a moment where I saw the future in which we had both forgotten it and in which it had agreed to teach us the errors of our forgetfulness all at once. I yanked it out and rolled it to the far bend where the sand was cooler and said, “If we survive, you buy me dinner. If we don’t, I don’t care.”

She nodded as if I were telling her the temperature. She had put a generator between the cabin and the filter shed, and I pulled it too until it stood in the circle of new dirt like a stupid pet. The hose spat water that found the parched topsoil and sizzled invisibly. The air had taken on the tingle, the first whips of ash, little wrecks that folded on my forearm and disappeared. The foundation under the house was bricked with cinderblock, a pretension, and I hacked at the stiff dried weeds in its shadow with the Pulaski until the blade had that nice wet chew again and the weeds flew in arcs that were almost pretty.

“You could have called,” I said finally, because if I didn’t say it it would be the only sentence in this valley.

“Your tower could have answered,” she said back, and we were at the is/ought of two girls in their teens. It should have made me rage. It made me grin. “The repeater’s down,” I said, and then heard the idiocy of it. She was not the Forest. She was not the budget. “The phone’s dead. I tried.”

“You always try,” she said, but her tone had shifted to something like love.

“If you think I came to steal your goats, you can scratch that out of your Bible verse list,” I said, and the goats, hearing themselves named, came like compliments. I put my palm on the black head of the one with the flat stare and it decided we were begun and bit at my belt loop as if to hold me still. The smoke thickened enough now that it moved like weather you might draw. This is where it layers, I thought. This is where the air will become a being.

“I can get the truck,” June said, and shaded her eyes with the back of her palm to see through the flat. She guffed again at the pump and another loop of wetness fell onto the grass and was taken. “But the road’s going to be choked with idiots trying to outrun their own cigarettes. Better to go down to the slide and up the other side on foot.”

“You cannot lead goats through black,” I said.

“You can lead anything through black if it has once learned greed,” she said. She had named them Sugar and Salt and Pepper and then had stopped naming. They rubbed their flanks on each other because we had stolen their favorite and given it a new location.

“If you’re determined to stay,” I said, changing the shape of the sentence so I could hear it twice before it stuck, “then we protect the cabin and then we go to the creek and we get in.”

“That creek in August won’t take your knees,” she said, and it was true. You could lay your body down in it and the part of you that had expected to be refracted and saved would only be politely cooled. You would be cooked by air. A person could run to the far meadow where the grass had been grazed to a biblical plain. We might do better there with our lungs shutting like books.

She went into the house and came out with two N95 masks, which she had bought for smoke six summers back when the whole state had decided to teach people the difference between September and a phone call to your friends in another life. “God bless the woman who left these in the bin at the feeding store,” she said. She handed one to me. I put it on and tightened the elastic and my glasses fogged and then the fog burned off because there was no more water left in what I was exhaling, apparently. She tied her hair with a third twist tie, made a little nest of her own head, and looked at me like we were a team. I wanted to bruise something and also kiss her. She had the light in her eyes you see in those who have chosen their danger.

“What do you need?” I said, because this was how we used to be when our mother was drunk and our father was working and our kitchen was deciding which appliance to resent most. We triaged. We made lists. We went to the pantry and met the dust.

“Photographs,” she said, and then laughed like a bark. “I don’t have any.” She lifted her chin at the cabin. “It’s all in me. I don’t need that plate of lemons. You can keep it. Did you see what I did there. I made a joke.”

“Bad,” I said, and reached into my pack and took the lemon half-saucer out anyway and set it on the inside sill where the glass was clouded with kitchen. She looked at it and shook her head very slowly. “We are our mother,” she said. “We are her favorite, both of us. Are you ready.”

You can hear it coming. That is not legend. It is like a slow train when the train’s engine has decided that it is the only true thing. The air grew hot in a way I understood and did not. The crackle came from too many directions at once to be a sound. Embers landed on the dirt and made bright eyes and then vanished on their own or hissed when I stomped them. The goats crowded to the dark place where the porch shadow was and made their conversation of worry and little greed-based hopes. “They will not be good companions,” June said. “But neither would your friends.” She crouched and spoke to them in language we had made up when we were small, nonsense that had the shape of loyalty. They turned their black eyes to her and back to the space where the porch met the plank and back to her. A chicken flew in an unpracticed parabola to the roof and made its complaint in a voice more honest than mine.

A rim of flame showed on the low ridge to the south like a smile you might have had a year ago. It stayed, then it inched. The smoke swam over us and the sun was a raw coin in a cloth. “If we’re going,” I said.

“We’re going,” she said, and she coiled the hose and left it spitting into the biggest wash tub in case a thing with fur wanted to be wet, then left it and shook her head at her own sentimentality and turned and went to the shed and came back with a old wool blanket, the color of an argument, and another one in green, and she thrust the green at me like invitation.

“The meadow,” she said. “Other side. The place where the elk lay.” She said it like a direction to a party we had both promised to leave early.

We let the goats out. They made a boiling and then did exactly what goats do: they ran to the old rope swing and then stopped and watched the edge of the world coming. We released the chickens. They became punctuation. We did not bother with the truck. We went to the meadow, which was not far, a minute of a racketing walk now made into eight by ash and the pull of the air. The green of the world went to gray and then to a color that isn’t named because no child will ask for it on a birthday. The pine bark gasped, made its elbows into cunning, and let go.

We lay down in the shallow ditch where, in the rains, water would snake between one pool and the next. June put the blanket over both of us like a wedding and we pulled it around our heads and left a hole the size of time to breathe through. We had cellophane in the first aid kit, left from something foolish I had thought to do, and we put it over our faces and then the masks over that and the blanket over all. The roar came like a crowd at a game that has decided to end in blood. The heat took what was left of the little hair at my wrist and then forgot the smaller things because the larger ones were struggling in a different room. She put her hand in mine and we held not like children or like women with opinions, but like two small animals who had once been friendly under someone’s bed and now were needing each other more entirely than they had agreed they would.

Something fell in the trees and became a scream and then silence. Ember fell in the ditch with us and rolled and we pressed it to dirt with the backs of our fingers and our fingers burned in small and particular ways and we didn’t slap, because we knew not to, because slapping is for television. We sang under the blanket without deciding to sing, the one song we had known together in high school on the bus, the one about the man leaving because he couldn’t bear to be loved anymore. We were not that man and we were not the woman in the song, but the sound had a shape we could push against.

We lay there. We breathed when we could. It went on longer than it had any right and then it went on past that. Then, like all things, it lessened. The air lightened by a degree only the wrist can know. The roar went to a rumble, to a far dish of giants. The blanket stuck to our cheeks, little pieces of ancient wool making conversation with the oil of our faces. We didn’t move because moving is for the unafraid and the naive.

The goats had long since become possible coals in my mind, then had spun up again into goatness and then had left for whatever country a goat imagines. June slipped her hand from mine and made the shape of a cross on her chest with two fingers. It might have been a prayer. It might have been old Catholic muscle. It made me think of our mother lighting a match at the burner and then lifting it to her cigarette with a sort of joy.

“Are we alive,” I said into her hair. I could not hear my voice. I felt it in my jaw.

“We are some version,” she said. Then, she laughed, small, and it was like a bird huddled in a stovepipe. “When this lets us, we crawl to the water,” she said. “Not yet. We’ll stay until the air thinks we belong.”

When it let us, we rolled like people pretending to be brave. The sky had taken on the exhausted pink of a town’s neon sign going out. The mountain was black and also not. Little posts of flame stood up where the meadow had unspooled and the fire was content there, busy. Under the porch of the cabin a thing glowed as if remembering sun. The roof had a bright edge and then the bright edge bent.

June stood. The blanket slid off her shoulders and made a cape that became a thing explained by heat. She walked to the line of the ditch and looked toward the house, or where the house had been. She did not make a sound I could put into a sentence. She put one hand to her mouth. She kept it there a long time. The goats were in the far fence that bordered nothing. They had found the remnant leaf of a grape somewhere and were fighting over it as if it tasted like return.

A helicopter thudded its way up the drainage, too late and also right on time. Everything in the world is like that. It dragged a red bag that flicked and winked in menace and then in joy. It dropped a sheet of water on the far ridge in a throw that made me think of my arm as a girl, how hard I’d learned to toss a rock across the irrigation ditch, how far it had not gone, and how proud I had been anyway.

“I had a picture,” June said, suddenly conversational. “Of us, on the porch step, when we were little. Our knees were knobs. We had jam on our cheeks. I had made a list of where to put it so it wouldn’t be here when I couldn’t be. I could never decide on the place.” She looked at me with smoke in her lashes. “We pretend we left things different than we did,” she said. “We did not. We left fingerprints where we ate. We left our cut hair in the drain. That is all.”

“We did not leave,” I said, and it was neither argument nor peace. It was a naming. The shape of my mouth felt like the shape of hers. She grinned in a way that was not entirely a grin. Then we turned, together, toward the creek. The ash had settled as if it had always been there. The water was dull, then bright, then dull again between stone. We knelt and put our hands and then our wrists and then our faces in it and we let it say the only thing it knows to say to people who insist on surviving. We would go down the draw whether that was the wise thing or not. We would walk the long road that did not know which of us we had been, and we would walk it until the cell towers saw us as their own and someone would put both of us into a small loud room and give us water from a bottle and ask us who we loved and we would answer as if practicing.

Behind us, the roof of June’s cabin leaned and then went into the shape that fire makes finally, which is not cruelty but simply preference. The goat bells sounded like cutlery in a drawer no one wanted. June stood in the creek and drew her hands down her forearms as if she were removing sleeves, and when she looked up at me there was ash in her eyes in the place where tears might have been in a different novel. “I’ll stay,” she said, which was now a joke, or a toast, or a protest. “I’ll stay.”

“You’ll go,” I said. “For a while. You will not forgive me yet, because forgiveness today would be charity and we were not raised that way.”

“We weren’t raised,” she said. “We sprouted in a ditch like this one during a hundred-year weather.” She put one hand on the bank and levered herself up and the shale under her palm made the sound of coins. “Help me, will you,” she said, very soft. “Help me down the road to where I can call what I have to call.”

We had to go, and so we did. We walked with blankets, with a Pulaski, with a lemon plate broken into a shape that resembled our mother’s laugh. Above us the helicopter thudded again and the radio in my pack was a square of foolish weight. The air, in deciding it had done enough for the moment, let a little cool find my knee, and the creek shouldered its breath. A jay flicked through the black birch and made the sound of an old telephone again, and this time it was not a joke. It was an omen, and we were ready enough for it that we did not falter. We set our feet in front of each other as if we had always trusted that it was the feet that would save us.
