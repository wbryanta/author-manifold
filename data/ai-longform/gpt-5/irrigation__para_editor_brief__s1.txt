By noon the dirt had gone scabbed and white. Cal knelt in it anyway, leaning on the haft of the shovel until it bit. The blade made that dry, cracking sound, like breaking a big cookie, except the earth just dented and flaked and refused to separate. Out past the rows the canal dazzled bright as a sheet of tin, and the cottonwoods on its bank trembled along their edges as if the heat were a wind.

Eli was late, which Cal would have found funny, a reversal of all their years, except that the leak had turned the lower quarter-acre to pudding and there was no time if they were going to catch the afternoon run. He rocked the shovel back and forth in its notch, pressed his boot down near the toe where the steel had worn thin to a crescent, and tried to believe he was not hearing the rush of lost water under there, squandering itself in a private river that never reached any tree.

He had cut back across that lot on his second day home and seen the color—a darker, almost greenish brown among the dun rows—and thought: there. There was the soft pit, and the glint of little black flies working at the skin on the mud. In the ditch on the far side some of the burlap sandbags had come undone and spilled. The repair took both hands and more time than the thermostat allowed.

A truck creaked up the access road. Eli killed the engine somewhere behind Cal and took those heavy steps he had always taken, as if his boots had extra weight in the soles. By the sound of it Eli carried something that clanked.

“You already start?” Eli said.

“I lost my nerve without the boss,” Cal said. He raised a piece of pipe he had uncovered, dull and chalky. He had brushed the dirt off it with his hand; his palm now matched the pipe.

Eli squinted at the ground. He had put on weight in his face over the years and lost it in his eyes. His eyes were lighter than their father’s had been, a washed blue that set hard when he focused. He wore a cap from a seed company and a shirt he had probably gotten free at a Co-op dinner, and he had smeared green primer, or maybe grease, on one cuff. He set down a five-gallon bucket that had PVC couplers and a bottle of cement rattling around inside it, and a pair of hacksaws and a spare slip-fix, telescoping, on top.

“You dug wrong,” he said, but without heat.

Cal looked up. His back popped when he straightened. “The sound’s here.”

“It’s traveling the pipe. The dirt’s driest here because it’s draining that way.”

He pointed a dozen feet to the right, where the slope fell away a little toward the creek that was only a rumor when you got to it.

“Okay,” Cal said, knowing that was a lie, too. In this place, in this summer, water did not travel much of anywhere. It sank as fast as it could and hid.

Eli toed a fresh place and drove his shovel so easily that Cal felt the small greed that was always there when he watched his brother do something he thought he could do. It had always been that way: Eli knew where to step and where the dirt would give. He took two neat squares out and propped them aside like sod, good for replacing. Then he was down on his knees, scraping forward with his fingers among the roots.

They worked without words for half an hour. The sun parked itself on the back of Cal’s neck and sat there. His shirt clung to him. Something about the smell under the surface made his molars ache, a sour, old, iron smell that ran under every June he had lived through in that field. He conjured up what their father used to say as if it were a shade that could move the air off him.

Lightning don’t strike the same place twice, he'd say to a new man, leaning on his shovel as if he might take a nap on it. But gophers? Those little bastards? Every time.

The gopher mounds dotted the berms between rows like little crownings. They’d tried traps, baits, flares that smoked sulfur into the runs. The gophers had shrugged and recircuited. The backhoe teeth scar at the end of the block where Eli had taken a section out last winter made a tangle when you looked at it too long, a ruin that the eye kept trying to reassemble into a plan.

“You hear from her?” Eli asked, without looking up.

“Who?”

“Who, he says,” Eli muttered. “You know who. The realtor. Wallis.” He said the name with the liquid contempt he saved for anyone who drove a new SUV.

“Last week,” Cal said. He rested his forearms on the rim of the trench. They had reached a place where the soil stained their fingers like tea. “She said we have decent frontage. That this road’s going to—hell, I don’t know. Connect to something. That there’s a plan.”

“A plan,” Eli said, as if he were testing the taste of the word. He scooped and his fingernails scraped against something harder than dirt. He paused and glanced up. “Listen.”

Cal held still. The sound was there, no longer the imagined echo he’d been chasing. It was a steady hiss and a faint percussive knock.

“Right under it,” Eli said.

He cleared a little more, then wiggled the pipe with his fingers. A fissure, hair-fine at first, widened to the length of Cal’s little finger when Eli pressed, and then as if the pipe had been waiting for the contact, a seam let go and a slit opened. The water breathed out at them with the relief of a wound finally finding air. It gushed for a second, then steadied, filling the gap as quick as a dam break in miniature.

“Well,” Eli said, grinning in spite of himself. He reached for the bucket and passed Cal the shutoff key. “Run back and turn it off at the riser before this turns into a rice paddy.”

Cal stood and his knees held. He jogged the row, the ruts stiff under his boots. The trees to either side wore their leaves dusty and tough, the undersides silvering in the heat. It was the old block of apricots, cultivar they’d inherited from their grandfather. Cal never remembered the name but could feel the weight of all the summers tied up in the threads of it. In June, when they were small, they had played baseball with the windfall fruit, splashing the yellow flesh. Later, their father had sent them down the lines to pluck earwigs from the sprinkler heads—black and bad as curses when they pinched—and taught them the sound to listen for: not the big rush of the canal when the headgate was opened just right, but the tiny mad applause when every micro-sprinkler caught and started to turn.

He found the riser at the end of the block. The battered galv that rose out of the ground leaned a little to the left. The wheel valve on it had rusted into a shape that looked like a metal flower before a drought. He put the key on and turned. For a second he had the old panic: it’s stuck. He leaned into it and felt the old wheel give and then, with a jolt that traveled up his arms, snap one of its spokes. He swore more out of habit than pain. The wheel turned, grudging, and the hiss in the rows softened, and the ground, still spitting, lost its frenzy.

When he got back, Eli had the trench opened like a surgeon’s field. The pipe’s white ribs were bare under the mud smears. Eli had wiped most of the water away with his shirt. The crack ran six inches along the belly of the line.

“It was riding on a root,” Eli said, tapping the black line that crossed the trench where an old feeder had swelled like a vein. “Greased itself all those seasons and finally said the hell with it.”

He had already measured and was sawing. The hacksaw made a dust of plastic that clung to the sweat at his wrists. He finished the cut and lifted the section out like a bone sawed loose, cradled for a moment, then tossed aside. Water glugged into the void. Eli took the slip-fix and dry-fitted it once and shook his head.

“We’re going to need a coupler. Hand me—” He pointed and Cal passed one, and Eli eyed it the way he had once eyed the distance to the porch when they were playing catch: calculating, adjusting in his head.

While they worked, gnats trapped themselves on the men the way burrs trapped themselves on pantlegs. The bottle of purple primer in the bucket had not been closed right, and the smell got into everything, vinegar and gas and something violet that had nothing to do with the color. Eli smeared the brush around the inside of the coupler and along the chamfered end of the pipe and said, “We’re getting down to the end of this.” He meant the primer. He might have meant everything.

“It was never a good color,” Cal said, in the tone of someone making a note to the gods.

Eli laughed without looking at him. “My son tells me pink’s a boy color in, I don’t know, Persia. So we been wrong all along.”

“Your son,” Cal said, the same way people used to say my horse, as if it were a part of you and also something you fed.

Eli pressed the coupler on and twisted, held it, the pope of PVC. You had to hold it while the glue grabbed; that was what their father had taught them, and what he had not taught them was how long to hold. Cal watched Eli’s thumb whiten on the joint and felt the valley between his shoulder blades itch and sweat. When Eli let go, the coupler did not boing off. He gave it a tentative tug and then nodded as if he had gotten away with something. You always felt that way. A little hydraulics money you had borrowed from the future.

They were silent for a time. The birds in the trees kept close counsel too. In the distance, beyond the cottonwoods, a truck honked something like laughter.

“Wallis said she could have us an offer by Fourth of July,” Cal said, not because he needed to fill the air but because the air seemed to be curling in on itself.

“That fast?” Eli said. He ran his finger along the new joint and scraped off a little purple skin that had dried there.

“Developers don’t need a permit to think about something. They can move faster than we can blink.”

Eli snorted. “Developers. What’s to develop? This ground broke three good men. It’ll eat a different kind of man alive the same way.” He set the slip-fix like a telescope across the gap and marked it with his thumbnail.

“That’s exactly the point,” Cal said, too bright, like a man walking into a thing he had not rehearsed. “We’re not that different kind of man. It’s not a crime to say it. I don’t think I’m the wrong sort of son because I don’t want to watch you die dehydrated. You want to talk numbers? The electric alone—”

“Did I ask you for your mercy?” Eli said, his voice flat now. He did not look up. He drew back the violet brush and walked around the trench to get a better angle.

Cal could hear his own voice and hated it already. He had not slept well in the house, which sounded at night like every room was trying to breathe separately. He had gotten up three times to drink in the dark kitchen, blinking at the red eye of the coffee maker, and each time he had felt the same little mechanical twitch in his heart that meant there was something still to say to the man who was not there to hear it.

“I don’t want to run it alone,” Eli said, maybe to himself. “I don’t want to watch it sink by pennies. But I don’t want to sell it to some outfit that’s going to rip the trees out and pave over the birds and name it Stonefield.” He made his voice fancy on that word.

“They might leave some trees,” Cal said, a weak joke. The last time he had driven through what used to be a neighbor’s pear block they had left a few along the entrance to shade the sign. Those trees had taken an odd, theme-park posture, as if their trunks had been redressed for the part of a tree.

“Uh-huh,” Eli said. He reached for the glue. “And your Wallis, what did she say? What’d she call this? Prime?”

“Transitional,” Cal said, and they both had to smile. He knelt and steadied the pipe end with one hand while Eli worked. For a second there was only the joint and the mission of their hands, and then the pain in Cal’s thumb when Eli mashed the slip-fix with purpose. It was a real pain, not something from his head, and it calmed him.

“Hold,” Eli said, and Cal held. He looked at the tree trunks nearest, the way the sun limned their rows in dappled bars. He allowed the place to lay itself down over him like a skin he had worn for a decade once and then shed.

He had not only left. He had talked as if leaving would fix something that had not been broken yet. That was the part he did not look at straight on. When he finished the state college in the city to the north—where the summer heat was a rumor and you could always find shade—he told Eli that he just needed a year to “see things.” He had used that phrase as if the world were a museum and he had a pass. He had not counted how much time lay inside a year when you were the one waking before dawn to listen to the weather.

He had been in a walk-up in Portland when their mother called and told him their father had fallen out of the rowboat at the pond behind the citrus block and could not seem to stand. Eli had been the one to put their father in the truck and drive him to County with the good radiology, not the one down the road where the tech who used to play shortstop for the high school football team took the pictures. He had been the one to pull the first frost night alone when their father sat bundled in two jackets and a blanket like a man dressed as somebody else.

Cal cataloged those facts and told his other facts the same way he told them when he tried to sleep. He had sent money when he could. He had returned those three summers and picked with the cousins until his nails peeled. He had set the ladder wrong once and speared the roof of the shed. He had come home that time three years after their father died because he felt like if he went home any later he would have to change the story of who he was when he told it to people.

“Go shut it on again,” Eli said. “We’ll see if my art holds.”

Cal stood, the ache in his knees a sharp outline of the work. He ran back to the riser and turned the wheel with care. When the threads first caught, the pipe in his hands vibrated like a throat clearing. Somewhere a bird misinterpreted the sound and started its call and ended it at once. Back in the trench a seam sweated and then held. The joint did not blow. The new piece sat proud and ridiculous, purple-scarred, shining in a place where everything else was dull. Eli set his palm on it the way a man might lay a hand across a child’s head, not in blessing but in attendance, counting the breaths.

There was no applause yet. The micro-sprinklers that worked this section had been off too long; many of them had filled with earwigs and spun the wrong way or not at all. Eli rose and stooped along the row, flicking the emitters with a fingernail, setting some to spatter, breaking the legs off earwigs with a mild disgust that had no malice in it, like a tailor nipping a loose thread. Cal went the other way. He took the toothpick Eli handed him and he punctured each tiny hole and freed it. He got a sweet taste on his teeth, the plastic-straw flavor of water that had been stuck in a dark.

A hush lay on the field in which noise sat better. When the first half dozen emitters opened at once in a run, each hitting that little turn and starting to arc and chitter, Cal felt the thing he had felt as a boy—the idea that the orchard had a complicated happiness of its own that you could coax out of it with tools and time.

They met in the middle of the block. Eli straightened and rolled his shoulders. He took his cap off and wiped his head with it and put it back on in the same motion.

“I talked to him,” Eli said then, because the water seemed to have woken something other than thirst. “Two nights ago. Out loud. I mean, I probably do that more than I think. But this was on purpose. I opened the front door because it was too hot in the house and I said, I’m not giving it away, you old bastard. And I said, I will, however, consider making a sensible decision if one presents itself. Does that count for something?”

Cal, who had not yet talked out loud in that house, nodded once. “Who would have thought that was the deal?” he said. That you were negotiating with a man who could not answer and with a set of rows that might never sleep. “Maybe you should ask him where he hid the good valves.”

“I know where he hid the valves,” Eli said. “He hid them under the worst valve in the yard, so you’d never suspect.”

“Right,” Cal said, smiling once, the smile of people who knew the answer and said it anyway.

In the years since their father went, Eli had grown a beard. It was the same brown shot with blond their father had had in a thinner arrangement, and it had gathered white at the chin as if the man collected frost there on purpose. Up close, Eli smelled like the plastic glue and summer and whatever his laundry detergent was now. He had married his high school girlfriend eight years too late, and they had had a son and a daughter against the shorter odds. The kids moved through the house now as guests, as if waiting for someone to assign beds. Cal had made them pancakes at seven in the morning the day he arrived and burned the first one in the old pan just because he was trying not to.

“I don’t know if you’re hearing me, Cal,” Eli said, softer, and Cal knew what line they had reached. They always came to it, no matter what route. “I don’t know if what you’re saying is about money. Or if it’s about getting out from under a story you think you owe somebody. You can just say you don’t want to. I tried that once with Dad.” He smiled, a tired kind of smile. “He told me he’d dig my heart out with a spoon.”

Cal laughed, and it came out less forced in his own ears. “I remember that. I remember he was sitting in the recliner at Thanksgiving, and he had his plate on his lap, and he said it with gravy down his front. He taught me how to threaten and look stupid at the same time.”

“He taught me that it’s not really a threat if you’re going to do it,” Eli said. He looked over Cal’s shoulder, toward the far end where the rows fell into glare. “Look at that.”

They both turned. At the lower corner of the block a pipeline that fed the newer cherries stood on a saddle and then dropped. It had been their father’s last project, connecting the water to that block. He had slid a little as he measured and his numbers had not quite met his hands. The pipe settled into a bend. To one side a hawk on a post flexed and let out a scrape of a cry that seemed to belong to something with a higher voice.

The chitter of the emitters filled and filled. The ground took in the sound like a bowl under a faucet, and then when it could not hold, the sound overran.

The thing about orchards that people who bought fruit beside roads did not know: their noise. How it clucked and hissed and murmured when the wind came through, how it sighed and cracked its knuckles in the cold, how it swallowed a man’s noise in summer so that the only way you could hear yourself yell was to climb to the top of a ladder and holler out over the tops. How in August when the hummingbirds had found a way to drink from the leaks, the fliers’ wings sawed like little fans. How the irrigation, when it kicked right, made applause and scorn both at once.

They took a break. Eli had left the cooler in the truck in the thin slice of shade it cast. He brought it out and set it on the ground between the rows. He popped the lid and looked in. “Two beers, four Gatorades,” he said. “This is what it means to be a father.”

“You’re a saint,” Cal said and took a Gatorade. He cracked it and the smell of lemon-charge made him think of motel rooms he had used badly and of the plastic grocery bag he had tied around his father’s cast one summer to keep the bathwater out. Little chemical things carried whole afternoons with them.

They drank until the sweat on the bottles turned to little snakes and dripped.

“Do you want to walk the upper block?” Eli asked, seeming to be asking something else.

“I don’t care,” Cal said. He did care. He just did not know in which direction to care.

They shouldered loose the empties and walked up the row, brushing against the leaves and humming a tune neither would confess to knowing. There were not many apricots on this block this year. The winter had been warm when it needed to be cold, and then the cold had come too late, and the bloom had shown up confused and too soon and too all at once. The bees had shrugged, overburdened, and gone to their hives with the wrong dust on their legs. The fruit that had set were the size of small eggs, hard still in mid-June. Eli reached up and plucked one and thumbed it with knowledge and set it back. It swung a little on its stem like a man caught thinking about the past.

“Remember when he flooded and swore the neighbor stole time from him?” Eli asked. “Swore Bennie opened his gate too early and took our head. He went down to the ditch and laid in his belly on the concrete and watched the water come around the bend like it was a race and he had his money on it.”

“And then he got up and spit and called Bennie a liar to the air,” Cal said. “And Bennie, not being there to know he was in a fight, went on with the rest of his day.”

“He never could believe that other people had days that didn’t have him in them,” Eli said, and then he colored. “Listen to me. This is not the way we talk about the dead.”

“It is,” Cal said, kind. “Sometimes. It has to be all the ways. Otherwise we don’t know who we miss.”

They reached the place at the top where the pump shed threw a patch of deeper shadow. The old dog, Reba, who had made herself a bed under the foundation years ago and had given it to her puppies and then retrieved it when the puppies were too dumb to keep it, lay with her chin on her paws. She thumped once. Her eyes, the tea-color that lived in every dog’s head no matter the coat, flicked and measured their approach. She was deaf now. Eli squatted and rubbed her ribs; she breathed a long, gathering sigh and accepted the hand on her as the condition of the afternoon.

“D’you ever think we should have planted olives like Ruy did?” Eli said. “He’s got that press. People like to pour green oil on bread and make faces at each other. We could have made something fancy with a foreign name and paid our water bill with one bottle.”

“I think if we’d planted cactuses and gotten a license to milk them, people would have paid to watch,” Cal said. “I think it’s not about what kind of tree you put where, if this sky doesn’t change its mind.”

The pump shed door leaned on one hinge, as if embarrassed to be still attached. Inside, the motor was quiet for once. Every time it kicked on, it sounded to Cal like a promise made nervously.

Eli leaned in the doorway, letting the spotty shade lay across his bare forearms. “You asked me, other night, if I was angry with you,” he said.

“I did.”

“I said I didn’t have time.” Eli tilted his head to smile at his own line. “I said I didn’t have time to be that kind of angry anymore, that I had to pick and choose what got me.”

“And?” Cal said.

“And it’s true. But it’s also true that I keep a little jar of it somewhere. Not always, but sometimes. I unscrew the lid and sniff it like it’s some fancy salve and then I feel like my lungs have juice again. I’m going to do it today and then I’m going to put it back.”

Cal took that in with a feeling like he had taken a wrong turn and found a view instead. “I got my own jar,” he said. He wiped his wrist on his jeans. “That I take out and blame you with. Sometimes I put our mother in it, too.” He looked down. The pump shed threshold had rotted through in the middle; the splinters made a fossil shape of where a foot should not go. “You stayed,” he said. “Which made it so I could leave. So I leaned on that until the thing had a hole in it and I fell through.”

They let that talk lay a little, because it had weight and you did not carry something like that across a field without catching your breath.

“Hey!” a voice called across the rows then, breaking their quiet not unkindly. It was Skeeter, two lots over, who had been called David by his mother once, briefly. He came along the road that ran like a dry river behind the apricots, his long body in a long sleeve even in June, his hat a sun-battered smile.

“You boys got a lake in that bottom corner,” he called. “You floodin’ for fish now?”

“Fixed it,” Eli called back. “We like to make puddles for the hawks to look at.”

Skeeter laughed and picked his way down the road, coming to stand with his boot on a rock like a man boarding a train. “You look like your dad when you’re squinting into the sun mad,” he said to Eli, not unkind. “And you,” he said to Cal, “look like you think there’s a trick to it and you’re going to go find it at the hardware store.”

“We were talking about that,” Cal said, surprising himself. “About hardware and tricks.”

Skeeter nodded, and his face slipped into the mild sadness it carried sometimes when he talked about weather. “You hear they put up a sign at the end of Larkin? The brick people. Thirty-two homes starting from the low eights. They always put ‘starting from’ like they’re not going to end in you filling in the blank with your firstborn.”

Eli made a noise in his throat. “You selling, Skeeter?” he asked. He kept it neutral. A man’s finances were a way of thinking about his lungs.

Skeeter’s mouth did a following thing, chasing a word it did not want to say and catching it anyway. “We’re talking,” he said. “My boy’s in tech in Austin and says there’s money hiding in it like gold in creeks. Maybe there is. Maybe there’s money hiding in their creeks and none in ours. My Julie says she’s tired of the smell of mown grass making her happy and then sad, and if that’s not the dumbest reason to sell a place and the truest, I don’t know what is.”

“You’ll move into town?” Eli asked.

Skeeter shook his head. “I’ll move three miles. I’m not going all the way.” He scratched his collarbone with a blunt nail. “You boys take your time,” he said. “Don’t let a realtor tell you what your clock is. That’s all I came to say.” He tipped his hat—his hand did that motion like a bird—and then he was off down the road, his long back like a man who had been built in a shop that catalogued men in parts.

“Is there a jar for him, too?” Cal said, but too quietly for Skeeter to hear it.

“His is a bowl,” Eli said. “He chucks a stone in, and the sound it makes tells him the weather.”

They stood and watched their own water at work for a time, as if by seeing it get where it was going they could imagine the future as something that also moved when pushed. The heckle in Cal’s chest softened. The flinty part of him that had driven south with his window down just to smell the heat, that had told him that this was what he owed and what he could not pay, made a little room in itself where the day could sit.

“Help me with the far gate,” Eli said. “If we get it right, we can get the cherries a sip before dark.”

They walked the rows, stepping over the little rivers the emitters had made where they misfired. They came to the T where the late-add riser served the younger block. The valve that controlled it had a handle welded from a length of rebar their father had bent in the vice with the help of his two sons when they were too young to help in any other way. Cal remembered the vise’s block shape and the way his father had spoken to the metal as to a dog that would not sit.

Eli bent to the valve and put his weight on it and Cal heard the unsentimental voice in his own head say: it’s going to snap. But it did not. It turned. He imagined he felt the first seep of water into the other line. A hawk took flight overhead—not the same one as before; this one made no call, as if the air had answered enough for the day.

They squatted by the trench a while to make sure nothing shifted. Eli pressed a finger along the seam and came away dry. He wiped his hand on his pants and sat back on his heels.

“Dad told me once, coming out here in the dark when we were little, that water was time,” Eli said. “He said you could save it in the winter in the mountains and you could drive it along a ditch and you could measure it with a stick, but in the end, it moved how it wanted to move. He said, ‘You think you’re moving it, but it’s moving you.’”

“He told me water was money,” Cal said. “He said if I stood in it long enough I’d never waste a dollar again.”

“He told me it was a woman,” Eli said, grim, and they both shook their heads. “So he just tried all the metaphors out on us til one stuck.”

They sat very still for a time. The sun had softened a hair; you could feel ten degrees in your bones like somebody had taken their hands off your shoulders. Eli’s phone buzzed in his pocket. He looked at it, tipped it to block the glare, thumbed a text, put it away.

“June called,” he said. That was his wife. “Kids got back from the pool. Ava thinks her goggles gave her a nosebleed. Jack says he’s turning green from the chlorine and that if he does he will sue the county. June says if he turns green I should be home to see it.”

“You going to be?” Cal asked.

Eli nodded and stood. “I’m going to be, yeah,” he said. “We’ll go down to the pond later and throw rocks. They can argue about which rocks make the best sounds. Ava will pick flat ones and Jack will pick big ones and say you’ve got to admire the splash.”

“I’ll come,” Cal said, and then he heard his own voice add, “Unless you want it to be your—”

“Shut up,” Eli said, not cruel, with love. “If you think I’m going to sell this place to some man named Grant and then sit at a pond with just those two and pretend we don’t have an uncle, you can get in your car and drive home now and I’ll call you when it’s sold with a voice like a stranger.”

The word sold hung there like a fly, insistent and slow.

They began to fill the trench. They scooped dirt in by fingers first, to tuck it under and around like you tucked a blanket around a baby you were not sure yet you could pick up. They stamped it with their heels. Cal went back to the top and fetched the squares of sod and set them in like the lids of little graves, and then he wanted to scold his own brain for saying graves about a thing that had to do with water.

When they were done, they stood and wiped their hands on their pants and did not know for a second where to put their hands next. They were thirty yards down the row when the sound came. It was a squitter and a squawk and then a deeper note like a throat clearing. Cal spun and saw a glitter arc up and then fall. Eli jogged back and waded into a tiny moving mire; one of the emitters had burst. The belly of the line there had a bubble like a man’s hernia.

“It’ll hold,” Eli said, too quickly.

“Do you want the pliers?” Cal said, too quickly.

They stood and listened to it make its complaint. “It’s always the ones you thought would,” Eli said.

“Do we fix it now?” Cal asked.

Eli looked at the horizon, which was not a horizon but a thin, bright, moving line where the air had decided to turn into a river you could see. “No,” he said. “We let it cry this once. We go inside. We drink water.” He put his hand on the side of his head. “We’ll lift it tomorrow when it’s less hot than hell.”

They did as he said. They walked back to the truck and got in on cloth seats that had absorbed the day and gave it back to them. They drove the two hundred yards to the yard because they were fools, and then they were there and sweating and laughing because the distance was not a distance at all.

The house smelled like it always did when you opened it on a late afternoon: like lemon oil rubbed into wood, like the stale hollows behind pictures, like too many days of coffee added up. In the kitchen the fan in the corner bowed a little and then bowed again. The table had a ring of dust around the blue glass bowl. Eli filled two glasses and slid one to Cal and they drank the way men drink when a thing is free and worth more than anything else.

“You always had the good hand for joints,” Cal said. He looked at his palm and saw three little lines of purple like veins drawn in by a child.

“You always said thank you when I fixed a leak under your sink,” Eli said. “Even when it was our sink.”

They sat on the back step then, because there was no reason not to. The light on the yard did that trick it did: it made the tools look like they had been laid there on purpose by a painter, the shovel and the hoe and the length of string to mark a straight line. The elm by the back fence did not move in the heat but gave off a little health anyway, as if it knew other kinds of wind.

“You really would go?” Eli asked after a while. “If we sold it? You’d leave this town again on purpose?”

Cal thought about it. He thought about the way his feet knew which floorboard bent and which step caught and where the dog lay down so you should not swing the door too wide. He thought about the bread at the store that he liked and that always was on the wrong shelf and that he had to ask after. He thought about Wallis the realtor who had a man’s laugh from the bottom of a well and knew how to talk to Eli’s wife about colors as if colors could fix it.

“I think about it like this,” he said. “I’d leave, and then I’d come back. Not to the house. But to whatever was here. To see what they put where the pump shed was. To see if any bird mistook the new lamppost for a perch. And I’d hate it all at once and then I’d like some part of it without meaning to.”

Eli nodded, slow, his hand around the sweaty glass. “Yeah,” he said. “Me? I think if we sell it like that, I’ll wake up in an old-people’s place one day and look out my window and be glad there’s a strip of grass and a car that pulls up every twenty minutes and a kid who comes in and says, Hey Mister Jenkins, time for your pill. And I’ll think, fine. Taste is taste. But then I’ll smell diesel and wet dirt in my dream and I’ll go out of my mind a little.”

They sat with that. It was a good sitting and a hard one. The kind of sitting they had not done when they had wanted to be the other sort of men. The door behind them opened and June stood there, her hair drawn back and her hands empty. She had the face of someone who had tried on a hat at the fair and thought, maybe I can be this person, and then never bought the hat.

“You’re back,” she said mildly. “Ava’s convinced she’s going to bleed out. I told her her uncle is home and it cheered her up as if you were made of gauze.”

“I am,” Cal said, and stood. He felt the dirt bind his knee hair the way it did when he was thirteen and doing the worst job you could assign a son: pulling bindweed.

“A gusher?” she asked Eli.

“Just a hiss,” he said. “We’ll get it tomorrow. Lake Skeeter’s a puddle now.”

She looked out past them, at the bright world that did not know it had a name. “Your father would have said to get a flashlight.”

“He would have said to get six flashlights and a curse and all of your patience and a sandwich,” Eli said. He half-smiled. “He would have said we were all doing it wrong even when we were doing it right.”

June leaned her forehead against the doorframe, the wood smoothing the week out of her. “We can eat in an hour,” she said. “If you have the energy to taste.”

“We’ll have it by then,” Eli said.

She went back in. Eli and Cal listened to her set down plates in a rhythm that had come to their house late and then made itself a home.

“After,” Eli said. “Pond.”

“Pond,” Cal said.

They rose and crossed the yard like boys wearing costumes of their grown self. The first shade of evening had begun to sort itself out from mere lack of sun. The cottonwoods on the canal snapped their leaves with more sound as if they were coming to attention because the sky the hawks lived in was less of a wall and more of a thing you could step into.

At the lower corner of the block the new joint shone duller already, dusted by the time it had lived. A cicada woke and announced itself with no shame. Cal reached down and touched the fresh backfill with the hoe of his boot and felt it settle and hold. He thought of all the holding the place had done for him when he did not know it was doing it.

They took the path behind the pump shed where the grass had the nerve to grow thin and green even now. The pond lay like a giant coin that had been dropped and forgotten. Reeds at its edge stood up as if surprised at their own steadiness. Jack came pounding across the field from the house and skidded to a stop in the dust, arms windmilling, a boy-shaped diagram of molecule and motion. Ava came more quietly, holding her nose like a person in a cartoon about bad smells, though it was dry and clean and just hers. Eli’s hand went out and caught Jack’s shoulder and steadied him without owning him.

“Throw rocks!” Jack said. “Now? Now?”

“Now,” Eli said. “Now, now, now.”

They fanned out along the shore. Cal walked down to the water and chose a flat one from where they had all always chosen. He turned it over in his palm and felt the cool seep of moss on its underside. He bent and sent it skipping and it did skip, twice, then sank, as if it had accepted its terms with style. Ava made a sound that started as scorn and ended as approval. Jack threw a handful of gravel and it went like a prayer—messy and everywhere and somehow fine.

Behind them the orchard went on with its little hisses and ticks, the small private affirmations of its own labor. The sun slid, just enough that their shadows elongated and became animals separate from them on the bank. Eli crouched and pulled a reed and used it to point, showing Jack something about angle. Cal picked another good rock and weighed it and, without showing off, put it across the water as if it were a note he did not need to write.

After a while, when the kids’ voices got less bright and the sweat dried on their backs to a sort of clean, June called from the house. Her voice traveled over the field like something you could see, narrower now, steadier. They obeyed it. They walked back, each at his own pace.

Past the lower corner the broken emitter had laid a little ring of mire. A pair of bluebirds had found it and were taking turns hopping into the mud and smearing their bellies. Cal pointed and Eli saw. They took it in without speaking, the ridiculous pleasure of it, the birds flaunting what rich people paid good money to get at easy.

“We’ll fix it in the morning,” Eli said, and it was the kind of plan that made a night worth having.

Cal nodded. His chest opened to it, the way it sometimes had when he lay on his back under the apricot tree in July and the leaves had shaken a map at the sky. He looked toward the house, which held its shape like a man you love better when you notice the crack in his tooth. He thought about the road and what might come down it and who they might be to it by the time it did.

He and Eli walked side by side up the row. The dust had settled where their footsteps had argued it to move; their tracks made a pair where before there had been single lines. Somewhere a pump clicked on. The first bats came out and stitched little braille notes into the drapery of evening. Ahead of them, June turned on the back porch light and it did what it always did—it made a circle and declared it safety, and bugs came and wrote news in it, and inside that circle you could make a meal and a promise and get both eaten.

They did not decide on the way in. It felt for once like not deciding was something they were doing together, like cocking their heads at the sound of the pipe and waiting for it to tell them if the glue held. The orchard, who did not care about their metaphors, drank. The leak at the lower edge kept its little breathing up like a child in its sleep. The men did neither more nor less than walk toward the house, and in the walking, the day, instead of ending, found a place to set itself down with all its usefulness intact.
