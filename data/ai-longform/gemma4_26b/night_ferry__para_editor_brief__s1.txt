The engine of the *Sterna* did not so much run as it labored, a heavy, rhythmic thrumming that vibrated through the soles of Elias’s boots and settled in the marrow of his teeth. It was a subterranean sound, the heartbeat of a dying beast, pushing the ferry through the black, viscous throat of the strait. Outside the thick, salt-streaked porthole of his cabin, there was nothing but the erasure of the world. The darkness was absolute, a heavy velvet that seemed to press against the glass, occasionally interrupted by the rhythmic, white ghosts of the wake, which flared briefly before being swallowed by the tide.

Elias sat on the edge of the narrow bunk, his hands resting on his knees. He kept them still, though his fingers twitched with a restless, subcutaneous energy. He had spent the first hour of the crossing pacing the narrow corridor, counting the linoleum tiles, listening to the creak of the ship’s superstructure, but the movement had only served to increase the sense of being trapped. Now, he sat in the dark, waiting for the geography to change, waiting for the water to deliver him to a shore that held the possibility of a finality he wasn't sure he wanted.

The news had come in a telephone call that felt less like a tragedy and more like a clerical error. It had been a man from the local constabulary, his voice stripped of all inflection, speaking of a recovery near the coastal rocks, of a body found in a swell, of a certain set of identifiers. He had not said *your brother is dead.* He had said, *We believe we have located the individual reported missing three weeks ago.*

The uncertainty was the sharpest part of the blade. If the body was not Kael, then the absence remained an open wound, a haunting, unresolved note. But if it was him, the wound would be cauterged with a terrible, permanent heat.

He closed his eyes, and the image of Kael appeared, unbidden, as he had been the last time they had spoken—not in a moment of grandeur, but in a moment of profound, mundane irritation. They had been standing in a grocery aisle, the fluorescent lights humming overhead, arguing about something trivial, perhaps the brand of coffee or the necessity of a heavy coat. Kael had looked at him with that particular, sideways squint, a look of weary detachment that Elias had always interpreted as arrogance, but which, in the cold light of the crossing, felt more like a symptom of a slow, vanishing. Kael had been retreating from the world for years, a gradual evaporation of presence, until he was less a man and more a series of absences.

The ferry shuddered, a deep, metallic groan echoing through the hull. Elias gripped the edge of the bunk. The ship was entering the deeper channel, where the currents of the northern strait swirled in unseen, predatory eddies. He thought of the water beneath the hull, miles of cold, unlit depth, and wondered how much of Kael was already part of that darkness.

He stood and moved toward the small, cramped sink. He splashed cold water on his face, the sensation jarring and sharp. In the mirror, his own eyes looked back at him, hollowed by the glare of the small, dim light. He looked like a man composed of shadows. He wondered if the police would recognize him, or if he would be as unrecognizable to himself as the man in the morgue might be to the law.

He left the cabin and climbed the narrow stairs toward the upper deck. The wind hit him like a physical blow, a frigulcent, salt-laden gale that tore at his collar and stung his eyes. The air here was different—thinner, sharper, tasting of iron and ancient ice. He gripped the cold steel railing, leaning into the wind.

The deck was nearly empty. A few figures moved like ghosts through the mist, hooded in heavy oilskins, their faces obscured. A man near the bow was staring intently into the blackness, his silhouette a jagged tear in the fog. There was no conversation, only the roar of the wind and the relentless, churning churn of the propellers. The isolation was profound. On this ship, in this stretch of water, they were all suspended in a state of transition, caught between the certainty of the mainland and the grim ambiguity of the destination.

Elias looked out over the rail. The sea was a churning mass of charcoal and slate. He thought about the mechanics of the identification. The visual cues: the shape of the jaw, the way a certain scar notched the eyebrow, the distinct, uneven gap between the front teeth. But the sea was a corrosive thing. It smoothed edges; it stripped away the specifics of a person until all that remained was a generic, pale architecture. He felt a sudden, irrational surge of anger at the ocean for its capacity to erase. It was a thief of detail.

He began to walk the length of the deck, his boots clanking rhythmically on the metal grating. Each step felt heavy, as if the gravity of the strait were pulling at him, trying to drag him toward the rail. He found himself rehearsing the words he would say to the official. *I am here to identify the deceased.* He would try to sound professional, detached, a man performing a necessary, if unpleasant, civic duty. But beneath the rehearsed veneer, the thought looped endlessly: *Please let it be someone else. Please let the mistake be mine.*

As the hours bled into one another, the pre-dawn light began to bleed into the sky, a bruised, sickly purple that offered no warmth. The horizon line was a smudge, a blurred boundary where the grey sea met the grey sky. The ship began to slow, the heavy, rhythmic thrumming transitioning into a lower, more laborious pulse. They were approaching the pier.

The lights of the small port town flickered into view, low and amber, looking like dying embers scattered along the shoreline. They were not welcoming lights; they were utilitarian, casting long, flickering shadows across the black water. The town looked skeletal, a collection of huddled warehouses and salt-worn dwellings, clinging to the edge of the land as if afraid of being swept away.

Elias felt a profound sense of vertigo. The movement of the ship, the shifting of the light, the approach of the land—it all felt like a descent. The uncertainty was beginning to crystallize into a terrifying, singular focus. The ferry bumped against the rubber fenders of the pier with a dull, heavy thud, a sound that signaled the end of the liminal space.

He watched as the gangplank was lowered, a heavy tongue of metal descending into the dark. The other passengers began to move, a slow, shuffling procession of the weary and the displaced. He stood alone for a moment, watching the dockworkers move in the dim light, their movements efficient and indifferent. They were preparing for the arrival of more cargo, more people, more lives to be processed.

He stepped onto the gangplioned, his weight causing the metal to groan. His legs felt strangely light, as if he were still part of the sea, part of the darkness he had just traversed. As he reached the pier, the cold, damp air of the land enveloped him, smelling of wet stone, diesel, and rotting kelp.

The terminal was a small, low-slung building of concrete and corrugated iron. A single light flickered above the entrance, casting a pool of jaundiced light onto the wet pavement. Elias walked toward it, his shadow stretching long and thin before him, a dark precursor to his arrival. He reached the door and paused, his hand hovering over the cold metal handle. He took a breath, a shallow, trembling thing, and pushed.

Inside, the air was still and smelled of stale coffee and floor wax. A small, glass-partitioned desk sat in the center of the room, occupied by a woman whose face was etched with the profound boredom of the night shift. She did not look up as he approached, her eyes fixed on a ledger, her pen scratching a rhythmic, mindless pattern across the paper.

Elias stood at the window, the glass a cold barrier between him and the world. He waited, the silence of the terminal pressing in on him, heavier than the wind on the deck. He looked down at his hands. They were steady now, but his heart was a frantic, trapped thing against his ribs. He realized then that the crossing was not the ordeal. The crossing was the reprieve. The ordeal was the standing here, in the light, waiting for the truth to be revealed.
