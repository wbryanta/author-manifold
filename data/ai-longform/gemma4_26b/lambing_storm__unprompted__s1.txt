The wind did not arrive so much as it reclaimed the land, a scouring, hungry thing that had been gnawing at the edges of the valley all through March and now, in the cusp of April, decided to take everything. It clawed at the corrugated tin of the lambing shed, a rhythmic, percussive violence that made the heavy timber beams groan in their sockets. Inside, the air was thick with the scent of wet wool, old straw, and the sweet, cloying musk of ewes in the late stages of pregnancy. Clara sat on a low stool, her fingers moving rhythmically through a tangle of twine, but her eyes were fixed on the darkness beyond the windowpanes, where the world had ceased to exist. There was no horizon, no fence line, no distant silhouette of the hayloft—only a swirling, white erasure.

Beside her, Leo was cleaning a set of forceps, his movements methodical and unnervingly quiet. He was sixteen, but in the amber glow of the kerosene lamp, the soft lines of his face had hardened into something that looked far older. He had his father’s jaw, a stubborn, squared set to it that appeared whenever the work grew heavy. It was a feature Clara found both comforting and agonizing. When she looked at him, she saw the ghost of Thomas—not the man himself, but the blueprint of him, the way he held his shoulders when he was tired, the way he breathed through a long night of labor.

"The wind is shifting," Leo said. His voice was low, barely rising above the rasp of the metal against cloth. "It’s coming from the north now. The temperature is going to drop."

"I know," Clara said. She didn't want to admit she had been watching the thermometer, or that she had felt the sudden, sharp bite of the draft through the gaps in the door. She didn't want to admit that the silence of the farmhouse, miles away up the slope, was a weight she couldn't shake.

"The power went out at the main barn ten minutes ago," Leo said, looking up. "The lights in the hayloft flickered and then just... died. I saw the porch light go."

Clara felt a small, cold knot tighten in her stomach. "The generator is primed. If the shed's circuit trips, we'll move to the lanterns."

"It’s not just the power, Ma. It’s the heat. If the furnace in the house goes, we'll be freezing by morning."

"We have the ewes," Clara said, her voice steadying into the tone she used for business. "The heat from the huddle will keep the lambs warm enough if we can keep the drafts out. Focus on the heavy blankets. We need to double-pad the corner pens."

They worked in a practiced, wordless synchronicity that had been forged in the crucible of the last three months. Since the funeral, since the earth had been turned in the frozen mud of the cemetery, the rhythm of the farm had been broken, then reconstructed into something stranger and more solitary. There were no more discussions over morning coffee about the spring planting or the repair needed on the tractor. There was only the work, and the shared, unspoken understanding that the survival of the flock was the only metric of their success.

A sudden, sharp bleat pierced the low drone of the wind. It was a distressed, bubbling sound, coming from the far end of the shed where the older, more temperamental ewes were housed. Clara stood, her knees popping with a protest that mirrored the creak of the shed. She grabbed the lantern, the flame dancing wildly as she moved.

"That's Bess," she said.

Leo was already moving, grabbing a clean towel and a bottle of antiseptic.

They walked down the aisle of the shed, where the shadows stretched and contracted against the walls like breathing lungs. The ewes were huddled in the center of the pens, a mass of dark, undulating shapes, their breath rising in small, ghostly plumes. The storm was pressing harder now, the pressure change making the sheep restless, their heavy bodies shifting and bumping against the slats of the wooden partitions.

Bess was a large, sturdy ewe, the matriarch of the flock, but she was currently struggling. She was lying on her side, her flanks heaving, her eyes wide and rolling, showing the whites in the flickering light. The lambing was protracted, the labor dragging into a grueling, unproductive rhythm.

"She's been at it for four hours," Clara whispered, leaning over the gate. She could smell the metallic tang of blood and the heat of the animal’s distress. "She's too tired, Leo. The cold is draining her."

"We can help her," Leo said. He reached through the gate, his hands steady, pressing firmly but gently against the ewe’s abdomen.

Clara watched him. She saw the way he didn't flinch when the sheep lunged in a spasm of pain. He had stepped into the void Thomas had left, not by filling it, but by expanding to meet the new shape of their lives. It was a heavy thing to witness—the loss of a childhood to the necessity of a winter.

"Check the position," Clara commanded, her hands moving to the fleece, parting the thick, matted wool. "Is it a breech?"

Leo's face was grimed with dirt and lanolin. "I think so. I can feel a leg, but it’s tucked. It's tangled."

The wind gave a sudden, violent heave, slamming against the shed with such force that a piece of loose tin stripped away from the roof, clattering loudly onto the hay. The sudden noise sent a ripple of panic through the pens. A dozen ewes rose at once, their heavy movements thumping against the gates, their bleats joining in a discordant chorus.

"Stay calm," Clara said, though her own heart was hammering against her ribs. "Keep your hands on her, Leo. Keep her grounded."

The darkness outside the shed seemed to be encroaching, swallowing the edges of the pens, leaving them in a small, fragile island of light. The power failure had stripped away the illusion of modern stability; they were back in the era of the candle and the kerosene, of the primal struggle against the elements.

"I can't get a grip on the hoof," Leo muttered, his voice strained. He was kneeling in the straw, his shirt soaked with sweat despite the plummeting temperature. "The more she pushes, the more it slips."

Clara reached in, her fingers finding the slick, warm pressure of the ewe’s birth canal. The heat of the animal was immense, a stark contrast to the icy draft whistling through the cracks. She felt the familiar, terrifying tension of a difficult birth—the moment where life and death are balanced on the edge of a well-placed hand.

"Deep breaths, Bess," Clara murmured, her voice a low, rhythmic chant. "Easy, girl. Just a little more."

She thought of Thomas. She thought of how he would have known exactly which lever to use, which way to tilt his head to encourage the animal. The absence of him was a physical ache, a phantom limb that throbbed in time with the wind. She felt a sudden, irrational surge of anger at the storm, at the sheep, at the very persistence of life. Why must it be so hard? Why must the cycle continue so relentlessly, indifferent to the wreckage left in its wake?

"The lamb is stuck against the pelvic bone," Leo said, his voice tight. He was looking at her, his eyes searching hers for a solution.

"We have to turn it," Clara said. "We have to reach in and rotate the pelvis. It's going to be hard."

"I'll hold the gate," Leo said. "You do it. I'll steady her."

As Clara worked, the world narrowed to the space within the pen. The wind, the loss of the house, the empty chair at the kitchen table—all of it vanished. There was only the resistance of muscle and bone, the slippery texture of the lamb, and the desperate need to bring something safely into the light. She braced her feet against the straw, her muscles screaming as she applied the necessary, agonizing pressure. She felt the ewe heave, a monumental, guttated groan that seemed to vibrate through the very floorboards.

For a moment, there was a sickening sense of stagnation. The lamb seemed anchored, a permanent part of the ewe’s internal geography. The cold was seeping into Clara’s bones now, the dampness of the straw soaking through her heavy trousers. She felt a tremor in her hands.

"Don't stop," Leo whispered. He was leaning his full weight against the wooden gate, his face flushed, his breath coming in short, ragged bursts. "Don't stop, Ma."

She found a reserve of strength she didn't know she possessed, a reservoir of grit that only surfaced when the margin for error disappeared. She reached deeper, her fingers searching for the small, hard point of the leg. She felt it—a slight, subtle shift.

"There," she gasped. "I have the hoof."

With a slow, agonizingly careful movement, she began to pull. She guided the limb, maneuvering the small body through the narrow passage, her mind focused entirely on the geometry of the exit. The ewe let out a long, shuddering moan, her head falling back against the straw.

A sudden, wet sliding sound, a heavy splash against the floor, and then... silence.

The silence was more profound than the wind. It lasted only a second, but in that second, the world held its breath. Clara sat back on her heels, her hands trembling, her lungs burning. She stared at the small, dark shape lying in the straw, unmoving.

"Is it...?" Leo started, his voice breaking.

Clara didn't answer. She grabbed a clean, dry cloth and moved toward the small, sodden creature. She began to rub it vigorously, her movements frantic, the friction generating a small, desperate warmth. She cleared the mucus from its nose, wiped its eyes, her heart a drum in her ears.

"Come on," she hissed. "Come on, little one. Breathe. Just breathe."

The lamb remained limp, a small, insignificant lump of dark flesh in the vast, cold darkness of the shed. The wind roared, a reminder that the storm was far from over, and that they were utterly alone.

"It's not moving, Ma," Leo said. He had let go of the gate; he was slumped against the wood, his head bowed.

Clara felt a coldness that had nothing tolam with the weather. It was a familiar coldness, the one that had settled in her chest the day the funeral bells had stopped ringing. She looked at the lamb, and then at her son, and for a moment, she felt the urge to simply let the darkness take them both.

But then, a tiny, spasmodic twitch. A small, wet sniffle.

The lamb's legs, thin and wobbly, kicked out against the straw. A high, thin bleat, almost too weak to be heard above the creak of the rafters, broke the tension.

"It's breathing," Leo breathed, a ragged laugh escaping his throat.

Clara slumped back, her forehead resting against the cool, damp wood of the gate. She closed herlam, tears finally escaping and tracing hot, salt tracks through the dirt on her cheeks. "It's breathing," she echoed.

They stayed like that for a long time, the mother and the son, anchored to the earth by the tiny, struggling life between them. The ewe, Bess, had settled into a heavy, exhausted sleep, her flanks rising and falling in a slow, rhythmic tide. The storm continued its assault, the snow piling up against the sides of the shed, sealing them into their small, wooden universe.

As the hours ticked toward dawn, the exhaustion began to settle over them like a heavy shroud. The power remained out, the house remained dark and cold, and the landscape remained erased. But inside the shed, there was a new, fragile equilibrium.

Leo moved to the corner to stoke the small coal stove they had brought in, the orange glow casting long, dancing shadows across the floor. He looked older than he had an hour ago, his face etched with the fatigue of a man who had stared into the void and refused to blink.

"We should try to get some sleep," he said, his voice thick. "We can't stay awake for the rest of them."

"There's more coming," Clara said, looking toward the other pens, where the heavy breathing of the other ewes was a constant, low-frequency reminder of the task ahead. "The storm isn'ulum. We'll need to be ready."

"I'll take the first watch," Leo said. "You sleep for an hour. I'll wake you if anyone starts struggling."

Clara looked at him, really looked at him, in the flickering light. She saw the strength in his hands, the quiet competence in his eyes, and the profound, unasked-for burden he carried. She saw the man he was becoming, and the man he would never be, because of the life they had been forced to build.

"Thank you, Leo," she said, her voice barely a whisper.

He didn't respond with words, only a small, tired nod. He sat down on a bale of hay, the lantern between them, a solitary beacon against the encroaching night.

Clara retreated to her small, makeshift pallet in the corner, wrapped in layers of heavy wool and canvas. As she lay there, listening to the relentless, scouring wind, she found herself thinking not of the loss, but of the continuity. She thought of the lamb, now tucked against its mother's warmth, and the way the heavy, rhythmic breathing of the flock seemed to pulse in time with the very earth itself. The storm would pass; the sun would eventually find the valley again, even if it only revealed a world transformed and scarred. They would endure, as they always had, as the sheep endured, driven by the stubborn, irrational necessity of staying alive.

The darkness was deep, and the cold was biting, but as sleep finally began to pull at her, Clara felt a strange, flickering sense of peace. The house was empty, and the man was gone, but the life—the fierce, difficult, unyielding life—was still here, pushing through the dark, demanding to be heard.
