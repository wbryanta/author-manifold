The ferry moved through the water not as a vessel, but as a puncture in the dark, a blunt instrument carving through the heavy, unlit history of the strait. It was a slow, rhythmic violence, the hull meeting the black swells with a sound like something being dragged across velvet. Inside the cabin, the air was a sediment of stale coffee, diesel, and the damp, salt-heavy breath of the North Atlantic, a climate that seemed to settle in the lungs of the passengers, coating their thoughts in a fine, grey silt. Elias sat by the window, watching the way the light from the overhead fluorescents died against the glass, swallowed by a darkness so absolute it felt structural, as if the night were not an absence of light but a presence of weight.

He had spent much of his life avoiding this particular geography, the way the land and water here did not so much meet as collide, creating a landscape of jagged edges and uncertain boundaries. Now, the boundary was all there was. The ferry was a liminal thing, a floating room suspended between the memory of the mainland and the terrifying possibility of the shore. He thought of the phone call, the voice on the other end—not a voice of news, but a voice of fragments, a sequence of disconnected facts: a sighting near the Skraal rocks, a nameless man recovered from a life raft, a set of clothes, a certain way the jaw set in the cold. It was a dossier of uncertainty.

He traced the condensation on the window with a thumb. The moisture was cold, a biting reminder of the temperature outside, where the sea lived in a state of permanent, freezing hunger. He wondered if Julian would have recognized this movement, this slow, dragging passage through the dark. Julian had always been a man of the edges, a person who existed in the periphery of things, much like the light that flickered intermittently in the corridor behind him. To know Julian was to know the way he disappeared into a crowd, or a forest, or a sudden downpour, leaving only a residue of presence, like the scent of woodsmoke after the fire has been raked into ash.

The estrangement had not been a singular event, a sudden rupture or a shouted argument, but rather a gradual erosion, a slow leaching of shared language. They had become two different dialects of the same forgotten tongue. There was no anger left to fuel a confrontation, only the vast, quiet distance that had grown between them, as measurable as the miles of black water currently churning beneath the ferry’s keel. He remembered, with a sudden, piercing clarity, a summer in the far north, years before the silence had set in. They had been standing on a pier that smelled of pine tar and rotting kelp, watching the tide go out. Julian had pointed to something submerged, something dark and shifting in the shall, and said, *Everything is just waiting to be found, Elias. We’re just waiting for the tide to change.*

Now, the tide was changing, but it was bringing with it something that might be a ghost.

A man sat three rows back, his head bowed over a small, handheld radio that emitted a low, rhythmic hiss, a white noise that seemed to sync with the vibration of the engine. The sound was a pulse, a mechanical heartbeat that anchored the ship to its purpose. Elias looked away, his eyes drawn back to the blackness. Occasionally, a whitecap would break the surface, a brief, frantic gesture of light before being smothered by the dark. It was a landscape of erasure.

He thought of the clothes mentioned in the report. A heavy wool coat, navy, frayed at the cuffs. A certain way of wearing a scarf, looped with a careless indifference. He tried to conjure the image of his brother’s hands, but they were blurring, the edges of the memory softening like a photograph left too long in the sun. He could see the way the hands moved when he spoke—restless, searching the air for something to grasp—but the skin, the scars, the very texture of him was becoming a ghost. To identify a body is to perform an archaeology of the self; you must look through the layers of decay and the obscuring veil of death to find the recognizable truth. He was terrified that when he stood in that cold room, under those sterile, unblinking lights, he would look at the face and see only a stranger, a hollow vessel that had been stripped of the history they had once shared.

The ferry groaned, a deep, metallic protest as it navigated a particularly heavy swell. The sudden jolt sent a tremor through the floorboards, up through the soles of his boots, into his very bones. For a moment, the ship felt fragile, a thin membrane of steel separating him from the crushing depths. He closed his eyes, and in the darkness of his own lids, he saw the coastlines of their childhood—the broken, rocky inlets, the way the heather clung to the granite, the sense that the earth was a ledger of everything that had ever been lost.

He remembered the way Julian used to collect stones, smoothing them in his pockets until they were as slick as river glass. He had a way of seeing the potential in the mundane, a way of finding the hidden architecture in a pile of rubble. He had been a person of intense, quiet observations. If the man in the morgue was Julian, then the world would have lost its most perceptive witness.

The cabin lights flickered, a brief, stuttering hesitation in the electrical current. In that moment of dimness, the ship seemed to vanish, leaving him suspended in a void, adrift in the center of the strait, where there was no up or down, no arrival or departure. He was alone with the weight of the possibility. The uncertainty was a physical sensation, a pressure in his chest, as much as the cold.

He thought of the news of the recovery again. There had been no name, only a possibility. A man had been seen in a tavern in a port three days prior, a man who moved with a certain recognizable limp, a man who had been running from something, or perhaps toward something. The idea of Julian running was almost more palatable than the idea of him being gone. Running implied a trajectory, a direction, a momentum. Being dead was a stasis, a sudden cessation of all movement, a freezing of the narrative.

The ferry began to slow. The engine's roar transitioned into a lower, more guttural drone, and the rhythmic slapping of the waves against the hull changed its tempo. The direction of the ship was shifting, turning toward the unseen pier, toward the mouth of the harbor. The arrival was approaching, and with it, the necessity of the gaze. He would have to look. He would have to search for the familiar in the unfamiliar, for the brother in the bone.

He stood up, his legs feeling heavy, as if he, too, were made of the same silt and salt as the seabed. He walked down the narrow aisle, the linoleum floor squeaking under his weight. The air in the corridor was colder, smelling of damp wool and the sharp, metallic tang of the approaching land. He passed the man with the radio, who was now a silhouette in the dim light, a dark shape etched against the pale glow of the vending machine.

As the ferry drifted closer to the dock, the lights of the harbor began to emerge from the gloom—not bright, triumphant lights, or beacons of hope, but small, weary flickers of amber and white, struggling against the surrounding shadow. They were lonely lights, marking the edges of a place that seemed equally prepared to be swallowed by the sea.

He reached the heavy steel door that led to the deck. He pressed his hand against the cold metal, feeling the vibration of the ship’s deceleration. He thought of the face he would encounter—the possibility of a recognizable eye, a certain curve of the lip, a scar that spoke of a childhood accident. He thought of the way a person’s identity is held in the smallest, most insignificant details, and how easily those details can be erased by the sea.

The ferry bumped against the pier with a dull, heavy thud, a final, jarring punctuation to the night’s journey. The engines began to whine down, the sudden silence following the cessation of the noise even more profound than the noise itself. It was a silence that demanded to be filled, a vacuum that pulled at his very breath.

He pushed the door open. The wind hit him instantly, a freezing, salt-laden gust that tore at his coat and stung his eyes. The air was sharp, tasting of iron and old wood. The pier was a dark, skeletal structure, illuminated only by the intermittent sweep of a distant lighthouse. The water beneath the pier was a churning, black mass, restless and unyielding.

He stepped out onto the deck, his boots finding purchase on the slick, wet metal. He stood there for a moment, looking out toward the strait, toward the vast, unreadable expanse of the night. Somewhere out there, in the dark, the truth was submerged, waiting for the tide, waiting for someone to reach into the cold and pull it back into the light. He turned his gaze toward the shore, toward the waiting lights, toward the morgue, toward the end of the journey, and the beginning of the identification. The uncertainty remained, a heavy, unmoored thing, but the movement had ended, and the arrival had begun. He began to walk, his footsteps echoing on the hollow metal of the pier, a rhythmic, solitary sound in the vast, listening dark.
