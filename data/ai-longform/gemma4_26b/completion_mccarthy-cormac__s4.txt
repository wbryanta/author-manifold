He woke with a violent convulsion, his heart hammering against his ribs like a trapped and dying thing, the terror of the mob still clinging to his skin in a cold and oily residue. He lay for a long time in the grey and featureless dark, his breath coming in ragged and shallow pulls, waiting for the hands to find him, waiting for the weight of the crowd to press him down into the earth, but there was only the heavy and indifferent silence of the room and the slow and rhythmic breathing of the woman beside him. The heat of his own fear was a singular and burning presence in the void. He turned his head and saw her silhouette, a dark and static shape against the nothingness, her face unreadable and lost to the gloom, and he felt the terrible and familiar ache of the dream receding into the recesses of his mind like a tide of black bile. He sat up and the movement was stiff and graceless and the cold of the room settled upon his bare shoulders with a sudden and biting ferand, a reminder of the world that remained when the visions were stripped away.

The room was a husk of a thing, a structure of rot and grey timber that seemed less built than merely assembled from the debris of some forgotten catastrophe. The wind moved through the gaps in the wall with a low and mournful soughing, carrying with it the scent of wet ash and the bitter and metallic tang of the river, and he watched the way the shadows shifted in the corners, indistinguishable from the shapes of the furniture or the slumped forms of their meager belongings. He could hear the woman stirring, the sound of her limbs shifting beneath the coarse and heavy blankets, a sound that was both intimate and profoundly alien in the vast and hollowed-out space they occupied. She did not speak. There was no need for speech in the quiet of that hour, for they had long since abandoned the utility of words, finding them to be nothing more than empty and brittle things that broke against the hardness of their reality.

He rose and moved toward the small and guttering ember of the fire, his feet finding the uneven and packed earth of the floor with a familiarity born of many such nights. He knelt and began the slow and painstaking work of coaxing the heat back into the hearth, his fingers brushing against the cold and grit-laden ash, and as he worked he felt the presence of the weight in her belly, a heavy and silent consciousness that seemed to anchor them both to the dying world. It was a weight that neither of them spoke of, a burden that lay between them in the dark like an unexploded and ancient thing, a promise of a future that felt like a transgression against the desolation. 

The light eventually took shape, a dim and bruised grey that bled through the cracks in the wood and revealed the desolation of their surroundings. The room was filled with the detritus of their flight: the battered pannikins, the ragged duffels, the knives and the scraps of salt-cured meat, all laid out in a grim and functional geometry. He looked at her then, and she was sitting upright, her eyes dark and deep and reflecting nothing of the dawn, her hands resting heavily upon the mound of her abdomen. She watched him with a gaze that was neither loving nor hateful but merely observant, a witness to the slow and inevitable erosion of their lives.

They ate in the same wordless communion that had characterized their many days of travel, the food bitter and dry and tasting of the dust and the struggle, and then they began the ritual of preparation. There was a grim and mechanical precision to their movements, the packing of the gear, the checking of the blades, the securing of the straps, all performed under the shadow of the coming day. The world outside was beginning to reveal itself in shades of slate and bone, a landscape of stunted trees and grey stones that stretched out toward a horizon obscured by a low and choking mist. There was no sun to herald the morning, only a thickening of the light, a slow and pallid brightening of the gloom that offered no warmth and no hope.

When they finally stepped out from the shelter of the ruins, the air was cold and tasted of frost and old iron. The river lay below them, a sluggish and leaden vein cutting through the broken terrain, its surface obscured by the swirling and heavy vapours of the mist. They began their descent, moving with the same single-file and labored rhythm that the travelers in the sun had once possessed, their shadows long and indistinct on the frost-rimed ground. The path was a treacherous and winding thing, a scramble over scree and through thickets of dead and brittle scrub that tore at their clothes and their skin with a mindless and stinging cruelty.

As they moved, the memory of the dream began to intermingle with the reality of the path, the two becoming a single and seamless tapestry of dread. He saw in the twisted limbs of the dead oaks the reaching arms of the prophet's supplicants; he saw in the grey and churning water of the river the darkness that had fallen when the sun buckled. The landscape itself seemed to be part of the conspiracy, a vast and indifferent witness to the slow and agonizing dissolution of all things. There were no birds to sing in the heights, no insects to hum in the grass, only the sound of their own breathing and the rhythmic and heavy thud of their boots upon the earth.

They reached the riverbank as the mist began to thin, revealing a world that was even more desolate than the one they had left behind. The river was a wide and stagnant expanse, choked with the debris of upstream collapses, with rotting logs and heaps of grey silt clogging its flow. The banks were scarred and eroded, the mud thick and black and smelling of ancient and anaerobic decay. They found a place of relative shelter beneath a shelf of overhanging rock and began to set about the business of the day, the task of finding sustenance and sustenance alone.

The woman moved with a slow and heavy grace, her movements burdened by the child she carried, yet there was a strength in her that seemed drawn from the very stone and grit of the landscape. She searched the edges of the river for anything of use, for edible roots or perhaps the small and hardy crustaceans that occasionally thrived in the stagnant pools, and he watched her from the periphery of his vision, a dark and persistent figure in the grey light. He felt a sudden and sharp pang of something that might have been pity or perhaps merely a recognition of the cruelty of their circumstance, for she was a creature of immense and silent fortitude, a person who had endured the collapse of the sun and the death of the light without so much as a murmur of protest.

The afternoon brought no reprieve, only a deepening of the grey and a thickening of the air. The mist returned, heavier now, a wet and clinging shroud that obscured the far bank and turned the world into a claustrophobic and airless cell. They sat by the edge of the water, the river's low and rhythmic lapping the only sound in the void, and he found himself staring into the dark and swirling eddies, seeing there the faces of the mob, the eyes of the pariahs, the mouths agape in their silent and horrific howling. He wondered if the prophet had truly been there, if the eclipse had truly been a moment of promised salvation that had curdled into a permanent and inescapable night, or if the dream was merely a projection of the rot that had taken hold of his own soul.

He looked at the woman, and she was staring at the river as well, her expression as inscrutable as the water itself. He wanted to speak to her, to ask if she too felt the encroaching dark, if she too felt the weight of the unnamed thing within her as a harbinger of the end, but the words would not come. To speak would be to acknowledge the reality of their predicament, to give voice to the terror that lay dormant beneath the surface of their endurance, and so they remained in their shared and heavy silence, two ghosts moving through a graveyard of a world.

As the light began to fail once more, retreating into the bruised and purplish hues of the late afternoon, they began to move again, heading west along the river's edge. The terrain became more broken, a labyrinth of sandbars and mudflats and dense thickets of willow that seemed to close in around them with a predatory and mindless intent. The shadows lengthened and merged with the rising gloom, and the sense of being watched, of being pursued by the very darkness they inhabited, grew more acute and more insistent. He felt the eyes of the dead upon him, the gaze of the starved and the broken, and he found himself quickening his pace, driven by a primal and irrational need to outrun the memory of the mob and the crushing weight of the coming night.

They found a small and hollowed-larval depression in the earth, a shallow pit carved by some ancient and nameless flood, and there they made their camp. It was a meager and precarious thing, a mere indentation in the landscape, but it offered a small and illusory sense of sanctuary from the wind and the damp. They lit a small and sputtering fire, the flame a weak and flickering defiance against the encroaching black, and as they ate their meager rations, the shadows of their forms danced in a grotesque and distorted ballet against the walls of the pit.

The woman sat by the fire, her face illuminated by the orange and dying light, and for a moment, just a moment, he saw in her eyes a flicker of something that might have been recognition or perhaps a shared and silent grief. It was a look that spoke of the shared burden of their existence, of the shared knowledge that they were the last of a dying lineage, the survivors of a world that had long since passed into the realm of myth and nightmare.

He lay down on the hard and cold ground, his limbs heavy and aching, and as he closed his eyes, the darkness of the night rushed in to meet him, a vast and suffocating ocean of blackness. He felt the presence of the woman beside him, the warmth of her body a small and fragile heat in the encroating chill, and he felt the weight of the child within her, a silent and pulsing life in the heart of the void. And as he drifted toward the edge of sleep, he heard, or perhaps only imagined he heard, the distant and muffled sound of a crowd, a low and rhythmic chanting that rose from the depths of the earth, a sound of hunger and of hate and of an inexorable and approaching doom.

The fire died down to a bed of glowing and red embers, and the world became a singular and absolute darkness. In that dark, there was no distinction between the man and the woman, between the living and the dead, between the dream and the reality. There was only the heavy and rhythmic breathing of the two of them, a small and rhythmic pulse of existence in the middle of a vast and indifferent emptiness, and the sense that the sun would never again breach the horizon, that the eclipse was permanent, and that they were forever lost in the long and shadowless night of the world.

He dreamt again, but this time there was no prophet and no square and no multitude. There was only a vast and featureless plain of grey ash, and he was walking across it, alone and without direction, carrying a heavy and nameless vessel that he could not see but could feel with every step. The ash rose in fine and choking clouds around his feet, and the sky above him was a flat and featureless expanse of leaden grey. He walked for a time that had no measure, through a landscape that had no landmarks, until he reached the edge of a great and bottomless abyss. He stood at the precipice, the edge crumbling beneath his weight, and he looked down into the infinite and swallowing dark, and he realized that the vessel he carried was empty, and that he, too, were as hollow and as void as the space he stared into, a mere phantom of a man wandering through the ruins of a dead and forgotten universe.

When he awoke, the dawn was not a dawn but merely a slightly less profound shade of black. The air was even colder now, a biting and crystalline frost that had turned the mud to stone and the breath to mist. The woman was already up, her movements slow and deliberate as she prepared the meager supplies for the day's journey. She did not look at him as he rose, nor did she acknowledge the way he trembled as the cold took hold of his bones. They were simply two more shadows in a landscape of shadows, two more remnants of a broken and discarded era, moving through the wreckage of a world that had forgotten the very concept of light.

They began the trek westward, following the river's uncertain and winding course. The landscape became increasingly rugged, a series of steep and crumbling ridges and deep and shadowed ravines that seemed to trap the mist and the cold in a perpetual and suffocating embrace. They moved through forests of dead and skeletal trees, their branches interlaced like the fingers of a drowning man, creating a canopy of grey and brittle wood that blocked out any possibility of seeing the sky. Every step was a struggle against the terrain and against the exhaustion that was settling into the very marrow of his bones, a fatigue that was not merely physical but something deeper and more fundamental, a weariness of the soul.

As they climbed a particularly steep and treacherous slope, he stumbled, his foot slipping on a patch of loose and treacherous shale. He went down hard, his hands clawing at the cold and unforgiving earth, and for a moment, a flash of pure and unadulterulated terror seized him. He felt as though the ground itself were opening up to swallow him, as though the abyss from the dream had finally found him in the waking world. He lay there for a moment, gaspon and gasping, the taste of grit and blood in his mouth, and he saw the woman standing above him, her silhouette a dark and judgmental shape against the grey. She did not reach out to help him; she merely waited, her presence a silent and heavy demand for him to continue, to endure, to persist in the face of the inevitable.

He forced himself up, his muscles screaming with the effort, and he continued the ascent, his eyes fixed on the back of her head, on the rhythmic and slow movement of her shoulders. They reached the summit of the ridge and looked out over a vast and undulating expanse of grey wasteland, a sea of broken hills and stagnant valleys that stretched toward a horizon that was nowhere to be seen. The world was a monochrome of ash and stone, a landscape of utter and profound desolation, and there was no sign of any life, no sign of any movement, no sign of anything other than the slow and agonizing erosion of the earth itself.

The river below had disappeared, lost beneath a layer of dense and impenetrable fog that clung to the lowlands like a shroud. They were moving through a world of ghosts, a world where the boundaries between the past and the present, between the dream and the reality, had been irrevocably dissolved. He felt a strange and hollowed-out sense of peace, a resignation to the darkness that had settled over him, a realization that there was no escape from the shadow, no refuge from the weight of the existence they endured. They were part of the landscape now, part of the rot and the decay and the slow and silent death of the world, and they would continue to move, single file and slowly, until the last of the light had faded and the last of the shadows had been consumed by the infinite and absolute night.

The path ahead was obscured by the thickening mist, a grey and feature and featureless void that swallowed the very idea of a destination. They pressed on, their footsteps muffled by the soft and damp earth, their presence unrecorded by any living thing. The wind began to rise, a low and mournful wailing that seemed to emanate from the very bones of the earth, and the cold deepened, a piercing and relentless chill that threatened to freeze the very blood in their veins. And yet they moved, driven by a nameless and unreasoning compulsion, two shadows drifting through a landscape of ruins, towards a future that was nothing more than a more profound and inescapable darkness.
