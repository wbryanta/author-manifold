He kept his gaze low, focused more on the path ahead than the eaves of the houses passing him by. To look too closely at the windows of the ramshackle dwellings was to invite the scrutiny of those peering through the shutters, and in a land where a stranger’s very presence could be interpreted as a threat or an opportunity, scrutiny was a heavy thing to wear. The village, which the locals called Blackwood but which Jacob knew only as a collection of rot and river-silt, was settling into the humid gloom of evening. The sun, once a blinding orb of gold within the fog, had retreated behind the dense canopy of the treeline, leaving the world in a state of bruised purple and deepening grey.

Regina’s hooves made a rhythmic, hollow sound against the damp earth, a solitary percussion in a landscape that felt as though it were holding its breath. As he moved deeper into the settlement, the architecture became more skeletal. Houses of pine and cedar, weathered to the color of bone, leaned precariously against one another. Some were perched on stilts to escape the seasonal swelling of the river, looking like spindly insects frozen in mid-stride. He passed a blacksmith’s forge, the coals long dead, and a small storehouse where the scent of cured meat and salt fish hung heavy and cloying in the stagnant air.

It was near the edge of the village, where the trail curved toward the larger plantation estates, that the first sign of the "lawless laws" manifested. 

A group of men stood in a clearing near a communal well, their silhouettes jagged against the twilight. They were white men, their clothing stained with the dust of the roads and the sweat of labor, yet they carried themselves with the predatory ease of those who had been granted the legal right to violence. At the center of the group, a younger man, perhaps no more than twenty, was pinning a black man against the rough bark of a cypress tree. There was no struggle, or at least, none that was permitted. The black man stood with a terrifying, resigned stillness, his eyes fixed on a point somewhere above the white man’s shoulder, as if he were already elsewhere, in a place where hands could not reach him.

The white man was shouting, his voice high and frantic, fueled by a cocktail of cheap ale and the intoxicating certainty of his own immunity. He was accusing the laborer of some perceived slight—a failure to move quickly enough, a look that lasted a second too long, a theft of nothing in particular. Jacob felt the familiar, cold tightening in his chest. He did not look away, for to look away might be seen as a lack of solidarity, but he did not look too closely, lest he be drawn into the orbit of their cruelty. He saw the moment the man’s hand struck—a heavy, swinging blow that sent the laborer’s head snapping to the side—and he saw the others in the group nod, a collective, silent affirmation of the right to strike. 

He spurred Regina forward, his heart drumming a frantic rhythm against his ribs. He could not intervene; he was a man with a note and a satchel, a man with no stake in this particular theatre of misery. To intervene was to invite the "license" that the new laws so freely distributed. He rode past the sound of the man’s heavy breathing and the rhythmic thud of the next blow, focusing instead on the way the light died in the swamps.

By the time he reached the inn—a low-slung structure with a wide, sagging porch—the darkness was nearly absolute. The inn, a place called The Sloop, was the only building that seemed to possess any semblance of warmth, a dim amber glow spilling from its shuttered windows. He dismounted, his muscles aching from the long day of riding, and led Regina toward the stables. The hostler, a man whose face was a map of scars and sun-damage, appeared from the shadows of the stable block. He did not speak, merely took the reins with a gruff nod, his eyes darting momentarily to the heavy leather satchel strapped to Jacob’s side.

Jacob approached the main door of the inn. The wood was damp and soft, yielding slightly under his weight. Inside, the air was thick with the smell of woodsmoke, tallow, and the sour tang of unwashed humanity. A single tallow candle burned on a central trestle table, casting long, dancing shadows across the faces of the few patrons present. They were a motley assembly: a grizzled sailor with a limp, a woman wrapped in a heavy, grease-stained shawl, and a man in the fine, though frayed, linen of a clerk. 

He approached the bar, where a man with a thick, bristly beard was wiping down a pewter mug.

"A room," Jacob said, his voice sounding unnaturally loud in the quiet room. "And a meal, if there is anything left that isn't salt or smoke."

The innkeeper looked him up andably. He saw the dust of the trail, the weary set of the shoulders, and the way Jacob’s hand hovered near the strap of his satchel. "A room is two pence. The meal is a bowl of pottage and a heel of bread. If you want meat, you pay for the privilege of the butcher’s loss."

Jacob produced the coins. The metal felt cold and honest in his palm. "The meal will suffice."

As he sat in a corner, away from the center of the room, he began to eat. The pottage was thick with leeks and perhaps a stray bit of gristle, but it was hot, and that was the primary requirement. He ate slowly, his eyes scanning the room, practicing the prudence that had become his constant companion. He watched the sailor, who was muttering to himself in a language that sounded like the grinding of stones; he watched the woman, whose gaze was fixed on the door, waiting for someone who clearly would not arrive.

Every movement in the room felt weighted with the tension of the era. There was a sense that the peace of the room was a thin veneer, a fragile crust over a boiling pot. The laws of the colony had turned every neighbor into a potential executioner, and every stranger into a potential spy. He thought of the "people’s war" mentioned in the histories—the way the recent violence had not ended the conflict, but had merely codified it into a system of legalized terror. The gentry had won, but they had won by turning the very fabric of society into a weapon.

A sudden commotion at the door broke his reverie. Two men entered, their breathing heavy, their clothes splashed with mud. They were local men, likely laborers from the nearby plantations, and their presence brought with them the scent of the swamp and the palpable energy of recent exertion. They didan’t sit; they stood by the hearth, their eyes searching the room.

"Did you see him?" one of them hissed, his voice low but carrying the sharp edge of panic.

"I saw nothing but the fog," the other replied, his hand gripping the hilt of a hunting knife at his belt. "The patrols are out near the creek. They say they found another one. A messenger, or so the word goes."

The patrons of the inn grew deathly still. The sailor stopped his muttering. The woman’s grip on her shawl tightened. Jacob felt a sudden, sharp prickle of dread. He pulled his satchel closer to his thigh, the leather cool against his leg. He was a man traveling with purpose, and in 1682 Virginia, purpose was a dangerous thing to possess.

"They're looking for anyone who doesn't have a master or a deed," the first man continued, his eyes drifting toward the corner where Jacob sat. "The new law says any man without papers is to be questioned. And anyone who answers wrongly is to be dealt with as a rebel."

Jacob lowered his head, focusing on the last morsel of bread. He tried to make himself part of the furniture, a shadow among shadows. He was just another traveler, another piece of the landscape, drifting through the golden fog and the muddy reality of the coast. But he knew that the fog was lifting, and as it did, the world was becoming far more visible, and far more lethal.

The men at the hearth eventually sat, their presence a heavy, brooding weight in the room. The innkeeper returned to his cleaning, though his movements were now more agitated, the cloth scrubbing the pewter with a frantic, rhythmic screech. The tension in the room began to ebb, replaced by a weary, sullen atmosphere, but the sense of being watched remained.

Jacob finished his meal and stood, the weight of his coins and his secrets pressing upon him. He needed to find a place to sleep where the walls were thick and the windows were barred. He needed to find a way to navigate this landscape of "lawless laws" without becoming another casualty of the order being imposed.

As he climbed the narrow, creaking stairs to his small, cramped room, he passed the woman in the shawl. For a fleeting second, her eyes met his. There was no recognition in them, only a profound, hollowed-out exhaustion, a look that spoke of a soul that had long since stopped hoping for anything more than the end of the day.

In the room, the air was stale and smelled of old hay. He bolted the door and sat on the edge of the thin, lumpy mattress, the darkness of the room pressing in on him. He reached into his satchel and pulled out a small, leather-bound journal and a single, wax-sealed letter. He did not open the letter; he did not need to. The weight of it was enough.

Outside, the sound of the Atlantic surged against the shore, a relentless, rhythmic pounding that seemed to echo the heartbeat of the very land itself. It was a sound of ancient, indifferent power, a sound that had preceded the plantations, the wars, and the laws, and which would surely remain long after the fever of this particular era had broken. But for now, in the stifling heat of the Virginia night, the sound felt like a warning.

He lay back, staring up at the dark rafters. His mind wandered back to the golden fog, to the way the world had looked when it was still a dream, before the mud and the blood and the heavy, suffocating light of the sun had revealed the jagged edges of the truth. He thought of the woman in the street, the man against the tree, and the silent, watching eyes of the mothers. He thought of the way the eyes of the hungry look when they choose.

He closed his eyes, but sleep was a distant country, and the shadows in the corner of the room seemed to pulse with the heavy, rhythmic breathing of the world outside, a world that was waiting, with a terrible, patient hunger, for the next movement in its long, violent dance. He stayed awake, listening to the wind stir the live oaks, and to the distant, unmistakable sound of a horse being led through the mud, its steps heavy and uncertain, approaching the village of Blackwood.
