The ferry groaned, a heavy, metallic sound that seemed to vibrate upward through the soles of Elias’s boots and settle in his marrow. It was a rhythmic, industrial ache, the sound of a massive heart struggling against the pressure of the North Atlantic. Outside the narrow, salt-crusted porthole, the world had dissolved into a singular, bruising shade of indigo. There was no horizon, only the slow, heave-and-fall of the strait, where the black water met a black sky in a seam so fine it was invisible.

Elias sat in the corner of the passenger lounge, a space that smelled of stale coffee, diesel, and the damp, unwashed wool of men who had spent too long at sea. A single fluorescent light flickered overhead, casting a rhythmic, sickly pulse across the linoleum floor. He kept his hands in his pockets, his fingers tracing the jagged edge of a copper coin, a nervous, repetitive motion that kept him anchored to the seat.

He had been on the boat for six hours. He had spent the first three staring at the back of a man’s head in a faded yellow raincoat, and the last three wondering if the man in the heavy shroud was truly his brother.

The call had come three days ago, delivered in a voice that was too professional to be kind. It had been the local constable from the island, a man whose name Elias couldn's quite recall, speaking with a clinical detachment that made the tragedy feel like a clerical error. A body had been found on the skerries, washed up after a storm that had stripped the coast of its dignity. They had a description. They had a vague location. They had a name that had been whispered in the periphery of Caleb’s life for years.

*It might be him,* the constable had said. *The identification needs a family member. Someone who knows the features.*

Elias closed his eyes, and suddenly he was back in the summer of their nineteenth year, the heat of the mainland pressing down on them like a heavy blanket. They had been sitting on the edge of a pier, their legs dangling over the green, murky water, sharing a single, melting orange. Caleb had been talking about leaving, about the way the land felt too small, too much like a cage. Elias had listened, paralyzed by a sudden, irrational fear that if Caleb moved, the gravity of the world would fail. He had stayed, and Caleb had gone, drifting north, then further north, until the distance between them was measured not in miles, but in the silence of unreturned letters and the slow, steady erosion of shared memory.

The ferry shuddered, a violent lurch that sent a tray of empty cups sliding across the laminate counter. The sound broke his reverie. He looked back at the window. The sea was even darker now, a churning void. He wondered if Caleb had died looking at something like this—this vast, indifferent nothingness. He wondered if the last thing Caleb had felt was the cold, or if there had been a moment of warmth, a sudden, sharp clarity, before the water took him.

The engine's thrum changed pitch, a deeper, more strained note. They were approaching the harbor.

The arrival was a blur of mechanical noise and sudden, jarring light. The ferry docked with a heavy, rhythmic thud against the rubber fenders, and the gangway descended with a screech of metal. Elias stood, his legs feeling strangely disconnected from his torso, and joined the small, silent exodus of passengers. The air that hit him as he stepped off the boat was a physical blow—a freezing, salt-laden wind that tasted of iron and ancient ice.

The harbor was a hollowed-out place, a collection of grey stone buildings huddled together against the wind. The lights of the town were dim, struggling against the fog that was beginning to roll in from the strait. There was no welcoming committee, no fanfare. There was only a man in a dark parka standing near the edge of the pier, holding a small, handheld flashlight.

"Elias?" the man called out. His voice was muffled by a thick scarf.

"Yes," Elias said, his own voice sounding thin and strange to his ears.

The man nodded, a brief, jerky movement. "The station is open. We should move. The wind is picking up."

The drive to the clinic was a descent into a landscape of shadows. The road wound upward through steep, jagged cliffs, the headlights of the small police cruiser cutting narrow, desperate paths through the mist. Elias watched the silhouettes of the rocks pass by, massive and inscrutable, like sleeping beasts. Every movement of the car felt like an intrusion upon the stillness of the island.

The clinic was a low-slung building of concrete and glass, perched precariously on the edge of a slope. Inside, the air was heated but lacked any sense of comfort; it was a dry, sterile warmth that seemed to bypass the skin and go straight to the lungs. The constable—the man from the phone call—was waiting in a small, brightly lit office. He looked older than he had on the phone, his face etched with the fatigue of a man who spent his life documenting the ends of things.

"The coroner has prepared the room," the constable said, not offering a hand, but gesturing toward a heavy door at the end of a corridor. "It's... it's not much, but it's private."

Elias walked down the corridor. The linoleum was white and blinding under the overhead lights. His footsteps sounded like gunshots in the silence. He felt a profound sense of vertigo, as if the floor were tilting, as if he were still on the ferry, caught in that endless, oscillating motion between the mainland and the unknown.

He reached the door. He hesitated, his hand hovering inches from the cold metal handle. He thought of the orange they had shared on the pier. He thought of the way Caleb used to laugh—a sudden, loud sound that always seemed to catch Elias by surprise. If he opened this door, that memory would be finished. It would be overwritten by something heavy and permanent.

He pushed the door open.

The room was small, dominated by a stainless-steel table that caught the harsh light. The air was colder here, smelling of antiseptic and something else—something faint, metallic, and undeniably organic. The body was covered by a white sheet, the fabric draped in heavy, ungraceful folds.

Elias approached the table. His heart was a frantic, trapped thing in his chest. He felt a strange, detached sense of observation, as if he were watching someone else move through the room. He reached out, his fingers trembling, and grasped the edge of the sheet.

He pulled it back.

The face was pale, the skin waxy and translucent, like the underside of a leaf. The eyes were closed, the lashes matted with salt. For a moment, there was nothing to recognize. It was just a face, a piece of anatomy, a vessel emptied of its occupant.

Then, he saw the scar.

It was a small, jagged line, almost subcutaneous, running through the left eyebrow. It was a relic of a childhood accident, a moment when they had been climbing the old oak tree behind their father’s house, and a branch had snapped, slicing into the boy who refused to come down. Elias had been the one to bandage it, his small hands shaking, his eyes wide with a terror he couldn't name.

He breathed in, a sharp, jagged inhalation that tasted of the room's sterile air.

He looked closer. The structure of the jaw, the way the philtrum dipped—it was him. The bone beneath the skin was the same. The identity was there, written in the architecture of the bone.

But as he stared, a profound, hollow doubt began to seep into him. He looked at the hands, which were resting stiffly at the sides of the body. They were thick-fingered, the nails broken and stained with something dark. They were not the hands he remembered. The hands he remembered were restless, always moving, always grasping at the world. These hands were still. They were parted from the person they had belonged to.

He realized then that the man in the room was not his brother. The man in the room was merely the evidence of a death. The brother—the Caleb who had drifted, the Caleb who had been a promise and a resentment and a memory—was something else entirely. That person had died long before the sea had claimed this body.

Elias let the sheet fall. The sound of the fabric hitting the skin was a soft, final thud.

He stood there in the clinical light, the silence of the clinic pressing in on him. He could stay and confirm the identity, providing the legal closure the constable required. He could sign the papers, and the process of burial and bureaucracy would begin. He could claim the death, and in doing so, claim the end of his own connection to the North.

Instead, he turned away. He walked back down the long, white corridor, past the empty office, and out into the night.

The wind had grown even more violent. The fog had swallowed the clinic, leaving him alone in a world of grey, swirling nothingness. He walked toward the edge of the cliff, where the land simply ceased to be, and looked out toward the strait.

Somewhere out there, in the black, undulating mass of the water, the ferry was returning. The engine’s vibration would be felt again, a distant, rhythmic ache. He thought of the man in the yellow raincoat, and the way the sea takes everything, leaving only the salt and the cold. He thought of the scar, and how it remained even when the person was gone.

He stood there for a long time, a single, stationary figure against the moving dark, waiting for a tide that would never bring anything back.
