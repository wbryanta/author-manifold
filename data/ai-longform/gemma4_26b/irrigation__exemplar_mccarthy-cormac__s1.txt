The sun rose over the ridge in a wash of pale and caustic light and the heat arrived before the shadows had even retreated from the foot of the mountains. It was a dry heat that seemed to emanate from the earth itself. Elias stood by the edge of the orchard where the main line ran, a heavy vein of rusted iron that bled a slow and rhythmic pulse of silted water into the dust. The ground was parched and the cracked earth clutched at the leaks as if trying to swallow the very thing it lacked. He watched the water seep and darken the soil and he thought of the years that had passed since the water had flowed with any true purpose.

Caleb came up the rise carrying the heavy wrench and a length of new coupling. He did not speak. He dropped the tools in the dirt and wiped his brow with a forearm stained by the grease of the machinery. He looked at the weeping pipe and then he looked at his brother.

The line is gone, Elias said.

It's just a leak, Caleb said.

It is a failure of the whole, Elias said.

Caleb knelt in the dirt and began to clear the silt from the circumference of the iron. The sun beat down upon them with a weight that was almost physical. The peach trees stood in the distance, their limbs gnarled and grey and the fruit small and bitter and laced with the blight of a long drought. They were ghosts of a harvest. 

He's coming back on Tuesday, Caleb said.

Who?

The man from the valley. The one with the papers.

Elias watched a beetle struggle across a dry leaf. The man is looking at the acreage, not the fruit.

He's looking at the water rights, Caleb said. And he's looking at the way the house is leaning.

The house stands where it stands, Elias said.

It's leaning toward the ground, Caleb said. Like everything else here.

They worked in silence for a time. The sound of the wrench against the iron was a sharp and discordant note in the vast stillness of the afternoon. The heat deepened. The air grew thick with the smell of hot dust and the scent of decaying organic matter from the fallen fruit. Elias felt the ache in his lower back, a dull and rhythmic throb that seemed to synchronize with the pulsing of the broken line. 

You could take the offer, Caleb said. He did not look up from his work. We could go north. To where the rain actually falls.

The land does not move, Elias said.

The land is dying, Elias. Look at the trees. They are nothing but kindling.

They are the inheritance, Elias said.

They are a debt, Caleb said. A debt we are paying in sweat and in nothing.

The younger brother pulled the coupling into place and tightened the bolts with a frantic and mechanical energy. His hands were scarred and callotous and the skin of his neck was burned a raw and angry red. He worked as if the act of repair could somehow mend the larger fracture in their lives.

The man says the developers want to run a substation through the lower grove, Caleb said. They'd pay enough to clear the mortgage and then some. We could leave the rest to the scrub.

Let the scrub have it, Elias said. But not the grove.

You are a fool. You are dying in a graveyard of your own making.

Elias stood and walked to the edge of the trench. The water had begun to pool in the hollow of the excavation, a dark and muddy eye reflecting the white sun. He looked out over the orchard. The trees were stunted and the leaves were curled like the fingers of the dead. He thought of his father standing in this same light, years ago, when the water had been a gift and not a struggle. He thought of the way the world had once seemed to promise a continuity that was now revealed to be a mere illusion of the light.

There is a dignity in the stewardship, Elias said.

There is no dignity in starvation, Caleb said.

The wrench clattered against a stone. Caleb sat back on his heels and stared at his brother. His eyes were bright with a sudden and terrible frustration.

I am not going to watch you rot here, Elias. I am not going to stand in this dust and watch you pretend that the world hasn't changed. The world is gone. There is only what is left.

The world is what we make of it, Elias said.

Then you are making nothing.

The afternoon progressed with a slow and agonizing lethargy. The shadows lengthened, stretching out across the orchard like dark fingers reaching for the mountains. The temperature did not drop so much as it grew heavy, a humid and suffocating shroud. They continued the repair, the iron pipe eventually yielding to the new coupling, the leak ceasing with a sudden and hollow sound. The water began to flow again, a steady and unceasing stream that carved new channels in the thirsty earth.

They sat by the edge of the trench and shared a canteen of tepid water. The liquid was metallic and tasted of the plastic and the sun.

He is coming Tuesday, Caleb said. He said he would bring the surveyor.

The surveyor will find nothing worth measuring, Elias said.

He will find a map, Caleb said. A map of what could be.

Elias looked at his hands. They were caked in the red mud of the orchard. He felt as though he were part of the earth itself, a vestigative part of a biological process that had long since lost its meaning. 

The trees will not survive another summer, Caleb said.

Then we will see them through the summer.

And if they do not?

Then we will have seen them through.

Caleb shook his head and wiped his face. He looked toward the mountains, where the peaks were turning a bruised and purple hue in the twilight. 

I cannot stay here, Elias. I cannot.

I know, Elias said.

You could come with me.

There is no 'with me,' Elias said. There is only here.

The sun dipped below the horizon and the light failed. The orchard disappeared into the grey and the dusk, leaving only the sound of the water flowing through the iron pipes, a subterranean murmur that seemed to echo from the very core of the world. The two brothers sat in the encroaching dark, two solitary figures amidst the dying trees, bound together by a lineage of struggle and an inheritance of dust, while above them the first stars began to pierce the void, cold and indifferent and ancient.
