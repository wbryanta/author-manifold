The ferry moved through the dark like a needle through heavy, wet fabric, stitching the black expanse of the strait to the bruised underside of the sky. There was no horizon, only a blurring of textures where the cold salt of the water met the biting humidity of the fog. Inside the cabin, the light was a jaundiced, flickering thing, casting long, uncertain shadows that seemed to detach themselves from the passengers and drift toward the corners of the room. Elias sat by the window, his forehead pressed against the glass, feeling the vibration of the engine through his skull. It was a low, rhythmic thrum, a mechanical heartbeat that felt less like propulsion and more like a persistent, dull ache.

He was traveling through a geography of ghosts. To his left, a man in a heavy wool coat slept with his mouth slightly open, his breath fogging the air in short, rhythmic plumes. To his right, the empty seat held only the memory of a weight. Elias thought of the letter, the brief, clinical notification that had arrived in a plain envelope, stripped of all sentiment, bearing only a case number and a location. It was a piece of paper that had acted as a puncture wound, letting the coldness of the north leak into his quiet, southern life.

He tried to remember the last time he had seen Julian. The memory was fragmented, a collection of disparate images that refused to coalesce into a coherent narrative. There was the glint of sunlight on a broken beer bottle in a summer driveway; the way Julian’s shoulders had looked, hunched and defensive, under a frayed denim jacket; the specific, jagged silence that had stretched between them for seven years, a silence so dense it had become a physical object, something they both carried but never acknowledged. They had been brothers, once, part of a single, breathing entity, but time had acted like the tide in this strait, slowly eroding the shoreline of their shared history until there was nothing left but salt and stone.

The ferry groaned, a sound of metal protesting against the unseen weight of the waves. Elias watched a single droplet of condensation track its way down the windowpane, a slow, translucent path through the grime. He wondered if the body in the morgue was similarly marked by the elements, if the cold had already begun to etch its own map onto the skin, a cartography of frost and stillness. He thought of the way Julian used to map things—the cracks in the sidewalk, the constellations in the summer sky, the precise architecture of a lie.

The passenger across from him stirred, a rustle of synthetic fabric that sounded like dead leaves skittering across pavement. A woman, her face obscured by a heavy scarf, looked up for a moment. Her eyes were dark, reflective, catching the dim overhead light like the surface of a stagnant pool. For a second, Elias felt a terrifying surge of recognition, as if she were a stranger he had known in a previous, more luminous life. But then she looked away, returning to the book in her lap, and the moment dissolved, leaving him more isolated than before.

The strait was a place of transitions, a liminal zone where the identity of the water changed from the open sea to the narrow, claustrophobic channel. He felt himself undergoing a similar transition. He was no longer the man who lived in the sunlight of the city, with his predictable routines and his carefully curated silences. He was becoming something else, a man defined by a search, a man moving toward a confrontation with a stillness.

He closed his eyes and the engine's thrum became a voice. It whispered of the things lost to the water: the rusted anchors, the sunken timber, the forgotten names of ships. He imagined the sea as a vast, unreadable ledger, where every death was recorded in a language of currents and silt. If the body was Julian, then the ledger was being closed. If it was not, then the ledger remained open, an agonizingly infinite loop of possibility and dread.

A sudden lurch of the vessel sent a wave of nausea through him. The ferry had hit a swell, a sudden, violent upheaval of the dark mass beneath them. The lights flickered and died for a heartbeat, plunging the cabin into a true, absolute blackness. In that second, Elias felt the boundaries of his own body dissolve. He was the engine, the fog, the salt, the cold. He was the heavy, unmoored weight of the ship. He was the unnamed shape waiting in the morgue, a hollow space waiting to be filled with a name.

Then the lights surged back, a weak, yellow glow that felt intrusive, like a sudden, unwelcome light in a darkened room. The man in the wool coat was still sleeping. The woman was still reading. The world had returned to its precarious, trembling equilibrium.

He thought of the house they had grown up in, the way the light used to pool in the hallway during the late afternoon, turning the dust motified air into something golden and sacred. There had been a period, before the estrangement, when they had been able to sit in that light and speak of nothing, their presence a shared, easy breath. Now, that house felt like a ruin, a place inhabited only by the echoes of the people they had been. He realized then that he wasn't just traveling to identify a body; he was traveling to see if the version of himself that existed in Julian's presence was also dead.

The ferry began to slow. The engine’s roar descended into a low, laborious grind. The mist seemed to thicken, pressing against the windows, turning the world into a white, featureless void. The dock would be coming soon, a jagged, skeletal structure emerging from the nothingness.

He stood up, his legs feeling heavy and disconnected, as if he were walking through deep water. He gathered his small bag, the weight of it a reminder of his singular, lonely purpose. As he moved toward the exit, the air in the cabin felt thinner, stripped of its warmth. He stepped out onto the deck, and the cold hit him with the force of a physical blow. It was a sharp, crystalline cold that seemed to penetrate the very bone.

The wind was a low, mournable sound, whistling through the railings and the rigging of unseen boats in the harbor. The sea was a churn of black and grey, heavy with the scent of decay and ancient, frozen things. He stood at the edge, looking out into the fog, where the boundary between the ship and the abyss had vanished. He waited for the sight of the land, for the first sign of the shore, terrified that the moment the earth appeared, the truth would become something he could no longer flee. He stood there, a solitary figure in the dark, a man poised on the edge of a discovery that promised nothing but the completion of a loss.
