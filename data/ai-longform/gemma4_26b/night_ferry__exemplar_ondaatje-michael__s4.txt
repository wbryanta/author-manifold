The ferry moved through the dark like a needle through heavy silk, stitching the black water to the bruised sky. Inside the cabin, the air was thick with the smell of burnt coffee and the damp, metallic scent of salt-spray clinging to the coats of the few remaining passengers. Elias sat by the window, his forehead pressed against the cold pane, watching the way the light from the ship’s prow cut a temporary, violent path through the fog. The vibration of the engine traveled up through the glass, into his bone, a low-frequency thrumming that seemed to be trying to shake loose the memories he had kept tightly packed in the dark.

He thought of the strait not as a body of water, or even a boundary, but as a place where things went to be forgotten. A vast, churning throat of grey and iron. It was the kind of place that could swallow a person and leave nothing but a ripple, a momentary disturbance in the surface tension, as if the sea had simply closed its mouth.

He had received the call in the middle of a Tuesday, a day of unremarkable light and the mundane sounds of traffic. The voice on the other end had been professional, stripped of all inflection, speaking of a discovery near the shoreline, of a body recovered from the tidal flats, of a possible identification. There had been no name mentioned, only a set of descriptors that felt like a map of a life he had tried to unlearn. A man, mid-thirties, no identification found, a distinctive scar near the hairline, a certain way of carrying the shoulders.

Elias closed his eyes, and for a moment, the ferry was not a ship but a ghost, drifting through a landscape of broken pieces. He saw Julian. Not the Julian of the last few years, the one defined by silence and the long, jagged gaps in their correspondence, but the Julian of the summer when they had lived by the edge of the inland lakes. He saw him standing in the shallows, the water swirling around his ankles, his skin bronzed and salt-crusted, looking as though he were part of the earth itself. They had been inseparable then, two halves of a single, wandering thought. They had shared everything: the secrets of the woods, the maps of the stars, the heavy, unspoken promises of youth. 

Then came the years of the slow divergence, the way a river bends away from its original course, so gradually that you do not realize you are in a different country until the landscape has entirely changed. Julian had moved north, into the harsher, more singular geography of the coast, and Elias had remained in the city, caught in the grid of streets and the relentless, predictable rhythm of the seasons. The distance between them had grown not in miles, but in the density of the silences they exchanged.

The ferry lurched, a heavy shuddering as it hit a swell, and the lights in the cabin flickered. A woman at a nearby table, wrapped in a thick, woollen shawl, looked up, her eyes catching the light for a second, a momentary flash of recognition or perhaps just fatigue. She turned back to her book, her movements slow and rhythmic, like the turning of a tide.

Elias looked back at the window. The fog had thickened, erasing the horizon, leaving the ship suspended in a void. He found himself thinking of the anatomy of loss. How a person is not lost all at once, but in increments—a forgotten birthday, a missed phone call, a letter left unread in a drawer. The body, when it is found, is merely the final, physical punctuation of a long, vanishing sentence.

He remembered a day, years ago, when they had walked along the cliffs of the old estate. The wind had been fierce, tearing at their jackets, and Julian had pointed down at the churning surf, his face alight with a strange, terrifying kind of joy. *Everything eventually returns to the weight of the water,* he had said, his voice barely audible over the roar of the wind. At the time, Elias had thought it was merely the dramatic flair of a man who spent too much time reading poetry. Now, sitting in the vibration of the engine, the words felt like a physical weight, a stone placed in his palm.

The ferry's intercom crackled, a burst of static that sounded like breaking bone. An announcement, muffled and distorted, spoke of an approaching berth and the need for passengers to prepare for disembarkation. The time was approaching. The transition from the moving, liquid world to the fixed, terrestrial one was imminent.

He stood up, his limbs feeling heavy, as if the sea itself were beginning to seep into his veins. He gathered his small bag, the nylon strap digging into his shoulder. As he moved through the narrow corridor, he passed a man sleeping in a corner, his head tilted at an awkward angle, his breath a soft, rhythmic rasp in the quiet of the cabin. He looked like a piece of driftwood, exhausted and shaped by the currents, left behind by a receding tide.

Stepping onto the deck, the cold hit him with the force of a blow. It was a damp, biting cold that seeped through his layers, settling in the marrow. The air was thick with the smell of diesel and decaying kelp. The ferry was slowing now, the great engines groaning as they reversed, the sound a deep, guttural moan that echoed across the empty water.

The pier began to emerge from the fog, a skeletal structure of wood and rusted iron, appearing like a phantom limb. The lights of the small port town were dim, blurred into amber smudges by the mist. Everything was obscured, softened, as if the world were being viewed through a veil of gauze.

He thought of the body. He thought of the stillness of it, the way it would be lying under a white sheet, stripped of its history, reduced to the geometry of bone and flesh. He wondered if there would be any part of Julian left in that shape—a certain tension in the jaw, the particular slope of the brow, or if the sea had already begun the work of erasure, smoothing out the features until the man was nothing more than a stone, a piece of the landscape.

The ship bumped against the fender, a dull, heavy thud that resonated through the deck. The gangplank was lowered, a narrow, swaying bridge connecting the moving world to the stationary one. 

As he walked down the ramp, the sound of his own footsteps on the metal was unnervingly loud, a sharp, solitary percussion. The pier was deserted, save for a single figure standing near the edge of the dock, a silhouette draped in a heavy raincoat, watching the ship approach. The figure did not move, did as if they were part of the pier itself, a stationary sentinel in the grey light.

Elias reached the end of the pier and paused. The ground felt unnervingly stable, too solid, as if the earth were pretending to be something it wasn't. He looked toward the town, where the lights flickered behind a curtain of rain. Somewhere in that dark, damp expanse, in a room with sterile walls and the smell of antiseptic, the identification would take place.

He began to walk toward the town, his movements heavy and uncertain. The street was paved with uneven cobblestones, slick with moisture, and as he passed under the dim glow of a streetlamp, he saw his own shadow stretching out before him, long and distorted, a dark shape that seemed to be leading him toward an inevitable encounter.

He thought of the way they used to navigate the woods as children, using the moss on the trees and the direction of the sun to find their way back to the house. They had always known the way home. But now, there was no way back, only the forward movement into the fog, into the uncertainty of the light, into the possibility of seeing a face and finding only an absence where a brother should be.

The rain began to fall in earnest, a fine, stinging mist that blurred the edges of the world. He felt the water seeping into his collar, a cold, persistent intrusion. He turned a corner and saw the lights of the infirmary, a bright, clinical glare that cut through the gloom. He stopped, his breath hitching in the cold air. He stood there, a solitary figure in the middle of the empty street, waiting for the moment when the memory and the reality would collide, when the person he had lost would finally, irrevocably, be found. 

He thought of the river and the road, as he had seen in a book once, how they could merge and diverge, how a life can be lived as a tale told backwards, where the end is merely a return to a beginning that has been forgotten. He stood in the rain, and for a moment, he was neither the man on the ferry nor the man in the town, but merely a part of the landscape, a shadow moving through the mist, a fragment of a story that was still, painfully, being written.
