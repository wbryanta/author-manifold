The sun rose over the parched rim of the canyon and the heat came with it, a dry and scouring heat that seemed to leach the very marrow from the orchard trees. The peach trees stood like graying specters in the dust, their limbs gnarled and brittle, the bark peeling in strips like dead skin. There was no green left in the valley, only the faded ochre of the dying grass and the white, calcined bone of the earth where the irrigation had failed. 

Elias sat in the dirt beside the trench and held the heavy iron wrench against a rusted coupling. His hands were mapped with fine cracks of dried mud and his skin was the color of cured leather. He tightened the bolt and the metal groaned a low and grinding protest. Beside him, Caleb leaned on a spade, his shadow short and dark against the furrowed ground. He watched the slow trickle of water escaping from a hairline fracture in the PVC pipe. The water did not flow so much as it wept, a meager and pathetic loss to the thirsty soil.

It’s no use, Elias said.

Caleb wiped the sweat from his eyes with the back of a gloved hand. He looked up at the ridge where the heat shimmered in waves above the scrub oak. We just gotta patch the leak. Once the pressure holds, the whole line will take.

The pressure won't hold, Elias said. The ground is too far gone. The roots are desiccated. You can see it in the leaves. They’ve gone brittle.

He was looking at a peach tree near the edge of the trench. Its canopy was a skeletal lace against the bright, unforging sky. 

We paid for the new valves, Caleb said. We paid for the sealant. It’s worth the effort.

The effort is a vanity, Elias said. He let go of the wrench and sat back on his haunches. He looked at his brother. The man is coming from Austin on Tuesday.

Caleb did not move. He stayed leaning on the spade, his eyes fixed on the damp patch of earth where the water was pooling. The man is a surveyor, Elias. He’s just looking at the acreage.

He’s looking at the value, Elias said. He’s looking at the water rights. He’s looking at what the land is worth when the trees are dead and the orchard is nothing but a graveyard of wood.

Caleb spat into the dust. The spit disappeared instantly into the parched grit. It’s our land.

It was our father’s land, Elias said. Now it is only a debt.

A silence fell between them, heavy and hot as the air. The only sound was the rhythmic, distant clatter of a cicada in a nearby thicket and the slow, rhythmic drip of the water hitting the floor of the trench. The sun climbed higher, an impartial and blinding eye.

There is a buyer for the north pasture, Elias continued. He spoke with a flat, toneless cadence. The rancher from over the canyon. He wants the riparian strip. He wants to extend his grazing.

Caleb pushed the spade into the earth with a sudden, violent motion. He turned and faced his brother. His face was flushed, the veins in his temples pulsing. You talk like a man who’s already moved out. Like you’ve already packed the house.

I am talking like a man who sees the end of a thing, Elias said. There is no shame in recognizing when the season has passed. The fruit is gone. The water is gone. There is only the dust and the heat and the ledger.

The orchard has been in this family for four generations, Caleb said. His voice was low, vibrating with a restrained fury. You don've got the right to just decide the lineage ends with us.

The lineage ended when the aquifer dropped ten feet, Elias said. It ended when the blight took the blossoms in May. We are just the ghosts left to haunt the ruins.

He reached into the trench and picked up a handful of the loose, sandy soil. He let it sift through his fingers. It fell in a fine, silent stream, settling into the hollows of the earth.

The man from Austin, he has a proposal, Elias said. He wants the whole parcel. He’d pay enough to clear the liens on the house. Enough to get you settled in San Marcos. Somewhere with actual shade.

San Marcos, Caleb said. He let out a short, bitter laugh. You think I want to live in a place where I don't know the smell of the rain? You think I want to sit in a room and listen to the traffic and pretend I’m not mourning?

You aren't mourning, Elias said. You are clinging. There is a difference.

Caleb turned back to the pipe. He knelt in the trench, his movements jerky and uncoordinated. He began to scrape at the mud around the leaking coupling, his fingernails digging into the grit. The water continued its slow, rhythmic escape.

I can fix this, Caleb said. I’ll dig deeper. I’ve got the epoxy.

You are patching a corpse, Elias said.

He watched his brother work. He watched the way Caleb’s shoulders hunched, the way he labored with a desperate, misplaced industry. It was a frantic sort of piety, a refusal to witness the truth of the landscape. The heat was now a physical weight, pressing down on the valley, flattening the light, turning the world into a monochrome of ash and amber.

The shade of the great pecan tree at the edge of the yard cast a long, distorted shadow toward the house. The house was a white-washed structure, its paint peeling in long, sun-bleached flakes. The porch sagged on one side, and the windows were dark and empty, like the eyes of an animal that had died in the night.

The trees are dying, Caleb said, not looking up.

They are dead, Elias said. They are merely still standing.

They can be revived, Caleb said. If we can get the irrigation steady. If we can bypass the blockage in the main.

The blockage is the drought, Elias said. The drought is the era. You cannot bypass the era, Caleb. You cannot outrun the sun.

Caleb stopped digging. He sat back in the mud, his clothes soaked with a mixture of sweat and the weeping water. He looked at his hands. They were caked in the dark, wet silt of the trench. He looked at his brother, and for a moment, the anger had drained from his face, leaving only a hollow, exhausted vacancy.

What am I supposed to do, Elias? he asked.

To live, Elias said. To move. To find a place where the water still flows and the earth still yields. To not let this place pull you down into the dirt with it.

I don't know how to be anything else, Caleb said.

The sun began its slow descent toward the western mountains, turning the sky a bruised and heavy purple. The heat did not dissipate; it merely settled, becoming a dense and suffocating presence. The shadows of the dead peach trees lengthened across the orchard, stretching out like grasping fingers toward the house.

Elias stood up and brushed the dust from his trousers. He looked toward the road that led out of the valley, a long and shimmering ribbon of heat that disappeared into the haze of the distance.

The man will be here Tuesday, Elias said. I have already told him we would be available to discuss the terms.

Caleb did not answer. He sat in the trench, a small and broken figure amidst the ruins of the irrigation line. The water continued to leak, a slow and rhythmic pulse in the darkening earth, marking the time of a world that was passing away.

They sat in the gathering gloom as the first stars began to prick through the canopy of the sky, cold and distant and indifferent to the struggles of men or the death of orchards. The wind rose from the canyon, a dry and hollow sound that moved through the leafless branches of the trees with a sound like breathing. In the distance, the mountains were black silhouettes against a sky of deepening indigo, ancient and immovable, watching the slow and inevitable dissolution of the valley below. Elias watched the road. Caleb watched the water. The earth waited for them both.
