The vibration of the engine was not a sound so much as a structural truth, a low-frequency hum that seemed to originate within the marrow of Elias’s own bones. It lived in the soles of his boots and traveled up his spine, a relentless, rhythmic pulse that mediated his existence between the heavy, dark weight of the North Sea and the salt-crusted steel of the ferry’s deck. The *Nordlys* moved through the strait with a slow, grinding momentum, a hulking shape carving a path through water so black it appeared viscous, like oil. There was no moon tonight, only the rhythmic, sweeping finger of the lighthouse beam that periodically cut through the mist, illuminating the churning wake for a fraction of a second before swallowing the world back into a suffocating, monochromatic gloom.

Elias sat in the corner of the passenger lounge, a space designed for transit and nothing else. The furniture was molded plastic, bolted to the floor, colored a faded, institutional teal that looked sickly under the dim, flickering fluorescent lights. He held a paper cup of coffee, long since gone cold, the surface of the liquid reflecting nothing but the overhead glare. He hadn't slept. Sleep required a surrender to the motion of the ship, a loosening of the muscles that he could not afford. To sleep was to let the possibility drift into the realm of certainty, and he was still clinging, with a desperate, clawing tenacity, to the possibility that the phone call had been a mistake.

The call had come at four in the afternoon, a voice thin and distorted by the wind of a coastal terminal, speaking of a recovery near the skerries, of a body pulled from the swell, of a jacket that matched a description, of a name that had been whispered with a terrifying, hesitant gravity. *Julian.* The name had felt like a stone dropped into a well—no splash, just a long, receding silence that seemed to hollow out the room around him.

He stared at the window, though there was nothing to see but his own reflection, a pale, translucent ghost superimposed over the void. He looked older than his thirty-four years. The shadows under his eyes were deep, etched by a week of frantic, undirected movement through cities and train stations, a migration driven by a singular, agonizing purpose. He was traveling toward a morgue, toward a cold room with stainless steel tables and the smell of antiseptic, to look at a face that might be a stranger’s, or might be the face of the person he had spent the last five years trying to forget.

The estrangement had not been a sudden fracture, but a slow, tectonic shift. There had been no climactic argument, no slamming of doors. Instead, there had been a gradual thinning of the air between them, a series of unreturned texts, a silence that grew more comfortable with each passing season until it became a permanent fixture of their relationship. Julian had always been a man of the periphery, someone who drifted toward the edges of things, toward the harder, colder landscapes where the social contract felt optional. He had chased ghosts in the fishing villages and seasonal labor, seeking a kind of asceticism that Elias, settled in his quiet life of spreadsheets and predictable rhythms, could never understand. And yet, the silence had been a choice Elias had participated in. He had let the distance become a canyon.

A heavy door groaned open behind him, admitting a gust of frigid, salt-laden air and the muffled sound of boots on linoleum. A group of fishermen, thick-necked and smelling of diesel and old brine, filed into the lounge, bringing with them the clamor of the world. They spoke in a dialect that was thick and guttural, their laughter jarring against the rhythmic thrum of the ship. Elias tried to shrink, to become part of the teal plastic and the dim light, but he felt hyper-exposed, as if the grief he was carrying was a bright, neon signal.

He thought of Julian’s hands. They had been scarred and calloused, the fingernails perpetually stained with the dark earth of whatever work he was currently undertaking. He remembered them gripping the edge of a pier, or perhaps holding a glass of cheap whiskey in a dim tavern, the movement restless and kinetic. If the body was Julian, those hands would be still. That was the thought that been gnawing at him since the call: the cessation of movement. The idea of Julian, who was all momentum and evasion, rendered static.

The ferry hit a swell, a heavy, rhythmic heave that sent a shudder through the deck. The coffee in his cup sloshed against the rim, a dark stain spreading across the paper. Elias watched the droplet, a tiny, encroaching darkness. He wondered if identity was much like that stain—something that starts small, a single, localized event, and then slowly, inexorably, obscures everything else.

He closed his eyes, and suddenly he was ten years old, standing on the edge of a frozen lake, the ice groaning beneath his weight. Julian had been ahead of him, running, his bright red jacket a defiant spark against the white expanse. *Don’mic be afraid, El,* he had shouted, his voice clear and arrogant with the invincibility of youth. *The ice is thick enough. I checked.* Then came the sound—not a crack, but a dull, heavy thud, the sound of something breaking deep within the earth. He remembered the terror of watching his brother disappear into the milky, obscured depths, the way the water had swallowed the red jacket, leaving only a widening circle of bubbles. He had stood there, paralyzed, until the adults arrived, until the world became a frantic blur of heavy coats and shouting. He had never told anyone that he had seen the ice fracture. He had kept that secret, a small, frozen knot in his chest, for twenty-four years.

The intercom crackled, a burst of static that made him flinch. A distorted voice announced the approaching docking at the northern terminal, a sequence of syllables that signaled the end of the transit, the end of the liminal space. The ferry began to slow. The heavy, grinding rhythm changed, shifting from a steady pulse to a labored, decelerating heave.

He stood up, his legs feeling strangely disconnected from his torso, as if he were walking through deep water. He moved toward the deck, needing the air, needing to feel the sting of the cold on his skin to prove he was still inhabitant of his own body.

The wind on the deck was violent, a physical force that pushed against his chest. The spray from the bow was freezing, crystalline needles that stung his cheeks. The lights of the terminal were beginning to emerge from the fog, a cluster of amber and sickly white, huddled together against the dark. The harbor was a skeletal landscape of cranes and shipping containers, stripped of color and warmth.

As the ship drifted closer to the pier, the scale of the landscape became apparent. This was a place of industry and survival, a jagged coastline where the land seemed to be actively struggling against the encroachment of the sea. It was a hard place to live, and a hard place to die.

He leaned against the railing, the cold metal biting through his gloves. He thought about the autopsy report, the vague, inconclusive details provided by the local constable. *Found near the rocks. Clothing consistent with local maritime wear. Identification pending.* The ambiguity was a torture. If the body was not Julian, then the tragedy was merely a tragedy—a nameless casualty of the sea, a statistic in the ledger of the North. But if it was him, then the tragedy was personal. It was an indictment. It of a life unlived, of a bridge burned and never rebuilt.

He saw a figure standing on the pier, a lone silhouette beneath a streetlamp, waiting. The person was motionless, a dark smudge against the industrial grey. He wondered if that was the person who would meet him, the one tasked with the grim ceremony of recognition. The thought sent a wave of nausea through him. He felt a sudden, irrational urge to turn around, to jump from the moving ferry and let the dark water take him, to vanish into the same obscurity that threatened to consume his brother.

But the ferry was committed to its path. The momentum of the ship, like the momentum of his own life, was too great to arrest. He was being propelled toward the truth, whether he was prepared for it or not.

The gangplank descended with a heavy, metallic clang, a sound that echoed through the hollow spaces of the pier. The engines were down to a low, idling growl. The ferry groaned as it nudched the fenders, the friction of rubber and steel a harsh, grating protest.

He walked down the ramp, his footsteps loud and hollow on the metal. The air in the terminal was heavy with the smell of wet concrete and diesel. He felt as though he were moving through a dream, a sequence of events that had been preordained, a script written in the cold, unyielding language of fate.

The terminal was nearly empty. A single clerk sat behind a plexiglass barrier, her face obscured by the glare of a computer screen. He approached the desk, his heart hammering a frantic, uneven rhythm against his ribs.

"I'm looking for the station sergeant," Elias said, his voice sounding strange to his own ears, thin and brittle. "I'm here regarding the... the incident at the skerries."

The clerk looked up, her eyes tired, reflecting the fluorescent light. She stared at him for a long moment, her gaze scanning his face, searching for something—recognition, perhaps, or some sign of the grief she knew was coming.

"The morgue is closed for the night," she said, her voice flat, devoid of any empathy. "You'll have to wait until morning. There's a small inn across the street. They have rooms."

"I... I understand," Elias said. He felt a strange, hollowed-out sensation, a lightness in his chest that was almost more terrifying than the weight of the dread. He had been denied the confrontation. The night had been extended, the period of uncertainty stretched out into a grueling, sleepless vigil.

He walked out of the terminal and into the night. The inn was a low, squat building, its windows dim and yellowed. The street was slick with rain, reflecting the amber lights of the harbor like spilled oil. Everything was silent, save for the distant, rhythmic thud of the ferry's engines and the mournful cry of a single gull.

He checked into his room, a cramped, utilitarian space that smelled of stale tobacco and laundry detergent. He sat on the edge of the bed, the springs creaking under his weight. He didn't turn on the light. He sat in the dark, listening to the sound of the sea, the relentless, indifferent pulse of the water against the shore.

He thought of the red jacket. He thought of the way the water had swallowed it. He thought of the silence that had grown between him and Julian, a silence that was now being filled by the weight of this approaching, nameless truth.

He realized then that it didn't matter if the body was Julian. The person he had known—the vibrant, reckless, infuriating boy on the ice—was already gone. That Julian had died long ago, lost in the slow, quiet erosion of their shared history. The man in the morgue, if it was him, would merely be the physical remains of a ghost. The true tragedy was not the death of the body, but the death of the possibility.

He leaned back against the thin, lumpy pillow, his eyes fixed on the shadows on the ceiling. The darkness of the northern night pressed against the window, heavy and absolute. He waited for the morning, for the light to reveal what the night had hidden, knowing that whatever he found, he would be carrying it with him, a permanent, cold weight, for the rest of his life. The engine of the ferry, far off in the harbor, continued its low, rhythmic thrum, a constant, unyielding heartbeat in the void.
