The ferry groaned, a heavy, metallic protest against the swell of the Skagerrak. It was a rhythmic, industrial sound, a shuddering vibration that Elias felt in the soles of his boots and up through the marrow of his shins. The North Sea at three in the morning was not a place of movement so much as a place of weight, a vast, undulating pressure that seemed intent on flattening the steel hull of the *Sølvgås*.

Elias stood on the observation deck, though there was nothing to observe but the dark. The fog had rolled in somewhere between the mainland and the islands, a thick, suffocating shroud that erased the horizon, leaving him suspended in a grey void. The air tasted of salt, diesel, and the ancient, cold rot of deep water. He pulled his collar higher, the wool scratching against a jawline that had grown increasingly gaunt over the last month.

He kept thinking about the way the water looked in the daylight—how it could be a deceptive, brilliant blue, hiding the currents and the wrecks and the things that had drifted up from the seabed. That was how Caleb had been. A man of deceptive surfaces.

The news had come via a phone call from a man named Arvid, a fisherman Elias hadn't spoken to in seven years. Arvid’s voice had been thin, stripped of its usual joviality by the gravity of the report. A drift net had brought something in near the Lofoten banks. Not a fish. Something wrapped in a heavy, waterproof parka, tangled in the nylon mesh. A body, the official word had been, found by a crew that had been too tired and too spooked to look closely. The authorities had a tentative identification based on a lost wallet and a distinctive scar on the left hand, but the sea had been unkind to the features. They needed a relative. They needed Elias.

Elias turned away from the railing, retreating into the narrow, dimly lit corridor of the passenger deck. The linoleum was worn thin in the center of the hall, a path carved by decades of transient feet. He passed a vending machine that hummed with a lonely, electric hunger and a small cafe that had long since shuttered for the night, its windows dark and smeared with condensation.

He found himself in the small, cramped lounge, seated in a vinyl chair that smelled of stale tobacco and damp coats. He didn't turn on the reading light. He simply sat, watching the shadows dance as the ferry pitched.

His mind, unwilling to rest, drifted back to the last time he had seen Caleb. It hadn't been a dramatic departure. There were no slammed doors, no shouted accusations that would provide the catharsis of a tragedy. There had only been a slow, agonizing erosion. A series of missed holidays, unreturned texts, and the gradual, mutual decision that the distance between them was easier to maintain than the effort of closing it. Caleb had moved north, toward the edges of the world, chasing some phantom of autonomy, while Elias had stayed, anchored to the predictable rhythms of a life built on stability and silence.

The estrangement had become a third person in their relationship, a silent guest at every imaginary dinner, a shadow in every shared memory. And now, that shadow had potentially taken physical form, a corpse waiting in a sterile room in a fishing village, waiting for Elias to grant it a name.

The ferry’s engines surged, a deep, guttural roar that vibrated through the floorboards. The ship was gaining speed, pushing harder against the resistance of the strait. Elias closed his eyes, and for a moment, he wasn't on a ship; he was back in their childhood home, the smell of pine needles and woodsmoke thick in the air, watching Caleb climb the old oak tree, a boy who always seemed to be looking at something just beyond the reach of anyone else.

A sudden jolt sent a shudder through the lounge. A heavy wave had struck the bow. The lights flickered, a dying pulse of yellow light, and for a heartbeat, the ship plunged into total darkness. In that darkness, the fear was visceral—a primal realization of how thin the steel was between him and the crushing blackness of the North Sea. He felt the sudden, irrational urge to run, to jump, to vanish into the fog along with the uncertainty of his brother's fate.

When the lights hummed back to life, a woman was sitting across the aisle, her eyes fixed on him with a startled, piercing intensity. She was wrapped in a heavy, knitted cardigan, her face etched with the lines of a long life spent in the wind.

"Hard night for a crossing," she said. Her voice was low, gravelly.

Elias cleared his or throat, feeling the sudden awkwardness of his own presence. "Yes. The weather is turning."

"It’s always turning," she said, nodding toward the dark windows. "The sea doesn't like to stay still. It's never quite settled, even when it looks like glass."

"I suppose not."

"You look like you're carrying a heavy load, son. Not the kind you can put down when you reach the pier."

Elias looked down at his hands. They were trembling, just slightly. He clenched them into fists. "Just a long trip. A lot of things to settle."

The woman studied him for a moment longer, a look of recognition crossing her weathered face, as if she were seeing the ghost of her own grief mirrored in his posture. She didn't ask anything else. She simply turned back to her book, a small, battered volume with a broken spine.

The rest of the night passed in a blur of monochromatic motion. The fog never lifted. The ferry continued its rhythmic, agonizing crawl through the dark. Elias found himself caught in a loop of repetitive thoughts: the wallet, the scar, the drift net, the cold. He thought about the logistics of death—the paperwork, the funeral arrangements, the burial in the frozen earth of a coastal cemetery. He thought about the indignity of being found in a net, stripped of personhood, reduced to a biological fact.

As the first grey light of dawn began to bleed through the fog, the ship approached the harbor. The lights of the pier emerged from the gloom like drowned stars, flickering and unstable. The town was a cluster of low, dark shapes huddled against the rocky shoreline, a collection of warehouses and salt-crusted dwellings.

The gangway descended with a heavy, mechanical clatter. Elias stood among the small group of passengers—mostly fishermen and local laborers—as they disembarked into the biting morning air. The air here was even colder, stripped of the ship's warmth, smelling sharply of brine and wet stone.

He was met at the edge of the pier by a man in a heavy, waterproof jacket. He was thick-set, with a face that looked as though it had been carved from driftwood. This was Arvid.

"You came," Arvid said. He didn't offer a hand; the greeting was too heavy for that.

"I came," Elias replied.

"The police, they're waiting at the station. The doctor, he's already been. It's... well, it's not much to look at, Elias. The sea, it does things. It's hard to tell."

"I understand."

They walked in silence through the narrow, winding streets of the village. The ground was slick with a fine mist that turned everything into a mirror. The town was waking up, but it was a slow, reluctant awakening. A single light flickered in a window; a shutter creaked in the wind. The silence of the town was different from the silence of the sea. The sea was a presence; the town was an absence.

The police station was a small, concrete building that looked more like a utility shed than a place of authority. Inside, the air was stagnant, smelling of floor wax and old paper. A young officer, whose eyes were rimstered with red, led Elias to a back room.

"The morgue is in the basement," the officer said, his voice devoid of any inflection. "The coroner is still finishing the preliminary report. He'll be with you in a moment."

Elias sat on a wooden bench in a small, windowless waiting area. The walls were a pale, sickly green. He listened to the muffled sounds of the station—the ring of a telephone, the heavy tread of boots on linoleum, the distant, rhythmic thud of the tide against the pier.

The door opened, and a man entered. He was older than Elias had expected, with a calm, clinical demeanor that felt strangely jarring in the face of his dread. He carried a clipboard and wore a lab coat that was unnervingly white.

"Mr. Thorne?" the man asked.

"Yes."

"I am Dr. Lind. I've prepared the room. I must warn you, the identification process may be difficult. The swelling, the salt exposure... it isn't an easy thing to recognize a face."

"I understand," Elias said, his voice sounding foreign to his own ears.

"I will leave you alone for a few minutes. There is no need to rush. Take whatever time you need to compose yourself."

The doctor left, and the silence returned, heavier than before. Elias sat in the green-walled room, his heart hammering against his person like a trapped bird. He thought about the scar. The left hand. He thought about the way Caleb used to pick at his cuticles when he was nervous, a habit that had left his fingers perpetually raw.

The door opened again. This time, it was the doctor. He motioned for Elias to follow.

The basement was a labyrinth of cold, tiled corridors. The air was refrigerated, a sharp, biting chill that pierced through Elias's coat. At the end of a long hallway, the doctor stopped in front of a heavy, steel door. He opened it, and the smell hit Elias—a cloying, sterile scent of formaldehyde, ozone, and something deeper, something primal and metallic.

The room was small, lit by a single, harsh fluorescent light that hummed with a low-frequency vibration. In the center of the room, beneath a shroud of heavy, white plastic, lay the body.

The doctor moved toward the table, his movements efficient and practiced. "I will uncover only the face and the hands," he said softly. "The rest is... quite difficult."

Elias stood by the door, his hands deep in his pockets, his knuckles white. He felt as though he were watching a stranger, as though this were a scene from a film he had seen a long of time ago.

The doctor reached for the edge of the plastic. The sound of it sliding against the metal table was deafening in the quiet room. Slowly, the shroud was pulled back.

The first thing Elias saw was the hand. It was pale, almost translucent, the skin puckered and white from the brine. He leaned forward, his breath hitching in his chest. He searched the knuckles, the tendons, the shape of the thumb. He looked for the scar. There, near the base of the index finger, was a jagged, faded line, a pale mark against the blanched skin.

His heart surged, a painful, violent thud. It was him. The hand was his.

Then, the doctor moved to the face.

The skin was swollen, the features distorted by the pressure of the water and the passage of time. The eyes were closed, the lids heavy and bruised. The mouth was slightly agape, a dark void in the center of the face. It was a mask of flesh, a grotesque caricature of a human being.

Elias stepped closer, drawn by a morbid, irresistible gravity. He looked at the brow, the bridge of the nose, the curve of the jaw. He searched for the expression Caleb always wore—that subtle, inward-looking squint, as if he were constantly solving a puzzle in his mind.

He looked at the closed eyes. He felt a sudden, desperate hope that if he stared long enough, the eyes would open, and the stranger would vanish, and his brother would be standing there, annoyed by the cold, asking if they could leave.

But the eyes remained closed. The face remained a landscape of unknown terrain.

The doctor stood back, his expression unreadable. "The features are significantly altered, Mr. Thorne. I cannot, in good conscience, confirm this is your brother based on facial recognition alone. We will need to wait for the dental records or the DNA results."

Elias stared at the body. He looked at the hand, with its broken, salt-crusted fingernails and its singular, identifying scar. He looked at the distorted, unrecognizable face.

A strange, hollow sensation began to spread through him, a numbness that started in his chest and worked its way outward to his limbs. He realized then that the identification didn't matter. Whether this was Caleb or a stranger, the result was the same. The silence between them had reached its final, immutable form. The estrangement was no longer a state of being; it was a permanent condition. The person he had known, the brother he had loved and resented, was already gone. He had been gone for years. This body was merely the physical punctuation at the end of a long, unfinished sentence.

"Do you wish to stay?" the doctor asked.

Elias looked at the white plastic shroud, which the doctor was beginning to pull back over the face. He watched the features disappear, the distorted nose and the bruised eyelids vanishing beneath the sterile, unfeeling sheet.

"No," Elias said. His voice was steady, devoid of the tremor that had plagued him on the ferry. "I don't think I need to see any more."

He turned and walked out of the room. He walked through the labyrinth of cold, tiled corridors, past the waiting officer, and up the stairs toward the light of the morning.

Outside, the town was fully awake. The sound of a fishing boat's engine throbbed in the harbor, a heavy, rhythmic pulse. The fog was beginning to lift, revealing a sky of bruised purple and pale, watery grey. The air was still biting, but the sun, a weak and colorless disc, was beginning to burn through the haze.

Elias walked toward the pier. He didn't look back at the station, or the morgue, or the dark, churning water of the strait. He walked toward the edge of the town, where the road led back toward the mainland, toward the world of light and movement and the long, arduous process of continuing. He carried nothing with him but the weight of the silence, and for the first time in a long time, he found that he was strong enough to bear it.
