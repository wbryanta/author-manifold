The sun rose over the ridge like a cauterized wound and the heat was already a presence, a heavy and stagnant thing that sat upon the orchard and pressed the breath from the lungs of the trees. The trees were old and their bark was grey and fissured like the skin of the dead. They stood in rows of skeletal permanence, their limbs reaching upward into a sky that held no promise of rain, only a vast and bleaching light that stripped the world of its color.

Elias stood by the edge of the trench and watched the dust rise from the boots of his brother. The dust was fine and pale, a flour of pulverized stone and ancient organic rot. Caleb was down in the cut, his shoulders hunched, the muscles of his back moving like subterranean things beneath a shirt soaked in salt and grit. He was working the spade into the hardpan, the sound of metal against sun-baked earth a rhythmic and hollow percussion in the stillness.

The irrigation line lay in the trench, a length of black polyethylene that shimmered in the heat. It was split, a jagged aperture through which the moisture of the earth escaped, leaving only a dark and damp patch of sand that was already being reclaimed by the heat.

You gotta patch it deeper, Caleb said. His voice was dry, raspy.

Elias looked down into the trench. The dirt was clotted and heavy. We buried it deep enough last time.

It wasnt deep enough. The ground is shifting. The drought is pulling the soil apart.

The drought is a season, Elias said.

It is a state of being, Caleb said. He paused, leaning on the spade, his face a mask of dust and sweat. This isnt a season. It is the end of the thing.

Elias did not answer. He turned to the heavy coil of replacement pipe leaning against the rusted fender of the truck. He began to unspool it, the plastic clicking and groaning as it uncurled. The sound was lonely in the wide expanse of the orchard, a small and mechanical protest against the silence of the dying trees.

The orchard was a graveyard of fruit that never came. The peaches were small and bitter and the almonds were shriveled husks. There was no scent of blossom in the air, only the smell of parched wood and the metallic tang of the heat.

They worked in a silence that was not empty but weighted with the bulk of what remained unsaid. The spade struck stone. The shovel bit into the compacted clay. The sun climbed higher, a white and eyeless orb that seemed to watch them with a terrible indifference. 

Caleb rose from the trench, wiping his forehead with a forearm. He looked toward the house, a low-slung structure of weathered timber that sat slumped in the shadow of the hills. The windows were dark, like the eyes of a skull.

The man from the valley came by yesterday, Caleb said.

Elias continued to pull the pipe. The plastic dragged across the dry grass with a dry, rasping sound.

The man from the valley.

He had an offer.

Elias stopped. He looked at the black line of the pipe. It lay like a dead vein across the earth.

The offer.

He spoke of the acreage. He spoke of the water rights. He spoke of turning the orchard over to the solar arrays. He says the land wont bear fruit no more. He says the cost of the water alone is a suicide pact.

The land bears what it can bear, Elias said.

He is a businessman, Caleb said. He is not a poet. He sees the math.

The math is a lie if you forget where the numbers come with, Elias said.

Caleb wiped his hands on his trousers, leaving streaks of dark mud. There is no more coming, Elias. There is only the going. We are just sitting here waiting for the dust to cover us.

Elias knelt by the trench. He reached down and felt the dampness of the soil near the leak. It was a small, pathetic warmth. He took the coupling and the heavy rubber gaskets and began to fit them to the split pipe. His fingers were calloused and stiff.

There is a way to mend it, he said. You just have to find the source of the instability.

The instability is the world, Caleb said.

He leaned against the truck, his shadow stretching long and thin across the parched ground. The heat was shimmering now, making the distant hills appear to float, a mirage of blue and grey.

The developers, they dont want the trees, Caleb said. They want the sun. They want to lay the glass and capture the light. They want to turn this place into a machine.

And what is that more different than what we are doing? Elias asked.

We are trying to keep something alive. They are just harvesting the death of it.

The death is already here, Caleb said. Look at the trees. They are ghosts. They are walking corpses. There is no fruit in them. There is no future in them.

Elias tightened the bolt on the coupling. The metal groaned. He felt the tension in his own joints, a mirroring of the strain in the pipe. He thought of their father, a man of sinew and silence, who had spent forty years fighting this same dust, who had believed in the permanence of the orchard as if it were a covenant written in the stone.

The trees are still standing, Elias said.

They are standing because they have nowhere else to go.

They are part of the earth, Elias said.

So are we. And the earth is moving on.

He stood up, his knees popping. He looked at the pipe. It was a continuous, broken line. He looked at his brother. Caleb’s eyes were hard, reflecting the glare of the sun, a brightness that lacked any warmth.

The offer is on the table, Caleb said. He said he could have the papers ready by the end of the month. He wants a clean break. No litigation over the water. No mess.

There is no mess to clean, Elias said. There is only what is left.

There is nothing left, Caleb said.

The argument was old. It was a thing they had carried like a heavy stone through the years, a weight that had been passed from the father to the sons. It was a struggle between the will to endure and the will to escape.

Elias picked up the shovel. He began to push the earth back into the trench, over the new coupling, over the patched wound in the line. He worked with a focused, rhythmic intensity, as if the act of burying the repair could bury the logic of the sale.

The dirt fell in heavy clumps. Each shovelful was a burial.

He does not understand the cost, Caleb said. He thinks it is just land. He does not see the weight of it.

The weight is what makes it real, Elias said. Without the weight, it is just space.

You call it weight. I call it a burden. I am tired of the burden, Elias. I am tired of watching everything we touch turn to ash. I am tired of the thirst.

Elias stopped shoveling. He leaned on the handle, his breath coming in short, unbidden gasps. The heat was a physical weight upon his shoulders now, a crushing presence.

The thirst is what makes the water precious, Elias said.

The water is gone, Caleb said. The well is failing. The aquifer is dropping. You can patch the pipe all you want, but you are patching a vein in a corpse.

He looked down at the trench. The repair was hidden now, beneath a layer of pale, thirsty dust. The pipe was there, intact, but the ground around it was settling, the dry earth pulling away from the new dampness, seeking to consume it.

The sun began its slow descent, though the heat did not diminish. The light turned a bruised and heavy gold. The shadows of the trees lengthened, stretching across the orchard like the fingers of a drowning man.

Caleb sat on the fender of the truck and stared out toward the horizon, where the mountains met the sky in a line of jagged, unyielding purple. He looked like a man who had already left, a man who was merely waiting for his body to catch up with his mind.

We could move, Caleb said. To the coast. To something else. Something that does not require a war with the sun.

There is no something else, Elias said. There is only here.

There is a world outside this valley, Elias. A world that is not dying.

The world is dying everywhere, Elias said. It is just dying more loudly here.

Caleb looked at him then, a long, searching look that was filled with a profound and weary pity.

Then why stay? he asked. Why stay for the funeral?

To see it through, Elias said.

To what end?

To the end, Elias said.

The silence returned, more profound than before. It was a silence that contained the entire history of the orchard, the decades of planting and pruning and praying, the seasons of plenty and the long, encroaching years of drought. It was a silence that held the memory of the father’s hands in the soil and the mother’s voice in the kitchen and the laughter of children who had long since grown and departed.

The irrigation line sat beneath the earth, a hidden and fragile artery. The water began to move through it, a slow and pulsing pressure, seeking the gaps, seeking the weaknesses.

Elias watched the shadows grow. He thought of the trees, their roots deep in the dry, ancient dark, clinging to the memory of moisture. He thought of the man from the valley, with his papers and his solar arrays and his clean, mathematical end.

He thought of the dust. It was everywhere. It was in his lungs, in his eyes, in the very fabric of his being. It was the beginning and it was the end.

Caleb stood up. He wiped the grit from his face and reached for the keys in the truck.

I will talk to him, Caleb said. I will tell him we are considering it.

Elias did not move. He did not look at his brother. He watched a single, withered leaf fall from a nearby peach tree, spinning through the golden light before it settled into the dust.

Do what you must, Elias said.

The truck engine roared to life, a violent and mechanical intrusion upon the stillness. The dust rose in a great, choking cloud, obscuring the orchard, obscuring the house, obscuring the man.

Elias remained by the trench. He picked up the shovel and began to smooth the earth, leveling the ground where the heat had buckled it. He worked until the light failed, until the orchard was nothing but a collection of dark, indistinct shapes in the twilight, until the only thing left was the rhythmic, lonely sound of the shovel against the earth, and the memory of the water, moving unseen through the dark and the dust.
