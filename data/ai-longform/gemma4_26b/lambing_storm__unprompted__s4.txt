The wind did not arrive so much as it collided with the valley, a sudden, violent intrusion of Atlantic cold that turned the mild April evening into something unrecognizable. It arrived with the scent of bruised pine and wet stone, rattling the corrugated iron of the lambing shed until the very ground seemed to shudder. Inside, the air was already thick with the heavy, sweet-sour scent of damp wool, straw, and the warm, visceral musk of ewes. Clara pulled her collar higher, her fingers stiffening as the temperature plummeted. The power had flickeler and died twenty minutes ago, leaving them to the guttering, amber light of a single kerosene lamp and the rhythmic, uncertain beams of two heavy-duty flashlights.

She watched Elias. He was crouched in the corner of the pen, his silhouette a jagged shadow against the straw. At sixteen, he was caught in a strange, awkward transition, his limbs too long for his frame, his movements possessing a tentative gravity that Clara found both heartbreaking and unnerving. He was trying to mimic his father’s stillness, the way Silas used to sit and observe a ewe without disturbing her, but there was a tremor in his hands that he couldn't quite suppress.

"The wind's picking up," Elias said. His voice was low, competing with the frantic drumming of sleet against the roof.

"It's a late frost," Clara said, though they both knew the lie. This wasn't a frost. This was a blizzard, an unseasonable upheaval that had turned the soft, greened world of April into a white-out wasteland. "The ewes will feel it. We need to check the newborns in the back pen. The heat isn't holding."

They moved together through the narrow aisles, their boots crunching on the soiled straw. The silence between them was not an empty thing; it was a heavy, pressurized space, filled with the ghost of the man who should have been leading the way. For forty years, the rhythm of the lambing season had been a duet, a synchronized dance of checking temperatures, cleaning noses, and reinforcing bedding. Now, the rhythm was fractured. Every time Clara reached for a tool or turned to ask a question, her hand met only the cold, empty air where Silas’s presence used to be.

They reached the back pen, where the newest arrivals were huddled. The light from Elias's flashlight swept over the ewes, their eyes reflecting the beam like small, dark moons. A large, mottled ewe, one of the veterans, let out a low, distressed bleat.

"She’s straining," Elias whispered, his eyes wide.

Clara knelt, the dampness of the floor seeping through her waxed trousers. She could feel the ache in her knees, a permanent souvenir of decades of farm work, but it was nothing compared to the hollow ache in her chest that flared whenever the house felt too quiet. Here, in the shed, the noise was a distraction, a way to drown out the stillness of the farmhouse.

"Get the light steady, El," she commanded, her voice steady despite the sudden spike of adrenaline. 

She moved into the ewe's space, her hands finding the familiar warmth of the animal. The ewe was restless, her breathing shallow and rapid. As Clara worked, her mind drifted, unbidden, to the way Silas would have handled this. He had a way of talking to the animals, a low, melodic murmur that seemed to settle their nerves. He would have known exactly when to intervene and when to wait. Clara felt like she was improvising a play whose script had been lost in the storm.

"Is she okay?" Elias asked. He was leaning over her, the flashlight beam dancing erratically across the straw.

"She's fine. Just a difficult position. Help me move this hay bale so we can see better."

As they worked, the storm intensified. A sudden gust of wind caught the edge of the shed, a sound like a physical blow, and for a terrifying second, the structure groaned as if it might lift from its foundations. Elias gasped, his grip on the flashlight slipping.

"Stay with it," Clara said, her voice sharp, an anchor in the dark. "Don't let the light wander. We need to see what we're doing."

"I know," he snapped back, a flash of the adolescent frustration that had been surfacing since the funeral. "I'm trying."

She let the remark pass. She didn't have the energy for the friction of grief. They were two survivors lashed together by necessity, navigating a landscape that had become fundamentally unstable. 

The labor of the lambing was a grueling, tactile reality. There was the slickness of amniotic fluid, the grit of the straw, the heat of the animals' bodies, and the biting, intrusive chill of the air. Every few hours, a new life would emerge—a small, wet, struggling thing that required immediate, frantic care. Clara and Elias worked in a feverish loop: cleaning the nostrils, stimulating the lungs, rubbing the damp fleece with dry straw, ensuring the umbilical cord was secure. 

Around midnight, the storm reached its zenith. The snow was no longer falling; it was being driven horizontally, a white shroud that seemed to be erasing the world outside the shed's walls. The temperature inside had dropped significantly. The sheep were huddling closer, a singular, breathing mass of wool and warmth, but the periphery of the shed was a zone of frost.

"The heater's struggling," Elias said, gesturing toward the small, portable propane unit in the center of the aisle. Its orange glow was dimming, struggling against the draft that leaked through the gaps in the door.

"We'll have to keep moving," Clara said. She was exhausted, her muscles screaming, her skin coated in a fine layer of grime and laneway. "If we stay in one place too long, we'll lose our heat. Keep checking the lambs in the far corner. The wind is hitting that side hardest."

She watched him as he retreated into the shadows of the far pen. He moved with a new, grim determination. He was no longer just her son; he was her partner in this vigil. There was a heaviness in his shoulders that hadn't been there a month ago, a weight of responsibility that mirrored her own. They were both learning the terrible architecture of a life built around a missing pillar.

In the middle of the night, a ewe in the middle pen began to struggle. This wasn't the rhythmic, productive straining of a healthy birth; this was a panicked, frantic movement. Clara was by her side in seconds. 

"She's stuck, El. Get the kit. Get the lubricant and the clean towels."

Elias moved with a practiced efficiency that surprised her. He didn't hesitate, didn't ask for instruction. He grabbed the supplies and returned, his face illuminated by the stark, white light of the headlamp he had begun wearing. 

The procedure was grueling. The cold made the task much harder; the sheep's body was tight, the muscles resisting the intervention. Clara worked by touch and by the dim, flickering light, her hands navigating the heat and the pressure. Every minute felt like an hour. Her mind, ever the traitor, drifted to the memory of Silas's hands—large, calloused, and incredibly gentle. She remembered him coming into the shed after a long day, his face smudged with dirt, carrying a thermos of coffee and a quiet, steadying smile. He had been the one who could read the weather in the way a poet reads a stanza. Now, the weather was just a chaotic, unreadable force.

"I can't feel my fingers," Elias whispered. He was holding the light, but his voice was small, the voice of the boy who used to hide in the hayloft when he was scared of thunderstorms.

"Keep holding it," Clara said, her teeth chattering. "Just a little longer. I think I have a limb."

The tension in the shed was palpable, a physical pressure that seemed to vibrate in time with the wind. The sheep sensed it; a low, collective anxiety had settled over the flock. Then, with a sudden, wet heave, the obstruction gave way. A small, dark shape slid into the straw.

"There," Clara breathed, her heart hammering against herting ribs. "There we go."

The lamb was limp, its breathing almost imperceptible. For a heartbeat, the world narrowed down to that tiny, struggling life. Clara worked frantically, rubbing the lamb with a dry towel, her movements rhythmic and desperate. "Come on," she hissed. "Come on, little one. Breathe."

Elias leaned in, the light of his headlamp focused on the small, wet mass. "Is it...?"

"Wait," Clara said. 

A tiny, sputtering cough. Then a weak, high-pitched bleat. 

The tension broke, replaced by a profound, hollowed-out exhaustion. The lamb began to struggle toward its mother’s teat, its legs wobbly and uncoordinated. 

"He's okay," Clara said, leaning back against the wooden gate, her head thumping against the timber. "He's okay, El."

Elias let out a breath that was half-sob, half-laugh. He slumped down into the straw, the flashlight beam resting on the floor, illuminating the swirling dust and the golden fragments of hay.

They sat in silence for a long time, the only sound the relentless, driving roar of the storm and the heavy breathing of the sheep. The power was still out, the darkness pressing in from the edges of the shed, but the immediate crisis had passed. 

As the hours crawled toward dawn, the work continued, but the intensity had shifted from panic to a weary, mechanical endurance. They moved through the pens like ghosts, checking, cleaning, tending. The storm began to lose its violent edge, transitioning from a horizontal assault to a heavy, suffocating blanket of falling snow. The wind died down to a moan, a mournful sound that seemed to seep through the very cracks in the wood.

Clara found herself watching the way the light changed. The pre-dawn grey was a subtle, shifting thing, turning the shadows from deep black to a bruised purple, then to a pale, watery blue. It was a slow, agonizingly beautiful revelation. 

"Look," Elias said, pointing toward the far window.

The snow had stopped falling so heavily. Through the frost-rimed glass, a sliver of pale, watery light was beginning to bleed into the sky. The world outside was gone, replaced by a featureless, white expanse, but the light was there. It was a cold, indifferent light, but it was light.

"The sun's coming up," she said.

They were both covered in the debris of the night—mud, wool, dried blood, and sweat. Their clothes were stiff with cold, and their bodies felt brittle, as if they might shatter if they moved too quickly. But as the first true light of morning filtered into the shed, illuminating the heaps of straw and the sleeping ewes, a profound sense of continuity settled over them.

The storm had tried to break them, to sweep away the remnants of their life and leave them adrift in the white void. But the lambs were there. The ewes were there. The work, however grueling, had been done.

Elias stood up, his joints popping audily in the quiet of the shed. He wiped his face with the back of a grimy sleeve, and for a moment, Clara saw him clearly—not as the boy who was grieving, but as the man who had stayed.

"We should go check the perimeter," he said, his voice raspy from the cold and the lack of sleep. "See if the fences held."

Clara nodded, her eyes tracing the line of his silhouette against the brightening shed. "Yes. We should check. And then we'll see about the woodpile."

She stood, her muscles protesting with every movement, and felt the weight of the day ahead. There would be more work, more repairs, more of the long, quiet struggle of the spring. The house would still be empty, and the silence would still be waiting for them when they returned. But as she looked at the small, breathing lives gathered in the warmth of the straw, she felt a flicker of something that wasn'ed quite hope, but was perhaps something more durable: a stubborn, dogged persistence.

They stepped out of the shed together, into the biting, crystalline air of a winter morning that had forgotten it was April. The world was transformed, a blinding, pristine landscape of white and shadow. The tracks they left in the fresh snow were deep and heavy, two sets of footprints marking their passage through the wreckage of the night, heading toward the farmhouse, toward the light, toward the long, slow work of beginning again.
