He paused there, the silence between them growing heavy, the kind of weight that settles in a room when a man has finished a truth and isn't sure if he wants to start another one. He looked down at his hands, hands that were now spotted and dry, nothing like the hands that had gripped the side of a transport plane or held a coconut knife on Johnston. Inez watched the way he fumbled with the sugar tongs, a small, sharp sound in the quiet, the silver clicking against the porcelain like a tiny, frantic signal. She didn't say anything. You didn't interrupt Jack when he was navigating those waters, those deep, murky stretches of memory where the light didn't quite reach. You just waited, like you were waiting for the weather to clear on an atoll, sitting there in the humidity, hoping the sky would eventually show you something worth the wait.

He reached for his glass, the ice clinking against the crystal, a sound that seemed too loud in the dimness of the parlor. He took a sip, swallowed, and then his eyes drifted back to the window, looking past the manicured garden and the darkening street toward something much further away. 

The return was the hardest part, he said, his voice dropping an octave, losing that bright, observational edge and becoming something more hollowed out. Coming back from the islands. You think you’re coming back to the real world, you know? You think you’re leaving the theater behind and stepping back into the lives of people who actually matter, people with mortgages and grocery lists and regular, predictable tragedies. But you weren't. You were bringing the dust with you. Not just the literal dust—though God, that stuff was everywhere, a fine, invisible grit that seemed to settle into the very pores of your skin, into the fibers of your flight suits, into the very fabric of your thoughts. You were bringing the heat with you, too. That particular, searing brightness of the detonation, it stayed behind your eyelids. You’d be sitting in a diner in San Francisco, or walking down a street in Manila, and the sun would hit a chrome bumper or a glass window just right, and for a second, the world would flash—that same, impossible, terrifying pink—and you’d be right back on the edge of the lagoon, waiting for the shockwave to hit.

He rubbed his thumb over the rim of his glass, a rhythmic, unconscious motion.

It wasn't just the radiation, he continued, though he wouldn't call it that, not in those days. It was the knowledge of it. The knowledge that the sky wasn't just a sky, but a canvas that could be torn open. We were all just spectators at a game where the rules were being rewritten in real-time, and nobody had bothered to tell the players. We were just there to watch the light. And the light was so beautiful, Inez. That’s the part people don't get. They think of the bombs, they think of the destruction, the mushroom clouds and the scorched earth, and they think of horror. But they don't think of the aesthetic. They don't think of how, for a few seconds, the universe seems to reveal its true, terrifying splendor. It was a moment of pure, unadulterated clarity. A moment where the math and the physics and the raw, primal power of the sun all converged in a single, breathtaking burst. And we were there, perched on our little sand spits, like children watching a firework display, completely oblivious to the fact that we were watching the birth of a different kind of end.

He let out a short, dry laugh, the kind that doesn't quite reach the eyes.

The technical guys, they were the real architects of it, he said. They didn't see the pink sky. They saw data. They saw pressure waves and thermal radiation and neutron flux. They were looking at the numbers, trying to see if the reality matched the calculations, if the computer models were holding up. They were the ones who lived in the world of the measurable, the world of the concrete. And they were just as haunted as the rest of us, though they’d never admit it. They’d hide it behind their clipboards and their specialized instruments, behind that intense, focused professionalism that you use as a shield. But you could see it in the way they’d look at the horizon after a shot, a sort of stunned, silent reverence that they’d quickly try to mask with a discussion about yield or debris patterns. They were looking for the math in the middle of a miracle, and they couldn't quite find it.

Inez shifted in her chair, the silk of her skirt rustling, a sound that felt strangely intimate in the heavy atmosphere of the room. She looked at him, really looked at him, noting the way the shadows pooled in the lines around his mouth. He was telling her this as if it were a confession, or perhaps as if he were trying to convince himself that it had all been a dream, a beautiful, terrible dream that had ended long ago.

Do you remember the souvenir, he asked, his gaze snapping back to her, suddenly sharp, almost searching. The little thing from Manila.

Inez smiled, a small, sad movement of her lips. I remember, she said. It’s in the drawer. The little wooden bird.

He nodded, a slow, heavy motion.

It was carved by a man on Johnston, he said, his voice softening. A man who had spent a month on the island, just like I had. He’d brought it back with him, tucked into his kit, a little piece of the islands to remind him that there was something other than sand and steel and the endless, waiting sky. He’d traded it for a tin of something, I don't even remember what. But when I saw it, I thought of you. I thought of the way you used to look when you were dreaming, that same, distant, faraway expression, as if you were somewhere else entirely, somewhere much more beautiful than this.

He fell silent again, the weight of the memory pressing down on the room. The shadows in the corners seemed to deepen, the edges of the furniture blurring into the gloom. Outside, the wind picked up, a low, mournified moan that stirred the trees in the garden, a sound that, for a moment, felt like the distant, approaching roar of a wave, or the first, low rumble of an explosion far out at sea.

He took another sip of his drink, his eyes fixed on a point somewhere far beyond the walls of the house.

There were times, he whispered, so low she almost missed it, when I thought we’d never come back. Not really. That we’d just stay there, part of the atoll, part of the cycle of the tests, caught in that loop of waiting and watching, of the pink sky and the smell of gardenias and the sudden, blinding light. That we’d just become part of the landscape, as permanent and as transient as the sand spits themselves. And in a way, I think we did. We left pieces of ourselves there, on those islands, in the heat and the humidity and the salt spray. We left the versions of us that believed in the permanence of things.

He looked at her then, his eyes meeting hers in the dim light, and for a second, she saw it—the reflected flash of that impossible, beautiful, terrifying pink, a flicker of the old, unshielded Jack, the observer who had seen the world torn open and had found it, against all reason, to be something worth beholding. And then, as quickly as it had appeared, the light vanished, leaving only the shadows and the heavy, settling silence of the room, and the man who was no longer there.
