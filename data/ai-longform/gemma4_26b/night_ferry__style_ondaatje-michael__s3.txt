The engine was a low, percussive heartbeat, a rhythmic shudder that traveled through the soles of the feet, up the marrow, into the teeth. It was the only thing that anchored the ship to the present, preventing the ferry from dissolving into the grey, indeterminate smudge of the strait. Here, the water did not so much move as exist in a state of heavy, liquid suspension, a black muscle flexing beneath a skin of mist. Everything was obscured: the distant shoreline, the wake of the vessel, the very concept of a horizon. There was only the hull, the salt-slicked metal, and the encroaching density of the fog.

Elias sat in the corner of the passenger lounge, a space populated by the ghosts of a transit-class existence. There were others, of course—a woman in a heavy wool coat staring into the steam of a paper cup, a man sleeping with his head tilted against the vibrating window—but they felt like projections, translucent figures layered over a landscape of memory. To be on this ferry was to be in a state of suspended animation, a transition between two geographies that no longer seemed to belong to the same world.

He thought of the maps they had used as children, the ones spread across the scarred pine table in their father’s study, where the edges were curled and the ink had bled into the grain. They had traced the veins of rivers with trembling fingers, imagining the water as something sentient, a circulatory system for the earth. Julian had always been obsessed with the points of arrival, the way a line on a map could resolve into a pier, a house, a person. Elias had been obsessed with the departures, the way a line could simply vanish into a smudge of unprinted white.

Now, the white was everywhere.

The news had arrived not as a person, or even a voice, but as a series of fragmented textures: a telephone call in the middle of a Tuesday, the sudden, sharp cold of the air in the room, the way the light seemed to have lost its ability to illuminate, becoming instead a way to merely define the shadows. *A body found near the narrows. Unidentified. Potential match for the description.* The description was a hollow thing, a collection of adjectives that felt like insults: *male, mid-thirties, weathered, unrecognizable.*

He closed his eyes, and the memory of Julian surfaced, not as a face, but as a collection of sensory details. The smell of cedar shavings and old tobacco; the particular, uneven rhythm of his breathing when he slept; the way his hands, always scarred from some undocumented mishap, would move with a sudden, frantic precision when he was explaining something complicated. There was a specific geometry to Julian’s presence, a way he occupied space that made the air around him feel denser, more significant.

To identify the body was to complete the map. It was to take the vague, drifting uncertainty of a rumor and fix it, with the cruel finality of a needle, into the topography of his own life.

The ferry lurched, a heavy groan of metal against tide. The woman with the coffee had stood up, her movement creating a brief, sweeping arc in the dim light. Elias watched her, wondering if she, too, was carrying a census of ghosts. We are all just collections of what we have lost, he thought, and the thought felt uncomfortably heavy, like a stone swallowed in childhood.

He remembered a summer in the north, years before the silence had grown between them. They had been camping near the edge of a different water, a lake so still it functioned as a mirror for the sky. Julian had gone out on the water in a small, battered canoe, seeking the exact center of the stillness. Elias had stayed on the shore, watching the way the ripples moved outward, concentric circles of disruption that eventually smoothed back into nothingness. He had waited for Julian to return, a sense of dread beginning to coil in his stomach, a premonition of the way the world could simply swallow a person whole, leaving only the surface, undisturbed and indifferent.

The ferry pushed deeper into the strait. The fog seemed to be thickening, pressing against the windows, attempting to find a breach. The light in the lounge had turned a bruised, oceanic blue. It was the color of things forgotten, of the deep places where the pressure is too great for breath.

He thought of the last time they had spoken. It had not been a confrontation, but a thinning. A gradual erosion of the common ground they had built. They had been standing in a train station, the air tasting of ozone and wet stone, and the words had been sparse, used only to fill the gaps between the announcements of departing departures. Julian had looked at him, and for a second, Elias had seen the boy from the lake, the one who believed that everything could be traced back to a source. But the man standing there was a stranger, a person composed of different, more jagged elements.

*I am going north,* Julian had said. *There is a clarity there.*

*There is only cold,* Elias had replied.

The ferry’s intercom crackled, a burst of static that sounded like breaking bone. An announcement, unintelligible and muffled by the heavy air, informed them of an approaching delay. The ship was slowing. The engine’s pulse shifted, dropping into a lower, more labored register.

He stood up, his limbs feeling heavy, as if the humidity of the strait were seeping into his joints. He walked toward the window, pressing his forehead against the cold glass. Outside, the world had ceased to exist. There was only the ship, a flickering light in a vast, swallowing dark.

The movement of the ferry was a slow, agonizing oscillation. He found himself counting the seconds between the vibrations, a way to impose a structure on the chaos of his own anticipation. He thought of the morgue, the sterile, white-tiled reality of it. The way the light would fall on a tray, the clinical detachment of the hand that would pull back the sheet. He imagined the face—or the lack of a face—and the sudden, violent realization that the person he was looking for was no longer a person, but an object, a fact to be verified.

There is a specific kind of grief that belongs to the unidentified. It is a grief that cannot find its footing, a mourning that wanders without a destination. It is a ghost looking for a haunt.

He remembered the way Julian used to collect stones from the riverbeds, small, smooth objects that he would press into Elias’s palm as if they were precious, as if they held the weight of the earth itself. *Hold this,* he would say. *See how it stays.*

Now, Elias felt as though he were the one being held, caught in the grip of a vast, indifferent mechanism, being carried toward a shore where the truth would be revealed in all its cold, unadorned simplicity.

The ferry began to dock. The sound of it was a symphony of industrial violence: the grinding of ropes, the thud of the hull against the rubber bumpers, the sudden, jarring deceleration. The lights in the lounge flickered and then steadied, casting long, distorted shadows across the floor.

He moved through the gangway, his boots echoing on the metal plating. The air on the pier was sharp, tasting of salt and diesel and the decay of old wood. It was a biting, predatory cold that seemed to strip the heat from his skin instantly.

The town on this side of the strait was a small, huddled thing, a collection of low-slung buildings that looked as though they had been bleached by the wind and the sea. Everything was grey, a spectrum of ash and slate. He walked toward the small, municipal building that housed the coroner’s office, his breath blooming in front of him in rhythmic, ghostly puffs.

The interior of the building was lit by a single, humming fluorescent tube. The air was stagnant, smelling of floor wax and something metallic, something medicinal. A man sat behind a high desk, his skin the color of parchment, his eyes hooded by heavy, tired lids. He did not look up immediately; he was busy stamping a stack of papers, a repetitive, rhythmic sound that mirrored the engine of the ferry.

"I'm here to see about the identification," Elias said. His voice sounded strange to his own ears, thin and brittle, as if it might shatter in the quiet room.

The man looked up then. His gaze was not unkind, but it was profoundly weary, the look of someone who had seen the end of too many stories. He stood, his joints popping in the silence.

"The room is ready," the man said. He didn't ask for a name. He didn't ask for a relationship. He simply gestured toward a heavy, steel door at the end of a narrow corridor.

Elias walked. The corridor was a tunnel of shadow. The walls were painted a shade of green that felt sickly, an artificial color that had no place in the natural world. As he moved, he felt the sensation of the ferry again, the swaying, the sense of being suspended between what was known and what was about to be discovered.

He reached the door. It was heavy, cold to the touch. He hesitated, his hand hovering over the handle. In that moment, the entire geography of his life seemed to hinge on this single, physical movement. If he opened the door, the map would be completed. The uncertainty would vanish, replaced by a devastating, unchangeable truth. The brother who had been a collection of memories, a series of fragmented images and unrecorded sounds, would be permanently transformed into a corpse.

He thought of the lake. He thought of the canoe, drifting in the center of the stillness.

He pushed the door open.

The room was small, dominated by a single, stainless-steel table that gleamed under a harsh, overhead light. The temperature was low, a biting chill that seemed to emanate from the metal itself.

And there, beneath the light, lay the shape.

It was partially obscured by a white sheet, but the outline was unmistakable. The broadness of the shoulders, the particular tilt of the head, the way the arm lay at an angle that suggested a sudden, uncoordinated collapse.

Elias approached the table. His footsteps were silent on the linoleum. He reached out, his fingers trembling, and grasped the edge of the cloth.

He pulled it back.

The face was a landscape of trauma, a map of a struggle that had been lost. There was bruising, a deep, mottled purple that obscured the features, and a jagged laceration across the brow. But beneath the damage, beneath the layers of cold and violence, there was a familiarity. He saw the bridge of the nose, the slight, uneven curve of the nostril, the way the skin seemed to pull tight over the cheekbone.

He leaned closer, his breath hitching in his chest. He looked for the scar, the small, white line near the temple that Julian had received when they were children, a souvenir of a fallen branch.

He found it.

It was a tiny, almost imperceptible mark, a silver thread in the greyed, deathly pallor of the skin.

Elias closed his eyes. The room vanished. The hospital, the pier, the ferry, the grey strait—all of it dissolved into a single, piercing point of light. He was back at the pine table, watching Julian trace the rivers. He was back at the lake, watching the canoe drift away.

The identification was complete. The map was finished. The line had reached its end, and there was nothing left but the vast, unprinted white of the fog. He stood in the silence, a man who had finally arrived, only to find that there was nowhere left to go.
