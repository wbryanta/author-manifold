The ferry moved through the strait like a slow, heavy thought, a rhythmic, churning weight that seemed to be less traveling across the water than displacing it. It was the kind of darkness that felt physical, a thick, saline velvet that pressed against the portholes of the lower deck, blurring the distinction between the sea and the sky. Elias sat in the corner of the passenger lounge, a space that smelled perpetually of burnt coffee, diesel, and the damp, woolly scent of travelers who had been sitting in the same clothes for too or many hours. He kept his hands in his coat pockets, his fingers tracing the jagged edge of a loose thread, a small, frantic anchor of sensation in a world that had become entirely fluid.

Outside, the North Atlantic was a void. There were no lights from distant coastlines, no rhythmic flashes of buoys to mark the passage. There was only the vibration of the engine, a low-frequency thrum that he felt in his molars, and the occasional, mournful groan of the hull as it met the swell. He had been on the boat for four hours, though time had lost its linear utility. The clock on the wall, a cheap plastic thing with a cracked face, ticked with a clinical, indifferent precision that felt like an insult.

He thought of the phone call. It had come at three in the morning, a jagged intrusion into the silence of his apartment. The voice on the other end had been professional, stripped of any capacity for empathy, the voice of a man reading a grocery list. *A man was found near the wreck site. Mid-thirties. Unidentifiable due to the exposure. We are looking for next of kin to provide a way to confirm.*

The "may or may favor" of the situation was the only thing keeping Elias from a complete collapse. If the body was not Callum, then the world remained intact, however fractured. The estrangement had been a long, slow erosion rather than a sudden break. It was a series of unreturned calls, of messages left in the silence of a disconnected line, of a shared history that had simply been allowed to fray at the edges until the threads no longer held. They hadn't fought; they had simply drifted, like two ships caught in the same current but navigating by different stars. Callum had always been the one to seek the periphery, the one who found the center too suffocating, the one who preferred the cold, unmapped reaches of the coast to the steady, predictable light of the mainland.

Elias closed his eyes, but the darkness behind his eyelids was no different from the darkness outside. He saw Callum’s face, not as it was in the recent, grainy photographs sent from a distance, but as it had been ten years ago—sun-scorched, laughing, the skin tight over a jawline that already promised a certain hardness. He remembered the way Callum used to look at the sea, not as a barrier or a graveyard, but as a vast, unwritten ledger. There was a recklessness in that gaze, a hunger for the unknown that Elias had always found both terrifying and deeply enviable.

The lounge was nearly empty. A single woman sat near the vending machine, her head bowed over a paperback, the light from a small reading lamp casting a sickly yellow halo around her. She didn't look up. No one looked up. On an overnight ferry, everyone is a ghost, a transient soul caught in the transition between a place left and a place yet to be reached. They were all suspended in this liminal, salt-soaked purgatory, waiting for the dawn to give them back their identities.

The boat shuddered, a violent, jarring motion that sent a ripple through a half-empty cup of tea on a nearby table. The engine's pitch changed, a deeper, more labored growl. The crossing was lengthening. The strait was widening, or perhaps the tide was simply pulling harder against the ferry’s progress.

Elias found himself thinking about the small things. The way Callum used to whistle when he was nervous, a high, thin sound that pierced the silence of their childhood home. The way he would always leave a single, unwashed mug in the sink, a small, domestic rebellion. These were the details that felt most precarious now. If the man in the morgue was Callum, these memories would cease to be living things; they would become artifacts, preserved in the amber of a finished story. He felt a desperate, irrational urge to protect the uncertainty. He wanted to stay on this boat forever, trapped in the middle of the strait, where the identity was still unverified, where the brother he had lost to distance might still be alive, merely lost in the fog.

He stood up, his joints stiff from the cold that had begun to seep through the metal walls of the cabin. He needed to move, to walk the length of the deck, to feel the spray of the sea against his skin, to remind himself that he was still a solid object.

The upper deck was an assault of wind and salt. The air was so cold it felt sharp, like breathing in microscopic shards of glass. The wind whipped his hair across his eyes, and for a moment, the sheer ferocity of the elements made him feel small, an insignificant speck of biological matter being tossed across a liquid wasteland. The sea was a churning, black mass, the waves rising in great, heaving mountains that disappeared into the gloom. Every time the ferry crested a swell, there was a moment of terrifying weightlessness, a suspension where the engine’s roar seemed to vanish, leaving only the sound of the wind screaming through the railings.

He leaned against the railing, the cold metal biting into his palms. Far off in the darkness, he thought he saw a light, but it vanished as quickly as it had appeared, leaving him more alone than before. The strait was a place of disappearance. It swallowed things—cargo, ships, memories, men.

He thought of the last time they had spoken. It had been a brief, functional conversation about a shared inheritance, a legal necessity that required them to acknowledge eached other's existence. Callum had sounded tired. Not the tiredness of a long day, but the profound, bone-deep exhaustion of someone who has been fighting a current for too long. He had sounded like he was already halfway gone.

*I'm heading north, Elias,* he had said. *Just to see how far the edge goes.*

*The edge is a dangerous place to be, Cal,* Elias had replied.

*It’s the only place where you can actually see the horizon,* Callum had said, and then the line had gone dead.

Now, the horizon was nowhere to be found.

The ferry began its slow, grinding approach toward the island port. The lights of the pier emerged from the gloom, not as welcoming beacons, but as pale, sickly smudges of amber and white. The dock was a skeletal structure of wood and rusted iron, appearing and disappearing as the boat swayed. The silence of the arrival was more unsettling than the roar of the crossing. The engine died down to a low, rhythmic pulse, and the heavy, clanking sound of the mooring lines being thrown echoed across the water.

Elias descended the gangway, his boots thudding on the wet planks. The air on the pier was stagnant, smelling of rotting seaweed and old oil. The town was a cluster of low, dark buildings huddled together against the wind, their windows dark and unblinking. He felt like a trespasser in a graveyard.

The official was waiting for him in a small, sterile office near the dock, a room that smelled of floor wax and old paper. The man was middle-aged, with skin the color of parchment and eyes that had seen too many things that could not be unlearned. He didn'istically offered a hand; instead, he gestured toward a chair.

"The identification will be at the clinic," the official said, his voice a low, monotone rasp. "They are waiting for you. They have the preliminary reports."

"Is there... is there anything else I should know?" Elias asked. His voice sounded foreign to him, thin and brittle in the small room.

The official paused, his hand hovering over a stack of forms. He looked at Elias, and for a fleeting second, there was a flicker of something—pity, perhaps, or a recognition of the shared weight of the night. "The weather has been difficult. The recovery was... complicated."

Elias nodded, though the information offered him no clarity, only a deeper sense of dread. He left the office and began the walk toward the clinic, a small, white building that sat on the edge of the cliffs, overlooking the churning throat of the strait.

The clinic was illuminated by a single, harsh fluorescent light that flickered with a rhythmic, neurological twitch. The hallway was narrow and smelled of antiseptic, a scent so clean it felt aggressive. As he walked, the silence of the building pressed against his ears, a heavy, pressurized silence that made the sound of his own footsteps seem deafeless.

He reached the door marked *Examination Room 2*. His hand hovered over the handle, a moment of profound hesitation. He could turn around. He could walk back to the ferry, board the next morning's crossing, and return to his life of quiet, managed estrangement. He could pretend that the news was a mistake, a tragic error in a sea of misidentifications. He could live in the "perhaps."

But the "perhaps" had become a weight, a density in his chest that prevented him from breathing. He needed the truth, even if the truth was a terminal wound.

He pushed the door open.

The room was cold. The light was even harsher here, a sterile, uncompromising glare that stripped the world of its shadows. A nurse stood by a small sink, her back to him, her movements methodical and drained of all warmth. In the center of the room, beneath a single, bright surgical lamp, lay a shape.

It was a figure beneath a white sheet, a pale, indistinct mound that seemed to absorb the light rather than reflect it. The nurse turned, her expression a mask of professional neutrality.

"The coroner is finishing the final documentation," she said softly. "He will be with you in a moment."

Elias did not move. He could not move. His gaze was locked on the shape. He was looking for a sign, a fragment of a person that his mind could recognize. He was looking for the jaggedness, the specific, unruly geometry of his brother.

The door opened again, and a man in a heavy lab coat entered. He was older, with slumped shoulders and a face etched with the fatigue of the night's work. He did not offer condolences; he simply approached the table.

"Mr. Thorne?" the coroner asked.

"Yes," Elias said.

The coroner moved to the side of the table. He did not pull the sheet back immediately. Instead, he looked at Elias, a long, searching look that seemed to weigh the man's readiness. "The identification is not certain. There is significant trauma. However, there are features that may provide a lead."

Elias felt a strange, detached sensation, as if he were watching this scene from a great distance, as if he were a spectator in a theater of the absurd. "Show me," he whispered.

The coroner reached for the edge of the sheet. The sound of the fabric sliding against the metal table was loud, a harsh, rasping hiss in the quiet room. He pulled it down, slowly, revealing the face.

Elias leaned forward, his breath hitching in his throat. He looked at the forehead, obscured by a dark, matted patch of hair. He looked at the nose, broken and swollen. He looked at the cheek, where a deep, purplish bruise bloomed like a dark flower.

And then, he saw it.

On the edge of the man's jaw, partially obscured by the swelling, was a small, crescent-shaped scar, a white, puckered mark left by a childhood accident with a broken toy. It was a tiny, insignificant detail, a microscopic piece of a larger, shattered whole.

Elias felt the world tilt. The fluorescent light seemed to brighten, turning the room into a blinding, white void. The breath left his lungs, leaving him hollow, a vacuum of a man.

"The scar," Elias said, his voice a mere vibration. "The scar on the jaw."

The coroner remained silent, his eyes fixed on the body. He did not confirm, nor did he deny. He simply waited, as if allowing the weight of the moment to settle.

Elias looked closer. He searched the face for more, for the eyes, for the mouth, for the specific, defiant set of the chin that had always characterized Callum. But the face was gone, replaced by a landscape of trauma and cold, unresponsive flesh. The scar was a clue, a single, fraying thread of a connection, but it was not a person. It was merely a mark on a map of a territory that no longer existed.

He realized then that the person he was looking for was already gone. The man who had sought the edge of the world, the man who had breathed the salt and the storm, had vanished long before this body had been found. The brother Elias had known had been lost in the years of silence, in the miles of distance, in the slow, inexorable drift of their separate lives. What lay on this table was merely the physical residue of a life that had reached its conclusion.

The uncertainty remained, but it had changed its nature. It was no longer a question of whether this was Callum; it was a question of what was left of Callum now that the person was gone.

Elias stepped back, his boots clicking on the linoleum. He felt a sudden, overwhelming need for the cold, for the wind, for the indifferent, churning darkness of the strait. He needed to be back on the water, where things were lost and found in a cycle that had nothing to do with identity or grief.

"I cannot stay," Elias said, his voice steadying, though his heart felt like a wounded thing.

"Of course," the coroner replied, his voice soft, almost apologetic.

Elias turned and walked out of the room, down the long, sterile hallway, and out into the night. The air on the pier was even colder now, the wind biting into his skin with renewed ferocity. He walked toward the docks, toward the retreating lights of the ferry, toward the vast, swallowing dark of the sea. He did not look back at the clinic, nor at the town, nor at the shape beneath the sheet. He only looked toward the horizon, where the black water met the black sky, and where the only certainty was the rhythmic, indifferent pulse of the tide.
