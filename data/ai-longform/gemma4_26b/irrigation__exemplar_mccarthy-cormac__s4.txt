The sun hung in the midday sky like a brass plate and the heat rose from the parched earth in shimmering waves that distorted the horizon. The orchard lay in a state of slow and silent expiration. The trees, once heavy with fruit, stood now as skeletal sentinels, their bark fissured and grey, their limbs reaching upward as if to supplicate a sky that had long since withdrawn its mercy. The dust moved in low, drifting currents across the floor of the orchard, coating the dying leaves in a fine and ochre shroud.

Elias knelt in the dry trench, his hands deep in the calcined soil. He worked with a rusted spade, his movements slow and deliberate, a ritual of futile persistence. Beside him, Caleb sat upon a discarded coil of heavy rubber hose, wiping sweat from his forehead with a forearm stained by grime.

The pipe is gone, Caleb said.

Elias did not look up. The earth is too dry. The seal won't take.

It wont take, Caleb said. Nothing here will take. Look at the trees, Elias. They are ghosts. They are nothing but wood and memory.

The man stopped his digging and sat back on his heels. He wiped his brow with the back of a hand that was cracked and blackened by years of toil. The trees are sleeping. They are waiting for the rain.

The rain isnt coming, Caleb said. The drought is here to stay. The developers from the valley, they were asking after the acreage again. They see the water rights. They see the potential for the subdivision.

Elias looked at the boy. He was not much a boy now, but his face bore the same soft edges he had possessed in the summers of their youth, though now those edges were hardened by a cynical pragmatism.

They are vultures, Elias said.

They are businessmen, Caleb said. And they are offering a price that would let you retire. That would let us both move. Somewhere with actual soil. Somewhere where the water doesnt cost more than the crop.

Elias picked up a length of heavy iron coupling and turned it over in his hands. The metal was pitted with rust. He looked at the dry, cracked throat of the irrigation trench. He looked at the way the sun bleached the very bones of the world.

This land is not for sale, he said.

It is a carcass, Caleb said. We are just scavenging what is left.

The heat intensified, pressing down upon them with a physical weight, a silent and indifferent god. A crow landed on a low-hanging branch of a dead peach tree and watched them with a dark and unblinking eye. It let out a single, harsh croak that seemed to punctuate the emptiness of the valley.

The water must flow, Elias said. Without the flow, there is nothing.

The flow is a ghost, Caleb said. We are chasing shadows in the dust.

He stood up and walked to the edge of the trench. He looked out over the expanse of the orchard, where the rows of trees vanished into the shimmering heat of the distance. The landscape was a study in desolation, a vast and monochromatic expanse of brown and grey and burnt umber.

You think you can hold back the tide with a patch of rubber and some iron? Caleb said. The bank does not care about your sentiment. The debt is a living thing, Elias. It grows even when the trees die.

Elias rose. He was a larger man, his frame thickened by decades of labor, his movements imbued with a heavy and certain gravity. He stood in the trench, the dust swirling around his boots.

I do not care for the bank. I care for the blood in this ground. My father laid these rows. His father before him. You would see it all paved over. You would see it all buried under asphalt and streetlamps and the hollow noise of people who have no roots.

Caleb spat into the dust. The dust swallowed the moisture instantly. 

Roots in a desert are a death sentence, he said. We are clinging to a corpse.

They moved in silence for a time, the only sound the scrape of the spade and the labored breathing of the men. They worked on the broken section of the line, an old segment of PVC that had been buckled by the shifting, settling earth. Elias struggled with the heavy clamps, his knuckles bleeding where the jagged edges of the plastic bit into his skin.

The sun began its slow descent toward the jagged teeth of the western mountains. The light turned a bruised and sickly orange, casting long, distorted shadows across the orchard. The heat did not abate; it merely changed character, becoming a heavy and suffocating presence that clung to the lungs.

The repair was a failure. The new coupling held for a few minutes, a momentary victory, before the pressure of the groundwater—what little there was—forced a breach in a secondary fracture. A plume of muddy, silt-laden water erupted from the earth, bubbling up through the cracks in the trench before being swallowed once more by the thirsty soil.

It is useless, Caleb said. He stood over the trench, his shadow stretching long and thin across the dead leaves.

Elias watched the water vanish. He watched the way the earth drank the lifeblood of the orchard with a rapacious and unthinking greed. He felt a profound sense of the futility of his own existence, a recognition of the cosmic indifference that governed the rise and fall of all things.

We will try again tomorrow, Elias said.

Tomorrow is just another day of dying, Caleb said. 

The brothers stood in the twilight of the orchard, two figures etched in silhouette against the fading light. The mountains were black shapes against a sky of deepening violet. Around them, the trees stood in their silent and monumental grief, and the wind began to stir, a cold and lonely wind that brought with it the scent of dust and the coming of the night.

Elias picked up the spade. He began to fill the trench, covering the failed repair with the dry and bitter earth. Each shovelful of dirt was a heavy and rhythmic interment. He worked until his arms ached and the light had failed entirely, until the orchard was nothing but a dark and inscrutable mass of shadows, and the only certainty was the weight of the earth and the approaching dark.
