The sun rose over the ridge in a shroud of pale and heat-sickened light and the orchard lay beneath it like a graveyard of grey and gnarled things. The trees were ancient and their bark was etched with the deep and thirsty fissures of a long drought and the fruit hung small and bitter and hard within the canopy. Elias stood at the edge of the north grove and watched the heat begin to shimmer above the dust. He held the spade in hands that were mapped with dirt and calloused by a lifetime of the same repetitive and futile labors. There was no sound in the valley save for the dry rattle of the wind through the parched leaves and the distant, rhythmic thud of Caleb’s shovel somewhere toward the western boundary.

He walked toward the sound. The earth was baked hard as kiln-fired clay and the dust rose in small and choking clouds around his boots. He found his brother kneeled in a shallow trench, the sweat sheeting his back and turning the dust to a dark and heavy mud. Caleb did not look up. He was working a section of the poly-pipe that ran along the base of the old pear trees. The plastic was sun-bleached and brittle and it lay in the dirt like a discarded vein.

There is a break further down, Caleb said. His voice was low and raspy. Near the creek bed.

Elias stood looking down into the trench. The earth was scarred where the spade had bitten deep. It is the heat, Elias said. It shrinks the ground and pulls the couplings loose.

It is more than that, Caleb said. He sat back on his haunches and wiped his brow with a forearm. The ground is turning to ash. You can feel it. It is not holding.

The irrigation line is the only thing holding, Elias said.

Caleb looked up then. His eyes were bloodshot and set deep in a face that had grown gaunt in the summer months. The line is a fiction, he said. We are pumping water into a sieve. We are watering the dust.

Elias turned his gaze to the trees. The pear trees were skeletal and the leaves were curled like the burnt parchment of old manuscripts. He thought of his father standing in this same light thirty years ago, much the same way, looking at the same horizon with the same unyielding and terrible expectation.

The water will come, Elias said. The reservoir will fill.

The reservoir is a bowl of mud, Caleb said. The permits are gone. The state is drying us out. There is no water coming, Elias. There is only the drought and the debt.

He did not answer. He began to walk along the line, his boots heavy on the scorched earth. He traced the path of the pipe, following it through the rows of trees that stood like silent and indifferent sentinels. He could see where the earth had subsided, where the weight of the dry soil had crushed the plastic into the silt. Each break was a wound. Each leak was a slow hemorrhage of the family’s remaining life.

He reached the section near the creek bed where the ground fell away into a shallow and sandy gully. There, the pipe was split wide, a jagged rupture that wept a thin and pathetic trickle of water into the thirsty sand. The water did not pool. It did not soak. It was simply swallowed by the earth, vanished instantly into the insatiable maw of the drought.

Caleb appeared behind him, moving with a slow and labored gait. He carried a roll of heavy black tape and a wrench. He knelt by the rupture and began to clean the edges of the split with a rag.

The man from the valley came by the house yesterday, Caleb said.

Elias watched the trickle of water. The man from the valley.

He was in the truck, Caleb said. He did not even get out. He just leaned out the window and spoke. He spoke about the acreage. He spoke about the development rights.

Elias felt a coldness in his chest that had nothing to do with the shade. The developer.

He offered a number, Caleb said. A number that would settle the bank. A number that would pay for the equipment and the new well and the rest of it. He said they would pave the orchard. They would build the houses with the wide porches and the manicured lawns. They would bring the water back. They would bring the water from the north.

The orchard is not for paving, Elias said.

It is a corpse, Caleb said. We are just tending the decay. Look at it, Elias. Look at the trees. They are dead. They are only waiting for the wind to knock them down.

The trees are dormant, Elias said.

They are dying, Caleb said.

He gripped the wrench so tightly his knuckles were white and the metal groaned. Elias looked at his brother’s hands. They were his father’s hands. The same thickness of the fingers, the same scarring at the base of the thumb. To see those hands tending to the end of the land felt like a betrayal of the very blood that flowed through them.

The land belongs to us, Elias said.

The land belongs to the drought, Caleb said. The land belongs to whoever can pay the taxes. We are squatters on a dying dream.

He turned away from his brother and began to work on the pipe. He wrapped the black tape around the rupture with a frantic and precise energy. The plastic hissed as the pressure of the line pressed against the new seal. Elias watched him. He felt the weight of the sun upon his neck, a physical pressure that seemed to push him down into the dust. The heat was an entity, a vast and mindless force that moved through the valley, consuming everything in its path. It dided the moisture from the leaves, it sucked the marrow from the bones, it eroded the very memory of the rain.

The work continued in a heavy and rhythmic silence. The sun climbed toward its zenith and the light turned a harsh and bleached white. The shadows shrank beneath the trees. Every movement was a struggle against the thickening air. They worked on the next break, then the next, moving down the line like mourners processing toward a grave.

There is a life beyond the fence, Caleb said, his voice breaking the stillness of the afternoon.

Elias did not look up from the trench.

He spoke of the city. He spoke of the work in the mills. He spoke of a place where the ground does not break under your feet every time you try to stand. He said he could not stay here and watch the world turn to dust.

You have a responsibility, Elias said.

To what? To a pile of stones and a handful of dead wood? To a name that no one remembers?

The name is in the soil, Elias said.

The soil is leaving us, Caleb said.

He stopped his work and stood up. He was covered in a fine grey grit. He looked toward the house, a low and slumped structure that sat at the edge of the orchard, its white paint peeling like dead skin. The windows were dark and vacant.

The house is empty, Caleb said. The rooms are full of dust. Even the air in there feels old. Like it is waiting to be buried.

Elias reached into the trench and felt the cool, damp earth near the pipe. It was a small, fleeting sensation. A ghost of moisture. He thought of the years when the orchard had been a lush and verdured thing, when the scent of blossoms had hung heavy and sweet in the June air, and the sound of the water in the channels had been a constant and musical presence. That orchard was gone. It was a memory of a memory.

If we sell, the orchard is gone forever, Elias said.

It is already gone, Caleb said. We are just the last ones to realize it.

He sat back down and resumed the task of the tape and the wrench. The argument had reached its terminus. There was nothing left to say that the heat would not swallow. The sun beat down upon them with a blind and indifferent fury. The trees stood in their rows, gnarled and silent, bearing witness to the slow and agonizing dissolution of their world.

The afternoon waned and the light began to stretch and yellow across the valley. The shadows of the trees grew long and distorted, reaching out across the dirt like the fingers of the drowning. The work was not finished. There were more breaks in the line, more places where the earth had failed, more leaks to be stemmed.

They moved slowly now, their movements heavy with exhaustion. The shadows merged into a deep and bruised purple. The temperature did not drop so much as it shifted, moving from the searing heat of the day to a heavy and suffocating warmth that clung to the skin.

Elias found himself looking toward the ridge. The mountains were a dark and jagged silhouette against the fading sky. They were ancient and unmoved by the struggle in the valley. They had seen the forests rise and they would see them fall. They had seen the water come and they would see it vanish.

Caleb was ahead of him, a dark shape moving through the dimming rows. He was walking toward the house.

We will talk in the morning, Elias said, though he knew the words were hollow.

Caleb did not stop. He did not turn. He only continued his progress toward the house, leaving the broken line and the dying trees to the encroaching night.

Elias remained in the orchard. He stood by the ruptured pipe, which was now leaking only a minute and rhythmic drip into the dark and thirsty earth. He watched the last of the light depart from the sky. The orchard was a place of shadows now, a landscape of obscured shapes and uncertain boundaries. He felt the vastness of the silence around him, a silence that was not merely the absence of sound but a presence in itself, heavy and profound and eternal. He thought of the developer and the houses and the paved roads and the manicured lawns. He thought of the water brought from the north. He thought of the end of the lineage.

He reached down and touched the dry and cracked earth. It was cold now, or perhaps it was only his fingers that had lost their heat. He stayed there in the dark, a solitary figure amidst the ruins of a legacy, while above him the stars began to pierce the shroud of the night, bright and distant and utterly indifferent to the fate of the land or the men who had tried to hold it.
