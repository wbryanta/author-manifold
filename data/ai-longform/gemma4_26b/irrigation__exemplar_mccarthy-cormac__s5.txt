The sun rose over the orchard in a wash of pale and caustic light and the heat began its slow ascent from the scorched earth long before the shadows had retreated from the base of the trees. The almond trees stood like grey sentinists in the dust, their limbs twisted and brittle, the bark flaking away in parched scales. There was no green to be seen in the grove, only the muted tones of decay and the pervasive, fine grit of the valley that coated everything in a shroud of silt. Elias knelt in the shallow trench, his hands deep in the dry clay, working a length of heavy poly-pipe into the broken coupling. His movements were slow and rhythmic and possessed a certain grim mechanical precision.

His brother, Silas, stood above him, leaning on a spade. The sweat had already begun to track through the dust on Silas’s neck, carving pale lines in the grime. He watched the work with eyes that were hooded and restless.

The leak is getting worse, Silas said.

Elias did not look up. He tightened a clamp with a wrench, the metal protesting with a dry, screeching sound. It is the heat. The ground is shrinking. Everything is pulling apart.

It is the drought, Silas said. It is the end of it. There is no more water to be had in this well.

Elias paused. He wiped his forehead with the back of a gloved hand, leaving a smear of dark mud across his brow. We have the permit for the deeper bore. We talked about this.

We talked about a dream, Silas said. We did not talk about the cost. The cost is eating us. It is eating the house and it is eating the trucks and it all goes into the dirt and for what. For a grove of dead wood.

The man stood and pushed the spade into the earth. The sound of the blade biting into the parched soil was a dull, hollow thud. He looked out across the rows of trees, toward the far edge of the property where the hills rose up in a jagged and scorched silhouette against the white sky.

It is not dead yet, Elias said.

It is a corpse, Silas said. We are just tending the grave.

The brothers worked in a silence that was heavy and thick, punctuated only by the rhythmic clatter of tools and the distant, lonely cry of a hawk circling the thermal currents above. The heat grew more oppressive, a physical weight that pressed against the lungs. The air tasted of dust and old copper. 

Elias began to lay the pipe further down the line, his muscles aching with the effort of the heavy plastic. He moved with a dogged persistence, a refusal to acknowledge the widening rift between himself and the man standing above him. He thought of the maps in the farmhouse, the old surveys with their faded ink and the names of men long dead, and he thought of the way his father’s hands had looked when they rested on the same heavy iron valves—calloused and stained with the same grease and the same permanence.

You could go to the city, Elias said, his voice low. You could take the job in Fresno.

And leave you to rot in this dust, Silas said.

I am not rotting. I am working.

You are digging a hole for yourself, Silas said. There is a man. A developer from the coast. He came by the station while you were out here.

Elias stopped. He sat back on his heels, the pipe resting in the dirt like a dead serpent. What man.

A man with a suit that cost more than this entire acre, Silas said. He spoke of orchards. He spoke of vineyards. He spoke of things that do not require the mercy of a failing well. He spoke of a future where the ground is not a burden.

Elias looked at his hands. They were cracked and bleeding in the fine lines of his palms. The blood was dark, almost black in the noon light. The words he spoke were quiet.

The land is not a commodity, Silas.

To the man in the suit, it is exactly that, Silas said. He stepped down into the trench, his boots disturbing the loose earth. It is a ledger. It is a balance of debt and credit. And the credit is gone, Elias. The credit has been gone for years.

The brothers sat in the heat. The sun was a white and sightless eye in the center of the sky. A light wind stirred the dust, but it brought no relief, only the movement of the grit from one place to another.

The trees were once something, Elias said. 

They were something, Silas agreed. They were a legacy. But a legacy that kills you is a curse.

Elias picked up the wrench. He began to work again, his movements more frantic now, a desperate attempt to reassert order over the encroaching chaos of the drought. He tightened the bolts until his knuckles whitened. He cinched the seals until the plastic groaned. He labored as if the very act of repair could stave off the inevitable, as if the integrity of the pipe could somehow mend the fracturing of the world.

The irrigation line stretched out into the distance, a black vein running through the grey and broken landscape, carrying a meager and precious flow of water to a thirsty and dying host. The water trickled through the cracks in the earth, disappearing into the thirsty dust before it could even reach the roots.

The shadows began to lengthen across the orchard, stretching out like long, thin fingers reaching for the dark. The heat did not leave with the sun; it merely settled, becoming a more permanent and brooding presence in the cooling air. They finished the repair in the twilight, their bodies heavy with fatigue and the silent weight of the argument. 

They walked back toward the house, two figures moving through the gloom of the orchard, their silhouettes indistinguishable from the twisted and skeletal limbs of the trees they tended. Behind them, the pumps continued their rhythmic and lonely throb, drawing the last of the deep, cold water from the earth, pumping it into a desert that had forgotten the meaning of rain. The moon rose, a pale and indifferent disc, casting a light that turned the dust to silver and the dying trees to ghosts, and the land lay waiting in the dark, as it always had, and as it always would.
