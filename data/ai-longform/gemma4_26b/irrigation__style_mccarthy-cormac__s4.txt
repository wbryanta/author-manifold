The sun sat upon the valley like a weight of hammered copper and the heat rose from the parched earth in visible shivers. There was no wind. The orchard lay in a heavy and breathless stillness where the trees stood like a congregation of the dead, their limbs grey and stripped of leaf, their bark fissured by the long drought. Elias knelt in the dust of the furrow, his hands coated in a fine and ochre grit that worked its way into the deep creases of his skin. He held the heavy wrench against a rusted coupling, his muscles tensed against the stubbornness of the metal.

Caleb stood a few feet off, leaning on the handle of a spade. His face was darkened by the sun and his eyes were squinted against the glare of the white sky. He watched his brother work with a look that was not quite pity but a kind of exhausted detachment.

You can tighten it all you want, Caleb said.

Elias did not look up. He wrenched the tool. The metal groaned and a small spurt of muddy water escaped the seam, hissing as it hit the hot dirt.

It’ll hold, Elias said.

It won't hold the summer. The pressure is too high and the pipe is too old. It’s all too old.

Elias wiped his forehead with the back of a muddy forearm, leaving a dark streak across his brow. He took up a length of heavy black rubber and began to wrap it around the fracture, his movements slow and methodical. The heat was a physical presence, a pressure against the eardrums.

The trees are dying, Caleb said. They’ve been dying since the spring. There is no sense in patchwork.

Elias pulled the binding tight. He cinched it with a knot that required the full strength of his shoulders. He sat back on his heels, breathing hard, the air tasting of dust and dry rot.

There is sense in doing the work, he said.

The work is a way of pretending, Caleb said. There is no more water coming. The well is dropping every week. You can see it in the gauges.

Elias looked out over the rows of trees. They stretched away toward the foothills, a skeletal army marching into the heat. The mountains in the distance were blurred by the heat haze, a pale and ghostly blue. He thought of the orchard as it had been in his father's time, the heavy scent of blossom and the deep, dark green of the leaves, but that memory felt like something stolen from a different life.

The man in town, the one from the developers, he called again yesterday, Caleb said. He was staying at the motel. He spoke like he was doing us a kindness.

Elias did not answer. He picked up a trowel and began to pack the earth back around the repaired section of the pipe.

He’s offering a fair price, Caleb said. More than the land is worth in crops. He’s offering what it’s worth in real estate. We could move. We could go north.

There is nowhere to go, Elias said.

There is anywhere.

Elias stopped digging. He looked up at his brother. The younger man’s shirt was soaked with sweat, clinging to his thin frame. He looked like a stranger in this landscape, a man unmoored from the soil.

We are here, Elias said.

We are lingering, Caleb said. Like the rot.

The silence returned, heavy and profound. A crow circled far above, a black speck against the furnace-white sky, and its lonely cry drifted down through the heat, thin and inconsequential. Elias turned back to the trench. He worked the earth with a grim persistence, smoothing the mound, trying to seal the moisture in, trying to protect the small, fragile line of life he had just mended.

Caleb watched him for a long time. The spade remained upright in the dirt between his feet.

He says they want to put in a vineyard, Caleb said. High-end. They’s bringing in irrigation from the river. They’ll bring the water back.

The river is a trickle of silt, Elias said.

They’ll build the infrastructure. They have the capital. We don't. We are just two men holding onto a graveyard.

Elias felt a sudden, sharp spike of anger, a heat that had nothing to do with the sun. He stood up, his knees cracking in the silence. He was larger than Caleb, his frame thickened by decades of labor, his skin leathery and cured by the elements. He looked down at his brother, and in the harsh light, his eyes seemed dark and hollowed out.

This is the land, Elias said. It is the only thing that is real. Everything else is just a way of passing the time.

And what is the use of the real if it is only death? Caleb asked.

The use is the trying, Elias said.

Caleb shook his and turned his gaze toward the house, a low, sagging structure of weathered wood that sat at the edge of the orchard. The windows were dark and vacant.

The house is falling down too, Caleb said. The roof is bowing. The foundation is settling. Even the wood is tired, Eli.

Elias looked at the house. He saw the decay, the way the shadows pooled in the eaves, the way the porch steps had begun to pull away from the earth. He saw it all. But he also saw the ghosts of the hands that had built it, and the weight of the names carved into the lintel.

I am not leaving, Elias said.

Then you are staying to watch it burn, Caleb said.

Caleb turned and began to walk toward the house, his boots kicking up small puffs of dust that hung suspended in the stagnant air. Elias watched him go. He watched the slumped shoulders and the way the heat seemed to swallow the man as he moved through the rows.

Elias returned to the pipe. He checked the binding one last time. He pressed his hand against the cold, black rubber, feeling for the pulse of the water underneath. There was a slight vibration, a rhythmic thrumming that spoke of the struggle, of the pressure of the earth against the flow. He sat in the dirt and stayed there, a solitary figure in a landscape of bone and dust, waiting for the sun to descend, waiting for the night that would bring no relief, only a different kind of shadow.

The afternoon progressed with a slow and agonizing constancy. The sun did not move so much as it bore down, a relentless and unblinking eye. The heat grew dense, almost liquid, making the very air feel heavy in the lungs. Elias moved through the remaining leaks in the line, a grim procession of repairs. Each one required the same effort, the same bracing of the spine, the same cursing of the rusted fittings and the stubborn, unyielding earth.

By the time he reached the far end of the north grove, his hands were numb. The grime had been ground so deeply into his flesh that it felt like a part of him, an extra layer of hide. He sat in the shade of a particularly stunted peach tree, the only respite in the scorched expanse. The shade was meager, a thin and broken veil of grey.

He thought of the man from the development company. He thought of the way Caleb had spoken of the north, of a place where the rain was a regular visitor and the soil was dark and rich and untroubled by the memory of drought. It sounded like a myth, a story told to children to keep them from fearing the dark. To Elias, the north was a phantom, an abstraction that had no place in the visceral reality of the grit and the heat.

He heard the sound of footsteps before he saw Caleb. The younger man was returning, carrying a canteen and a small tin of salted meat. He moved with a lethargy that mirrored the landscape.

Eat something, Caleb said, dropping the supplies in the dust near Elias.

Elias shook his head.

You haven't eaten since dawn, Caleb said. You're going to collapse.

I'm fine, Elias said.

You're stubborn. There is a difference.

Elias reached for the canteen. The water was lukewarm and tasted of the plastic and the sun, but he drank it greedily. The coolness was a fleeting mercy.

The man called again, Caleb said, his voice low. He was at the diner in town. He told me the offer expires at the end of the month.

Elias stared at the dry earth. He watched an ant struggle across a crack in the soil, its tiny body buffeted by the heat.

Let it expire, Elias said.

He's not a bad man, Caleb said. He's just a businessman. He sees the math. He sees the deficit. He sees what we are.

And what are we, Caleb? Elias asked.

We are people who are wasting our lives on a corpse, Caleb said.

Elias stood, his joints protesting. He felt a sudden, frantic need to move, to work, to bury the thought of the sale beneath more layers of earth and more tightened bolts. He picked up the wrench.

The trees will come back, Elias said. If we get the water right. If we can bridge the gap to the next season.

The next season is just more of this, Caleb said. Look around you, Eli. There is no more 'next.' There is only the end.

Elias walked past him, heading toward the next broken section of the line. He did not look back. He could feel Caleb’s gaze on his back, a heavy and accusing weight.

The sun began its slow descent, turning the sky into a bruised expanse of orange and deep, dusty violet. The shadows lengthened, stretching across the orchard like the fingers of a drowning man. The heat remained, though it had lost its sharp, piercing edge, settling instead into a thick and suffocating blanket.

Elias worked by the fading light. His movements were more erratic now, driven by a frantic, unconscious energy. He patched, he tightened, he dug, and he packed. He was building a dam against the tide of time.

As the first stars began to prick through the deepening gloom, he finished the last repair near the edge of the orchard. He stood in the twilight, his body trembling with an exhaustion that went deeper than the muscle. The orchard was silent, save for the rhythmic, subterranean pulse of the water moving through the repaired lines.

He saw the light in the house. A single, yellow glow in the window, casting a long and lonely streak across the yard. Caleb was inside, probably sitting at the table, looking at papers, looking at maps, looking at the future.

Elias turned his eyes to the mountains. They were black silhouettes now, massive and indifferent, marking the boundary of his world. He felt the vastness of the desert pressing in from all sides, the immense and ancient void that waited for the last of the trees to fail, for the last of the water to vanish, for the last of them to be gone.

He picked up his tools, the metal cold and heavy in his hands. He began the long walk back toward the house, his boots treading softly on the cooling dust. Behind him, the orchard lay in the dark, a graveyard of silvered wood and silent roots, waiting for the sun to rise and begin the slow, inexorable process of erasure once again. He did not look back at the broken lines or the dying trees. He only looked ahead, at the small, flickering light in the darkness, and the heavy, unyielding earth beneath his feet.
