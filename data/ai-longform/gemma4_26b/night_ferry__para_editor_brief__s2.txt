The vibration of the engine was a constant, low-frequency ache that seemed to have migrated from the deck of the *Spero* into Elias’s own marrow. It was a rhythmic, grinding thing, a heavy percussion that paced the slow progress of the ferry across the Skagerrak. Outside the thick, salt-crusted windows of the passenger lounge, there was nothing but the dark. The sea was not black, not exactly; it was a bruised, impenetrable violet, visible only where the ship’s prow sliced through it, churning up a white, frothing wake that vanished almost as soon as it was born.

Elias sat in a corner booth, the vinyl of the seat cracked and cold against his thighs. He had been traveling for six hours, and the sensation of being suspended between two shores—between the life he had left in the city and the grim uncertainty of the port ahead—had begun to settle into a state of profound dissociation. He felt less like a man on a journey and as more like a ghost haunting his own transit.

On the laminate table before him sat a paper cup of coffee, long since gone cold, a thin skin of oil shimmering on its surface. He watched the reflection of the overhead fluorescent light in that oil. It flickered, a rhythmic stutter that coincided with the heavy thud of the hull hitting the swells.

He thought of Caleb. He thought of him not as a man, but as a series of increasingly violent disruptions. Caleb had always been a rupture in the fabric of things. When they were children, he was the sudden crack in the windowpane, the unexpected bruise on a knee, the loud, unbidden shout in a quiet room. As they grew, the ruptures had become more structural. The estrangement hadn't been a single event, a dramatic severance, but rather a slow erosion of shared language. There had been years of unreturned calls, of brief, biting exchanges during holidays that felt more like ceasefires than reunions, until finally, the silence had become the only thing they truly shared.

And then, the call. Not from Caleb, but from a woman in a coastal town Elias had never visited, her voice trembling with a professional, detached sort of grief. She had spoken of an accident, of a drift, of a body recovered near the cliffs. She had used the word *possible*.

*It is possible the identification can be made through dental records or DNA, but a visual confirmation would be preferred.*

The word *possible* sat in Elias’s mind like a piece of grit in an eye. It was the uncertainty that was the cruelty of it. If it were definitely Caleb, there would be a terrible, heavy finality—a weight he could eventually learn to carry. But the possibility of it *not* being him offered a treacherous, shameful hope. It allowed for a version of reality where his brother was still out there, somewhere, lost in the margin of the world, still breathing, still capable of being the person Elias had once known.

He closed his-eyes, but the darkness behind his eyelids was no different from the darkness of the strait. He could still feel the ship. He could still feel the heavy, wet cold of the North.

He remembered the last time he had seen Caleb. It had been three years ago, in a cramped, dimly lit apartment that smelled of stale tobacco and damp wood. They had been arguing about something inconsequential—the ownership of an old watch, perhaps, or a slight remark about a job—but the subtext had been the entire history of their resentment. Caleb had been leaning against a radiator, his face shadowed, looking older than his thirty-four years. He had looked at Elias with a kind of weary contempt, as if Elias’s stability was a personal insult to his own chaos.

"You've become so careful, Eli," Caleb had said, his voice raspy. "You've trimmed all the edges off yourself. There’s nothing left to grab onto."

"There's nothing left to break, either," Elias had replied.

That had been the end of it. A quiet exit, a closing of the door. No slamming, no grand gesture. Just the slow, cooling embers of a relationship.

A sudden, sharp jolt sent the coffee cup sliding across the table. Elias startled, his heart hammering against his ribs. The ferry had hit a particularly large swell, or perhaps something had drifted in the path of the hull. The lights in the lounge flickered and dimmed, leaving the room in a murky, underwater gloom.

He looked around the lounge. There were others, though they were mostly shadows. A woman in a heavy wool coat sat several rows away, her head bowed over a book she hadn't turned the page of in an hour. An older man was slumped in a booth near the galley, his breathing heavy and rhythmic, a soft, wheezing sound that competed with the engine's drone. They were all part of this transit, part of this liminal state, passengers in a purgatory of salt and steel. None of them knew that he was carrying a death sentence in his pocket, or at least the possibility of one.

The fog began to roll in then, thick and suffocating. It swallowed the wake, swallowed the horizon, until the ship seemed to be floating in a void. The world had shrunk to the dimensions of the *Spero*. The engine's thrum seemed to grow louder, more intimate, as if the ship were breathing directly into his ear.

He found himself thinking about the physics of the sea. How much pressure it took to crush a lung, how much salt it took to preserve a wound. He thought about the body—the anonymous shape in the morgue, wrapped in plastic, waiting for a stranger to grant it a name. The indignity of it. To be reduced to a set of characteristics, a height, a hair color, a dental history. To wait in the dark for a brother to recognize a face that had once been his own.

The shame of his hope returned, more biting than the cold. He wanted it not to be him. He wanted to arrive at the pier, to walk through the rain-slicked streets of Kalsund, and to find that the news had been an error, a mistake of geography or timing. He wanted to be able to go home to his quiet, edge-less life and pretend that the rupture had never happened, that the silence between them was merely a period of waiting rather than a permanent state of being.

But the ship was moving forward. The engine would not turn back. The geography of his life was being forced toward a single, unavoidable point.

As the hours bled into one another, the light began to change. The impenetrable violet of the sea faded into a bruised, sickly grey. The fog did not lift so much as it thinned, becoming a translucent shroud that obscured the details of the world but allowed the shapes of the land to emerge. Low, jagged silhouettes of cliffs and pine trees began to haunt the periphery of his vision.

The ferry began to slow. The heavy, rhythmic thudding of the engine shifted, a downward modulation in pitch, as the ship prepared for the approach to the harbor. The grinding of the gears was a harsh, mechanical protest against the silence of the morning.

Elias stood up. His legs felt stiff, as if they were turning to stone. He gathered his things—his coat, his wallet, the small, crumpled piece of paper with the address of the clinic. He felt a strange, sudden vertigo, as if the floor of the ship were tilting away from him.

The arrival was not a moment of clarity, but of mounting dread. The lights of the port began to flicker into existence, small, lonely amber sparks against the grey morning. The pier loomed out of the mist, a heavy, industrial limb reaching into the water. The sounds of the harbor—the low moan of a foghorn, the clanking of metal against metal, the cry of a single, scavenging gull—drifted across the water, sounding hollow and distant.

As the *Spero* nudged against the rubber bumpers of the dock, the sudden stillness was more unsettling than the motion had been. The engine died, leaving a vacuum of sound that felt like a physical pressure against his eardrums.

He walked through the gangway, his boots echoing on the metal plating. The air that met him as he stepped onto the pier was brutally cold, tasting of diesel and rotting kelp. It bit into his lungs, a sharp, antiseptic chill.

The harbor was deserted, save for a few figures moving in the shadows of the shipping containers. The town of Kalsund lay beyond the docks, a cluster of dark, huddled buildings that seemed to be huddbled together against the encroaching dawn.

Elias stood on the pier for a long moment, watching the ferry disembark the remaining passengers. He watched the shadows move, the heavy bags being hauled onto the concrete, the silent, efficient work of the dockhands. He felt a profound sense of isolation, as if he were the only person in the world who was truly awake, the only one aware of the gravity of the morning.

He began to walk. The pavement was slick with a fine, freezing mist. His footsteps were the only sound in the hollow quiet of the port. Each step felt heavy, an immense effort of will, as he moved away from the sea and toward the town.

The streets were narrow, winding between buildings that seemed to lean inward, as if listening to his approach. The light was increasing, a pale, watery grey that drained the color from everything it touched. The world was rendered in shades of slate, charcoal, and ash.

He found himself looking at the faces of the few people he passed—a man pulling a cart, a woman wrapped in a heavy shawl—searching for some sign, some cosmic clue that might tell him what awaited him. But there was nothing. There was only the indifferent, rhythmic movement of a world continuing its course.

He reached the outskirts of the clinic, a small, utilitarian structure of concrete and glass that sat on the edge of the residential district. It looked like a place where nothing of importance ever happened, a place of routine and sterile procedure.

He stopped in front of the heavy, metal door. He could see his own reflection in the glass—a pale, blurred shape, eyes hollowed by sleeplessness, a man stripped of his edges, much like the life he had once accused his brother of leaving behind.

He reached for the handle. The coldness of the metal seeped through his glove, a sharp, stinging reminder of the reality of the moment. He took a breath, the freezing air catching in his throat, and pulled. The door opened with a low, mechanical click, admitting him into the quiet, fluorescent-lit interior, leaving the grey, uncertain world of the morning behind.
