# The Crossing

The letter arrived on a Tuesday, forwarded three times through addresses he'd tried to forget. It was printed on the kind of institutional letterhead that made his stomach contract, and it contained only the essential facts: a body had been found in the waters near the northern port, and based on identifying markers—a distinctive scar, some dental work, a tattoo—they believed it might belong to his brother, David. They needed someone to confirm. The letter was dated four days prior.

Marcus sat in his kitchen for an hour, the paper flat on the table in front of him, reading it again though the words never changed. His coffee went cold. He thought about not going, about calling them and saying they must have made a mistake, that David was somewhere else, that he didn't want to know. But he knew, in that settled way of knowing that bypasses hope, that he had to see.

The ferry departed at nine in the evening. He'd booked a cabin and driven to the port that afternoon, parking in the lot adjacent to the terminal where other cars sat abandoned, their owners already aboard or waiting in the cafeteria. The light was already failing, though it was only late October, and the water between the mainland and the northern islands had the color of old slate.

He sat in the cabin for two hours before departure, on the narrow bed with its thin mattress, listening to the sounds of the ship: workers shouting to each other on the dock, metal clanging, the distant hum of engines preparing themselves. A young couple moved into the cabin next door; he heard them laughing, then the sound of water running in pipes, then their television through the thin wall. He was grateful for the noise. It made him feel less alone.

When the ship pulled away from the dock, he felt it in his body before anything else—a subtle dropping sensation, as though gravity had shifted. He went up to the deck and stood at the railing, watching the lights of the port city recede. The water was dark and moved with a slow, deliberate motion that suggested depths he didn't want to think about. This was how David had been found, or this was where, or this was roughly where. The letter hadn't specified exactly. It had said "recovered from the strait" but nothing about how or when.

They hadn't spoken in eleven years. It wasn't the kind of estrangement that carried dramatic weight—no great betrayal or ideological rupture, nothing that would have made for a good story. Instead, it was the small, ordinary kind that accumulated from indifference and geography, from the fact that when David moved north, Marcus stayed south, and when they tried to maintain the connection across that distance, they found they no longer knew how. Their mother had died when David was twenty-three and Marcus twenty-six, and that event, which should have bound them together, had somehow driven them further apart, as though grief had made them strangers to each other. In the years afterward, Marcus had built a life in the lower province—an unremarkable life, marked by an unremarkable career, an unremarkable apartment, unremarkable routines. He didn't date seriously. He didn't maintain many friendships. He worked in insurance, assessing claims, determining what was worth paying for and what wasn't.

He'd heard about David's life only in fragments, through the occasional email, through what their mother's sister had mentioned when they'd run into each other at a grocery store several years ago. David had worked construction, had lived near the coast, had never married. The last email had come four years ago and contained only a photo of a house he'd helped renovate, a beautiful structure with a view of the water, and the single line: "It's turning out nice."

Marcus had never responded.

The cabin felt smaller as the night deepened. He lay in the dark, fully clothed under the duvet, listening to the ship's movement. It was rougher now, the swells more pronounced, and the boat creaked in a way that suggested it was working. He wondered if David had been awake when it happened, if it had been quick, if there'd been any moment in which he understood. He wondered if David had thought of him. He wondered if David had thought of him in all the years since their mother's funeral, if he'd ever wondered what Marcus was doing, if he'd considered reaching out as much as Marcus had considered reaching out and then not done it.

Around midnight he got up and went to the small porthole. The moon was almost full and illuminated the water in a ghostly way, the waves appearing as dark, moving shapes beneath a skin of light. Somewhere out there, in some corridor of this strait, David's body had been found. It must have been in the water for a while, long enough for things to happen to it, for it to be damaged in ways that would require dental records and scars to identify. He thought about the specific details he'd always remembered about David's body: the long scar on his left forearm from falling into broken glass when he was twelve, the tattoo on his shoulder that he'd gotten when he was nineteen—a raven, poorly executed by a friend, the lines wavering slightly. These markers, which had been so specific and so particular to David, to his individual existence, were now the details that would prove he was dead.

Toward dawn, Marcus slept fitfully, his dreams fragmented and strange, full of water and searching and his brother's voice calling from somewhere he couldn't reach. When he woke, dawn was just breaking, the sky a pale gray that was almost white. They would be arriving soon. He showered in the narrow stall, the water surprisingly hot, and dressed in clean clothes. He took a small bottle of antacid from his bag and swallowed it without water, needing the familiar chalky taste.

The port town materialized gradually as the ship approached, not dramatically but slowly, buildings and docks and cranes becoming distinct from the gray background. It was larger than he'd expected, more developed. He wondered how many times David had seen this view, if he'd grown accustomed to it, if it had become invisible to him through repetition.

A woman with a clipboard was waiting for him at the terminal. She was thin and professional, probably in her fifties, dressed in clothing that suggested efficiency rather than comfort. She introduced herself as Dr. Sarah Leclerc and said she was the coroner, or the pathologist, or something—he didn't quite catch the title because he was focused on the fact that she was real and the situation was real and there was no longer any possibility of it being a mistake.

"How long has he been in the water?" he asked as they walked to her car.

"Best estimate is approximately three weeks," she said. "The body was found ten days ago. Given the temperature of the water and the current patterns, we think death occurred sometime in late September."

Late September. He tried to think what he'd been doing in late September. He'd been at work, probably. He'd eaten lunch at his desk, as he did most days. He'd gone home and watched television. While David was dying, Marcus was living his small, contained life, unaware that anything significant was happening. He wondered if the universe was supposed to register such moments, if there should be some atmospheric shift, some indication that someone you were bound to by blood was no longer alive.

"I should tell you what to expect," Dr. Leclerc said as she drove. The town was small enough that its center gave way to residential streets within a few minutes. "The body has been in the water. There will have been decomposition, some predation by marine life, damage from rocks and currents. We've had to rely quite heavily on the identifying characteristics. Dental records and the physical markers match the missing person description you provided to the police."

"David," Marcus said. "His name is David."

"Yes," she said. "David."

The morgue was in a hospital, of course, and they had to go through several corridors, down stairs, through doors that required key cards. It was all very institutional and sterile, and Marcus found that the normalcy of it was somehow worse than if it had been dramatic. They were just moving through a regular building, doing a regular, if morbid, job.

In a small room with very bright lights and a cold that seemed to emanate from the very walls, they prepared him. Dr. Leclerc explained that they would show him the face, that they would not uncover more than that unless he requested it. He nodded. His mouth had gone dry.

When they pulled back the sheet, Marcus found himself looking at a face that was both familiar and not. The features were bloated and discolored, and the eyes were closed, sunken slightly. There was a wound on the left side of the head, dark and ragged. But underneath the damage and the water damage and the time, there was something that was undeniably David. The shape of the forehead, the line of the jaw. He could see their mother in the structure of the face, the particular arrangement of features that she'd passed down and that he carried too, though in different proportion.

"Is this your brother?" Dr. Leclerc asked gently.

Marcus nodded. He couldn't speak.

"Are you certain?"

He nodded again. He wanted to say something, to articulate in some way the experience of recognizing someone you'd loved and hadn't spoken to in eleven years in the face of a corpse, but there were no words for it. It was like looking at a photograph from a time before a war had happened, before choices had been made that could never be unmade. It was looking at a possibility that was now extinguished, though he hadn't even known it was still possible.

"We still need to complete some tests," Dr. Leclerc said. "But you've confirmed the identification. That helps us. Were you close?"

The question was asked professionally, but it landed like an accusation. Had they been close? Once, when they were children, David had punched another boy in school for making fun of Marcus's stutter. Marcus had gotten over the stutter eventually, but he'd never forgotten that moment, that raw and uncomplicated loyalty. But closeness was a long way from that, and they had lost their way to it.

"We were brothers," Marcus said.

She nodded, as though this answered her question, when it didn't answer anything.

He was given forms to sign and asked to provide information about David's next of kin, his employment, his known associates. Marcus filled out what he could, leaving blank the sections where he didn't know. When he got to the question about known relatives, he wrote his own name and address, then left the rest empty.

"Do you know where he was living?" Dr. Leclerc asked.

"No," Marcus said. "You'd have to look through his things, or ask around."

She nodded. "We'll find them. Someone always knows where someone else was living."

He was allowed to leave after that. There were services to arrange, but she said that could wait, that families usually wanted time. As if there were some normal timeline for processing death, for making arrangements, for learning how to be a person whose brother had just been identified on a coroner's table.

He went to a café near the hospital and ordered coffee that he didn't drink. The windows looked out onto a street where people were going about their normal lives—buying groceries, walking dogs, going to work. None of them knew that David was dead. None of them would ever know, or if they did, it would be the smallest possible fact, a brief mention in a news report or something heard secondhand. He thought about how David had lived here, had probably walked these streets, had had a life that had been accumulating all these years while Marcus was elsewhere. He wondered if they had ever been in the same place at the same time without knowing it, if they had ever passed each other on the street.

The ferry back didn't leave until evening. He had most of the day to kill. He found a hotel and rented a room by the hour, not caring about the price. He lay on the bed and stared at the ceiling and tried not to think about David's face, the particular color of the water damage, the wound on the head. He wondered if it was suicide, if David had stood on a bridge or a cliff and decided that the water below was better than whatever life he was living. Or if it was an accident, if he'd gone out on a boat or been doing something to do with his construction work. The letter hadn't specified how he'd ended up in the water. It had only said that the cause of death was undetermined pending further investigation, and that they would contact him with the results.

He got up and went to the window. From his vantage point on the third floor, he could see the harbor. The ferry was there, preparing for evening departures. It was the same ship he'd arrived on, he realized, or another just like it. He was going to spend another night aboard it, crossing the strait in the opposite direction, returning to his life as if this had been a brief interruption, a small blip in the otherwise continuous sequence of ordinary days.

He thought about David at nineteen, getting a raven tattooed on his shoulder by a friend. What had he been thinking? What had seemed important then? Had he imagined the life he would have, living by the water, working with his hands, alone? Had he thought about his brother, or had Marcus already been peripheral to his thoughts, an absence that had become so complete it ceased to register as an absence at all?

On the ferry, Marcus kept to his cabin. He could hear the couple next door, but they were quiet, perhaps sleeping or perhaps just being considerate. He ate a sandwich from the cafeteria that tasted like cardboard and drank several small bottles of whiskey from the minibar. He didn't get drunk, exactly, but he achieved a state of numbness that he supposed was close enough.

In the dark, with the ship's motion beneath him, he thought about what comes after. There would be a funeral, probably, though he didn't know if David had any friends who would attend, or if it would just be Marcus and a minister and the sound of wind in a cemetery. He would have to arrange it, would have to make decisions about coffins and plots and flowers. He would have to notify their extended family, the aunts and cousins they'd lost touch with years ago. He would have to explain, again and again, that his brother was dead, and watch the surprise and sadness cross people's faces, and accept their condolences for a relationship that had been dead long before David's body ended up in the strait.

Somewhere in the deep part of the night, as the ship cut through the dark water, it occurred to him that they would never have the conversation he'd been not having for eleven years. They would never sit down and explain themselves to each other, never articulate the reasons for the silence, never have the chance to repair what had been broken or allowed to break. The possibility had existed this morning, had existed yesterday, had existed every day for the past eleven years. It had existed in the form of a phone call not made, a letter not written, a visit not taken. It had existed in the possibility of choosing differently, of reaching across the distance and saying something, anything, that might have begun the process of reconnection.

Now it didn't exist. Now there was only the fact of David's absence, the fact of his body in a drawer in a morgue, the fact that all the things that might have been said would remain forever unsaid.

Marcus stood on the deck in the hour before dawn, when the sky was just beginning to lighten. The water was vast and empty, and he stood looking out at it, thinking about the moment when David went in, or fell in, or decided to walk into it. He thought about the cold, the shock of it, the reflex of the body to survive. He thought about the point at which that reflex would have failed, at which David would have accepted what was happening and let himself sink. He thought about the absence that must come with that kind of letting go, the absolute absence of self that death presumably offers.

He thought about his own life, waiting for him on the other side of this journey. He thought about going back to his apartment, going back to work, going back to the quiet accumulation of days that had defined his existence for the past several years. He thought about whether it was possible to change that, to choose something different, to reach out to the people he'd let slip away, to rebuild connections that had frayed into nothing.

But mostly, he stood there and held onto the railing and watched the water lighten from black to gray, and felt the full weight of everything that could no longer be undone.
