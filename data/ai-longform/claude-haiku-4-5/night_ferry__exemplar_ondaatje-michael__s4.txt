# The Crossing

The ferry left at dusk from Thunder Bay, and Marcus stood on the upper deck watching the city recede into its own darkness. The October water had already begun its winter preparations, moving with a kind of urgency, as if it knew what was coming and wanted to accomplish all its turbulence before the ice arrived to still it. He held the letter in his jacket pocket unopened for the first hour, then for the second hour, then through most of the crossing itself, as though the un-read state of it kept all the uncertain things in his life suspended in a possibility that reading would destroy. The body was in Fort Frances. A man, they said. Found three weeks ago in a warehouse near the old lumber mill. No identification. Approximately forty-five years old. Dark hair, now graying. The constable's voice on the phone had been neither kind nor unkind, simply factual in the way that people become when they have delivered too much bad news to ever again soften their approach to it.

Marcus had not seen Thomas in seventeen years. There had been the letter in '85 after their mother's funeral, a few words written on paper that was already yellowing, the handwriting unfamiliar and careful as though Thomas had learned to write his own name from someone else's instructions. And then nothing. Nothing through the years when their father had his first heart attack and then the second, nothing through the decade of silence that had followed their father's death, nothing through the sale of the house or the division of property or any of the ordinary bureaucratic severances that death administers to a family. Their brother David, seven years younger than Thomas and four years younger than Marcus, had reported occasional sightings. Thomas in Vancouver. Thomas in the Kootenay region. Thomas at a farm outside Nelson. Thomas in a treatment center. Thomas working at a ski resort. Thomas. Thomas. Thomas. The name had become abstract through repetition, no longer attached to any particular body or face, but rather to a kind of absence that moved through the western provinces like weather, like rumor, like the kind of ghost story you hear and halfway believe because you need to believe something about the world, even if it is terrible.

The ferry's engine made a sound like something in pain, a steady thrumming that you felt in your sternum after the first hour and didn't really notice after the second. Marcus descended to the interior cabin around midnight, having exhausted the deck's particular form of companionship with the dark. The cabin smelled of diesel fuel and the peculiar soup of human proximity—the accumulated breath and perspiration of maybe forty people in a space designed for sixty. Most were sleeping. An older woman sat knitting near a window, her needles clicking in a rhythm that seemed to counterpoint the engine's drone. A teenager with headphones on was hunched over a laptop, his pale face illuminated from below like something from a science fiction film about the future being a place where nobody looked at anything except machines.

There was a small restaurant that served sandwiches and mediocre coffee. Marcus ordered both and sat with his back to the room, as people do when they want to disappear in public. He thought about the letter, still in his pocket. He had not opened it because the constable had said that, yes, they had found a letter addressed to him in a backpack in the warehouse. Personal effects. Clothing. A sleeping bag. Notebooks filled with what the constable described as "writing" in a way that suggested he had looked at a page or two and decided it was not a genre he could classify with confidence. The letter had been in an envelope with no postage, just Marcus's name written on the front in handwriting that could have belonged to anyone. That was the problem with not seeing someone for seventeen years. Their handwriting was the only thing you had, and that could change too, could become shaky or careful or damaged in ways you would never know about. You could study it and convince yourself of anything.

He pulled the letter out finally, around one in the morning, when the ferry seemed to have settled into a kind of maritime rhythm that felt almost like being back on solid ground, or at least like being so far from solid ground that the distinction no longer mattered. The envelope was cream-colored, expensive-looking in a way that suggested it had been stolen or found rather than purchased. He opened it slowly, aware that he was performing the gesture for no audience, and therefore, in a sense, truly alone with it.

*Marcus,*

*If you're reading this, then several things have happened in a sequence. I am probably not alive. Or I am alive but I've decided that you should think I'm not. The distinction, I've found, is not as meaningful as it once seemed. What matters is the doing and the undoing. I have spent so many years constructing myself—brick by brick, like a man building a wall in the dark—that I've forgotten what the wall was supposed to protect. So I tore it down. And now I'm standing in the rubble wondering if I was the wall or the thing the wall was meant to protect, or perhaps something else entirely. Third thing. A stranger that looks like both of us and neither of us.*

*You won't know me anymore. I barely know myself. But I wanted you to know that I remember. The summer when you taught me to swim in the river behind the farmhouse and I kept sinking because I didn't believe the water would hold me up. You kept saying it will, it will, and I kept saying no, and then one day I did believe it, just for a moment, and I swam. I swam maybe three feet and then I panicked and I sank, and you pulled me out, and we both lay on the rocks laughing so hard we couldn't breathe. I remember that. I don't remember much else clearly. But I remember that.*

*If it's me in the warehouse, and I suspect it is, then I want you to know I wasn't running from anything anymore. That came later, the running. At the beginning I was just running toward. Toward the mountains. Toward the river. Toward something I thought was more real than the life I was living. But that's a lie. Or it's true. I can't tell anymore.*

*When the constable calls, and he will, tell them yes. Tell them it's me. And then come up here if you can. Stay a few days. Look at the river. Look at how the light falls on the water at different times of day. It changes completely. You would think it's the same river, the same water, but it's not. It's never the same twice. I wanted you to see that.*

*—Thomas*

The letter had no date. The handwriting was shaking at points, then controlled, then shaking again, as though Thomas had written it over several days and not all at once, or as though his hands had simply betrayed him at intervals. Marcus read it three times sitting in the ferry's restaurant cabin, the sandwich untouched beside his coffee cup which had gone cold enough to be undrinkable. The teenage boy with the headphones glanced over at him once, then looked away quickly, as though he could sense that Marcus was engaged in something private and didn't want to intrude on it.

By four in the morning, they were close enough to Fort Frances that you could feel the change in the water, a different texture to the rocking, a sense that they were no longer in open crossing but approaching land, even if land was still invisible. Marcus went back to the deck. The sun was beginning its preliminary work on the eastern horizon, a kind of suggestion of light rather than light itself. There was a woman leaning on the railing, perhaps sixty years old, dressed in a heavy coat that had been expensive once. She had the look of someone who had been standing there for hours.

—Beautiful, isn't it, she said without turning to look at him.

—The darkness?

—The transition. When you're between things. It's the only time I ever feel like I know what's happening.

Marcus didn't respond. He was thinking about transitions, about how you could spend your whole life in them if you weren't careful, moving between states without ever arriving at one. He was thinking about Thomas in a warehouse in Fort Frances. He was thinking about a moment seventeen years ago when his brother had simply not come home one day, when their father had waited on the porch for three hours and then called the police, when the police had come and taken a report and written Thomas's description down on a form as though he were a missing animal or a piece of stolen property, something that could be tracked through the world if you knew the right techniques.

Fort Frances appeared suddenly, the town seeming to assemble itself out of the pre-dawn murk as though it had been waiting just beyond the visible world the whole time. The docks were empty except for a single uniformed man who was smoking a cigarette and reading a newspaper that was already a day old. This was Constable Weber, though you could tell it without being told, the way certain people announce their function simply by the way they stand. He greeted Marcus with a handshake that was neither warm nor cold, simply official in a way that made it clear this was a transaction.

—Body's at the medical examiner's, he said. —About ten minutes from here. They've kept it preserved. Waiting on identification.

—I read the letter, Marcus said.

Weber nodded. —Then you already know what you're going to tell me.

The medical examiner was a woman named Dr. Patricia Kwan who had the bearing of someone who had made peace with the fact that most of her conversations would be about death. She led Marcus down a corridor that smelled of chemicals and something underneath the chemicals that no amount of modern ventilation could quite eliminate. The body was behind a door marked with the number eight. She didn't ask if he was ready. People never were, and the asking was just prolonging things.

The body was covered with a sheet. Dr. Kwan pulled it back to the shoulders, revealing a face that was both familiar and entirely foreign. The skin had the waxy quality of preservation, but underneath you could see the structure of it, the bones that had emerged as the flesh had given up its purchase. It was Thomas's face and not Thomas's face. It was a question rather than a statement. It was what you would get if you asked a stranger to describe Thomas from memory and they did their best but not perfectly, getting most of the features right but the proportions slightly off, the spacing between the eyes not quite what it should be.

—Take your time, Dr. Kwan said.

But time was the one thing that made it worse. The longer Marcus looked at it, the less certain he became. There was something about the particular curve of the jaw, something about the shape of the forehead that seemed right, that seemed to contain the memory of Thomas. But there was also something that didn't match, something that his mind kept trying to reconcile and couldn't. The body bore a scar across its left ribs, a long ragged line of old tissue. Marcus didn't know if Thomas had that scar. He didn't know because he hadn't seen his brother shirtless in seventeen years, and before that he might not have been paying attention, might have been looking at something else entirely, as people do with their siblings, often not really looking at them at all because they're too familiar, too much like looking at your own hands, something you take for granted and don't actually observe.

—There's a scar, Dr. Kwan was saying. —The medical examiner here found it during the initial examination. No medical records for anyone named Thomas Garrett in the system, which doesn't mean much. A lot of people move around, don't get regular care. The scar is old. Ten, fifteen years maybe. The cause of death was pneumonia, complicated by what looks like advanced tuberculosis. He had been coughing blood into a cloth. We found it in the backpack. He went into the warehouse, probably, to get out of the weather. It was cold that week. First real cold snap of the season.

Marcus was looking at the face, waiting for it to tell him something. He thought about the letter. He thought about the river and the light falling on it at different times of day. He thought about the moment when Thomas had surfaced from the water, gasping, having nearly drowned from believing he would drown, and how for just a moment the world had seemed salvageable, seemed like it might be a place where things worked out if you just had faith in them.

—It's him, Marcus said.

He said it not as a certainty but as a choice, and Dr. Kwan seemed to understand the distinction. She pulled the sheet back up slowly, as though they were tucking him in, and the face disappeared into the material again, became an abstraction, a shape under cloth rather than something that had once inhabited the world.

—I'm very sorry, she said.

Marcus left the building and walked toward the river without consulting a map, following an instinct he didn't know he had. The morning was already settling into the ordinary rhythms of a small town, people moving through the streets with purposes that had nothing to do with death or identification or brothers lost and found and lost again. He found the water after about twenty minutes, a stretch of it that was indeed beautiful in the way the ferry woman had described beauty, transitional and uncertain, the light on its surface changing moment by moment as clouds moved overhead and the sun continued its ascent.

There was a bench and he sat on it, still holding the unopened newspaper that someone had left there, holding it the way you hold something that doesn't belong to you but which you've been given anyway, out of habit or accident or the kind of social momentum that prevents you from simply dropping it. The river moved with great indifference to the fact of his presence. Thomas would have known this river, would have stood where Marcus was standing and looked at it and thought things. Or not thought. Sometimes rivers are just water, just the accumulated weight of mountains finding its way toward the ocean, and the meaning is something you bring to it rather than something it contains.

He opened the letter again and read it once more, more carefully this time, looking for clues, for some kind of code or instruction or something between the words that would tell him what he was supposed to do now. But it was just a letter, just a man trying to say something to another man he had once known. The horizon was clearing. In a few hours the day would be fully arrived and fully ordinary, and the ferry would leave and Marcus would be on it or not on it, depending on what happened next, depending on what the body had been in the warehouse meant to say to him.

He folded the letter and put it back in his pocket. He stood and walked down to the water's edge, where the ground turned soft and then immediately wet, where the boundary between land and river became a question rather than a line. The water was very cold. He could feel that from a distance. He stood there for a long time, watching how the light fell on the surface, how it changed and changed and changed again, never the same twice, always in transition, always in that state between things where perhaps the only clarity available was the clarity of watching it happen and knowing that nothing held. Not even this. Not even the moment itself.
