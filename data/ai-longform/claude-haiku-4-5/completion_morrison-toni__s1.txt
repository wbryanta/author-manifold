He kept to the trail, watching for movement in the thick growth on either side. The horse sensed his wariness and occasionally tossed her head, her ears alert to sounds he could not hear. The day was advancing and heat pressed down through the canopy, making the air thick and difficult to breathe. Jacob had traveled through worse, but he was older now and less inclined to test his luck against circumstance.

By late afternoon the trail widened and he could see signs of habitation—cleared land, burnt stumps, a creek that had been widened for washing. He was approaching settled country, which brought both relief and new concerns. The note he'd signed meant nothing if someone decided it did. The horse he rode was stolen, or as good as, the deposit foregone being a calculation of the hostler's fear. In these times a black man on a fine horse was advertisement of his own misfortune.

He came upon the property without warning, rounding a bend where the trail became a road packed hard by regular use. The house was substantial, two stories with good timber and a brick chimney. Barns and outbuildings arranged around it in the manner of someone with wealth enough to organize his holdings. Fields stretched beyond, and in those fields he could see people working, their movements small and deliberate in the distance. Jacob's jaw tightened. He had left such places behind, or thought he had.

A young boy appeared from nowhere, stepping into the road with a stick in his hand. He couldn't have been more than eight or nine, pale-skinned and thin, with the kind of direct stare that spoke of a child accustomed to being obeyed.

"You got business here?" the boy asked.

Jacob drew Regina up short, keeping his expression neutral. "Passing through. This the Vaark property?"

"No sir. This is Sorrow's Clearing." The boy said it with pride, as though he himself had cleared the land and planted the fields. "My father owns it."

"Then I've taken a wrong turn. Looking for the Vaark place." Jacob prepared to wheel the horse around, but the boy stepped forward.

"You can't go back that way. The trail floods after rain and you'll be stuck till morning. Best to go through to the river road. My mother says all travelers must stop and take hospitality or they bring bad luck on themselves."

There was something both genuine and rehearsed about the statement, as though the boy had been taught the words but believed them deeply. Jacob studied him, trying to read what lay beneath.

"Your mother says that?"

"Yes sir. She says people need kindness or the world gets harder than it already is."

Behind the boy, Jacob could see someone emerging from the house. A woman, moving slowly, one hand pressed against the small of her back. She wore a linen shift and an apron, her hair covered in cloth, and there was something in her carriage that suggested she was unaccustomed to the physical labor her dress indicated.

"I don't want to impose," Jacob called to her, though he remained mounted, maintaining the distance that might allow him to ride away if necessary.

The woman came closer, squinting against the late afternoon light. When she was near enough to see his face clearly, something shifted in her expression. He could not quite name it. Not fear exactly, though there was an element of caution. Something more like recognition, as though she were seeing something she had expected to see but was still surprised by its actual arrival.

"We have fresh water and cornbread," she said. "There's a place in the barn where you can stable your horse. You're welcome to sleep there as well, if you prefer."

The offer was precise in its measurements. A barn to sleep in, not a room in the house. Water and cornbread, not a full meal. But she was offering something, and in 1682 Virginia, that was not nothing.

"I appreciate the kindness," Jacob said, and he meant it. "The boy said your name is Sorrow?"

"No." The woman shook her head. "The clearing is called Sorrow's Clearing. I'm called Rebekah. My husband is gone to the northern territories on business and won't return for some weeks yet. My son is called Nathaniel." She turned slightly toward the boy. "Nathaniel, fetch water from the well and put it in the barn. This man will want to wash. Tell Lina to prepare something to eat."

The boy scampered off with the kind of obedience that came from genuine affection rather than fear, and Jacob relaxed slightly. A house where children moved freely and spoke directly was a house less likely to harbor violence in the immediate present, though he had learned that violence could come from places one did not expect it.

He dismounted and led Regina toward the barn, aware of Rebekah watching him with that strange expression still on her face. The barn was well-maintained, the stalls clean and organized. A horse was already there, a sturdy chestnut that lifted its head in interest at Regina's arrival. Vaark brushed down the mare with care, talking to her quietly as he worked, feeling the accumulated tension of travel ease somewhat from his shoulders.

By the time Nathaniel returned with a bucket of water and a cloth, Jacob had settled Regina with hay and oats. He washed his hands and face, the water cool enough to raise goosebumps on his skin. The light in the barn was taking on the amber quality of approaching evening, and somewhere distant he could hear the first calls of insects beginning their night songs.

Lina brought the food herself, a woman considerably older than Rebekah, with the weathered face and competent hands of someone who had worked in service for decades. She set down a wooden plate with cornbread, cold chicken, and a small bowl of something that looked like butter mixed with herbs.

"Eat," Lina said, not unkindly. "Miss Rebekah says you're to eat proper."

Jacob found himself hungry in a way he hadn't expected. The food was good, clean, and made with care. Whoever this Rebekah was, whatever her circumstances, she was attempting something decent. He ate slowly, prolonging the pleasure, knowing it would be a long time before he ate again if he continued as he had planned.

The night deepened around him. Through the barn door he could see the house go dark room by room, until only a single lamp remained lit. Eventually that too was extinguished. The sounds of the property settled into the regular rhythm of night—the creak of wood cooling, the movement of animals shifting their weight, the soft chorus of frogs and insects from the direction of the creek.

Jacob had meant to sleep in the barn as offered, spreading a blanket across clean straw in a stall far from the horses. But sleep did not come easily, and when it did come, it was troubled by dreams he could not quite remember upon waking.

He rose before dawn and was prepared to leave, saddling Regina as the sky began its transition from black to deep blue. He had nearly completed his preparations when Rebekah appeared in the barn doorway, carrying a bundle.

"Provisions," she said, holding it out to him. "Bread, dried meat, fruit. Enough to see you several days if you're careful."

Jacob took the bundle, unsettled. Such kindness was not common, and it made him cautious. "Why?"

"Why?" Rebekah tilted her head slightly, as though considering the question seriously. "Because you need it. Because kindness is its own reason. Because I saw something in your face when you arrived and I recognized it, though I don't know how I could recognize something I've never seen before." She paused. "My husband built this place from nothing. He has plans to build something greater still. But I know that great things built on cruelty don't stand long. I know that because I can feel the weight of cruelty in the air itself sometimes, pressing down on everything and everyone. I don't want to add to that weight."

Jacob secured the bundle to Regina's saddle without responding. What could he say? That her kindness terrified him more than an outright threat? That he had learned to distrust good intentions, having seen what they often masked?

"Where are you headed?" Rebekah asked.

"North," Jacob said. "I own a property there. It's not much yet, but it will be."

"Then you understand," Rebekah said. "You understand what my husband is trying to do. To build something. To make something from the raw land and your own labor."

"I understand the desire," Jacob said carefully. "Whether the land allows it is another matter."

"Will you tell me your name? Not Jacob Vaark—that's what you signed, isn't it? Your real name?"

Jacob was quiet for a long moment. Giving his real name was a kind of transaction, a claiming of identity that could be used against him. But something in Rebekah's direct gaze suggested that she would know if he lied, and perhaps would accept the lie as an answer in itself.

"Vaark," he said. "That's my name now. It's the name I've chosen for myself, which is more real than the one I was given."

Rebekah nodded as though this made perfect sense. "Then that's the name you should keep. Build your property on that name, Mr. Vaark. Don't let them take it from you."

He rode out as the sun broke the horizon, turning back only once to see her standing in the barn doorway, watching him go. He did not wave. He did not want to encourage hope on either side of that gesture. But something had shifted in him, some small piece that had been hardened beyond softening had cracked open slightly, letting in the possibility that the world contained more than cruelty and transaction.

The northern territories would be waiting. His land, modest though it was, would be waiting. The work of building something, anything, from the wilderness would consume him. But he carried with him now something he had not possessed in years: the memory of a house where kindness was offered without calculation, where a woman with kind hands had looked at him and seen not a threat or a tool, but a person attempting to build a life as she was attempting to build hers.

The road ahead was uncertain, as all roads were. Virginia was still a mess, and the laws were still lawless in their cruelty. But Jacob Vaark rode north with something that might have been hope, or might have been merely the absence of despair. Time would tell which it was. Time, and the unforgiving land that transformed all who tried to bend it to their will.
