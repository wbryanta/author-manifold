# Crossing

The ferry cut through dark water that seemed almost solid in its blackness, as if the vessel were not moving forward but rather descending through layers of something thick and mineral. Marcus stood on the deck with his coat collar raised though there was no wind, only a cold that came from the water itself, radiating upward into the night air. Behind him the lights of the last port had dissolved an hour ago. Ahead was nothing he could see.

He had booked the crossing only after the phone call, only after the woman's voice—a constable, official and stripped of all inflection—had asked if he had a brother named David, if David had any distinguishing marks, scars, anything. The body had been found three days in the water. Three days. It was now the middle of November and the water was approaching that temperature at which the body becomes something else, something less like a person and more like cargo, evidence, a problem to be catalogued and filed away.

They had not spoken in seven years. Marcus carried this fact like a stone in his chest, aware of it always but having grown so accustomed to its weight that he rarely examined it directly. There had been no dramatic rupture, no single moment of betrayal that could be pointed to and understood. Instead there had been a slow recession, a gradual turning away, as if they had been standing back-to-back and one of them—perhaps both—had begun to walk in a different direction, and eventually the distance became too great for the voice to carry.

David had written once, four years ago, a postcard from somewhere in the north—a town Marcus had never heard of, a place so small it barely registered as a location. The message had been brief: I'm well. I'm thinking of you. That was all. Marcus had read it several times, had placed it on a shelf, had eventually thrown it away, then later regretted throwing it away, but had not tried to retrieve it. There had been no return address.

He went inside the ferry where it was warm and loud. The common room smelled of machine oil and human proximity—dozens of people in heavy coats, all of them bound for the same small northern city, all of them carrying their own reasons. An old woman sat with her hands folded in her lap, staring at nothing. A young couple played cards at a corner table with the solemnity of people engaged in something more significant than a game. A man about Marcus's age read a newspaper from that morning, the pages already yellowed slightly, already historical.

Marcus bought coffee from a kiosk and sat by a window where his own reflection stared back at him, ghostly and indistinct. He had brought nothing with him—just the clothes he was wearing, his wallet, his phone. He had taken leave from work with a vagueness that bordered on rudeness, telling his supervisor only that there was a family matter, that he might be gone for several days. His supervisor, a man named Gerald whom Marcus had never liked, had nodded with what seemed like relief, as if Marcus's absence would somehow improve the office environment.

He had no family, really, not anymore. His parents had died within eighteen months of each other—his mother to cancer, his father to something less identifiable, something the doctors called cardiac arrest but which Marcus understood as a failure of the will to continue. That had been eleven years ago. David had come to the funerals, both of them. He had worn a dark suit that didn't fit him properly, his shoulders too broad, his wrists extending too far from the sleeves. He had held himself apart during the services, standing slightly away from Marcus, as if proximity might be contagious.

The coffee was too hot and Marcus set it aside. He watched his reflection drink it anyway, watched this other Marcus move through the motions with a kind of automatism, the way the body continues its functions even when the mind has traveled elsewhere, to other times, other configurations of the world.

The constable had explained that the body was found near the breakwater, caught in rocks and kelp. A fisherman spotted it. She had asked if Marcus could come north immediately, if he could identify the remains. She had not said "remains," actually. She had said "the body," as if the word "remains" would be too violent, too suggestive of incompleteness. But Marcus understood. He understood that the sea had been working on whatever they found, and that what he would be asked to identify might bear only the most tenuous resemblance to a person he had once known.

They had been close as children. There were eight years between them and David had been the kind of older brother who looked after the younger one with a tenderness that seemed almost paternal. He had taught Marcus to swim in a lake near their house, holding him up in the water while Marcus thrashed about, convinced he would drown. Don't fight it, David had said. Just let the water hold you. Let it do what it wants to do. And eventually, impossibly, Marcus had floated.

In their teenage years something had shifted. David had grown distant, had begun spending time alone, long walks in the woods that stretched for hours. When he was at home he was polite but unreachable, as if his mind was always elsewhere, conducting some private negotiation with something Marcus couldn't see. Their parents had worried but said nothing directly, had only exchanged glances across the dinner table, those small economies of marital communication that children are supposed not to understand but always do.

The ferry's engines changed pitch and Marcus felt the vessel shift slightly, responding to some current or change in water temperature. How deep was the strait at this point? How far down would you have to sink before the light failed completely, before the pressure became so great that your bones would remember nothing of their original shape?

He finished his coffee and walked through the corridors of the ferry, past cabins where people were presumably sleeping, past the bridge where he could see the captain's silhouette against instrument panels, a man doing his job with a competence that seemed almost meditative. They were past the point of return now. There was nothing ahead but the destination, and nothing behind but water that would take hours to traverse.

When David was nineteen he had announced that he was leaving, that he had accepted a position at a nature preserve somewhere in the northern territories, and that he would be gone for at least a year. Their mother had wept silently. Their father had said nothing. Marcus, who was eleven, had felt something collapse inside him—not dramatically, not with any identifiable moment of fracture, but rather like a gradual settling, as if the house itself had shifted on its foundations and would never quite be level again.

David had come back, but never for long, and each subsequent visit had been shorter than the last. He had become a man increasingly defined by absence, by the spaces he occupied in the world that Marcus was not. Eventually the visits had stopped altogether. He had sent postcards for a while, then those too had ceased.

Marcus had not looked him up. Had not tried to track him through the various means available—social media, mutual friends, the thousand small ways in which the modern world makes hiding nearly impossible. This had not been a considered decision, exactly. It was more that the longer the silence extended, the more impossible it became to break. To contact David after so long would be to acknowledge the rupture, to say I notice that you are gone, and maybe David did not want to be noticed. Maybe his absence was intentional and complete and to reach toward it would only cause him pain.

The ferry rocked gently. Most of the people in the common room had dispersed, had gone below to sleep or to their cabins to sit in silence. Marcus remained where he was, watching the darkness beyond the window, which was the same darkness he could see when he closed his eyes, which seemed to be the fundamental darkness of the world, the thing from which all light was borrowed.

He wondered what he would feel when he saw the body. Would he recognize it? Would some deep part of him know, the way the stories always suggested that blood knows blood, that there is some sensate knowledge that exists beneath consciousness? Or would the dead thing be only that—a thing, a collection of degraded cellular matter that no longer held any connection to the person it had once been?

The constable had mentioned that there was a jacket. The body had been wearing a jacket, and in the jacket was a wallet, and in the wallet was an identification card that matched the name David Marcus Solomon. But identification cards could be stolen. Bodies could be misidentified. The world was full of such confusions, such terrible mistakes that came too late to be corrected.

By the time the first light began to show on the horizon—a thin grey line that seemed almost reluctant to appear—Marcus had not slept. He had sat and watched the slow turn of night into pre-dawn, the way the water gradually revealed itself, the way the stars faded with an almost apologetic dimness, retreating into the sky as if they had been intruding.

The other passengers began to emerge as the light grew stronger. They moved in the particular slowness of people who have slept poorly, who are dragging themselves toward whatever obligations await them on land. The old woman who had stared at nothing was still staring at nothing, though her position had shifted slightly, her head now tilted toward the window as if drawn by the emerging light.

Marcus went to the bathroom and washed his face in water that was colder than seemed necessary. In the mirror his own reflection looked like something dredged up from the sea—pale, uncertain, barely animated. He needed to shave but had brought no razor. He would arrive at the constable's office looking like a man who had been traveling, which was true, though the travel had taken him across distance far greater than the strait, into territories from which he wondered if he would be able to return.

The port appeared gradually, emerging from the morning mist as if it had been hidden and was only now revealing itself. The buildings were grey and worn, weathered by salt and wind into a kind of functional plainness. There were fishing boats, commercial vessels, the usual apparatus of a working harbour. Nothing beautiful. Nothing that suggested anything other than utility.

Marcus gathered his coat and joined the other passengers moving toward the disembarkation point. No one had much luggage. Everyone seemed accustomed to arriving with almost nothing, to taking what the world offered and asking few questions about its origin or nature.

The constable was waiting inside the terminal building, a woman perhaps ten years younger than Marcus, with dark hair pulled back severely and the particular exhaustion that seemed to be the permanent state of people who worked in law enforcement. She introduced herself as Constable Hayes and extended her hand and Marcus shook it and felt the coldness of her fingers, which seemed to be a permanent condition in this place, something about the climate that made warmth difficult to maintain.

– You came straight from the ferry, she said. It was not quite a question.

– Yes.

– I'm very sorry for the circumstances. I know this must be difficult.

Marcus said nothing to this because it seemed both true and inadequate as a description of what he was experiencing, which was something more like numbness, as if his mind had learned to protect him by withdrawing, by suspending its normal processes of feeling and response.

They drove through the small city in a vehicle that smelled of old coffee and something vaguely chemical, a cleaning agent or air freshener that never quite masked the underlying odors of the place. The streets were quiet. It was early morning and most people were still sleeping, still wrapped in their beds, still living in the world before this one, the world before identification and confirmation and whatever came after.

The facility was at the edge of the city, a low modern building of the sort that seems designed to be forgotten, to fade into the landscape through sheer bland competence. Inside it was very clean and very cold and smelled of refrigeration and disinfectant.

– I should prepare you, Constable Hayes said as they walked down a corridor. The body has been in the water for some time. There's been degradation. It may not look the way you expect.

– I understand, Marcus said, though he did not understand, could not imagine, had deliberately refrained from imagining anything specific about what he would see.

They stopped at a door and the constable placed her hand on the handle but did not open it immediately.

– Is there anything you need before we proceed? she asked.

Marcus shook his head. What would he need? What preparation existed for a moment like this?

The door opened onto a room where the air was even colder, where there was a body on a table, covered with a sheet. The body was roughly the right size. The constable approached and pulled back the sheet and Marcus saw the face and he understood immediately, with a certainty that seemed to come from somewhere outside himself, that this was not his brother. Could not be his brother. The features were wrong, the shape of the skull beneath the waterlogged skin, the way the light caught what remained of the eyes. This was a stranger, a body that had been someone's brother perhaps, someone's father, someone's love, but it was not David.

He said so. He told the constable that this was not his brother, and he watched the particular disappointment cross her face—not the disappointment of having made a mistake, but rather the disappointment of someone who had hoped to resolve something and found the resolution impossible.

– The identification in the jacket, she began.

– It's not him, Marcus said. I'm certain.

And he was. He was more certain of this than he had been certain of anything in years. His brother was still alive. Still out there somewhere in the world, still absent, still refusing the connection that might have been built if either of them had been willing to reach toward the other. Marcus turned away from the body, turned away from the sheet and the table and the apparatus of the cold room, and walked back through the door without waiting for the constable.

He walked through the facility and out into the grey morning of the northern city. He walked without direction, without purpose, only aware that his feet were moving, that the cold was still coming from the water, that the world was still turning beneath him. Somewhere ahead, or behind, or in some direction he could not currently perceive, his brother was alive, was breathing, was moving through the world with his own purposes and his own silences and his own refusals.

Marcus did not know if he would try to find him. He did not know if he would contact Constable Hayes and tell her about the misidentification and suffer through the paperwork that would surely follow. He did not know if he would stay in this city or return to the ferry or simply begin walking north and see what the land contained.

What he did know was that the distance between them had shifted somehow. It had been transformed from something final into something still open, still negotiable, still containing within it the possibility of spanning. He walked through the city with this knowledge like something alive inside him, something he could feel moving, something that hurt and healed simultaneously, like a wound in the process of becoming a scar.
