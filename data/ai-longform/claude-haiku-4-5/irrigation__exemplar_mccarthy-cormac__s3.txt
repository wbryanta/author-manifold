The morning was already hot when they came down through the orchard with their tools and the old pump that had not run since April. The trees stood in their rows like something meant to endure but enduring poorly, and the earth between them was cracked in patterns that suggested some larger geometry of failure. The younger brother carried the pump and the elder walked ahead with the wrench and the length of copper pipe wrapped in canvas.

They did not speak.

At the far corner where the line had burst they set down their burdens in the dust. The elder knelt and examined the break without touching it. The copper was green with oxidation and split along a seam where the pressure had finally overcome the metal's patience. He rose and looked back toward the house, a small structure of adobe and wood frames visible through the shimmer of heat, and then he turned to his brother.

It was going to be three hundred dollars minimum. Probably more.

His brother was coiling the canvas and did not look up.

I know what it's going to cost, he said.

Do you. The elder uncapped the wrench and laid it in the dust beside the broken pipe. Because I don't know that you do. I don't know that you understand the cost of anything anymore.

I understand it fine.

You understand nothing. You come here once every few months and look at the place like you're inspecting something that doesn't belong to you. Like the trees are supposed to apologize for their existence.

The younger brother set down the pump with a violence that was restrained only by his awareness of how it would cost him if he broke it. He straightened and looked at his brother with an expression of such consolidated anger that the elder seemed momentarily to regret his words, though he did not say so.

I'm here aren't I, the younger said.

You're here. That's not the same as being present.

Jesus Christ.

The younger brother turned and walked back through the orchard toward where they had left the truck, his stride long and deliberate. The elder watched him go and then returned his attention to the burst pipe. He picked it up and examined the breach more closely, running his thumb along the torn metal. The seam had been weakened for years probably, the metal degrading in ways invisible until the moment of failure. There was nothing you could do about such things. You could only discover them after they had happened.

When the younger brother returned he was carrying a shovel and a bucket and a pair of work gloves that left dust in the air as he dropped them. The elder said nothing.

They dug out the earth around the broken section. The ground was packed hard and the work was slow. Their shadows shortened as the sun climbed. By ten o'clock the younger brother had removed his shirt and his skin was dark with sweat and dirt. The elder worked steadily in his long sleeves, his hat pulled low.

Do you remember when Dad ran the whole place, the younger said. When it was actually producing.

It never actually produced. It barely kept us alive.

It was better than it is now.

Most things are better than they are now. That's not an argument for anything.

They cut out the damaged section of pipe. The copper fell away in their hands and they laid it aside. The earth around the break was damp and smelled mineral. The younger brother studied the exposed line and then looked at the elder.

The whole thing is shot, he said.

Not the whole thing.

Most of it. You know it. I know it. The buyers know it.

We're not selling.

We should be.

The elder was fitting the new section of pipe, measuring it against the cut ends. He did the work with the precision of someone who had done it many times before, or perhaps with the precision of someone performing a ritual that had lost all meaning but required completion nonetheless.

I'm not having this conversation, he said.

We have to have it.

No. We don't.

You can't keep throwing money at this place like it's going to fix itself. Like somehow if we just keep patching the pumps and replacing the lines the trees will magically start producing again. They won't. The soil is exhausted. The water table's dropping. We're running on fumes and you know it.

The elder sealed the joint with his wrench and sat back on his heels. He looked out across the orchard toward the far gate where the property line ended in a fence that had been broken in several places for years. Beyond it was the neighbor's land, which had been sold two years prior to a development company, and which was now in the early stages of becoming something else entirely. From where they sat they could not see what was happening there, but they could see the machinery sometimes in the distance, and the dust that rose from the work of demolition.

Your mother thought we should sell, the elder said.

You're going to use that. You're going to use what Mom thought to support what you want to do.

She was right about most things.

She was wrong about this.

How do you know.

Because I know what this place means.

To whom.

To us. To Dad. To the family.

The elder began cleaning the work area, collecting the old pipe and the tools they'd used. His movements were methodical and without waste.

Your father bought this land in nineteen fifty-two, he said. He paid cash for it. Five acres. He planted the first trees himself, one after another, in the heat of August. Your mother was pregnant with me and she would bring him water and he would rest for five minutes and then go back to digging. It took him two months. When he finished there was nothing here but five acres of holes and five acres of saplings that were barely alive. He knew they would probably die. He knew the climate was marginal and the soil was poor and the water situation was uncertain. He knew all of it. He did it anyway.

He did it because he had nowhere else to go.

That's one way of looking at it.

It's the only way of looking at it. He didn't have a choice. We have a choice.

The younger brother stood and wiped his forehead with the back of his hand. He was breathing heavily though the work was not recent enough to account for his breath.

I'm not spending the rest of my life trying to save something that's already dead, he said.

Then don't.

What.

I said don't. Make your choice. Sell your half. Take your money. Go somewhere else and make your life.

The younger brother stared at him.

Just like that.

Just like that.

You'd buy me out.

I didn't say that. I don't have money to buy you out. I'm saying you should sell your half to someone else if that's what you need to do.

And what happens when they come in here with their bulldozers and half the land is gone.

I imagine it will be gone.

The elder had finished gathering the tools and he rose slowly, holding his back as he straightened. He was fifty-three years old and moved like someone older.

I'm going to get the pump, he said.

The pump won't work if we don't get this line functioning, the younger brother called after him.

The elder did not answer. He walked back through the orchard toward the truck, his long shadow preceding him in the heat shimmer. The younger brother watched him go and then sat down on the edge of the trench they had dug, his feet dangling into the darkness below ground.

When the elder returned with the pump they worked in silence for another two hours. They installed the new section of line and sealed all the joints and then they positioned the pump at the lowest point of the system. The elder had brought tools for priming it and he worked the plunger while the younger brother held the intake steady. After several attempts water began to flow, first in reluctant spurts and then in a steady stream that ran out into the earth.

They stood watching it. The water was clear and cold and it spread through the soil and was absorbed almost immediately.

It's working, the younger brother said.

For now.

They shut down the pump and capped the line. The work was done. They gathered their tools and the remains of the old pipe and made their way back through the orchard in the early afternoon. The shadows were small and dense beneath the trees. A few birds called from high branches, their cries sparse and infrequent. The place had a quality of silence that seemed to accumulate rather than diminish, as though the quiet were something that could be collected and stored.

At the truck the younger brother loaded the tools while the elder sat in the cab with the door open. The breeze from the gap in the Jarillas had not yet begun to blow. The heat was complete.

Do you remember the year it froze, the younger brother said. When we lost most of the crop.

I remember it.

I was nine. I thought it was the end of everything. I thought the land would never produce again.

It didn't, not really. Not after that.

But we kept going.

We did.

Why.

The elder did not answer immediately. He looked out across the hood of the truck toward the house.

Because the alternative was unacceptable, he said finally.

And it isn't now.

It's becoming so.

They drove back toward the house in the truck that was older than both of them combined. The radio did not work. The air conditioning had ceased functioning some years prior. The leather seats were cracked and the dashboard was faded to a color that suggested bone. They rode in silence while the heat pressed against the windows and the land scrolled past with a monotony that seemed designed to illustrate some argument about futility.

At the house the younger brother did not turn off the engine immediately. He sat with his hand on the steering wheel and looked at the structure before them. The paint was peeling in large sections. Some of the window frames had begun to rot. The porch sagged slightly on one side.

I got an offer, the younger brother said. Four hundred and twenty thousand. For all of it.

When.

Three weeks ago.

Why didn't you tell me.

Because I knew what you'd say.

And what would I have said.

That it wasn't enough. That we should hold on longer. That things would get better.

Would I have been wrong.

Probably not. But they won't get better either. That's the thing you don't seem to understand. It's not going to change. It's just going to stay like this, slowly degrading, slowly requiring more and more of us to do less and less. Until one day we'll both be too old and it won't matter anymore because we won't have the strength to keep it going anyway.

The elder sat with his head back against the seat. His shirt was soaked through with sweat and dirt. His face was burned from the morning and his eyes were closed.

Your mother had a sister, he said. Lives in Arizona. Married a man who built shopping centers. They had a comfortable life. Retirement accounts. Healthcare. The things that normal people have. Your mother used to wonder what that would have been like.

To leave.

To choose something other than this. To make a decision based on what was good for her rather than what was owed.

Did she regret staying.

I think she did. In the end.

The younger brother turned off the engine. The silence that followed was complete and seemed to press inward like something with weight and substance.

I'm going to sell, the younger brother said.

I know.

I want you to know that I'm not doing it to hurt you.

I know that too.

They sat in the dead air of the cab and neither of them moved. The house stood before them as it had stood for seventy years, as it would stand for some years more regardless of whether they were in it or not. The trees behind the house were invisible now in the brightness of afternoon but they were there, hundreds of them, growing old in their rows, their roots drawing what they could from exhausted soil.

The elder opened his door. He swung his legs out into the heat and stood with difficulty.

I'm going to lie down, he said. You should drink some water.

He walked toward the house with his tools in one hand. The younger brother watched him go and then sat alone in the truck with the windows rolled down and the sun coming straight down through the windshield, unobstructed and absolute.
