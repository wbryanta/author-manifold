# 

The morning heat was already thick when Marcus found David standing beside the broken section of pipe, arms crossed, surveying the damage with the expression of a man looking at a wound that wouldn't heal. The irrigation line had split near the old apricot grove, and water was bleeding into the soil in a way that made Marcus's chest tighten. He'd known this was coming. They both had. The line was forty years old, installed by their father when optimism still seemed like a reasonable position.

"It's worse than I thought," David said, not turning. His voice had that particular flatness it took on when he was bracing himself for something. At thirty-eight, David carried their father's solidity in his shoulders, the weight that seemed to accumulate in men who stayed in one place too long.

Marcus was two years younger, but sometimes the gap felt wider. "The whole section needs replacing. Maybe more." He'd already gotten a quote from Henderson's supply, though he hadn't shown it to David yet. The number made his stomach turn.

They stood in silence for a moment, the only sound the soft hiss of water escaping the fracture. Behind them, the orchard stretched out in rows that seemed to go on forever, though Marcus knew exactly where it ended—at the road his father had refused to widen when the county came asking, in 1987, back when he still believed the property would matter more than convenience.

"I've been thinking," David began, and Marcus felt something settle into stone in his abdomen, because he knew that particular cadence. David had been thinking. Which meant David had been thinking for a while, probably talking to someone about it, probably lying awake at three in the morning running numbers through his mind the way he used to run his hands across his wife's shoulders before she left, searching for some kind of answer in the topography of familiar terrain.

"Don't," Marcus said.

"You don't even know what I'm going to say."

"Yes, I do."

David finally turned to look at him. His brother's eyes were lighter than his own, a hazel that caught the sun and held it, and they held it now with something like pity, which might have been worse than anger. "The Hendersons made another offer. A good one. The development company is willing to wait until we get the water situation sorted—"

"No." Marcus bent down to examine the pipe, though there was nothing to see that he hadn't already seen. The crack ran through the copper like a accusation.

"You haven't even heard the numbers."

"I don't want to hear them."

"That's not how this works," David said, and there was something in his tone that made Marcus straighten up, some edge that hadn't been there before. "It's not how it works anymore. We share this equally. Dad made sure of that. Which means you don't get to just decide for both of us."

The orchard had been divided in their father's will with the precision of a man who'd thought very carefully about how to create maximum complexity. The land itself was held in common, but decisions required mutual agreement, a provision their father had made in what he'd clearly believed was a fit of wisdom but which had actually been a way of ensuring his sons would have to talk to each other, would have to remain in some kind of perpetual relationship, even after he was gone. Especially after he was gone.

"I'm not deciding anything," Marcus said. "I'm just saying no. There's a difference."

"There really isn't."

They'd had versions of this conversation before, had danced around it carefully in phone calls and brief visits, but this was the first time they were standing on the land itself, with evidence of its deterioration at their feet, actual water running away into dirt that desperately needed it but couldn't hold it, and Marcus understood now that this conversation had been waiting for them all along, waiting for the right catalyst. He'd been naive to think they could keep managing it in abstracts, in hypotheticals.

"This place means something," Marcus said.

"It means something to you." David's voice wasn't unkind, which somehow made it worse. "It doesn't mean the same thing to me."

"It meant something to Dad."

"Dad's dead. He's been dead for four years. At some point we have to live our own lives, not in service to his mythology."

Marcus turned away and walked back toward the house, needing to move, needing space, but David followed him, the same way he'd followed him through childhood, the loyal shadow who'd wanted everything Marcus wanted but was willing to want it differently, in smaller increments.

"Rebecca and I are thinking about moving south," David said. "Somewhere warm. Somewhere that doesn't require you to wake up every morning and immediately feel behind schedule because the land is demanding something from you. I'm tired, Marcus. I'm tired of the calendar controlling my life."

Rebecca was David's second wife, a woman who'd never understood why they maintained this property when there were perfectly good orchards everywhere, run more efficiently, without the weight of family history behind every row. Marcus had tried to like her. She seemed competent, even kind in her way, but she was fundamentally uninterested in anything that predated her own arrival in the world.

"So that's what this is about," Marcus said. "You want to cash out so you can buy some retirement fantasy."

"It's not a fantasy. It's a reasonable desire to not be sixty-five years old and still fixing pipes on land that barely breaks even."

They'd reached the house, their mother's old place, though she'd been gone almost as long as their father. The structure sagged slightly at one corner, the paint peeling in places, and Marcus suddenly hated that David saw this—really saw it—instead of seeing what it had been.

"I can buy you out," Marcus said.

"With what money?"

The question hung between them because they both knew the answer. Marcus had nothing. He lived in the cottage his father had built for the farm manager back when they'd had a farm manager, and he worked a job in town as an agricultural consultant, a position that had slowly revealed itself to be mostly about talking to people like David, people who wanted permission to leave, wanted someone to tell them it was okay to stop trying.

"I could get a loan," he said, and heard how stupid it sounded even as he said it.

"Using what collateral? This land isn't worth what you think it is. The water situation, the market conditions, the infrastructure—it's a liability, Marcus. You know it is."

But knowing it and accepting it were different things. Marcus looked out over the orchard, at the neat rows of apricot and almond and plum trees that his grandfather had planted, that his father had tended, that he'd been tending since his father's stroke had made it clear that tending was now his responsibility. The orchard had never been profitable in the way modern agricultural operations were profitable, but it had been alive, had sustained them, had contained within it something that Marcus had never quite been able to name but which he'd felt, especially in June, when the light lay across the rows in a way that made the world seem clarified, made it seem possible that things could be exactly as they ought to be.

"Give me the rest of the season," he said. "Let me get the irrigation fixed, let me get through the harvest. Then we can talk."

"We're talking now."

"Then let me finish my point. Give me six months. I'll see if I can make the numbers work. If I can't, I won't fight you. But I need to try."

David was quiet for a long time. Marcus could see him wrestling with something, could see the moment when his brother's better nature—the thing that made him a decent man, made him capable of loyalty even when he felt it was misplaced—won out over his impatience.

"Okay," David said finally. "Six months. But we need to talk to a financial advisor. Real numbers, real projections. Not just hope."

"Fine."

"And we need to figure out the irrigation today. That's not going to wait."

So they spent the rest of the day in the trenches, literally, digging out the damaged section of pipe. The work was mindless in a way that Marcus appreciated, something he could lose himself in. David worked beside him with the easy competence of someone who'd grown up doing this, though his movements had a rustiness to them now, the hesitation of someone who'd spent too long in air-conditioned offices, making decisions with his mind instead of his body.

By late afternoon, they'd removed the broken section and laid out the new pipe. The cost was going to be significant, money Marcus didn't have, but at least it was actionable. At least it was something he could work with.

"I was thinking about the apricot grove," David said, breaking the long silence that had settled between them as they worked. He was sitting on an overturned bucket, drinking from the thermos of water Marcus had brought, and he was looking at the grove in question with something that might have been nostalgia. "Remember when we were kids, and Dad had us pick the early fruit? Before it was quite ripe?"

"For the preserves." Marcus remembered. His mother had made preserves every summer, had canned them in the kitchen in batches that seemed endless, had arranged the finished jars on shelves with the precision of someone maintaining a small library. When she died, they'd found thirty years of preserves in the cellar, layers of amber and gold, enough to feed a family for decades, now feeding nothing but memory.

"I was thinking," David said, "that we could still do that. Even if we sold. We could negotiate to keep a small section, maybe hire someone to maintain it. Just for the preserves, just for that."

There was something in the offer that made Marcus's eyes burn, some attempt at compromise that his brother was making from across a divide that neither of them had quite figured out how to bridge. But it wasn't enough, and they both knew it. The preserves without the whole orchard would be a lie, a mimicry of something real reduced to sentiment.

"Maybe," Marcus said, but his voice was elsewhere, was already engaged in the calculation of what six months might mean, what he might accomplish in that window of time.

They finished the installation as the sun began its descent, and the light changed the way light always changed in June, becoming something almost liquid, gilding the dust they'd kicked up during the digging. They stood back and looked at the repaired line, and Marcus felt something like satisfaction, though he knew it was temporary. There would be other breaks. There would be other failures. But this one was fixed, and that had to be enough for today.

"I'm going to head back," David said. "Rebecca's expecting me."

"Okay."

"Marcus." His brother waited until he turned. "I'm not doing this to hurt you. I want you to know that."

"I know."

"And I'm not sure you're wrong. I'm just not sure you're right, either. That's the thing I can't get past. I used to be sure about things. I used to know what mattered. But somewhere in the last few years, I stopped knowing, and now everything feels negotiable."

There wasn't anything Marcus could say to that, so he just nodded, and David got into his car and drove back toward the main road, toward his life in town, toward Rebecca and whatever future they were assembling together, piece by piece.

Marcus stayed in the orchard as the light faded. He walked the rows, running his hands along the bark of trees he'd known his whole life, and he thought about his father, who'd believed that some things were worth the struggle, that meaning could be made through persistence, through the simple act of returning day after day to the same patch of ground and tending it as though it mattered.

His father had been wrong about a lot of things. But maybe not about this.

The darkness settled fully, and Marcus made his way back to the cottage, where he sat at his kitchen table with a pad of paper and began to write numbers. Costs and revenues, depreciation and yield. All the things that could be quantified. All the things that had nothing to do with light on dust, with the weight of a full bucket of apricots picked in early morning, with the feeling of dirt under your fingernails that meant you'd worked, that meant you'd built something that existed in the world, that existed because you'd chosen to make it exist.

He wrote through the night, and by the time dawn broke over the eastern edge of the property, he had a framework. It was thin, and it required several assumptions that might not hold, and it depended on commodity prices remaining relatively stable and on Marcus himself not needing to eat, apparently, based on the minimal salary he'd allocated himself. But it existed. It was a possible version of the future.

He stood on the porch of the cottage and looked out at the orchard as it emerged from darkness, row by row by row, each tree a small miracle of survival, of continued existence. Somewhere in the distance, a rooster called. The water in the repaired irrigation line would be running now, flowing where it was supposed to flow, nurturing what it was supposed to nurture.

Marcus pulled out his phone and called David. It was early, too early, and his brother answered with the irritation of someone woken from sound sleep.

"I'm sorry," Marcus said. "But I need you to come back."

"What's wrong? Did the line—"

"No. It's fine. I just want to show you something."

There was a pause, and then David said, "Give me an hour."

When his brother arrived, Marcus laid out the papers across the kitchen table, and David looked at them with the expression of someone being presented with evidence in a case they'd hoped was already closed. But he read through them carefully, following the logic, checking the math, and when he finished, he looked up at Marcus with something complicated in his eyes.

"This is really tight," David said.

"I know."

"And it doesn't account for—"

"I know what it doesn't account for. But it's not impossible."

"No," David agreed. "It's not impossible."

They stood in the kitchen in the early light, two men trying to hold something between them that kept threatening to slip away, and Marcus understood that this was all he could offer. Not certainty. Not guarantee. Just the possibility that he might be capable of doing what his father had believed could be done, might be capable of writing a version of the future where the land remained, where the orchard continued, where some things endured because you chose to make them endure, every single day, for as long as you were able.

"Let me think about it," David said finally.

"Okay."

"And Marcus? Don't disappear. Don't just assume I've already decided. Let's actually talk about this."

"I will."

After David left, Marcus walked the orchard again, and this time he noticed things differently. He noticed how some of the trees were older than others, how the spacing varied depending on which decade they'd been planted, how the whole landscape was a history written in wood and root and the accumulated decisions of men who'd come before him. He noticed which trees bore the heaviest fruit, which ones seemed to struggle, which ones thrived.

There was a craft to it, he realized. Not just in the tending, but in the attention, in the care of looking closely enough to understand what each particular plot of ground needed. His father had possessed this craft. His grandfather had possessed it. And maybe, Marcus thought, running his hands across the bark of an old plum tree, maybe that was something that could be learned. Maybe that was something worth learning.

The June sun climbed higher, burning off the promise of morning, and the day became itself, ordinary and difficult and real. And Marcus, standing in the center of his orchard, felt something shift inside him, something settle into place. Not certainty, but something close enough. Not a solution, but a direction.

He would tend this land for six months, and he would tend it well, and then he would see what happened. He would see if David could be convinced, or if he would have to convince himself that his brother's choice was the right one, even if it wasn't the choice Marcus would have made. He would see if the numbers worked, or if they remained eternally out of reach. He would see if meaning could be made through persistence, or if that was just another romantic notion his father had passed down like genetic material.

He would see, he thought, walking back toward the cottage as the heat of the day settled in around him, because that was what you did. You stayed. You tried. You saw.
