She stood at the window of the front room—not her front room, never really hers though she had lived here, though her shoes had scuffed the hardwood and her weight had compressed the floorboards in specific, repeated patterns that no one else would ever mark—and watched the man with the dolly tilt her mother's china cabinet into the air like a baby in a dangerous hold. His partner, younger, with the kind of neck that belonged on someone who hadn't yet learned what the world could do to a person, guided from behind, calling out adjustments in a voice too bright for the work. The cabinet rose higher than seemed possible or advisable, tetering against the doorframe, and Gloria thought about her mother's hands arranging those plates, the blue willow pattern that had meant company was coming, that the house was about to matter in some official capacity. Her mother had kept those plates pristine. Never used them. That was the thing that had bothered Gloria the most when she was young—the pristine-ness of it, as though beauty existed to be preserved rather than inhabited, as though a house was a museum of potential rather than a place where people actually lived.

The cabinet made it through. The dolly wheels squeaked against the porch boards and then down the brick walkway, leaving no mark, changing nothing. This was the thing about objects. They were so temporary. You thought they mattered, thought they anchored you or proved you existed, but they could be gone in an afternoon, loaded into a truck, sent somewhere else to mean something different to someone different. Gloria turned away from the window and looked at the room behind her—already diminished, already becoming a memory of itself. The watermark on the wall where the cabinet had stood. The indentation in the carpet. Evidence of presence that would fade.

The auction house had sent three people. The man with the neck was William. The older one with the dolly was Marcus. There was also a woman named Diane who was supposed to be evaluating items, determining what had value and what didn't, and this task seemed to fill her with a kind of contemptuous energy, as though she had been forced into a relationship with other people's pasts and resented it deeply. She moved through the house with a clipboard and a mouth pressed into a line, examining Gloria's mother's life with the neutrality of someone counting inventory at a warehouse.

"This?" Diane gestured toward the Secretary desk in the corner, the one Gloria's mother had inherited from her own mother, the one that still smelled faintly of the lavender sachet her mother had kept in the lower drawer.

"The wood's nice," Gloria said. "It should be worth something."

Diane ran her finger along the edge without actually touching it, which Gloria found to be a particular kind of violence, the refusal to make contact. "Veneer. And it's been in the sun. See how the finish is uneven here? It's faded."

Gloria had never noticed this. She had never noticed that sunlight could damage wood, could mark it as less-than, could reduce value through the mere act of existing in a room where windows faced east.

"Does it matter?" Gloria asked. "If it meant something?"

Diane looked at her, and in that look was an entire philosophy of the world—the understanding that meaning was a luxury good, available only to those who could afford it, and everyone else was just moving things around, trying to convince themselves that the arrangements meant something. "Not to anyone buying it, no," Diane said, and she made a note on her clipboard with a pen that she kept in her mouth like a cigarette.

Marcus was now on the stairs, carrying her mother's bedroom set down piece by piece. The footboard. The headboard. The nightstands. Gloria had slept in that bed when she was sick as a child, had lain in it after her own divorce at thirty-three, had sat on its edge the night she got the call that her mother had fallen, that there wasn't really a fall so much as a moment when her mother simply decided to go down to the floor and didn't get back up. That bed had held so much. Now it was being broken into components, rendered into parts that might be reassembled elsewhere, in some other woman's bedroom, in some other house in some other town, where it would mean nothing but functional sleeping surface.

William appeared with a lamp—the brass floor lamp that had stood in the corner of the living room for as long as Gloria could remember. She had read under that lamp. Her mother had stood beneath it while on the telephone, rotating slowly, the cord wrapping and unwrapping around her body like she was caught in some kind of gentle spiral. The lamp itself was probably worthless. The shade was yellowed. The brass had developed a patina that Diane would call tarnish.

"Where does this go?" William asked, and he meant the basement or the truck or the discard pile, but Gloria heard underneath it the larger question: where does anything go, what happens to all the objects that contain the lives of dead people, where is the graveyard for things?

"Take it," Gloria said. "I don't care."

She had said this now several times during the afternoon, and it was becoming a kind of refrain, a spiritual assertion. She had said it about her mother's wedding ring—too small to resize and therefore worthless to anyone but her, and she had not been able to put it on, had not been able to bear wearing her mother's marriage compressed around her own flesh. She had said it about the photograph albums, and Marcus had looked at her with something like genuine concern when she chose not to go through them, not to insist that these memories be kept together, kept safe, kept in the narrative sequence her mother had arranged them in.

"You sure?" Marcus had asked, holding the first album like it was a hostage. "Most people want to at least look through—"

"No," Gloria had said, and she meant it. The version of herself contained in those photographs—the child, the teenager, the young woman who had not yet learned that love could be insufficient, that understanding could be irrelevant, that the life you built with someone could be dismantled just as easily as this furniture was being dismantled, just as easily as everything was being dismantled in her mother's house on this humid July afternoon when the sky was so white it looked like it might be closing down on top of them all.

Her mother had kept photographs of Gloria's father on the dresser. He had left when Gloria was seven, and Gloria's mother had never explained it properly, had simply rearranged the rest of her life to fill the space where he had been. She had worked two jobs. She had kept the house clean. She had maintained standards. These seemed to have been the things that mattered to her—not understanding why her husband had left, but rather the management of a life that went on regardless. Gloria understood now that this was a kind of strength, though at the time she had simply experienced it as coldness, as an inability or unwillingness to acknowledge the hole in the middle of their family.

She had tried to be different with her own daughter, had tried to be emotional, present, engaged, all the words that had been missing from her own childhood. But her daughter had moved to California and called once every few months, always saying she had to go, always cutting the conversation short with the kind of gentle firmness that Gloria recognized as her own avoidance coming back at her like a boomerang, like a punishment, like a message in a language she had finally learned to understand.

"This one?" Diane was holding up a small carved box, the kind that had held her mother's buttons and loose change and the mysterious things that mothers kept. Gloria's mother had let her open it once, just once, and inside there had been a photograph Gloria had never seen before—her parents at some kind of party, young and laughing, caught mid-motion in a way that made them look like different people entirely, like the people they might have been had certain choices been different, had certain moments unfolded otherwise.

"What is it?" Diane asked, and Gloria realized she had been staring, that her face had probably given something away.

"Keep it," she said. "Sell it or keep it. I don't want it."

But this was a lie. She wanted it. She wanted all of it—every object in this house, every smell and stain and indication that her mother had existed, had moved through rooms, had held things in her hands and arranged them with intention. But she also wanted none of it, wanted to burn it all down, wanted to reduce it to the pure fact of emptiness, the way a room actually looked when no one was inside it anymore, the way a life actually looked when you stripped away all the objects and decoration and pretense.

The sun was moving across the floor. The light that had faded the Secretary desk was now angling through the western windows, laying golden rectangles on the walls. Her mother had loved this time of day. She had called it the golden hour, which Gloria had thought was a term from photography, but her mother had just meant the hour when the light was beautiful and the day was almost over and you could rest without feeling like you were wasting time. Gloria had never understood this need for permission, for the natural world to provide a signal that rest was acceptable. She had spent so much of her life fighting against this, pushing through the golden hour, staying up past the point of sense, refusing the instruction that daylight was offering.

"Is there anything you want to keep?" Marcus had asked earlier, and Gloria had gestured vaguely at the kitchen, where the appliances were old and the counters were Formica and nothing was worth keeping and yet the entire room was saturated with the presence of her mother, was basically a shrine to the idea of her mother even though her mother had probably not thought of it as a shrine but simply as the place where she made food and cleaned things and managed the practical business of remaining alive.

"There's a recipe box," William offered helpfully, and he pointed to the small wooden box that sat on the counter, the one that was overflowing with her mother's careful handwriting, recipes clipped from magazines, recipes written in pencil that had faded almost to invisibility, recipes in her mother's own hand, passed down from her own mother, generations of women trying to feed their families in slightly different ways, trying to assert some form of individual creativity within the constraints of flour and sugar and eggs.

Gloria went to the counter. The recipe box felt warm from the afternoon sun. She opened it. The smell that rose up was her childhood—not any specific smell, but rather the idea of smell, the idea that a box could contain so much, could preserve so much, could mean so much when you opened it and saw your mother's handwriting on index cards, the pencil marks where she had circled favorite ingredients, the note she had written in the margin of a cake recipe: "Alex loved this. Make for her birthday." Alex. That was Gloria. Her mother had called her Alex sometimes, a nickname that had been shorthand for love, for a relationship that was simpler than the one they had actually lived.

She put the recipe box down on the counter and pressed her palms against it, and for a moment she could feel her mother there, not in some supernatural sense but in the practical sense that her mother had also pressed her palms against this counter, had also stood in this kitchen and thought about the future and the past and whether any of it meant anything at all.

"Take it," she said to William. "Sell it."

"You don't want—"

"No." She looked at him. "I don't want any of it. My mother kept so much. So carefully. And I don't want it. Do you understand? I don't understand how that's possible, but it's true."

William was embarrassed. He was too young to know what to do with this kind of confession, this kind of honesty. He looked toward Marcus for guidance, but Marcus was gone, was already in the truck, was already moving on to the next house, the next afternoon, the next collection of objects that had meant something to someone and would mean something else to someone else before eventually meaning nothing to anyone.

The house was emptying. The walls were becoming walls again, just surfaces, just planes of space with no character. Gloria walked through it slowly, noting the absence. The living room. The dining room with its table and chairs where she had learned to eat properly, where she had been corrected when she held her fork wrong, where she had sat across from her mother for thousands of meals, talking about school or weather or nothing, her mother's face across the table as familiar as her own face but never fully understood, never fully penetrated, always slightly behind glass.

By the time the sun touched the horizon, it was done. The house was nearly empty. There were boxes stacked on the porch—items deemed to have some value, to be worth trying to sell, to be worth the space in the truck, the time at the auction house, the small chance that someone would want them. Diane was writing out a receipt. Marcus was tying down the load. William was asking if there was anything else, if she wanted any of the furniture from the bedrooms, if there was perhaps a chair or a dresser that had sentimental value.

She told him no. She told him there was nothing.

This was not quite true. There was the recipe box, which she had taken from the counter and put on the back seat of her car when she thought no one was looking. There was the memory of her mother's face on those photographs she hadn't looked at. There was the weight of the house itself, which she would have to sell now, would have to let go of, would have to allow other people to move into, to paint over, to arrange according to their own needs and whims. There was the knowledge that her mother was not anywhere in this house, had never been in this house in any meaningful sense, had only been the person who occupied the space, who moved through the rooms, who tried to make the rooms matter by the force of her own will and discipline.

Gloria locked the front door. She walked down the brick walkway where the furniture had gone. The sun was almost gone now. The sky was turning purple. The streetlights were coming on, one after another, illuminating the block with their sodium glow, making the neighborhood look artificial, look performed, look like a stage set for someone else's life rather than the place where she had actually lived, where she had actually grown, where she had learned, without anyone telling her, that the objects we hold onto are just ways of refusing to believe that we can be left, that we will be left, that leaving is the fundamental condition of being alive.

She got in her car. The recipe box was on the back seat, and she did not look at it. She started the engine. Behind her, the empty house sat dark against the darkening sky, and she thought about her mother in it, about her mother's hands arranging things, about her mother's voice calling her name, about all the ways a person could be present in a space without ever being truly known, without ever being truly understood, without ever knowing, herself, why any of it mattered or if it mattered at all.
