The morning the smoke came up, Margaret was reading yesterday's newspaper, the one that had arrived three days late via the resupply volunteer who came up once a month. She sat in the metal chair that had belonged to the tower for thirty years, the paint worn through to rust in the places where countless elbows had rested, and she was reading about a senator's indictment when she noticed the column rising from the drainage basin to the southwest, maybe four miles as the crow would fly, maybe six the way the terrain actually ran.

She set the newspaper down on her lap and watched it for five full minutes without moving. The tower swayed almost imperceptibly in the wind, a motion so subtle that newcomers never noticed it but that Margaret had learned to feel in her bones like a second heartbeat. Twenty-three summers of this, and she knew the difference between a cooking fire and a structure fire before anything else became visible. This one was too large, too dark. There was something about the smoke's texture that spoke of chemical accelerant or pressurized propane, though she kept such judgments to herself. The Forest Service didn't want cowboy interpretations anymore. They wanted measurements. Data. Things you could defend in a meeting.

She went inside to the radio.

The radio had stopped transmitting Tuesday. The receiving function still worked fine—she'd heard the crackle of dispatches all through Wednesday and Thursday—but the transmit switch did nothing but activate a small light that had no corresponding voice to carry her words downslope to the ranger station. She'd reported it, naturally. Carlos, the supervisor, had said he'd try to get someone up here before the resupply next month. Probably couldn't make it sooner, tight budget situation, you know how it is, Margaret. She did know. She'd known for three years that this was her last summer. They'd hired a new lookout—a woman named Dimitri, which Margaret assumed was short for something Slavic—and she was being trained at the tower on the opposite range. Next summer this position would be considered obsolete, a relic, part of the shift toward satellites and remote sensors that no one really believed could do what a human being could do but which cost significantly less to maintain.

Margaret was not bitter about this. She had made her peace with obsolescence a long time ago, around the same time she'd made her peace with the fact that she'd never married, that she'd never had children, that her sister Carolyn had chosen not to see her for twelve years running, and that there were probably good reasons for all of these things that had nothing to do with anyone's failures and everything to do with the way lives simply accreted their own trajectories like minerals in flowing water.

She tried the radio again. Nothing but the flat hiss of dead transmit. The morning light was perfect and clear, the kind of light that comes after a cool night in late July, and it fell through the glass panels of the tower's cab with the clarity of a prism. From her chair, Margaret could see perhaps forty miles in any direction—the whole northeastern slope of the range, the valleys branching downward like the veins on the back of an old hand, the distant blue confusion of lower elevations. On a clear day, which this was, she could even make out the small structure that had been Carolyn's cabin for the past fifteen years, though she never allowed herself to look at it directly. It existed in her peripheral vision like a small ache she'd learned to ignore.

The smoke column was getting darker.

She pulled on her Carhartt vest and went back outside. The ladder extended from the cab down to a platform, and Margaret descended carefully. Her knees weren't what they'd been, though at sixty-one she still moved better than most people half her age. The forest service had wanted her to retire at sixty. She'd negotiated for this final summer. This final, final summer, she'd been promised.

The lookout tower stood at 9,847 feet, on a granite prominence surrounded by whitebark pine and scattered subalpine fir. The access road was eight miles of switchbacks from the nearest trailhead, a distance that had been manageable when she was younger but that had become, in recent years, an increasingly abstract proposition. She'd hiked it once a week to begin with, then once every two weeks, and then—shamefully, she thought—only on her monthly resupply trips. Mostly she stayed put, radioing in her observations, reading, watching the light change through the hours.

She went to the small shed where the binoculars were stored and retrieved the pair she preferred, the older Zeiss models that had actually belonged to the previous lookout, a man named Howard who'd retired in 1991 and who'd sent a postcard from Oregon every few years until those postcards had stopped arriving sometime in the early 2000s. Through them, the smoke column became suddenly, undeniably real—not a smudge but a plume with structure and motion, with terrible purpose.

The cabin was definitely on fire.

Margaret lowered the binoculars slowly and allowed herself to think for a moment about whether she had a responsibility here. The cabin belonged to Carolyn. Carolyn did not want Margaret's involvement in her life, had made this extraordinarily clear through her refusal to accept emails, through the returned Christmas cards, through the single letter Margaret had sent—the only letter in all of twelve years—which had apparently been received but never answered. And yet. And yet there was someone Carolyn might be with during the summer, a woman Margaret had never met but whose name she had inadvertently learned through the casual conversation of the resupply volunteer, a woman named Sarah with whom Carolyn had apparently made a life.

The radio was still dead when she came back inside. Margaret sat down in her chair and looked at the silent equipment. She had maybe six hours before the smoke would dissipate enough to go unnoticed by others, if anyone else was paying attention at all. The nearest ranger station was at least two hours downhill, even at an aggressive pace. By the time a truck could make it up the access road and then down into the drainage, it would be dark.

What she should do was walk.

The realization came to her with the weight of something inevitable. She should walk down to the trailhead, to the parking area where her truck sat waiting, then drive around to the basin access, then hike in. It was a distance of perhaps four miles by car and another two miles on foot, entirely doable in good weather, except that it was the kind of thing she'd been told very specifically not to do. The Forest Service had a protocol for emergencies. The protocol did not include individual action by lookout personnel. The protocol involved communication, coordination, proper channels.

But the radio was dead.

She gathered what she thought she might need: a day pack, three liters of water, a first aid kit, her jacket, her phone (dead in most of the mountains, but worth bringing), a flashlight. She locked the tower door behind her—unnecessary, but habit—and began the descent.

The trail down was steep and technical, lined with gray talus and cairns marking the way. At this altitude and in this season, the descent took somewhere between ninety minutes and two hours depending on how quickly she was moving. Margaret had done it dozens of times over the years, though admittedly not as recently as she should have practiced it. The first mile was brutal on her knees, a pounding descent that made her wonder when exactly her body had become so fragile, so insistent on its own limitations.

By the time she reached the trailhead parking area, her lower back was locked into a tight knot and her thighs were burning. The truck was exactly where she'd left it three weeks before, covered in a light layer of road dust, the gas tank nearly full from the resupply volunteer's thoughtfulness. She sat in the driver's seat for a long moment without starting the engine, just breathing, just being still.

The road around to the basin access was slow going. Forest roads, unpaved, barely maintained. It took her forty minutes to drive what couldn't have been more than eight miles. The smoke was still visible above the trees, darker now, more established. As she drove, Margaret allowed herself to think about Carolyn, which she generally tried not to do. Carolyn, eleven months younger, with their mother's dark coloring and someone's—their father's, maybe—capacity for joy that Margaret had always felt she lacked. They'd been close once, in the way that sisters in rural areas with few options often are. They'd played house in the irrigation culverts behind their parents' ranch. They'd hidden together during family fights. They'd made elaborate promises about always being there for each other, childish vows that had evaporated over time like water in the desert sun.

The thing that had caused the breach—the specific incident—had been so small. Margaret had said something cruel at Thanksgiving dinner twenty years ago, a comment about Carolyn's first marriage that had been true but unkind. Carolyn had left the table and never quite come back. There had been attempts at reconciliation, years of them, from Margaret. There had been no reciprocation. At some point, Margaret had stopped trying, and that choice had been more definitive than any argument could have been.

The trailhead for the basin access was unmarked, just a gap in the trees where someone had parked before, tossing crushed cans among the pine needles. Margaret locked the truck and started walking. The trail descended quickly through a stand of whitebark pine, then more gently through subalpine fir and a beautiful riot of wildflowers—Indian paintbrush and columbine and some delicate alpine forget-me-nots. The smoke was closer now, the smell of it thick and chemical.

She emerged onto a rocky slope overlooking the drainage and could see the cabin directly below, maybe a quarter mile downslope. The fire had already consumed the roof, and flames were visible through the dark wood skeleton. The structure was essentially gone; there was nothing to be saved there. She stood for a long moment looking at it, waiting for some emotional response that didn't come. The cabin had never been part of her life. Carolyn had bought it with money that had nothing to do with Margaret, had decorated it, had created a life there, and Margaret had remained outside of all of it, information about it arriving only through the peripheral gossip of strangers.

She was about to turn back toward the truck when she saw movement at the edge of the clearing.

A woman, running toward the fire with something in her hand—a bucket, maybe, or a bag. Small, compact, dark hair pulled back. The woman didn't seem to notice Margaret, didn't seem aware of anything except the burning building. She moved toward it with an urgency that spoke of panic, of irrationality, of the kind of desperation that Margaret recognized because she'd felt it a long time ago, in a different context, about a different thing.

Margaret started down the slope.

The descent was treacherous, broken granite and loose scree that shifted under her feet. She moved faster than was wise, faster than her body wanted to, driven by something that felt like necessity. By the time she reached the bottom of the slope, her ankle had twisted at least once, and there was a sharp pain in her knee that she simply set aside, compartmentalized, refused to acknowledge.

"Stop!" she called out to the woman. "Stop, you can't—"

The woman turned, and Margaret's breath caught. For a moment, Margaret thought she was looking at Carolyn, that the twelve years of separation had somehow rewound themselves, but of course it wasn't. This woman was younger, probably in her mid-forties, with a sharp, intelligent face that was currently lit up with something close to madness.

"The photographs," the woman said. "Our photographs, from Japan, from before—they're in a box under the bed, and I can—"

"You can't," Margaret said. "The structure's too compromised. You'll die if you go in there."

"You don't understand," the woman said. She was still holding whatever she'd been carrying—it looked like an empty bird cage, which seemed inexplicably sad. "Those pictures are all we have. The insurance won't—Carolyn, she needs—"

"Carolyn isn't here," Margaret said. She didn't know this to be true, but it seemed probable. "Is she inside?"

"No, no, she went to town this morning, she's at work, she won't be back until—" The woman's voice cracked. "I just need the pictures. I just need to—"

Margaret grabbed her arm. Close up, she could see the woman's hands were burned, red and blistering. Margaret had no idea how long she'd been trying to get into that building, but it had been long enough to do damage.

"We need to get you away from here," Margaret said. "Come on. Where's your car?"

"By the house. But I can't—"

"You can't save photographs," Margaret said. The woman was fighting her, but Margaret held firm. "You especially can't save them if you're badly burned. Come on."

She pulled the woman back from the building, away from the heat that was now intense enough to raise visible waves in the air. The woman was crying, making small helpless sounds, and Margaret—contrary to every habit of her life, contrary to the entire structure of her personality—was making soft murmuring sounds that might have been comfort, might have been prayer, might have been nothing but the noise of someone trying to convince another person that survival was possible.

The car was a small Subaru, parked behind the cabin where they'd somehow had a view of it. Margaret used the woman's keys to open the passenger door, then helped her sit down, gravity pulling at them both. The woman was shaking badly now, adrenaline draining, leaving only the residue of trauma.

"I'm Margaret," Margaret said. "Margaret Henley."

The woman looked up at her, and Margaret saw recognition flicker across her face—not of Margaret herself, but of the name.

"You're Carolyn's sister," the woman said.

"Yes," Margaret said. "I'm Carolyn's sister."

"She didn't... I didn't know you were..."

"No," Margaret said. "No one did." She pulled out her phone to call for help and remembered that service up here was non-existent. "We need to get you down to where there's cell reception. Can you walk to the truck?"

The woman nodded, though she looked uncertain about whether her body would comply with the request. Margaret helped her stand, and they made it to the truck—Margaret's truck, though the woman didn't seem to register the peculiarity of this, that a stranger had arrived on the property with her own vehicle and a seeming knowledge of the emergency protocols.

The drive down to lower elevation took thirty minutes, the longest thirty minutes Margaret could remember experiencing in years. The woman—whose name turned out to be Sarah, which Margaret had known and forgotten and now knew again—kept apologizing for the photographs, for the fire, for not being able to say the things she needed to say. Margaret didn't ask her to explain. Sometimes people needed to fill silence. Margaret had learned, over twenty-three summers alone, that silence could be a mercy but could also be a burden.

At the point where the road dropped below the ridge and into an area where cellular service worked, Margaret pulled over and called 911. She reported the structure fire, gave the location, provided the information about Sarah's injuries. The dispatcher wanted details, and Margaret provided them with the clarity of long practice at the radio. By the time they hung up, two fire trucks were already en route, coming from the district station twenty minutes away.

"They'll be here in about fifteen minutes," Margaret said.

Sarah was staring out the window at the forest. "She's going to hate me for not saving them," she said quietly. "Those photographs. They're irreplaceable. We had copies, but they were in the cabin, and she loves—"

"She'll be glad you're alive," Margaret said.

"You don't know her," Sarah said.

"No," Margaret agreed. "I don't."

The fire trucks arrived with their lights blazing, and the paramedics went immediately for Sarah, assessing her burns, wrapping them in gauze, asking the standard questions. They wanted to take her to the hospital in town, and she didn't resist. She looked at Margaret once, just before they moved her to the ambulance.

"Thank you," she said.

"You're welcome," Margaret said.

The fire chief—a woman Margaret vaguely recognized from previous seasons—came over to ask how Margaret had found Sarah and what she was doing at the cabin. Margaret explained about the radio malfunction and her decision to hike down and check on the fire. She left out the part about Carolyn, about the twelve-year estrangement, about the fact that she'd been watching that cabin from the tower for so long that she could probably have drawn it from memory. Some things didn't need explanation. Some things were just facts, and facts stood on their own.

By the time Margaret made it back to her truck and began the drive back up to the tower, the sun was starting its descent toward the western ridges. The cabin was still burning, though less actively now that the structural integrity had failed completely. She could see the fire crew's trucks positioned around it, could see the water arcing from hoses, could see the systematic reduction of flames to smoke to ash.

She drove slowly, thinking about nothing in particular, or perhaps about everything at once. The road was rougher on the return journey, or perhaps she just felt it more, her body reporting the day's exertions with increasing specificity. By the time she pulled up to the tower's parking area, it was nearly dark, and the stars were beginning to emerge with their familiar clarity.

The climb back up the trail took longer than the descent had, her legs protesting with each switchback, her breath coming harder than it should have. But Margaret climbed steadily, without stopping, without allowing herself to consider the possibility of stopping. By the time she reached the tower's door, the night had fully arrived, and she was standing beneath the wide arc of stars that had been her constant companions for so many summers.

Inside, the radio remained silent. Margaret poured herself a glass of water from the supply tank and sat in her chair, looking out at the forest. Somewhere down there, Carolyn was probably learning about the fire. Somewhere in a hospital, Sarah was having her burns treated, receiving the kind of medical attention that Margaret had prevented her from needing. Somewhere in the vast landscape of human estrangement and small redemptions, the world was turning on exactly as it had been, indifferent to the choices that divided people and the unexpected moments of grace that sometimes—rarely, but sometimes—stitched them back together.

Margaret picked up yesterday's newspaper and finished reading the article about the senator's indictment. It seemed less important now, though she couldn't have said why. The fire column had completely dissipated, and the night was thick with the smell of smoke, with the smell of something ending, with the complicated smell of survival.

She would leave this tower in eight weeks, when the summer was finished. She would probably never see Carolyn again. But she had done what she could, when it mattered, and that would have to be enough. The radio would be fixed eventually, or it wouldn't be. The tower would be decommissioned, and someone else—probably Dimitri, with her youth and her ability to transmit via satellites and whatever future protocols the Forest Service determined—would take the position, and Margaret would become another relic, another story about the old days.

The night was clear and cold and full of stars, and Margaret was still here, still breathing, still capable of moving through the world and affecting it, if only for a moment, if only in the smallest of ways. She closed her eyes and let the swaying of the tower, that imperceptible movement that had become like her heartbeat, rock her toward something that might have been peace.
