The congressmen wanted to see the power, wanted to feel it, wanted to go home and tell their constituents they'd stood in the presence of something historic and terrible and American. Jack Lovett understood this. He'd seen it happen at every test site, this hunger in civilian observers to be near the thing itself, to have their hair blown back by the shock wave, to have their bodies register what their minds couldn't quite process.

What most of them didn't understand was that you couldn't really see it, not the way they imagined. The light got you first, a brightness so complete it erased the concept of darkness. Your eyes shut down. Your skin felt the heat before your brain registered the flash. Then came the sound, which wasn't really sound the way you knew it—it was pressure, a physical thing that entered your body through your bones and rearranged something fundamental in your cells. By the time you heard the actual boom, it was over. By the time you could think about what you'd witnessed, you were already forgetting the details.

He'd tried to explain this to Inez once, years before, back when he still thought such things could be explained. Back when he still thought anything could be explained to anyone. She'd listened in that way she had, not interrupting, giving him the space to talk himself into or out of whatever position he was constructing. She was good at that, had always been good at it. It was one of the reasons he'd stayed involved with her, one of the reasons he kept finding excuses to show up at her various functions and diplomatic dinners, one of the reasons he'd become, in his way, indispensable to her.

The thing about the tests was that they created a strange intimacy among the people involved. You were all complicit. You were all there to witness an exercise of power so absolute it made questions of morality seem quaint, obsolete. You signed agreements. You understood the implications. You showed up anyway. This created a bond, a brotherhood almost, though not in any way you'd want to examine too closely. The photographers and the physicists and the technical guys and the observers, the handful of congressmen and their wives—you all knew something together that couldn't be unknowown, that couldn't be discussed except in fragments and off the record and usually with alcohol involved.

There'd been a woman at one of the Nevada events, wife of a congressman from Arizona, and she'd gotten drunk the night before a shot and tried to walk out into the desert. Someone had to go after her. Jack had volunteered. He'd found her about a mile out, standing in the darkness with her shoes off, looking up at stars. She was crying, though whether from fear or clarity or something else he couldn't determine. He'd taken her arm and walked her back without speaking. The next day she'd asked him not to mention it to her husband. He'd agreed. He'd kept that agreement, along with a hundred others like it.

Now he sat across from Inez in whatever suite the State Department had arranged for her, probably the best room in whatever hotel was closest to the Capitol, and he told her about the gardenias because it was safe, because it was something true that couldn't hurt either of them to acknowledge. The tests were over now. The underground testing had continued for years after the atmospheric tests stopped, a quieter business, less theatrical, more bureaucratic. That was over too. Most people had forgotten it had ever happened. History had moved on to other horrors, other displays of national capability.

But Jack remembered. His memory was one of his assets, one of the reasons people like Inez valued his company. He could hold events in his mind with clarity and precision. He could recall conversations from years ago with enough detail to be useful. This made him valuable to certain branches of government, to certain individuals who needed someone who could remember what had actually been said in rooms where the record had been deliberately vague.

"You remember everything," Inez said now, not as a compliment but as a statement of fact.

"Not everything," he said. "I forget a lot. Useless things. But the important things, yeah, those tend to stick."

"And you've decided to tell me about the gardenias now because why, exactly?"

He smiled. This was what he'd always liked about her—that she cut through the sentimentality immediately, that she understood a story was never just a story, that there was always an agenda buried inside.

"Because you're thinking about the past," he said. "You've got something coming up, some decision you haven't made yet, and you're wondering what it would mean, what it would cost. I can see it in how you've been sitting. You've got that particular kind of tension you get when you're trying to weigh something that can't actually be weighed."

She didn't respond immediately. She got up and walked to the window, looked out at whatever Washington night was happening below them. The city at night was a study in controlled lighting, he'd always thought. Strategic illumination. Nothing left to chance. The monuments lit from below so they'd cast the right shadow at the right angle. He'd observed the planning that went into it, the committees that had to approve the lighting designs for various government buildings.

"My husband's being considered for a particular position," she said finally. "Not his first choice. Not mine. But it's important to the administration, and they're applying a certain amount of pressure."

Jack nodded. He'd already known this. He made it his business to know what was being said in the corridors where power accumulated. He'd heard the rumors about Dick Victor's next move, the potential appointment that would either elevate him or destroy him depending on how you looked at it.

"The position in question," Inez continued, "involves relationships with certain foreign governments. Certain arrangements that have been in place for quite a while. Arrangements that probably shouldn't have been made, but were made, and now have to be either continued or carefully unwound."

"And you're worried that if Dick takes the position, he'll find out things he shouldn't know," Jack said.

"I'm worried that he already knows things he shouldn't know," Inez said. "And that knowing them might be exactly why they want him for the position."

Jack stood and joined her at the window. Washington stretched out below them, a city built on the principle that information was currency and secrets were leverage. He'd spent his entire career navigating that principle, understanding its implications, profiting from it in ways that didn't usually involve money.

"The thing about the tests," he said, returning to his earlier narrative, "was that they proved something that everybody already knew but nobody wanted to acknowledge. They proved that we had the power to end everything. Not to win a war. Not to achieve any particular objective. Just to end everything. To turn the world into glass. That's what those pink skies meant, when you really thought about it. That's what the light was showing you."

"I'm not sure I understand the connection," Inez said.

"The connection is that once you know something like that, once it's been demonstrated to you in a physical, sensory way, you can't unknow it. You have to make decisions about what you're going to do with that knowledge. Some people use it to argue for peace. Some use it to argue for more weapons. Some people just try to forget it ever happened. But everybody has to do something with it. Everybody has to make a choice."

He could see her in the reflection of the glass, the way she was holding herself, the particular tightness around her mouth that indicated she was working through something difficult.

"If Dick takes this position," she said, "he'll be responsible for maintaining relationships with certain individuals and governments that we probably shouldn't be maintaining relationships with. But if he doesn't take it, someone else will take it, and that someone else might be much less careful about how those relationships are maintained."

"So you're thinking about the lesser evil," Jack said.

"I'm thinking about the world that actually exists," Inez said, "as opposed to the world that should exist. I'm thinking about the fact that power is always messy and that choosing not to exercise it is still a choice, still has consequences."

Jack understood this perfectly. It was the argument he'd made to himself a thousand times, the argument that had justified every morally questionable decision he'd made in the service of various administrations and various private interests. It was the argument that ultimately proved circular—that you couldn't step back from power once you'd acknowledged its existence and its necessity. You could only try to manage it, to shape it in directions that seemed less harmful than the alternatives.

"There's a man," Jack said carefully, "who came through Bikini in 1954. A physicist. Brilliant guy, one of the people actually designing the devices. And at some point during the waiting period—and there was always a waiting period, waiting for the weather, waiting for congressional approval, waiting for the bureaucracy to align itself properly—he asked me what I thought about what we were doing."

"What did you say?" Inez asked.

"I told him what I'm telling you now. I said that the world is full of people making decisions they shouldn't make, doing things that probably shouldn't be done, and that the only question that matters is whether you're going to participate in that process or whether you're going to step aside and let other people do it. Because the process continues whether you're involved or not. The only difference is whether you have any influence over how it proceeds."

Inez turned away from the window. "And did that convince him?"

"It convinced him to sign on for another year," Jack said. "I saw him ten years later, at a conference in Geneva. He'd won prizes by then. International recognition. He was drinking heavily and having trouble sleeping. I asked him if he still thought we'd made the right choice."

"What did he say?"

"He said that choices of that magnitude don't have right and wrong answers. They only have consequences. And that the consequences are things you carry with you forever."

Inez sat back down on the sofa, pulled a pillow toward her, held it against her chest in a way that made her look younger than she was, more vulnerable. But Jack knew better. Inez was never vulnerable, not in any way that lasted. She had a capacity for decision-making that he'd always admired and feared in roughly equal measure.

"You're telling me I should encourage him to take it," she said.

"I'm telling you that you're going to encourage him or you're not, and that whatever you decide will probably be the right decision at the time, and it will probably come back to haunt you later. That's how these things work. That's how the world actually functions."

He walked over to the bar that was set up in one corner of the suite and poured himself a drink without asking. She didn't object. She watched him do it, watched him make his way back to a chair across from her, watched him settle into the kind of posture that indicated he was prepared to sit there as long as necessary.

"The thing about power," Jack said, "is that it requires complicity. You can't exercise it alone. You need people around you who are willing to do things, willing to participate in arrangements that don't bear too much examination. You need people who understand that certain conversations shouldn't be documented, certain decisions shouldn't be thoroughly explained, certain actions are necessary but not defensible."

"And you think Dick has those people?" Inez asked.

"I know Dick has those people," Jack said. "I know because I'm one of them. I've been one of them for twenty years. And if he takes this position, he'll need to lean on those relationships, depend on people who understand how the system really works, as opposed to how it's supposed to work."

He took a drink. The whiskey was good, smooth, the kind of thing that the State Department provided when it wanted people to feel special, accommodated, taken care of.

"But here's the thing," he continued. "If you go into something like this, if you agree to be part of this kind of structure, you have to understand that you're making a bargain. You're trading certain kinds of moral clarity for a certain amount of power. You're saying that you're willing to look the other way on certain things in exchange for the ability to shape events on other things. That's the bargain. That's always been the bargain."

Inez set the pillow aside. She looked at him directly now, with the kind of attention that made him understand why people listened to her, why she'd been effective in every position she'd ever held.

"You're making it sound like a bad bargain," she said.

"No," Jack said carefully. "I'm making it sound like a real bargain. Like something that has actual costs and actual benefits, and that the costs don't disappear just because you've decided the benefits are worth it."

They sat in silence for a while. Outside, Washington continued its business, people moving through corridors, decisions being made in offices lit by carefully controlled lighting, arrangements being maintained that no one wanted to examine too closely. Jack had been complicit in various aspects of this system for long enough that he could see it clearly now, could understand how it perpetuated itself, could recognize the precise moment when someone like Inez was deciding whether to become more deeply enmeshed in it.

"I think I already know what I'm going to do," she said finally.

"Of course you do," Jack said. "You've known for days. You just needed to talk through it with someone who wouldn't tell you that you were making a mistake, because we both know you're not making a mistake. You're making the only choice available to you, which is not the same thing as making the right choice, but which is something that has to be made anyway."

She smiled slightly, a real smile, the kind that suggested she was relieved to have this conversation with someone who understood what was actually at stake.

"When will you tell him?" Jack asked.

"Tonight, I think. After dinner. I'll tell him it's an opportunity. I'll tell him it's what he's been working toward. I'll tell him all the things that are true and that will convince him that this is something he wants, even if some part of him knows that it's something he shouldn't want."

"And what will you tell yourself?" Jack asked.

She looked away again. "I'll tell myself that I'm doing it for the right reasons. I'll tell myself that someone has to do this work and that if Dick does it he'll do it carefully, thoughtfully, with some regard for the consequences. I'll tell myself that the alternative would be worse."

"Will you believe those things?"

"Some of them," she said. "On some days, I probably will."

Jack finished his drink. He set the glass down carefully, noting the slight ring it left on the table where condensation had formed on the outside of the glass. These small marks, these tiny traces of presence—they were all that remained of most human actions, he'd come to think. Everything else was forgotten or distorted or deliberately erased.

"There's something else you should know," he said. "Something that will probably matter down the line."

"Tell me," she said.

"The administration is planning something," Jack said. "I don't have all the details yet, but there are people involved—people outside the normal chain of command—who are preparing for a situation that they think might develop with one of the countries your husband will be dealing with. It's not authorized. It's not going to be authorized. It exists in that space where things happen that officially never occurred."

Inez absorbed this information without apparent reaction. But Jack could see the calculation happening behind her eyes, the immediate reassessment of what she'd just agreed to.

"And you're telling me this because?" she said.

"Because if Dick's going to take this position, he should know that he's walking into a situation that's more complicated than what's going to be presented to him in official briefings. Because people like you and me, we have an obligation to the people we care about to make sure they understand the terrain they're walking into. Even if we don't tell them explicitly, even if we only hint at it, we owe them that much."

She nodded slowly. "Is it something that can be stopped?"

"No," Jack said. "But it might be something that can be influenced. That depends on whether Dick has the kind of access and credibility this position would give him. That depends on whether he's willing to use that access in ways that the people who appointed him might not have anticipated."

"That's asking for a lot," Inez said.

"Yes," Jack agreed. "It is. But you're asking for a lot by encouraging him to take this position. You're asking him to be complicit in a system that operates on multiple levels, some of which he won't fully understand until he's already committed to them. So if you're going to ask him to do that, you should probably give him some idea of what he's actually walking into."

Inez stood up and walked to the bar. She poured herself a drink—gin, over ice, very little vermouth—and returned to her seat. She took a sip before speaking again.

"How long have you known about this?" she asked.

"About two weeks," Jack said. "I've been trying to figure out how to tell you, or whether I should tell you at all. But it seems like the kind of thing that matters, especially now."

"Can you stop it?" she asked.

"No," Jack said. "But I can probably get some information about how it's likely to proceed. And if Dick's in the right position, if he has the right kind of access, he might be able to shape how it develops. He might be able to make sure that when things go wrong—and they will go wrong, these things always do—that they go wrong in ways that are somewhat less catastrophic than they might otherwise."

This was the world they lived in, Jack thought. This was the actual substance of power and influence and responsibility. Not the clean narratives of history textbooks, not the official statements and diplomatic cables, but these conversations in hotel suites, these careful discussions of what someone might do if they had leverage and access and just enough complicity to make them useful.

"You should go," Inez said. "Dick will be back soon, and this isn't a conversation he needs to walk in on."

Jack stood. He set his glass carefully on a side table and moved toward the door.

"One more thing," Inez said as he reached the threshold.

He turned back.

"Thank you," she said. "For being honest. For telling me about the other thing. For understanding what this costs."

"I didn't do it for gratitude," Jack said. "I did it because you deserve to know what you're actually walking into. Because some small part of me still believes that matters."

He left her there in the suite, sitting alone in carefully controlled light, contemplating the choice she'd already made and its various consequences, visible and hidden. As he walked through the hotel corridors toward the elevator, he found himself thinking again about those Pacific tests, about the pink sky he'd described, about the strange moment when observation becomes complicity and complicity becomes choice and choice becomes the only thing that remains.
