# The Return

The letter had arrived on Tuesday, cream-colored stock with an engraved border that suggested both expense and doubt. Margaret's name was written in a careful hand that she recognized immediately as belonging to someone who had learned penmanship from a particular book, one that emphasized the importance of making a good impression on people you would never know. The invitation was to the wedding of Claire Whitmore and Thomas Kendall, to be held at the Mariposa Hotel, which had been rebuilt seven years after the fire with, according to the announcement in the Register, "meticulous attention to historical detail and modern safety standards." Margaret had not been to the Mariposa since the morning she walked out of it at six o'clock, still in her chambermaid's uniform, with her hair singed on one side and her left hand wrapped in gauze that would need changing by evening.

She told herself that she would not go. The reasons seemed obvious: she had no real connection to Claire Whitmore beyond a brief employment at the Whitmore house in 1962, during which she had dusted furniture and washed windows and once found Claire's birth control pills in a drawer beneath her stockings; she had even less connection to Thomas Kendall, whom she had never met; and the Mariposa Hotel represented, if not actively, then in some passive and immutable way, a period of her life that she had been quite deliberate about putting behind her. Yet the moment she had read the announcement of the hotel's reopening—a three-paragraph feature that mentioned the fire only in passing, as a "notable incident in the hotel's distinguished history"—Margaret had understood that she would, in fact, go. She had understood this the way one understands a commitment one has already made: not as a choice but as an inevitability disguised as one.

Her mother called on Thursday. "Why would you want to go back there?" her mother asked, and Margaret could hear in her voice the particular anxiety that had characterized their conversations for the better part of three decades, the anxiety of a woman who had nursed a daughter's burns and who believed that her daughter had recovered only to the extent that recovery was possible, which was to say not very far at all. "It's not even your hotel anymore."

"That's true," Margaret said. She was sitting at her kitchen table with coffee that had gone cold some time ago. "But Claire Whitmore has invited me to her wedding."

"You barely knew Claire Whitmore."

"No," Margaret agreed. "But she knew who I was."

The distinction seemed to matter, though she could not have articulated why. Her mother, who had spent years trying to articulate precisely these distinctions on Margaret's behalf, to various doctors and sympathetic relatives and eventually to herself, gave up. "Get a nice dress," she said finally. "Something you feel pretty in. When you look pretty you feel less anxious. I read that somewhere."

The weekend before the wedding, Margaret went to the department store and looked at dresses for an hour without buying anything. She was fifty-three years old and had the kind of face that had never been beautiful and had merely continued being unremarkable as she aged. She bought a dark blue dress that was probably more appropriate for a funeral than a wedding, and a necklace made of pearls that had been cultured in a tank somewhere and would fool no one. She drove to the coast on Saturday morning, which was earlier than necessary, but she wanted time to sit in the car in the parking lot and look at the building before she went inside.

The Mariposa had been enlarged, though not in any way that seemed to improve it. The new section had been constructed to match the architectural style of the original, which meant it matched it the way a copy matches an original—precisely enough to be visibly false. The original section looked diminished beside it, as though the fire had shrunk it down to a different size. Margaret sat behind the wheel of her car, which was a Chevrolet that she had bought used in 1978 and that still ran well enough, though it had developed a way of pulling slightly to the right on the highway. The morning was overcast, the kind of coastal morning that suggested fog but had not yet committed to it.

She had worked at the Mariposa for eight months, from March to November of 1962. She had been twenty-four years old and newly arrived in the state, and she had taken the job with the understanding that it was temporary, that she was waiting for something better, which was what everyone who worked at the Mariposa was waiting for. The hotel attracted this kind of person: people who were between things, who believed themselves to be in transition to something larger or more important or at least more interesting than the making of beds and the removal of towels. Margaret's reason for being in transition was the ordinary one, which was that she had recently ended an engagement and had moved away from the town where the engagement had ended, and she was waiting to discover who she was when she was not engaged to anyone and not living with her parents.

In fact, she had discovered who she was quite quickly, and the discovery was disappointing: she was a person who was rather good at making beds and removing towels, who did not mind the physical work and did not mind the hierarchy of the hotel, which was strict and comprehensible and did not require her to be anything other than competent. She had a room on the third floor of the staff quarters, a narrow room with a view of the parking lot, and she had begun to think that she might stay longer than she had planned to stay, might stay for a long time, in fact, which had seemed to her at the moment to be a viable plan rather than a failure of imagination.

The fire had started in the laundry room on the second floor of the east wing, where Margaret had just delivered a cart of soiled linens. It was November, and it was early morning, and there were not many guests in the hotel—it was that period between the summer season and the holiday season when the Mariposa, like all such establishments, waited for people to have reasons to be somewhere other than home. She had delivered the linens and was turning her cart toward the hallway when she heard something, not a sound exactly but a quality of sound, a thickness to the air, and when she turned around the laundry manager, a small man named Reeves who had worked at the hotel for nineteen years, was standing very still with his hand on the pressing machine, looking at something Margaret could not yet see.

What she remembered afterward was not the fire itself but the moment before it, the fact that Reeves had time to turn to her and say "The line," pointing to the electrical line that had come loose from the wall, before the moment when she should have been afraid because she was not afraid yet, she was only beginning to understand that something bad was happening, and then the moment when she was afraid but also somewhat distant from the fear, as though it were happening to someone else and she was watching it with the kind of detached interest one brings to a film. She had run. Or rather, she had walked very quickly toward the door while Reeves was still pointing at the electrical line, as though the identification of the problem would solve it, and she had made it to the door and into the hallway just before the moment when running would have been necessary.

The fire department had been there within fifteen minutes. She had never understood how it could have been so quick, but people kept telling her that it was very fast, that she was lucky, that the fire had been contained to the laundry room and two adjacent rooms, that no one had been seriously hurt, that she should consider herself fortunate. They said this while treating the burns on her hand and arm, which was why she understood that "seriously hurt" was a category that did not include her. Reeves, they told her later, had been burned more extensively, though he had also been treated and released, and had moved to Arizona with his wife some months later, and Margaret had never seen him again, though she had sent him a card when she heard he was leaving, a generic card about new beginnings, which seemed both relevant and not relevant at all.

She had stayed in the hospital for three days. The burns were not severe, the doctor told her, but they would scar. Her mother had driven up from two hours inland and had stayed in a motel and had spent the days sitting beside her bed with a look of concentrated pain on her face that had seemed, to Margaret at the time, like an additional injury. "You could have been killed," her mother had said, and Margaret had understood that what her mother meant was "Why weren't you killed," that her mother had understood, in some way, that people who were in transition as severely as Margaret was in transition were the kind of people who died in fires, and her mother had been waiting for this news, and had not received it, and was trying to make sense of a reprieve that did not feel like a reprieve.

The Mariposa had closed for renovations. When it reopened, six months later, Margaret did not go back. She had enough money saved to live on for a few months, and she had decided that the fire was a message, though she could not have said a message about what. She took a job at the state university as a research assistant in the biology department, a position that required no particular qualification beyond a willingness to wash beakers and organize data, and she had stayed in that job for thirty years, had become indispensable in the way that people who wash beakers carefully become indispensable, and had found it satisfying in a way that did not quite compensate for the satisfaction she had abandoned but came close enough that she had stopped thinking about it much.

The scars on her hand and arm had faded over time. People noticed them less as Margaret aged, not because they had disappeared but because Margaret's age made them seem less tragic, less like the mark of a dramatic event and more like the kind of thing that happened to people who had lived for a while. She had never married again. She had had a boyfriend once, a man named David who worked in the physics department and who had been kind and interested in her and who she had allowed to love her for three years before she had understood that she could not love him back in any way that was not performative, and she had ended it as gently as she could, which meant she had ended it and then allowed him to be angry about it, which was his right.

The parking lot at the Mariposa was fuller than she had expected for a Saturday afternoon. She had planned to arrive at four, to have the hotel mostly to herself while she adjusted to being there, and then to go to the rehearsal dinner, which was to be held in one of the smaller dining rooms, the kind of dinner where Claire would look stressed and Thomas would look affable and Margaret would sit at a table with strangers and would be kind to them and would go back to her hotel room and would sleep deeply and without dreaming. But the lot was full, which meant that people were already here, which meant that she could not arrive quietly.

She made herself get out of the car. She carried her dress in a garment bag and her pearl necklace in her purse and she walked across the parking lot toward the entrance. The entrance had been redesigned. The original entrance had been a revolving door with a kind of architectural severity to it, and this new entrance was the same door but flanked by something that the architects had presumably thought was welcoming, though what they had created was just more doors, more entrances, a proliferation of access that somehow made entering more difficult rather than less.

The lobby had been enlarged. Margaret knew that it had been enlarged because the lobby where she had worked had been a certain size, had had a certain proportion, and this lobby was larger while attempting to maintain the same proportion, which made it feel like a dream version of itself, like the lobby she remembered but not quite right. There was a new front desk with new staff and a new computer system and a potted plant that was approximately the size of a small tree. The young woman at the front desk looked up as Margaret approached, and her expression, which had been pleasant in a neutral way, became more alert, as though she sensed something in Margaret that required particular attention.

"I'm here for the Whitmore-Kendall wedding," Margaret said.

"Of course," the young woman said. "Let me just check your reservation."

Margaret gave her name. The young woman typed something and then looked up again, with an expression that suggested apology. "I'm not finding a reservation under that name."

"Claire Whitmore invited me," Margaret said. "Perhaps it's under a different name."

"May I ask your relationship to the bride?"

This was the question that Margaret had been afraid of, the question that required her to explain something that she could not explain, which was that she had barely known Claire Whitmore and that Claire Whitmore had barely known her, but that something about their brief overlap had been memorable enough that Claire had invited her, decades later, to her wedding. "We knew each other a long time ago," Margaret said. "Perhaps you could check with Claire directly."

She was made to sit in the lobby while the young woman called upstairs. Margaret sat on a chair that looked uncomfortable and probably was, in the way that the Mariposa's chairs had always been uncomfortable, designed to prevent guests from becoming so comfortable that they stopped moving. She looked around at the enlarged, proportional lobby, at the new carpet that was meant to resemble the old carpet, at the artwork on the walls that had been selected by someone who had thought carefully about what kind of artwork belonged in a hotel lobby, and she understood that what she was looking at was not a restoration of the Mariposa but a replacement of it, a building that had been constructed to approximate the feeling of the Mariposa without actually being the Mariposa, which was exactly right, which was exactly what the insurance company would have required.

Claire appeared in the lobby fifteen minutes later. She was wearing a nightgown and a robe, which suggested that she had been asleep or had been in the process of preparing for the rehearsal dinner, and she was moving quickly, the way brides move when they are on their way to do something else and must therefore condense all the time they have into something efficient. She was fifty-three years old now, the same age as Margaret, which had not been obvious when Claire was young and Margaret was the woman who dusted her room and washed her windows and pretended not to notice her birth control pills.

"Margaret," Claire said, and she sounded surprised and pleased, which Margaret understood to be the sound of someone who has done a kind thing and is being rewarded for it. "You came. I wasn't sure you would."

"I was surprised to receive an invitation," Margaret said.

"I know," Claire said. "But I remembered you. I remembered that you worked for my parents, and then I remembered reading about the fire—I must have seen it in the paper at some point—and I thought, That was the girl, and then I thought, I wonder what happened to her, and then I thought, I'm getting married, wouldn't it be nice to have her there, and so I asked my mother if she knew how to find you, and my mother actually knew your mother, or knew of her, and so I found your address. Is that all right? Is that not strange?"

"It is strange," Margaret said. "But I'm glad to be here."

This was the kind of thing one said, and it seemed to be what Claire needed to hear. She put her hand on Margaret's arm—a gesture that was intimate in the way that gestures between women of a certain age could be—and told her that she was in room 407, that there was a dinner that evening, that Margaret should rest for now and that she would see her at dinner. Then Claire excused herself and departed, leaving Margaret in the lobby with the young woman at the front desk, who was now searching for a reservation under a different system, who had apparently found something, and who was watching Margaret with an expression that suggested she had, at some point, understood who Margaret was, or at least understood that Margaret was someone, and that this was important information.

Margaret's room was on the fourth floor, where it had not been difficult to arrange a room, in fact, where rooms appeared to be available, which suggested that while the hotel had been successfully reconstructed, the problem of empty rooms on weekend nights had not been solved, that success, even in the context of a hotel that had been rebuilt with meticulous attention to historical detail, was something that remained elusive. The hallway on the fourth floor was quiet. The room was clean in the way that hotel rooms are clean, which is to say that it had been cleaned thoroughly and recently and in a way that would be entirely erased by any actual use of the space.

Margaret stood in the middle of the room for a long time. The window looked out over the parking lot toward the highway, and beyond that toward the ocean, which was visible from certain angles and invisible from others, and which she could hear if she was quiet. She set her suitcase on the rack at the foot of the bed and her garment bag on the back of the chair. She sat on the bed and tried to understand what she was doing here.

The answer came to her, eventually, in fragments: she was here because she had been invited; she had been invited because Claire remembered her; Claire remembered her because she had been the girl in the fire, the girl who had worked in her house and then had been destroyed in a modest way and had disappeared; Margaret was here to prove that she had not, in fact, been destroyed, or if she had been destroyed, that the destruction had not been permanent. This was a kind thing that Claire was doing, even if Claire did not understand that it was a kind thing, even if Claire had simply thought that having Margaret here would be interesting in some way, would provide the wedding with a certain texture.

Margaret unpacked her suitcase. She hung her dress in the closet, which smelled like cleaning solution. She arranged her toiletries on the bathroom counter. She looked at her hand in the mirror, at the scarring that had faded but not disappeared, and she understood that she was looking at evidence, that the evidence was still there, would always be there, but that no one particularly needed to see it anymore because no one particularly cared. She had been afraid that returning to the Mariposa would feel like stepping backward into a time that had not yet been resolved, but what she understood now was that the time had been resolved—not in the way that you resolve something by fixing it, but in the way that you resolve something by living past it, by allowing it to become history instead of current events.

At six o'clock she put on her funeral dress and her pearl necklace and took the elevator downstairs to the dining room where the rehearsal dinner was beginning. Claire saw her enter and smiled, and in that smile Margaret recognized something that she had not expected to find: the understanding that they were, both of them, simply women now, middle-aged women at a wedding, and that whatever had happened between them in the past was not relevant to who they were now, which meant that it was both infinitely important and not important at all.
