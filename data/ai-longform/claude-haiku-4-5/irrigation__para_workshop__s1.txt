# Irrigation

Marcus was down in the ditch before sunrise, which meant Daniel had to follow. The water hadn't run in four days, and what was left of the apricot trees had begun their dying in earnest—a subtle curling of the leaves that Daniel recognized from childhood, from the years their father had let things slip. That was before he'd died, before the responsibilities stopped being theoretical.

"Hand me the wrench," Marcus called up. His voice emerged thin from the concrete channel, swallowed by its own echo. The June heat hadn't yet arrived in full; the morning still held a memory of cool, but Daniel could feel it gathering in the eastern sky, patient and inevitable.

Daniel handed down the wrench. Marcus had always been the one who knew how things worked. Five years younger and still he'd gravitated toward the mechanical world while Daniel hung back with the ledgers, the contracts, the slow mathematics of decline. Their father had encouraged this division of labor, though he'd never put it that way. He'd simply ask Marcus to look at the pump, ask Daniel to figure out if they could afford to fix it.

"The coupling's cracked," Marcus said. "You see it? Here." He held up a piece of metal, dark and split along one edge like a fruit that had dried in the sun.

"Can we replace it?"

"Should be able to. But first we have to get the line drained. That means upstream." Marcus pulled himself out of the ditch with the easy strength he'd maintained even as the orchard failed around them. Dirt had already worked into his beard, graying it prematurely. He was forty-two. Daniel was forty-seven.

They walked the line north, toward the irrigation pond that had shrunk that year to a size that embarrassed them both. Their grandfather had dug it in 1956, had envisioned it as the heart of something permanent. In photographs from the sixties, it looked like a small lake, ringed with willows, the orchard spreading toward the horizon in geometric rows that suggested infinite productivity. Now the bottom showed in places, cracked clay visible in the June light like some exposed nerve.

"Dad let it go," Marcus said, which was true but unfair. Their father had let a lot of things go in the last decade, distracted by first their mother's illness and then his own. He'd died eight years ago, and Daniel had spent the intervening period simply trying to maintain the status quo, which turned out to be impossible. The status quo required constant forward motion, required money, required a kind of faith in the future that Daniel couldn't seem to locate in himself.

"We're letting it go now," Daniel said.

"No," Marcus said. "We're trying to save it." He had crouched down at the valve near the pond's edge, examining the mechanism that controlled water flow. "This one's not stuck, at least. Come on."

They opened the valve and spent the next hour draining the line, watching the water run down the slope of the property toward the county road. It was wasted water, water that would never nourish anything, and Daniel found himself thinking about money again—the way it seemed to slip away regardless of their efforts, like water finding its level.

"Sell it," Marcus said suddenly.

Daniel turned. "What?"

"The orchard. Just sell it. That's what you're thinking about, isn't it? That's what you think about every day."

They were standing on the sloped ground between the pond and the first row of trees. From here you could see across the entire property, all 140 acres of it, though it felt like much less. The trees—those that remained, those they'd managed not to pull out in the lean years—cast long shadows toward the west. In the distance, the Sierra Nevada held snow in its high reaches, pale against the morning blue.

"Someone has to think about it," Daniel said.

"And someone has to fix the irrigation line." Marcus had already started walking back toward the pump house, where the spare coupling waited in the cool darkness of the shed. "Someone has to do the work that actually matters."

This was their recurring argument, the one they'd begun having in earnest about three years ago, when the bank had first started to be gentle about payments, which meant they would eventually stop being gentle. The arguments had a rhythm to them now, predictable as a heartbeat. Daniel would cite figures, Marcus would cite duty. Daniel would mention the developers who'd been sniffing around, Marcus would mention their father. Daniel would say that love of the land didn't pay property taxes, and Marcus would say that selling the land wouldn't get them out of debt anyway, they were too far gone for that, they might as well keep what mattered.

"I think we should talk to the Realtor again," Daniel said, following him down into the ditch. "The one from Sacramento. She said the land's worth more if we sell the development rights, or maybe sell it in pieces. Some of it could be—"

"No."

"You're not listening to me."

"I'm listening. I'm just saying no." Marcus was already underneath the line, positioning himself to remove the broken coupling. His phone had fallen out of his pocket and lay in the dirt of the ditch bottom, face-up, its screen still glowing. A text message from his ex-wife, asking again about whether he'd be able to make his daughter's graduation. He wouldn't be able to, because he was here, in a ditch, trying to save something his brother wanted to sell.

Daniel picked up the phone and turned it off.

"Hey," Marcus said.

"You need to focus."

"You're not my father."

"No," Daniel said. "I'm the one who actually runs this place."

It came out wrong, the way these things always did. It came out as an accusation when he'd meant it as a simple statement of fact. Marcus went very still underneath the metal line, the wrench suspended in his hand.

"You want to know what I think?" Marcus said quietly. "I think you never wanted this. I think you'd be happy to sell it off and move to Portland or somewhere, some city where nobody knows what we gave up."

"I didn't want this," Daniel said. "You're right about that. But we have it anyway. And we have to make decisions about it. Real decisions, not just—not just fixing the irrigation line and pretending everything's fine."

"Everything was fine. It was working. We were making it work."

"We were drowning."

Marcus pulled himself out of the ditch and stood very close to his brother, close enough that Daniel could see the crescent moons of dirt under his fingernails, the broken blood vessel in the white of his left eye.

"Go to the house," Marcus said. "Get the new coupling from the shed. The one we ordered."

Daniel had intended to say more, had intended to enumerate the ways in which they were drowning, the shortfalls and late notices and conversations with the bank that left him awake at three in the morning. But he recognized the tone in his brother's voice. It was the tone that meant Marcus was going to do the work regardless, that Marcus had always been going to do the work, that Daniel could either help or get out of the way.

He went to the shed.

The coupling sat in its cardboard box on the workbench, gleaming slightly, unused. It was a small thing, two pieces of metal that would allow the water to move through the system without loss. Daniel picked it up and felt its weight, its solidity. It was the kind of object that felt like it should last forever, that should be made of the same permanent material as the land itself. In his hands it felt like a promise they had no business making.

When he returned to the ditch, Marcus had removed the broken coupling and was cleaning the connection with a rag, whistling something tuneless. The sun was higher now, heat beginning to gather in earnest. They were going to be down here all day, Daniel realized. They were going to spend the entire day in this ditch, fighting against the failure of machinery that had already failed them a dozen times before.

"Hand it here," Marcus said, and Daniel did. He watched his brother's hands work, watched him thread the coupling into place, watched him tighten the bolts with careful precision. Marcus's hands knew this work in a way that Daniel's never would. It was embodied knowledge, the kind that came from growing up watching your father fix things, from learning to love the world through the process of maintaining it.

"There," Marcus said. He pulled himself out again and offered his hand to Daniel. For a moment Daniel didn't take it, some stubborn part of him wanting to maintain a separation. But it was foolish, and he was tired, and Marcus's hand was steady.

They refilled the line and ran water through it, checking for leaks. The water came through clear and cold, beautiful in the way that water could be when you'd been without it. It ran down toward the orchard and Daniel thought of his father, thought of the years his father had spent learning to read the subtleties of this land, the way you could tell from the color of a leaf whether the tree was getting too much water or not enough, the way the soil in different parts of the property behaved differently, had different histories, different needs.

His father had loved this place in a way that had seemed perfectly natural when Daniel was young and in a way that seemed increasingly like a kind of illness as he got older. It had colonized his father's entire life, had made him unable to think about anything else, had left him at the end looking out at the orchard with an expression of defeat, as if the land had finally won whatever contest he'd been engaged in with it.

"We should eat something," Marcus said. It was past noon and his shirt was dark with sweat. The water was running in the main line now, the pump humming beneath them with what sounded like relief.

They sat in the shade of the storage shed and ate sandwiches that Daniel had made the night before, anticipating another day of work. The bread was already a little dry, and the cheese had begun to separate from the ham, but neither of them commented on it. They ate in the way that people do when they're tired, mechanically, as a kind of fuel.

"Sarah's graduating next week," Marcus said.

"I know."

"She wants me there."

"So go."

"The coupling—"

"The coupling's done. The line's running. Go to your daughter's graduation."

Marcus looked at him with something like surprise, or maybe just recognition. "You'll manage here?"

"I'll manage." Daniel had managed for eight years, had managed every day in a state of low-level panic, had managed to keep this place operating on fumes and the weight of familial obligation. He could manage another day alone.

"The trees in the south section are going to need attention. The ones we didn't pull out—they're going to need serious pruning if we want them to come back next year."

"I'll do it."

"And the tank by the new gate needs to be checked. The valve was sticking last month."

"Marcus. Go to your daughter's graduation."

His brother finished his sandwich and balled up the plastic wrap, throwing it accurately into the trash can they'd left out here years ago, back when they'd been more optimistic about maintaining systems. The trash had blown around it for so long that the can had become something of a monument to their failures, surrounded by the desiccated remains of its own purpose.

"The Realtor," Marcus said. "You really think someone would buy it?"

"Yes."

"How much?"

"Enough to pay off most of the debt. Not all. But enough."

Marcus nodded slowly. He was still looking at the trash can, at the scatter of waste around it. "What would you do? If we sold?"

"I don't know. Move somewhere. Get a job where I don't think about money all the time."

"And where would I go?"

This was the question that had kept Daniel from pushing harder, that made the arguments feel somehow beside the point. Marcus had never lived anywhere else. He had a daughter in the town where they'd grown up. He had an ex-wife who lived ten miles away. He had a life here, or what passed for a life here, the kind of life that was made up of work and obligation and the thin thread of connection to a place that was slowly becoming incapable of sustaining anything.

"I don't know," Daniel said honestly.

"That's what I thought."

They sat in silence for a while. A white truck drove past on the county road, slowing slightly as it passed, and both of them watched it. Real estate agents did this sometimes, drove past in the afternoons, assessing, calculating. The truck picked up speed again and disappeared beyond the tree line.

"I had a dream," Marcus said, "the other night. That the orchard was different. Not abandoned or sold, but different. Like, the trees were still here, all of them, and they were still producing, and there were people harvesting. Except I didn't recognize any of the people. And the fruit was a different color. And when I tried to eat one, it tasted like copper."

"That's a terrible dream."

"I know. That's why I'm telling you. Because you're the one who thinks we should let go of it, and maybe you're right, but that dream—I woke up thinking about that dream and about how even the fantasies don't feel right anymore. Even when I try to imagine it working, it feels like I'm imagining something that belongs to someone else."

Daniel wanted to say something about this, wanted to acknowledge that his brother had just articulated the central tragedy of their situation, which was not that they were poor or that the land was failing but that they had lost whatever connection to it might have made the struggle feel worthwhile. Their father had possessed that connection. Their grandfather certainly had. But it had not been inherited, or it had been inherited as a kind of curse, a weight that both brothers felt without understanding.

"We could do it differently," Daniel said. "You and I. We could hire people to help, scale back to what we could actually manage. There are grants for small orchards now, incentives if you're willing to do it organically, or if you—"

"We already tried that."

"We tried once."

"We tried and it didn't work. The market was bad, and we didn't have capital, and we were both doing this and working other jobs, and you were always stressed about money. And I was always stressed about work. And none of it felt like it mattered."

This was true, and Daniel recognized it as the crux. They had tried. Three years ago, with more optimism than was reasonable, they had applied for grants and attended workshops and spent an entire year being more actively engaged in the orchard than they had been in the previous decade. It hadn't been enough. The debt had kept accumulating, the market hadn't shifted, and they'd eventually slid back into the position they occupied now, which was maintaining the status quo at the cost of everything else.

"We could try again," Daniel said, but without conviction.

"No," Marcus said gently. "We couldn't."

The afternoon wore on, and they didn't talk much. Marcus drove into town for parts they'd realized they needed after all—new washers, some additional PVC—while Daniel stayed and checked the line again, making sure everything was flowing correctly. The water moved through the irrigation channels with what seemed like gratitude, and Daniel found himself understanding, for the first time in years, why his father had loved this. Not as a romantic notion, not as some connection to the land in a larger sense, but simply for the satisfaction of making something work, of participating in the ancient transaction between human effort and the world's willingness to sustain itself.

It was temporary satisfaction. It wouldn't last through the summer or the next planting season or into the future that Daniel could see quite clearly now, whether they kept the land or sold it. But in the moment, with the water running and the pump humming and the sun moving across the sky in its predictable arc, it was enough. It was almost enough.

Marcus returned as the heat was beginning to fade, bringing with him the supplies and also cold drinks, which they consumed sitting in the bed of the truck, feet dangling toward the ground. The orchard looked almost beautiful in the late afternoon light, the geometry of the remaining trees creating patterns that suggested intention, that suggested someone had planned this, had imagined a future and acted accordingly.

"I'll tell Sarah I can't make it," Marcus said.

"No."

"It'll take at least two more days to finish the whole line. There's the section near the south gate, and we need to check the main valve again, and—"

"Go to the graduation. You'll come back and help finish. Or I'll finish it myself."

"You can't do it alone."

"I can try."

Marcus turned to look at him, and Daniel saw something settle in his brother's face, something like the acceptance of an inevitable outcome. "I'm going to call the Realtor," Daniel said. "I'm going to ask her to come out and do another assessment. A real one. And I'm going to ask about the timeline, about how long this would take, about what would actually happen if we went through with it."

"Okay."

"I'm not saying we'll do it."

"I know."

"But I think we need to know. I think we need to stop pretending that we can sustain this indefinitely, and we need to actually look at what the alternatives are."

"Okay," Marcus said again. He was quiet for a moment, and then: "What if no one buys it?"

"Then we keep doing what we're doing."

"Until it all fails."

"Yes."

The light was changing now, the sky taking on the particular quality it had in early June, that luminescence that seemed to come from the land itself rather than the sun. They were both quiet, watching the trees, watching the water continue to move through the system they'd spent the day repairing. It was a temporary fix. The coupling would eventually fail again, would crack under the pressure of use. But it had been worth doing, worth the effort and the heat and the argument that had preceded it.

"She was very happy," Marcus said, "when she found out about the graduation."

"I can imagine."

"She wants me to meet her boyfriend."

"You'll meet him."

"He's in a band."

"A band?"

"Some kind of garage rock thing. Sarah says it's good. She says he's good. She's proud of him."

Daniel tried to imagine this, tried to construct an image of Marcus at his daughter's graduation, watching her walk across some stage, meeting some young man with ambitions in music. He could see it, could see the three of them standing awkwardly together in some gymnasium or gymnasium-adjacent space, trying to understand each other across the gap of generations and choices.

"You should ask him about the band," Daniel said.

"I don't know anything about music."

"You don't have to. You just have to be interested."

They finished the drinks and walked the property one more time, checking that everything was running correctly, that the water was distributed evenly through the channels. In some sections the trees responded immediately, their leaves lifting slightly as if in recognition. In other sections, particularly the older trees, the response was less dramatic. The land had memory, Daniel thought. It remembered abundance and seasons of care. It would be a long time before it forgot, if it ever did.

By the time they locked up the pump house, the sun was down and the temperature had dropped back into something almost cool. They drove separately to town, Marcus heading home to his small rental house near the school, Daniel heading to the house where he still lived, the house where he and Marcus had grown up, where their father had spent his final years looking out the window at the orchard, waiting for something that never came.

Daniel poured himself a glass of water from the tap—water that came from the county, not from the orchard—and sat at the kitchen table where his mother had kept the ledgers, where his father had paid bills, where the family had conducted the business of staying alive. He opened his laptop and began to write an email to the Realtor, and as he did, he tried to imagine what would come next, what the landscape would look like when the orchard was gone and the land had been converted into something else, something that could generate return on investment, something that didn't require love.

He couldn't quite see it. The vision wouldn't form. What he could see was Marcus at his daughter's graduation, standing a little awkwardly in new clothes, trying to listen to what some young man was saying about music. He could see the water running through the irrigation lines tomorrow, feeding the trees, continuing the ancient transaction for another season. He could see himself here, in this kitchen, continuing the work of deciding what mattered and what could be let go of, though he had no confidence that he would ever get the calculation right.

He finished the email and pressed send, and then he sat in the silence of the house, waiting for the response that he knew would come, the knock on the door that was already traveling toward him across the landscape he had inherited, through the dark, under the stars that had looked down on all of this for a very long time.
