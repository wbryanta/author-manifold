# 

The water between the islands holds its cold like a kept secret. Marcus stands at the rail on the passenger deck, watching the mainland recede into what might be fog or might be the beginning of forgetting. The ferry cuts through November with the indifference of something that has made this crossing ten thousand times and will make it ten thousand more. He has not slept since receiving the call from the RCMP detachment in Port Hardy—a woman's voice, professional and careful, asking if he had a brother named David, if David had a distinctive scar on his left shoulder blade, if he could possibly come north to confirm. The voice did not say the body had been in the water. The voice did not say decomposition. These words Marcus supplied himself, alone in his apartment in Toronto, staring at his phone as if it might contradict him.

The ticket agent had asked if he wanted a cabin, and Marcus had said no, he would stay above deck, as if movement could prevent arrival, as if the night spent upright in a vinyl chair might dissolve the reason he was traveling. Now, watching the dark water absorb the ferry's lights, he understands that he has been traveling toward this moment for twelve years—since the last time he saw his brother, since that particular argument in their father's kitchen that had seemed permanent in its conclusiveness. David leaving. David not calling. The slow accumulation of absence until it had the weight of death anyway, a kind of death practiced over years.

A woman sits down two chairs away from him, unwrapping something in cellophane. She is perhaps sixty, with the weathered competence of someone who takes regular ferries. She does not acknowledge him, and he is grateful for this. Around them, other travelers settle into the night crossing like people boarding a ship to sleep through their own funerals. An old man reads a paperback without turning pages. Two teenagers examine their phones as if seeking prophecy. A woman with a child whispers a story that dissolves into the engine's rhythm.

Marcus has brought nothing to read. This has struck him as honest—what narrative would be adequate to this crossing? What story could contain the possibility that David is both alive and dead, both the brother he has not spoken to in twelve years and a body on a metal table in a northern mortuary? The duality makes him dizzy. He has lived so long in a state of not-knowing that the prospect of knowing feels like a kind of violence.

The ferry pushes north. Marcus feels the continental shelf drop away beneath them, the water deepening into the cold and dark of older things. He thinks of David as he was in his twenties—lean and restless, always looking toward the next horizon, the next coast, the next absence that might contain what he was looking for. Their father had called it selfishness. Marcus had called it freedom, though perhaps he had been wrong about that. Perhaps it was simply the same restlessness in a different name, something genetic and purposeless, driving their family north toward cold water the way it drives salmon upstream.

The woman unwrapping cellophane eats what appears to be salmon jerky. The smell reaches Marcus—salt, smoke, the taste of the Pacific rendered portable. He thinks of their father's fishing trips, David always eager to go, Marcus always finding reasons to stay behind. Their father had preferred David's company anyway. This had seemed like simple geography at the time, a matter of personality and preference. Later, Marcus understood it as prophecy. He had known, somehow, that David was the kind of person who would not survive very long, and he had moved away from that knowledge the way one moves away from a window during a storm.

By midnight, most of the other passengers have found their cabins. Marcus remains on deck, though the temperature has dropped enough that his breath makes shapes in the darkness. The woman with the jerky has left behind a wrapper, which moves slightly in the wind created by the ferry's passage. Marcus watches it. It is a small thing to watch, and watching it feels like the most honest work he could be doing.

He thinks of the detective's voice again—how careful it had been, as if she were used to delivering news that destroyed the specific gravity of people's lives. She had not asked how he was taking it, which was kind. She had simply asked when he could come, and Marcus had looked at his calendar as if his schedule mattered anymore, as if anything could matter more than this. He had booked the flight while still on the phone. He had packed a bag. He had moved through the hours before his departure like a man operating underwater, each action requiring an enormous effort of will, each moment arriving and then dropping away forever.

The ferry is smaller than he expected. This surprises him. He had imagined something vaster, more ceremonial, but it is simply a utilitarian vessel carrying him north, indifferent to his interior weather. This is comforting in some way. The ship does not care whether David is dead or alive. The ship does not care that Marcus has not visited his brother in twelve years, has barely answered his letters, has constructed an entire life in the southern provinces as if fleeing something. The ship simply cuts through the water with its usual efficiency, carrying this man and forty others toward whatever morning will bring.

Marcus walks to the other side of the deck. Here, the light from the cabin spills out less, and the darkness is more complete. He can hear the water more clearly—not the sound of it but the impression of it, the great weight of it surrounding them, holding them up. He has never been afraid of deep water, but he is becoming afraid of it now, or of something the water suggests about the nature of the world, about the fragility of the continuities upon which he has built his understanding of himself.

What if it is David? What if it is not?

In the first case, Marcus will have to acknowledge that his brother's life is finished, that all the possibilities for reconciliation have ended, that the arguments and silences have calcified into fact. In the second case, he will have to begin searching again, will have to live in a state of not-knowing that might extend infinitely forward, a kind of suspended animation that will poison all his subsequent relationships. There is no good outcome. This is what the night crossing teaches him. This is what the cold water whispers against the hull.

At two in the morning, exhaustion finally overtakes his resistance, and Marcus goes inside. The cabin he had rejected is gone, and he finds a chair in the common room, where the fluorescent lights have a quality of false daylight that suggests all hours at once. An old television mounted on the wall shows a muted cooking show. A man in white is doing something elaborate with fish. Marcus sits in the chair and closes his eyes and thinks of David filleting salmon, their father directing him, their father's hand on David's shoulder, all of them in the kitchen on a Saturday morning when the light came in gold and the possibility of being happy seemed like something that might actually be sustained.

He dreams and does not know he is dreaming. In the dream, he is waiting in a hallway. David is behind a door. When Marcus opens the door, David is standing there exactly as he was at twenty-three, with the scar on his shoulder blade visible, with the particular expression he had always worn when disappointed by his older brother's caution. David does not speak. He simply looks at Marcus with the weight of twelve years of silence compressed into that single gaze. Marcus tries to say something—to apologize, to explain, to ask what he has done—but his voice will not work. This is when he realizes it is a dream, and he wakes.

The common room has changed character in his sleep. The cooking show has been replaced by a documentary about glaciers. Three other people are present now, watching the ice calve into the sea. Nobody looks at Marcus when he opens his eyes. Nobody has seen him dream. He stands and feels the ferry moving beneath him, carrying him still northward, still toward the morning, still toward the moment when he will either see his brother's face or be returned to the particular hell of not-knowing.

He goes back outside. The stars are going out. The darkness is becoming less absolute. Somewhere in the distance, the northern coast is approaching, though it is not yet visible. But Marcus can feel it—the way the water has changed, the way the air has developed a texture of land about to emerge from the sea. He stands at the rail and thinks of David traveling in the opposite direction years ago, leaving the islands and the northern cold, seeking something warmer. He wonders if David had ever thought of turning back. He wonders if, in the moment before he died or did not die, his brother had felt the weight of all the words he had not said.

The sun rises without ceremony. The islands emerge from the darkness as if they had never been submerged, as if they have been visible all along and he simply lacked the eyes to see them. They are more beautiful than Marcus remembers, more intricate, the forest rising from the rocks in a way that suggests permanence and great patience. He thinks of how these islands have been here for ten thousand years, or a hundred thousand, indifferent to the lives enacted upon them, indifferent to his crossing, indifferent to the body in the mortuary that may or may not bear his brother's face.

The ferry begins to slow. The other passengers have returned to the deck, and they are gathering their belongings, their faces assuming the expressions of people preparing to re-enter the world. Marcus has no belongings. Everything he needs is apparently something that cannot be packed. He watches the port grow clearer—the docks, the small town, the figure of a woman standing motionless near the terminal entrance, watching the ferry approach as if waiting for someone who may or may not arrive.

It takes an hour to disembark. Marcus moves with the other passengers through the narrow corridor, down the metal stairs, across the gangway that flexes slightly with each step. His legs are stiff from the night of not-sleeping. His mouth tastes of the ferry's recycled air and his own fear.

The woman from the rail is standing near him in the line to exit. She turns and looks at him directly for the first time.

"First time?" she asks.

Marcus nods because it is easier than explaining that he is returning to a place he has not been in twelve years, to a place he has always been trying to leave.

"It gets easier," she says. "The crossing."

He wonders what she means. Whether the physical motion becomes easier, or whether something else—the transition itself, the liminal space between places—becomes easier to tolerate. He thanks her and moves forward toward the exit, toward the woman in the terminal, toward the moment when he will have to confirm or deny that the body is his brother's.

The mortuary is three blocks from the ferry terminal, though it might as well be three thousand miles. Each step is an exhausting negotiation with his own weight. The detective meets him outside—a woman named Sarah Chen, dark-haired, with an expression of profound patience that suggests she has done this many times, has guided many people through this particular doorway toward their own deaths.

"Before we go in," she says, "I want to prepare you. The body has been in the water for approximately four weeks. There is significant decomposition. The scar on the left shoulder blade is relatively intact, which is how we've been able to identify the remains as consistent with your brother, but the facial features are not suitable for visual identification. We'll be relying on the scar and on dental records if we can locate them."

Marcus nods. He understands this is information that is supposed to help him, supposed to make what is about to happen less shocking. Instead, it has made it more real. His brother has been in the water for a month. His brother is decomposed. His brother is bones and tissue and the accidental architecture of drowning.

"Do you want a few minutes?" the detective asks.

Marcus shakes his head. There is no number of minutes that would be adequate to this moment. There is no amount of time that would prepare him for what he is about to see. He steps through the door.

The room is colder than he expected, and bright in a way that suggests no secrets could survive here. The body is covered with a sheet. The detective pulls back only the shoulder, exposing the flesh beneath—gray, marked with the damage of time in water, and marked as well with the distinctive scar he knows as well as he knows his own reflection. The scar that David had received from a falling fishing hook when he was seven years old, that had left a white streak across his shoulder blade shaped like a question mark, or like the path of something that had almost killed him and had marked him instead with the memory of its passage.

Marcus stares at the scar. He is looking for some other feeling—denial, shock, the merciful numbness of trauma. Instead, what he feels is recognition, and beneath that recognition, something he has no name for, something that feels like relief and grief occurring simultaneously, like his body finally exhaling after twelve years of holding its breath.

"That's him," he says. "That's David."

The detective covers the shoulder again. "I'm very sorry for your loss."

Marcus nods, though he is not sure what he is sorry for, what exactly has been lost, or whether it was lost twelve years ago or only just now, in this fluorescent room with its unbearable brightness, with its cold that is not the cold of the water but the cold of a kind of finality, a kind of ending that cannot be undone.

When he leaves the mortuary, the light outside is blinding. November has turned the world gray and white, and Marcus moves through it like a man who has forgotten how to navigate by landmarks. He walks back toward the ferry terminal without knowing why. He walks past the terminal and continues toward the ocean, and he stands at a different rail and watches the water from this side of the crossing. The ferry that brought him here is loading new passengers. It will return to the mainland tonight, and it will cross this water again, and again, indifferent to the journeys it enables, indifferent to the returns it permits or denies.

He thinks of David crossing this water in the opposite direction, leaving the north, leaving their father's house, leaving Marcus and all the accumulated silences between them. He thinks of David arriving on the southern coast and beginning a new life, building whatever it was he had come to build. He thinks of his brother in the water, thinking or not thinking of the life he was leaving, thinking or not thinking of the older brother he had left behind.

The sun is setting now. The light has turned the water gold, and for a moment it is beautiful enough to forgive everything—the years of not-speaking, the silence, the particular architecture of disappointment that had built itself in their family like a structure whose foundation no one could identify. Marcus stands at the rail and lets the wind move across his face, and he does not try to shape the feeling that moves through him into something he can name. He lets it be what it is—a crossing, a return, a confirmation of something that has been true for twelve years and will now be true in a different way, in the way that all true things are true once they have been acknowledged.

The ferry pulls away from the dock. Marcus watches it leave, carrying other people toward other recognitions, other silences breached or deepened. He remains at the rail as the light fades entirely, as the stars emerge in their familiar configurations, as the night resumes the shape it had held the previous evening—vast, cold, indifferent to the particular gravity of human loss.

In the morning, he thinks, he will make arrangements. There will be a funeral. There will be a grave. The machinery of death will move forward with the same efficiency with which the ferry moves through water. But tonight there is only the dark and the cold and the slow acceptance that some crossings cannot be reversed, that some silences, once they reach a certain depth, become the only language anyone speaks.
