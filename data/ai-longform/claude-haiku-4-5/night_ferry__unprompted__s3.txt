# The Ferry

The announcement came at dawn—a crackling overhead, a voice describing procedures for disembarkation—but Thomas had not slept and was already standing at the rail, watching the mainland emerge from grey water like a memory becoming solid. The ferry groaned and shouldered through the final swells, its iron belly resonating with a sound that belonged to nothing living. He was one of perhaps thirty passengers who'd made the crossing in the darkness, most of them sleeping now in the reclined chairs of the passenger lounge or curled against the windows. He alone had spent the night on deck, watching the lights of the town gradually strengthen on the horizon.

The message had come to him three days ago through a police officer in a place he'd never been. His name was David—or had been—though Thomas preferred not to think of him as existing in any temporal form at all. Their mother had mentioned him twice in the past decade, both times with the careful neutrality of someone discussing a decision that had long since calcified into fact. Thomas had not asked questions. There were no questions worth asking about a person who had chosen erasure.

The officer had been apologetic in his tone, which Thomas found more disturbing than any official briskness would have been. A body had been recovered from the water near the harbor. Male, approximately the right age, with no identification. They were working through possibilities, checking missing persons, dental records—the systematic processes by which the dead were confirmed to be the dead. But there was a tattoo. A bird, the officer said. A raven, possibly, or a crow. Done in the rough style of amateur work, on the left shoulder blade.

Thomas did remember that. Or rather, he remembered David mentioning it one afternoon when they were both younger and still occupied the same kitchen, the same house, the same world. David had said something about freedom, about the mythology of escape. The tattoo had seemed important to him in a way that nothing else did. Thomas, who had been seventeen and certain that David's certainties were merely different versions of weakness, had said nothing at all in response.

The officers in his city had had to explain to Thomas where this town was. They showed him on a map—a island off the northern coast, accessible only by ferry, the kind of place where people went to disappear. The officer had used that phrase without irony, and Thomas understood that it was literal. People went there. They did not come back. Then sometimes, the sea gave up its secrets, and the work of identification had to begin.

Now, standing at the rail as the ferry groaned toward the dock, Thomas felt the strange suspension that had characterized the past three days. He had not yet decided whether he wanted the body to be David. This absence of preference seemed to him both cowardly and honest. If the body was David, then his brother had remained David-like—choosing dissolution, unreachable, a problem that solved itself through absence. If it was not David, then David was somewhere else, still existing in that distant state of non-being that he had chosen or been consigned to. Either way, Thomas was unchanged. Either way, the past remained what it had been.

The gangway descended with a mechanical wail. Thomas gathered his small bag—he had packed for two days, though he suspected he would not need them—and made his way through the slow-moving queue of passengers. An old woman with a walker. A younger man with the weathered face of someone who worked boats. A teenage girl in an expensive coat looking at her phone with the intensity of someone receiving terrible news. Life, Thomas thought, happening in its small ways on the deck of a ferry, each person carrying their own weights toward whatever shore awaited them.

The police station was three blocks from the dock, a modern building that seemed out of place in a town whose other structures were either genuinely historic or pretending to be. Inside, it was aggressively warm. There was coffee in a waiting area, and a woman behind a desk who smiled at him with practiced sympathy. She must have been told to expect him.

The officer he met was not the one who had called. This was a younger man, perhaps Thomas's own age—which made Thomas wonder if he was old or if all authority figures had simply begun to seem that way—with red hair and the formal courtesy of someone from a strict background. His name was Detective Morrison. He shook Thomas's hand and offered him a chair and asked if he wanted coffee, which Thomas declined.

"I appreciate you coming," Morrison said. "I know this isn't an easy thing."

Thomas did not respond immediately. He was trying to calibrate the appropriate emotional register for this conversation. Grief seemed presumptuous. Anxiety seemed performative. He settled on something between neutrality and concern, which he hoped appeared neither cold nor overwrought.

"Tell me about the body," he said.

Morrison pulled out a folder. Thomas noted this—the physical object, the actual papers, the refusal of abstraction. "Male, we believe mid-to-late forties. Found approximately one mile south of the harbor, in the rocks. There was some deterioration, as you might expect. But identifying characteristics were still present. Clothing was consistent with someone who'd been in the water for perhaps five to seven days, based on current patterns and seasonal degradation."

He opened the folder. There were photographs. Thomas looked at them with the focused attention he might have given to an X-ray of someone else's body. The face was bloated and mottled. The features had become somehow generic—not unrecognizable so much as stripped of the specificities that made a face an individual face. What remained was the shape: the structure of bone, the proportion of features. It could have been David. It could have been no one Thomas had ever known.

"The tattoo," Thomas said.

Morrison turned to another page. A close-up, in color, of a shoulder blade. The image was degraded but still legible: a bird with wings spread, rendered in what was indeed the style of amateur ink work. The lines were thick and uneven, given a strange quality by the bloating of the surrounding skin.

Thomas stared at it. He was trying to remember exactly what David's tattoo had looked like, and found that he could not. Memory had already simplified it, reduced it to the concept of a bird rather than the specific bird that had actually existed on his brother's skin. Any bird would match this memory. No bird would match it perfectly because the memory itself was imperfect, a ghost of a thing that had never been precisely observed in the first place.

"It's difficult," Morrison said carefully. "I understand. We have dental records from when he was in his twenties, but there's no recent dentistry on file. Do you happen to know if your brother was under the care of a dentist in recent years?"

"I don't know," Thomas said. "We weren't in contact."

He wanted to ask how long, but the question seemed wrong. Not wrong because it was disrespectful—he no longer believed in respecting the dead who had made no effort to be known—but wrong because it would suggest an intimacy of information that he didn't actually possess. He knew David's absence. He didn't know David's presence, his habits, his dental care, the shape of his daily life.

"Do you recognize the clothing?" Morrison asked.

There were photographs of the garments: a wool sweater in a dark blue, jeans, boots that were beginning to deteriorate. Nothing distinctive. Nothing that Thomas recognized, but nothing that would have surprised him either. David had always dressed plainly, as if clothing were a necessity he resented.

"I can't say," Thomas replied.

"All right." Morrison closed the folder. "What I'd like to do is have you observe the remains directly. I know it's unpleasant, but it will give you a better sense than photographs. Sometimes details become apparent in person that don't show clearly in images."

Thomas nodded. He was grateful for the procedural specificity, the way Morrison had framed this as a task rather than an ordeal. It made it possible to move forward without examining too closely what he was moving toward.

The body was in a small room in the basement, or what passed for a basement in a building that was only three stories tall. It lay on a metal table under fluorescent lights that hummed with a sound like insects. There was a technician present, a woman who introduced herself with the same practiced sympathy as the woman at the front desk. Everyone here, Thomas realized, had been trained for this particular emotional register. It was almost soothing, actually—the idea that his response had been anticipated, that there was a correct way to behave in this circumstance and that deviating from it would be understood and accommodated.

The sheet was pulled back to the level of the sternum. The face beneath it was worse in person—not more degraded exactly, but more present, more emphatically itself. This was a body. This had been a living person who had moved and thought and chosen things. Thomas could see that now, could feel the weight of it. The face was puffy, the skin discolored, the eyes slightly sunken. The mouth was open in a position that suggested either surprise or simply the slack of death.

He tried to overlay this image onto his memory of David. He tried to place this face in relation to faces he remembered—in their mother's kitchen, at school, in the yard of their childhood home. He tried to make the connection, to establish the thread that would prove this body had been that boy, that man, that absence.

He could not do it. Or perhaps he could, but it made no difference. The connection—if it existed—was not something he could feel or confirm through the act of looking. The face remained a face he did not recognize because he had not, in a sense, ever recognized David when he was alive either. His brother had been a figure moving through spaces that Thomas occupied, but ultimately separate. Ultimately unknowable.

"Is this your brother?" Morrison asked quietly.

Thomas looked at the tattoo again. Yes, it was the same—or similar enough that the difference no longer mattered. The bird with spread wings. The symbol of freedom that David had carried on his shoulder blade. The symbol of escape.

"I can't be certain," Thomas said. "But I think—yes, I believe it might be."

There was a silence. The humming of the lights continued. Somewhere in the building, footsteps passed in a hallway.

"We can do DNA testing," Morrison said. "If you're not certain, we can rule it in or rule it out definitively. He would have had a doctor at some point. We can get his DNA from medical records."

"No," Thomas said. "No, I think—yes. This is him. I'm sure enough."

It was not quite a lie. He was sure enough, just as he had always been sure enough about David's guilt in whatever Thomas had decided he was guilty of. Sure enough to act. Sure enough to let silence sit where words might have gone. Sure enough to accept absence as confirmation.

He turned away from the body and from the small room and from the woman in her professional sympathy. Morrison was waiting outside the door, holding it open.

"I'm sorry," Morrison said. "I'm sorry for your loss."

Thomas nodded. He wondered if Morrison knew that this loss had occurred long ago, that what was being mourned now was merely the confirmation of something that had already been mourned a thousand times—or not mourned at all, depending on one's philosophy of grief. He wondered if it mattered.

Back upstairs, there was paperwork. Morrison explained the process: a coroner's report would be issued, the body would be released to a funeral home, there would be costs. Did Thomas have a preference for how things should be handled? A burial? A cremation? Was there a will? Did David have assets, debts, family beyond Thomas?

Thomas heard himself answering in a voice that was his own but seemed to come from a great distance. No will that he knew of. No assets. Their mother was dead now—had been dead for three years. There were no other close relatives. It would be cremation, he thought. That seemed simplest. There could be a small service, or no service at all, depending on what was required.

"I can help you with some of this," Morrison said. "The funeral home will guide you through the next steps. But I want to give you a contact for a counselor. Sometimes it helps to talk to someone, even if you weren't close with the deceased."

Thomas took the card. He did not look at it. He knew he would not call.

He was back at the ferry terminal by early afternoon. The next ferry north wasn't until evening, which gave him several hours to wait. He found a small cafe near the waterfront and ordered a sandwich that he did not eat. He sat instead watching the water, the way it moved in patterns that appeared random but were presumably governed by laws he did not understand. Currents, tides, the gravity of the moon. There was order in the chaos, which was somehow worse than if it were merely chaos.

He thought about David—not as a body in a basement, but as a figure in memory, which was the only way David had ever been real to him. A boy who had needed something that their house could not provide. A young man who had made choices that seemed designed to sever the thread connecting him to everything familiar. An absence that had been methodical and complete.

Thomas wondered if David had been happy, here in this town, wherever he had lived and worked and moved through his days. He wondered if his brother had found whatever it was he'd been looking for, or if he had spent his life continuing to search, never arriving. He wondered if the method of death—the water, the rocks, the dissolution of the body—had been chosen deliberately or had simply happened, the way things happen to those who place themselves in precarious positions.

These were not answerable questions. Thomas understood that now in a way he had not before. The dead kept their secrets. They could not be interrogated. All that remained was the shell, the body, the evidence that they had existed. The rest was interpretation, projection, the story one told about absence to make it bearable.

The ferry terminal filled gradually as evening approached. There was the old woman again, with her walker. Different passengers this time, heading back north or making the journey for the first time. Thomas watched them move through the building with their purposes and their certainties, their lives still ahead of them in whatever form such things could take. He felt very old suddenly, though he was not old, had lived less than half a century. It was the weight of knowledge, perhaps—the knowledge that some gaps could not be closed, some silences could not be broken, some absences would persist even when the physical form of the absence had been located and identified and properly disposed of.

When he boarded the ferry at dusk, he did not go to the bar or the lounge. He climbed directly to the upper deck, where the wind was cold and the light was failing rapidly, and he stood at the rail just as he had that morning. Behind him, the town was beginning to light up—streetlights coming on, windows in buildings beginning to glow. Somewhere in that town was a funeral home that would prepare his brother's body for cremation. Somewhere was Morrison in his station, filing reports, moving on to whatever other cases occupied his attention.

The ferry pulled away from the dock. The lights on shore receded. Thomas remained at the rail, watching the water darken and the sky fade to black. He thought about the journey of the body—however many days it had spent in the water, moving with the currents, rolling among the rocks, being acted upon by forces it no longer had the capacity to resist.

He thought about the journey of a living person who leaves and does not return, who chooses or is forced into absence, who becomes a word rather than a presence. He thought about the journey of those left behind, who cannot help but interpret, cannot help but imagine, cannot help but carry the weight of the unchosen separation.

The ferry hummed beneath him. The night deepened. And Thomas stood alone at the rail, moving across the dark water back toward whatever life he would return to—changed, perhaps, but not transformed. Confirmed in his belief that some distances could not be crossed, even when one tried. That some people, once lost, remained lost regardless of whether the body could be recovered.

The lights of the northern shore appeared gradually, becoming clearer as the ferry drew closer. He would disembark in an hour. He would drive back to his city. He would continue. This was what one did. This was how one lived after loss that was not quite loss because there had been nothing to lose. This was how one survived the confirmation of an absence that had never truly been absent at all.
