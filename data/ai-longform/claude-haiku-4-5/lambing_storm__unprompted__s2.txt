# 

The first ewe went into labor just before the lights failed. Margaret had been checking the pens every half hour, moving through the shed with the economy of motion that twenty-three years of lambing had taught her—no wasted steps, no excess warmth bleeding away from her body into the cold. The radio on the shelf above the mixing station crackled with storm warnings, something about a late-season system that had surprised everyone by turning north instead of east, but Margaret had stopped listening to weather reports the way other people did. Weather was no longer a matter of inconvenience or interest. It was simply the next thing that would happen, and you prepared for it or you didn't, and then you lived with the consequences.

The ewe—number forty-three, Margaret knew her by the yellow marking on her wool—was pacing the corner pen, her sides visibly contracting. Margaret pressed her hand against the animal's distended belly and felt the lamb shift inside. Good. Head-first, by the feel of it. She had the supplies laid out on the metal table: clean towels, iodine, the lambing chains, the wooden handles for pulling if it came to that. The heat lamp hung above the pen, creating a small island of warmth in the cavernous space of the shed. Outside, the wind was already picking up, rattling the corrugated metal walls with a sound like something trying to get in.

"Mom?" Her son Danny appeared in the doorway of the back room, the one they used as an office and break room now, though it had been her husband's domain when he was alive. Danny was nearly sixteen, tall enough that he had to duck slightly under the door frame even though he shouldn't have to. He'd grown three inches in the past year, and his hands had become suddenly large and capable, though his face still held something unformed about it, as if the boy in him hadn't yet entirely evacuated and made room for the man coming behind. "Should I turn on the backup?"

"Not yet," Margaret said. "The batteries won't last long if we need them. How's number thirty-seven?"

"Bag's started leaking. Shouldn't be long." Danny moved to the heating stove in the corner—an ancient cast-iron box that burned wood they'd stacked through February—and added another log. The door creaked open, and a pulse of heat rolled out. The shed was as warm as Margaret could keep it, which meant somewhere in the high forties, cold enough that she could see her breath if she stayed still long enough. "The wind's getting worse."

She could hear it without looking at him. The storm had been forecast for three days, but forecasts were imprecise things, full of percentages and cone warnings and models that changed every six hours. What mattered was what was actually happening, and what was actually happening was that the wind was picking up and the sky had turned that particular shade of gray-white that meant snow was coming down in volume.

"How many are we at?" Margaret asked.

"Thirty-eight live births. Six dead."

The number settled in her chest like a stone. Six dead was acceptable, more or less. Some years were better, some worse. Her husband—she still thought of him that way, though it had been fourteen months—would have made some comment about the genetics of the flock, about whether they should consider introducing different bloodlines, something that would have sparked a half-serious argument about tradition and risk that would have continued for weeks until it resolved itself into a plan of action. Now there was no one to argue with, only the work and Danny, who tried to help but didn't yet know most of what mattered.

Number forty-three let out a long, groaning bleat. The first lamb was coming. Margaret moved to the pen and knelt in the hay, feeling the familiar ache in her knees, the one that had gotten worse every year since her thirties. The ewe turned her head and made a huffing sound—recognition, or resignation, or simply the sound of pain.

"Hand me the towel," Margaret said, and Danny was there with it immediately, had been watching her hands, waiting. This was the one thing he'd learned to do well: anticipate. It was what farming required, and what losing his father had accelerated in him—not childhood exactly, but the particular oblivion that childhood afforded, the luxury of not thinking several steps ahead.

The lamb emerged gradually, the ewe pushing, Margaret guiding, a slipperiness and then sudden heaviness in her hands. The amniotic fluid soaked through the towel and warmed her fingers. The lamb's eyes were still closed, its lungs hadn't yet drawn breath. Margaret cleared the fluid from its mouth and nose, rubbing it briskly with the towel, and then the animal convulsed and gasped and drew its first breath with a sound like a tiny desperate complaint.

"Live one," Danny said.

"Yes."

She placed the lamb on the straw next to its mother. The ewe began licking it immediately, that fierce focused attention that was present in every mammal, that ancient knowledge that said: this is yours, this must live. Margaret sat back on her heels and drew a hand across her forehead, leaving a trail of mucous and blood and afterbirth. She was forty-seven years old. She had a boy to raise on her own and four hundred and thirty-seven acres of land that was supposed to sustain them both, and a flock that wouldn't stop being born even though the world had stopped making sense.

"I'll watch her," Danny said. "You should check on the others."

She could hear the wind more clearly now. It wasn't howling yet—it was still in the building phase, that preliminary rattling that preceded the real assault. The lights in the shed hummed their yellow brightness, and Margaret found herself grateful for them, for the way they pushed back the darkness, even though she knew they wouldn't last. Winter storms that knocked out power usually did so definitively, brought down lines under the weight of ice or wet snow, left you suddenly in a darkness that felt absolute.

She made her way back to the mixing station and checked the battery on her headlamp, clipped it to her belt loop. On the shelf, the radio was still playing—some station out of Boston, a voice talking about cancellations and road closures and a state of emergency being declared in three counties. Margaret turned it off. The information was useless. They were here, and the animals were here, and there was nothing else to know.

Number thirty-seven was indeed in active labor. Margaret found her in the second pen, a younger ewe, maybe four years old, with a tendency toward dystocia. The lamb was presenting fine—Margaret could see the hooves, pink and perfect, emerging from the body. But the progress was slow. The ewe was tired already, her contractions weaker than they should be.

"Come on," Margaret said quietly, pressing her hand against the sheep's side. "Come on, you can do this."

The ewe bleated, pushed, but the lamb stuck. Margaret had brought the chains, just in case. She could feel around the lamb's legs, thread the chains, and pull if necessary. But pulling wrong could break ribs, could kill the lamb or the mother or both. She waited, breathing, feeling the sheep's body work against her palms. The rain had started. She could hear it on the metal roof, a patter underneath the wind's roar. Not rain, though. In early May, at this altitude, in this temperature, it would be snow by the time it reached them.

The lamb came suddenly, slippery and large, and Margaret had to hold it before it fell into the straw. She worked the towel over it, cleared its airway, felt for heartbeat and breath. The mother was already turning toward it, making those low urgent sounds, and Margaret placed the lamb on the straw where the ewe could reach it.

Two live. Four more pens to check.

By the time the power failed, there were forty-three living lambs. The lights simply went out—no flicker, no warning, just a sudden and absolute darkness that Margaret had, illogically, not expected despite decades of storms. She stood perfectly still for a moment, her heart beating in a way that felt excessive, and then she pulled the headlamp from her belt and switched it on.

Danny appeared, a shadow-figure in the beam of her light. His face looked pale and young, hollowed out by the darkness around it. "Backup?" he asked.

"Yeah. Turn it on."

The backup generator was in a smaller shed attached to the main structure, accessible through a door in the back room. Danny went to fire it up while Margaret moved to the mixing station and began lighting the oil lamps they kept for exactly this purpose. The flames steadied and bloomed, casting dancing shadows across the shed. It wasn't bright, exactly, but it was light—warm light, which mattered more in a lambing shed than brightness did.

The generator rumbled to life with a stuttering sound. The fluorescent strips on the far wall flickered and steadied, providing a pale bluish glow that was less comforting than the oil lamps but broader in reach. The battery indicator on the wall—something her husband had installed two years before he died, back when the farm had still felt like it might have a future—showed three-quarters full. They had maybe eight hours, maybe ten if they were careful and the generator didn't go through fuel too quickly.

"How many did we get before the lights went?" Danny asked.

"Forty-three live. Seven dead." Margaret was already checking her notes, written in a ledger that lived on the mixing station. Sheep by pen number, times of birth, any complications. Her husband had started the ledger, but she'd continued it without thinking, the way you continued breathing after a loss—because the alternative was unacceptable and because you simply did.

"That's actually not bad," Danny said. He was trying to sound like his father, she realized, trying to say the kinds of things the older man would have said. Making the facts mean something, imposing some kind of narrative structure onto the numbers. She wanted to tell him that it was fine, that the facts didn't need meaning, that they were what they were. But she didn't, because he was only fifteen, and he was trying to do the right thing, and sometimes the work of pretending to understand was its own kind of valor.

"We're not done yet," she said instead. "Two more pens haven't started labor. They'll come tonight, or tomorrow. Either way, we need to watch."

The wind picked up further as the hours passed. Margaret sat in one of the folding chairs they kept by the stove, a notebook on her lap, watching pen four. The ewe in that pen was number sixty-one, a good mother, reliable. She'd been pacing since early evening, but active labor hadn't started. Sometimes you could watch for hours, and sometimes everything happened in the space of twenty minutes. There was no way to know.

Danny came and went, checking the other pens, bringing coffee from the back room in thermoses they'd filled before the power failed. The coffee was already cool, but it was sweet and warm enough and something to do with his hands. He sat with her for a while, both of them watching the ewe, listening to the wind.

"Do you think it'll be a bad one?" Danny asked.

"Snow's coming down pretty hard. We might lose the drive for a day or two."

"Dad always said the worst storms were the wet ones."

She didn't correct him—didn't say that her husband had said this, not his father, that Danny's father had been that man and was now mostly a memory that Danny was reconstructing from fragments and the things that men said to their sons about weather and farming. It was close enough. It was the truth in the way that mattered.

"He was right," Margaret said. "Wet snow pulls down the lines. Wet snow sticks to everything."

Number sixty-one began active labor around midnight. Margaret was still in the folding chair, had been dozing, and she came awake at the sound of the ewe's distress—that distinctive bleat, part pain and part demand. She pulled herself up and moved to the pen, her knees creaking. Danny emerged from the back room, where he'd been lying on the cot, presumably sleeping.

This lamb was breech. Margaret could feel the hooves, the wrong hooves—the back legs first, the lamb coming backwards into the world. It happened sometimes. Usually the ewe could manage it, but number sixty-one was already exhausted, and the lamb was large.

"Go get the chains," Margaret said.

While Danny retrieved them, Margaret worked her hand deeper into the birth canal, trying to get a sense of the lamb's position. There was a moment—there was always a moment—where the problem became clear and concrete rather than theoretical. She needed to turn the lamb, or she needed to pull it backward, or she needed to do neither and trust that the ewe's body knew what to do better than her hands did. The test was whether she could feel the head, could orient herself in that dark wet space.

She couldn't.

"Okay," she said quietly. "When I pull, she'll push. The chain goes around the back legs, as far up as you can get it, then you help me pull. But we go slow. We don't rush."

Danny nodded. He'd done this before, or close to it. Margaret had walked him through it during last year's lambing season, before the funeral, before everything collapsed and had to be rebuilt from parts that no longer quite fit together.

They worked in concert, Margaret's hands inside the ewe, Danny maintaining tension on the chains, the three of them locked together in an effort that felt like something more than farming. It was a kind of prayer, maybe, or a kind of covenant—this commitment to pull the lamb forth, to refuse to let it remain unborn, to accept whatever came from that refusal.

The lamb came backward, the rear legs first, and Margaret was pulling now too, her palms against slippery fur, and there was a moment of terrible resistance and then a moment of terrible giving, the ewe crying out, and the lamb emerged in a rush, breech and alive and gasping.

Margaret held it while Danny found a towel. The ewe was bleeding more than Margaret would have liked, but the bleeding was slowing, and the maternal instinct was already overriding the pain. The ewe turned her head and began licking her calf, and number sixty-one made that low satisfied sound that meant she was going to be fine.

"Is she okay?" Danny asked.

"She will be."

Margaret stood slowly, carefully, feeling the age in her joints, the accumulated weight of fourteen months of managing alone. She'd been wrong to think of that time as a collapse and rebuilding. It wasn't that dramatic. It was more like the difference between standing on stable ground and standing on a slope. You could keep your balance on the slope, but you had to attend to it constantly. You couldn't let your attention waver.

The wind was terrible now. The shed seemed to be moving with it, or perhaps that was just the cold affecting her perception. She moved through the space checking on the animals, moving the headlamp beam across their shapes, counting the small bodies that meant the birth had held. Forty-four live births. Eight dead. Not bad. Not terrible.

She was heading back to the folding chair when she heard a sound that might have been a crack. It came from above, from the roof where the snow was accumulating. She stopped, listening. Danny appeared next to her, and they both looked up into darkness—the oil lamps didn't reach that high, and the backup generator's light was insufficient.

"What was that?" Danny asked.

"Probably nothing. Snow moving."

But she was already thinking about what wasn't probably nothing. The metal roof could collapse under enough weight. It was unlikely, but possible. The shed was old—older than she was, older than her husband had been. Built in the 1950s, reinforced over the decades but never substantially reconstructed. There were weak points, places where the metal had begun to rust through, corners where water had begun to seep in during heavy rains.

She didn't want to tell Danny this. She wanted to pretend that the crack was nothing, that the snow was just snow, that her anxiety was the result of fatigue and grief and the particular vulnerability of being responsible for too much alone. But honesty was cheaper than false comfort, and Danny would know if she was lying.

"We should check the roof," she said.

It meant suiting up, going out into the blizzard, climbing the access ladder on the north side of the shed. It meant leaving the animals unattended, meant taking time away from the lambing. It meant all the things that made it the last thing she wanted to do.

She was pulling on her heavy coat, the one her husband had bought her five years ago—a good coat, expensive, with a lining that had finally begun to wear through—when Danny said, "I'll go."

"No."

"I'm faster. I can get up there and back."

"No," she said again, but she could feel the logic of it working at her, wearing at her resistance. He was faster. He was also young and male and had less awareness of his own mortality, which meant he would take risks she wouldn't. But she was the responsible party, and responsible parties didn't send their teenage sons into blizzards.

She was about to repeat the refusal when a ewe in pen three let out a bleat that sounded different—deeper, more urgent. Both of them turned. Margaret moved to the pen without finishing the conversation, and Danny followed.

The ewe was number twelve, and the lamb had presented wrong. Not breech exactly, but twisted somehow, one leg forward and one back, making passage difficult. The ewe was pushing hard, working, but the lamb was stuck.

This one might not make it, Margaret thought, and then she thought about the roof, about the weight of the snow, and the impossibility of being in two places at once became suddenly, sharply real.

"Go," she said to Danny. "Get the ladder and go up there. Tell me if anything looks wrong, anything at all. Don't linger. Two minutes, maximum, and then you come back down."

She didn't wait to see if he complied. She was already moving back to pen three, already working her hand into the birth canal, already feeling for the lamb's legs and trying to determine whether she could fix this or whether it was already lost. The ewe was breathing hard, seemed almost frantic with pain, and Margaret was working blind—literally blind, because the information came through her fingers and through the ewe's body, and neither of them could see the problem clearly.

She heard the door open behind her, a gust of wind and snow pouring in, Danny going out into it. The temperature in the shed seemed to drop immediately. She worked faster, trying to straighten the lamb's legs, trying to reposition—and suddenly one leg came free, and then the other, and the lamb began to come.

The head emerged, then the shoulders, and Margaret was supporting the weight now, feeling for the back legs, pulling gently. The ewe was making desperate sounds, and the lamb slipped free all at once, and Margaret held it and cleared its airway and began the familiar work of bringing it back from the edge.

It gasped and convulsed and opened its eyes, and Margaret felt something in her chest release, some knot of tension that she hadn't known was there until it came undone.

Danny came back through the door, already starting to say something, but she held up a hand and finished with the lamb, made sure it was breathing well, placed it next to its mother. Only then did she turn.

"Well?" she asked.

"There's a dent," Danny said. His face was red with cold, his hair white with frost. "North corner of the roof, where the wall meets the metal. It looks like the snow is pushing down pretty hard, but it's not torn or anything. It held."

"You sure?"

"Yeah. I put my hand on it. It's solid."

She believed him. Danny didn't lie—not about practical things, not about things that mattered. It was one of the things she was grateful for, though gratitude seemed inadequate for what she actually felt, which was something closer to awe that they'd somehow managed to produce a human being who met the world with such precision.

"Okay," she said. "Come on. We should get more wood on the stove."

The blizzard raged on through the night. Margaret and Danny worked in shifts, one watching the pens while the other rested as much as they could on the cot in the back room. By dawn—though there was no real dawn, just a gradual lessening of darkness—there were fifty-two live births. The backup generator was down to a quarter tank, which meant maybe four more hours if they had to ration the lights.

Around six in the morning, Margaret was kneeling next to pen eight, helping a ewe through a difficult delivery, when Danny came and squeezed her shoulder. She looked up and saw him pointing toward the eastern wall of the shed, where pale light was beginning to show around the edges of the metal panels.

"Snow stopped," he said.

She wanted to tell him not to be premature, that the storm could turn back around, that forecasts were unreliable and weather was a living thing that didn't obey human predictions. But the light was definitely there, was definitely getting brighter, and the wind—the wind had changed, had shifted from that sustained assault to something more ordinary, even gentle by the standards of a spring blizzard.

The lamb in front of her came free, and she held it, and it breathed, and she placed it next to its mother. Behind her, through the wall, the sun was rising on whatever came next.
