# The Carrying Away

The house breathes different now. Margaret knows this the way she knows the rhythm of her own sleep—by what's missing. All morning the men have come through with their measured steps and their efficient hands, and the rooms have been opening up like hands unclenching, releasing what they held. A sofa that carried the weight of her mother's body for thirty years now carries nothing but air and moves out the door as if it never belonged here at all, as if it was always meant to be elsewhere, in some other living room where it would sit beside other people's sighs.

She stands in the kitchen doorway with a cup of coffee that has long since gone cold and watches a man in a gray shirt—his name was Eddie or maybe it was Eric, something beginning with E—fold up the card table where her mother had sorted buttons. Red ones. Blue ones. Pearl ones that had belonged to her grandmother's wedding dress. The buttons had been there in a mason jar for three decades at least, maybe more, and her mother would sit at this very table on rainy afternoons and separate them by size, by color, by some taxonomy that made sense only to her, and Margaret would watch from this same doorway while her mother's hands moved with the precision of a woman doing something sacred, something that required the whole of her attention.

Now the jar is gone too. Margaret had packed it herself, carefully wrapping it in newspaper, feeling the weight of all those buttons like a responsibility, like evidence. Evidence of what? She doesn't know. She'd put it in a box marked "Kitchen—Do Not Sell" but in the careful inventory of this day—the day of the carrying away—nothing was truly safe from the market price of belonging.

The man—Eric, she decides, it was Eric—folds the card table into a rectangle and carries it out with the kind of care men show when they've been doing this work for a long time. There's no violence in it, but there's no tenderness either. It's simply the careful movement of one object through space into another space, which is all a table ever was, all it could ever be.

Margaret's mother had been named Delia. Not Dell, not Dee. Delia. She'd been particular about this. "It's got two syllables," she'd say when people tried to shorten it. "It came that way." Delia had been born in this house in 1921, in the bedroom upstairs that is now empty of everything but dust and the memory of labor. She had died in the same house seventy-three years later, also in pain, also alone, though Margaret had sat beside her for the last part, holding her hand as it grew colder and smaller, like a bird's foot, like something already halfway to becoming another kind of thing.

The funeral had been three weeks ago. The service. The flowers whose names Margaret couldn't remember. The church basement afterward where women brought casseroles in dishes they'd marked with tape—"Please return to Ruth" or "Marilyn's"—as if the food itself might be claimed back, as if in the feeding of grief they hadn't given up their ownership of the vessels. Margaret had stood there in her black dress and accepted condolences from people whose faces she recognized but whose names had become interchangeable. Her mother had had friends. That was the shock of it. All these women, coming through, telling stories about Delia—about how Delia had nursed three of them through illness, how Delia had once driven forty minutes to bring someone soup, how Delia's hands could fix anything that broke, how Delia had never, in all their years of friendship, spoken poorly of anyone, though Lord knows, they had.

Margaret had never known this about her mother. Not in any way that mattered. She had known her mother as the woman who made her bed every morning without exception, who kept the refrigerator clean, who read the newspaper with her glasses on and her coffee nearby. She had known her mother as quiet. Competent. A woman who had raised Margaret alone, without complaint or explanation, and who had never asked for anything, which Margaret now understood was not the same as not wanting anything. The not asking had perhaps been its own kind of asking, a question posed in silence, waiting all these years for an answer.

The living room is nearly empty now. The imprint of the furniture remains visible on the carpet in slightly less-faded squares—archaeology of habitation. The china cabinet is gone, the one with the glass doors that Margaret had broken when she was twelve, and which her mother had never replaced the glass in, so that inside, the good dishes and the crystal glasses and the milk glass vases sat open to the air, to dust, to the passage of time. Margaret had asked once, decades ago, why she didn't fix it. "Doesn't matter," her mother had said. "They're still there."

A woman has come now, middle-aged, with carefully styled hair and the kind of tailored clothes that suggest she makes decisions about value. She's walked through the house once already this morning, touching things, making notes on a clipboard. Now she stands in the doorway to the dining room and holds up a lamp—brass base, cream-colored linen shade with a few water stains—and looks at Margaret with a question in her eyes.

"That one?" Margaret says. It's not really a question. She doesn't have a right to these decisions anymore. The house, the contents, the entire material accumulation of her mother's long, careful life—it's all become inventory now. Price tags are being applied. Decisions are being made by strangers about worth.

"I can take it," the woman says. "Maybe fifteen dollars."

Margaret nods. Fifteen dollars. She thinks about how long her mother must have owned that lamp, how many evenings she must have sat in the dining room reading under its light. Fifteen dollars. There's something about the figure that's obscene and also perfect, the precise mathematical reduction of a human life to something manageable, something that fits on a shelf in an auction house, something that can be photographed with a number and sold to someone who will never know who held its switch, whose hands were shaped by its light.

By afternoon, the living room is gone entirely. The furniture, the pictures on the walls, the end tables with their doilies and their offerings of tissue boxes and paperbacks. The television set that her mother had watched in the evenings—the news, always the news, as if the world's suffering could be tracked and understood if she just paid close enough attention. The sofa cushions that held the shape of her body so distinctly that Margaret found herself sitting in the impression one last time before they came to get it, trying to fit herself into the worn place, trying to know her mother through her absence.

Margaret had been born in this room. Not in it exactly, but she had spent the first months of her life in a bassinet in the corner, and she has memories—or the idea of memories—of lying there as an infant, looking up at the cream-colored ceiling, at the light fixture, at the shadows of people moving across the walls. She doesn't know if these are real memories or stories she's told herself so many times that they've become real. This distinction, she thinks, hardly matters anymore.

In the hallway, there's a photograph of her in her mother's hands. Margaret at six months. Margaret at two years old wearing a dress with white trim. Margaret at five in front of the school building on her first day. The photographs are still on the wall—the estate company hasn't gotten to them yet—and Margaret finds herself studying them as if they're evidence of a crime, as if they might reveal something she's forgotten about who she was when her mother was still the center of everything.

She goes upstairs. The stairs creak the way they always have, and she traces her hand along the banister, which is still there, still solid, still doing its work. Upstairs, the bedrooms echo. Her mother's room is bare now except for the bed frame—they're coming back for that later, she was told—and the dresser, which will go tomorrow. The wallpaper, which is the same wallpaper from when Margaret was a child, holds its pattern: small roses, pale blue, repeating endlessly. She remembers this wallpaper. She remembers her mother pasting it up, standing on a ladder while Margaret handed her the strips. Her mother had been younger then, or it had seemed like younger, though now Margaret realizes she probably wasn't young at all. She was probably already old, already tired, already living a life that required more of her than it gave.

The closet still holds clothes on hangers. A wool coat. A cardigan. A dress for church services. Margaret reaches out and touches them but doesn't take them down. They smell like her mother, which is to say they smell like nothing specific—just the smell of fabric that has lived a long time in the dark. She closes the door.

In her childhood bedroom, nothing remains but the bed frame and the window seat where she used to sit and read or pretend to read, which was a kind of reading too. The walls still hold the faint marks of tape where posters used to hang—a boy singer whose name she cannot now recall, a band, something that had mattered enormously when she was fifteen. These marks are like scars. They show where she had been attached to something, how she had pulled away.

She sits on the bed frame. The mattress is gone. The springs show through. She thinks about all the nights she spent in this bed, all the mornings she woke in this room, all the hours of her childhood measured in this room's dimensions. She thinks about the girl she was and how that girl became this woman, how the becoming happened without her noticing, how one day you're young and full of certainty about the future and the next day you're in your fifties, and your mother is dead, and her house is being dismantled, and you're sitting on a bedframe in an empty room trying to understand what it means that you're here and she's not.

Downstairs, she can hear the men moving. The piano is coming out now—she can hear them positioning it, the heavy scraping sounds, the careful language: "Watch the doorframe." "Tilt it left." "Easy now." The piano. Her mother had not played it in thirty years at least, maybe more. Before Margaret was even born, her mother had played—this is something she'd learned from the stories people told at the funeral. Delia used to play. Delia had beautiful hands. Delia could have done something with music if things had been different.

Margaret never learned to play. Her mother never asked her to. They had simply lived with the piano in the house, an instrument that was ornamental, that was a piece of furniture first and a piano second, that was evidence of a version of her mother's life that had not come to pass.

She listens to it being moved. She listens to her mother's past being carried out the door by strangers. The sound is immense—the piano seems to take up all the air in the house as it leaves, as if it's been holding everything in place this whole time, and now that it's gone, the house might collapse in on itself, might fold up like the card table and become something portable, something that can be carried out and sold at market value.

But the house doesn't collapse. It just stands, and the rooms continue to empty, and Margaret sits on the bed frame in her childhood bedroom and doesn't move, doesn't try to stop it, doesn't try to save anything. She understands that this is necessary, that things must go, that you cannot keep everything, that the dead have no use for their possessions and the living must learn to let them go. She understands this the way her mother must have understood it, waiting at the end, letting go of her breath, letting go of her hold on the world, accepting that what you leave behind becomes someone else's problem, someone else's inheritance, someone else's weight to carry.

When the man—Eric—comes upstairs to ask if there's anything else, Margaret shakes her head. There's nothing. There's only the empty room and the girl she used to be, sitting in the dark, waiting for something that never came, and the woman she became, who has learned that waiting is its own kind of answer.

By evening, the house is nearly empty. The front room is gone. The dining room is stripped. The kitchen holds only the appliances—the refrigerator, the stove—because these were not hers to sell. They belong to the house. The house will be cleaned, and then it will be listed, and then it will be sold, and then it will belong to someone else, and they will fill it with their own furniture, their own lives, their own small sacred rituals performed in the spaces where her mother performed hers.

Margaret locks the back door—a gesture that seems absurd now—and walks through the empty house one last time. She walks through it like she's saying goodbye to something, though she's been saying goodbye since she arrived that morning. The goodbye is not singular. It multiplies. Every room she leaves is a goodbye. Every step toward the front door is a goodbye. Every breath in this air that holds only the ghosts of her mother now is a goodbye.

At the front door, she stands on the threshold. Behind her is the empty house, and before her is the street, the town, the rest of her life, which will continue to happen without her, as it always has, indifferent to the fact of her presence or absence.

She thinks of her mother's hands sorting the buttons. She thinks of her mother's voice saying, "It's got two syllables." She thinks of her mother on the last day, her hand growing smaller, smaller, until it was barely there at all, barely something Margaret could hold.

Then she closes the door, and there's a sound like a sigh, and she walks toward her car, which is parked on the street, which will take her away from here, which will carry her toward whatever comes next.
