The old man had left the orchard to them both but he'd left it to them as a burden and they were men enough to know it. They came out at first light with the spading fork and the wrenching tools and a length of black pipe that had cost more than either of them wanted to acknowledge. The valley was already warm. The sky a pale bleached thing. They stood at the upper gate where the water came down from the mountains and had always come down, at least in the living memory of men, and studied the ditch where the earth had cracked away and subsided into a shallow gully.

The breaks had been coming for three seasons now. Each year lower. Each year requiring more labor and more materials and the materials themselves growing scarce and dear. Thomas stood with his hands on his hips and looked at the work ahead of him. Daniel squatted at the edge of the ditch and ran his fingers through the dry earth.

How bad is it? Thomas said.

It's pretty bad.

Can we get it done today?

I dont know. We can try.

They dug for an hour before they found the break. The old pipe was split along a seam, burst by the freezing of water trapped inside it, and the water that had once run through here had run out into the soil for months. They laid out the new pipe on the ground and it looked bright and false against the weathered landscape. Thomas measured the space while Daniel went back to the barn for the coupling hardware.

When he returned he brought his son, a boy of fifteen who moved with the slack efficiency of youth and the disinclination toward labor that seems to be the natural condition of that age. The boy carried a beer in one hand and he drank from it as he walked.

Put that down, Thomas said.

The boy set it on a rock.

I meant for you to finish it.

The boy drank the rest in three swallows and set the can down beside its brothers, for there were others there, other cans from other days, other repairs.

You want me to work or you want me to stand here?

I want you to work.

Then I cant do both.

Daniel said: He's right. Let the boy be.

He's not my boy.

That's not his fault.

They worked through the morning and by noon they had removed the old pipe and fitted the new one into place. The work was not difficult but it was exacting, and the sun was relentless and the sweat came freely and without relief. At midday they stopped and sat in the shade of the single cottonwood that grew near the ditch, an old gnarled thing that had survived longer than most men. They ate the sandwiches that Daniel's wife had made, though she did so with the same listlessness that characterized all her efforts since the old man died. The bread was dry. The meat was stringy. But they ate it.

Is Melanie coming to the funeral? Thomas said.

She's in Portland.

She know about it?

I sent word.

Will she come?

I dont know.

It's family.

Daniel looked at his sandwich. He seemed to be studying the structure of it, the way the components were arranged. I dont think she considers this family. Not anymore.

Whose fault is that?

I dont know. Mine, probably. His, probably. Life's probably.

Thomas ate. He looked out across the orchard where the trees stood in their rows, most of them old, some of them very old, and all of them thirsty. The irrigation line ran through the center of the property and then branched and subdivided and by the time it reached the farthest rows it was barely a trickle. Even when it had worked properly it was barely a trickle. The old man had fought with it for forty years.

Hale came by yesterday, Daniel said.

I know. He called me.

What did he say?

Same thing he always says. He wants to buy it. He's willing to go up.

How much?

Enough.

Enough for what?

Enough to matter.

Thomas set down his sandwich. The boy was sitting ten yards away, smoking a cigarette, watching the traffic of ants across the dry ground as if he might learn something from their purposefulness. Daniel watched his brother consider the question that hung between them now like something visible, like smoke.

You want to sell, Daniel said.

I didn't say that.

You didn't have to.

I'm saying that Hale's offer is legitimate and it's something we should consider. That's all I'm saying.

You want to sell.

I want to know why we're breaking our backs out here to keep alive something that's already dead. I want to understand what the purpose is.

The purpose is that it's ours. The purpose is that he left it to us and we're not quitters.

He left it to us because he didn't want to sell it to Hale and he didn't want to leave it to his wife and so he left it to the two men he raised. He left it to us as an anchor and that's a terrible gift and I think he knew it.

Daniel was quiet for a long time. He looked at the boy and then back at his brother.

I'll sell if you want to sell, he said finally.

That's not an answer.

It's the only one I got.

Thomas stood and walked toward the ditch. He stood at its edge and looked down into it, at the exposed earth and the broken pipe and the new pipe that they had fitted with such care. The water was running now, he could hear it, a small sound but a sound nonetheless. The valley swallowed it immediately. The ground drank it with an infinite thirst.

How much would it be? Daniel said. If we sold.

Hale said three-fifty. Maybe four if we clean it up.

That's not much money these days.

No. It's not.

What would you do?

I don't know. That's the part I can't figure. I don't know what to do if I'm not doing this.

You could work for Hale. He said so. He'd hire you on, him or his son. You could run the equipment. You know how to do it.

I don't want to work for Hale. I don't want to see this place in Hale's hands. I don't want to drive by here and see what he'll make of it.

Which is what?

Which is money. Which is nothing but money. He'll clear-cut the trees and plant something else, something that doesn't take as much care or as much attention. Something that can be managed with chemicals and equipment. Something that doesn't have a history.

Thomas came back and sat down beside his brother. The boy was asleep now, his head against the tree, the cigarette burned down to a stub between his fingers.

The old man knew it would end, Thomas said.

Probably.

That's why he left it to us. He wanted us to have to choose. He wanted us to have to make the decision to let go instead of him having to make it himself.

I don't think that's true. I think he just wanted it to matter to someone. I think that's all he wanted.

They sat for a while longer without speaking. The sun moved across the sky in its merciless way, and the shadows of the trees contracted and reformed, and the boy slept on. Daniel finished his sandwich and crumpled the paper and put it in his pocket. He looked back at the ditch and the new pipe they had laid.

When the old man was our age, how much of this was already broken? Thomas said.

Most of it, probably. More of it.

And he kept fixing it anyway.

He did.

Year after year.

He did.

Until he couldn't anymore.

Until he couldn't anymore.

Thomas nodded slowly. He seemed to be absorbing this, or accepting it, or coming to some kind of understanding that he had been resisting.

All right, he said. We'll keep it then. We'll keep fixing it. We'll figure out how to keep it alive.

For how long?

Long enough. Long enough for it to matter. Long enough so that when it finally goes we can say we tried. We can say we did the work that was required.

That might be a long time.

Probably will be.

And what about after that? What about when we can't do this anymore?

Then we'll pass it on. Or we'll sell it. Or we'll let it die and go home. We'll figure it out when the time comes. We'll figure it out then.

Daniel stood and woke the boy, who came up swinging at first, dreaming of some violence, some old hurt from school or home. Daniel steadied him and the boy came back to himself and looked at the two men as if they were strangers in his own life.

We've got more work, Daniel said.

The boy nodded, still half asleep. He seemed willing enough now. They went back to the ditch and the three of them worked through the afternoon, fitting the connections and filling the earth back in and testing the line for leaks. By late afternoon the water ran clear and the earth beside the ditch darkened and the smell of wet soil rose up into the hot air like something that had been dead and was now alive again. It was probably an illusion. Probably the work accomplished nothing in any larger sense. But it accomplished something and that was enough.

They cleaned up as the sun moved down the western slope of the valley. They collected their tools and the empty cans and the paper from the sandwiches. The boy carried the spading fork across his shoulder like a soldier. Thomas cleaned the dirt from under his fingernails with his knife. When they reached the barn Daniel looked back at the ditch and the new pipe and the darkened earth and the trees beyond, standing in their rows with their arms outstretched as if waiting for something, or appealing to something, or simply resigned to the weight of their own being.

He thought of the old man out here in the heat and the cold and the rain, performing this same labor, year after year, knowing that it was futile, knowing that the orchard would fail and the land would be sold and his life's work would become something else. And yet he had done it anyway. He had done the work that lay before him and he had asked no more than that. He had asked that the work be done and done well and that some small regard be paid to the effort. Perhaps that was all any man could ask. Perhaps that was everything.

He locked the barn door and the three of them walked back down the lane toward where the truck was parked. The boy was already talking about other things, about a girl and a party, about plans that had nothing to do with irrigation or orchards or the slow death of things that men had built. Thomas was quiet. He seemed to be thinking through what had been said and what had been decided, and perhaps already regretting it. That would come. That would come later in the evening when he was alone and could not avoid the truth of his own weakness. But for now he simply walked, placing one foot in front of the other, as men do, as all men do, in the direction of whatever comes next.
