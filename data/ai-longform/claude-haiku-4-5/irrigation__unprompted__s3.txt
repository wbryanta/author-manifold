# June

The pump house smelled of rust and diesel, a smell that Marcus had learned to associate with failure. He stood in the doorway with his hand on the jamb, watching his older brother kneel beside the cast-iron coupling, wrench in one hand, the other braced against the concrete floor. Above them, the June sun pressed against the metal roof like something living and malevolent.

"It's going to let go again," Marcus said. "Look at that corrosion. You can see it spreading like a stain."

David didn't look up. He was thirty-eight, seven years older than Marcus, and he had their father's hands—thick-fingered, capable, but running now to stiffness in the joints. He worked the wrench back and forth with controlled determination.

"It'll hold through the season," David said.

"Which season? The one where we pump for six weeks and then watch the trees die anyway?" Marcus stepped inside, letting the door swing shut behind him. The temperature dropped at least fifteen degrees in the shade, though the air was still thick enough to feel like breathing through cloth. "Come on, David. We both know—"

"I know what you think," David interrupted. He sat back on his heels, finally looking at Marcus with an expression that was entirely unreadable. "I know exactly what you think, because you think it every morning and every night and somewhere in between you probably think it in your sleep. You think it so loud I can hear it even when you're not saying it."

Marcus opened his mouth and closed it again. Through the single window of the pump house, he could see the eastern section of the orchard—neat rows of peach and apricot trees that had begun the season looking almost promising, the way a cancer patient looks promising when they're having a good day.

"It's not like I'm wrong," Marcus said finally. "You know I'm not wrong."

"Wrong and right," David said, turning back to the coupling. "Those aren't even the categories that matter anymore." He worked the wrench again, and this time Marcus heard something give—a sound like a small bone breaking. David withdrew the coupling and held it up to the light from the window. Bits of corrosion fell away in flakes. "Wrong and right matter when you're building something or you're destroying something deliberately. But we're not doing either of those things. We're just trying to keep something alive that doesn't particularly want to stay that way."

Marcus leaned against the far wall and crossed his arms. Their father had built this pump house in 1987, the year David turned eighteen. Marcus remembered being six years old and watching Dad pour the foundation, remembered the way the concrete had looked like a kind of grey milk, the smell of it still drying when they'd come to pick cherries that summer. The trees had been younger then, and the whole operation had felt less like a stubborn argument with the land and more like a conversation you might actually win.

"I talked to the Hendersons yesterday," Marcus said. "They want to come look at the property. They're thinking maybe three point two million, which is actually—"

"No."

"You haven't even let me finish."

"I don't need to." David set the coupling aside and wiped his hands on his jeans, though they were already stained with oil and dirt beyond any cleaning that one gesture would accomplish. "The Hendersons or whoever else wants to buy it can come look all they want. This is where I'm staying."

Marcus had heard this before—not with the specific names of various potential buyers, but the essential shape of it. It was the same speech David had made to their mother before she left, before she'd packed her car with whatever pieces of her life she could fit and driven north to her sister's place in Oregon. It was the same speech he'd made to their father in the hospital three years ago, standing by the bed and listening to the old man say in a voice that had been reduced to gravel and whisper that maybe it was time to let it go.

"So what's the plan?" Marcus asked. "We keep pumping water onto trees that produce forty percent of what they did ten years ago? We keep fixing this thing"—he gestured at the pump—"every time it corrodes through? We wait for the aquifer to drop another hundred feet and hope that there's still something down there worth pulling up?"

"If that's what it takes."

"It won't work."

"Probably not," David agreed, and something in his tone made Marcus look at him more carefully. His brother's face was still angled toward the mechanical parts arrayed on the floor between them, but his eyes seemed to be looking at something much further away. "But failing to do something has never been a good enough reason for me to not do it. I tried that once, and I never liked how it felt."

Marcus knew what he was referring to. It was the thing that lived between them like a third person, the thing they almost never spoke about directly because it had happened twenty-two years ago and they had both decided, without ever explicitly deciding it, to live around it rather than through it. Sarah Chen, who had been beautiful and serious and had wanted to move to Los Angeles to dance. She had wanted David to come with her.

"That's not fair," Marcus said quietly.

"Fair?" David laughed, but there was no humor in it. "You want to talk about fair? Fair would be if the person you love just decides that they're going to stay, that they're going to put down roots in a place that doesn't make them feel like they're dying a little bit every day. But that's not what happened. I made a choice, and I've been standing by it for more than two decades. And now you're asking me to unmake it, to say that all of that meant nothing, that I should have just gone. But I can't do that. I can't say yes to that without saying that I wasted my whole life."

The pump house seemed very small suddenly, and very hot despite the thick walls and the shade. Marcus could hear the hum of the electrical line that ran from the house, could hear the faint whisper of wind through the gap where the door didn't quite close, could hear his own breathing.

"Staying isn't the same as wasting," Marcus said.

"I know that. But it feels the same sometimes, and I'd rather spend my days with my hands in the dirt, trying to do something impossible, than spend them pretending that I don't feel that." David stood up, joints cracking audibly. He was getting older—they both were, but it was more visible in David somehow, as if the weight of choosing to stay in one place had accumulated in his joints and his shoulders. "You want to sell. I understand that. You've got a kid now, and you think about money in ways that I don't. You think about security and college funds and retirement. Those are good thoughts. They're the kind of thoughts that responsible adults are supposed to have. But they're not my thoughts."

Marcus had come prepared for this conversation. He had spreadsheets on his phone, projected water costs, property tax trajectories, comparative analyses of what similar acreage had sold for in adjacent counties. He had research on drought-resistant crops, agritourism opportunities, the rapidly increasing property values in the foothills as climate migration pushed people inland. He had, essentially, tried to make the unprovable provable, to take the weight of pure feeling and convert it into the concrete language of numbers.

None of it seemed to matter now.

"I'm not trying to make you leave," Marcus said. "I would never—you know I would never do that."

"I know. You're trying to save me from myself."

"I'm trying to be realistic about what's possible."

David moved toward the window and looked out at the orchard. From this angle, the trees looked almost viable—the morning light was still generous in June, hadn't yet developed the harsh, bleaching quality of July. But Marcus had been out among them two weeks ago, had seen the way the leaves were already showing that peculiar yellowing that didn't precede autumn so much as death.

"When Dad got sick," David said, "do you remember what he told me?"

"There were a lot of things he told you. He was in the hospital for three months."

"The last week, I mean. When he knew it was really the end." David pressed his palm against the glass, and his reflection appeared ghostly and insubstantial. "He told me that he'd spent forty years trying to prove that you could make a life work in a place that wasn't going to cooperate. He said that for forty years, he'd been in a conversation with the land, and the whole time he thought they were negotiating. But in the end, he understood that the land was just waiting him out. It was never going to negotiate. It was just going to wait until he was gone and then it would do whatever it was going to do."

Marcus had not heard this story before. David rarely volunteered information about those final conversations, and Marcus had learned not to press.

"So what did you say?" Marcus asked. "When he told you that?"

"I didn't say anything. I just sat there and listened to him breathe, and I thought about Sarah, and I thought about how I'd already made my choice. And I thought about how sometimes people need to have conversations with things that will never answer back, because the alternative is to have no conversation at all." David turned away from the window. "I'm going to keep talking to this land. I don't know if it's going to talk back, but I need to keep trying."

Marcus thought about his daughter, Livvy, who was seven and who had spent last summer complaining about the heat and the dust and the fact that there was nothing to do out here except watch TV or ride her bike in circles on the long driveway. He thought about his wife, Jennifer, who had supported his suggestion to explore selling the land with an eagerness that suggested she'd been waiting years for permission to leave. He thought about the job offer in Sacramento that he'd been offered—director of a water rights management consultancy, the kind of work that actually felt like it was building toward something rather than trying to sandbag against the inevitable.

"I can't stay," Marcus said.

"I know."

"If you sell, I could—I mean, there's enough there for both of us to—"

"I know," David repeated, and his tone was so gentle that it made Marcus's throat tighten. "That's what makes this hard. If you were being selfish about it, or greedy, I could fight you. But you're trying to do the right thing, and I'm trying to do the right thing, and they're just different things."

They stood in the pump house in silence for a while. Outside, a Swainson's hawk was wheeling in the sky above the orchard, hunting for something among the trees. Marcus watched it through the window and felt something like envy for its clarity of purpose—hunger or no hunger, the choice was simple.

"So what happens?" Marcus asked. "We just—what? I leave, and you stay?"

"For now, maybe. Eventually something will have to give. The land will probably solve it for us one way or the other. The aquifer will run dry, or the costs will become so prohibitive that even I can't justify it, or I'll get too old to keep going." David picked up the old coupling and held it in his hands as if it were something precious rather than a corroded piece of metal. "But today isn't that day. Today I can still pump water onto these trees, and tomorrow I'll do it again, and I'll keep doing it until I can't anymore."

Marcus reached down and helped his brother install the new coupling, working with practiced efficiency to seal it and get the pump running again. The sound of it starting up was the familiar rhythm of their lives—a stuttering growl that settled into a steady thrum, water beginning its long journey from the depths of the earth up through the pipes and out into the broken infrastructure of their lives.

When it was done, they stood together in the doorway of the pump house, looking out at the orchard. The morning was already getting hot, the kind of heat that would build toward ninety by noon and peak at a hundred and two by four in the afternoon. In two months, if the heat held, some of the trees might die anyway. The stress was too much, the water too little.

"I'm giving notice at the firm in two weeks," Marcus said. "The Sacramento job starts in September. I'll help you figure out what you need to do about your half. We can get a lawyer to handle the paperwork for a partial transfer, or we can—I don't know, whatever you want. But I think it's going to be time for me to go."

David nodded slowly, as if he'd been expecting this particular sentence for a long time, as if he'd rehearsed his response to it in the quiet hours of early morning before anything else was awake.

"Thank you," David said, "for trying. For the spreadsheets and the research and all of it. For caring enough to think that maybe there was a way to save this."

"I'm sorry I couldn't convince you."

"Don't be. I think maybe I'm relieved." David smiled, and it was a sad expression, but also somehow lighter than anything Marcus had seen on his face in years. "I think I've been waiting for permission to let this be enough—just the trying. Just the doing. You trying to convince me to leave, that somehow makes it okay for me to stay. Does that make sense?"

Marcus didn't think it made sense, but he understood it anyway. He reached out and squeezed his brother's shoulder, and they stood together watching the irrigation water begin its work among the struggling trees, trying to prove something that probably couldn't be proven, knowing better and doing it anyway, which seemed to him the most human thing that anyone could ever do.
