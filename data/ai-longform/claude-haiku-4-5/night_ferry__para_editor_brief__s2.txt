# The Crossing

The ferry lurched against the dock with a sound like artillery, and Marcus gripped the railing hard enough to feel his knuckles compress. Around him, other passengers were already shouldering their bags, checking their phones, preparing for the small movements that constitute arrival. He remained motionless, watching the gap between ship and shore narrow into nothing.

He had not seen his brother in eleven years. He had not spoken to him in twelve. The last conversation had taken place on a Tuesday in early spring, over the phone, with Marcus standing in the parking lot of an office building while rain sheeted down and obscured the windshield of his car. They had not argued. That might have been worse—argument would have implied continued investment. Instead, there had been a kind of mutual acknowledgment that the machinery of their relationship had finally seized, and neither of them possessed the tools to repair it. His brother had said, "All right then," and Marcus had said nothing, which amounted to agreement, and that was that.

The body had been recovered from the water three days ago. Caught in the nets of a commercial fishing vessel operating out of a small harbor town that Marcus could barely locate on the map, a place whose name he pronounced differently each time he saw it written down. The Danish police had done a preliminary identification based on clothing and personal effects, but the condition of the remains made a visual confirmation necessary. They had called the emergency contact listed on his brother's driver's license—their mother, who had been dead for four years. They had called the apartment listed on various documents and reached a landlord who had not seen the tenant in some months. They had searched through records and databases until they arrived at Marcus's name in an old employment file, some bureaucratic vestige of a time when they were still family enough to list each other as next of kin.

The woman at the terminal desk had the kind face of someone accustomed to delivering difficult news to people in transit. She held a placard with his name written in blocky capitals. He approached her slowly, as though the speed of his movement might somehow determine the outcome of what was to come.

"Mr. Petersen?" she said, and he nodded. "I'm Eva. I'll be taking you to the morgue. It's about fifteen minutes from here by car. You're all right? Do you need water?"

He shook his head. They walked through the terminal, down concrete corridors that smelled of diesel and salt and industrial cleaning products. The woman—Eva—maintained a professional distance, her hand hovering near his elbow without quite touching him, as though she understood that contact might cause him to shatter. Outside, the wind came off the water with a cold that seemed to belong to a different climate than the one he had left behind on the mainland. It cut through his jacket and found the bare skin of his neck.

The drive wound through the harbor town, past shops with signs in Danish, past low stone buildings the color of old bone. Eva made minimal conversation, which he appreciated. She told him the weather had been unusually mild, that the strait crossing could be rough this time of year but that he had been fortunate in that regard. He had not noticed the crossing at all, had spent most of the night in his cabin, a small room with a single porthole that overlooked only darkness and the occasional phosphorescent wake of the ship's passage.

The morgue occupied a low, modern building that looked like it might have housed a clinic or a dental practice. Inside, everything was very clean. A man in a white coat was waiting, and he shook Marcus's hand with an appropriate degree of solemnity. He explained the procedure in English with a slight accent. They would take him to see the deceased. He should take his time. If he could not make a positive identification, they would proceed with dental records and DNA analysis, though those would require more time.

"Do you have any questions?" the man asked.

Marcus did not. He was already functioning in that strange mode that crisis induces, where the self observes its own responses with detached curiosity. This was his body walking down this hallway. These were his eyes that were taking in the details of the walls—the particular shade of beige, the institutional lighting—as though documenting evidence that he was here, that this was really happening.

The body lay under a white sheet on a metal table. The man in the white coat pulled back the covering to reveal the face, and Marcus felt something inside him go very still.

It was not his brother.

He knew this with a certainty that felt almost violent in its clarity. The face was bloated from water, discolored by the processes of decomposition, barely recognizable as human. And yet Marcus knew with absolute conviction that it was not his brother's face. The bone structure was wrong. The teeth—visible where the lips had receded—were wrong. Some essence of identity persisted even through ruin, and that essence did not match what he carried in his memory.

"No," he heard himself say. "That's not him."

The man in the white coat turned to him. "You are certain?"

Marcus nodded. He was examining the corpse with more intensity now, searching for something he could articulate, some specific feature that would justify his conclusion. But it was not a matter of features. It was recognition in reverse—the absence of the familiar.

"Can you tell me why you are certain?" the man asked. His tone suggested this was important, that Marcus's certainty needed to be grounded in something more concrete than intuition.

"His hands," Marcus said finally, though even as he spoke he knew this was not actually the reason. "His hands were smaller than that. The fingers were longer."

This might have been true. Marcus could not quite remember his brother's hands with clarity. What he remembered was the shape of his brother's face when they were children, the specific timbre of his laugh, the way he had always stood with one hip slightly higher than the other, a posture that suggested permanent skepticism. These things did not reside in the corpse before him.

Over the next several hours, Marcus provided statements, answered questions, filled out forms. The Danish authorities seemed neither surprised nor particularly concerned by his inability to identify the body. They explained that they had received reports of a man matching his brother's description in several locations along the coast over the past six months. It was possible that his brother had been somewhere else when he died, or had changed his appearance, or was still alive. Marcus signed documents confirming that he could not make a positive identification and that he was authorizing them to pursue other avenues.

Eva drove him back to the terminal. He had time for another crossing, she informed him. The ferry that had brought him north was returning to the mainland in two hours. Unless he wanted to stay the night, explore the town.

"I'll take the ferry back," Marcus said.

He was waiting at the terminal with two cups of coffee when it occurred to him that he had failed at the only task he had come north to accomplish, and that this failure felt, in some obscure way, like a reprieve. His brother was not dead. Or at least, the body in the morgue was not his brother. These were not the same thing, but they moved in parallel.

The terminal was mostly empty at this hour. A few other passengers waited in scattered clusters, locals probably, people who routinely crossed this strait and thought nothing of it. Marcus sat near a window and watched the ferry being prepared for its return journey. Workers in high-visibility vests moved with practiced efficiency, directing vehicles into the hold, checking lines, going through their rituals of safety and order.

His phone buzzed. A text from his ex-wife: "How did it go?" She had been the one to call him when the Danish police first made contact, having maintained some lingering connection to the old contacts in his phone. He did not know what she was doing looking through things old enough to still have his name as next of kin, but he did not ask. They maintained a kind of mutual surveillance, minimal but persistent, the way people do after a marriage ends but before they have fully committed to the idea of the other's absence.

He typed: "Not him."

She responded almost immediately: "Where are you now?"

"Terminal. Waiting for the ferry back."

There was a longer pause this time. Then: "Are you going to look for him?"

Marcus did not respond. He set the phone down and returned his attention to the window. The ferry loomed larger now, more imposing in the daylight than it had seemed in the darkness of the crossing. He could see people moving inside, heard the sound of an announcement in Danish and English calling boarding passengers to begin the return voyage.

When he had been young, his brother had wanted to be a meteorologist. He had kept notebooks full of cloud sketches and temperature readings, had constructed a weather station on the roof of their house using materials scavenged from hardware stores and their father's garage. Their father had found it amusing at first, then had torn it apart one afternoon when he was drunk, throwing the pieces into the yard. His brother had not rebuilt it. He had stopped recording the weather. For a while after that, he had stopped doing a lot of things.

They had shared a room as children, and Marcus remembered falling asleep to the sound of his brother's breathing, a rhythm as reliable as the ticking of a clock. When his brother left home at seventeen, Marcus had been nineteen and already in college. He had thought the distance was temporary, a phase. He had not understood that some separations are permanent, that people can become estranged not through dramatic rupture but through a kind of slow erosion, the way water wears down stone.

Marcus boarded with the other passengers, moving up the ramp and into the hold of the ship. His cabin was assigned to him—a different one this time, on a different deck. He climbed the stairs and found it: a small room with a single bed, a porthole, a desk. He sat on the edge of the bed and felt the engines begin to vibrate beneath him. The ship was pulling away from the dock. He could hear the groaning of metal, the reversal of the thrusters, the specific sounds of departure.

He did not go to the deck to watch the coastline recede. He remained in the cabin, watching through the small porthole as the town disappeared into distance and then into the general blue-gray of the strait. The water was rough today, choppier than it had been on the northbound crossing. The ship began its sway, that rhythmic tilting that would take hours to subside.

There was a television mounted on the wall, and Marcus turned it on to a news channel. A woman was speaking Danish about interest rates and financial projections. He did not understand the language but found the familiar pattern of intonation and concern in her voice oddly soothing. He sat and listened to her speak about things that mattered to other people, things that would continue to matter after he left this room and returned to his life.

Somewhere, his brother was either alive or dead. This seemed like a binary that should contain the totality of possibility, and yet Marcus understood that many other states were available to him. His brother could be alive and entirely unaware of this investigation, living under a different name, cultivated into a new person by the years of separation. He could be dead in a location where no one had yet found him, lost to some other institution's unknown dead, unidentified and unmourned. He could be dying slowly in some place that was not this place, moving through the world with the specific kind of invisibility that only the truly estranged can achieve.

Marcus pulled up a browser on his phone and searched for his brother's name. The same results appeared as before: a Facebook profile that had not been updated in eight years, an old LinkedIn page, some mentions in local news archives from their hometown. Nothing recent. Nothing that suggested a living, present person going about the business of existence.

He closed the browser and lay back on the narrow bed, staring at the ceiling. The ship pitched and recovered, pitched and recovered. Around him, the framework of the ferry creaked and settled into its familiar rhythms. Somewhere on this ship, other people were having their own private experiences—eating meals in the cafeteria, sleeping, reading, watching films, making phone calls to people waiting on the far shore. Marcus was one person among many, and no one else knew that he had traveled to identify the dead and had failed at it, or that this failure felt like a kind of salvation.

His brother, if he was alive, might never know that the police had called Marcus asking him to look at a dead body and determine whether it contained the remains of the person he had not spoken to in twelve years. This seemed, to Marcus, a fitting arrangement. His brother would go on not knowing him, as he had been going on not knowing his brother. The machinery of their estrangement would continue to run without interruption, and Marcus would return to his life on the mainland, and he would not think about this strait or this ferry or the man on the metal table in the morgue who had not been his brother. Or he would think about it, intermittently, at unexpected moments—while driving, while eating, while lying in bed beside someone new—but he would not be able to explain the thinking to anyone, and so eventually he would stop trying.

The ferry pushed on through the cold water, carrying Marcus and the other passengers back toward the mainland, back toward the life they had interrupted for the business of crossing. The sun was setting now, painting the strait in shades of amber and deep blue. Marcus watched it through the small porthole, this light that was the same light that had looked down on his brother's childhood, that had moved across the roofs of their house, that had illuminated the face of the man in the morgue who was not his brother and might never have been anyone's brother at all. The light was passing now, fading into the darker hours that would carry them across the water and back to whatever waited on the other side.
