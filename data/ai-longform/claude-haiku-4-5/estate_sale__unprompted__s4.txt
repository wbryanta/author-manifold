# The Dispersal

Margaret had forgotten that her mother's furniture was so aggressively brown. Not the warm, honeyed brown of walnut or cherry, but a dull, institutional brown—the color of waiting rooms and unambitious lives. She stood in the hallway watching a young couple haggle with the estate sale coordinator over a dresser, the man pointing out a water ring on the surface that had been there since Margaret was in college. Her mother had always kept a glass of water on that exact spot. Margaret wondered if her mother had done it deliberately, a quiet insistence on imperfection, or if it had simply happened and then become permanent through habit.

"Your mother has some lovely pieces," the coordinator—whose name was Denise, or possibly Denice—said brightly as Margaret passed. "This buffet is particularly nice. Solid construction."

Margaret paused to look at the buffet. It was indeed solid, built in an era when furniture was expected to survive its owners, when it bore the weight of actual family meals instead of serving as a place to stack mail and newspapers and the kind of things people couldn't quite bring themselves to throw away. In the dark wood, Margaret could see the faint outline of where the good china had sat for fifty years—a pale rectangle marking the sacred space of holidays and company dinners. She had eaten many turkey sandwiches sitting at the dining table beside this buffet, working through her mother's puzzles in comfortable silence, back when silence felt comfortable.

"I don't think I want to sell this," Margaret heard herself say.

Denise or Denice blinked. "I'm sorry?"

"The buffet. I'll keep it."

The woman consulted her clipboard, frowning slightly. "It's listed in the sale. The preview was yesterday. There's already some interest." She said this gently, as if explaining a basic fact of physics to someone confused about gravity.

"Then un-list it," Margaret said. She had not planned to keep anything. When she'd hired the estate company two weeks ago, sitting in this very hallway with her laptop and her coffee and her impenetrable sense of purpose, she had been clear: sell everything. Her mother's house, built in 1952, was being sold to developers. The whole neighborhood was. In six months, the house would be gone, replaced by something called "luxury townhomes." Margaret had laughed when she read the brochure. This neighborhood had never aspired to luxury. It had aspired to quiet, to reasonable property taxes, to being the kind of place where you could raise children and retire without incident.

"It's a beautiful piece," Denise said carefully, "but I'm not sure—"

"I don't care. I'm keeping it. Deduct it from the sale price or send me an invoice. Whatever." Margaret walked away before the woman could respond, heading toward the living room where more strangers were examining the books on her mother's shelves.

The books were a problem. There were hundreds of them, maybe thousands. Her mother read constantly, voraciously, indiscriminately. Mysteries and historical novels and biographies and romance paperbacks with muscular men on the covers mixed together in no particular order. Margaret had asked about the seemingly random collection once, and her mother had looked at her as if she'd asked why she bothered breathing. "Because they're different," she'd said. "Why would I want to read the same thing twice?"

Margaret had not understood this. She read the same books repeatedly, each time finding new things in them, or finding them the same and taking comfort in that repetition. She had tried to organize her mother's books last month, separating fiction from nonfiction, alphabetizing by author, and her mother had watched with an expression of pained tolerance before undoing the entire project as soon as Margaret's back was turned. "I know where everything is," she'd said.

Now Margaret would never know where anything was. The books were being sold by the box lot to someone who had bid sight unseen, presumably hoping for a first edition or a signed copy. Margaret had looked through them carefully, and as far as she could tell, there was nothing of value. Just thousands of hours of her mother's attention, marked with dog-eared pages and margin notes, now being auctioned off to a stranger.

She was standing in the dining room, the room her mother had used least of all—the room preserved for occasions that increasingly never came—when a man asked her if she was the daughter.

He was perhaps sixty, with the diffident bearing of someone used to not taking up much space. He held a small oil painting, a landscape, the kind that came from a gift shop or a hotel lobby somewhere and had been hanging in her mother's bedroom for as long as Margaret could remember.

"I am," Margaret said.

"Beautiful home," the man said. "Very well-maintained. I grew up in one of these. Different town, but the same builder, I think. You could always tell by the way the light came in." He gestured vaguely upward. "The placement of the windows. Everything was about light, back then."

Margaret had never thought about this. She looked around the dining room, and indeed, the late afternoon light was coming through in a particular way, illuminating the dust motes that had been invisible until the furniture started being moved. The light was golden and seemed to make the empty room beautiful in a way it never had been when crowded with her mother's possessions.

"What are you buying?" she asked.

"This painting. My wife collects them. She likes to decorate with pieces from different people's lives. She says it makes a house feel less like a museum and more like a home." He smiled apologetically. "I think she just likes the story. Where it came from, who owned it. She'll probably research your mother once she gets it."

Margaret found this oddly touching and oddly invasive. "My mother didn't have a story with it. She just liked landscapes."

"Everyone has a story," the man said gently. "Even if it's just where you were when you decided to buy something."

After he left, Margaret went upstairs to her mother's bedroom, the room she'd been avoiding. This was where her mother had spent her final months, not quite sick enough for the hospital but too tired for anything else. Margaret had sat in the chair by the window—the chair that was now gone, sold yesterday—and they had talked about things that seemed important and then didn't seem important at all. Her mother had talked about Margaret's childhood, about things Margaret had forgotten. About the time she'd found Margaret in the backyard trying to bury a bird, about the terrible dress she'd worn to her eighth-grade graduation, about how she'd always known Margaret would move away but hadn't known it would mean they'd see each other only at Thanksgiving.

"You had to," her mother had said. "You're not like me. You needed to get out."

"I come back," Margaret had protested weakly.

"I know. And that's enough. But it's not your home. Not really. Not anymore."

Margaret sat on the bare mattress now—the bed frame had been sold that morning—and felt the particular quality of grief that only unfamiliar rooms could contain. In familiar rooms, grief was manageable. You knew where to put it. But here, with the furniture removed and the walls bare where pictures had hung, grief seemed to have no place and therefore existed everywhere at once, in the floorboards and the light and the silence.

Downstairs, she could hear the sounds of commerce. The hollow echoes of her mother's life being redistributed. Someone was buying her mother's good china. Someone else had apparently claimed the piano—the old upright that had been in the corner of the living room since before Margaret was born. Margaret had taken lessons for exactly six months at age seven before announcing she would never take another lesson, and her mother had never pushed her, which was perhaps the cruelest thing, because then Margaret had felt guilty about wasting the piano.

She found Denise—definitely Denise, she'd heard someone call her that—in the kitchen, checking off items on her clipboard as movers carried things out. "The piano sold?" Margaret asked.

"Yes, about an hour ago. A nice gentleman who teaches music to children. He seemed very pleased."

This was somehow worse. Not going to a collector or a dealer, but to someone who would actually play it. Someone would fill her mother's piano with music while Margaret sat in a condo in Columbus with no piano at all, no way to even attempt to play if she suddenly decided to undo her seven-year-old self's decision.

She went to the front porch and sat on the concrete step, watching the movers carry the last of the major pieces. The dining room table—where she'd eaten ten thousand meals, where she'd done homework and puzzles, where she'd sat across from her mother in the last weeks—went into a truck marked "Midwest Antiques: Des Moines."

A woman came to sit beside her, uninvited. She was young, maybe thirty, wearing expensive athleisure and smelling of something chemical and pleasant. "Are you selling the house?" she asked.

"No. Just the contents."

"Lucky you. My mother's still in her house, and I keep telling her we need to deal with this, but she won't hear it. She thinks she's going to live there forever." The woman shook her head. "It's going to be my problem eventually. All of it."

Margaret could have told her that her mother had thought the same thing, but her mother had been eighty-three when she died, and eighty-three had seemed like she'd had plenty of time, except that she hadn't. Eighty-three had arrived suddenly, and then it had been over, and then Margaret was sitting in a lawyer's office trying to understand terms like "testamentary" and "probate" while wanting only to go home and call her mother and ask her what these words meant.

"Your mother probably thinks she'll deal with it later," Margaret said. "Everyone does."

The woman was quiet for a moment. "That's depressing."

"Yes," Margaret agreed. "It is."

After the woman left, Margaret went back inside. The house was becoming unrecognizable. The living room, denuded of its furniture, looked larger and sadder, like the shell of something. The carpet was marked with the ghost-shapes of furniture, slight discolorations showing where things had sat for decades. In one corner, there was a small pile of items that hadn't sold: a lamp with a broken shade, a framed needlepoint sampler that said "Home Sweet Home" in faded stitching, a stack of National Geographic magazines from the 1980s. These would go to charity or the dump. Even garbage had its destination, its final resting place.

Margaret picked up the needlepoint sampler. She'd made it in high school, in a fit of enthusiasm for a craft that had lasted approximately as long as her piano lessons. Her mother had framed it anyway, had hung it in a place of honor, though Margaret had clearly seen that it was amateurish—the stitches uneven, the letters slightly crooked. She'd never understood why her mother would display it, when she could have hidden it or thrown it away.

"That's not selling," Denise said, appearing beside her. "Everything else went, but people kept passing on that one."

"I'll take it," Margaret said. "Add it to the buffet."

By five o'clock, the house was essentially empty. A few miscellaneous items remained—the items no one wanted, the stray socks of a dispersed life. The estate company would clean up tomorrow and deliver the keys to the realtor. Margaret walked through the empty rooms, her footsteps echoing off the bare floors, and tried to remember what it had felt like when all of this was full.

In the kitchen, she could still smell her mother's cooking somehow, though no one had cooked here in months. Margaret's last visit had been in the spring, and her mother had been too tired to do anything but order pizza, which they'd eaten sitting at the kitchen table, talking about nothing of importance. Margaret now understood that her mother had probably been sick then, or knew she was going to be, and had been trying not to burden Margaret with that knowledge. She had always been like that—considerate to the point of self-erasure.

Margaret found Denise in the front room, making final notes. "How did it go?" she asked. "From a financial standpoint?"

"Very well, actually. Your mother had good taste in furniture, and the timing was good. If this had been a few years ago, it would have been harder to move. People are interested in vintage pieces again." Denise consulted her papers. "I'll have a detailed report by the end of the week. You'll net approximately forty-two thousand dollars after my commission and the cleaning fees."

Forty-two thousand dollars. It was more than Margaret had expected and less than felt right, somehow. It was the distillation of her mother's material life into a number that would barely buy a car. Her mother had been a secretary, had lived carefully and without extravagance, had saved where she could. Margaret had always assumed there would be more, though more of what, she couldn't have said.

"Is that okay?" Denise asked, reading something in Margaret's expression.

"It's fine. It's good. I just... she had so much stuff."

"They usually do," Denise said kindly. She'd probably had this conversation a hundred times. "People accumulate. And then all of it becomes someone else's problem to solve."

After Denise left, Margaret locked the front door and stood in the empty house for a moment. The silence was profound. There was no sound from the street, no sound from neighbors, no sound from anywhere. The house seemed to be holding its breath, waiting for demolition, waiting to be wiped away and replaced with something new.

She walked upstairs one last time, to her childhood bedroom. It too was empty, the walls showing the faint squares where posters had hung, the floor marked by a bookshelf that had stood against the far wall for years. This room had been hers and then it hadn't been hers and then it had become her mother's study. Now it was nothing—just walls and floor and window.

Margaret sat on the bare floor, her back against the wall, and cried the way she hadn't been able to cry since the funeral, with the loud, ugly sounds she'd learned to suppress by her twenties. She cried for her mother and for this house and for the fact that nothing lasted and all the furniture eventually had to go somewhere else and all the homes eventually got packed up and dispersed, and there was no way to stop it. You could only watch it happen, could only try to remember what things felt like before they were emptied out.

When she was done, she sat quietly for a long time. The light was fading from the window, turning gold to orange to purple. In an hour or so, it would be fully dark. The house would sit empty tonight, and tomorrow and the next night, until the realtor could get someone to show it. Eventually someone would buy it—the developers, probably, or an investor. And then it would be gone, and there would be townhomes here instead, and people would live in them and they would have their own furniture and their own lives, and no one would know that Margaret had sat in a small bedroom on the second floor and cried for her mother.

She stood up, brushed off her pants, and went downstairs. In the foyer, the buffet and the needlepoint sampler were waiting. She'd have to arrange to ship them or rent a truck. They were hers now, the only things from this house she was taking back to her life in Columbus. She would find places for them, and they would sit in her condo, and no one would ask her about them, and she would look at them sometimes and remember this day and her mother and the sound of strangers' footsteps walking away with pieces of her childhood.

She locked the back door and then the front door, and she drove away from the house slowly, taking in the street she'd grown up on, the houses on either side that had also changed and would also eventually be sold or demolished, the church on the corner where she'd been confirmed, the park where she'd played as a girl. Tomorrow she would drive back to Columbus, and she would unload the buffet and the needlepoint sampler, and she would put them in her condo, and she would pour a drink and sit on her modern couch in her carefully curated apartment and she would try to remember what it felt like to be the kind of person who could live in a house like her mother's, who could stay in one place for fifty years, who could let furniture accumulate and not need to get away from it.

But for now, she drove through her mother's town, past the homes of people she'd known in school and didn't speak to anymore, past the diner where her mother used to go with her bridge group, past all the places that held her history and were about to be erased. And she felt the weight of things—the weight of time, the weight of memory, the weight of all the furniture that gets left behind. And she understood, finally, why her mother had let the water glass sit on the dresser, why she had never tried to cover the ring. Some things were too heavy to carry. Better to let them mark the surface and then let someone else deal with the mark.
