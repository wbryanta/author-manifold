# The Northern Crossing

The ferry terminal hummed with the particular loneliness of three in the morning—fluorescent lights casting everything in a sickly pallor that made the few waiting passengers look like extras in some dim film about bureaucratic purgatory. Marcus stood with his single overnight bag, watching the gate attendant scan tickets with the mechanical precision of someone who had done this ten thousand times and hoped never to do it again. The boat beyond the windows was already lit, enormous and improbable, a working vessel masquerading as a ship in a travel magazine.

He hadn't thought about James in months. Years, if he was being honest. The sort of not-thinking that comes from an active decision, repeated so often it becomes a kind of background hum in your life. Don't think about the phone call that went badly. Don't think about the letter that was never answered. Don't think about the fact that your brother is somewhere in the world—or was, or might be—and you had no idea where. But the call had come from a hospital administrator in Fort Mackenzie, speaking in that carefully neutral tone they must teach in administrator school, and suddenly the not-thinking became impossible.

"We have a deceased male, unidentified," the woman had said. "Found in the strait three days ago. Fisherman brought him in. We have some personal effects—a wallet with an old address that traces back to your family. The physical condition is... advanced, but dental records might help if you have access to those."

Advanced. He had pictured bloat and discoloration and things his mind skittered away from. He had asked how old the remains appeared to be. Thirty to forty, perhaps, she'd said. Which was nothing. Which was everything. James was forty-three. Maybe. Marcus had to think to calculate it.

The ticket agent—a woman with severe cheekbones and reading glasses on a beaded chain—waved him forward. "Single or returning?" she asked, already knowing the answer by the set of his shoulders.

"One way," he said, though that wasn't strictly true. He would be coming back. He would have to come back. There were things in Toronto that still demanded his presence—an apartment, a job he'd put on hold with the kind of vague half-explanations that suggested he was having a minor health crisis. More or less true. His brother was a health crisis, the kind that operated on a delayed fuse.

The gangway was steep and slick with Atlantic spray despite the summer season. He climbed slowly, his bag tucked under one arm, and the boat swayed beneath his feet in that particular way that made your inner ear question your sense of up and down. The promenade deck was mostly empty. A few other passengers were already settled in, spread out on the lounge chairs like survivors of a disaster, reading mysteries and memoirs and one man who was just staring out at the dark water with the intensity of someone looking for something he'd dropped overboard.

Marcus found a cabin on the starboard side. Everything about it was compact and vaguely nautical in a way that suggested someone had once visited the ocean and tried to capture the feeling through industrial design. A narrow bunk, a small porthole, a bathroom that doubled as a closet if you were careful about which way you turned. He set his bag on the bunk and sat down. The mattress was firmer than expected. He lay back and stared at the ceiling panel, which was the color of old dishwater.

He wasn't going to sleep. He knew this. His body knew this, already alert with a terrible wakefulness that had been his constant companion since the hospital call. That had been two days ago—was it two days? The timeline had already begun to blur. He'd gotten on a plane in Toronto. He'd rented a car in Halifax. He'd driven seven hours to this town he'd never heard of, Port Mackenzie, or Mackenzie Point, or something like that. He'd checked into a hotel whose internet connection was unreliable at best, where he'd tried unsuccessfully to eat a sandwich and tried even more unsuccessfully not to look at his phone.

The ferry's horn sounded—a long, mournful note that vibrated through the cabin and made something rattle in the walls. He felt the almost imperceptible shift as the boat began to move, the cables releasing, the water churning beneath the hull. They were going north. Toward a body. Toward an answer or toward a question that would finally resolve itself into a different shape than the one he'd been carrying around.

He had always known, in some abstract way, that James might be dead. People got lost. People made mistakes. People walked into the water or jumped from bridges or drove their cars too fast on mountain roads. It was the simplest mathematics: everyone died eventually; James might simply have accelerated the process. But knowing this abstractly and receiving a hospital administrator's phone call were two different things, operating in different registers of reality.

They had fought. It was important to remember that, though Marcus had spent years trying to forget it. They had fought about money—James had gotten himself into trouble with a business venture, something involving cryptocurrency and promises and people with the kind of patience that only exists in nightmares. Marcus, who had been doing reasonably well in those years, had said he could help, but not if James was going to be stupid about it. Those had been his exact words. Not stupid about it. Not smart about it, was what he'd meant, but his mouth had formed the cruder version, and James had heard it that way, and the conversation had descended into the kind of ugly place where two people who share DNA find themselves saying things that can't quite be unsaid.

That had been six years ago. The last time they'd spoken. Marcus had meant to call, had meant to apologize, had meant to send something, some gesture that would indicate he had been wrong. But time had filled itself with work and a relationship and then the end of the relationship and new work and the simple fact that not calling got easier the longer you didn't call, until suddenly it was years and the moment to call had passed into some untouchable past where apologies didn't really apply anymore.

He got up and went to the porthole. The boat was moving faster now, into deeper water. He could see lights from the coast receding, the port town becoming a scattered arrangement of glints and darkness. There was nothing ahead but the Atlantic, and somewhere across that water was the answer to a question he wasn't sure he had the right to ask.

The cabin felt too small. He left it and made his way up to the promenade deck, where the wind was sharp with salt and the promise of weather further north. A few more passengers had come up to watch the departure, but most had retreated below. He found a chair at the rail and sat, letting the wind work at his hair.

He had brought the hospital's email with him, printed on paper he'd folded and unfolded a hundred times. The wallet had contained two hundred and thirty dollars in cash and a debit card with no name—or rather, with a name, but a name that might or might not belong to anyone James had become. The personal effects included a heavy leather jacket, size large, dark blue with rips in the sleeves. James had worn jackets like that, years ago. He had thought them a waste of money.

There was also a tattoo. The administrator had mentioned it carefully, as though discussing body marks was somehow delicate work. A compass rose, old and faded, on the left shoulder blade. Did James have any distinguishing marks like that?

Marcus had said that he didn't know. That they hadn't spoken in a long time. That it was entirely possible his brother had any number of tattoos, and he would have no knowledge of them.

The light was beginning to change on the horizon—not quite dawn, but the suggestion of it, the sky going from black to dark blue to a shade that was almost purple. It was nearly five in the morning. He had been awake for more than twenty-four hours, maybe more than thirty. He couldn't quite calculate it. Time had become elastic.

A woman approached him with two cups of coffee and a expression of careful sympathy. She was older, perhaps in her seventies, with the kind of weathered face that suggested she spent time on boats regularly.

"You look like you could use this," she said, offering him one.

He took it. "Thank you."

"First time on the northern routes?"

"Yes."

She nodded and sat in the chair next to him, though not too close—maintaining the kind of respectful distance strangers maintain at sea. "It's a hard crossing for some people. Not the water, usually. Just being out here. Being in between."

He sipped the coffee. It was terrible, but it was hot. "Where are you going?"

"Home," she said. "I live in Falkland Bay. My daughter lives here—well, near here. I visit twice a year. The boat is the only way that makes sense." She looked out at the water. "You going to Falkland too?"

"Further. The hospital there. I'm..." He didn't know how to complete the sentence. Meeting a body? Identifying a corpse? Hoping his estranged brother is dead, because the alternative is that his brother is alive and he's spent six years not calling. "There's someone I need to see."

The woman made a small noise of understanding that was either sympathetic or judgmental, and he couldn't tell which.

"Loss comes in different shapes," she said after a moment. "The losing that happens when someone dies, and the losing that happens while they're still alive. I've experienced both. The dead kind is worse in some ways—cleaner, anyway. But only in some ways."

He didn't respond. She didn't seem to expect him to. She drank her coffee and watched the water change color as the sun continued its invisible ascent toward the horizon.

They sat together for perhaps an hour. Other passengers began to emerge—awakening from their cabins, drawn by the promise of breakfast and the sight of new coastline. The water had changed texture, becoming rougher, more complicated. The woman eventually left, offering a small wave that he returned.

He stayed until the sun actually broke the horizon, arriving so suddenly that it seemed almost rude. The sky bloomed orange and gold, colors so vivid they seemed false, like someone had spilled paint across the sky. He watched the light spread across the water, picking out individual waves, making the entire surface into a moving map of light and shadow.

By the time the ferry pulled into Falkland Bay, the morning was fully committed to daylight, and Marcus had managed something like two hours of sleep in a chair, his head at an angle that would punish him later. The hospital was visible from the water—a low, functional building on a hill overlooking the port. He could see the red cross from here, that ancient symbol meaning help or sanctuary or, in this case, the place where you identified your dead.

He disembarked slowly, moving with the other passengers, trying to look like he belonged to this place when everything about him announced that he did not. His shirt was wrinkled from the ferry. His jaw was dark with stubble. He had the particular gray exhaustion of someone running on empty, running on fear and coffee and the specific adrenaline that comes from not knowing what you're about to learn.

The hospital directory pointed him to the morgue in the basement. He took an elevator that smelled like disinfectant and sadness. The morgue attendant was a man not much older than Marcus, with the kind of gentle demeanor that came from constant exposure to grief.

"You're here about the drowning?" the attendant said, checking his paperwork. "From three days ago?"

"Yes."

"Condition's not good," the attendant warned. "He was in the water for probably five to seven days before he was found. I can show you what we have, but identification might be difficult visually."

Marcus nodded. He was not prepared for this, he realized. You couldn't be prepared for this. All his preparation—the long drive, the ferry, the sleepless night—was just theater, just a way of pretending he could condition himself for the moment of seeing.

The attendant led him to a viewing room. There was a sheet-covered form on a table. It was smaller than Marcus had expected it to be. Everything about death, he was learning, was smaller than you expected—more contained, more manageable. As if the body, at the moment of ceasing to be, had agreed to take up less space.

"I'll give you a moment alone," the attendant said.

Marcus stood at the foot of the table. His hand was shaking slightly. He had not expected his hand to shake. He reached out and gripped the table's edge to steady it.

He had no idea how to do this. There was no protocol for lifting a sheet off a drowned body that might be your brother. Should he be gentle? Should he be brisk? Should he say something?

He took the corner of the sheet and lifted it.

What he saw was not James. What he saw was not much of anything—a shape that had been a person but had spent too long becoming something else. The face was bloated and discolored, the eyes sunken. The features were indecipherable, compressed beneath swelling and decay. He could not have recognized his own mother in that face.

He looked away quickly and lowered the sheet. Then he made himself look again. The attendant had mentioned a tattoo. He had to look at the shoulder to know. He reached out and carefully turned back the sheet to expose the left shoulder blade.

There was a tattoo. A compass rose, faded and blurred by water damage, but recognizable. The cardinal points were marked with letters—N, S, E, W. In the center, someone had added something else: a small "M" in cramped letters. Marcus's initial. His initial on his brother's body, permanently inked there when they were young and it had seemed like that might mean something, that they would always be the center of each other's navigation.

He stared at the letter until it stopped making sense, until it was just a mark on damaged skin, evidence of nothing, proof of something that no longer mattered.

"It's him," he told the attendant, who was waiting in the hallway.

"Are you certain?"

"Yes." Marcus heard his own voice coming from very far away. "It's him. It's my brother."

The attendant nodded and made notes on his form. There would be paperwork now. There would be a death certificate and questions about funeral arrangements and what to do with the body. There would be the practical machinery of death grinding into motion.

Marcus signed the forms without reading them carefully. He gave the name James Michael Garrett. He spelled out the middle initial. He provided the family dentist's information for the official dental comparison, though he suspected they might not bother now that he had made his identification.

"I'm very sorry," the attendant said. "Do you have someone you can contact? Someone to take you back—"

"I'll be fine," Marcus said. He wasn't fine. Would never be fine. But there was a certain relief in the simple binary of it. James was dead. The not-knowing was finished. The possibility of reconciliation was closed, sealed, as final as a body in a morgue could make it.

He left the hospital without any clear plan. The morning had advanced while he was underground. The sun was high and bright and indifferent. People walked past him on the sidewalk, people who had their own concerns and didn't look at the man standing motionless in front of a hospital, trying to remember how to breathe normally.

He found a bar—it was too early for a bar, but this was the north, and bars opened when they wanted to—and he ordered a whiskey. The bartender, an older man with a cigarette behind his ear, didn't ask questions. Marcus sat at the bar and drank and felt nothing except a kind of vast, quiet absence where feeling should be.

His phone had service here, apparently. He could call someone. He could call the family lawyer and begin the process of whatever came next. He could call his ex-wife, though he had no idea why he would. He could walk into the ocean, perhaps, and find out what it had felt like to James, that final moment of water closing over your head.

Instead, he thought about the compass rose. He thought about James at nineteen years old, finding a tattoo artist and sitting while needles marked his skin with his younger brother's initial. There had been a dinner that night—his parents were alive then, were alive in ways that seemed impossible now. They had seen the tattoo and his mother had cried. Not sad crying, not exactly. Something more complicated.

"It means he'll always know where home is," his father had said, trying to make it mean something optimistic, though they both knew it meant James was lost already, that he was always looking for something he couldn't quite find.

Marcus finished his drink. He sat in the dark bar in the middle of the day, in a town he didn't know, in a country that felt increasingly foreign the further north you went. And he realized that James had been lost for a long time before the water had found him. Maybe since that night. Maybe since birth. Maybe from the moment two brothers had been separated by the simple, uncrossable fact of different personalities, different choices, different gravitational pulls toward different futures.

He should have called. Six years ago, or five years ago, or any point in between. He should have swallowed his pride and picked up the phone. But he hadn't, and James was dead now, and that was the terrible mathematics of it—simple, final, and irreversible. The compass rose on James's shoulder would never navigate anywhere again. And Marcus, who had been at the center of that compass, would spend the rest of his life trying to figure out which direction home was supposed to be.

He paid for his drink and walked back out into the light.
