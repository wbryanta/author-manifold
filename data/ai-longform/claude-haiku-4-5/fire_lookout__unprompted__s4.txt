# The Smoke Column

Eleanor had been counting the days wrong all summer. She discovered this on a Tuesday in late August when she checked the small calendar pinned above the desk and realized she'd been marking off dates with the certainty of someone who had actually been paying attention. The calendar showed seven days remaining, not the twelve she'd been casually assuming. Seven days until the Forest Service came to retrieve her, until they locked up the tower for good, until the decades of fire-watching that had consumed nearly every summer of her adult life would simply stop.

She stood at the eastern window of the cab, letting the news settle into her bones the way wind settles into the pines. The tower was high enough that wind was always a presence here, a constant negotiation with the air itself. At five thousand feet, weather arrived differently than it did down in the valleys where normal people lived. Clouds became topographical features. Rain announced itself three miles away. Smoke, which had been her true work for thirty-four summers, became a language she could read in its most elemental form.

That Tuesday morning, the air was the particular kind of clear that happens just before a change. The barometer on the wall had been dropping steadily since dawn. Eleanor made her coffee on the camp stove—the tower had no electricity, hadn't for twenty years—and settled into the wicker chair with her binoculars and her radio. The radio had been crackling intermittently for days, a problem she'd reported twice to the district office. Twice they had said someone would come up to fix it. Twice no one had arrived. It didn't much matter anymore. There were no active fires reported in the region, hadn't been a single report all summer. The drought had broken early in July. Moisture had returned. The season was winding down without drama.

Eleanor scanned the landscape methodically, as she had done every clear morning for thirty-four years. North toward the ridgeline. East across the drainage. South along the highway, where she could see three miles of asphalt before the trees closed in. West toward the private lands, where the ridge fell away toward the valley and the scattered cabins that served as retreats for people with money and a desire to own a piece of the forest. One of those cabins belonged to her sister.

She had not thought of Sarah in weeks. This had been her strategy for the entire summer: the simple act of not thinking. The tower made this possible. Up here, removed from the world below, Eleanor could let her former life—her failed marriage, her estrangement from her only sibling, the general disappointment of her accumulated years—remain below with everything else that required actual human interaction.

The coffee had gone lukewarm by the time she noticed the smoke.

It was small at first, barely a wisp, rising from the southwestern drainage where Willow Creek ran. Eleanor set down her cup and raised the binoculars. The smoke column was thin, almost insubstantial, the kind of thing that could easily be dismissed as a cooking fire or a carelessly maintained firepit. But Eleanor had been trained to notice such things, and her training had become instinct long ago. She watched as the column thickened, as it caught updrafts and began to rise with more authority.

She reached for the radio. "Dispatch, this is Tower Seven. I have a possible fire in the southwestern drainage, approximately three miles from my location, elevation forty-eight hundred feet."

Static answered her. She tried again, adjusting the frequency. More static, then a burst of white noise that sounded almost like speech before dissolving again.

Eleanor set the radio down and picked up her binoculars again. The smoke was definitely growing. It was moving upslope, which meant wind was pushing it from lower elevation. That made sense given the temperature patterns. But the column was behaving oddly, spreading wider than it should have been if it were a contained fire. It looked almost deliberate in its expansion, as if someone were adding fuel in calculated increments.

She tried the radio again. "Dispatch, Tower Seven. Still monitoring the fire in the southwestern drainage. Smoke column is increasing. Request status on radio malfunction repair."

Nothing. Not even the usual static with its undertone of human presence. The radio had simply gone dead.

Eleanor stood and walked the perimeter of the cab, looking from different angles. The smoke was unmistakable now, a grey-brown pillar rising from the trees. She could make out the location more precisely: it was coming from the drainage directly above the road where the turnoff led to the private cabins. The turnoff that led to Sarah's cabin.

She didn't let herself think about that. She tried the radio one more time, cycling through the frequencies. Dead on all channels. She checked the battery backup—full. She checked the antenna—nothing obviously wrong. The radio had simply decided to stop functioning, which was consistent with everything else about the Forest Service's budgetary neglect of her tower.

The fire was moving faster now. She could see the spread of it across maybe two acres, the flames still mostly obscured by the trees but evident in the smoke's urgency. It was moving upslope through what looked like dense timber, which meant it was going to burn hot and fast until something changed the conditions.

Eleanor had protocols for this, though they hadn't been invoked in actual practice for over a decade. Fires had become rare in this region. Fire management had become controlled burns and prevention campaigns. Real emergencies had a way of happening elsewhere now. But Eleanor knew the protocols. Without radio contact, she was supposed to abandon her post and proceed to the nearest ranger station, which was eight miles downhill on a road that had been barely maintained in recent years. Alternatively, she could use the emergency phone mounted on the external wall of the tower. She hadn't checked whether that worked in the past month, or the month before that, or any of the months before.

She climbed down the external ladder—forty-five metal rungs bolted to the side of the structure, leading down to the catwalk and the access door. The phone box was beneath a corroded aluminum cover. Eleanor opened it and lifted the handset. Dead silence. Not even a dial tone. She jiggled the connections with fingers that had gotten softer in recent years, fingers that had once known how to tie every knot a firefighter might need. Nothing changed.

Below her, the forest stretched away in all directions, familiar and indifferent. The smoke column had grown even more visible from this lower vantage point. It was definitely multiple acres burning now, maybe five or six from what she could see. The wind was pushing it northward, toward the ridge, which meant it could spread significantly if it reached the higher elevations where the timber was even more dense.

Eleanor stood in the wind and made a decision. She had seven days remaining in her tower. Seven days before the building was decommissioned. Seven days before she would have to figure out what came after five thousand feet and thirty-four summers. She was not going to spend those seven days in a ranger station filling out incident reports and submitting to the concern of people who had long since stopped thinking of her as anything but a relic.

And besides, the road down was long, and she was fifty-eight years old, and there was really no guarantee that the emergency situation justified the abandonment of her post when the fire was clearly miles away from any structures except the private cabins, which weren't her responsibility. The fire would either be contained by wind and terrain, or it would be discovered by someone else. People lived down there. Someone would see it. Someone would call it in.

Eleanor climbed back up the ladder, her knees protesting the exertion. Inside the tower cab, she settled back into the wicker chair and continued her observation. She had paper and pencil for exactly this purpose. She would document the fire's progression, its characteristics, its apparent rate of spread. When the time came to turn this information over to someone with actual authority, the documentation would be thorough and complete.

The morning advanced. The smoke continued to build. Eleanor made notes and drank water and tried not to think about the fact that the cabin in that direction was where Sarah would have gone. Sarah, who had money from her second marriage, who had always wanted to own a piece of the forest to escape to. Sarah, whose last communication with Eleanor had been five years ago, a card sent after their mother died, a card that had gone unanswered.

By noon, Eleanor could see the actual flames in gaps between the trees. It was a substantial fire now, twenty acres at least, maybe more. The wind had shifted slightly, pushing the smoke more northward. The fire was climbing the slope steadily. If it continued at its current rate and direction, it would reach the ridgeline in perhaps four to six hours.

Eleanor left the tower at one o'clock. She packed quickly, muscle memory from training exercises guiding her actions: water, matches, first aid kit, the fire shelter that she hadn't actually deployed since 1997. She locked the tower doors behind her and began walking down the road.

The road had once been maintained for ranger vehicle access. Now it was barely more than a trail, overgrown with brush that scraped against her hiking boots. Eleanor had walked it hundreds of times over the years, so many times that she could navigate it almost without looking. Today, she found herself looking anyway, paying attention to the way the light fell through the canopy, the way the forest smelled of resin and decay, the way her own breathing sounded in the quiet.

She could smell smoke before she'd gone a mile. The fire was behaving differently from her higher vantage point—it was no longer a column but something more diffuse, spreading horizontally through the canopy as the wind pushed it. The temperature was rising. Eleanor kept walking.

At the turnoff to the private cabins, she stopped. The road split here, her primary route continuing downslope toward the ranger station, while a secondary road curved upslope toward the scattered dwellings where the wealthy came to play at rusticity. Eleanor could smell the fire clearly now, could see smoke rising above the treetops. She looked at the secondary road for a long moment.

Then she began to climb.

The secondary road was in worse condition than the main road, barely passable without significant effort. Eleanor climbed it anyway, the smoke getting thicker as she went. Her lungs, which were not as young as they had once been, protested the exertion combined with the particulate matter in the air. She could see the fire now through gaps in the trees, flames visible in the middle distance, maybe a quarter mile away.

The cabins began to appear through the smoke: small structures, well-maintained, the kind of places that rich people visited for a week or two a year and then forgot about. Most were clearly unoccupied. But Sarah's cabin, the one at the end of the road, had smoke rising from its chimney.

Eleanor stopped, staring at the structure. It was exactly as she remembered from the one time Sarah had invited her to visit, years ago: a compact A-frame with large windows and a wide porch, nestled against the slope of the hill. It looked almost peaceful, despite the wildfire burning less than a thousand meters upslope.

Eleanor walked toward it and found her sister on the porch, watching the fire.

Sarah looked smaller than Eleanor remembered, though they had always been approximately the same size. She was wearing jeans and a flannel shirt, her dark hair—which had gone mostly grey—pulled back in a braid. She didn't seem surprised to see Eleanor, which struck Eleanor as remarkable, since they hadn't laid eyes on each other in five years, and Eleanor had just emerged from the smoke-filled forest, her hair matted and her face dirty.

"Is that your fire?" Eleanor asked.

"Yes," Sarah said. She was holding a cup of coffee, as if this were a normal afternoon, as if the entire forest wasn't burning upstream. "I was hoping it would stay contained to the lower elevations, but it looks like it's going to climb."

Eleanor found it difficult to process this statement. "Are you burning your own forest down?"

"Just the deadfall," Sarah said. "I had a burn permit. I was being careful. But we haven't had rain in so long, and today the wind—" She stopped. "Why are you here?"

Eleanor didn't have a good answer. "The radio at the tower is broken. I was coming down to report the fire."

"You were coming down the main road," Sarah said. It wasn't a question.

"I was," Eleanor agreed. "But then I changed my mind."

Sarah finally turned to look at her directly. Her eyes were the same color as Eleanor's, a kind of greenish-grey that neither of them had given much thought to until now. "You should go," Sarah said. "I have supplies for maybe twenty-four hours in the cabin. There's a water connection. I'm not going anywhere until I have to."

"That's insane," Eleanor said.

"Probably," Sarah agreed. "But I'm not leaving. I'm going to stay and watch it burn."

Eleanor stood on the path, smoke rising around her in lazy spirals. Her training was screaming at her to leave, to get to lower elevations, to get clear of the fire perimeter. Her professional instincts were insisting on the absolute irresponsibility of both of them standing here discussing philosophy while a wildfire made its way upslope. But something else in her, something she hadn't known was still present, kept her standing in place.

"If we're both being irrational," Eleanor said finally, "I might as well be irrational with you."

She climbed the porch steps and sat down beside her sister. They sat in silence, watching the smoke rise and the flames dance in the middle distance. The heat was becoming noticeable now, not yet dangerous but undeniable. Small embers were starting to drift downwind, landing on the porch railing and winking out.

"Tell me what you've been doing," Sarah said after a long time. "For the five years. Tell me something true."

Eleanor thought about the tower, about the wicker chair and the radio that mostly didn't work, about the way the view changed every hour as the sun moved across the landscape. She thought about the other fire towers, how many of them had been decommissioned, how the tradition she'd devoted her life to was slowly disappearing. She thought about the loneliness of it, the way it had suited her perfectly, the way she'd never known until this moment how lonely it had been.

"I watched fires," Eleanor said. "I watched for fires and then I watched them burn."

Sarah nodded, as if this made perfect sense. The fire was close enough now that they could hear it, a low rushing sound beneath the crackle of individual flames. The temperature on the porch was rising noticeably. By Eleanor's estimation, they had perhaps two hours before this location became untenable.

"We should go inside and pack," Eleanor said. "We're going to have to leave."

"I know," Sarah said. She still hadn't moved. "I wanted to stay longer. I wanted to stay until I had to leave."

"That's foolish."

"Yes," Sarah agreed. "But I've spent the last five years being sensible, and I'm tired of it."

Eleanor understood this. She understood it in the way only someone who had spent decades in relative isolation could understand the hunger for some kind of release, some kind of breaking of pattern.

They sat together for another fifteen minutes, watching the fire approach. Then Sarah stood and went inside the cabin, and Eleanor followed her, and they gathered the things that mattered—which turned out to be very few—and carried them to the truck that was parked at the side of the structure.

They left as the fire reached the property line, as the trees at the edge of the clearing began to burn. In the truck, descending the switchback road that led down to the main forest road, Eleanor watched her sister's hands on the wheel and wondered when exactly the distance between them had been established, and whether it could be crossed now, with the fire burning at their backs and the future uncertain.

"I didn't set out to burn it all down," Sarah said, as they navigated a particularly tight turn. "I really was trying to do a controlled burn. It was supposed to be contained."

"Burns escape," Eleanor said. "That's the thing about fire. You can contain it or you can predict it, but you can't really do both at the same time."

"Is that a metaphor for something?" Sarah asked, and there was the ghost of humor in her voice.

"Maybe," Eleanor said. "Or maybe it's just a fact about fire."

They drove in silence, descending toward the valley. Behind them, the smoke rose into the sky, visible for miles in any direction. By the time they reached the ranger station, both of them knew that what had started as Sarah's controlled burn would likely be declared an official incident, that there would be investigations and reports, that the fire would eventually be contained and extinguished but not before it had consumed a significant portion of the forest.

And Eleanor would file her report, and she would document her observations, and she would complete her final seven days in the tower—or not, if the tower was evacuated due to the fire. And after that, something would have to change, because the life she had been living could not continue indefinitely. The tower was being decommissioned. The work was disappearing. The view from five thousand feet was going to become simply a memory.

But that was future work, future reckoning. For now, Eleanor and Sarah sat in a ranger station office and gave statements to investigators, and Eleanor found that the story she told was not exactly the story of what had happened, but rather a version that was true in a different way, a version that acknowledged the complexity of wildfire and negligence and family estrangement and the simple fact that sometimes people made choices that didn't make sense but felt necessary anyway.

When they were finally released, Eleanor and Sarah stood in the parking lot and looked at each other, these two women who had managed to avoid being in the same place for five years and who had now been bound together by smoke and fire and the simple act of watching something burn.

"Do you want to stay at my place?" Sarah asked. "It's a motel, but it's clean, and there's a shower."

Eleanor, who still smelled of smoke and had dirt under her fingernails and hadn't slept anywhere but the tower in thirty-four summers, nodded. "Yes," she said. "I do."

They drove to the motel in Sarah's truck, and Eleanor sat in the passenger seat and watched the familiar landscape pass by, everything transformed by smoke into something new and strange. She thought about the tower, waiting empty at five thousand feet. She thought about the fire, still burning somewhere in the forest. She thought about the seven days remaining, or perhaps none, depending on how the season ended. And she thought about her sister, this woman who was also her stranger, and the way that fire had a way of changing topography, of clearing old growth, of making space for something new to grow.
