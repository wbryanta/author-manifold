# 

The daylight through the front windows seemed thinner than Margaret remembered it, though the house had been dark for three months now, the curtains drawn against the glare that would have faded the upholstery her mother had protected so carefully. The auctioneers had opened everything this morning—every blind, every drape—and now the rooms were exposed in a way that felt like a violation, though Margaret understood she had arranged this violation herself.

She stood in the doorway between the living room and the hall, holding her purse against her ribs like a small animal that needed calming. A man in a gray company polo shirt was securing canvas straps around the wingback chair where her mother had sat every evening with her needlework, the chair worn smooth on the arms from decades of use. The chair, according to the printed sheets scattered across the side table, was expected to fetch between seventy and one hundred twenty dollars. Margaret wondered if whoever bought it would notice the nearly invisible repairs her mother had made to the seams, the way she had matched the fabric so exactly that only someone who knew would see the scars.

"That's a nice piece," the man said, though he wasn't speaking to Margaret. He was speaking into the radio clipped to his shoulder, describing the chair to someone else, somewhere else, reducing it to dimensions and materials and estimated value. "Early nineteen fifties, good condition."

Margaret said nothing. She was not supposed to say anything. The auctioneer had been clear about that. She was supposed to stay out of the way, let them work, perhaps answer questions if they had them, but otherwise remain invisible in her own childhood home. It was her mother's house technically, had been her mother's house, which meant it was Margaret's house now, which meant she was paying strangers to remove her mother from it piece by piece, and she was doing this because there was no one else to do it and because houses required living in or selling, and Margaret lived eight hours north in a place where the winters were worse and the people were different and her life had become, somehow, the real one.

She climbed the stairs slowly, gripping the banister. The carpet was soft under her feet, worn down the center where her mother had walked the same path ten thousand times. Margaret tried not to think about this. She tried instead to focus on the small tasks she had assigned herself: checking the bedrooms, ensuring nothing of value had been missed, making sure the auctioneer's crew hadn't overlooked any of the jewelry or documents or other items Margaret had already sorted and boxed.

Her mother's bedroom was exactly as she had left it that last morning in early June, when she had complained of a pain in her chest and by evening had been dead, just like that, collapsed between the kitchen sink and the counter, her hand still reaching for the dish towel. Margaret's sister Joan had called from Arizona, where she lived with her husband and two children and a life that seemed to require her permanent absence from Ohio. Joan had wept and said she would fly out and then hadn't, citing a conference her husband was attending, and Margaret had not asked her to come again. They had not spoken except in texts and one brief, stilted phone call since the funeral.

The bed still had the quilt folded across its foot, the one Margaret's grandmother had made in the 1970s from scraps of old dresses. Margaret had been tempted to keep it, to fold it into a suitcase and take it home, but home was an apartment with limited space and a landlord who was already pushing to raise her rent. The quilt was on the inventory list. It would sell for perhaps thirty dollars. Someone would use it, or sell it again, or eventually it would end up in a landfill. This was how things went.

In the closet, her mother's clothes still hung in neat rows. Margaret had kept a few pieces—a winter coat with a good collar, a silk scarf printed with a pattern of birds. Most of the clothing had gone to Goodwill, though Margaret had made sure to check the pockets first, a task that felt absurdly intimate, reading the fragments of her mother's life: a receipt from the grocery store dated three days before she died, a hard candy wrapped in wax paper, a dollar bill folded small. These items Margaret had thrown away.

She moved to the dresser. The mirror above it reflected the room behind her and her own face, which she did not want to see. At fifty-three, she looked more like her mother than she ever had before. The same lines around the eyes, the same set to the jaw. She had always been told she was like her mother—patient, practical, dutiful—and she had spent much of her life trying not to be.

The auctioneer's crew had already been through here. They had tagged the dresser, the nightstands, the bed frame. They had looked at the mirror and found it not quite valuable enough to take separately but valuable enough to sell with the dresser. Everything here had been reduced to a code and a price range. The labor of living in these rooms for fifty years, the small rituals and quiet mornings and the weight of continuity that had sat here like furniture itself—this had no value. This was the part Margaret had not anticipated, that the house would be easier to empty than she would be to leave it.

She sat down on the edge of the bed. This was not helping. This was actively unhelpful. She should return to the living room, should oversee the inventory, should make sure they took the boxes of books and documents she had set aside in the garage. But instead she sat and listened to the sounds below—the scrape of furniture across hardwood floors, the murmur of voices, the radio chirping with confirmations and corrections. The sounds of her mother being removed.

Twenty years ago, Margaret had tried to convince her mother to move, to sell the house while it was still practical, while her mother could still oversee the transition and help decide what to keep. Her mother had refused entirely, had looked at Margaret with a kind of sadness that suggested Margaret had proposed something indecent. "This is my home," her mother had said, as if the phrase meant something Margaret's generation had lost the ability to understand.

Margaret had lived in four different places since leaving Ohio. An apartment in Columbus when she was twenty-five, working at a nonprofit that had folded after eight years. An apartment in Cleveland where she had been briefly, foolishly engaged to a man who worked in finance and whom she had grown to dislike. A small house in Pennsylvania where she had lived alone for four years and which had felt like a success until a bad job market had required her to move for work. Now the apartment in upstate New York, temporary-feeling still though she had been there nearly five years. She had thought of this as freedom, this moving, this refusal to be rooted. She had thought it meant she was unfixed in a way that was modern and urbane.

Standing up, she left the bedroom and went down to the second floor hallway, to the room that had been hers. It was smaller than she remembered, which was a cliché but true nonetheless. The walls were still painted the pale blue she had chosen at sixteen, had painted with her father on a Saturday when he had still been alive, had still been the kind of man who could help you paint a room. The radiator under the window still clanked the same way it always had, though there was no heat in the house and no reason for it to clank. Muscle memory in metal, perhaps.

The room was nearly empty. She had emptied it herself over the years, coming back to visit and gradually collecting the artifacts of adolescence, the yearbooks and journals and trophies. What remained were the fixtures—the built-in shelves, the closet rod, the window seat where she had read entire summers away. An auctioneer's appraiser had come through and tagged nothing. There was nothing here worth selling, which meant Margaret was leaving it behind, leaving the bones of herself in this room to be inherited by whoever bought the house.

Unless the buyers didn't want to keep the bones. Unless they would paint over the blue, rip out the shelves, renovate the room into something useful and modern. This seemed likely. The house would be bought by people from Columbus or Cleveland, probably, professionals who would appreciate the lot size and the bones of the structure but would immediately set about making it theirs, erasing her mother, erasing Margaret, erasing everyone who had lived inside this particular envelope of space and time.

She heard a crash from downstairs—something falling, something breaking—followed by a curse and a moment of tension, then conversation resuming at a normal level. Whatever had broken would probably be Margaret's problem, though the auctioneer had insurance and the terms of the sale had been explicit. The auctioneer was not responsible for breakage, though he had seemed almost disappointed to say it, as if he would have preferred to be responsible for something.

Margaret returned to the main floor and found the crew repositioning the sofa—the long gray sectional that her mother had bought in 1994 and that had been reupholstered twice since then—to navigate it through the front door. One corner of the room was now empty, and the removal of the sofa had revealed the outlines on the floor where it had sat for nearly thirty years, dust and sun creating a border that looked like a kind of evidence.

"This is heavy," one of the workers said, and Margaret realized he was speaking to her, expecting her to respond, expecting her to validate his assessment.

"Yes," she said. "It is."

"Your mom pick this out?"

"Yes. A long time ago."

"Good quality. These days people don't buy things to last like that. This sofa will probably outlast all of us." He said this cheerfully, not realizing the gallows humor of it, and Margaret did not correct him. She simply watched as he and his partners angled the sectional through the doorway, a maneuver that required unfolding it, splitting its components, and she thought about how her mother had never unfolded it all the way, always kept one section firmly against the wall, had used it as a kind of anchoring point in the living room.

By mid-afternoon, the living room was nearly bare. The coffee table was gone, the side tables, the lamps. What remained were the heavy pieces that would go out last—the dining room set, the china cabinet, the oversized bookshelf her father had built into one corner. Margaret had ordered lunch for the workers, sandwiches from the deli that still operated on Main Street, the same deli her mother had visited every Thursday for thirty years to buy potato salad and cookies. The workers had thanked her, seeming surprised that she would think of them, and now they were resting on the front porch, eating and making plans for the second half of the day.

Margaret used the respite to go to the kitchen. She had already removed the items she wanted to keep—a set of blue dishes that her grandmother had given her mother as a wedding gift, a wooden spoon worn to a glossy finish, a recipe box with her mother's careful handwriting on index cards. The rest of the kitchen was being auctioned off: the appliances, the cookware, the glasses, the linens. Someone had made Margaret an inventory list with prices, all of it reduced to a marketable value, and she carried that list in her purse like a kind of betrayal.

The refrigerator—which her mother had owned for twenty-two years, which Margaret had seen break down once and be repaired, which had never quite closed properly on the bottom left corner—had already been tagged. It would sell for perhaps one hundred fifty dollars if the buyer came prepared to haul it. Margaret had noticed in the last three days of organizing that the refrigerator held no food. She had emptied it herself, throwing away the mysterious containers of leftovers, the jars of condiments, the drawer of vegetables that had begun to rot. It felt important to her that it be empty when it left, though she couldn't articulate why. She didn't want strangers finding evidence of her mother's last habits, her last attempted meals.

She ran her hand across the counter—the laminate worn in places, gouged in one corner where her father had dropped something heavy years ago. This counter had been here her entire life, had been here before her life, had hosted a thousand meals, a thousand moments of ordinary life. And yet someone somewhere had probably already ordered a new kitchen for this house, had created a Pinterest board of finishes and hardware, was ready to erase all of this in favor of something clean and contemporary.

Margaret poured herself a glass of water from the tap, which still ran cold and fast, and sat at the empty kitchen table. She had left the kitchen table on the inventory, though she had been tempted to keep it. It was too large to fit in her apartment, was made of solid oak and would have required shipping, would have required her to build a life that could accommodate it. So she had left it, and someone would buy it, and it would feed someone else's family, and the cycle would continue without her.

She heard the workers coming back inside, heard the radio chirp back to life, heard the sound of the front door opening and the first of the afternoon's pieces being carried out. It was time, apparently. Time to watch more of her mother disappear. Time to approve or disapprove or simply bear witness to the removal of her past.

The dining room set took forty minutes. The table itself was substantial—a pedestal base with claw feet, the top scarred from decades of use, rings where glasses had sat, grooves from place mats that had shifted over years. The chairs were set of six, all with the original upholstery though it was worn and faded. Her mother had tried to have them reupholstered once, Margaret remembered, had gotten an estimate and then decided it was too expensive, had instead purchased fabric covers that she kept mostly in place but would adjust or remove when she felt the effort was worth it.

Margaret watched as they disassembled the table, wrapping the pedestal base carefully in blankets, layering the top with cardboard, bundling the chairs together. The workers moved with the efficiency of people who had done this hundreds of times, and Margaret tried not to watch their faces, tried not to see them as people capable of care or consideration. It was easier if she thought of them as a kind of absence, a force of nature that was simply clearing the house the way weather cleared a landscape.

By four o'clock, the house had become mostly empty. The bones remained—the stairs, the fixtures, the radiators, the closets. But the flesh was gone, the accumulated details that had made it a home rather than a structure. Standing in the living room, Margaret could hear the echo of her footsteps in a way she couldn't have before. The windows seemed larger without the furniture to anchor them. The room was a husk, and she was the only living thing inside it.

"Just a few more things," the auctioneer said, finding her there. He was a man in his sixties with a measured way of speaking, the tone of someone who had learned to be gentle with people in the middle of disruption. "Your mother's bedroom furniture, and then we're basically done for the day. We'll do a final walk-through tomorrow when we pick up what didn't sell or that you're keeping."

"Tomorrow," Margaret repeated, though she had already scheduled it, had already booked a hotel room for the night, had already arranged her life around the assumption that she would not be able to drive north until the next day.

"You can go ahead and leave whenever you want," the auctioneer said. "We can lock up. We have the key from the realtor."

Margaret said nothing. She had not considered that she could leave, that she could simply depart and let others do the final work of closure. She had imagined herself here until the end, standing sentinel, making sure that nothing essential was overlooked or mishandled. But there was nothing essential. That was the whole problem and the whole point.

"I'll stay," Margaret said. "I'll close up."

The auctioneer nodded, understanding that this was important without needing to be told why, and he excused himself to prepare the final pieces. Margaret walked through the house one more time, moving through rooms that were now so empty that they seemed like memories of themselves, reconstructions of spaces that had once held actual life.

She found herself in her mother's bedroom, standing at the window. From this vantage point, she could see the street below, the houses across the way, the trees that had grown substantially in the years since she had lived here. Mrs. Henderson still lived three doors down—Margaret had seen her at the funeral, older but recognizable. The Kramers had moved away sometime in the nineties, and now there was a young family there, a couple with children who probably had no idea that Mrs. Kramer had been a terrible gardener, that the Kramers' marriage had been quietly dissolving across decades, that certain dramas had played out behind those front windows that had seemed monumental at the time and now left no trace.

This was what Margaret thought about as she stood in her mother's room, watching the light change across the afternoon: how completely people could be erased. Her mother's life had been lived so carefully, so fully within this structure. She had cooked and cleaned and sewed and read and lived here for fifty-some years, and now there was no trace of her except what existed in Margaret's mind and in the minds of a few neighbors and a cousin or two scattered through the state. In one generation, she would be forgotten entirely. In two, she would be a name on a death certificate, accessible only to researchers and genealogists.

This thought should have been devastating. Margaret felt that it should devastate her. Instead, she felt a kind of lightness, which made her feel guilty, and the guilt felt like a stone in her chest, and she sat down on the bed again because she had run out of places to go.

The workers called to each other downstairs, hauling the dresser. Margaret listened as they navigated it through the narrow bedroom door, heard the scrape of wood against the frame, heard them curse quietly as one corner caught. They would get it down the stairs and out the front door and load it into the truck, and it would be taken somewhere and cleaned and displayed for sale, and that would be that.

When they finally left—the sun sinking lower over the roofline, the light in the house turning golden and then amber—Margaret did a final walk-through as the auctioneer had suggested, making note of anything left behind. In the guest bedroom, she found a single sock caught under the dresser, forgotten in the chaos of removal. In the kitchen, someone had left a small pile of dust and wood shavings where the appliances had stood. In her mother's bathroom, a bottle of prescription pills sat on the shelf, half-full, dating from two years ago. Margaret gathered these items, felt the weight of them in her hands, and threw them away.

By the time she left, full darkness had come. She locked the front door with the key the realtor had provided, and she sat in her car in the driveway for a long moment before starting the engine. Tomorrow the realtor would come for their final photos. Tomorrow the real estate listing would go live, pictures of empty rooms, the bones of the house made ready for its new inhabitants. Next week, probably, there would be an open house, and strangers would walk through the rooms, imagining their own lives here, never knowing what had lived here before.

Margaret pulled out of the driveway and drove past her mother's house one last time, seeing it in the rearview mirror, seeing it shrink and disappear. She turned onto Main Street, past the deli and the library and the bank, past the church where her mother had attended services every Sunday, past the pharmacy where she had filled her prescriptions, and she kept driving until she reached the edge of town, where the last lights gave way to darkness and the road opened up toward wherever she was going.
