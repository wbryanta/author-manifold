# The Tuning Route

The truck rattled across the county line just after dawn, its bed rattling with the tools of a trade that had become something like archaeology—excavating sounds from instruments time had buried. Marcus Webb was seventy-four years old and on what he suspected would be his final circuit, though he hadn't announced this to anyone yet, not even to himself in a way that felt permanent. He had been making this route for forty-three years, and it had the quality of ritual now, automatic as breath.

The first stop was the Hendersons' place, a colonial that had once been kept immaculate but now sagged slightly on its foundation like a tired face. The piano sat in the formal living room where nobody sat anymore, visible through the window as Marcus pulled into the driveway. It was a 1987 Yamaha, a competent instrument that had never known real use. The Hendersons' daughter had taken lessons as a child in the early nineties—he remembered the girl, Sarah, solemn-faced at age eight, the kind who played without feeling. She had quit after two years. The piano had been silent since then, more than thirty years of silence.

Margaret Henderson answered the door before he could knock, a woman about his age with careful gray hair and a face that registered something between relief and resignation. She always registered the same expression.

"I was afraid you might not come this year," she said.

"Why wouldn't I?"

She shrugged, a gesture that seemed to encompass everything—the decline of the county, the diminishment of all things that had once seemed permanent. "You're getting to an age where people retire."

"People retire from jobs they dislike," Marcus said, stepping into the foyer. "Bring me some water if you have it. And I'll need the key."

The piano was in perfect condition, which is to say it was in perfect condition for an instrument that had never been played. The bench sat unoccupied, the fallboard closed, dust visible even from the doorway. Marcus opened his toolbox—it contained his stethoscope for listening to the interior mechanisms, his tuning hammer with its weighted head, his voicing needles, his electronic tuner as backup, though he rarely used it anymore. His ear had become the thing he trusted most in the world.

The sound that emerged when he played the lowest A was like the voice of something waking from a long sleep, disoriented and uncertain. He worked methodically, one note at a time, his fingers moving with the unconscious precision of someone who had performed the same action forty-three thousand times. The hammer was an extension of his hand. His ear attended to the frequencies the way a doctor listened to a heart, detecting arrhythmias that existed below conscious notice.

Margaret had left him alone, as they all did. This was part of the ritual too, this solitude with the instrument. The work was meditative in a way that resembled prayer without requiring any particular faith. Marcus had never been a religious man, but he understood what drew people to music, or rather to the possibility of music. Every piano he tuned was an instrument of potential. The moment it was back in tune, it contained within it the possibility that someone—a granddaughter, a recovering memory—might sit on that bench and remember that they'd once loved the sound of these notes together.

It never happened. In his experience, it never happened. But the possibility remained, pure and untouched.

By ten o'clock he was rolling toward the Carmody house, which sat on twelve acres of land that used to be a working farm. The piano there was a Steinway grand, and it was the finest instrument he tuned on the route. The Carmodys' had bought it in 1975, and their son had been a prodigy—actually talented, Marcus meant, not just dutiful. The boy had played competitions, had been accepted to Juilliard. But in 1992 he'd had some kind of crisis, the kind that nobody in polite company discussed thoroughly. He'd stopped playing, stopped attending the school, stopped talking about music altogether. He was a tax attorney now in Boston. Marcus had never seen him play again, though his publicity photo on the firm's website showed a man with his father's stern expression and his mother's precise posture.

The Steinway still gleamed in the sunlit room where Marcus had attended a handful of recitals forty years ago. He remembered the boy's hands on the keys, remembered the impossible velocity of the Chopin ballade that had been his signature piece. He remembered thinking that he was in the presence of something rare, the way an athlete's body sometimes reveals capabilities that seemed to exceed human possibility.

Now the bench stood empty. The keys were yellowed slightly, even covered—the Carmodys must have put something over them. When Marcus lifted the fallboard, a faint smell emerged, something like old paper and the mineral scent of wood that had absorbed decades of sunlight.

He played middle C. The note emerged pure and whole, ringing with the authority that only a Steinway could produce. He closed his eyes and let the vibration move through him, let it remind him what the difference between a good instrument and a great one felt like.

Mr. Carmody came in while Marcus was working on the upper register. The man was older than Marcus, must have been, though his posture remained military-straight. They had never spoken much beyond basic greetings, though Marcus had been coming here for four decades. The relationship between the piano tuner and the piano owner was peculiar—intimate without being close, familiar without being friendly.

"How is she?" Carmody asked, which was what he always asked.

"Good," Marcus said. "Better than she has any right to be. You don't play her."

"No."

"You could have him play. The boy."

"He doesn't play anymore," Carmody said, and something in his voice suggested this was not a recent development but rather a permanent condition, an extinction event that had occurred in the past and now simply had to be lived with, like the death of someone beloved.

"Shame," Marcus said. "I heard him play once. Must have been 1985 or so. He was very good."

"Yes."

Marcus turned back to the piano. There was nothing else to say. He understood that the piano sat in this room as a kind of monument, a memorial to something lost. Perhaps the boy would die someday and someone would find it and sell it, and it would be taken by another tuner, or perhaps no tuner at all, and the strings would gradually go slack and the wood would warp and eventually it would become just another object in a thrift store, a beautiful thing made cheap by disuse. This was the fate of many fine instruments. This was the fate of most beautiful things, if you thought about it.

He finished the Carmody piano by noon and drove south through the afternoon, passing through the small downtown of Millbrook where he'd lived his entire life. The main street had hollowed out over the decades. There had been eight restaurants here in the 1980s; now there were two, and one of those was a chain. The movie theater where he'd taken his wife had been converted into a medical office. The library had shrunk to part-time. But the hardware store still stood, and the bank, and O'Malley's, which was where he took his coffee some mornings.

He didn't go in today. He had three more stops and wanted to finish before dark.

The Henderson sisters lived in a farmhouse that had belonged to their family since the 1890s. Two pianos sat in the music room—a spinet and an upright, both unremarkable instruments, both in various states of disrepair. The sisters were in their eighties, both widows, both living in the house where they'd been born. The spinet had belonged to their mother; the upright had been purchased by their father as a gift for the younger sister's twenty-first birthday, a gift that had been received with confusion and ultimately with nothing approaching enthusiasm.

"How long has it been since anyone played?" Marcus asked, already knowing.

"Eleanor plays sometimes," the older sister, Catherine, said. Eleanor was the younger one, also in her eighties. "Don't you, El?"

Eleanor shook her head. "Not in years. I don't have the hands for it anymore. The arthritis. I can't make them do what I want."

Marcus nodded. This was the same reason he would eventually have to stop, he supposed. When the hands gave out, when they could no longer be trusted to know what they knew. He pushed the thought away as he always did.

He worked on the spinet first, and the tuning revealed the instrument's essential sweetness—it was a cheerful piano, suited to simple songs and children's pieces, the kind of piano that had probably heard Czerny etudes and "Fur Elise" played earnestly and imperfectly. He could hear the history in the way the strings held their tension, in the minor scratches and scars of the felt hammers.

"It's a nice instrument," he said, to be kind, though nice was not the word he would have chosen for it. Nice meant acceptable. Nice meant it wouldn't embarrass anyone.

"Mother loved it," Catherine said. "She played every day when we were children. Just simple things. She wasn't talented—she had no training at all. But she played anyway. She said it made her happy."

"That's a good reason," Marcus said.

"After she died, no one wanted to touch it. We thought we might learn, but Eleanor was already into her forties by then, and I was older, and it seemed like it would be foolish to start."

"You could learn now," Marcus said, though he didn't believe this was true. Learning an instrument at eighty-five was not something people did. They were past the age of possibility. What remained was memory and maintenance, the careful preservation of what had already been established.

He spent an hour on the two pianos, and by the time he was done, the light had taken on the golden slant of late afternoon, that particular quality it had in autumn, as if the year was remembering its beauty before surrendering to winter. He drove without hurrying toward his final destination.

St. Catherine's Church stood on a hill that overlooked the valley, the white steeple visible from miles away. Marcus had not been inside the church in many years—he was not a believer in any systematic way—but he had come regularly in the 1960s and 1970s, back when David had still been his son in the way that meant something, before adolescence had carved them into separate creatures, before David had become someone Marcus only half-understood.

The church piano was a small upright, a modest instrument built in 1952, probably cost four hundred dollars when it was new. It had been painted and repainted, the wood now a pale cream color that had yellowed slightly. The bench sat in front of it like it was waiting for someone, though no one had sat on it in months.

Father Donnelly was in the vestry and came out to greet him, a young man who had been at the parish for five years. He had never met Marcus before, or if he had, he didn't remember it.

"I appreciate you coming out," the priest said. "The piano hasn't been tuned in nearly a year. The choir director says it's gotten quite bad."

"It will be fine," Marcus said. "These old uprights are durable. Simple wood and metal. They last longer than people do."

He set up his tools and pulled back the fallboard. The bench smelled like old hymnals and furniture polish. He sat and played the low A, and the sound that came back to him was dusty, uncertain, out of tune enough to cause him mild physical discomfort.

"Will it take long?" the priest asked.

"A few hours. You can leave me be. I'll lock up when I'm done."

"All right," the priest said. "The side door is open."

Marcus was grateful to be alone. He began the methodical work of listening and adjusting, and as he worked, the memory surfaced—not unwanted, but not invited either, simply occurring to him as memories do, unbidden and suddenly present.

David had been seventeen when he played his recital here. It was May of 1979, a warm evening, the windows open to let in the spring breeze. There had been about seventy people in attendance—family, friends, some of David's school friends who didn't understand what they were hearing but came out of loyalty. David had been studying piano seriously for six years by then, having started late, at eleven, after months of pestering. Marcus had wanted him to take it up, wanted something for his son that would last, that couldn't be destroyed by the accidents that befell human bodies and human hearts.

The boy had surprised everyone. He had talent—not prodigy-level talent, but real talent, the kind that meant he could learn a difficult piece and actually master it, could convey something of the composer's intention. Over seven years he had become decent, accomplished. He could sit at a piano and make it sound beautiful.

The recital had consisted of six pieces. Marcus remembered four of them: a Bach prelude, a Chopin nocturne, a Beethoven sonata, and Debussy's Clair de lune. David's fingers had moved with precision and grace. His face had held the kind of concentration that seemed to turn him into something other than a teenager, something more essential. When he finished the last piece—he remembered this vividly—when he lifted his hands from the keys and the final notes faded, there had been a moment of complete silence, the kind of silence that only comes when everyone in a room feels the presence of something small and genuine and true.

That was the last time David had played in public. He never played again, as far as Marcus knew. After college—David had studied business, had become an accountant—they had drifted further apart, the way fathers and sons sometimes do, particularly when they are people who don't know how to speak about important things. David had moved away, had married, had children whom Marcus saw twice a year and only barely knew. He had never asked David why he'd stopped playing. That conversation seemed too dangerous, too fraught with possible meanings.

Marcus tuned the church piano carefully, giving it the attention he gave all instruments, no more, no less. But his mind kept drifting to that recital, to the boy at seventeen who had sat where Marcus was sitting now and made something beautiful happen. That boy no longer existed. David had become someone else, someone competent and respectable and distant. Marcus had become someone whose hands still knew how to do the thing the boy had loved, even though the boy didn't anymore.

It was six o'clock when Marcus finished. The piano was back in tune, its voice restored to something like clarity. He played a scale—all the notes aligned now, the intervals true. He played the Bach prelude that David had played. His fingers didn't remember it consciously, but something in his hands knew the shape of the notes, the way they moved. He played slowly, not perfectly, but with the kind of care you used for something precious.

The sound filled the empty church. There was no one to hear it except himself. The choir director would hear it on Sunday, would note with satisfaction that the piano was playable again. The choir would sing next to it, their imperfect voices mixing with its refined sound. And then it would return to being an accompaniment to hymns, a support for the liturgy, useful and unnoticed, the way most beautiful things are.

Marcus locked the side door behind him and walked out to his truck. The sun was nearly down, the sky an orange color that promised a clear night. He sat in the cab for a moment before turning the key, looking back at the white church with its narrow steeple, the building where he had witnessed his son become temporarily someone he recognized.

Tomorrow he would not make the route. Tomorrow he would do what he'd been thinking about doing for months now—he would call his assistant, a younger man named Richard who had been his apprentice for eight years, and tell him that he was ready to retire. Richard was competent. He would take good care of the pianos. They would continue to be tuned even though no one played them, continued to hold the possibility of music even in their silence.

But tonight, Marcus drove slowly home through the autumn dark, his hands still remembering the touch of the keys, still able to call forth the sound of a boy who had once made something beautiful and then had simply stopped, as if he had been a song that had finished, or a story that had ended, or a life that had been lived and was now over, leaving only the memory of its own brief music.
