The first thing they took was the credenza from the hallway—the one with the curved legs that her mother had refinished twice, once in dark mahogany and again, after a magazines subscription had convinced her otherwise, in a pale oak that never quite suited it. Margaret watched from the kitchen doorway as two men in matching navy t-shirts maneuvered it through the front entrance, tilting it at angles she hadn't known were possible, their faces contorted with the small mathematics of moving objects through doorways. Her mother had bought that credenza in 1987, Margaret thought. She remembered because her parents had driven to Indianapolis for it, and her father had complained the entire way about the money.

The house was already beginning to look emptied, though they'd only been there twenty minutes. The living room, which had seemed so crowded with furniture just days ago, now appeared merely full of air. The afternoon light came in at a different angle without the heavy drapes, and the walls—beige, her mother had settled on beige—seemed almost shocked by their own visibility.

"That's lot forty-two," one of the men called out, checking a clipboard. Margaret nodded, though they weren't talking to her. The estate sale company had been professional but efficient in a way that suggested her mother's possessions were not entirely unique, that this particular assembly of things had only the value that the market would assign to it. Margaret found this oddly comforting.

She had been back in Ashbrook for nine days. Nine days since her brother Michael called to tell her that their mother had died in her sleep—which was what everyone said, though their mother had actually died during her afternoon nap, which Michael had discovered when he came by with groceries because their mother could no longer be trusted to remember how to grocery shop, or at least that was what he'd told her over the phone. The tone suggested judgment, though Margaret couldn't quite decide if it was directed at their mother or at Margaret herself, for not being the one who discovered her.

She had taken a leave from her job in Pittsburgh—not requested, but taken, simply not answering emails after Thursday afternoon. Her boss would understand or she would not; Margaret found she no longer had the energy to predict which. There were systems in place, other people trained to handle the accounts. The world, she'd realized somewhere in the past decade, was surprisingly indifferent to any single person's participation in it.

The dining room table was next. That one hurt in a way she hadn't anticipated. It was dark cherry, or what the estate sale company's appraiser had called "cherry-finish laminate," and it had been the site of sixteen years of family dinners, the scratch on its edge from when Michael knocked over a candle during an argument about college, the stain underneath that no one ever discussed but everyone knew was red wine from the night her father came home from the hospital and announced that the angiogram showed nothing definitive but that he was taking early retirement anyway, just in case.

Margaret had eaten breakfast there this morning alone, cereal and coffee, before the men arrived. She'd thought of cooking something more elaborate, something that would require the use of the pots and pans that were already sold to someone she'd never meet, but in the end she'd settled for the quiet consumption of something simple. The kitchen, at least, would be the last room stripped bare. Kitchens were functional spaces; people wanted them equipped.

She moved to the window and watched a young couple across the street loading items into their car. The woman, who couldn't have been more than thirty, held a lamp—not a particularly nice lamp, Margaret knew this one, a brass floor lamp from the nineties with a linen shade that had started to fray at the seams—and turned to the man with an expression of genuine delight. He smiled back. They drove away with the lamp in the back seat, and Margaret wondered what story they would tell about it, what place it would occupy in the mythology of their life together. Eventually, if they stayed together long enough, they would forget where it came from. It would simply be the lamp they'd had forever.

Her mother had kept everything. Not in a hoarding sense—Margaret had seen hoarding, had visited a friend's mother's house in Columbus and understood the real geography of pathology—but in a systematic, willful way. She had kept the outgrown clothes from Margaret and Michael's childhoods in labeled boxes in the attic. She had kept the school photographs, the dental records, the letters they'd sent from summer camp. The estate sale company had found three boxes of photographs from the 1950s belonging to people Margaret didn't recognize, and when Margaret had asked about them, the appraiser had simply shrugged and catalogued them as "vintage photo lot."

Margaret was supposed to sort through the personal effects, the company had said. They would handle the furniture and the decorative items, but the papers, the photographs, the items of obvious sentimental value—those were Margaret's responsibility. They were in the upstairs bedroom, piled on the bed, waiting. Margaret had not yet brought herself to approach them.

Instead, she had spent the morning doing inventory of the kitchen, which was absurd because someone else had already done this inventory, but it gave her something to do with her hands. She had counted the place settings—eight, though their family had only ever numbered four, and after her father died, three. She had examined the contents of the junk drawer, where she found a collection of restaurant loyalty cards from establishments that had closed years ago, a broken measuring tape, seventeen twist ties of varying ages, a single earring that matched nothing she'd ever seen her mother wear. There was a logic to the accumulation, but it was a private logic, one that had lived entirely inside their mother's head and had died with her.

The bedroom furniture was next. The two men came upstairs, and Margaret heard their footsteps above her head, heard them moving through her mother's room with the casual intimacy of strangers hired to remove the evidence of a life. She stayed in the kitchen. She didn't want to watch them take the bed where her mother had slept for forty years, where Margaret's father had slept beside her for thirty-eight of those years, where her mother had ultimately chosen to close her eyes for the last time, either peacefully or not peacefully—the coroner's report said "natural causes," which meant nothing, which meant everything.

When Michael called the evening before, he'd asked if she wanted him to come help. She had said no. She had said it was fine, she could manage it, she would call if she needed anything. Michael lived in Shaker Heights with his second wife and her two children from her first marriage, and they had tickets to something—a concert or a show, Margaret had not processed the details. He had sounded relieved when she declined, though he'd protested once, making a show of his willingness. Margaret knew her brother well enough to know that his willingness and his actual desire were not the same thing. This was not a failing, she thought. It was simply the truth of how he was built.

By two o'clock, the living and dining rooms were entirely empty. The impressions of furniture legs remained in the carpet, darker squares against the slightly faded remainder. The house now had the appearance of a stage after the play has ended and the props have been struck, all possibility of narrative drained away. Margaret walked through the rooms and found them disorienting in their emptiness, as if she'd been moved to an entirely different house overnight.

She had lunch in the kitchen—a sandwich from the deli on Main Street, which she ate standing at the counter next to the window. Outside, the April afternoon was mild, almost warm. Ashbrook looked as it always did, which meant it looked slightly abandoned, though this was not something that had changed in her mother's lifetime or probably in anyone's lifetime. The town had survived the decline of its manufacturing base by contracting gradually, like a slow exhalation. There was still a downtown of sorts, though several storefronts were dark. The library had moved to smaller quarters. The high school, which Margaret and Michael had both attended, had been absorbed into a regional district system, and the building itself now served as offices for some county agency.

Her mother had never left Ashbrook, except for vacations to places like the Outer Banks or Gatlinburg, brief excursions from which she returned grateful to be home. Margaret had found this contentment almost mystifying when she was younger, had interpreted it as a failure of ambition. Now, at fifty-three, she wondered if it had been something else entirely—a genuine love of place, or simply the knowledge that wherever you went, you took your interior life with you, and that interior life was usually the problem. Her mother's interior life had been tidy, or at least she had presented it as tidy. She had been a woman who believed in proper procedures: you made your bed in the morning, you dressed in clothes appropriate to the day's activities, you said grace before meals even though her faith seemed to Margaret to be more cultural than spiritual.

She was finishing her sandwich when a woman came to the kitchen door. She was probably in her seventies, Margaret thought, with perm-curled gray hair and a cardigan despite the mild afternoon.

"I'm looking for a china cabinet?" the woman said. She pronounced it like a question, which Margaret found endearing. "The company said they'd point me toward the best things. I thought your mother might have had a china cabinet?"

Margaret stared at her for a moment. The china cabinet—where had they moved it? She had spent so little time watching the process, had let it happen at the periphery of her attention.

"There was one," Margaret said finally. "In the dining room. But I'm not sure where they've put the sold items."

"Oh, it's not sold yet. They said it might still be available. I'm interested in it."

The china cabinet had belonged to their grandmother, Margaret's father's mother, who had left it to them in her will. It was walnut with glass doors and tiny hinges and brass fixtures that would tarnish if you didn't polish them regularly. Her mother had polished them regularly. For as long as Margaret could remember, the china cabinet had held the good china, the Lenox set that was used maybe three times a year, on Thanksgiving and Christmas and Easter. The rest of the time it sat there, beautiful and unused, a kind of insurance policy against the possibility that someday important people might come for dinner and it would matter if the plates matched and were fine.

"It's in good condition," Margaret heard herself say. "The hinges sometimes stick a little bit, but that's just age."

"That's all right," the woman said. She smiled. She had a kind face, Margaret thought. She was the kind of person who probably had a china cabinet in her home because it held meaning to her, not because it was the kind of thing you were supposed to have.

"I'll ask them where it is," Margaret said.

The woman looked around the kitchen, taking in the evidence of the morning's work. "It must be strange," she said, "seeing your mother's things go out into the world."

Margaret didn't answer immediately. It was strange, yes, but not in the way the woman meant. It was strange because it didn't feel like her mother's things anymore. Her mother had been evacuated from them. The furniture and the dishes and the small decorative objects had become items in a catalog, assigned lot numbers and estimated values. It was a kind of posthumous dissolution, and Margaret found it not terrible but clarifying. This was what happened to everything eventually. You died, and the things you'd loved became inventory.

"Yes," Margaret said. "It's strange."

She found the china cabinet in the hallway, waiting to be transported. She brought the woman to see it, and the woman circled it slowly, the way you might circle a horse you were considering purchasing. She opened and closed the glass doors, checked the joints, ran her fingers along the wood grain.

"It's beautiful," the woman said. "Really beautiful. Do you think I could make an offer?"

Margaret looked at the china cabinet, and for just a moment, a small moment that she would forget within an hour and remember again in five years when the light fell at a certain angle in her Pittsburgh kitchen, she wanted to say no. She wanted to say that this cabinet had been in her family for three generations and that it should stay with her, should continue to hold the Lenox china and the special occasions that were, in the end, the small ceremonies by which you marked the fact that you were alive and continuing to move through time with people you supposedly loved.

Instead, she said, "Let me ask the men in charge. They'll do the paperwork."

The woman paid six hundred dollars for the china cabinet, which was less than the appraiser had estimated but which the woman seemed to think was generous. Margaret watched them load it into a van, securing it with blankets and careful knots of rope. The woman waved as she drove away, and Margaret waved back, and that was the end of the china cabinet.

After that, she finally forced herself upstairs. The bedroom was a mess of half-packed boxes and scattered papers. The bed was gone, and without it, the room looked shockingly small. The carpet was a faded rose color that Margaret remembered had been controversial—her father had wanted to refinish the wood floors, but her mother had installed wall-to-wall carpet, claiming it was more luxurious, more restful.

The personal effects were still on the second bed, the one that had been Margaret's childhood bed and that her mother had kept in the guest room, with a quilt Margaret's grandmother had made in 1962. The quilt had a lot number now. Someone would take it home and appreciate it or not, would eventually donate it to Goodwill or use it as a moving blanket or let it deteriorate in a closet.

Margaret sat on the edge of the guest bed and looked at the boxes. She opened one at random and found her high school yearbooks, her school photographs, a collection of certificates acknowledging perfect attendance and academic achievement. She opened another and found letters, real letters, written on actual paper and mailed in envelopes, from college friends and distant cousins. Some were dated back to the 1980s. People no longer wrote these kinds of letters. Margaret's own correspondence, if it existed at all, existed in the cloud, in deleted email folders, in the temporary memory of servers she didn't understand.

She found a photograph of herself at twenty-five, at her college graduation, standing between her parents. Her mother had her arm around Margaret's waist, and her father was squinting into the sun. Margaret looked at her own face in the photograph and tried to remember who that girl was, what she had wanted or expected. The girl had expected, Margaret thought, that her parents would age slowly and then, after a very long time, die, and that she would be sad about it in the way people were sad about these inevitable things. She had not expected that by forty her father would have a heart attack while getting ready for work, that he would die four years later anyway, his heart unreliable, his will to continue compromised. She had not expected that her mother would drift into a kind of softness after that, a fog that wasn't quite depression and wasn't quite acceptance but something in between.

Margaret placed the photograph back in the box. She didn't take anything. She told herself this was because she had limited luggage and a limited life in Pittsburgh—a small apartment, a job that didn't require reminders of where she'd come from. But she suspected it was something else. She suspected she couldn't bear to choose what to keep, and so she was choosing to keep nothing, which felt like a kind of honesty.

It was nearly four o'clock when the last pieces of furniture were carried out. The living room, the dining room, and the bedrooms were now just spaces, rooms without furniture, windows without curtains. The kitchen remained as it was, fully equipped for a life that was no longer being lived. Margaret walked through the house and found it almost beautiful in its emptiness. This was what remained when you stripped away the evidence of living: just the bones of the structure, just the walls and the floors and the light coming through the windows.

She sat in the living room on the floor—there was nowhere else to sit—and called the estate sale company to report that she was leaving the house. They said someone would pick up the remaining items tomorrow, that the house would be locked by evening, that the keys should be left with the real estate agent who was handling the sale.

"Do you want to keep anything?" the man on the phone asked, and Margaret realized this was not actually part of their standard procedure, that he was asking out of something like kindness.

"No," Margaret said. "No, I don't think so."

She locked the kitchen door behind her and drove the keys to the real estate office on Main Street. The agent was a woman named Sandra whom Margaret had not gone to school with but had heard of, the kind of person who had moved to Ashbrook from elsewhere and established herself through competence and consistency. Sandra took the keys with the same kind of gentle efficiency she would bring to everything in her life.

"Your mother was a lovely woman," Sandra said, which was what people said, and which was true, though Margaret was never sure what it meant for a woman to be lovely, what it added up to, what it changed.

Margaret drove out of Ashbrook as the light began to lose its afternoon brightness. She did not look at the house as she passed it, did not look at the familiar streets, did not think about the ninety miles back to Pittsburgh and her apartment and her job that was waiting for her with the patience of institutional systems. She was thinking instead about the young couple with the lamp, about the woman with the china cabinet, about the fact that her mother's life had now been dispersed among strangers, that it would continue in small ways—a lamp that lit a room, a cabinet that held dishes, a quilt that covered a bed—and that this was probably not the worst way to be remembered. Probably it was, in its way, a kind of immortality, the immortality of usefulness, of continuing to serve a purpose even after the original purpose had been forgotten.

She thought about this for the rest of the drive, and when she got home, she didn't unpack right away. She sat in her apartment, which was very quiet and very small and very full of her own objects—the furniture and the dishes and the small decorative items that constituted the visible evidence of her interior life. She wondered, looking at these things, who would eventually carry them out a door, who would receive them, what stories they would enter into next. It didn't seem important. What seemed important was the living, the continuing movement through time, the small ceremonies and large structures by which a life was built and then, eventually, dismantled.
