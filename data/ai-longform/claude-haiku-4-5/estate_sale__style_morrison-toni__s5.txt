The morning had begun with coffee she didn't taste and the signing of papers that meant nothing until they meant everything, and by afternoon Carolyn was standing in what used to be her mother's sitting room watching a white man with a clipboard and teeth the color of old piano keys direct two younger men—sons, maybe, or just workers, she couldn't tell and didn't ask—through the logistics of removal. The credenza first. The secretary desk. The chairs her mother had reupholstered twice, three times maybe, in fabrics that grew progressively lighter as her mother aged, as if she were trying to fill the rooms with air instead of weight.

It was July, that thick July that makes Ohio feel like the inside of something, a mouth or a closet, and Carolyn had worn the wrong clothes—a long cardigan despite the heat, stockings though no one would see her legs, things that made sense only in the context of the dead and the formal arrangements of death, which she was still, three weeks and two days later, formally arranging. The clipboard man—Edwin, his name was Edwin—had assured her this was the fastest option, the least painful, a phrase that struck her as medical though he was selling furniture, not offering diagnosis. Everything would be gone by six o'clock. Everything. The word had weight.

She'd arrived at six in the morning to walk through the house alone, her key still working though the house wasn't hers and had never been, not really, despite the fact that she'd been born in the upstairs bedroom and spent thirty-four years walking these floors. Ownership had a different taste entirely. Her mother had owned it. Carolyn had only ever been a visitor who forgot to leave.

The light was different in the morning. Harder. She'd stood in the kitchen where her mother had stood for fifty-six years—Carolyn had counted, had done that arithmetic without meaning to—and she'd counted the cabinet doors. Twenty-three. She'd opened each one knowing what would be inside and was not surprised to find precisely that. The good glasses, wrapped in old newspaper from 1987. The hand-painted serving dishes from an aunt in Georgia. Salt and pepper shakers in the shape of little black children, a thing no one would say anymore, a thing her mother had displayed on the shelf next to the window with the same unconscious ease she displayed her faith, her newspaper subscription, her opinions about the neighbors.

She hadn't slept in the house. She'd gotten a room at the Ramada two towns over, telling herself it was practical, she didn't want to be distracted by memories, by the weight of thirty-four years pressing down on her in the dark. But the truth was that she was afraid. Afraid of what she might become if she lay down on the bed where she'd lost her virginity at seventeen, where she'd cried about her father, where she'd sat at two in the morning at thirty-eight deciding whether to leave her husband or not—and she had, in the end, left him, or he had left first and she had simply acknowledged the fact of it. The house held all of that. The walls were thin and had ears.

Now, at two-thirty in the afternoon, with the dining room half-empty and the living room looking like a mouth with teeth knocked out, she stood in her mother's bedroom and watched the younger of the two men—he had a name tag that said Marcus, though that seemed unlikely to her, seemed like something he would have made up—attempt to maneuver the bed frame around the doorway. The bed was walnut, or said it was walnut, though Carolyn suspected her mother had gotten it on sale at Lazarus in 1962 and that "walnut" was a marketing term for "dark brown." Still, watching it leave felt like watching architecture collapse.

Her mother had slept there for forty-three years, alone for the last twenty of them. Not sleeping, actually, not in the way that younger people slept, in the way that Carolyn had slept as a child with her mouth open and her dreams operating in full color and volume. Her mother had lain there in the dark and waited. For what, Carolyn still didn't know. For her husband to come back? He wouldn't. For her daughter to move home? She wouldn't. For her life to make sense in retrospect? It hadn't. Now it never would.

The bed caught on the doorframe and Marcus swore, a small and efficient thing, a word that sounded like it came from his shoulders rather than his mouth. Edwin appeared in the doorway with his clipboard held against his chest like a child. "Might need to angle it," he said, speaking to Marcus but also speaking to Carolyn, or rather speaking to the space where Carolyn stood, which is the way people spoke to her now, as though she were grieving but not present, as though her mother's death had made her into a kind of ghost in reverse, visible but not quite there.

She'd been there when her mother died. That was the thing no one understood. She'd been right there in the room, her hand on her mother's hand, which was warm and then not, which was soft and then something else, something that no longer belonged to the category of things you could touch in comfort. She'd been present for that and had felt completely absent, as though the event were happening to someone else, as though she were watching herself watch her mother die, a doubling that had never quite resolved. Three weeks and two days later, she was still watching herself watch, still out of her own body, still floating above the proceedings like a delayed apparition.

The bed made it through the door. Marcus was sweating through his shirt in an impressive way, dark continents spreading across his back and his chest. He was young, maybe twenty-five, with hands that looked like they knew how to move things, and Carolyn thought about how her mother had never known this man's name and would never know it, and how he would carry her bed out to the truck and into someone else's bedroom, and that person would sleep there and would wake there, would live there in a way that her mother could no longer do.

There was something obscene about it. She had said that to someone—to David, her ex-husband, when she'd called him to tell him about the sale. "It feels obscene," she'd said, and David had made a sound of agreement, though they hadn't spoken in three years and he'd hated her mother with a consistency and gentleness that was its own kind of love, the kind of love you gave to something you despised because despising it was better than indifference. "She'd hate it," he'd said, and Carolyn had almost laughed, because he was right, her mother would have hated it, would have hated the violation of strangers handling her things, and this was, in fact, the final violation, the last thing she'd be forced to endure.

Except her mother was dead and so she would endure it peacefully, unconsciously, the way the dead endured everything.

By four o'clock, the downstairs was mostly empty. Edwin came to find her where she was sitting on the back porch in a chair that was not yet sold, a plastic thing from the lawn furniture section of a long-ago summer, peeling blue paint and the smell of rust underneath. The heat had gotten worse, or she had gotten more aware of it, or both things were true simultaneously. The sky was white with it.

"Almost done," Edwin said, and she understood that he was giving her permission to have feelings about this, or at least that he was acknowledging the possibility that she might, that it was not unprecedented for people to feel something when their dead mother's furniture left their dead mother's house.

"My mother bought that chair," Carolyn said, and it was the first sentence she'd spoken all day, and it came out rough and uncertain, like something that had traveled a long distance to reach her mouth.

"The plastic one?" Edwin looked at it as though it might have secret value, might be worth something after all.

"The piano," Carolyn said. "In the living room. My mother bought it in 1968. I took lessons for four years. I was awful. I was really, truly terrible."

Edwin adjusted his clipboard. He was a good man, she thought. A kind man. A man who had seen many people stand in their dead mothers' houses and had developed what seemed to be a genuine sympathy for the activity, which was not selling furniture but rather selling the evidence of a life, the physical manifestation of decades, the things you accumulate as proof that you were here.

"Piano's already gone," he said gently. "That couple from Columbus got it. They were excited. Said they had a daughter who played."

This was meant to be comforting. Carolyn understood this. The piano would not be destroyed. It would be used. It would have a second life, a kind of resurrection, which was more than most things got. She nodded because that was what was expected.

"Your daughter know you're getting rid of all this?" Edwin asked, and Carolyn realized she'd never mentioned having a daughter, so either Edwin had misremembered or he was testing her, or he was being kind in the way that some people were kind when they detected a gap, the space where something should be but wasn't.

"I don't have a daughter," Carolyn said. "I had a miscarriage in 1981. I had a son who was stillborn in 1985. And I had a daughter for exactly fourteen minutes in 1989, and then she had a heart condition that was not compatible with living, and she died, and I decided that was enough. That that was my quota."

This was too much information. Edwin's clipboard had become very important to him. He studied it as though the next item on his list—the nightstands, she guessed, or the lamps—might contain wisdom about how to respond to a woman who had accumulated dead children like her mother had accumulated furniture, without meaning to, through the simple act of existing in time.

"I'm sorry," he said, and he meant it, she could hear that, and it was worse somehow than if he hadn't meant it, because his sorrow added nothing, changed nothing, simply existed as proof that he had heard her.

She'd called David again after the miscarriage. Not the 1981 one but the most recent one, the one that had sent her husband away finally, or given him permission to leave, or simply made clear to him that she was a vessel that could not hold what was being poured into it. David had said, "Maybe it's a blessing," and she had said, "If you say that one more time I will kill you," and he had taken her at her word and divorced her.

The piano had been her mother's idea, the lessons. "You need an accomplishment," her mother had said, which was her way of saying that Carolyn was a disappointment, that her daughter was growing into a shape her mother had not anticipated, had not desired. The piano would fix that. The piano would make her daughter into the kind of girl who could sit straight and play things that other people had written and would be recognized for it, would be seen as accomplished and refined and the kind of girl that mothers could point to with satisfaction.

Carolyn had learned three songs badly and quit, and her mother had paid the piano teacher for the lessons she would have taken if she'd continued, out of principal or shame or the simple inability to admit failure, and the piano had sat in the living room for fifty-two years, a daily reminder that her daughter was who she was, regardless of intervention.

"You can go," Edwin said, and at first she didn't understand that he meant her, that he was releasing her from her post. "We're almost done. We don't need you here for this part. Most people don't stay."

This was better. This was mercy. Carolyn stood and felt her knees complain, felt her body remember that she was fifty-six years old, a woman in the middle of her life who was now, officially, an orphan. She'd used that word in her head and had immediately felt foolish—orphans were children, weren't they? Or at least young people, people who had a future in front of them that might be shaped by this loss. She was too old to be an orphan. She was simply a woman whose mother had died, which was the natural order of things, which happened to everyone.

Except it hadn't happened to her before, and she had discovered that there was no age at which you were ready for your mother to stop existing, no point at which her opinions stopped mattering, her absence stopped creating a vacuum, her things stopped being evidence of a life that had mattered, even if she'd kept that life small and careful, even if she'd spent fifty-six years not understanding her daughter, not seeing her, not knowing her in any way that might have bridged the gap between them.

"Thank you," Carolyn said, and she walked to her car, which she'd parked on the street in front of the house, as though she were visiting, as though she might leave. Her purse was on the passenger seat. Her keys were somewhere in it. The sky was beginning to turn that particular shade of late afternoon that meant the worst of the heat was over, that evening was coming, that another day was dying.

She sat in the car with the door open and the heat pouring out of it and she did not start the engine. Through the windshield she could see Edwin directing Marcus to bring out the china cabinet, the piece her mother had been most proud of, the thing she'd kept stocked with serving pieces that were never used, decorative objects that belonged to a version of her mother's life that had never quite existed, or had existed only in her imagination, in her plans, in the shapes of things left empty.

She would drive to the Ramada. She would sit in her room and order food that she would not eat. She would watch television in a language she didn't speak, because by tomorrow her childhood would be gone, translated into someone else's life, integrated into someone else's home in the way that everything alive must eventually be translated into something else—memory, absence, regret, or simply forgetting, which was its own kind of death, smaller and quieter but somehow more final.

The piano was gone. Her mother was gone. Soon this house would be gone, sold to someone who would paint over the walls and tear out the carpets and construct their own life on top of the architecture of her mother's life, and there would be nothing left to prove that she had been here, that Carolyn had been here, that the two of them had occupied this space together in a kind of armed truce, never touching, always present, for thirty-four years.

She turned the key and the engine started. The radio came on to a station she didn't remember setting, and an old song was playing, something from her childhood, and her hands were shaking on the wheel, and she could not decide what she was grieving—her mother or herself, or this thing in between, this love that had never been articulated, this knowledge that she would never now be loved in the way she had needed to be loved, that it was too late, that her mother had taken that particular possibility into the ground with her.

Carolyn pulled away from the curb slowly, carefully, the way you drove when you were trying not to shake, when you were trying to remain present in your own body, when you were trying very hard to keep yourself from flying away altogether.
