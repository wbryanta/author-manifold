# The Depths

The truck bed was still wet when Margaret pulled into the overlook, though the sun had been climbing for three hours. She cut the engine and sat for a moment, hands on the wheel, watching the light hit the exposed mudflats where Calloway Lake had been retreating for eighteen months now. The water that remained clung to the lowest points like a pale eye, half-closed and receding further with each passing week.

She was fifty-three years old. The last time she'd stood at this particular overlook, she was twelve.

The surveying equipment sat in its cases in the truck bed—the transit, the measuring wheel, the theodolite, her grandfather's old brass compass that still worked better than anything digital. She'd brought more than she needed. It was easier to overpack than to admit she was nervous.

The county engineer had been straightforward about the assignment. The reservoir was at thirty-four percent capacity, the lowest since the dam was constructed in 1962. The geological survey wanted updated maps of what was underneath, the original contour lines redrawn for the new topography that was emerging from decades of silt and memory. Margaret had done similar work before—had mapped exposed riverbeds, had walked the skeletons of valleys that had temporarily lost their skin to drought and emergency releases. But she had never mapped the place where she was born.

She'd left Calloway at eighteen, moved to Sacramento, became a surveyor because her father had been a surveyor, and because the work kept her outside and moving and pointed toward problems that had definitive solutions. You could measure the world. You could make it make sense on paper.

She pulled the cases from the truck bed and set them on the packed earth of the overlook parking area. The asphalt was cracked, edges curling up where the heat had loosened the seal. A few other cars were parked here—tourists, probably, come to see the disaster as if it were a scenic attraction. She'd passed them on the way in: a couple taking selfies with the dying lake behind them, a man in a fishing hat with binoculars around his neck, staring at the water as if waiting for it to tell him something.

The town itself was still three miles down the old highway, accessed now by a service road that had been buried since 1962. The county had made her aware that the road was rough, that she should watch for sink holes, that the exposing of the drowned town had revealed unstable ground in places. The state was going to drain the lake entirely by November if the drought continued. They wanted documentation of what had been down there, just in case the rains came and they had to flood it again. Just in case the world went back to normal and Calloway Lake needed to be a lake again, not a grave.

Margaret loaded her pack—compass, measuring wheel, two surveying poles, her notebook, a water bottle she'd filled that morning in the motel room. She left the more sensitive equipment in the truck. The walking survey would come first. Tomorrow she could set up transit stations and get proper measurements. Today she just needed to see it.

The service road started behind a chain-link fence that had been cut, the edges pulled back like lips. She stepped through and began walking downward.

The slope was steeper than she remembered, or perhaps her memory of it was a child's memory, where distances were longer and angles more pronounced. The exposed earth was pale, almost white where it had been underwater for so long. The sun had baked the surface into a crust that held her footprints only shallowly. Deeper down, the earth was darker and held the smell of lake bottom—a mineral, loamy smell that brought back something complicated and old.

She'd been here when they'd flooded it. She remembered that, suddenly, with specificity. She was twelve. They'd had a week's notice, and she'd ridden her bike around town saying goodbye to things as if they were people. The general store where Mrs. Chen had always given her a butterscotch when her mother brought her in. The school where she'd spent first through fifth grade. The Methodist church where she'd been confirmed the summer before. The park where there was a swing set and a tetherball pole.

Her father had spent those final days with his surveying instruments, taking final measurements, documenting what was being lost. She'd watched him work, his eye to the transit, his mouth moving slightly as he calculated. When it was done, he'd put the instruments away and they'd gone home and packed.

The road descended into the bowl of the valley, and as she walked, the landscape began to resolve into something recognizable. She could see where the asphalt had been, darker lines in the pale earth. She could see what had been the ditch along the eastern side, still holding the darker color of settled water-silt. She could see the outline of the first house—a foundation made of concrete, exposed now and marked with the white salt deposits that had accumulated along its edges during sixty years of wet-and-dry, wet-and-dry cycles.

She stopped and pulled out her notebook, made a sketch. The foundation was roughly rectangular, maybe thirty by forty feet, with a chimney stack visible in the northeastern corner. The hearth was still there, built of river stone, still relatively intact. She measured the perimeter with her measuring wheel, noted the orientation, marked the chimney.

The house that had stood here had belonged to the Kowalskis, she thought. Or maybe that was the one two streets over. The names were coming back to her, but imperfectly, like voices from another room.

She continued down the road, and the town began to reveal itself in layers. More foundations appeared, arranged in loose rows where the streets had been. She could see the shape of it now—a small town, perhaps two hundred structures when it was alive, arranged along three main streets with residential sections spreading out toward the valley walls. The lake had taken all of it.

The center of town was where the banks of the old creek had been, before the dam. This had been the lowest point then, and it was the lowest point now, the water pooled there in a gray-silver sheet that seemed to repel the light rather than reflect it. She stood at its edge and looked across it.

The far shore of the water pool, such as it was, showed more exposed earth, more foundations. She could see what had been the main commercial district, buildings larger and more complex than the residential structures. The hardware store, maybe, and the bank, and the grocery. She'd walked these streets as a child, but not with any real awareness of them. She'd lived them, the way children lived in their childhoods—completely, without the architecture of comparison or memory.

She pulled out her compass and took a bearing. The sun was higher now, nearly overhead. She'd been walking for two hours. There was still most of the day left, but she was feeling the accumulation of emotion in her chest, a tightness that came from being here, from seeing the shape of her past mapped out on the earth like an archaeological dig.

She decided to move methodically, to work as a surveyor rather than as a person with history here. She established a baseline, running east-west through the center of town, using the old road as her reference. She placed a stake at the western edge and another at what she estimated was the center, and then began a systematic accounting of what lay between them.

Foundation 1: Rectangular, approximately 24 x 36 feet. Intact chimney. Hearth visible. Calcification on northern edge indicating higher water line. Debris scattered in a two-foot radius: what appeared to be ceramic tile, fragments of corroded metal, unidentifiable materials calcified together by decades of saturation and weathering.

Foundation 2: Roughly L-shaped, perhaps a house with an addition. Chimney intact. Larger foundation, maybe 32 x 42 feet with an L-extension of approximately 20 x 20 feet. More debris, including what looked like a cast-iron cookstove, heavily oxidized.

Foundation 3: Very small, maybe 12 x 16 feet. Possibly a small cottage or outbuilding. No chimney. The foundation was cracked in two places, damage from settling or frost heave or the simple pressure of being underwater for sixty years.

She worked her way through the afternoon, moving through the streets methodically, measuring and sketching and noting. The town arranged itself for her in terms of dimensions and coordinates, in relationships of distance and orientation. It was easier to think of it this way, in terms of the work. The work had rules. The work made sense.

It was close to five o'clock when she came to what had been the school.

She knew it was the school before she was fully aware of knowing. The building was gone completely, but the foundation was large and complex, a cross-shaped footprint that resolved itself into a main building with wings extending north and south. She'd walked these halls. She'd sat in Mrs. Reeves' fifth-grade class with the windows that looked out toward the mountains. She'd eaten lunch in the cafeteria on the southern wing, the smell of the county's institutional food hanging in the air—mystery meat and canned vegetables and chocolate milk in paper cartons.

She stood in the center of the cross and tried to orient herself, to remember where things had been. The main entrance would have been on the east side, where there was a break in the foundation, a gap where the doors had been. She walked to it, stepped over the threshold into where the lobby had been.

The interior walls were gone, of course, dissolved into the earth or broken down by the pressure of water and time. But the floor was there, a concrete slab that had kept its integrity, marked with cracks and discoloration but still essentially flat, still essentially itself. She could see the wear patterns in the concrete where feet had walked for decades. The heaviest traffic ran east-west, the main corridor from the entrance to the back of the building. There was a secondary pattern north-south, the hallway toward the classrooms.

She followed the east-west corridor, walking on the concrete floor of her own childhood, feeling the ghost of the present moment and the past moment occupying the same space in her body. The concrete was cold even though the sun was still warm. She was sweating. The back of her neck was flushed.

The main hallway ended in an intersection where the north and south wings met the eastern section. The cafeteria would have been to the south, she thought, and the classrooms to the north. She turned north.

The north wing had collapsed partially, its concrete cracked and heaved, the surface spalled and rough where the ice had expanded in the cracks year after year. But she could still see the edges of the individual classroom spaces, the floor slab divided into sections by the weight-bearing walls. She counted them as she walked: five classrooms in a row, each one roughly twenty by twenty-five feet. The third one would have been Mrs. Reeves' class. She stopped in front of it, stood in what would have been the doorway.

The room was empty of everything but the outline of itself. She could see the impression where a radiator had been, the rust stains marking its footprint. She could see where the chalk dust had accumulated along the edges of the room, creating a pale shadow against the darker concrete. She could almost see the desks, the students, herself at age eleven, bored and restless and completely unaware that this place was going to stop existing in a week's time.

She'd thought about it as an escape route at the time. She'd thought about the flooding, and instead of sadness, she'd primarily felt excitement. The town was going away, and with it would go her elementary school, her small church, her small library, all the constraints of a small place. She was going to Sacramento where her father had been offered a job, where there were more people and more possibilities. She'd been ready to leave. She'd been more than ready.

But she was also twelve, and she'd stood in this classroom, or walked past it, on her last day of school, and had felt something turn over inside her, something that recognized that this room, this place, these people would not exist in the same way again. They would not exist at all, as a place. They would become something else. A concept. A memory. A drowned town underneath a drowned lake, sealed away in the earth and the water, inaccessible except as narrative.

Margaret turned away from the classroom and walked back to the entrance. She sat down on the threshold, on the concrete edge of what had been the door frame, and she cried, which surprised her. She was not a person who cried often. She was not a person who let feelings accumulate until they spilled out this way, suddenly, into the place where they occurred.

The sun was lower now, sliding toward the western hills. The light had taken on that slanting quality it had in the late afternoon, picking out details in the exposed earth, the texture of the silt, the geometry of the structures. In this light, the town was almost beautiful. The cruel exposure had a kind of honesty to it. You could see what had been here. You could map the contours of the lost place.

She dried her face with the back of her hand and looked at the work ahead of her.

She had come here to map this place, to create a document of what was underneath, to satisfy the county's need for updated surveys. It was work she could do. It was work she would do. But standing here, on the threshold of her childhood, she understood now what she'd kept away from when she'd accepted the job. She'd understood that she would have to see it. She'd understood, even if she hadn't admitted it, that she would have to measure the place where she'd stopped being a child and become someone who needed to leave.

Tomorrow she would bring her full equipment. She would set up transit stations and take elevation readings and create a proper map, something that could be used by engineers and planners and the county board. She would do the work exactly as she always did it, with precision and care and professional distance.

But tonight, she would walk the town alone. She would try to find her house. She would see if the swing set in the park had left any trace on the earth. She would walk the main street and see if she could understand what it had meant to be a person in this place, before it was taken away.

She stood up, picked up her equipment, and walked toward what had been the main street, where the larger foundations of the commercial buildings were marked out in the dimming light. The sun touched the western hills and lit them up gold and red. The remaining water caught the light and held it, a small pool of reflected fire in the middle of the drowned town.

Margaret walked toward it, measuring as she went, documenting what had been lost, recording the coordinates and dimensions of the place where she had once been a child and everything had made sense because she hadn't yet understood that sense was temporary, that places could disappear, that the solid ground beneath your feet was more fragile than you could ever imagine when you were eleven years old and the town was still alive around you, still holding you in its small, finite arms.

The darkness came on gradually, and she walked through it, guided by the slope of the land, by her memory of the shapes of the streets, by the deep knowledge in her bones of where she was supposed to be. Behind her, the truck sat in the overlook parking area, waiting. Ahead of her, somewhere in the exposed earth, was the place where her house had been, where her father had taken his measurements, where her mother had packed boxes, where she had ridden her bike for the last time through streets that would never be streets again.

She was going to find it. She had come a long way to map the drowned town, and that is what she would do. But first, she had to remember how to walk its empty streets.
