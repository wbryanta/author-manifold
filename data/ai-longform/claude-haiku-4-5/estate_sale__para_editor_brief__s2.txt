# Excerpt

The first piece they took was the china cabinet, the one that had stood in the corner of the dining room for thirty-seven years. Margaret watched from the kitchen doorway as the two men, brothers maybe, angled it through the frame with the practiced efficiency of people who had done this a thousand times. The glass doors caught the afternoon light, threw prisms across the oriental rug that was already marked with a red dot—sold—for some woman from Columbus Margaret would never meet.

"Careful with that," she heard herself say, though there was no reason to say it, nothing inside that wasn't already catalogued and assigned a price. Her mother's Limoges china had been sold three days ago at auction, the silver went to her cousin in Cincinnati, the good glasses to the Methodist church for their annual fundraiser. The cabinet itself had fetched only ninety dollars. Margaret had not expected it to hurt.

She turned away and went back to the small desk in the corner, the one where her mother had paid bills in the same careful hand for forty-two years, where Margaret herself had done her algebra homework while her mother sat in the chair by the window, reading book club selections that never seemed to make her happy. The desk was Margaret's. She had claimed it early, before the estate sale company came with their clipboards and their digital scales and their ability to reduce a life to line items and lot numbers.

The day had been unseasonably warm for October, nearly seventy degrees, and the front door had been propped open since nine in the morning. The smell of cut grass from the yard next door kept drifting in, along with the sound of footsteps on the porch, the low murmur of the estate sale manager with potential buyers, the occasional scrape and thud of furniture being moved. Margaret had learned to tune out most of it by focusing on the papers before her: her mother's medical bills, a stack of letters held together with a rotted rubber band, receipts for purchases made in 1998.

A woman appeared in the kitchen doorway, holding a lamp.

"Is this item two-forty-seven?" the woman asked. She was Margaret's age, maybe slightly younger, dressed in the uniform of weekend estate sale shoppers—quilted vest, sensible shoes, reading glasses on a beaded chain.

Margaret squinted at the lamp. It was brass, with a pleated cream shade that had yellowed to the color of old teeth. She could not immediately place it in any room of the house, though she must have seen it thousands of times in peripheral vision, one of those objects that ceased to exist the moment you truly registered its presence.

"I think so," Margaret said. "The numbers are on stickers on the bottom of things."

The woman nodded and left, and Margaret returned her attention to the papers, but her concentration was broken now. She found herself listening for the footsteps to fade, listening for the woman to come back and ask something else. The silence when it came felt worse than the noise had been.

By two o'clock, at least half the furniture was gone. The living room had begun to look like a stage set in the final act, full of absence. Margaret had not ventured in there since morning, unwilling to see the rectangles of unfaded wallpaper where pieces had hung, the dust patterns on the hardwood where chair legs had pressed for decades. Instead, she had stationed herself in the kitchen and the hall, moving from room to room in her mother's house like a ghost haunting her own history.

At some point—she had stopped checking the time—the estate sale manager came to find her. His name was Derek, he was very young, probably thirty, with the kind of elaborate beard that seemed to require its own grooming schedule. He held a clipboard.

"We're making good time," he said. "Lot of traffic this afternoon. People love estate sales in the fall, I think. It's like picking through someone's memory without any of the guilt."

Margaret didn't respond to this. She had learned over the past few days that Derek had a way of reading emotions onto situations that weren't really his to read, making observations that were meant to be philosophical but landed instead like a stranger's hand on your shoulder—well-intentioned but fundamentally unwelcome.

"There's a question about lot one-eighteen," Derek continued. "The floor lamp in the bedroom. The woman wants to know if the shade is original. I wasn't sure about that one."

"It is," Margaret said. "My mother bought that shade in 1987. I remember because she was particular about lampshades. It had to match the wallpaper exactly."

Derek made a note. "Great. Thanks. You're being super helpful, by the way. A lot of people get weird about this part. They don't want to stick around, or they get emotional about everything. You've been great."

Margaret nodded and said nothing. The truth was more complicated than being helpful or not being weird. The truth was that she had spent the night before the sale lying in her childhood bed—the bed was already sold, collected by a college student from Athens, Ohio—and had made a deal with herself that she would not cry, not once, not even a little. Not in the house, not around the strangers, not in front of Derek and his clipboard and his observations about people picking through memory. So she kept busy, kept moving, kept her voice even and informative, and this apparently looked like grace.

After Derek left, Margaret went upstairs. It was cooler in the hallway up there, quieter. The morning sun had moved around the house, so different light came through the windows now, showing dust motes and the texture of wallpaper she had stopped seeing years ago. Three doors opened off this hallway: her mother's room at the end, Margaret's childhood room on the left, and a smaller room on the right that had functioned as everything from guest room to sewing room to storage space depending on her mother's changing needs and inclinations.

Margaret's room was nearly empty now. The bed was gone, of course, and the dresser, and the bookshelf. What remained were the things pinned to the walls, the items her mother had never known how to categorize and therefore had left for Margaret to deal with: the birth announcements, the elementary school honor roll certificate, the dried corsage from a prom date Margaret had not thought about in thirty years.

She sat on the hardwood floor—it was cold through her jeans—and looked at these artifacts. The corsage was deep rose, brittle as paper, with a satin ribbon and the name of the florist in Ashtabula embossed on it in gold. The prom date's name was Chris. He had driven a red Mustang and had kissed her in the football field parking lot with more enthusiasm than skill. That was 1988. Margaret had been seventeen years old and had thought that moment would determine the shape of her entire future, that Chris would matter, that the prom itself—the decorations in the gymnasium, the DJ, the way the light hit the mirror ball—would persist in her memory as a threshold. Instead, it had become one more thing the mind stored indiscriminately, pulled up occasionally when triggered by the sight of a corsage pinned to a wall in her mother's house.

Downstairs, there was a sound of something heavy being set down, a man's voice saying, "That's a heavy sucker."

Margaret remained where she was for another few minutes, then stood up carefully, her knees protesting, and went to her mother's room.

The room was also stripped nearly bare. The bed was the most significant loss—a double bed with a cherry wood frame that had been the subject of a minor dispute at the auction house. Margaret's cousin thought it should have fetched more; the estate sale company had explained that twin and double beds were a difficult sell because most people wanted either queens or singles. Her mother had slept alone in that double bed for forty-three years, first by circumstance when Margaret's father had his stroke, then by design after his death. Margaret had sometimes wondered if she found it lonely or comfortable, this expanse of mattress to herself, but had never asked.

The closet door was still open. Inside, a handful of wire hangers remained, and the rod that had held her mother's clothes—sensible dresses, cardigan sweaters, one good winter coat—was now bare. The rod was an old one, wooden, with a slight warp in the middle from decades of weight. Someone had tried to sell it separately, had even put it on the manifesto—lot two-oh-three—but no one had made an offer, so it remained.

Margaret reached up and touched it. The wood was cool and smooth from handling. She remembered her mother standing on a step stool in this very closet, hanging things up, taking things down, the soft clink of hangers against the rod. Not a particularly vivid memory, no specific emotion attached to it, just the fact of it, the record of it stored in her body's muscle memory even though her mind had nearly lost it.

"Excuse me?" A man's voice from downstairs, loud enough to carry. "Is anyone here? I have a question about lot two-thirty-two."

Margaret pulled her hand back, closed the closet door, and went back downstairs.

The man was in the dining room, pointing at one of the remaining pieces—a sideboard, oak, with a mirror attached to the back. It was a substantial piece of furniture, the kind that required coordination and muscle to move.

"Is this supposed to come with the contents of the drawers, or are those sold separately?" the man asked. He was older, with the weathered look of someone who spent time outdoors, and he was studying the sideboard with the intensity of someone making a significant financial commitment.

"Let me check," Margaret said, though she knew the answer. Her mother had been meticulous about these things, had made lists, had gone through the house room by room with the estate sale people, discussing what was included and what wasn't. Margaret had the detailed manifesto memorized by now, had spent the previous two nights reading through it like it was scripture.

"The contents of the drawers are lot two-thirty-four," she said. "They're listed separately. But they go together, if you want them both."

The man nodded, satisfied, and pulled out his phone to check his notes. He had already marked up his catalog with pen, had circled multiple lots, had clearly spent some time thinking about what he wanted. Margaret wondered where he was from, whether he was furnishing a new place or replacing damaged items, or if he just liked old things the way some people liked collecting, the way some people spent their weekends in dead people's houses, accumulating the physical record of lives they would never know.

"You know," the man said, not looking up from his phone, "that's a really nice piece. That sideboard. It's solid oak, good joinery. Somebody took care of it."

"My mother did," Margaret said. "She took care of most things."

The man nodded, still making notes, and Margaret left him there and returned to the kitchen.

The afternoon was beginning to move toward evening. The light had shifted again, was now coming through the west-facing windows at a lower angle, and the kitchen had taken on that particular quality of spaces nearing the end of the day—golden and tired and aware of impending darkness. Margaret made tea, heating water in the kettle her mother had used every morning and every evening of her adult life. The kettle was sold—lot one-fifty-seven—to someone from Columbus, but no one had picked it up yet, and Margaret saw no reason not to use it while it was still here.

While the water heated, she stood at the kitchen window and looked out at the backyard. The grass needed cutting again, though it was late in the season for it. The flower beds were empty now, cleared of the last annuals, and the hostas had begun to brown at the edges. The apple tree in the far corner was still putting out fruit, small apples that would likely fall to the ground and rot if no one collected them. Margaret had always meant to ask her mother what happened to all those apples, but had never gotten around to it, and now the question was permanently removed from the category of things she could answer.

The kettle whistled. Margaret poured water over a tea bag—not her mother's tea, that had been sorted through and most of it donated or thrown away, but a Lipton from Margaret's own pantry in Cleveland—and sat back down at the desk to wait for it to cool.

The papers in front of her had begun to blur together. She had been working her way through the desk drawers in chronological order, starting with the oldest documents and working forward, and she was now into the early 2000s. There was less of interest here than in the earlier years. Mostly bills paid, letters from the utility company, notices from the bank. Her mother had been the kind of woman who kept everything, or had at least convinced herself that she kept everything until the moment of sorting revealed that keeping and organized storage were not the same thing.

But there were fragments. A birthday card Margaret had made in second grade, construction paper and glitter, with a poem about love written in a child's careful capitals. A letter from Margaret's father to her mother, written during a hospital stay, in handwriting that had become shakier and more difficult to read as the lines progressed. News clippings about the local high school soccer team, circled in red pen with notes in the margins. Photographs, though not many—her mother had been more of a non-documenter, had let most moments pass unrecorded.

One photograph was of Margaret herself at about twelve years old, standing in front of this very house, wearing a school uniform of some kind, squinting at the camera with an expression of mild annoyance. Her mother had written on the back, in her precise script: "Margaret, March 1985, Easter picture."

Margaret had almost no memory of this photograph being taken. The uniform was unfamiliar—she had not worn a uniform to her public school. Had they gone to church that day? Had her mother forced her into some borrowed outfit for a Easter portrait? The not-knowing bothered her more than it should have, more than the greater losses of the day had bothered her.

She set the photograph aside and opened the next drawer.

Time passed. The light continued its slow shift across the room. At some point, the estate sale manager came to find her again, to tell her that they would be closing down around six, that most of the serious buyers had already made their selections, that they would process everything that evening and Margaret's belongings could be picked up tomorrow. She had designated three pieces: the small desk, her mother's wedding ring, and a quilted wall hanging that had hung in the hallway for so long Margaret had never quite registered it as separate from the wall itself.

"Is there anything else you want?" Derek asked, and Margaret looked around the kitchen as if she might find the answer written somewhere in the cabinets or the appliances.

"I don't think so," she said, and Derek made a note and left.

Around five-thirty, Margaret went upstairs one final time. The house was quieter now, the day winding down, and she moved through the halls feeling like an archaeologist in her own history. She paused in each room—her childhood room, which was now just four walls and empty hardwood; her mother's room, which had lost its essential purpose; the hallway, which had never had much purpose to begin with. She did not cry. She had made that deal with herself and she was keeping it.

In her mother's room, she sat on the bare floor—at least the carpet was still here, a neutral beige that showed every footprint—and tried to remember what it had felt like to be afraid of her mother. Because she had been afraid, in the way children are afraid of the people who have absolute authority over them. Not terrified, not abused, but afraid of disappointing her, of failing to be the right kind of daughter, of somehow revealing that she was not equal to her mother's expectations. That fear had shaped everything: her grades, her college choice, her first job, even in some measure her marriage and divorce, all of it conducted with some part of her attention tuned to how her mother would react.

And then, at some point, that had stopped mattering. Margaret had become too old, or had moved far enough away, or had simply realized that her mother's approval was not a thing she could reliably earn or lose. That realization had brought freedom, but it had also brought something else—a strange lightness mixed with regret, because the stakes had finally become very low, because her mother had become just a woman living in Ohio, not an oracle whose judgments carried cosmic weight.

She had spent the last decade of her mother's life visiting quarterly, having long telephone conversations, sending cards on birthdays and holidays. She had been, by most measures, a dutiful daughter. But there had also been distance, a careful management of interaction that she had thought was mature and which she now, sitting on her mother's bedroom floor, recognized as a form of protection against something she had never quite named.

The light was beginning to fade. Margaret could hear the estate sale people closing down outside, the sounds of a business concluding, doors being locked, clipboards being gathered. She stood up and brushed off her jeans and went back downstairs.

Derek was in the foyer, checking the final count, and he looked up as she came down the stairs.

"All set?" he asked, and Margaret nodded.

"All set," she confirmed.

"Your desk will be over tomorrow afternoon," Derek said. "And the other items. I'll text you the pickup window once we finish the final count."

Margaret said that was fine, and walked Derek and his crew to their trucks. The October evening was cool now, approaching actual cold, and the sky had turned that particular shade of blue that indicated autumn was moving toward winter. The house behind her was mostly empty, lit from within, and she could see straight through the windows to the walls on the other side. It looked smaller now, diminished, the way buildings always look when you remove everything that gives them dimension and purpose.

She locked the front door and stood for a moment on the porch, key in hand. Tomorrow she would come back to collect her three pieces. Tomorrow she would finalize the paperwork, close out her mother's accounts, contact the utility company about the final meter reading. Within a week, the house would be back on the market, sold to people who would paint over the wallpaper, probably remove the wall-to-wall carpeting, certainly erase every trace of the woman who had lived here and maintained it with such meticulous care.

Margaret turned away from the door and walked to her car. She did not look back at the house, did not allow herself any final gesture of farewell. She simply got in the car, started the engine, and began the drive back to Cleveland, back to her life, which was waiting for her with all the indifference of any other day.

The highway north opened before her, familiar and unremarkable. She would stop somewhere to eat dinner, would arrive home around nine, would go to bed and wake up and resume the business of living. The estate sale would be a story she told once or twice—how strange it had been to watch it happen, how surreal, how she had held up better than expected. And then it would become a thing that had happened to her, stored away with the other experiences that accumulate into the loose aggregate called a life.

At the edge of town, she passed the high school where she had squinted at a camera in an Easter uniform thirty-seven years ago. The football field was lit for a game, the bleachers full of people who had no idea they were sitting in the geography of someone else's memory. Margaret kept driving. The lights faded behind her, and the road ahead filled with darkness, and she drove on into it without hesitation, because there was nothing else to do, and doing it was what people did.
