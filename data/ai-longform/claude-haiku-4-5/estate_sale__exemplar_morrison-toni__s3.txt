# [Begin prose]

The house breathes different now. Not the breath of a living thing but something else—the exhale of a place emptied of its secrets, its particular darkness, the smell of my mother's body that lived in the walls and floorboards and the underside of quilts. Mrs. Chen and her crew had started at eight in the morning, their efficient hands moving through the rooms like water finding its level, and by noon the downstairs was mostly stripped. I stood in the doorway of what had been my mother's bedroom, watching a young man with kind eyes wrap her dresser in blankets the color of old teeth. The mirror, that mirror in which she had arranged her face every morning for forty-seven years, tilted sideways now, and in its reflection I caught myself catching myself—this version of me standing in my dead mother's doorway, that version watching, and underneath both of them another version much younger, much smaller, watching my mother comb her hair.

"Mrs. Garrett?" The young man's voice came from far away. "Is this all going?" He meant the dresser. He meant the mirror. He meant the small glass bottles of Chanel No. 5, her only extravagance, lined up on the doily like soldiers in a very fragrant army. I nodded, though nodding felt like a betrayal so specific I could taste it in my mouth—something metal, something like the pennies she kept in the blue dish.

The truth is I had not wanted to come. My sister Denise had called from Connecticut where she lived in a house bigger than this one, with a husband still living and adult children who visited, and said, "You're the one in Ohio. It makes sense." She had the particular voice of someone who had made a decision about fairness that had nothing to do with kindness. "There's a sale company that handles everything. You just need to be there. Just oversee it." As if I were the kind of person who could oversee anything. As if motherhood had not taught me that I was, in fact, the kind of person things happened to.

But here I was. Here I had always been.

The house sat on Maple Street in Grayson, Ohio, a town where the coal industry had performed its slow, long suicide, and the people, stubborn or unable, had simply stayed, as if leaving would be admitting something about the future none of them could face. My mother had moved here in 1967 with a husband who worked at the mill and a daughter in her belly—that would be me—and though the husband had left seventeen years later for reasons that were both obvious and never discussed, she had stayed. Stayed through the sixties and seventies and eighties when staying meant something. Stayed through the nineties and into the new century until staying was just what she did, like breathing, like waking in the dark and remembering all the things worth remembering and all the things better forgotten.

I had left when I was eighteen. That was the thing about being from a place like Grayson—you had to leave to prove you were smart enough, ambitious enough, good enough for something else. I had proved it. I had become a high school guidance counselor in Columbus, married a man named Paul who sold insurance, had two children who had become adults who did not call often, and now I was forty-eight years old and back in my mother's house, which was no longer my mother's house but some other thing—a collection of items, a sum total of a life that could be divided into lots and sold to people who did not care that my mother had liked that lamp because it cast a kind light on her face, or that she had kept the same kitchen table for thirty years because its familiarity was a form of prayer.

By one o'clock, the living room furniture was gone. The couch where I had done homework, where my mother had watched her stories, where she had died—not there but later, in the hospital, which was better and worse—was being maneuvered through the front door by two men who seemed to be discussing bass fishing. They did not know they were carrying out the location of my first kiss with Terry Morrison, that boy with the troubled eyes who became a troubled man who became a man in prison, though my mother had never said that. She had only said, "That boy is headed somewhere dark. Don't go with him." She was right about that. She was right about many things, and I had spent much of my life not speaking to her as punishment for her rightness.

"Mom?"

I turned. It was Emma, my daughter, walking down the stairs with my mother's jewelry box. She was twenty-four and worked in marketing, and she had driven up from Columbus because I had asked her to, because I could not do this thing alone, though I could not do it with her either. Being with Emma was like being in a room with a version of yourself you had once been—the same sharp jawline, the same way of moving through space that managed to be both graceful and aggressive, as if challenging the air itself to get out of the way.

"Don't open that," I said.

"I wasn't going to." She held it away from her body like it might explode. "Mrs. Chen said it needs to go in the sale. There's a lot of good vintage stuff in here."

Vintage. That was what they called the past now when it could be bought and sold. My mother's tangible history, reduced to a category.

"Let me see it," I said, though I didn't know what I meant by that. See it, hold it, put it back, undo everything?

Emma brought it to me and I opened it. The velvet compartments held the usual things—costume jewelry mostly, nothing valuable, a few pieces from her mother that had real gold, a pair of pearl earrings that Paul had given her, though Paul had left and she had never worn them again. But underneath, in the bottom drawer beneath the pullout tray, was something I had not thought about in thirty years: a photograph. Me and my mother, both young, standing in front of this house. I was maybe six. She was holding my hand. We were both smiling, though we were not the kind of people who smiled easily, so these smiles were precious, rare as good weather, and in looking at them I understood that this had been a moment of accord between us, some temporary alignment of wanting the same thing. I could not remember what that thing was. I could not remember if this photograph had been taken before or after she had hit me for something I had done, or if I had done nothing and she had hit me anyway because her own mother had raised her this way, passing down the particular logic of pain like other families passed down recipes or silver.

"What is that?" Emma asked. She had crouched beside me, and her voice had changed, become smaller.

"Us."

"You look happy."

I put it back in the drawer, underneath the tray, and closed the jewelry box. "People look like a lot of things in photographs. The camera doesn't know what's true."

"That's cynical."

"That's honest. There's a difference." I set the box on the window seat. "Tell Mrs. Chen to put this in the sale. All of it."

"Mom—"

"Don't." I stood up. My knees cracked. My back complained. I was my mother's age now, roughly, or getting close to it, and soon enough I would be older than she had ever lived. This seemed unlikely, like a mathematical impossibility. "Don't do that thing where you feel sorry for me."

Emma looked away. She was good at that—looking away when things got close to true. She had learned it from me, which meant I had learned it from my mother, which meant it came from before that, from a line of women who had learned that distance was safer than connection, that keeping something unspoken kept it from ruining things. But it ruined things anyway. It ruined them slowly, which was sometimes worse.

By three o'clock, the upstairs was mostly done. They had taken the beds, the dressers, the small desk where my mother had paid bills and written the few letters she wrote, mostly to relatives in the South she barely knew. They had taken the curtains that had been red then faded to pink, the rug that had covered a stain I had made with grape juice when I was eight and had never been removed, just covered. The stain was still there, a ghost-mark on the floorboards, and I found myself standing over it, staring down at proof that I had lived here, that something had happened, that there was a record, however small.

"Ms. Garrett?" It was Mrs. Chen herself now, a small efficient woman with gray hair and reading glasses on a chain. She had the kind of face that made you want to tell her things, though I would not. "We're pretty much done. Just some boxes in the basement, some items the family wanted to keep or donate. What's your preference?"

What's your preference. As if I knew. As if preference were something I had the right to.

"Donate the clothes. Anything with memories my daughter and I want to keep, we'll handle." This was a lie. I wanted to keep nothing. I wanted to take the gasoline and the match and burn it all, or I wanted to pack it carefully and keep every piece, or I wanted something in between that I could not name.

Mrs. Chen nodded. She had not been surprised by any emotion or lack thereof. She moved through this business—clearing out the lives of dead people—with a kind of grace that suggested she understood something about necessity, about how the body eventually leaves and the things remain and someone has to deal with the things. "There's a vanity in the basement. Real nice. Probably worth something. We could move it but the owner—" she meant me, "—might want to look at it first. Unmarked. Could be something nice."

"My mother didn't have anything nice in the basement."

"You'd be surprised what people keep down there."

So we went—me and Emma and Mrs. Chen—down the narrow wooden stairs, ducking under the low ceiling, into the damp smell of earth and concrete that had been the basement my entire life. It was darker than I remembered, or maybe I just remembered it darker than it was, the way memory works, pulling everything toward a center of darkness or light depending on what the heart needs to believe.

And there it was. A vanity, yes, but more than that—my mother's mother's vanity, or maybe her mother's mother's. A beautiful piece, mahogany, with a tri-fold mirror, the kind of mirror that let you see yourself from all angles, all sides, all the ways you could be wrong. There was dust on top and water damage on one leg, but the shape was undeniable. The grace was in the bones.

"Now that's something," Mrs. Chen said softly.

I touched the mirror and my hand came away dusty. In the reflection, I looked like something that might be found in a basement—unfinished, damaged, kept too long in the dark. I looked like my mother. I looked like her mother. I looked like a line of women extending backward into time, each one of them young once, angry, afraid, trying to figure out how to be alive in a body and a world that gave you very few good options.

"We'll keep it," I heard myself say. "My daughter and I will figure it out."

"You sure?" Mrs. Chen had the tone of someone who was used to people changing their minds, though I wouldn't. I had changed my mind about many things, but once a thing was decided in this certain way—with this particular feeling—I kept it. This was stubbornness, or it was strength. My mother would have called it stubbornness. My daughter might have called it strength. The truth was probably somewhere in the middle, which was where most truths lived, uncomfortable in their in-betweenness.

We carried it upstairs together—it took all three of us—and set it in the living room, which was now so empty that even the vanity couldn't quite fill the space. It sat there looking haunted, or noble, depending on how you looked at it. Emma ran her fingers along the top and I let her, though I wanted to tell her not to, wanted to keep something separate, but that was the sickness too—that division, that fear of connection, that making of boundaries.

"It's beautiful," Emma said.

"It needs work."

"Yes. But look at what it could be."

I looked at her looking at the mirror, at the way her face appeared in the clouded glass, slightly distorted, slightly younger, slightly more beautiful than she probably felt herself to be. This was the gift of old mirrors—they improved things, or they seemed to, which was the same as improvement if you needed to believe hard enough.

"My mother had one like this," I said. "Her mother, I mean. I remember her sitting in front of it in the dark. Just sitting there, looking at herself. I never knew what she was doing. Whether she liked what she saw or whether she was—" I stopped.

"Whether she was what?" Emma asked.

"Trying to recognize herself. Trying to know if she was real."

The house was completely quiet now. The sale crew had packed up and left, and the house had returned to being a house, just a structure of wood and nails, emptied of its contents but not its history. The sun was moving toward evening, that golden hour when everything looks like something it's not, like it might be beautiful or tragic or both at once. I could hear the neighbor's dog, could hear cars on Maple Street, could hear the ordinary sounds of a town going on without my mother in it, which seemed impossible and normal at once.

"What do we do now?" Emma asked.

I thought about the house, about whether we would sell it or keep it or let it sit empty until it fell down. I thought about my mother, about all the years she had stayed here, about whether she had been happy or resigned or some undefinable state that I had probably been too young to understand. I thought about the vanity downstairs, about what it meant to keep something that required you to look at yourself, really look, in all your mirrors at once.

"We go home," I said. "But first, we're going to sit on the porch for a while. Your grandmother used to do that. She used to sit there and watch the light change. I forgot that until just now."

So we did. We sat on the porch as the sun moved toward the trees at the edge of town, as the light turned from gold to orange to something deeper, and we didn't talk. We just sat there, two women separated by one generation and connected by something deeper than love—by the carrying forward of all the unsaid things, all the things that lived in the body and the basement and the mirrors that showed you what you looked like when you finally stood still long enough to see.
