# Estate Sale

Margaret stood in the doorway of her childhood home watching a man in a Carhartt jacket carry the wingback chair through the narrow entrance, angling it just so as the movers had done hundreds of times before with other people's memories. The chair was the exact color of burgundy her mother had chosen in 1987, a decision Margaret remembered because she'd been home from college that summer, working at the public library, and her mother had asked her opinion before buying the fabric. Margaret had said it was fine, practical, wouldn't show dirt, which was the kind of thing a twenty-year-old said when asked about interior design. Now she watched the chair disappear into the bed of a pickup truck bearing the logo of Goodwill Industries.

The estate sale company had orchestrated everything with the efficiency of a military operation. They'd arrived yesterday with clipboards and plastic tags, categorizing the house into lots: furniture, kitchenware, linens, books, decorative items, and miscellaneous. Margaret had signed paperwork indicating that she wanted the company to handle everything, to sell what they could, donate the rest, and cut her a check for the remainder. She'd told them she would be present for the first few hours of the sale this Saturday to ensure nothing irreplaceable walked out the door, though she wasn't entirely sure what qualified as irreplaceable anymore.

Her mother had died on a Tuesday in early March, fourteen months ago, and the house had sat mostly untouched since then. Margaret had come for the funeral, had spent two weeks sorting through documents and meeting with lawyers, had identified which bills would need to be transferred and which could be cancelled. She'd gone through her mother's closet and taken a few things—a cashmere cardigan, a silk scarf with a paisley pattern—but had been paralyzed by the rest. Touching those garments felt like a violation of some sort, a premature disposal of her mother's corporeal presence. So she'd closed the door and returned to Boston, to her job as an editor at a mid-sized publishing house, to her apartment in a brownstone in Somerville, to her life as a woman without a mother.

The estate sale company had convinced her that now was the time. Spring was the best season for sales, they'd said. The weather would bring people out, families with children looking for affordable furniture, young couples furnishing their first homes. Don't wait until summer, they'd advised, when people's attention turns to vacations and yard work. Don't wait until you're emotionally ready, their subtext implied, because you never will be.

Margaret had agreed, and now here she was at two o'clock on a Saturday afternoon, watching strangers pick through her history.

A woman in her forties emerged from the house carrying the small wooden table that had held the telephone in the hallway for as long as Margaret could remember. The table had a single drawer where her mother kept a small notepad and a pen attached to a string. Margaret had called home from college using that notepad to jot down her messages—what time to call back, who had phoned, was everything okay. She watched the woman load it carefully onto a sedan.

"Nice piece," the woman said to Margaret as she passed. "Are you the daughter?"

"I am," Margaret said.

"Your mother had good taste. My living room has been beige for three years, and I'm finally ready to add some color. This table will be perfect."

Margaret nodded, unsure what response was required. She wanted to tell the woman that the table's real purpose had nothing to do with color or design, that its true value lay in the hundreds of times she'd stood in the hallway reading messages written in her mother's careful cursive. But that wasn't the kind of thing one said to a stranger, and besides, the woman had already turned back to her car.

The day was unseasonably warm. Margaret wore a light jacket that she'd since unzipped, and the sun sat high and bright in a nearly cloudless sky. Inside the house, it was cooler, shadowed. She'd walked through the rooms this morning, noting what remained. The kitchen was mostly empty now—the refrigerator would go later, the estate company had said, along with a few other large appliances. The living room had been stripped of its major furniture, leaving only the bookshelf, which would probably remain unsold until the very end of the afternoon, when the company would price it down to fifty dollars or even give it away.

Margaret's childhood bedroom was upstairs, along with her mother's room and the small guest bedroom where her father had slept for the last five years of his life, after their marriage had crumbled into something that resembled more a practical arrangement than a partnership. Neither her father nor her mother had believed in divorce—or rather, they'd believed it was something that happened to other people, weaker people, people without sufficient resolve. So they'd maintained separate bedrooms, had taken separate vacations, had lived like cordial acquaintances in the same house until he died of a stroke at sixty-eight and released her mother into a kind of lonely freedom that seemed to distress her more than comfort her.

Margaret had been twenty-nine when her father died. She'd come home for the funeral and had stayed two weeks to help her mother go through his belongings, choosing which items to keep, which to donate, which to throw away. Her mother had been practical about the process. She'd kept his wedding ring, a few photographs, his leather-bound journal from his military service, his wool overcoat. The rest had gone into bags and boxes. Looking back now, Margaret realized that her mother had been training for this process, had understood with some deep instinctive knowledge that one day Margaret would stand in this house watching it all be removed by strangers, and that she should model a kind of efficient resignation.

A young couple emerged from the house carrying the dining room chairs—a set of six with spindle backs and woven rush seats, purchased, Margaret believed, sometime in the 1990s. The man and woman worked together seamlessly, having apparently done this before, moving in synchronized rhythm toward their truck. They were perhaps thirty, Margaret guessed, childless, still in that phase of life where acquiring other people's used furniture was an adventure rather than a necessity.

"Those chairs are really great," the woman said to Margaret as they passed. "How much were they marked?"

"I'm not sure," Margaret said. "The company handles the pricing."

"Well, they're a steal." The woman smiled at Margaret, warm and genuine, as though Margaret should feel pleased that her mother's belongings were being appreciated. And Margaret supposed she should. Her mother would have approved of the chairs going to people who would use them, who would sit around a table and eat meals together, who would perhaps someday tell a story about where they'd found such wonderful pieces.

By three-thirty, the house had begun to develop the hollow, echoing quality of a building no longer occupied. Voices carried differently through empty rooms. Footsteps sounded louder on the hardwood floors. The windows, which had always framed the neighborhood in a particular way, now seemed to look out at the world with a kind of blankness, as though the house no longer had opinions about what lay beyond its walls.

Margaret retreated to the kitchen, where she sat at the island counter—the island itself having been sold and scheduled for removal later—and waited. She'd brought a book but hadn't opened it. Instead, she found herself staring at the space above the stove where her mother's recipe cards had been pinned to a small corkboard. The cards were gone too, of course, sold in a lot with other kitchen miscellany. Margaret realized she'd never photographed them, never transcribed any of the recipes, never considered that one day they would be distributed among strangers or discarded entirely.

She tried to remember which recipes her mother had written out. There was definitely one for pot roast, which she'd made nearly every Sunday in winter. There was something with chicken and mushrooms, something Margaret had eaten hundreds of times but which she'd never learned to make herself, assuming it would always be available to her, that her mother would always be available to her. There had been a note in her mother's hand on one card—Margaret couldn't quite recall which recipe—that had said, in parentheses, "Margaret loves this." She'd seen it once or twice, had been touched by it, and then had forgotten about it until now, until it was too late to do anything but think about it.

Around four o'clock, the traffic slowed. The evening crowd was beginning to trickle in—people who'd seen the sale advertised and had wandered over after work or dinner. They moved through the house more slowly than the morning shoppers, lingering over items, trying things out, testing drawers and checking for damage. They didn't carry things out with the purpose of the earlier customers. Instead, they browsed, they considered, they often put things down and moved on.

Margaret remained in the kitchen, increasingly unnecessary. The sale would continue until evening, but the bulk of the transaction was complete. She felt oddly displaced, as though she'd been waiting for something that had now concluded without her quite noticing when it had begun.

The Realtor had told her the house would need cleaning and minor repairs before it could be listed. The estate sale company would handle the final removal of anything unsold by Sunday evening. After that, it would be empty—not just of furniture, but of the particular weight of lived experience that no amount of renovation could entirely erase. Or perhaps it would be erased. Perhaps empty rooms were simply empty rooms, and the things that happened in them only mattered as long as someone was there to remember them.

Margaret stood and walked through the house to its center, the living room where they'd gathered on holidays, where she'd opened Christmas presents and where her father had watched television every evening, his chair positioned at precisely the right angle to see the screen and the window. That chair had sold in the first hour, purchased by a man who'd sat in it, bounced slightly to test its springs, and then nodded with satisfaction.

The doorbell rang, a sound Margaret had heard thousands of times, and she realized that soon enough, it wouldn't be her doorbell anymore. She opened the door to find a woman about her own age, perhaps slightly younger, standing on the threshold.

"Hi there," the woman said. "I'm not sure if you're the seller, but I was hoping to ask about the china cabinet that was advertised? I saw it this morning but had to come back with my husband, and now I can't find it."

Margaret almost smiled. "I think that one was sold early. But let me check with the people running the sale. Come in."

She walked the woman to the back of the house where one of the employees was tracking sales and taking payment. Yes, the china cabinet had been purchased at around noon. The woman's face fell slightly—Margaret could see how much she'd wanted it, how she'd constructed in her mind the place where it would go, what it would hold, how it would look in her home.

"I'm sorry," Margaret said, though she wasn't sure why she was apologizing, since she had nothing to do with the sale anymore, had voluntarily removed herself from any decision-making capacity.

"It's okay," the woman said. "I'll keep looking. There are lots of good pieces here. But I really did have my heart set on that cabinet. I have this china that was my grandmother's, and I've been keeping it in boxes for years..."

The woman trailed off, and Margaret understood. Everyone who came to an estate sale was trying to preserve something, to honor some previous generation by keeping their objects in circulation, ensuring that what had been loved once would be loved again.

"If you tell me where you live, I can contact you if another cabinet comes up," the woman from the sale company offered, and Margaret watched as her mother's absence was catalogued and filed away, converted into consumer information.

By five o'clock, Margaret had returned to the kitchen and was scrolling through her phone, checking emails from work, reading the news, performing the small maintenance tasks that kept her life in motion when she was in her apartment in Boston. Here in her mother's house, these tasks felt particularly futile, particularly hollow.

She heard the doorbell again, and then another customer asking about a mirror that had been sold. Then a man looking for the Chanel No. 5 perfume that had been listed, a bottle that had been his wife's favorite and that she'd worn for years. Margaret directed him to the sale personnel, watched as his hope diminished when he learned it had already sold, watched as he left the house without buying anything.

Near six o'clock, Margaret decided she should leave. The sale would continue for another couple of hours, but her presence was not contributing anything of value. She wrote a check to the estate sale company for the balance of the proceeds and arranged a time for Monday to meet with the cleaning crew. The Realtor would be by on Tuesday to take photographs and prepare the listing.

Before leaving, Margaret walked through the house one final time. The bones of the house remained—the hardwood floors, the plaster walls, the fixtures that couldn't be easily removed. But everything that her mother had chosen to place within those bones was either gone or going. The imprint of her life remained visible only in absence.

Margaret paused in the doorway to her parents' bedroom, the room she'd never been entirely comfortable entering. Her mother had slept here alone for decades, this queen-sized bed with its matching nightstands and its cream-colored duvet. A framed photograph had sat on the dresser—Margaret's parents on their wedding day, 1956, her mother in white, her father in uniform, both of them smiling with a kind of determined happiness that Margaret had always found slightly unsettling.

The bed was still here. The estate company had deemed it unsellable, its age and condition placing it below the threshold of commercial viability. The photograph was also still here, but in a box, having been separated from its frame and placed in a lot with other framed pictures from around the house.

Margaret picked up the photograph and held it to the light. She'd never looked at it closely before, had always glanced past it on her way through her mother's room. Now she considered her parents as young people, as people filled with hopes and expectations, as people who'd had no idea how their marriage would deteriorate, how they'd come to inhabit the same house as strangers, how their daughter would someday stand in their bedroom and feel more connection to them through this image than through any direct memory.

She placed the photograph back in the box, though she knew it would probably end up in a donation center or in a landfill. She considered taking it, claiming it as hers, but something about that felt dishonest. Her mother had chosen not to frame it again after it had come down, had allowed it to be boxed with dozens of other photographs, had tacitly agreed to let it go. Margaret should respect that choice.

In the hallway, she paused before her own childhood bedroom, which had been stripped of its furniture days ago, even before the estate sale company had arrived. A woman from Margaret's yoga class had wanted the daybed—single, suitable for a guest room—and had come by to pick it up. The dresser had been sold at the estate sale, along with the small bookshelf and the desk where Margaret had done her homework, written letters to college friends, composed the email where she'd told her mother she was staying in Boston instead of moving home after graduation.

The bedroom was now completely bare, empty of everything except for the marks on the carpet where furniture had stood for decades and the faint shadows on the walls where posters had once hung. Margaret had spent eighteen years in this room before she left, and she would probably never spend another night here. The room would be shown to prospective buyers, would be described in the Realtor's listing as "charming bedroom with original hardwood floors," and would eventually be someone else's childhood room, or guest room, or office. It would be repainted, recarpeted, refinished into something new.

Margaret turned and walked downstairs and out the front door into the warm April evening. The sun was beginning to set, casting the neighborhood in that particular golden light that made everything appear nostalgic, even things happening in the present moment. She stood on the front porch for a moment, looking at the house, trying to fix in her memory the way it looked now, knowing that by next Saturday it would be significantly different, and that within a few months it would belong to someone else entirely.

She got in her car and drove to the hotel where she'd booked a room for the night. Tomorrow morning she would drive back to Boston, would return to her job and her apartment and her life as it currently existed. The house would be cleaned, listed, and eventually sold to people who would make it their own, who would paint over her mother's color choices and install new fixtures and create their own memories within those walls.

But tonight, Margaret sat in a hotel room and thought about the recipe cards, about the wingback chair, about the china cabinet that a woman had wanted for her grandmother's china. She thought about the particular way dust motes had moved through the afternoon light in the living room, and about the sound the front door had made when she'd closed it for what might be the last time. She thought about the equation that her mother had somehow understood decades ago: that all objects eventually become someone else's, that all homes eventually become other people's homes, that the only thing that truly remained of a life was the memory of it, which was fragile and fading and would itself not survive very much longer than the person who held it.

By the time Margaret fell asleep, she'd already forgotten what most of the recipe cards had said.
