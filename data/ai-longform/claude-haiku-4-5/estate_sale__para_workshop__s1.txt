# The Inventory

The first thing to go was the davenport, which Martha had never called anything but the sofa. Two men with straps and grunt-language maneuvered it through the front door at 9:47 AM—she'd been watching the clock—and she stood in the archway between the living room and foyer, trying to feel something about it. Trying and not finding much.

Her mother had bought that sofa in 1973. Martha remembered the fabric sample her mother had brought home, a geometric orange and brown thing that would have looked ordinary anywhere else but which had seemed wildly modern in their living room, almost dangerous in its commitment to the decade. Over fifty years it had faded to the color of old apricots, the pattern worn invisible by proximity, by television-watching, by the weight of their bodies accumulating Sunday afternoons.

"Where's this go?" one of the men called from the driveway.

Martha consulted her clipboard. The estate sale company had assigned lots, each item catalogued and tagged with a fluorescent sticker. Lot 47, the sofa. She flipped through her packet.

"It sold," she said. "It goes to the buyer."

The men didn't acknowledge this. They were already loading it onto a flatbed. She had never thought about the logistics of possession, how easily objects transition from one life to another. The sofa had absorbed fifty years of her mother's habits and accidents—a coffee stain on the left cushion from the winter Margaret had broken her collarbone and couldn't balance a mug, the small burn mark from her father's pipe before he quit smoking, the permanent depression in the middle where her mother had sat each evening to watch the news—and now it would be new again in someone else's house. The stains would become quirks of the thing's history rather than evidence of a specific life lived.

She had arrived at 7 AM, before the sale company opened up the house. She'd spent that two hours walking through the rooms, trying to do inventory in her head before the official inventory took over. The Depression glass collection in the kitchen cabinet. The quilts in the upstairs closet, folded with tissue paper, some of them sewn by her grandmother whose hands Martha had never actually seen work but whose presence lingered in the precision of the stitches. The books on the shelves—her mother's book club selections, mostly, the kind of contemporary fiction with watercolor covers that Martha's brother Frank called "mom books" with a tone meant to be affectionate but that always felt vaguely disdainful.

Martha had picked up one of the books at random. A Thousand Acres. She opened it to find her mother's marginalia in the pages, neat underlining in pencil, a few checkmarks in the margins where something had resonated. Martha had never known her mother was a reader who marked up books. She'd never really known many things about her mother at all, she was realizing.

By 10 AM, the house had been hollowed considerably. The davenport was gone, the wingback chairs were gone, the coffee table with its embedded glass top showing the landscape of Chester Harding prints was gone. The front room looked like a dental office now, all empty planes and light from the south-facing windows. Martha had moved through this room ten thousand times, she thought. Ten thousand ordinary afternoons and mornings, crossing from one side to the other without seeing it. Now the room was visible. Now that there was nothing in it, she could finally see its dimensions.

She heard the bell on the front door and turned to see a woman about her own age coming in, clutching the sale company's printed catalog. The woman had that tight-lipped expression of someone at an auction, that tension in the face that Martha associated with wanting something badly.

"I'm looking for Lot 84," the woman said, not quite making eye contact. "The china cabinet? Mahogany, approximately eighty-five inches?"

Martha consulted her clipboard, though she didn't really need to. She'd memorized the list. "That's in the dining room. It hasn't been moved yet. The buyers are coming at two PM."

"Can I look at it?"

"Of course."

Martha led her through the kitchen, past the cabinets that were already being emptied by another pair of men, whose movements had a practiced efficiency that made her feel lonely. They clearly did this every day. To them it was just work. To her it was her mother being disassembled.

The china cabinet stood against the far wall of the dining room. It was mahogany, was genuine, was the kind of piece that had made sense in the 1980s when her mother's second marriage had provided money and a certain idea of what taste looked like. The woman opened the glass doors and reached inside, running her fingers along the rim of one of the Limoges plates. Martha's mother's wedding china, displayed but never used. The woman looked at Martha with something like sympathy.

"Beautiful," the woman said.

"Thank you," Martha said, as if she'd made it.

By noon, Martha had ordered lunch from the sub shop on Main Street—a habit from her childhood, that specific sandwich shop that still existed, an anchor point in the decaying downtown. She sat in the kitchen eating the Italian Cold Cut Special and watching the men in her mother's dining room catalog and wrap china. One of them—his name was Derek, it said on his shirt—wrapped each piece in brown paper with a practiced origami of corners and folds. He'd wrap a plate, set it aside, pick up another. Wrap, set, pick up. His hands moved without his brain having to think about them.

"How long have you done this?" she asked.

"Sales? Seven years."

"Do you ever know what happens to the things after?"

He looked up. "No. I don't really think about it."

She nodded. This seemed honest, and she appreciated it.

By one PM, the house was substantially empty. The bedroom furniture had gone in a truck of its own, a matched set from perhaps twenty years ago, solid and cheerless. The bookcases had been cleared and removed. The kitchen table where her mother had eaten breakfast every morning of her seventy-nine years was loaded onto a truck and driven away. Martha watched it go from the kitchen window, that small rectangular table with four chairs, one of which was slightly wobbly because Martha's father had never quite got around to fixing the leg.

She should have fixed that, she thought. She should have visited more and noticed that table and fixed the wobbly leg at some point in the last forty-eight years of her life.

A car pulled into the driveway at 1:47 PM. Martha watched from the front window as a couple got out—he in khakis and a polo shirt, she in yoga pants and a fleece vest—and looked up at her mother's house with the specific expression of people about to invest their money in something. The man was carrying the sale company's catalog.

This was the buyer of the mahogany cabinet.

Martha let them in. She showed them to the dining room where their cabinet still stood, the only piece of furniture remaining in that echoing space. The woman touched the wood grain, opened and closed the doors, checked the hinges. The man got down on the floor to look at the bottom, examining feet and joins with the seriousness of someone assessing a car.

"We'll take it," the woman said finally.

Martha walked them back to the door. "It's a beautiful piece," she said.

"It is," the woman agreed. "We found it on the listing at three in the morning. We couldn't sleep wondering if someone would buy it first."

Martha imagined these people at three in the morning, scrolling through her mother's life on their phones, both of them awake with the same desire. She imagined them discussing it, prioritizing it, deciding that this piece—which her mother had bought on the recommendation of a friend, which her mother had carefully dusted every Saturday morning, which had held china that no one had ever eaten from—was worth the drive to Ohio, worth the trailer, worth the effort of loading and unloading.

She tried to feel something about this transfer of custody, but mostly what she felt was tired.

By three PM, only the furniture that hadn't sold remained. Her mother's bedroom dresser, which apparently had water damage on the back that the photos hadn't shown well enough. A china hutch from the fifties, walnut, with a fussy scalloped trim that had gone out of fashion. A divan from the guest room that had been priced at $300 but evidently was too narrow, or the color was wrong, or the era was just wrong. The estate sale company had told her that certain pieces simply wouldn't move, and she should be prepared to donate them.

She found herself sitting on that unsold divan, which still smelled like the room it had come from. The guest room where she'd last slept perhaps five years ago, when she'd come home for Christmas. The divan was uncomfortable, as divans always are. She remembered being twelve and lying on a similar divan—this divan?—in her grandmother's house, bored and angry in the way of twelve-year-olds, waiting for something to happen.

Her phone buzzed. A text from Frank, her brother, who lived in California and had not come to Ohio for the estate sale.

"How's it going?" the text said.

She stared at the question for a long time. How was it going? The house was being emptied. The furniture was being transferred to other people's homes. The objects that had accumulated over eighty years of her mother's life were being distributed like cards in a game none of them had agreed to play.

She typed back: "Fine."

Frank sent back a thumbs-up emoji.

Martha found herself getting angry, suddenly, at the emoji. Its cheerfulness felt obscene. She wanted to text him something sharper, something that would convey the weight of what was happening, but she didn't have the energy to write it. Instead she put her phone away.

The house began to get dark. The sales company had said they'd come back tomorrow morning to get the remaining pieces and transport them to a donation center. Martha had keys to lock up. She'd arranged to stay at the Holiday Inn, which was only slightly less depressing than staying in her mother's house would be. She thought about leaving now, just leaving the door unlocked and driving away, letting whatever happened next happen without her.

Instead, she stood and began walking through the rooms.

The living room was a cave now, all shadow. She could barely see the patterns in the carpet where the furniture had sat, the rectangles of relative cleanliness where the world hadn't faded the fabric. In five years, those ghost-shapes would disappear and the carpet would be uniform again. And then in another five years, someone else would pull that carpet out and put something new down, and this particular pattern, this particular evidence, would be gone entirely.

She climbed the stairs. The upstairs hallway was still lined with photographs—her parents on their wedding day, her father's face transformed by its former youth, her mother's face already settling into the set of her personality that Martha had always known. Pictures of Martha and Frank as children, school photos in frames that had been popular in their particular era. Pictures of Martha's wedding. No pictures of Martha's divorce, which had occurred fifteen years ago and which her mother had treated with a kind of polite silence that had somehow been worse than any judgment would have been.

The master bedroom was almost completely empty now. The mattress was still there, stripped down to its ticking. The bedroom window looked out at the neighbors' house across the street, the Hendersons, who had lived there when Martha was growing up, though it was definitely not the Hendersons anymore. Nothing stayed the same. She knew this intellectually but the reality of it, standing in her mother's empty bedroom, felt newly impossible.

She went back downstairs and stood in the kitchen. The appliances were still there—the refrigerator, the stove, the dishwasher—but the sale company was coming for those tomorrow too. The new owners of the house would presumably want to replace them anyway. The kitchen would become new. It would belong to people who didn't know that Martha had learned to make pie crust at this counter, that her mother had stood right there, by the sink, at six in the morning every day of her life, looking out at the backyard while she waited for the coffee to brew.

Martha opened the refrigerator. It was empty except for a jar of pickles and a few condiments her mother hadn't used in months or years. She closed it. The refrigerator hummed.

She sat at the kitchen table—no, the table was gone. She sat on one of the chairs, which the sale company had left stacked in the corner. She was aware, with a strange clarity, that she was experiencing what people meant when they talked about loss. She'd felt sad when her mother died. She'd felt sad at the funeral. But sadness wasn't loss. Loss was this—this disappearance of the concrete world her mother had inhabited. Her mother had been gone for six weeks now, but the house had still been full of her, the rooms still arranged around her preferences and habits. Now the house was being erased of her. In a week, there would be almost no evidence that Margaret Sullivan had ever lived here at all.

She thought of the woman at three in the morning, scrolling through pictures of the mahogany cabinet, unable to sleep with wanting it. She thought about that cabinet sitting in a new dining room, gradually accumulating new dust, new associations. Someone would eventually get married in that room, or celebrate a birthday, or have a fight, and the cabinet would be there, holding whatever china they chose to put in it, slowly becoming part of someone else's memory and life.

This was how life worked. This was the only way life could work. Things passed through. People passed through. You were here, you assembled your objects and your habits and your particular way of looking out at the world, and then you left, and someone else's world moved in to replace you, the way water fills in the space left by a hand.

Her mother had died of a stroke on a Tuesday. She'd been at her book club. She'd been discussing a novel about a woman who'd left her family and reinvented herself. Her mother had had opinions about whether the woman's choices were brave or selfish. Then, her mother had stood up to refill her coffee cup and had gone down, and she'd been in the hospital for four days, and then she'd been gone.

Frank had called Martha at work. Martha had left work and driven to Ohio and had sat in the hospital room with her mother's body, which was still technically alive but was no longer inhabited by her mother anymore. It was like someone had vacated the building, leaving only the structure behind.

In the hospital, Martha had thought about what to say to Frank. They'd sat with their mother's hand between them—or rather, they'd sat on opposite sides of her mother, not quite looking at each other—and Martha had wanted to say something about their mother, some crystallizing thing that would capture who she had been. But nothing had come. Her mother was already becoming a set of memories rather than a person, a collection of stories and characteristics rather than someone actual and present. And Martha couldn't figure out which stories to keep, which characteristics to emphasize, what the essential thing was that should be preserved.

The house was dark now. Martha stood and turned on the light in the kitchen, then turned it off again. She didn't want to see the light come on. She didn't want confirmation that the electricity still worked, that the house was still functional, that it would keep going perfectly well without her or her mother in it.

She locked the front door. She set the alarm code that the real estate agent had given her. She sat in her car in the driveway for a moment, looking back at the house, which looked like any other house on the street now, like a place with no particular story, no particular haunting.

She started the drive to the Holiday Inn. The town looked smaller than it had when she was growing up, or else she was seeing it with different eyes, the eyes of someone who no longer belonged here, if she ever had. The downtown was hollowed out like the house, most of the storefronts empty or converted into antique stores, those repositories of other people's abandoned goods.

The Holiday Inn was three miles out on Route 23, a chain hotel that had probably been built in the nineties. Her room was on the second floor and looked out at a parking lot and, beyond that, a strip mall. She lay on the bed without undressing and scrolled through her phone, looking at nothing in particular.

She found the estate sale listing and looked at the photographs of her mother's house as it had been before the sale—full, familiar, inhabited. She scrolled through the images slowly. Here was the living room as it used to be. Here was the dining room with the china cabinet. Here was her mother's bedroom with its quilted spread and its nightstands with lamps that Martha remembered from childhood. Here was the kitchen. In one of the photos, the morning sun came through the window and illuminated the dust particles in the air, and Martha stared at that image, trying to see her mother's ghost in the dust, in the light, in the ordinary tableau of a room that had been lived in.

She could think of nothing to do but sleep, so she slept.

The next morning, she met the donation people at the house. They arrived in a truck and efficiently loaded the items that hadn't sold. The dresser with water damage. The walnut hutch. The uncomfortable divan. Within two hours, the house was empty. Completely, utterly empty.

Martha stood in the center of the living room—the center of what used to be the living room, the center of the coordinates where the sofa had been, where her mother had sat and watched the news for fifty years—and felt the emptiness like a physical pressure.

She had to return the keys to the real estate agent. She had to drive back to Massachusetts. She had to return to her life, which hadn't been interrupted, really, which had been continuing along its normal trajectory while her mother's life had simply ended and been erased.

She locked the front door one last time. She left the keys in the lockbox that hung from the door handle, as instructed. She looked at the house one more time, seeing it now as a potential future, seeing it as it would be when someone else's furniture moved in, when someone else's life began to inhabit it.

A moving truck pulled into the driveway as she was putting her suitcase in her car. The new owners, probably, starting the process of reimagining the space according to their own needs and desires. Martha watched them for a moment—they were younger than her, a couple with a small child who was immediately running up the front steps—and then she got into her car and drove away.

She thought of the woman looking at the mahogany cabinet at three in the morning. She thought about standing in the empty rooms and the particular silence of a house from which all the objects have been removed. She thought about her mother's hands, which had touched every item that had been in that house, which had arranged and rearranged, chosen and maintained, loved and cleaned and cared for over a lifetime of days.

Those hands had been still for six weeks now. Those hands would never touch anything again. But the things they had touched would keep moving through the world, would keep getting reassembled in new spaces, would keep accumulating new stories, new meanings, new ways of being inhabited.

It was late afternoon when Martha reached the highway. The sun was low and brilliant, turning the landscape into something biblical and strange. She merged into traffic, joined the stream of cars all moving in their own directions, carrying their own burdens and their own solitary griefs into whatever future awaited them.

She did not look back at Ohio, though she did think about it—the house sitting empty before being filled, the rooms waiting to be known again, the dust settling on surfaces that her mother would never touch and that she, Martha, would never have occasion to see again. One life ending. Another life beginning. The inexorable transfer of all things from one state to another, one person to the next, until everyone was gone and everything was new and nothing remained but the stories people told about what had been.
