# 

The morning light came up copper-colored, filtered through smoke that had drifted overnight from the fires burning three valleys east. Margaret stood on the catwalk with her coffee cooling in an enamel cup, watching the ridge line materialize like a photograph developing in chemistry. The Cascades at this distance looked like a stage set—purple and blue and impossibly geometric against the pale sky.

She'd been at the tower for eighteen summers, and this was to be the last. The Forest Service had decided five years ago that satellite imagery and camera networks made the human presence obsolete, but bureaucracy moved slowly enough that they'd grandfathered her in, allowed her to finish out the season she'd started. This summer felt consequential in a way the others hadn't, each day weighted with finality. After August, she would descend from this tower for the last time and return to the world of continuous ground, of roads and neighbors and the kind of loneliness that came from proximity rather than distance.

She'd chosen fire watch precisely because of the isolation. A natural introvert elevated to near-hermit status by circumstance—or was it choice? The distinction blurred after years of living two hundred feet above the nearest dwelling, speaking to dispatch maybe twice a week during fire season, to herself the rest of the time. The loneliness had felt earned, purposeful, nothing like the loneliness that had driven her up here in the first place.

The radio crackled with the morning check-in from Crow's Peak, another tower two ridges over. She responded, logged the time and conditions, made notes about the smoke layer moving west. The equipment was old, analog, made to last. Not like the modern world with its planned obsolescence and constant updates. She'd heard rumors that they were replacing everything with automated systems, but from up here, the replacements seemed obscene somehow—all those cameras and sensors gathering their data into some distant server, no human consciousness required. No one to actually *witness* anything.

By midmorning, the smoke had thickened. Margaret could no longer see the valley floor where Highway 2 wound through the river drainage. She scanned the ridge systematically, a habit of decades, her eyes moving left to right, near to far, noting every plume and haze. Most was drift smoke from the major fires. She could track those with the radio, knew which ones were being attended to, which ones were expected to grow. But there was something else, closer, something that didn't match the trajectory of the known fires.

She picked up the binoculars and found it. A thin column rising from the northern drainage, the creek valley where the National Forest gave way to private land. Where Rachel had built her cabin twenty-three years ago, long before the estrangement had calcified into something permanent.

Margaret's hands steadied the binoculars, but her breath quickened. She watched the column, trying to assess its size, its urgency. Could be a campfire carelessly left. Could be a debris burn that had gotten away from someone. Could be the cabin itself, though she didn't allow herself to entertain that thought for more than a second. She moved to the radio and called it in.

"Dispatch, this is Lookout Seven-Seven, possible smoke in the North Fork drainage, approximately four miles west-northwest of station, bearing two-eighty-five. Size estimate small to moderate, appears to be low-intensity burn."

The dispatcher's voice came back calm and measured. "Copy, Seven-Seven. Stand by."

She waited, binoculars still raised, watching the column pulse and shift with the wind. The smoke was definitely lower-intensity, not the angry billowing of a structure fire, more the steady thread of a burn that had established itself. That was either good or not good, depending on what was burning.

"Seven-Seven, we're getting reports from two other sources on that location. Appears to be a deliberate burn on private property in the North Fork. We'll monitor from here. Thanks for the callout."

Margaret lowered the binoculars. Private property. Deliberate burn. The cabin was private property.

She could see the column clearly from the windows of the glass room. It had the character of a controlled burn, not chaotic, not spreading. Someone was burning something intentionally, and the most likely candidate was Rachel, who had mentioned in a letter—one of exactly three letters they'd exchanged in the past decade—something about clearing dead timber from the property. That had been in March. It was now mid-August. If Rachel was burning clearing, she was doing it.

Margaret made notes in the log. The radio remained quiet on the matter. She was cleared to go about her routine, but her routine felt impossible now, fractured by the presence of that column. She tried to read—she had a stack of library books that came in weekly supply drops—but the words wouldn't cohere into meaning. She tried to work on the inventory of equipment that was due at the end of the month, walking from station to station inside the tower, running her hands over the compass roses and the graduated circles, the brass instruments that had guided hundreds of human decisions about where fires were, how fast they were moving, whether homes needed to be evacuated.

By noon, the column had grown slightly. Margaret called it in again and received the same message: private property, deliberate burn, under observation. She ate lunch mechanically—a sandwich made from yesterday's bread and cheese that had been getting soft—and found herself back at the binoculars.

The thing about fire watch was that it taught you patience and impatience simultaneously. You learned to wait, to observe, to let things develop at their own pace. But you also learned that some things developed catastrophically, that the difference between a situation that resolved itself and one that became a disaster was sometimes measured in minutes. She'd seen fires triple in size in the span of two hours. She'd watched the color of smoke change from white to gray to black as a situation transformed from manageable to critical.

This column wasn't doing that. It remained stable, almost placid. It was a burn. It was controlled. It was fine.

But Rachel was there. Rachel was in the cabin, or near it, or probably near it, conducting a burn at a time when the valley was filled with smoke and the humidity was low and the fire danger was officially marked as high.

Margaret picked up the radio handset, then set it down. What would she say? She wasn't even sure if Rachel still lived at the cabin full-time. The letters had been spare, informational, devoid of the kind of detail that would have answered basic questions about whether they still spoke at all—and they didn't, except in these infrequent written communications that read more like legal documents than correspondence between sisters.

The rift had started over something small, as rifts often do. A comment Rachel had made about Margaret's decision to take the tower job, something about running away, about throwing away her education and potential. Margaret had responded that at least she wasn't wasting her life performing for a family that didn't actually see her. Rachel had said some things. Margaret had said worse things. Their parents had tried to mediate and had made it infinitely worse. And then the parents had died—their mother first, then their father three years later—and by that point, the estrangement had become so established that even the funeral hadn't been enough to bridge it. They had spent two days in the same house, used the same bathroom, and never actually looked at each other.

After that, letters came. Quarterly at first, then less often. Legal matters regarding the estate. Holiday cards. A brief note from Rachel when Margaret turned forty. Nothing warm. Nothing that acknowledged the broken thing between them.

At two o'clock, Margaret did something she hadn't done in eight years. She called the Forest Service office in Concrete and asked to speak to Dave Morrison, the supervisor who oversaw her tower.

"Margaret," he said, picking up immediately. "Everything okay up there?"

"Fine," she said. "Everything's fine. I was calling about something else. The deliberate burn we reported in North Fork this morning?"

"Yeah, I saw your callout. Why, is it doing something it shouldn't?"

"No, no. It's controlled. It's fine. I was just wondering—do we have any contact information for the property owner? In case we needed to verify something about the burn?"

There was a pause. Dave wasn't stupid. "Is there something you're concerned about?"

"No. Just routine. I like to know who's operating in my watch area."

"We can pull the county records if you really need them, but Margaret, dispatch is satisfied with the situation. You don't need to—"

"I'm not asking you to do anything. Just curious. Never mind."

She hung up before he could respond. Outside, the column still rose, steady and patient.

By four o'clock, Margaret made a decision. It wasn't a rational decision, and she knew that. It was a decision made by the part of her that had never fully accepted the estrangement, that had kept Rachel's cabin marked on her detailed maps of the region, that occasionally looked up information online during the rare times she went to town. It was a decision made by the part of her that had written and deleted a hundred letters over the years.

She packed a small bag: water, sunscreen, the first aid kit she was required to keep at the station. She left a detailed note about the burn location and her plan to descend early to verify the situation, knowing this was against protocol but also knowing that Dave would forgive her before he would forgive her for staying in the tower and ignoring what might be a problem. The road down would take her past the North Fork access.

The descent was eight miles, mostly downhill as promised, but it twisted and climbed occasionally, giving the elevation back in small chunks. Margaret hadn't walked it in over a year—the supply helicopter brought most of what she needed, and her trips to town came only quarterly. Her legs remembered the path even as her body protested. She moved quickly, driven by something she couldn't quite name. Not urgency, exactly. Not fear. Something more like a compulsion, the gravity of unfinished business pulling at her.

The smoke grew denser as she lost elevation. She could smell it now, not the acrid smoke of wild fire but something greener, the smell of wood being slowly consumed. She found the turnoff to the North Fork Road, a barely maintained track that wound down toward private land.

The cabin came into view around three o'clock, built into a small clearing with the forest pressed close around it. It was more weathered than Margaret remembered, the paint faded to a grayish-blue. But it was standing, and there—about a hundred yards behind it—she could see the burn area, a controlled heap of timber and brush with a small fire burning at its center.

And near the fire, moving back and forth with a shovel, was Rachel.

She was older, of course. Margaret had expected that, had counted years on fingers. But seeing it was different from anticipating it. Rachel's hair had gone mostly gray, and she moved with a careful deliberateness that suggested some kind of injury or pain. She wore work clothes—old jeans, a faded shirt—and she was focused intently on her task, pushing dirt and ash around the perimeter of the burn.

Margaret stood at the edge of the clearing, suddenly unsure. This wasn't a emergency, wasn't a crisis that required her intervention. Rachel was managing her burn perfectly well, alone and competent. There was no reason to announce herself, to break whatever routine Rachel had established over the past two decades of living here without her sister.

She was about to leave, to head back up the mountain, when Rachel looked up.

For a long moment, they simply regarded each other across the distance. Rachel lowered the shovel. She didn't say anything. Margaret couldn't read her expression at this distance, couldn't tell if she was glad or angry or something in between.

Margaret walked toward her.

It took several minutes to cross the clearing, and in that time, Margaret rehearsed and discarded a hundred openings. None of them felt right. When she reached the edge of the burn area, she simply said, "I saw the smoke from the tower."

Rachel nodded. "I know. I radioed it in myself before I started. Wanted to make sure nobody thought it was a wild fire."

Her voice was the same—lower and rougher than Margaret's, with the particular cadence of someone who didn't speak much to other people. Margaret almost smiled at that recognition.

"It's a good burn," Margaret said. "Controlled. You're doing it right."

"Been doing burns here for years," Rachel said. "You don't learn much living alone, but fire you learn fast." She paused, looked at Margaret directly for the first time. "Why did you come down?"

"I wanted to make sure you were okay."

"I'm obviously okay."

"I know. But I wanted to see."

Rachel set down the shovel and used the back of her arm to wipe ash from her forehead. Behind her, the fire burned steadily, releasing smoke that rose toward the thickening gray sky. "The tower's being decommissioned this year, isn't it?"

"At the end of summer."

"What'll you do?"

Margaret hadn't prepared an answer to this. She'd been avoiding the question herself for months. "I don't know yet. Find a job in town, probably. Figure out how to live with other people again."

"That'll be rough," Rachel said, and there was something in her tone—not quite sympathy, but recognition. The acknowledgment that certain kinds of isolation become difficult to break, once you've lived inside them long enough.

Margaret watched the fire. "You could help. If you wanted. With the transition. We could figure out—" She stopped, aware that she was falling into the old pattern of suggesting solutions to problems that weren't hers to solve.

"I couldn't come back to town," Rachel said quietly. "Not now. I'm settled here. The solitude—it suits me."

"I know it does." Margaret did know that. She'd understood it before, during their estrangement, but she understood it better now. Some people were made for isolation. Not because they couldn't connect, but because the world of constant connection felt like a kind of violence. The tower had been Margaret's refuge. The cabin had been Rachel's.

They stood in silence, watching the burn. The smoke had stopped rising and was now more of a drift, breaking apart in the wind. Rachel would finish this fire today, let it cool down, scatter the ash. By tomorrow, there would be nothing to show that anything had happened here at all.

"When you leave the tower," Rachel said, "you should come stay here for a while. Before you figure out anything else. You could help with the property. There's work. And you'd have time to think."

Margaret felt something shift in her chest, a weight lifting slightly. "How long?"

"As long as you need."

It wasn't a reconciliation. Margaret understood that. It was something smaller and possibly more sustainable—a temporary truce, a shared understanding that they were both people shaped by solitude, people for whom the ordinary world moved too quickly and expected too much. They would probably never repair what had broken between them. But they might find a way to exist in proximity again, to share space the way the tower and the cabin shared the same forest, separate but contained within the same landscape.

"Okay," Margaret said. "I'll come."

Rachel picked up the shovel again and gestured toward the fire. "Help me finish this, then. We'll get it squared away before dark."

Margaret took the shovel when Rachel offered it, felt the weight of it in her hands—familiar weight, honest work. The fire was nearly out already, just coals glowing in the ash. She worked beside her sister, not speaking, just moving in a careful rhythm. The smoke drifted around them, particulate and temporary. Above, the sky was beginning to clear slightly at the edges, the greater fires burning out toward the horizon, the smaller ones being addressed. The balance of the forest holding, for now.

By the time they finished, the sun was low. Margaret looked up at the ridge where her tower stood, invisible from here but present in her mind, a specific point in the landscape that had contained her life. In a few weeks, she would come down from that tower for the last time. The building would be secured, the equipment transferred or abandoned. Her tenure would become a historical footnote, if anyone bothered to remember it at all.

But that was ahead of her. Tonight she would help Rachel prepare the cabin for another person's presence. They would eat dinner together, not talking much, but together. In the morning, Margaret would climb back up the mountain to finish her summer, and the knowledge that there was a place to come when the summer ended would change something. Not everything. Not what was broken. But enough.

The fire died to embers and ash. The sisters stood watching it cool, the heat gradually releasing itself into the cooling air. Above them, the smoke that had filled the sky all day was beginning to dissipate, revealing the first stars at the edges of the darkening blue. Tomorrow there might be more fires, more smoke, more evidence of the world in flux. But tonight, there was this: the end of one fire, the presence of another person, the smallest possible bridge across a distance that had seemed, until very recently, impossible to cross.
