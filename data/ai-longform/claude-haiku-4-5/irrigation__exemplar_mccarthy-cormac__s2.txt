# Irrigation

They came out before the heat settled on the valley and walked along the west line where the water had not run in three weeks. The older brother carried a spade and the younger brother carried nothing and they did not speak. The morning was still cool and the grass along the irrigation ditch was brown and withered and dust rose from their boots in small clouds that did not dissipate but lingered in the still air like some visible measure of the land's abandonment.

The orchard beyond the ditch was a geometry of suffering. The peach trees stood in their rows with leaves curled inward as if in some private agony and the fruit that hung among them was small and hard and unsuitable for sale. They had planted these trees when their father was still living and he had stood among them on a morning much like this one and said that the land would provide if a man tended it properly. He was a man of few words but the words he spoke carried the weight of revelation. That was twenty-three years ago.

The older brother stopped and pointed with the spade at a section of the main line where the earth had subsided. There it is, he said.

The younger brother squatted and studied the break. The concrete had split in a jagged seam and the exposed interior was white and crumbling. He ran his hand along the fracture.

How long do you reckon that's been going on?

Three weeks. Maybe four.

Why didn't you tell me?

I'm telling you now.

The younger brother stood and looked back toward the house. From here you could not see it. There was only the orchard and the line of cottonwoods along the far boundary and beyond that the mountains that enclosed the valley like the walls of some ancient ruin.

Mama knows?

Yeah. I told her yesterday.

What'd she say?

She said we should get it fixed.

They walked the line for another hundred yards and found two more breaks and a section where the joint had separated and the water—what little there was—seeped into the dry ground in a dark stain that was already beginning to gray and disappear. The older brother made marks with a stick in the soil and said he would need to rent a trenching machine from town. The younger brother did not ask how much it would cost.

At noon they drove into town in a truck that had not been painted since their father died. The cab smelled of oil and dust and something organic that had begun to decay under the seat. They parked in front of the equipment rental place and the older brother went inside. The younger brother sat in the truck and watched the traffic on the main street. A few pickup trucks. A couple of cars. A woman carrying groceries across the intersection. Nothing had changed here in years. The stores on either side of the street had the same faded signs and the same windows, and he wondered if the people who passed through this town every day even saw it anymore or if it had become invisible to them the way his own hands had become invisible to him except when he looked at them.

The older brother came back with the rental agent, a man with a weathered face and a bandage on his hand. They discussed the terms and the older brother wrote a check and they shook hands and the agent said he would have the machine delivered first thing tomorrow morning. He asked what had happened to the line and the older brother said the concrete had failed and the agent nodded as if this was the most natural thing in the world, as if all things failed eventually and there was no point in being surprised by it.

They did not speak on the drive back to the orchard. The truck rattled over the county road and the younger brother looked out at the fields they passed. Some of them were still being worked. In one, a man on a tractor was turning the soil and the dust rose behind him in a great plume. In another, rows of lettuce stretched to the horizon with no one to tend them. These fields belonged to the Hendersons and the Hendersons had sold them twice already, first to a corporation that lasted five years and then to a holding company from the city whose name no one knew.

When they reached the orchard the older brother parked the truck and they walked to the house. Their mother was sitting on the porch with a glass of water and a book she was not reading. She asked if they had made arrangements and the older brother said they had and that the machine would arrive tomorrow. She nodded and turned the page of her book though her eyes did not move across it.

The younger brother said he was going back to the city tomorrow.

The older brother looked at him.

There's a job interview on Thursday. I told you about it.

You didn't tell me you were leaving tomorrow.

I'm telling you now.

Their mother closed her book and set it on the arm of the chair. How long will you be gone? she said.

A week. Maybe longer. Depends on how things go.

The older brother went into the house and closed the door behind him. The younger brother could hear him moving in the kitchen, the cupboards opening and closing, the sound of water running from the tap. Their mother looked out toward the orchard and she did not say anything. She had learned long ago that there was nothing to be said about things that could not be changed.

That evening they sat at the dinner table and their mother had made a roast and they ate it without much appetite. The older brother talked about the work that needed to be done once the machine was delivered. He would need to excavate the broken sections and pour new concrete and there was reinforcement to be laid and the whole job would take at least a week, maybe more if something else failed. The younger brother listened and ate his food and did not speak.

After dinner the older brother followed the younger brother out onto the porch. The sun was setting and the mountains had turned a deep purple and the sky above them was a color that had no name, something between blue and black and something else entirely.

You're not coming back, are you? the older brother said.

I don't know yet.

That means no.

It means I don't know.

The older brother leaned against the post and looked out at the orchard. The trees were black shapes now, indistinct from one another, merged into a single dark mass.

This job interview. What kind of job?

It's management. At a logistics company. It's the kind of thing I'm good at.

More money?

Better hours. Health insurance. The usual things.

You'd hate it.

I might. But at least I'd be doing something.

This isn't something?

You know what I mean.

The older brother did know what he meant. He had heard variations of this argument for the past three years, ever since the younger brother had moved to the city to escape exactly this kind of work, exactly this kind of life. He had gone to school and gotten educated and now he could not understand why anyone would choose to stay in a place where the land did not provide and the work was endless and the future held only the prospect of selling what their father had built.

If you take that job you'll never come back, the older brother said. Not for anything that matters. You'll come back for the funeral, maybe. Mom's funeral or mine. But nothing else.

The younger brother did not answer.

Someone needs to stay. Someone needs to decide what happens to this place.

Decide? It's already decided. We're going to sell it. You know we are. It's just a matter of time.

We're not selling it.

We can't keep it. There's no money. The trees don't produce. The water line fails every season. You work twenty hours a day just to break even. This isn't a life, man. It's not even close.

The older brother was quiet for a long time. When he spoke his voice was different, as if he was choosing his words with great care.

I remember when Dad took me out to the eastern boundary, he said. I was maybe eight or nine years old. We walked the whole perimeter and he showed me every corner and every tree. It took us all day. At the end of it he said that this land was ours, that it came with an obligation. He said that obligation meant we had to understand it, every inch of it, every aspect of its character and its needs. And that if we understood it properly, if we really knew it, then the question of what to do with it would answer itself.

That's poetry, the younger brother said. That's not actual advice.

No. But it's true.

It's not true. It's just something he said. Dad was dying when he said it. People say things when they're dying that don't mean anything.

The older brother turned to look at his brother and there was something in his expression that the younger brother had not seen before, something like pity or maybe forgiveness for an offense that had not yet been committed.

You're right, the older brother said. He probably didn't know what he was talking about. He was just a man who grew up in a place and stayed in that place and did the work that needed to be done. He probably believed in a bunch of things that don't make sense anymore. So forget what he said.

The younger brother wanted to argue further but something in him would not allow it. They stood in silence and watched the last light drain from the sky and the stars appear one by one, faint at first and then brighter, until the entire vault of the heavens was filled with them, and you could see how vast the darkness was and how small the two brothers were beneath it.

The machine arrived at dawn and the older brother signed for it and paid the driver and then he and two men from the rental company spent the morning digging out the broken sections of pipe. The younger brother had already packed his bag and he stood and watched the work from the fence line. The ground was hard and it took longer than expected. By noon they had excavated the first break and the older brother was examining the surrounding soil, turning it over with his hands, testing its composition.

He looked at his brother across the distance.

You should stay another day, he said. Help me with the pouring.

I can't. I have to get back.

The older brother nodded and turned back to the work. The younger brother went into the house and said goodbye to their mother and she embraced him with a fierceness that surprised him, as if she was holding onto something that was already gone. Then he drove down the county road toward the city and he did not look back at the orchard.

Three weeks later the older brother finished the repairs and the water began to run again. It moved through the new sections of pipe with a sound like distant speech and when it reached the main line and began to flow out through the secondary channels and into the ditches, the older brother stood and watched it and felt something like relief. The trees would get water for another season. There might be fruit. There might be something to harvest. It was not much but it was not nothing.

He did not hear from his brother for two months. Then a letter came. The job had not worked out. The younger brother had taken it anyway and lasted six weeks before quitting. Now he was doing contract work, short term projects, nothing permanent. The letter did not say whether he would come back. It did not say anything about the orchard or the land or their father's words or any of the things that mattered. It was as if their entire life before the city had been erased from his memory, or perhaps preserved in some compartment that he no longer opened.

That autumn the older brother hired a crew to help with the harvest and for three weeks the orchard was alive with activity. The men moved through the trees with ladders and baskets and the fruit came down and was packed and shipped and sold for barely enough to cover the costs. But the trees had produced. The water had flowed. The obligation had been met, at least for another season.

In the spring of the following year the older brother was working in the eastern section when he came upon a tree that had died. It stood among the others like a monument to failure, its branches bare and brittle. He marked it with a piece of cloth and made a note to remove it. But he did not remove it. Instead he left it standing and years later when people asked him about it he had no explanation. It was just a tree that had died. It was just something that happened. The land was full of such things. The land was full of failure and persistence in equal measure and it did not distinguish between them. It did not care whether you believed in it or understood it or had made some ancient promise to it. It simply continued, indifferent and eternal, waiting to see what you would do next.
