# 

The switchboard at Mountain Bell occupied the third floor of a building in downtown Denver that smelled perpetually of floor wax and electrical heat. Margot had worked there for six years, since she was twenty-three, and she knew the voice patterns of her city like a musician knows a instrument's particular frequencies and susceptibilities. She knew which salesmen called their girlfriends at lunch, which wives rang their husbands to relay bad news, which lonely men dialed the weather service just to hear someone speak. She could identify a caller's mood from the shape of their hello.

Tuesday evenings at six o'clock were reserved for a particular connection that had become, without her ever having quite decided it would be, something she anticipated. A man with a careful, educated voice would call from a number in Cherry Creek—a nice neighborhood, the sort where the houses sat back from the street and the lawns stayed green through August—requesting long distance to a woman in Omaha. Omaha was only four hundred miles away, hardly a long haul, but the calls came with that kind of deliberation that suggested distance mattered less than the fact of it being distance at all. Margot would plug in the cord, make the connection, and listen to the brief moment before the woman answered when the man took what sounded like a breath that had been waiting all week to be released.

"Hello, Margaret," he would say, and his voice would smooth out like something that had been creased all week, finally being unfolded.

The woman's voice was younger, or it carried the quality of youth even if it wasn't—something bright and aerating. "David, I thought you wouldn't call. The lines have been impossible."

This was partly her imagination, Margot knew. She shouldn't be listening so carefully, shouldn't be holding the connection open a breath longer than necessary, shouldn't know already what the arc of their conversation would be. There were professional standards. Her supervisor, Mrs. Chen, had eyes like a hawk and a way of appearing when you least expected her, clipboard in hand, noting violations. But Mrs. Chen was not at her station now; it was six o'clock on a Tuesday, and Mrs. Chen had gone home to her husband and three children in Lakewood. Margot was alone in the switchboard room save for Gerald, who worked the Denver-to-Cheyenne lines and kept his eyes fixed on his own board with the kind of intensity that suggested he was either dedicated or oblivious. She hoped it was the latter.

"The weather's been turning," David would say to Margaret in Omaha. "I saw my first real cold snap this morning."

"October," Margaret would say. "It creeps up on you."

These were the moments Margot lived for in a way she couldn't quite explain to herself. The inconsequence of what they said, the total lack of information being conveyed, made their conversation feel like a very specific kind of poetry. It was the equivalent of the space between notes rather than the notes themselves that mattered. These two people, whoever they were to each other, were marking time together across two hundred miles of copper wire and transformers and her own hands making the connection.

She had given them a history, of course. She couldn't help it. The man—David—had the voice of someone who had been trained in how to speak, which meant either education or performance, and probably both. He taught something, she thought. Philosophy or literature, something that required him to sit in a room with other people and make words mean things. The woman had a laugh that she deployed rarely but always at precisely the right moment, which suggested intelligence and deliberation. She was careful with David, which meant she cared about him deeply or not at all, and Margot believed it was deeply. The way she listened—and Margot could hear listening in the silence—was the listening of someone who had loved someone for a long time.

Were they married? Margot didn't think so. Married people called each other on Tuesday nights with such deliberation usually meant something else. They were lovers, probably. This was 1968, and such things were happening more openly, or at least in a different register than they had before. Denver was still conservative, still held its values close like a worn coin, but even here things were changing. Margot had seen them change in small ways—the types of conversations, the timbre of desperation that came through certain calls. People were rearranging their lives along new lines.

The calls lasted usually between seven and nine minutes. David would talk about something he'd read or a movie he'd seen. Margaret would tell him about her work—she worked in an office, something administrative that clearly bored her, though she bore it with good humor. They would exchange small observances that seemed to be a code for things they couldn't say directly over a long-distance line, even if that line was being monitored. "The roses did well this year," David might say, and Margaret would respond with "That's good. I'm glad they persisted." These were exchanges layered with meaning Margot could only guess at.

Once, Gerald had stepped away from his station for a break, and Margot had found herself speaking before she could stop herself.

"Still there?" David asked Margaret, and Margaret had laughed—that rare, careful laugh—and said, "I'm still here."

"Always?" he'd asked, and the word had hung in the wire between Denver and Omaha like something fragile.

"Always," Margaret had said.

Margot had felt something shift in her chest then, some small recognition of what these conversations really were. Not words being exchanged but promises being renewed. The ritual mattered because the ritual was what held it together.

She had created a small notebook where she marked each Tuesday. She told herself this was a record-keeping exercise, a habit from her filing-clerk days before she became an operator. But in truth, she was building a document of something, a proof of its existence. The calls had been regular for at least four years that she knew of. David had been calling Margaret every Tuesday at six o'clock with the precision of a man whose life outside these calls was orderly enough to maintain this chaos inside it. She wondered sometimes if they had planned it this way deliberately—the ritual, the time, the regularity. Perhaps it was the only way they could sustain it, by making it immutable.

She had a name for them in her mind: the Tuesday Pair. No one else would have understood if she'd told them, but she often thought about them during the hours when the switchboard was quiet. What did they do on Tuesdays at five-fifty-five, in those five minutes before David placed the call? Did Margaret sit by the phone and wait? Did David close his office door or go into a private room? What was the gesture that preceded this weekly permission?

She had also, inevitably, created a narrative about them. In her version, they had met years ago, perhaps at college. David had gone to California afterward for a job—or maybe Margaret had gone to Omaha, and he had stayed here in Denver, or come here later. There had been a decision made at some point that they couldn't be together, probably involving other people or other obligations. But they couldn't bear to lose each other entirely, and so they had created this architecture—these Tuesday calls, this careful distance maintained by telephone wire, this way of existing in each other's lives without disrupting the lives they had agreed to live in the real world.

It was a good story, and Margot added to it slowly over the months. David had mentioned in one call that he'd finally finished some book that had been giving him trouble, and she imagined him teaching at some small college in Denver, living in a house that was too large for him, with a wife who was kind but distracted. Margaret had mentioned her mother's illness once, had mentioned driving up to Nebraska to see her, and Margot imagined a dutiful daughter who had stayed close to home, who had married someone stable and adequate, who had a life that was pleasant and hollow in ways she'd learned to accept.

The Tuesdays were where they lived, Margot thought. The rest of the time was obligation.

She couldn't say why she became so invested in maintaining the connection. It wasn't professional, certainly. She should have been disturbed by her own impulse to listen, to wait, to mark the calendar. She should have reported it to Mrs. Chen or at least to herself as a kind of concerning behavior. But the work of being a long-distance operator was mostly invisible to the people who used it—the hands moving the plugs were rarely thought of as hands at all, just the efficient apparatus that made connection possible. To be invisible while witnessing something intimate gave Margot a strange kind of power. She was the reason David and Margaret could speak. She was the infrastructure that held their desire in place.

And then, one Tuesday in early November, the call didn't come.

Margot waited until six-fifteen, unusual enough that she found herself glancing at the clock multiple times, as though the time might be wrong, as though her reading of it might be somehow false. At six-twenty, Gerald looked over at her. "You looking for something?" he asked.

"No," she said quickly. "Just checking the time."

By six-thirty, she had accepted that he wasn't going to call. She told herself it was nothing. People missed calls. Life intervened. Perhaps David had been delayed at work or in traffic. Perhaps there had been an emergency. She reminded herself that she had no claim on this, that these calls were not her property, that her attachment to them was entirely invented.

But she couldn't quite believe it. Four years of regularity, and then, nothing.

She marked the date in her notebook with a question mark.

The following Tuesday came with a weight to it that she hadn't anticipated. Margot found herself arriving at the switchboard ten minutes early, straightening her headset, checking the connections as though they might have come loose since last week. She was aware of her own anxiety and irritated by it. She was a grown woman of twenty-nine with a life of her own—or if she didn't quite have that, at least the illusion of one. She lived in a studio apartment in an unfashionable part of town, she went to the movies on Friday nights, she had a colleague named Patricia who she sometimes had lunch with, and she had her mother to visit on Sundays.

At six o'clock precisely, the light appeared. Her hand moved to the jack before she even conscious of the decision to move it.

"Long distance for Denver," a male voice said, and her heart did something complicated in her chest. But it wasn't David. It was a different man, younger-sounding, and he was requesting long distance to a number in Kansas City.

She made the connection with movements that felt slightly strange to her own body, as though she were operating from a distance. By six-fifteen, she was certain. David wasn't going to call. Something had changed.

The week that followed was strange in a way she couldn't explain to anyone. Patricia noticed she was quiet at lunch and asked if something was wrong, and Margot made an excuse about sleep. Mrs. Chen appeared at her station at one point and reviewed her call log, which was perfect, as always. But Margot felt like she was operating under water, like there was a density to the air that hadn't been there before.

She wondered, with the particular fervor of the sleepless, what had happened. She constructed scenarios: Margaret had called him and told him they couldn't do this anymore, that it was wrong to continue, that she was going to confess to her husband or leave him or simply demand that David stop calling. David had been transferred to a new job, overseas, somewhere that made these Tuesday calls impossible. Or more darkly, something had happened to one of them. An accident. An illness.

By the time the following Tuesday came around, Margot was not expecting the call to come.

But at exactly six o'clock, the light appeared. A familiar number. The same Denver exchange. She connected it through with hands that were trembling slightly, which was unprofessional and she knew it.

"Long distance to Omaha," David said, and his voice was wrong. She knew his voice the way she knew the sound of her own breathing, and this was not it. The modulation was gone. The careful construction of his speech was gone. What was left was something raw and stripped down to its essential mechanism.

"Connecting you now," she said, her own voice coming out smaller than she'd expected.

Margaret answered on the first ring, as though she had been standing by the phone, waiting. "David?"

"Hello," he said, and the word seemed to contain multitudes of things he couldn't say.

"I was so worried," Margaret said. "I thought—when you didn't call last week, I thought maybe—"

"I know," David said. "I couldn't. I'm sorry. I wanted to but I couldn't."

Margot knew she should disconnect then, or at least release her attention. This was beyond the bounds of what she should be witnessing. But she found herself unable to move.

"Are you all right?" Margaret asked, and her voice had changed too. The careful brightness was gone, replaced by something more worried, more naked.

"She knows," David said. "Margaret, she knows. I don't know how, but she knows about you. She found something in my car, or someone told her, or she just—she was always too smart. She always knew."

There was silence then, a long stretch of it that Margot could hear the weight of.

"What did she say?" Margaret asked.

"That I have to stop," David said. "That if I don't stop, she'll leave. That she can't—she can't bear to know it's happening anymore. She said it was one thing when she could pretend, but this, knowing for certain, is something else."

Margot felt something crack in her chest, some small thing that she'd been maintaining without realizing she was maintaining it.

"So you're not going to call," Margaret said. It wasn't a question.

"I had to call once more," David said. "I had to tell you why it was stopping. I couldn't just disappear."

"And after this?" Margaret asked.

"After this, I need to stay away," David said. "I need to try to fix what I've broken. Or at least I need to try."

Margot's hand was steady on the board, but the rest of her felt like it was dissolving. She understood, in that moment, something she had been able to avoid understanding before. That these conversations, which she had thought of as a kind of preservation of feeling, were actually destructive. That David and Margaret were not sustaining something beautiful and impossible—they were sustaining something that required deception, and the deception, maintained week after week, had eventually become insupportable to someone else, someone who had a claim on David that superseded Margaret's claim, or at least that's what David had decided.

"I love you," Margaret said, and the statement was so simple and so final that it seemed to contain everything.

"I love you too," David said, and Margot could hear in his voice that he meant it and that it was precisely that meaning which was breaking him in half.

"Goodbye, David," Margaret said.

"Goodbye," he said, and then he hung up.

Margot sat for a moment with her hand still on the jack, the line still open. Margaret was still there, she could feel her, waiting to hear something else, but there was nothing else to hear. After a long moment, Margaret hung up too.

Margot carefully, precisely, disconnected the line.

She pulled off her headset and stood, telling Gerald she was taking her break. He nodded without looking up from his board. She walked to the bathroom on her floor and locked herself in one of the stalls and sat down on the toilet and let her breathing become uneven.

She thought about David making the call, knowing it would be the last one. She thought about him taking that breath and asking for the number, making sure the connection was right, hearing Margaret's voice one final time and then saying goodbye to it in a way that sounded like permanence. She thought about Margaret standing by the phone afterward, knowing that Tuesday at six o'clock would come again next week and would be empty, and the week after that would be empty, and next year would be empty, and she would have to begin the process of forgetting the ritual, unlearning what it meant to wait.

She had been waiting too, Margot realized. Not for David or Margaret, but for the pattern to continue. As though by witnessing it, by being the one who created the connection, she could somehow benefit from its magic. But there was no magic. There was just a man and a woman reaching toward each other across impossible distance and terrain, and eventually, the weight of that reaching became too much for someone else to bear, and it had to stop.

When Margot returned to the switchboard, she pulled out her notebook and looked at the page where she had marked every Tuesday call. She had documentation of their affection going back years, a record of something that had now ended. She looked at the final two entries—the blank Tuesday where the call hadn't come, and then the last one, the goodbye she had witnessed.

She closed the notebook carefully and placed it in her bag.

The rest of her shift passed without incident. She made dozens of connections, routed calls to California and Texas and Oklahoma, her hands moving with practiced efficiency. Her voice remained calm and professional through each interaction. No one would have known that something had ended inside her, quietly and without fanfare.

At the end of the night, as she was leaving the building, she thought about what would happen next. Margaret would survive her loss, Margot thought, because that was what people did. They survived. David would either repair his marriage or he wouldn't, but he would live with the choice he had made either way. And Margot herself would continue to go to work on Tuesday nights at six o'clock, would listen for calls that would never come, would gradually stop expecting them and then stop listening for them and then forget entirely that she had ever been waiting.

But standing in the cool October evening, waiting for her bus, she allowed herself one last image: David and Margaret in the last moment before their goodbye, connected through her switchboard, her hands maintaining the circuit that allowed them to exist in the same space across all that distance. It had been real enough to hold them together for four years. Perhaps that was enough.

She got on the bus when it came, took a seat by the window, and watched the lights of Denver pass by.
