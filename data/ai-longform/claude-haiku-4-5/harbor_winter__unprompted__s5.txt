# The Color of Closing

The morning air smelled like rust and salt, the particular combination that meant winter in Crescent Bay. Margaret held her grandfather's rough hand as they descended the wooden dock, their breath clouding in front of them like small ghosts being born and vanishing in quick succession. The water below was the color of old pennies today, which Grandpa Joe said meant something, though she couldn't yet remember what.

"Pay attention," he said, though he said it kindly. Everything he said was kind, even when it sounded like instructions. He stopped halfway down the dock and pointed to the surface. "What do you see?"

Margaret was nine years old and had been making this walk with her grandfather for exactly six weeks, ever since her mother had dropped her off at his house with a suitcase and promises that felt like lies. She'd been afraid at first, afraid of his silences and the way he seemed to be looking at something beyond whatever room he was standing in. But slowly, incrementally, like the tide coming in so gradual you couldn't mark the exact moment the wet sand became water, she'd stopped being afraid.

"Ripples," she said.

"Good. What kind of ripples?"

She narrowed her eyes. The water shifted and reformed under the gray sky, each tiny wave catching what little light existed and throwing it back toward them in broken pieces. She'd learned not to answer too quickly, not to guess. Her grandfather had the patience of someone who'd spent a lifetime waiting for things—for tides, for fish, for boats to arrive or depart—and he seemed to want to teach her the same virtue.

"They're going that way," she said, pointing northeast, toward the cannery with its skeletal framework and the three fishing boats that remained in the harbor, down from twenty-five just two years ago. "But they're small."

"Small current, small wind," Joe said, nodding. He was tall even in his sevention, though his spine curved slightly forward, as if he'd spent his whole life leaning into the future. His jacket was navy blue and worn at the cuffs. "Not much movement, not much urgency. The water's lazy this morning. Lazy water is honest water. When it's busy, when it's churning, it lies."

They continued walking. The dock was mostly empty. There had been a time, according to the photographs in Grandpa Joe's living room, when this dock held so much movement it would have been impossible to walk its length. Boats stacked beside boats. Men calling to one another in voices that carried across the water. Nets being mended, fish being unloaded, that particular smell of commerce and survival mingling with the salt. Margaret had asked him about that once, about why all the boats had gone away.

"They didn't go away," he'd said. "The fish did. And the people followed the fish. That's what people do. We follow what we need until we run out of it, and then we have to go somewhere else and start over."

"Are you going somewhere else?" she'd asked.

He'd smiled then, a small sad smile. "No. I'm too old to start over. I'm going to stay here and watch this place remember what it used to be."

The cannery was closing in March. Even Margaret, new to Crescent Bay, understood that this was significant. Her grandmother had died five years ago, and her mother—Joe's daughter—had never quite managed to give Margaret a father before deciding that single parenthood was incompatible with her ambitions, which involved a man named Richard in Seattle and a job in something called "tech." So Margaret had come to her grandfather, who had filled his house with silence and books and the knowledge of water.

"The cannery's closing," Margaret said, not really a question.

"Yes."

"Will you leave?"

"No."

"Why not?"

Joe stopped at the end of the dock and turned to look at her. His eyes were the same color as the water had been that morning—old pennies, oxidized, holding memory in their patina.

"Because I told you," he said. "I'm too old. And because this is home. Home doesn't become less of a home just because it's dying. Sometimes it becomes more of one."

That had been a week ago. Now they stood at the dock's end, and Joe was teaching her to read the water the way, Margaret understood, he'd been meant to teach her all along. Her mother had said he was lonely, that he'd been lonely since Grandma died, and that maybe Margaret could keep him company. But Margaret was beginning to suspect that what her mother called loneliness her grandfather called clarity. He wasn't lonely because he'd chosen to be present to this place, fully and without resistance. He wasn't waiting for his life to start happening somewhere else.

"Watch the water for a full minute without speaking," Joe said. "Tell me everything you see."

Margaret obeyed. She counted in her head—Mississippi, one-Mississippi, two-Mississippi—and she watched. The water moved. It seemed still, but it wasn't still. Nothing was still. The small ripples continued their northeast progression. But there was something else too, a subtler motion underneath, like the water was breathing. She could see the reflection of the cannery in it, that strange double exposure where the real building and its ghost occupied the same space. The color shifted slightly, warmer in some spots, cooler in others.

"The water's moving different ways," she said when her minute was up. "Different speeds."

"Why?"

"Because..." She thought about what he'd taught her. The tides. The wind. The temperature. The way water moved faster where there was deeper water and slower where it was shallow. The way currents could hide underneath the surface, invisible until something moved through them and their presence became apparent. "Because underneath isn't the same as the top."

Joe put his hand on her head. He did this sometimes, in moments of something like approval, though he was sparing with praise. She'd learned that his silences were more valuable than easy reassurance.

"You're beginning to understand," he said. "The water is like a book written in layers. Most people only read the surface. They see it's wet, it moves, there are fish in it—or there used to be. But the real reading is in learning to see all the pages at once."

They walked back into town, where the main street was less than two blocks long and half the storefronts were dark. There was a grocery store run by a woman named Linda who'd grown up in Crescent Bay and had left and come back, which Joe said was the truest kind of love for a place. There was a post office. A diner called Sal's that was open for breakfast and lunch only, closing at two p.m. And there was the cannery, massive and red and rusting, its whistle frozen in the position it had held since they shut down the packing line in October.

"Can we go to Sal's?" Margaret asked. There was a pie there called Marionberry that she'd become addicted to, though her mother—in the brief phone call she'd made on Margaret's birthday—had warned about sugar dependency forming in childhood.

"Yes," Joe said. "We can go to Sal's."

The diner was warm and smelled like bacon and coffee and the particular comfort of a place that had been fulfilling the same function for so long that the walls themselves seemed to remember all the people who'd sat in its booths. Sal, who was elderly and whom Margaret had never seen anywhere but behind the counter, looked up and smiled.

"The pilot and his first mate," Sal said. He was a big man with the kind of face that had been weathered by both wind and smiling. "The usual?"

"Please," Joe said. "For both of us."

Margaret's usual was two eggs over easy, hash browns, and toast. Joe's was black coffee and pie. They sat in their booth—the one by the window that looked out onto the street—and Margaret watched the traffic, or what passed for traffic in a town this small. A pickup truck with a rusted bed. A woman with a dog. An old car that seemed to move more through faith than mechanics.

"I want to understand the water like you do," Margaret said, dipping a piece of toast in her egg yolk. "Like it's language."

"It is language," Joe said. He was eating his pie with the same absence of irony someone else might bring to a gourmet meal. To him, pie was just pie; the consumption of it was secondary to the sitting still and the thinking. "Everything is language if you pay attention long enough. Water just happens to be easier to learn than most things because it never lies. It can't. It's too simple. It follows physical laws. It doesn't have the capacity for deception."

"People have deception?" Margaret asked.

"Yes. People are complicated. We hide things. We want things that contradict other things we want. The water never wants anything. It just moves according to what is."

The waitress, a young woman with tired eyes named Claire, brought their food. She set Margaret's eggs down gently, as if they were precious. "Let me know if you need anything else," she said, and Margaret had the sense, obscure but certain, that Claire was asking this of Joe rather than her, that the young woman was checking in on her grandfather in the way that people in small towns seemed to do without making it obvious.

After they ate, they walked to the harbor again. It was their ritual now: the walk to the dock, the lesson, the walk back. Margaret had begun to notice that the timing of these walks seemed to matter to Joe in a way he never explained. They came at different times depending on the season, depending on the light, depending on something she couldn't yet read.

Today, the water had changed. It was darker, more turbulent. The ripples had become waves. There was a wind that hadn't existed this morning, or had hidden itself until now.

"Storm coming," Joe said.

"How do you know?"

"The water tells you. You can learn to read the signs. The birds are gone. The pressure is dropping—your bones can feel it if you've lived long enough to listen to them. But mostly it's the water. It knows what's coming before the sky does."

They stood together at the dock's end, two figures of very different sizes staring at something that most people would have dismissed as unremarkable. But Margaret was beginning to understand that this was a kind of meditation, this standing and watching. It was a form of prayer, maybe, though her grandfather had never said so and she doubted he'd ever thought of it in religious terms. It was just attention. Absolute, unwavering attention to the way things actually were.

"Why is this important?" she asked suddenly, the question breaking out of her with a kind of desperation she didn't entirely understand. "Why are you teaching me to read the water when we're not going to use it? There are no more boats. The cannery is closing."

Joe was quiet for a long time. The wind moved his silver hair, which he never seemed to cut but which somehow maintained a kind of accidental elegance.

"I'm teaching you this because it's true," he said finally. "Because the water will be here long after the cannery is gone, long after I'm gone, long after Crescent Bay becomes something else or stops being anything at all. The ability to read the world as it actually is, without needing it to be something else—that's the only thing worth learning. Everything else changes. Everything else goes away. But the truth about what things are—that persists."

He turned to look at her directly. "Your mother couldn't stay here," he continued, and it was the first time he'd spoken directly about her mother's absence in any way that wasn't practical. "She needed the water to be something else. She needed it to be less lonely, less small, less full of loss. So she left. That's fine. That's one way to live. But you're learning to live differently. You're learning to love something as it is, not as you wish it would be."

Margaret felt something open in her chest, some understanding that went deeper than words. She thought about her mother, about the way she was always looking at her phone or looking ahead to the next thing, never quite present to whatever was actually happening. She thought about the way her grandfather had looked at this dying town and had chosen to love it anyway.

"Will you teach me everything?" she asked. "Everything about the water?"

"I'll teach you what I know," Joe said. "Which is limited. Which is one man's lifetime of attention. But it's a beginning. And you have time—more time than I do—to learn more."

That afternoon, alone in her grandfather's house, Margaret took out a notebook she'd been given for Christmas and started writing down everything she could remember about water. The way it moved. The way it reflected light. The storms it could predict. The currents that lived underneath the surface. The fact that it never lied. She thought about her grandmother, whom she'd never met, and wondered if she had been someone who paid attention, someone who loved things as they were. She thought about her mother in Seattle, and whether she was happy, and whether happiness mattered if you weren't ever actually present for your own life.

The storm came that night. Margaret watched from her bedroom window as the rain hammered sideways, as the wind bent the trees almost double. It was the kind of weather that seemed to threaten the small town with erasure, with obliteration. But when morning came, Crescent Bay was still there. The cannery still stood. The dock was still intact. And the water, furious and complex the night before, had calmed itself back to something like readability.

She and her grandfather walked out into the aftermath. The dock was slick with water and something else—seaweed, jellyfish, the ocean's garbage turned over and returned. Joe moved carefully, as if the wood beneath his feet was a text that required serious attention.

"This is what the storm wrote," he said, gesturing to the debris. "This is the water's record. It came in hard, knocked things loose, rearranged what we thought was settled. Now look—see how the ripples are different? The water remembers. It will take days for the current to redistribute everything. And then, eventually, it will return to something familiar again. Not the same. The water never repeats itself exactly. But recognizable."

Margaret picked up a piece of driftwood, smooth and pale and worn into something like beauty by years of being broken and reformed. She thought about the cannery, about the town, about the way things fell apart and were either rebuilt or not, and either way the world continued. She thought about her grandfather, who had chosen to stay and watch and learn. She thought about the water, which asked nothing of anyone and simply continued to be itself.

"I understand," she said, and it was not entirely a lie.

They walked the dock from one end to the other, and Joe showed her how to read the storm's aftermath like a language, like a narrative written in foam and seaweed and the shifted positions of things that should have stayed put. By the time they returned to shore, Margaret's hands were cold and her mind was full of questions, but she felt something like peace, or maybe not peace but a kind of wholeness that came from not requiring the world to be anything other than what it was.

The cannery would close in March. By April, the town would begin its long, slow decline into whatever it would become. The last fishing boats would eventually leave the harbor. More storefronts would go dark. But the water would continue, and so would her grandfather, sitting on his dock, reading the language of tides and currents. And she would stay—she understood this now, though no one had told her and she had no logical reason to believe it—she would stay long enough to learn everything he could teach her about how to live in a place that was dying, how to love it anyway, and how to find in that choice a kind of grace.

That night, Sal's Diner announced it would be closing at the end of March as well. Claire, the waitress, gave them the news with the kind of sadness that suggested she'd been practicing how to say it. Joe nodded, as if this was information he'd already known, the way he seemed to know most things about the water before they were confirmed. He ordered pie. Margaret ordered eggs and toast. They sat in their booth, and through the window, they could see the cannery, red and rusting and still, for a few more months, a place of employment and purpose and human presence.

"Everything changes," Joe said, not to Margaret but to no one in particular, to the fact of it, to the water he could see from where he sat if he looked at the right angle. "The only thing you can control is how you meet the change. With resistance or with attention. With wishes or with clarity."

Margaret, nine years old and already learning to read what others couldn't see, squeezed her grandfather's hand and said nothing, which was the right thing to say. The water continued its endless work of movement and change, and the two of them sat together in a place that was ending, and they were still.
