# An Irrigation

They came out at first light when the heat was still manageable and the water in the ditch ran clear and cold from the high country. The orchard stretched behind them in rows that had been planted by their grandfather when men still believed in such things and the trees themselves were older than anyone now living who could speak to their origins or their purpose. Some were dying. Some had been dead for years. Others bore fruit that fell to the ground and rotted there and drew wasps and yellow jackets that hung in the hot air like some biblical plague.

The break was in the north section where the main line crossed under the old road. They could see where the water was escaping, a dark patch of earth that had turned to mud and beyond it the ground cracked and white with salt as if something ancient and oceanic had once covered this place and left its mark. The pipe itself was corroded where it was visible above the earth, the copper gone green and brittle, and when Dale struck it with a hammer it flaked away like old paint.

Paul squatted at the edge of the excavation and looked down at the damaged section. You can see where it's been leaking for some time, he said. Months maybe.

Years, Dale said. He was older and he had the older man's habit of certainty about things he could not possibly have known. This whole line was installed in what. Nineteen fifty-six? Fifty-seven?

Nineteen fifty-eight, Paul said. I remember Dad and Grandpa arguing about it. He wanted to wait but Grandpa said you couldn't run an orchard on prayer.

Well the prayer's all that's holding it together now.

They worked through the morning with a shovel and a pry bar, removing the broken coupling and the section of pipe beyond it. The earth had settled over the line in a way that made the work difficult, the soil compacted and hard. Twice Paul struck his hand on a rock and drew blood and didn't mention it. A truck passed on the road above them and they didn't look up. The day was already warming and by ten o'clock the sweat ran down into their eyes and they could taste the dust of the valley in their mouths.

You talk to Henderson again? Paul said.

No.

Did you get the letter from his office?

Yes.

And?

And nothing. I'm not selling.

He's offering a fair price, Paul.

I don't care if he's offering twice what it's worth. I'm not selling. The orchard was supposed to go to Michael. Michael was supposed to come back and run it.

Michael isn't coming back.

I know that.

He's been gone for twelve years.

I know that too.

So what are we doing here? What is the point of fixing a broken irrigation line on property that loses money every year? What is the point of that, Dale?

Dale didn't answer. He was working the pry bar under the next section of pipe, trying to loosen it from the compacted soil. His face was the color of old brick and there were deep lines around his mouth that made him look older than his fifty-two years. When he spoke his voice was quiet.

The point is that it's ours. That's the point. This is ours and it was theirs before that and you don't just sell something because it becomes inconvenient.

That's not what this is about and you know it.

What's it about then?

It's about that we're not farmers. We never wanted to be farmers. Dad didn't want to be a farmer. He only stayed here because of some obligation he felt he couldn't get out from under. And he died an unhappy man because of it. Unhappy and broke and in debt up to his eyeballs and when he died I had to sell off ten acres just to pay the funeral expenses.

I remember.

Do you? Do you remember what he said to you that last time in the hospital?

Dale was quiet for a long time. He worked the bar back and forth, loosening the pipe. Finally he said, He said to take care of things. He said to keep it together.

He said it was a mistake. That's what he said. He said it was all a mistake and he wished he'd sold it when he had the chance.

I don't recall that.

You weren't there when he said it. It was just me. He was half asleep and he just said it. He said he wished he'd had a different life.

A lot of men say things in hospitals, Paul. Morphine talking. Dreams. It doesn't mean anything.

It means something. It means he knew. He knew what this place was. What it cost. What it takes.

They worked in silence for another hour, removing the broken sections of pipe and laying them out on the bank where the sun beat down on the corroded metal. The scale of the decay became apparent as they excavated further. The line was shot through at multiple points, pinhole leaks that had been weeping water into the ground for years. You could trace a history of failure in the pattern of the damage, each break a small catastrophe that had gone unnoticed or unaddressed until it became part of the larger system of decline.

Paul sat back on his heels and looked at the work ahead of them. You're going to need to replace most of this line, he said. Not just patch it. The whole section from the road to the pump house.

How much?

Dale made a face. Probably five thousand. Maybe more. Copper's expensive.

And if we don't?

Then we lose the whole north section. The trees will die.

Paul nodded. He looked back toward the house, three-quarters of a mile away, shimmering in the heat. The trees that were still productive didn't look like much from here either. Stunted and gnarled, bearing their fruit in a way that seemed half-hearted, as if even they had lost faith in the enterprise.

Henderson's paying forty thousand, Paul said. For everything. The house, the equipment, the trees, the water rights. Forty thousand dollars.

It's not enough.

It's enough to start over. To go somewhere and do something different.

It's not enough for what it's worth.

Worth to who? To us? We don't want it. Neither of us has wanted it for a long time. We're just here because of inertia and family obligation and because we don't know how to leave. That's all it is.

Dale began collecting the tools, putting them back in the truck bed in a precise way, each item in its place. When he had finished he looked at Paul directly for the first time that morning.

I'll pay you out, he said. Whatever Henderson's offering, I'll match it. You take the money and you go. Start over. Do whatever it is you want to do.

I don't want your money.

I know. But I'm offering it anyway. Because I'm not leaving. This is where I'm supposed to be. I don't know why and I can't explain it in any way that makes sense to you or probably to anyone else. But I know it the way you know some things that can't be argued with. So you can go or you can stay but I'm not selling and I'm fixing this line and next month I'll be fixing something else and the month after that something else again and that's how it's going to be.

And when you can't anymore? When you're too old to do this work?

Then I guess I'll sit here and watch it all fall apart. At least I'll know I didn't do it myself.

They drove back to town for the supplies, a thirty-minute trip down the valley through landscape that had been transformed in the last decade into a grid of new housing developments and shopping centers. The orchard had been at the edge of town when their grandparents bought it. Now it was engulfed, surrounded on three sides by sprawl and on the fourth by a highway. The way the valley had changed was something neither of them talked about directly but it hung over conversations the way the heat hung in the air—present and inescapable and slowly suffocating.

The supply house was run by a man named Carmichael who had been there for thirty years and who had sold pipe and fittings and equipment to their father and grandfather. He greeted Dale by name and seemed to take no notice of the fact that they wanted to replace a copper line with PVC instead. The economics of it made sense—the PVC was half the cost and would last nearly as long—but somehow it felt like a diminishment, a surrender of something that had once been done properly.

While Carmichael was getting the materials Dale stood at the counter reading a newspaper someone had left there. A story about farmers in the Central Valley losing their land, selling out to developers, moving on. The article had quotes from men about their sadness, about the end of an era, about the sense that something important was being lost. There was a photo of an old man standing in front of an orchard very much like theirs, his hand on the trunk of a tree, his face turned away from the camera.

You ever think about how long you could keep doing this if you really wanted to? Paul said.

Doing what?

This. All of it. Working the land. How many years you think you have left where you could actually do the work?

Dale folded the newspaper. I don't know. Ten years. Maybe fifteen if I take care of myself.

And then what?

Then somebody else will have to deal with it. Or nobody will and it'll just sit here and go back to the earth.

You could sell now while you're still young enough to enjoy whatever comes next.

I could.

But you won't.

No. I won't.

They drove back to the orchard with the truck loaded down, the springs compressing under the weight of the materials. The afternoon light was beginning to change, becoming more golden as the sun moved toward the west. The heat had reached its peak and now there was a subtle diminishment of intensity as the day began its long decline toward evening.

It took them until dusk to run the new line and connect it to the system. The work went faster with the PVC, which was lighter and simpler to work with than the old copper would have been. They worked without speaking much, moving in a rhythm that came from years of doing such things together, understanding what needed to happen without having to discuss it. When the line was pressurized and the water ran clear through the new section without leaking they stood back and looked at what they had done.

It's going to work, Paul said.

Yes.

For how long?

For as long as the rest of it holds together. A few years maybe. Longer if we're lucky.

And then we'll be back out here doing this again.

Yes.

The sun was dropping toward the edge of the world and somewhere down the valley a dog was barking. The orchard around them was settling into evening, the birds quieting, the insects beginning their night songs. In the fading light the old trees looked almost dignified, their twisted branches silhouetted against the sky, their gnarled trunks speaking of age and endurance and stubborn persistence.

Dale shut off the water at the main pump and they gathered the tools. The truck bed was a jumble of old pipe and excess fittings, the detritus of their labor. Tomorrow they would need to clean it up, to dispose of the copper somewhere, to return the unused materials to town.

You want to get some dinner? Paul said.

I could eat.

There's a place that opened up where the old drive-in was. Mexican. Supposedly pretty good.

All right.

They drove the truck across the orchard toward the house, the headlights pushing back the gathering darkness. Behind them the earth was still damp and dark where they had been working, the new line buried and hidden, running its water into the system unseen. By tomorrow it would be impossible to tell that they had done anything at all. The soil would settle, the grass would grow back, and the orchard would stand as it always had, the same and yet incrementally closer to whatever ending awaited it.

Neither of them spoke about the future or about Henderson's offer or about what would happen when the day came that one of them could no longer do this work. These were the things that sat between them always, unspoken but present, like stones in a field that you learned to work around but could never quite remove. They were brothers and they were bound together by blood and history and by a place that neither of them truly wanted but that neither could quite abandon either. And perhaps that was enough. Perhaps that was all that any of them had ever had anyway.
