He had learned to read the forest for signs—broken branches at shoulder height, the particular silence that meant eyes watching, the way certain birds fell quiet. Regina picked her way along the old trail, her hooves careful on the packed earth. Jacob kept his hand near the knife at his belt, though he knew a knife was little comfort if trouble found him in earnest.

The plantation he sought belonged to a man named Vaark—no relation, though Jacob had used the name without shame. His own name was less useful in these territories, marked as it was by the old country and the prejudices that clung to it like fog. The Vaark plantation was prosperous, or so he'd heard in the northern settlements where he'd built his reputation as a trader in goods both legal and otherwise. Beaver pelts, timber, indigo, and rum. The kind of man who asked no questions about origins and expected none about destinations.

The trees opened onto cleared land, and the fog had burned away enough that Jacob could see the cleared fields stretching in both directions, the neat rows of tobacco plants bending slightly in the thick air. Smoke rose from what must have been the main house, a substantial structure visible through the haze at a distance. Smaller buildings clustered nearby—the quarters, he assumed, and workshops. He counted perhaps twenty structures in all, which meant a considerable investment and labor force.

A man on horseback emerged from the tree line ahead, moving toward him at an angle that suggested neither flight nor aggression. Jacob slowed Regina further, showing his empty hands as the distance closed between them.

"Looking for the Vaark place?" the man called when they were perhaps thirty paces apart.

"I am," Jacob replied. "Business with the owner."

The man was perhaps fifty, his face weathered and his eyes sharp. He wore the clothes of an overseer—practical, worn, authoritative in their simplicity. "He's expecting someone?"

"Word was sent ahead from the north. Jacob Vaark."

The overseer's eyes flickered, taking in the name and the man with equal measure. "That so. Well, he's in the house. Go on up." He gestured toward the main structure. "Post your horse at the barn there. Someone will see to her."

Jacob nodded and turned Regina toward the indicated building. He could feel the overseer's gaze on his back, assessing and filing away details. The overseer would report on his appearance, his manner, the quality of his horse and saddle. Men like that were useful. They saw things clearly and remembered them accurately.

The barn was well-maintained, and true to the overseer's word, a boy appeared from its depths before Jacob had fully dismounted. The child—perhaps fourteen—took Regina's reins without comment and led her away, already examining the saddle's clasps with professional interest.

The walk to the house felt longer than the distance suggested. The heat had grown heavier, the sun climbing toward noon, and the smell of the plantation—tobacco, soil worked to exhaustion, smoke, and human waste—pressed down on the landscape like an actual weight. Jacob had traveled enough to know that every place had its own particular stench. This one was the smell of profit built on suffering, which was true of most profitable places, but Virginia seemed to wear it more openly than the northern colonies.

The woman who answered his knock was neither young nor old, with the bearing of someone accustomed to managing a household of size and complexity. She looked at him with the frank appraisal that came from never having been taught to dissemble.

"You're the trader," she said. Not a question.

"I am. Your husband is expecting—"

"My brother. My husband died seven years ago. Come." She turned and walked back into the house without waiting to see if he would follow.

The interior was dim after the brightness outside, and Jacob's eyes took a moment to adjust. The hallway was painted a dark red, and the air inside was only slightly cooler than without. She led him through the house to a study where a man sat at a heavy desk, quill in hand. He was younger than Jacob had expected—perhaps forty—with fair hair graying at the temples and eyes that held the clarity of someone accustomed to making decisions and living with their consequences.

"Vaark," the man said, rising. "I'm Vaark as well, which makes for a confusion I apologize for. You can call me Thomas." He extended his hand, and Jacob shook it. The grip was firm but not aggressive, the grip of a man secure in his position. "You've come a long way. Sit, please."

Jacob took the offered chair. Thomas's sister had vanished silently, which suggested either good manners or a disinclination to hear business conducted.

"I've come to discuss an arrangement," Jacob began. "I have capital—not vast, but sufficient—and I'm looking to invest it in something with more permanence than trading. A friend in the north suggested you might be open to such a proposal."

Thomas leaned back in his chair. "I might be. What manner of investment?"

"Land. Labor. A partnership in the production of goods for export. You have established connections, a going concern. I have ready money and a network of traders willing to move goods to markets in the Caribbean and beyond."

"And what makes you think I need such an arrangement?" Thomas asked. "I'm doing well enough as I am."

"I don't think you need it," Jacob said, meeting his eyes. "I think you want more. The men who are satisfied with enough are rare. I've rarely met one who stayed satisfied for long."

Thomas smiled then, a genuine expression that crinkled the corners of his eyes. "You're a candid man. I don't meet many of those either. The candid ones or the ones who stay satisfied." He rose and walked to a window that overlooked the fields. "What are your terms?"

"I would need to see the records. The production, the yield, the market prices. I would need to understand the labor situation—what you have, what you might acquire, what restrictions apply. I would need to know your debts and your assets."

"You would need to know everything," Thomas said, not turning from the window.

"I would."

Thomas was quiet for a long moment, watching the fields, his reflection faint in the glass. "There's a man coming," he said finally. "This week, I expect. A ship's captain I know who deals in a particular kind of merchandise. The prices are good if you're willing to own what you're doing. Are you willing?"

Jacob understood what was being asked beneath the question. "I came south knowing what I would find."

"That's not an answer."

"I'm a businessman, not a priest. I don't make judgments about what men do. I only understand the mathematics of it."

Thomas turned from the window. "That's honest, at least. Very well. I'll have my records prepared. You'll stay here as my guest while we conduct our examination. You can see the operation, understand what we're doing. Then we'll talk about partnership."

"I'm grateful."

"Don't be. If this works, you'll earn every penny. If it doesn't, you'll lose them. The mathematics is indifferent, as you say, but the results are absolute."

Over the next days, Jacob moved through the plantation like a man taking measurements. He saw the fields and the storage houses. He examined the records—meticulous, they were, noting yield by season and market price by port. He walked past the quarters early in the morning when the people still slept and late in the evening when the day's work was done. He rode the boundaries with the overseer, who proved to be a man named Sorrow, competent and without sentiment.

It was Sorrow who brought him to see what Thomas had not shown him directly. In the evening of the fourth day, as light was failing, Sorrow led him to a structure separate from the main buildings, partially underground.

"You should know what you're buying into," Sorrow said, and there was something in his voice that might have been warning or merely explanation. "Before you commit."

The building was divided into sections. In one, men and women sat or lay on platforms, their condition immediately visible as one of deterioration and illness. In another, children slept in heaps on straw. In a third, a woman hung from a post, her back a landscape of scars, her breathing shallow.

Jacob looked and then he looked away. This was not new information. He had seen worse in his travels. The question was not whether such things existed—they manifestly did—but whether he was willing to profit from them.

"Why show me this?" he asked.

"Because some men need to know and some men don't. Some men can pretend ignorance, and some men need the fact of it in their mind when they're counting the money. I'm showing you because Thomas won't, and because a man should know what he's becoming."

"And what am I becoming?"

"Yourself," Sorrow said. "Just more so. Whatever you were before, you'll be more of it here."

That night Jacob lay in the bed provided him in the guest room and thought about the woman hanging from the post. He thought about the choices that led to such moments, the small decisions that accumulated into atrocity, the way a man could look at such a thing and simply see the cost of doing business.

By morning he had decided. The mathematics were sound. The opportunity was substantial. The moral mathematics were beside the point—they always were, in the end. The world was built by men who had learned not to count those particular costs.

He told Thomas yes. They agreed to terms. A lawyer—a thin, nervous man who appeared from nowhere and seemed satisfied by nothing—drew up papers. Jacob signed them, and in signing, he bound himself to something larger than he had anticipated.

The ship arrived three days later, anchoring in a protected cove north of the plantation. It was a small vessel, built for speed rather than capacity, and it carried cargo that Thomas had paid dearly to secure. Jacob stood on the beach, watching the boats come ashore, and tried to feel nothing in particular.

There were forty-three of them. Men, women, and children. They were naked or nearly so, branded or marked, their condition suggesting a voyage of considerable duration. They were herded off the boats and toward the holding structure Sorrow had shown him. The children cried. The adults were silent, which was somehow worse.

Thomas stood beside him, watching with the satisfaction of a man whose calculations had proven sound.

"Good stock," Thomas said. "The captain did well. They're young enough to break to work, strong enough to survive the seasoning, and the price was reasonable. With these, I can expand into rice cultivation. There's money in rice, Vaark. Real money."

Jacob nodded and said nothing. The mathematics remained sound. His investment would pay, and it would pay well.

That night, unable to sleep, Jacob walked the plantation under the full moon. The light was bright enough to cast shadows, to show the detail of the place in a stark and unforgiving way. He found himself at the quarters, listening to the sounds that came from within—the crying, the coughing, the murmured prayers in languages he didn't recognize.

He had come south to build something. He had come with the idea that he would create a future different from his past, that money could be the alchemy that transformed one life into another. Now, standing in the moonlight with the sounds of human misery in his ears, he understood that he had only succeeded in becoming someone different, not better. The transformation was real, but not the one he'd wanted.

By morning, he had rationalized it. By the end of the week, he barely thought of it. By the time he left the plantation to return north, he had already begun the process of forgetting, or if not forgetting, then carefully filing the knowledge away where it wouldn't interfere with the day's business.

This was how men became villains, he thought. Not suddenly, not with dramatic purpose, but through a series of small arrangements with themselves. Each compromise minor. Each one manageable. Until one day you looked at your hands and no longer recognized them.

But your hands still worked. They still counted money and signed documents and rode horses through fog-bound lands. They still functioned, which was perhaps all that mattered in the end.
