The thing about those tests was they made you see things differently afterward. Made you understand that the world was more fragile than you thought, or maybe less fragile, depending on your angle. Jack Lovett didn't really know which anymore. He'd been living with both versions for so long they'd become the same thing in his head.

He was telling Inez this because she'd asked him about the dawn. She'd asked him what it was like to watch the sky light up like that, and he'd started talking and couldn't quite stop. That was the thing about Inez—she had a way of getting you to say things you hadn't planned on saying. Things you'd kept locked up for years because there wasn't anybody around who would actually want to hear them.

They were in a hotel room in Los Angeles. Some anonymous place in West Hollywood, the kind of place where people checked in under names that weren't theirs, where the staff didn't look at you twice. Jack had been in and out of places like this for thirty years. The rooms all looked the same. The beds were all too soft. The air conditioning always made a sound like something was dying inside the walls.

Inez was sitting on the edge of the bed, still half-dressed, smoking a cigarette. She was somewhere in her fifties now, though it was hard to say exactly. She had the kind of face that didn't age so much as it accumulated knowledge. Sharp cheekbones. Eyes that had seen too much and didn't pretend otherwise.

"So what happened after?" she asked. "After the pink sky?"

Jack was looking out the window at the street below. Sunset Boulevard in the late afternoon. Cars moving slowly. People on the sidewalk carrying shopping bags. The ordinary world going about its business, completely unaware of what men like him had spent decades preparing for.

"Nothing much," he said. "That was the strange part. You'd see this incredible light, this impossible thing, and then it was over. We'd all get back on the transport and fly back to the debriefing. Somebody would hand out coffee. We'd sit in a metal building with no windows and talk about what we'd seen like we were describing something that happened to somebody else."

"But you felt something," Inez said. It wasn't a question.

"Sure. We felt something. But you got used to it. Or you told yourself you did. After the third or fourth shot you stopped trying to hold onto the feeling. You just let it go past you like everything else."

Jack turned away from the window. Inez was watching him with that particular expression she got sometimes, the one that suggested she was looking at him and also looking through him at some calculation she was doing in her head.

"Why are you telling me this now?" she asked.

"Because you asked."

"That's not why. You've had thirty years to tell me this. We've spent thirty years going in and out of hotel rooms, and you've never once decided that today was the day to talk about the nuclear tests."

Jack sat down in the chair by the window. There was a minibar next to it. He thought about getting a drink but didn't move. After a while, you learned that thinking about it was often better than actually doing it.

"There's talk," he said finally. "Talk about something coming. Something big."

"What kind of talk?"

"The kind that gets passed along by people who know things. People in certain positions."

"And they've told you specifically?"

"Not told me. Suggested. Implied. Left breadcrumbs."

Inez put out her cigarette and lit another one immediately. She did this when she was thinking through something complicated.

"Are you in danger?" she asked.

"I'm always in danger. That's not new."

"That's a dodge."

"Maybe. But it's also the truth. You live the way I've lived, the danger is just part of the basic structure. It's not something that comes and goes. It's the foundation."

"So what's changed?"

Jack leaned his head back against the chair and closed his eyes. Through the window, he could hear the sound of the traffic on the street, the occasional siren in the distance. Los Angeles sounds. Constant motion, constant noise, everybody going somewhere else.

"I'm getting older," he said. "That changes things. You start to think about what you've done with the time you had. You start to wonder if any of it was worth it."

"Was it?"

"I don't know. I've spent most of my life in places I wasn't supposed to be, doing things I wasn't supposed to talk about, knowing things I wasn't supposed to know. I've watched governments fall apart from the inside. I've seen how the machinery actually works when you get behind the official version. And I'm not sure any of it made a difference. I'm not sure any of it mattered at all."

"But you keep doing it."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Jack opened his eyes. He looked at Inez for a long moment.

"Because I'm good at it," he said. "And because I don't know how to do anything else. And because once you're inside the system, there's no way out that doesn't involve becoming something other than yourself."

Inez stood up and walked to the window, standing next to the chair where Jack was sitting. She was close enough that he could smell her perfume, something expensive and subtle that she'd worn for as long as he'd known her.

"The last time we saw each other," she said, "you told me you were thinking about getting out. You said you were tired of living like this."

"I was."

"And now?"

"Now I'm tired of being tired. There's a difference."

She didn't respond to that. Instead, she moved away from the window and went into the bathroom. Jack heard the sound of water running, the small sounds of her moving around in there. After a while, she came back out.

"I have to leave," she said. "I have a dinner tonight. Some political thing. My husband will expect me to be there."

"The senator."

"Yes."

"You ever think about leaving him?"

Inez smiled, but it wasn't a happy smile. It was the smile of someone who had considered a question so many times that the smile had become a default setting.

"Every day," she said. "But I won't. He's useful to me, and I'm useful to him, and that's how these things work. We have an understanding."

"An understanding that includes me?"

"An understanding that includes a lot of things. He's not a stupid man, Jack. He knows what my life is. He probably knows more than I think he knows."

"Would that bother him? If he knew about us?"

"Probably not. As long as it stayed discreet. As long as I was careful."

Jack nodded. This was the way the world actually worked, he'd learned. Not the way the movies showed it, with passion and drama and people making impossible choices. Instead, it was all negotiation and compromise and people making peace with the fact that they would never get everything they wanted.

Inez gathered her things. She moved around the room with a practiced efficiency, collecting her clothes and putting them on, her movements economical and sure. Jack watched her without comment. She'd done this hundreds of times in hundreds of rooms like this one.

"When will I see you again?" she asked, once she was dressed.

"I don't know. There's some things I need to take care of first. Some people I need to talk to."

"About the talk you mentioned?"

"Yes."

"Is it dangerous?"

"Probably."

Inez turned to face him. She was fully dressed now, composed again, ready to return to her real life.

"Be careful," she said.

"I'm always careful."

"No, you're not. You're reckless. You always have been. You just hide it better than most people."

She came over and kissed him on the top of his head, the kind of kiss you'd give to a child or someone you were saying goodbye to for the last time. Then she left, and Jack was alone in the hotel room with the sound of the air conditioning and the traffic outside and his own thoughts.

He sat there for a long time without moving. After a while, he got up and went to the minibar and poured himself a whiskey. He drank it slowly, standing at the window, watching the light fade from the sky. It was a different kind of pink than the pink he'd seen at dawn in the Pacific, softer and sadder, the color of something ending rather than something beginning.

His phone rang around nine o'clock. He recognized the number immediately.

"Hello, Charlie," Jack said.

"We need to talk," Charlie said. His voice was tight, controlled. "Not on the phone. Tomorrow. Ten o'clock. The place you know."

"I'll be there."

"And Jack? Come alone. And watch for a tail."

The line went dead.

Jack turned off the phone and sat in the dark for a while. Charlie was one of the people who knew things, one of the people who passed along breadcrumbs to people like Jack. If Charlie was calling with that tone, with that level of concern, then the talk wasn't theoretical anymore. The talk was becoming concrete.

He thought about calling Inez back, about warning her about something he couldn't quite articulate yet. But that would be a mistake. The cleaner the separation between his world and hers, the safer she would be. That was rule number one, and he'd learned it the hard way, through years of seeing what happened to people who didn't maintain that kind of distance.

Jack lay back on the bed and stared at the ceiling. Tomorrow at ten o'clock, he would meet with Charlie and learn whatever it was that was coming. Tonight, he would stay in this room and think about pink skies and atolls and the machinery that turned the world from one thing into another without asking anybody's permission.

He thought about the observers he'd known, the men who had spent weeks on those islands waiting for the weather people to give the go. Most of them were dead now, their bodies slowly accumulating the dose from all those shots they'd stood and watched. He'd heard that they used to joke about it, back then, back when the danger still seemed theoretical, back when they believed the government's assurances that everything was under control.

But there was nothing under control. There never had been. That was the lesson that the Pacific tests had tried to teach, if anyone had been paying attention. The lesson was that you could build something that would destroy everything you cared about, and once you'd built it, you couldn't unbuild it. You could only watch it exist in the world and hope that nobody ever decided to use it.

Jack closed his eyes. Sleep didn't come easily anymore, but he let his mind drift toward it anyway, toward the place where the pink sky and the gardenias and the sound of coconuts being cracked open on a beach somewhere in the middle of the ocean all seemed to exist together in some kind of eternal present tense.

He would meet with Charlie tomorrow. He would learn what needed to be learned. And then he would figure out what came next, the way he always did. That was the only skill left to him anymore—the ability to figure out what came next, to keep moving, to stay one step ahead of the machinery he'd spent his life serving.

The neon from the street below came through the windows and cast everything in shades of red and blue. Jack watched the colors shift and change across the ceiling, and he thought about all the rooms he'd been in, all the conversations he'd had in the dark, all the small decisions that had accumulated over three decades into this: lying alone in a hotel room in Los Angeles, waiting for tomorrow, waiting to learn what the world wanted from him next.
