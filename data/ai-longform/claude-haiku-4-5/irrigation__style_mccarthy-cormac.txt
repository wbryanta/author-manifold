The old man had been dead three years when the line went out and they came to fix it. The water stopped flowing on a Tuesday morning in the second week of June and the peaches were already sizing on the trees in that helpless way they had when the heat came on and there was nothing to stop it. Tommy was the first to notice. He'd driven out from the city on Sunday and gone walking through the orchard in that methodical way he had, taking inventory of losses as if the land were some kind of ledger that could be balanced if you looked at it hard enough.

He found his brother already at the main valve when he came back down from the upper grove. James was sitting on the concrete pad with a wrench in his hand, studying the mechanism with the focused deliberation of a man who knows that something is broken but has not yet decided whether to fix it or accept it as final.

They didn't greet each other much anymore. It had been a year or more since they'd been here together, and the time before that was longer still. James lived in the house still, such as it was—a low sprawling thing built in the fifties with a covered porch that had begun to sag at one end like an old animal settling into sleep. Tommy lived in Portland now in an apartment that cost more per month than the gross income from the orchard in a good year. He was an accountant. He had a wife and no children and he moved through the world with the careful efficiency of someone who'd learned to calculate the value of everything.

The break was about a mile up the line where it ran through the western section near the road. They could see where the water had surfaced and run down the hillside in a dark stain that had already begun to dry and cake in the dust. The irrigation pipe was forty years old if it was a day. It had outlived the old man's optimism and now it was failing in the way old things fail, not all at once but in a series of small treacheries that accumulate until the whole apparatus grinds to a halt.

They took the truck up to the break. James drove because he still knew the roads the way you only know roads when you've driven them every day of your life. The truck was from the eighties, a blue Ford that the old man had bought new and that James had kept running through a series of improvised repairs that seemed to violate several laws of thermodynamics. Tommy sat in the passenger seat and looked out at the orchards rolling away on either side of them like a tide that had come in and frozen.

We could sell, Tommy said.

James didn't answer. He steered the truck around a pothole that had been there since at least the last time Tommy had been home.

The line's shot. The pump's shot. The irrigation system is basically shot. I ran the numbers. The land's worth more than the equipment.

Still nothing.

I talked to Bergstrom. He's been wanting to buy. He'd give us a fair price.

Fair according to who.

According to the market. According to what it's worth.

James brought the truck to a stop and shut off the engine. They sat there in the sudden silence with the sun pressing down on the hood and the heat already warping the air so that the trees in the distance seemed to shimmer and shift like something seen through water. 

My old man paid for this place with forty years of work, James said. He paid for it in heat and in drought and in frost that came in April when the trees were flowering. He paid for it in debt. In loans that took twenty years to pay off. In crops that didn't come. In prices that fell every time the harvest came in.

That's not an argument for keeping it.

No. But it's an argument for not selling it like it's nothing.

It's not nothing. It's a business asset that's failing to generate positive returns. We have an opportunity to realize the value and deploy the capital more effectively.

James looked at his brother for the first time since Tommy had arrived. His face was dark from sun and lined in a way that made him look older than his forty-eight years. His hands on the wheel were scarred and calloused and the nails were perpetually broken from work.

Listen to yourself, James said. You sound like someone who never lived here.

I lived here. I grew up here.

You lived here. Past tense. You left when you were eighteen and you've been leaving ever since. Every time you come back you leave a little more.

The heat was oppressive. Tommy could feel sweat starting at his hairline. He turned his face toward the window and didn't respond.

They got out of the truck and walked up the line toward the break. The grass on either side of the road had gone brown and brittle and it crackled under their boots. The sound of their walking was the only sound. No birds. No wind. Just the two of them and the weight of everything they'd never said to each other pressing down like the heat.

The break was worse than they'd thought. The pipe had separated at a coupling and the metal was corroded so severely that the threads were almost nonexistent. It wouldn't hold a repair. It would need to be replaced, at least in this section, which meant at least three hundred dollars for new pipe and fittings and another day of work at minimum.

This whole section should be replaced, Tommy said, looking at the line as it stretched away down the slope. Maybe more than this section.

James was already walking back toward the truck.

Where are you going?

To get the tools. We'll patch it for now.

We can't patch this. It's too far gone.

We can do it. It'll hold.

For how long? Another season? Two? Dad did this same patch five years ago.

So we patch it again. We do what needs doing.

That's not sustainable.

James stopped and turned around. His face was very still. Then nothing is sustainable, he said. None of it. You think this makes a difference? The patch or no patch? The land or selling the land? In fifty years we're both dead. In a hundred years nobody remembers us. In a thousand years this whole place is dust. The only question is what you do with the time you've got and who you do it for.

Tommy said nothing. He watched his brother walk back to the truck and begin pulling out the tools—the pipe wrench, the coupling repair kit, the rolls of thread tape that would never really seal anything but that would slow the leak long enough to make it through another season.

They worked through the afternoon. The sun didn't move so much as it intensified. It bore down on them with an almost personal malice and the heat rose off the earth in waves that made everything shimmer and shift. Tommy's shirt darkened with sweat and stuck to his back. His hands blistered where the tools had worn away the calluses he'd once had but had lost in the years of air conditioning and climate control.

James worked with the kind of quiet efficiency he'd learned from the old man. He didn't waste motion or energy. He didn't complain. He simply did the thing that needed to be done, one step following another in the way that work does when it's not going to end.

They got the coupling off eventually and cleaned the threads as best they could and installed the repair kit, wrapping it with fresh thread tape and tightening it down with a wrench that groaned with the effort. When they turned the valve back on, the water flowed without leaking. It wouldn't last. They both knew it wouldn't last. But it worked now, which was perhaps the only thing that mattered.

The sun was still high when they finished. There were hours of daylight left but the work was done and they stood there with the tools in their hands and nowhere to go. Tommy looked back toward the orchards. The peaches would get their water. They would swell and grow and in a few weeks they would be ready to pick. For one more season the enterprise would continue. For one more year the land would produce something and James would be here to tend it and Tommy would be in Portland in his apartment looking at spreadsheets and wondering what it all meant.

We should check the upper grove, James said. Make sure nothing else is broken.

Tommy nodded. They loaded the tools and drove up toward the higher elevation where the trees were older and less productive and where the old man had stopped irrigating five years ago because it no longer made economic sense.

The break was in the back section where the line ran along the fence line toward the stand of oak trees that marked the western boundary of the property. They could see it from a distance where the water had broken through and flooded the ground, creating a small pool that glinted in the sunlight like something alive.

This one's worse, James said.

This one's finished, Tommy said.

They walked out to it anyway. The pipe had split along a seam and the rent was too large to repair. The water was still running out of it in a steady stream that would eventually run down the hillside and be lost. It was wasteful and useless and in its way it was perfect.

We'd need to replace the whole section, Tommy said. From the valve at least. Maybe further.

Yeah.

Five hundred dollars minimum. Probably more if we're doing it right.

James didn't answer. He was looking out across the grove toward where the trees grew thinner and the land began to slope away toward the county road. The sun was starting to descend finally and the angle of the light had shifted so that everything was thrown into relief. The old trees cast long shadows across the ground and the dry grass was turning gold.

I could sell to Bergstrom, Tommy said. We'd each get close to two hundred fifty thousand. Maybe more if the market's right.

And do what.

Anything. Live. Retire. Travel. Take care of Mom better. She's in that place in Salem and it's costing seven thousand a month. We could afford better.

James turned to look at his brother. His face was very dark and very still and the lines in it seemed deeper than they had before.

Mom doesn't need better. She doesn't know where she is. She doesn't remember any of this. She sees me sometimes and I'm my father to her. That's fine. That's how it goes. But I'm not selling this place to buy her a nicer room in a building she doesn't recognize.

Then what do you want to do.

I want to keep doing what I've been doing. I want to walk this land every day and check the trees and make sure the water gets where it needs to go and harvest when the time comes and sell the fruit at the market and use the money to fix the things that break and then fix the things that break next year. I want to live the way Dad lived. The way his father lived.

You want to slowly go broke the way they slowly went broke.

Maybe. Probably. But at least it'll be honest. At least it won't be some kind of transaction. At least I won't wake up in the morning wondering what I sold away.

The sun was lower now, perhaps an hour from the hills. The light had taken on that particular quality it had in June when the days were longest and the sun seemed reluctant to set, as if it knew that its time was running out and wanted to extract as much heat and light from the world as it possibly could.

You're not going to change your mind, Tommy said.

No.

Even though it makes no sense. Even though the land is losing money and you're going to work yourself to death out here like Dad did.

Yeah.

And you don't think I'm being selfish.

I think you are. I think you want to do the right thing by the land and by the family and by the memory of our father. I think all of that is real and I think it's important. But I also think you've built a life somewhere else and you don't want to feel guilty about it.

Tommy looked away. Across the grove the shadows were lengthening and the trees were taking on a darker character. Somewhere a bird was calling but the sound was thin and distant.

Maybe, he said. Probably. But that doesn't mean I'm wrong.

No. It doesn't.

They stood in silence for a while longer. The broken pipe continued to run, wasting water that wouldn't help anything, that wouldn't even reach the roots of any trees but would simply seep into the ground and disappear.

We could fix this one too, James said finally. It'll take some work but we could do it.

It's not worth fixing. The line back to the valve would need replacement. The whole section is compromised.

Probably.

And you want to fix it anyway.

Yeah. I do.

Tommy looked at his brother. The sun was in his eyes and he raised his hand to shield them. James looked back, waiting for some kind of judgment or perhaps for agreement, but Tommy gave him neither. He simply stood there in the heat of the afternoon with the broken land around him and the knowledge that this would never be resolved, that there was no argument that would convince either of them to change their position, and that they would probably have this same conversation again in a year or in five years or in ten years, circling the same point like water circling a drain.

Alright, Tommy said. Let's fix it.

James nodded. He didn't smile. He simply turned and began walking back toward the truck to gather the tools they would need.

They worked until the sun was nearly at the horizon. The light was golden now and slanted across the ground at a low angle that made everything seem to exist at a distance from itself, as if they were all translucent and fading. The work was harder this time because the pipe had to be replaced for a longer section, and the old coupling was seized and resisted all attempts to remove it.

James worked with a blowtorch to heat the metal while Tommy held a bucket of water to cool it in cycles that would eventually break the corrosion. It took two hours. The sun moved steadily toward the mountains and the shadows grew longer and the heat moderated slightly, though not enough to be called pleasant. The sky turned orange and then red and then began to shade toward purple at the highest point.

When they finally got the old pipe out and the new section installed, it was nearly dark. They flushed the line and it ran clear and steady and there were no leaks. The water that had been spilling uselessly onto the ground now flowed back where it was meant to go, back toward the valve and the larger system and eventually to the roots of the trees that depended on it.

They stood and watched it flow for a moment in the gathering dark. Above them the first stars were beginning to appear, small and distant and indifferent to the concerns of two men and a broken piece of land.

You hungry, James said.

Yeah. I am.

I'll make something. There's some chicken in the freezer. Potatoes. We can eat on the porch.

Tommy nodded. He was tired in a way that was different from being tired in the city. It was a cleaner kind of tiredness, more honest somehow, though he wasn't sure what he meant by that exactly.

They drove back to the house in the truck with the windows down and the early stars wheeling overhead. The air was cool now and smelled of dust and the earth cooling after the heat of the day. Tomorrow the break in the upper line would probably start leaking again or another pipe somewhere would fail and they would have to go out and fix that too, and they would probably have to do this forever or until they were too old to do it, and at some point James would probably die out here or in the house or somewhere on the land and Tommy would have to decide whether to hire someone to keep it going or finally to sell it to Bergstrom or someone like him.

But that was tomorrow and he didn't think about it. Instead he thought about nothing in particular and let the sound of the truck and the smell of the land fill the space where thought would normally be. Beside him James drove with both hands on the wheel, staring out at the dark, carrying with him all the certainty that Tommy had lost somewhere along the way and that he knew now he would never get back.
