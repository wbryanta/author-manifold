# Lambing in the Dark

The wind had teeth. Margaret could feel them through the gaps in the lambing shed's old wooden frame, small sharp bites that made her pull the collar of her wool coat higher around her neck. She'd been doing this for twenty-three years—first with her father, then with Robert, and now alone, with only Thomas—but she'd never gotten used to the way a late-season blizzard could turn April inside out, make you forget that green things grew, that the world was anything but white and bitter.

"How many now?" Thomas called from the far end of the shed, his voice cracking slightly on the second word. Sixteen years old and already taller than his father had been, though without the breadth across the shoulders yet. He would grow into that, Margaret supposed, if he stayed. If he didn't leave like so many farm children did, trading lambs for cities.

"Eight," she said, not looking up from the ewe in front of her. This one was Dotty—Margaret named all the sheep, something Robert had gently mocked until their second year together, when he'd stopped and started doing it too. Dotty was having trouble. The lamb was presenting wrong, and Margaret's hands, which had brought hundreds of lambs into the world with a kind of instinctive grace, were moving with deliberate slowness, talking themselves through each motion. Turn the head. Check the hooves. There—yes, there was one folded back.

"Is she okay?" Thomas had moved closer without Margaret hearing him approach. His boots on the straw should have made noise, but the wind was a constant roar now, shaking the whole structure like something huge was trying to get in.

"She's fine. The lamb's just confused about which way is out." Margaret worked the folded leg free, careful, always careful. Dotty was pushing now, her sides heaving with effort. "Come hold her head. Talk to her."

Thomas moved into position the way Robert had taught him five summers ago, placing his hands on either side of Dotty's face, speaking low words that Margaret couldn't quite hear but that seemed to calm the panicked ewe. He had a gift for it, this calm he could project. Margaret wondered if it came from her side of the family or his father's. She'd never thought to ask Robert where he'd learned to do it so well.

The lamb came in a rush then, a dark wet thing that emerged almost suddenly into Margaret's hands. A ewe lamb, healthy by the sound of it, already bleating in that thin, outraged way they did. Margaret set it down in the straw and the mother immediately turned, nostrils flaring, beginning the essential work of knowing this creature that had just come from inside her.

"Good job," Thomas said, and Margaret looked up to find him watching her with an expression she couldn't quite read. Pride, maybe. Or something else—something that looked like wondering.

"Go put this one with the other singles," Margaret said, gesturing to the lamb. "Check the heat lamp. It was dropping before the power went."

Before the power went. That was when, exactly? An hour ago? Two? Time had gone strange the moment the lights died, the moment the hum of the shed's warming system simply stopped. It was 9:47 when it happened—Margaret remembered looking at the battery-powered clock she kept specifically for situations like this. The red digital numbers had announced the moment of abandonment to a world grown suddenly darker and colder.

Thomas picked up the lamb, cradling it against his chest the way he'd been taught, and Margaret watched him move toward the corner where the single lambs stayed, their heat lamp glowing dull orange in the darkness. They hadn't bothered with the generator. Robert had always said it was temperamental, and they'd both gotten in the habit of avoiding temperamental things. The generator had been sitting beside the shed all winter, unused. She'd noticed it when she came out to do the first check this morning, thinking vaguely that they should do something about it, and then there had been the mail and the fence line that needed repair on the east pasture, and life had done its ordinary thing, which was to move forward assuming tomorrow would be the same as today.

The wind gusted hard enough that Margaret heard something pop somewhere in the structure—a sound like wood might make if it was thinking about giving up its commitment to being solid. She set her jaw and ran her hands over her face, checking the time again. 10:23. Still the middle of the night. The blizzard might last until morning. It might last longer than that. In her childhood, there had been a storm that lasted three days. Her father had stayed in the barn, sleeping in a chair, because the animals couldn't be left and people could die in thirty seconds out in that kind of wind. People had died.

Margaret stood up, her lower back making a small sound of protest, and walked to the nearest window. She could see nothing but white and the faint ghost of her own reflection against the glass. Somewhere out there was the house, maybe a hundred yards away. Somewhere past that was the main road, probably impassable by now. Somewhere further was the rest of the world, but it seemed to Margaret that it might have stopped existing. The white had the quality of finality about it.

"Mom?" Thomas had returned, wiping his hands on his jeans. "There's another one coming into labor."

Of course there was. The blizzard hadn't slowed the ewes in the slightest. Evolution had decided that lambs would come in April whether there was a nor'easter or not, whether the power was working or the thermometer was dropping or God Himself was demanding it. Nature didn't negotiate. You could admire that, Margaret supposed, or you could hate it. She'd spent the last month doing both.

The ewe in question was a young one, a third-time mother named Polaris. She was doing better than Dotty had—the lamb was presenting normally, the labor progressing at a good pace. Margaret knelt in the straw, which had gotten damp in places from the birth fluids and spilled water, and began the familiar litany of checking and assessing. This was where her mind went quiet, where the grief that had been living in her chest like an extra organ since September could find a moment's rest. The sheep required presence. They required all of you, or at least they required all of the you that could be spared from the part that was broken.

"Can you get me a clean towel?" she asked Thomas. "And then sit with me."

He brought the towel and settled into a squat beside her, close enough that their shoulders nearly touched. He was wearing the jacket his father had worn, the ancient canvas thing that was still two sizes too big for him. Margaret noticed this the way you notice small cruelties.

"I've been reading about what to do if we get stuck here," Thomas said quietly. "In case the power doesn't come back by morning."

"Reading where? There's no power."

"I wrote it down. In a notebook. Last month sometime. I was just thinking about it." He paused. "I wasn't trying to sound like I know what I'm doing."

"I know you weren't," Margaret said. She didn't elaborate on the fact that he might know more than she did by now. Thomas had inherited his father's tendency to read about things methodically, building knowledge from the ground up the way you'd build a wall. Margaret was all instinct and experience, which meant she was good at the present moment but hopeless at the future. "What does the notebook say?"

"That we can keep them warm if we have to. The lambs. The heat lamp might not be enough if the power stays out. We might have to bring them inside."

Margaret nearly laughed at this. The image of her living room full of lambs, their manure scattered across the furniture Robert had chosen, seemed like the kind of detail that belonged to a joke she wasn't in on anymore. "Into the house?"

"Into the kitchen anyway. Maybe the mudroom if there are too many. Hypothermia is a real risk, and—"

"I know what hypothermia is, Tom."

"I know you do. I'm sorry. I'm just nervous."

Polaris began her hard labor then, the sides heaving in that explosive way, and Margaret turned her attention back to the work. The lamb was coming. She could see the snout, the small sealed eyes, the way the head was angled just right. Thomas stayed beside her, quiet, and she felt something shift in the space between them—some acknowledgment that they were neither of them really qualified for this and yet here they were, doing it anyway.

The lamb came in two strong pushes, a ram this time, bigger than the first one, already looking sturdy. Margaret caught it, held it as it gasped and struggled, that first desperate moment of understanding that it was in a new medium now, one that didn't support your weight or do exactly what your muscles expected. She cleared the mucus from its nostrils and watched as it took its first real breath, and then she handed it to Thomas.

"Here. Dry him off. Let her smell him."

Thomas took the lamb with the unconscious care of someone who'd been doing this since childhood—which he had been, she realized. He'd been born into this work. He didn't remember a time before lambs, before the endless cycle of breeding and birthing and selling and raising. He probably couldn't imagine a life that didn't have it in it.

The night wore on in that strange, stretched way that nights had when you were waiting for something. Margaret lost count of the time somewhere around one in the morning. There was another ewe in labor, and then another. Thomas discovered that if they left the windowed door of the shed open just a crack, snow would blow through and they could use it to wash their hands, which was actually cleaner than the water from the bucket that was getting increasingly milky with wool and placenta. Margaret found herself laughing at this—an actual laugh, the first one in months that didn't feel like something she was doing to be polite.

"What?" Thomas asked, and she shook her head, unable to explain. The laughter had surprised her as much as it had him.

By three in the morning, they had fourteen lambs. The shed had taken on the particular smell that only came after a long birthing season—life fluids and warm wool and straw and an underneath note of something mineral and ancient. The wind had actually dropped slightly, or maybe Margaret had just gotten used to it. The temperature had certainly dropped. She could see her breath now when she leaned back into the light of the heat lamp, and the lambs were clustering more closely, seeking warmth from each other.

"We should bring some in," Thomas said. He sounded exhausted. "If the power doesn't come back—"

"Not yet. Let's see what the morning brings." Margaret stood up, feeling every one of her fifty-two years and then some, and walked to the window again. The white had shifted slightly from pure white to something that contained the faintest suggestion of gray. Dawn somewhere in there, maybe two or three hours away.

There was another ewe ready to labor. Margaret moved toward her automatically, and Thomas moved with her—a unit of two now, working in the rhythm they'd been building all night. Push and check and assess and support. Birth and catch and clear and hand off. The endless repetition of it, the fact that you couldn't stop and couldn't sleep and couldn't do anything but keep going. This had killed her husband. Not directly, not in any way that showed up on a death certificate. The certificate said it was a heart attack, sudden and fatal, while he was out in the east pasture checking on the fence line. A man who'd checked a fence line maybe ten thousand times in his life, and on one ordinary day in September, his heart had decided it was done.

But Margaret had always suspected that the real cause was this—the relentlessness, the way the work consumed everything and gave back only the necessity of continuing. The way you couldn't imagine a life without it, and the way that knowing you'd never stop doing it for as long as you lived was the kind of thing that could break something in a person's chest.

The ewe pushing now—her name was Claire, and she was one of the good mothers—delivered her lamb without complication. A ewe lamb again, big and healthy, already bleating with that quality of outrage. Thomas took it and did the work while Margaret helped Claire to her feet, making sure she could stand, making sure the connections between mother and infant were forming the way they should.

"How many is that?" Thomas asked.

"Seventeen."

"And we only lost—"

"Two from last season. I know." Margaret hadn't needed to count. She knew exactly how many she'd lost, which ones, why. She could have drawn you a map of grief that was very specific to sheep. "You should sleep. A few hours anyway."

"So should you."

"I'll sleep later." She wouldn't, though. She'd learned in September that sleep was optional, that a person could function on almost nothing if they needed to. The body was more forgiving than you'd think, as long as you didn't ask it to feel anything.

Thomas didn't argue. He settled into the corner where the single lambs were, the living ones and the ones that would probably have to be brought inside soon. He pulled Robert's jacket around him and closed his eyes, and Margaret looked at her son's face in the dim light of the heat lamp and saw all of it at once—the child he'd been, the man he was becoming, the choices that were probably already being made whether he realized it or not.

Another ewe came into labor as the sky outside shifted from pure black to the slightly less pure black of pre-dawn. Margaret worked, moving from one mother to the next, her hands remembering what her brain was too tired to teach them. She'd brought eighty-three lambs into the world so far this season. Last season she and Robert had brought in one hundred and two. The year before that had been ninety-seven. Numbers that meant something, that represented money and food and continuation, or at least they would have, if the farm had been the kind of place that continued rather than simply persisted.

The eighteenth lamb came around five, with the first real gray of morning light starting to show at the edges of the windows. Thomas stirred and woke, or maybe he hadn't actually been sleeping at all. He looked at Margaret with something in his expression that was hard to read—that same wondering from before, but deeper now, or maybe just more honest.

"We made it," he said.

"Until the morning," Margaret agreed. "Yes."

"I mean through the night. We made it."

She understood what he was saying. They'd done the work. They'd kept the animals alive and birthed the lambs and managed all the small crises that arose from the fact of living things in a world that wasn't always kind. They'd done it without Robert, which was something they hadn't known if they could do.

"We did," Margaret said, and she sat down in the straw beside her son, exhausted and aching and more alive than she'd felt in months. "We did make it."

Outside, the wind had dropped to something that was merely fierce rather than apocalyptic. The snow was still falling, but more gently now, with something almost peaceful in the way it accumulated. The power lines were still down—Margaret could see that from the dark house in the distance—but there was a quality of light that suggested the rest of the world was still turning, still existing, still out there waiting for them to figure out what came next.

Thomas leaned his head against her shoulder, and Margaret put her arm around him, and they sat in the lambing shed as the day broke slowly into being, surrounded by the small warm bodies of the future they hadn't asked for and couldn't seem to stop building, one difficult birth at a time.
