Even with the relative safety of his skin, solitary traveling required prudence.

The trail narrowed as it wound through thickening forest, and Jacob slowed Regina to a walk. The canopy closed overhead, filtering the coastal brightness into scattered coin-sized patches that moved across his shoulders as the horse picked her way through roots and stones. He had come to Virginia with purpose, though the purpose had shifted several times during the voyage and the fog-bound arrival. Business, yes. But also something less definable—a testing of fortune, perhaps, or a need to put distance between himself and the settled questions of his northern life.

He had been riding for perhaps an hour when he heard the sound: not voices, but a single voice, high and thin, singing or keening. Regina's ears pricked forward. Jacob's hand went to the knife at his belt, though he knew well enough that a knife was poor defense against whatever might emerge from these woods. The singing continued, wordless and strange, and he saw through a gap in the trees a figure moving perpendicular to the trail—a woman, by the shape of her, dragging something. He decided to announce himself before she decided he was threat.

"Good day," he called out, his own voice strange after so much silence. "I mean no harm. I'm traveling to the settlement."

The woman stopped. The singing died. She turned toward him, and in the dappled light Jacob could see she was old, her face creased as a walnut, her movements stiff. What she had been dragging was a bundle of cloth tied with twine.

"You lost?" she asked.

"Not lost. Traveling through on business."

"Business." She said it like a foreign word. "What kind of business comes through here on horseback in the middle of a Tuesday?"

"The kind that requires finding people and making arrangements." He had not dismounted. The horse seemed calm enough, but he preferred the advantage of height. "I'm looking for a man named Vaark. Jacob Vaark. Might you know of him?"

The old woman's face creased further, her eyes narrowing to slits. "You are Vaark?"

"I am."

"Then you're looking for yourself on a horse." She picked up her bundle again, tucking it against her chest. "The man I knew named Vaark came through here six years back. Bought land then. Land and other things." She paused, studying him. "You look like him some. Mouth's the same. But Vaark I knew was younger or he was happier. Can't say which."

Jacob felt something shift in his chest. Six years. He had been in the north six years. His uncle had died nine months past, leaving him the business in New Amsterdam and a letter of introduction to contacts in Virginia—men who had known his uncle in younger days, when he too had traveled, bought, sold, speculated. The letter had mentioned land possibilities. He had meant to come sooner, but the grief and the work had kept him occupied, and by the time he had organized things properly, six years had vanished like that fog on the bay.

"What was his business?" Jacob asked. "The Vaark you knew."

The woman shifted the bundle. "He bought a woman. An old woman, which seemed foolish to me, but he paid in currency and deeds, so nobody stopped him. Then he bought land and he built on it. Built it up some. Last I heard he'd acquired a wife, a proper one, and they had a child or two between them. Stayed for a few years then moved north like so many do. Or died. Could've died. Hard to keep track in these times." She squinted at him again. "You planning to take over his business, or you just his ghost, come back to haunt the place?"

"Neither one, perhaps. I'm here because I have capital to invest and contacts who thought this region might be profitable."

"It's profitable for the gentry," the woman said. "For the rest of us it's just work." She shifted the bundle again. "I'm going to the settlement myself, if you're minded for company. Place gets strange when you're alone in it at night, and I'd as soon ride on the edge of a horse's shadow as walk alone these days."

Jacob nodded and brought Regina to a stop. The woman approached slowly, cautiously, as though she expected the horse to bolt. She was smaller than he'd thought, bent with age or labor or both, her clothes a patchwork of mending. He reached down to help her up, and she took his arm with a grip that surprised him with its strength.

She settled herself behind him, still clutching her bundle, and they moved forward at a walk.

"What's your name?" Jacob asked.

"Sorrow," she said. "That's what they call me now. My given name is longer and uglier. Sorrow does well enough."

"And the bundle?"

"Provisions. Medicine. Things needed in the settlement. I trade them for copper and corn and sometimes rum. I get by."

They rode in silence for a time. The forest opened gradually as they approached the settlement, and Jacob began to see signs of human activity—cleared land, a burned field lying fallow, the skeleton of a structure half-built and abandoned. When they emerged fully into open ground, the settlement revealed itself: a loose collection of buildings of varying sizes and states of decay, arranged without apparent design along a river. Perhaps two dozen structures, all told. Smoke rose from several chimneys. A woman was drawing water at a well. A man was mending a fence.

The ordinary poverty of the frontier, Jacob thought. The ordinary struggle against wilderness and weather and the simple difficulty of staying alive.

Sorrow directed him to a house set slightly apart from the others, a modest wooden structure with a garden running to seed beside it. He helped her down from the horse, and she touched his arm briefly.

"You'll find what you need here," she said. "Either what you came for or something that looks like it. That's how it works. The wanting and the finding are different things, but they wear the same face."

Jacob watched her shuffle away toward the main settlement, her bundle still clutched tight. He wondered what she had meant, or if she meant anything at all, or if the isolation of this place made sense into something stranger than logic.

The house was dark inside, windows few and small. He knocked on the door and heard movement—slow, hesitant. A woman opened it, younger than Sorrow but old enough to show the work of years, her face marked by sun and labor and something else that Jacob could not quite read. A scar ran from her left ear down her neck, thin and white and old.

"Yes?" she said.

"I'm looking for someone who might know about land sales. Someone named Vaark, or who knew him. I was told I might find information here."

The woman's expression did not change, but her hand tightened on the door frame. "Vaark?"

"Jacob Vaark. I'm his—" He paused. Cousin felt inadequate. Heir felt presumptuous. "I'm seeking him, or seeking what became of his interests in this region. I'm involved in trade and land speculation in the north, and I've been left information suggesting he established himself here."

"He did," the woman said. "Six years ago. He's not here now."

"Where did he go?"

"North. Or that's what was said." She opened the door wider, though not as an invitation. "I'm Rebekah. I was—" She hesitated. "I was in his employ for a time. He's dead now, if you must know. Died three years back of a fever. Or so the word came. His widow lives to the north somewhere, and there's a child, though I never saw the boy myself."

Jacob absorbed this. His uncle's letter was nine months old. If Vaark had died three years back, the letter could not have come from him, though it had been directed in his hand, or a hand so similar that the lawyers had authenticated it. He said nothing of this.

"The land he acquired," Jacob said. "What happened to it?"

"Passes to widow, I suppose. Or was sold. I don't know the particulars of his business, and I wouldn't speak them if I did. It's not my place, and the dead should be left to rest without the living picking over their affairs." She began to close the door.

"Wait," Jacob said. "I mean you no disrespect. I've come a long way on what might be a false promise. If you could tell me anything about him—what he was like, what his intentions were—I would be grateful. It might help me understand why I was contacted."

Rebekah studied him for a long moment, and Jacob saw something flicker across her face—pity, perhaps, or recognition of something she understood better than he did.

"He was a practical man," she said at last. "Hard-working and honest by the lights of this place, which means he was honest when he could afford to be and practical when he couldn't. He bought land because land was cheap and would be valuable. He bought people because that was how business was conducted. And he built something—a house, a holding, a future. Whether it was a good thing or not wasn't a question that occurred to him, I think. He was just doing what men like him do. What men like you probably do too."

"And what's that?" Jacob asked.

"What you can," Rebekah said, and shut the door softly but firmly.

Jacob stood in the fading afternoon light, holding Regina's reins, conscious of how little he had learned and how much had been suggested. He needed lodging, food, rest. He needed to understand what exactly he had inherited from his uncle's letter and his uncle's mysterious contacts. And he needed, though he did not yet know it, to understand what Rebekah meant by her final words, spoken with a resignation that suggested she had made peace with something he had not yet begun to comprehend.

A man emerged from one of the larger buildings—an inn, by the look of it. He was broad-shouldered, red-faced, with the bluff manner of someone accustomed to authority without subtlety.

"You need a room?" the man called out.

Jacob turned toward him. "I do. And a stable for my horse."

"We can provide both. Cost you tobacco or coin. Or goods if you have them to trade." The man came closer, studying Jacob with the same frank assessment Jacob had received from Rebekah and Sorrow. "You family to Vaark? You have his look about you."

"Some relation. You knew him?"

"Knew of him. Decent man, for an outsider. Ambitious. Came here with nothing and left with property and a wife and a child, which is more than most of us manage. But the fever got him like it gets everyone eventually." The man extended a hand. "I'm Hawkins. I keep this place."

Jacob shook it and followed him inside, where a fire burned in the hearth and the smell of cooking meat suggested at least one more meal before sleep. Other men were scattered about—laborers, by the look of them, and one well-dressed man who might have been a factor or an overseer for one of the plantations. The well-dressed man watched Jacob with interest as Hawkins led him to a small room off the main space.

The room was barely furnished—a bed, a small table, a chamber pot—but it was clean and dry. Hawkins quoted a price in tobacco, Jacob agreed, and within an hour he had been fed a stew of uncertain meat and vegetables, given water to wash, and left to himself as evening deepened into night.

Sleep did not come easily. Jacob lay in the dark, listening to the sounds of the settlement settling into night—voices, the barking of a dog, the wind in the eaves, the rustling of things in the walls. He thought of Rebekah's face, marked by that old scar. He thought of Sorrow's cryptic words. He thought of his uncle, whom he had barely known, and the letter that had drawn him here, and the possibility that what he had come to inherit was not land or business but something more complicated—a debt, perhaps, or a question that had no answer, or an understanding of what it meant to build something in a place where the only currency was struggle and the only law was what the strong could enforce.

Somewhere in the settlement, in the darkness beyond his window, Rebekah was sleeping, and Sorrow was sleeping, and whoever had been the first Vaark, the one who had bought land and built a future and then died of fever before he could see what he had made. And Jacob, who had traveled across an ocean to inherit something undefined, lay awake wondering what exactly he had come to possess.
