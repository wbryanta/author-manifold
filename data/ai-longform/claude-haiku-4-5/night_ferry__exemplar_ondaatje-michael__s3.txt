# CROSSING

The man named Victor sat in the ferry's observation lounge with his forehead against the cold window, watching the dark water accumulate beneath them like a liquid history he could not quite read. The vessel moved through the night with a kind of reluctant purpose, its engines a steady percussion that seemed to belong more to the body than to the machinery itself, a heartbeat enlarged and amplified across several decks. Around him other passengers slept in awkward postures, their heads tilted against seat backs or window frames, their breathing synchronized with the ship's rhythm as if they too had become part of its internal mechanics. He had not slept. He had purchased a ticket eleven hours ago in response to a telephone call from someone whose name he had immediately forgotten, though he had written it down on the back of an envelope and then thrown the envelope away without looking at it again.

The voice on the telephone had said: We believe we have identified the body recovered three days ago near Bathurst Inlet. There are distinctive markings. We need you to come. Are you family? Yes, he had said, though the word felt provisional, something borrowed from another man's language. A brother, he had added, and the voice had paused then, a pause that suggested the person on the other end was consulting something, a file perhaps, or a photograph, or simply gathering the appropriate tone with which to deliver bad news to a relative of an unidentified corpse.

The northern lights had appeared at some point during the crossing—he was not certain when. He looked up from his hands and they were simply there, the sky fractured into ribbons of green and white, trembling across the darkness like something alive, like phosphorescence stirred in deep water. A woman across from him had awakened and was watching too, her reflection ghostly in the window beside the actual aurora, as if she existed in two states simultaneously—the interior and the exterior, the woman awake in a ferry and the woman suspended in the sky. Their eyes met briefly in the glass and then she looked away, understanding perhaps that he did not want conversation, that whatever he was traveling toward required a certain silence, a preservation of whatever internal architecture still held him upright.

His brother—if it was his brother—had been missing for seventeen months. The last time Victor had seen him was in a diner on Bloor Street in Toronto, a place now demolished, replaced by a building that looked like all other buildings in that neighborhood, anonymous and provisional. They had not spoken in the three years before that meeting, and when Daniel slid into the booth across from him, Victor had not immediately recognized him. The face was older, not merely aged but changed by something the passage of time alone could not account for. There was a new scar at the corner of his eye, and his hands, which Victor remembered as precise and careful, moved with a kind of restlessness, always reaching for something that was not there—a glass, a napkin, the edge of the table. His brother had ordered tea and had not drunk it. He had talked for forty minutes, his voice moving rapidly through a landscape of recent events—a job that had not worked out, a woman who had not called back, a decision to move north, to the territories, where a friend had told him there was work on pipeline surveys, where a man could disappear into the landscape and be remade by it. Victor had listened and had said nothing of consequence. When Daniel left, he had not embraced his brother or even reached across the table. He had simply nodded and watched him navigate between tables toward the door, where he paused and turned back for a moment, as if he had forgotten something crucial to say, and then he was gone.

The letter had arrived six months later, postmarked from Yellowknife. It contained no greeting and no signature. It read: I saw a moose in the river this morning, standing water-high, its face turned away from me. I stood on the bank and neither of us moved. This is the kind of country where a man can stand very still and nobody comes looking. I am still here. I will be here. That was all. Victor had kept the letter in a book for three years without reading it again, and then one day it was not there. He had not thrown it away; he understood that somehow it had simply exhausted its purpose and become unnecessary, the way certain objects in a room eventually cease to exist functionally and are no longer really seen.

The ferry was beginning its descent into the strait. He could feel it in the way the vessel's motion changed, becoming less rhythmic and more negotiated, as if the captain was making decisions about how to proceed through these particular waters. The dark was absolute now, the sky and water indistinguishable, and the lights of the ship seemed insignificant in the vastness, a brief human assertion against the indifference of the north. Victor stood and moved through the lounge toward the door. A man in a uniform—security, perhaps, or a crew member—glanced at him but did not speak. He climbed stairs that curved upward in the ship's interior, passing doors marked Private and Authorized Personnel Only, until he reached the upper deck. The wind there was a shock, aggressive and cold, smelling of ice and distance. There were no lights from shore, no indication that anywhere existed beyond this vessel and the water it moved through.

He thought of his brother's hands, their nervous motion, the way Daniel had always needed to be in transit, mentally at least, already departed from wherever his body was located. They had shared a room as children, and Victor remembered his brother lying awake at night, staring at the ceiling, his body tense as if at any moment he might bolt upright and leave the house entirely, run into some other life that was waiting for him. Victor had been the still one, the one who could lie in darkness without reaching for anything, without needing anything. They had not been close as children, only co-located, sharing space in the way siblings do without necessarily belonging to each other. Their father had died when Victor was sixteen and Daniel fourteen. Their mother had remarried and moved to Ottawa with her new husband, leaving the boys in the care of their grandmother in Kingston. This is where the distance between them had become absolute, not because they actively avoided each other but because the avoidance had become so practiced that it had calcified into something resembling the truth—the truth that they had never been anything but strangers sharing a history.

The strait was widening. The ferry had turned and was now moving toward the port, though Victor could not yet see the lights of the town. He tried to imagine the body that was waiting for him, the way they had described it to the voice on the telephone: recovered in shallow water, waterlogged, difficult to date with precision, three days or three weeks or three months in the water, they could not say. What they had said was: distinctive markings. His brother had a birthmark on his left shoulder blade, shaped like a leaf, the color of spilled wine. Victor had seen it countless times—in childhood when they had bathed together, in adolescence in the locker room at school, and in that final meeting at the diner when Daniel had turned slightly to flag down the waitress and Victor had glimpsed it at the edge of his collar, faded with age but still there, still legible.

A woman came onto the deck, wrapped in a heavy coat, her face pale in the starlight. She looked out at the darkness for a moment and then seemed to remember something that upset her, and she turned and went back inside. Victor remained, his hands gripping the railing, the metal so cold it seemed to burn. He could feel the ferry slowing, making its final approach. The engines' rhythm changed, becoming urgent and then deliberate, controlled. Lights appeared then, the first indication of human habitation on the shore—scattered, sparse, as if the town was reluctant to announce its own existence. He could make out the shape of a dock, a building or two, vehicles waiting. A figure moved on the dock, a man in an official capacity, orange vest over dark clothes, preparing the ropes. The ferry's horn sounded, a long note that seemed to ask a question no one would answer.

Victor had brought nothing with him but his wallet and a small bag containing a change of clothes and the telephone number written on a slip of paper, though he had memorized it by now and the paper was merely a talisman, proof that this was real, that he had actually come. He descended back through the ship's interior, moving against the current of passengers now rising, gathering their belongings, preparing to disembark. A child bumped into him without apologizing, and her mother did not correct her. Everyone was moving toward what they expected to happen next, and he was moving toward something else entirely, toward the moment of opening a drawer or looking at a photograph or seeing a face drained of all blood and expression, toward the moment of saying yes or no, this is him or this is not, or perhaps saying nothing at all, simply nodding, simply confirming that he understood what he was being shown.

The gangway descended with mechanical precision. The night air rushed in, so cold it seemed to have texture, to have weight. He gathered his bag and joined the line of disembarking passengers, moving down the incline toward the waiting official whose face was obscured in shadow. The man had a clipboard and was checking people off, verifying something. When Victor reached him, he said: Victor Bellamy?

Yes.

Come with me, the man said, and began walking. Victor followed, his footsteps hollow on the dock, the ferry releasing its cargo of travelers around them, dispersing into the darkness toward whatever destinations they had arranged. The man said: They're holding it at the hospital. Dr. Chen can see you this evening if you're ready, or you can wait until morning. Most people want to wait until morning. You look tired. The drive is not long. Where are you from?

Toronto, Victor said.

Long way, the man said. They should have told you that you don't have to come, you know. Relatives often don't. They make the identification through photographs, or they wait for DNA results. Nobody comes in person anymore if they can avoid it.

Victor did not respond. He got into the truck that was waiting, the official taking the driver's seat, starting the engine. The heater came on immediately, releasing the smell of diesel fuel and something else, something animal-like and faint. They drove through the town, which was smaller than Victor had imagined, the houses set apart from each other, each one isolated in a way that seemed deliberate, as if proximity was something to be avoided. The official did not attempt further conversation. He drove with the radio on very low, something classical, something that seemed incongruous in the north, a piano piece that sounded like it was being played underwater.

The hospital was a small building with large windows and a covered entrance. They parked in a lot that was nearly empty. The official said: My name is Patterson, by the way. I should have said that earlier. You want to go in now?

Yes, Victor said, though his hand remained on the door handle, not yet opening it.

It's not as bad as you think, Patterson said. Whatever you're imagining right now, the reality will be better. It usually is. People are resilient. Even the dead. Especially the dead.

They went inside. The fluorescent lights were harsh and white, almost painful after the darkness. A woman in scrubs appeared, someone Dr. Chen presumably, though she did not introduce herself. She led them down a corridor lined with doors, their footsteps loud in the quiet building. At one point they passed a room where a man sat alone in a waiting area, his head in his hands, and Victor wondered if this man was also identifying someone, also carrying the burden of that particular knowledge. They descended a staircase. The air became cooler, more neutral, stripped of the institutional smell that had characterized the upper floors. Dr. Chen stopped before a heavy door and looked at Victor.

Are you ready?

He nodded. He was not ready. He would never be ready. But he had come all this distance and had slept through the ferry's night crossing, and he had stood on the deck and watched the northern lights, and he had descended the gangway and climbed into Patterson's truck, and he had driven through this town that looked like nowhere, and there was no point now in being unready. Dr. Chen opened the door.

The body was on a table, covered with a sheet that was pulled down to the mid-chest. The face was composed, arranged, the eyes closed as if in sleep. The skin had a waxy quality, preserved, and the color was wrong, grayish, the pigmentation altered by time and water and whatever chemical processes had occurred in the interval between death and this moment. The scar was still visible, a thin line at the corner of the eye, distinctive, particular, belonging only to one person. Victor stepped closer. The left shoulder was exposed, and he could see the birthmark, dark against the pale skin, shaped like a leaf, spilled wine. It was his brother. It had always been his brother.

He was aware of Dr. Chen waiting for him to speak, to confirm, to provide whatever verbal acknowledgment was required. But he said nothing. He looked at his brother's hands, resting on top of the sheet, and he thought of those hands in the diner, nervous and reaching, and he thought of those hands at thirteen years old, identical to his own, holding a shovel while they buried their dog in the yard behind their grandmother's house, and he thought of those hands as they must have been in the water, flailing or still, terrified or resigned. He did not touch the body. He did not speak. He simply stood there, maintaining witness, until Dr. Chen gently covered the face again, and the body became a shape beneath a sheet, no longer readable, no longer specific, only a fact to be recorded and filed and eventually forgotten.

Outside, in the hallway, Patterson was waiting with forms. Victor signed them without reading what he was signing. The doctor said something about details, about how the body had been recovered, about the condition and the time frame, but Victor did not really hear her. He was back in the diner, thirty years in the past or just three years, depending on how time was being counted. His brother was turning, was saying something, was leaving. The door was closing. The moment was disappearing even as it occurred, even as he sat there watching it go. This was what it had always been—a man becoming smaller and smaller until he was gone entirely, until he was just a shape moving away through traffic, just a figure dissolving into the crowd, just another person who had decided that distance was preferable to proximity, that the north was preferable to the south, that the water was preferable to the land, that absence was preferable to presence.

He was back in Patterson's truck. They were driving through the town again. The man was saying something about staying at the hotel, about there being paperwork to finish tomorrow, about the preliminary investigation into the cause of death. Victor stared out the window and saw lights in houses, people moving behind windows, families occupying the same spaces, existing together without apparent friction. He wondered if anyone had taught them how to do this, or if it had simply come naturally, some form of grace extended only to other people, withheld from brothers, from men like him and Daniel, who could not figure out how to stand in the same room without standing on opposite sides of it.

The hotel was small and warm. Patterson paid for the room with a credit card that belonged to someone else—the government, the municipality, the state. The clerk handed Victor a key attached to a heavy plastic fob. The room was clean and impersonal, the kind of room that could be any room, anywhere, and this is what Victor appreciated about it. He lay on the bed without undressing, and he listened to the building settle around him, listened to the wind against the windows, listened to the vast silence of the north pressing in from all sides. Somewhere in this darkness, his brother was lying on a table. Somewhere in this town or this region or this country, there were answers to how he had come to be in that water, how he had remained there, how he had been found. But these answers would not matter. The body would be released to him or to the state. There would be a funeral or a cremation. There would be a ceremony or the absence of one. And then his brother would be gone again, still, finally, and Victor would return to Toronto and resume his life, and this crossing would become memory, would become story, would become the kind of thing that happened to someone else, long ago, in a place that existed only at the very edge of the map, if it existed at all.
