# Lookout

The coffee had gone tepid an hour ago, and Margaret had stopped pretending she might drink it. She set the mug on the shelf beside the binoculars, where it joined three others in various stages of ceramic abandonment. The morning stretched empty before her—that peculiar emptiness of high places where the sky takes up most of the world. She was alone, as always, and as always she didn't mind.

The tower stood at nine thousand feet, a skeletal structure of bolted steel that swayed almost imperceptibly in the wind, enough that you felt it in your bones after a while. Margaret had been stationed here for twenty-three summers, arriving each June with her supplies and her routines, leaving each September when the snow began and the fire season ended. This was the last one. The Forest Service was decommissioning the tower next month, phasing out the old lookouts in favor of satellite imagery and drone surveillance and all the other efficient, invisible methods that rendered obsolete what had once been necessary. She had received the notification in March, a polite letter explaining the transition, offering her a position at the district office if she wanted it. She had not responded.

From her current vantage—sitting in the swivel chair before the fire-finding mirrors, the Osborne Firefinder fixed in place—Margaret could see approximately thirty miles in any direction. The valleys fell away in pleats of green, interrupted by the gray scars of old burns. To the east, the larger peaks still held patches of snow in their shadowed gullies. To the west, the land descrambled itself into foothills and then into the flatness of the great interior valley, invisible behind the haze that had begun accumulating in late July. Behind her, north, the reservoir caught the sun like a dropped mirror.

It was August third. The temperature had climbed to ninety-two yesterday, which was hot for this elevation. The fire danger index was listed as moderate to high, and she'd filed her morning report at eight o'clock as she did every morning, speaking to Tom at the ranger station below, describing the weather conditions and confirming her equipment. Tom's voice came through the radio sounding tired. They'd already responded to seven fires this season, and it was still early.

Margaret stood and moved to the windows that ran along all four sides of the cab. The binoculars hung from a leather strap around her neck—she'd worn them so long the strap had worn a permanent groove into her shoulders. She raised them and began her systematic scan, starting from the north and moving clockwise: the ridgeline above Granite Lake, the aspens below Snowslide Peak, the chaparral of the south-facing slopes, the complex topography of the Brandy drainage, the mixed forest of the western quadrant.

It was during this routine pass, when she was studying the Brandy drainage—a zone she'd scanned hundreds of times without incident—that she saw it.

Smoke.

Not the thin, dispersed wisp of a far-off distant fire, the kind she could identify in seconds and pinpoint on the map. This was thicker, whiter, boiling up from a specific location near the canyon floor. Margaret's heart rate increased in the way it did in those early moments of detection, before anything was certain, when adrenaline jumped ahead of information.

She lowered the binoculars and located the drainage with her naked eye, orienting herself with the peaks. Brandy Creek ran through there, yes. There was a clearing, maybe two acres. Her mind had already begun its work without her permission, cross-referencing geography, consulting the mental maps she'd built through decades of observation.

The cabin.

Katherine's cabin. She hadn't let herself think about Katherine's cabin in some years, though she hadn't forgotten its location. The structure had been there since the late nineties, when her sister had purchased the property through some real estate contact and built the small house with the help of friends and hired labor. Margaret had never been inside it. She'd seen it once, very briefly, from a distance, soon after construction was completed, when her own position at the lookout had still been new enough that she'd catalogued everything in the landscape, including the facts of other people's habitations. Katherine had moved out there after the divorce, making a life that was separate from the family entirely, which had suited Margaret fine.

They hadn't spoken in eight years.

Margaret picked up the radio handset, her reflexive action, and tried the frequency. "Ranger Station, this is Tower One. I have visual on a possible structure fire in the Brandy drainage, approximately three miles north of the confluence."

Static.

She tried again. "Tower One calling Ranger Station. Do you copy?"

The static held, blank and hollow.

She replaced the handset and checked the frequency dial. Seven-five. Correct. She tried the volume—it was already at maximum. The red indicator light that should have shown operational status was dark.

Dead.

Margaret felt a peculiar suspension occur in her chest, as if time had stuttered. She checked the battery connections, though she knew they were fine. They were always fine. She maintained this equipment with the precision of someone who understood that small negligences could cascade into large tragedies. The radio had worked at eight o'clock when she filed her morning report. That was less than three hours ago.

She moved to the emergency radio, the backup unit that hung on the wall behind the cot. This one ran on batteries. She switched it on and waited. Nothing. The batteries were new—she'd installed them herself two weeks ago.

Two radios, both defunct, both at the same moment. The probability of simultaneous failure required such an intricate convergence of circumstances that Margaret's mind rejected it as impossible, which meant that impossible was what had happened, or something else had happened that she wasn't immediately understanding.

She turned back to the windows. The smoke column was still rising, perhaps a touch thicker than it had been. She raised the binoculars again, trying to see more detail. The smoke was white-gray, the color that usually indicated wood and normal combustible materials rather than the black of chemical fires or accelerant-based blazes. That was something, at least. That was a single fact that was slightly less terrible than some other facts might be.

The road down was eight miles, switchbacking through increasingly dense forest, marked with signs warning of the grade and the hairpin turns. It took roughly fifty minutes in good conditions, sixty if you were being cautious, which Margaret always was. The tower key was in the drawer beneath the Firefinder. Her truck was parked in the small dirt lot at the trailhead, three hundred vertical feet below the cab. She could hike down there in forty minutes. The truck would take her to the ranger station where Tom was, and Tom could alert the hotshot crews and the incident commander, and they could send resources to the drainage.

But Katherine didn't call the authorities. This was something Margaret knew about her sister, though not from recent conversation. It was old knowledge, the residual wisdom of anyone who lived with a person long enough to understand their temperament. Katherine avoided institutions, official channels, regulatory bodies. She avoided the kinds of people who wore uniforms and carried clipboards. She had built her life in the Brandy drainage specifically to be far from oversight.

So the fire in the Brandy drainage was Katherine's fire, almost certainly. And Katherine would not want it reported.

Margaret sat down in the swivel chair. She was aware of the deliberateness of this action, the weight of it. She sat down in the chair that faced the mountains and the smoke, and she made the decision to stay.

The thing about living in the sky for this many years was that you learned to see weather patterns, jet streams, the movement of pressure systems across the continent. You learned the subtle signs that preceded changes. You learned that the air itself could be read like text, if you understood the language. That afternoon, the wind shifted. The column of smoke tilted slightly toward the west, which meant the fire was pushing in that direction, away from the nearest road access, away from the most obvious avenues of intervention.

This could have meant something. It could have meant that it would spread into the wild country where fires sometimes burned themselves out, found their own equilibrium with the landscape. Or it could have meant that it would follow the drainage upward, where it could crest the ridgeline and drop into the next valley, where other properties existed, where lives other than Katherine's might be at stake.

Margaret didn't know. She stood at the windows and watched the smoke continue its steady production, and she didn't know.

By six in the evening, the smoke column had darkened and expanded. It was visible now without binoculars, a substantial disturbance in the atmosphere. Margaret had spent the afternoon checking and rechecking the radios, performing tests that confirmed what she already understood: they were both beyond her capacity to repair. She had written down the time of detection and the coordinates, the bearing and distance, the estimated intensity. She had filled in the lookout log as if she were reporting it, as if the written record itself were sufficient to create some kind of accountability.

The kitchen in the tower cab was minimal—a camping stove, a propane canister, a collection of canned goods and dried foods. Margaret prepared a dinner she didn't taste and then stood on the small platform that ringed the tower, the space between the cab and the railing that prevented people from stepping off into nothing. The evening light had taken on a quality that was almost apocalyptic, the smoke turning the sunset orange and deep red, colors that belonged in older paintings of older catastrophes.

She could still go down. She could still hike to the truck, drive to the ranger station, report the fire even if it meant Katherine's anger, Katherine's righteous fury about the loss of her privacy and her property. She could choose to be the responsible party, the one who did the right thing regardless of consequence. There had been a time in her life when she'd practiced this kind of virtue regularly, when she'd believed in its necessity.

But that was before the years had accumulated. Before she'd understood that responsibility was sometimes a house you built that eventually burned down anyway. Before Katherine had stopped calling, and then before Margaret had stopped expecting her to.

They had been close, once. Not as children particularly—their mother had worked long hours as a nurse, and their father had been the version of men who were present in body but not in any functional way. But as teenagers, almost-adults, they'd formed an alliance. They'd shared a bedroom because their house was small, shared confidences because their circumstances demanded it. Katherine had been the dreamer, the one who imagined elaborate futures for herself. Margaret had been the practical one, the one who made lists and organized systems. It had seemed, then, that they would always be necessary to each other, the way different weights on a scale are necessary to find equilibrium.

When Margaret had taken the lookout position at twenty-eight, Katherine had thought it was romantic. She'd visited twice, climbed the tower, stood in the cab and marveled at the view. It had been before the divorce, before Katherine had become strange in ways that Margaret couldn't quite accommodate. Before Margaret had become, in her sister's assessment, obsessed with work, with routine, with the kinds of things that prevented you from actually living.

The final conversation they'd had was about something small, some comment Margaret had made about Katherine's choices that had been sharper than intended. The response had been sharp in turn. Neither of them had tried to soften it afterward. The silence that followed had eventually calcified into something that looked permanent.

That was eight years ago.

At dusk, Margaret saw a vehicle moving on the fire road below the drainage. It was moving fast, too fast for that kind of terrain, and it kicked up clouds of dust that were briefly visible through the haze of smoke. The vehicle pulled into the clearing at the Brandy Creek trailhead and stopped. A figure emerged and began running toward the drainage, toward the fire, carrying what looked like a bucket or a large container.

That would be Katherine. Or more precisely, that would be Katherine continuing to do what Katherine had always done, which was to believe that effort and will were sufficient to solve problems, that running toward a fire was better than running away from it.

Margaret went back inside the cab and sat in the dark. She could turn on the lights—the tower had a generator, fuel enough for weeks—but she didn't. She sat in the gathering darkness and listened to the wind move through the frame of the structure, listened to the creak of bolted steel that had been creaking for decades and would creak for a few more decades until the Forest Service dismantled it and hauled it down and sold it for scrap.

She thought about Katherine running toward her own fire.

She thought about all the summers she'd spent in this tower, watching for fires that were someone else's disaster, reporting them to people whose job it was to manage them, removing herself from the chain of consequence. She'd built her life around the principle of detection and delegation. Report what you see; let others act. It was a form of virtue, she'd always believed, or at least a form of honesty. You couldn't save everything, couldn't prevent every disaster. The best you could do was see clearly and speak truly.

But this was Katherine. And her radio was dead.

At eight p.m., Margaret took the tower key and the emergency pack she kept in the bottom drawer. She checked the propane, switched off the generator, secured the windows. The process took ten minutes. Then she began the descent.

The trail was rocky and steep, a scramble that required attention. Her knees protested by the halfway point—she was sixty-one years old, which was not ancient but was certainly past the age of being careless with descents. She moved carefully, using her hands when necessary, not allowing herself to rush despite the urgency that pressed at her chest like a second heartbeat.

The truck was where she'd left it, though it seemed impossible that it had been waiting there since dawn, innocent and patient, for this use. The keys were in the ignition. She drove down the forest road too fast and then slowed on the main road, remembering the hazards of panic, the way fear made you stupid.

The ranger station was closed when she arrived—it was full dark by then, past nine o'clock. She left her truck in the lot and went around back to the house where Tom lived with his wife and their daughter. She knocked on the door, her heart still racing, and Tom answered in his shirt sleeves, holding a beer, looking confused at the sight of her.

"The radio went out," she said. "Both of them. I have a fire in the Brandy drainage. Structure involved. I need you to call it in."

Tom's confusion shifted to alarm. He set down the beer and was already reaching for his phone. "When did it start?"

"Around noon. I don't know exactly. I saw the smoke at twelve forty-five."

"Jesus, Margaret. Why didn't you report it?"

She didn't answer, because there was no good answer, and Tom didn't wait for one anyway. He was already dialing, his voice shifting into the formal register of an official emergency report. Margaret stood in his doorway while he talked to the dispatch center, describing the location, the extent, the structure involved. She heard him say her name, heard him specify that she was the reporting party, heard him give coordinates that matched the ones she'd recorded in the log.

When he finished, he looked at her with an expression she couldn't quite read. "Do you want to go back up tonight or wait until morning?"

"I want to go to the drainage," she said.

Tom's wife appeared in the kitchen doorway behind him, holding a coffee mug, watching them. Tom turned to her and then back to Margaret. "I'll drive you," he said.

The fire road to the Brandy drainage was just barely navigable in Tom's truck. It climbed through burned forest—this was an area that had burned six years ago, and the new growth was coming in dense and dark. The headlights pushed through it weakly. They could see the glow of the fire long before they reached the trailhead, a reddish light in the sky where the canyon held the smoke.

When they arrived at the clearing, Katherine's truck was still there, but Katherine herself was not. The fire was perhaps a quarter mile up the drainage, burning on both sides of the creek bed where it had consumed a stand of dead lodgepole pines—trees that had been dying for years, succumbing to bark beetles and drought. It was moving slowly, the flames perhaps ten feet high, feeding on the fuel that had been drying all summer.

Tom immediately got on his radio and began giving updated information to the dispatch center. Margaret walked toward the fire, drawn by its light, pulled by something she didn't entirely understand.

She found Katherine at the edge of the burn area, sitting on a rock, her clothes smoke-stained, her hands blackened with ash. She looked very small in that moment, reduced by exhaustion and fire and circumstance to something more fundamental than Margaret had realized her sister could be.

"I couldn't stop it," Katherine said without looking up.

"No," Margaret agreed. "You couldn't have."

"I was burning some branches, clearing near the cabin. The wind changed. It happened so fast."

Margaret sat down beside her on the neighboring rock. This close, she could feel the heat radiating from the active burn, feel the way it changed the air, made breathing something more deliberate. In the light of the flames, Katherine's face was visible in all its detail—the lines that had accumulated, the gray that had infiltrated her hair, the expression of someone who had just watched a thing she had built begin its process of being unmade.

"The crews are coming," Margaret said. "Tom radioed it in. They'll be here in about an hour."

Katherine turned and looked at her, and for a moment the accumulated years of silence seemed to compress into something that could almost be spoken. Almost, but not quite. Instead Katherine asked, "How did you get here?"

"My radios died," Margaret said. "Both of them. At the same time."

"That's impossible."

"Yes," Margaret agreed. "It is."

They sat together and watched the fire consume what remained to be consumed. The crews arrived with trucks and equipment, and the night became organized and purposeful. They set up a perimeter, cut firebreaks, poured water and retardant on the active fronts. By dawn it was largely contained, controlled by the application of resources and experience and the collective will to prevent it from becoming something catastrophic.

By then Margaret and Katherine had not spoken again, but they had stood together through the hours of darkness, watching what fire does when it's given room to move. When the light began to come up and the helicopters arrived to drop more retardant, Katherine walked away toward her truck without looking back.

Margaret stood with Tom and watched the aftermath, the blackened trees and the steaming ground, the mess of violence and intervention. The cabin, it turned out, was fine. The fire had consumed the forest around it but had stopped before reaching the structure itself, held back by the firebreaks and the retardant and the simple circumstance of geography.

"You should probably get back to the tower," Tom said. "Call in a formal report."

"All right," Margaret said.

"You did good work yesterday. Spotting it early."

She didn't say anything to this. She drove back to the tower in the afternoon, and when she arrived, she found the radio magically functional again, both units working perfectly, as if the morning had never happened. She filed her formal report, speaking to a different ranger than usual, and confirmed the details of what had occurred.

Then she sat in the swivel chair and looked at the landscape, which had been subtly altered by fire and rescue and the interference of human effort. The burned section of the drainage was visible now, a black scar where green had been. It would heal, in time. Forests were good at that kind of healing, resilient in ways that other things weren't.

She had three more weeks at the tower. She didn't know what Katherine would do—rebuild, sell the property, walk away from it entirely. She didn't know if Katherine would try to call, or if the silence would simply continue, having been tested and found adequate.

The evening light came down on the mountains, and Margaret raised the binoculars and began her systematic scan. The light played across the landscape differently now, coloring everything in shades of ash and ember, the visual afterimage of disruption. She looked at her sister's land, and at all the other land stretching away in every direction, the forests and the mountains and the valleys, the whole enormous delicate arrangement that fire touched and people touched and time touched, and which continued nonetheless to exist in all its beautiful, terrible complexity.

She marked the coordinates in her log and filed her evening report. The summer was burning toward its end.
