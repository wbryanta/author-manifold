# June Irrigation

The valve stuck like it always did, and Marcus had to wedge the pipe wrench in at an angle that made his shoulder scream. Twenty-three years he'd been doing this, since he was fourteen, and the valve still fought him as though it were the first time.

"You're going to break it," David said from behind him, standing in that way he stood—loose-limbed, detached, like he wasn't quite committed to being present.

"I'm not going to break it."

"You're turning it the wrong direction."

Marcus pulled the wrench free and turned to look at his brother. David stood in pressed khakis and a polo shirt that probably cost more than Marcus spent on groceries in a month. He'd driven up from the city that morning in a car that hadn't existed when David left, five years ago. Everything about him announced that this wasn't his world anymore.

"It's a reverse thread," Marcus said. "Left loosens, right tightens, because of the water pressure. You'd know this if you ever came home."

"I know it. I'm just saying you're going to break it."

Marcus turned back to the valve and re-seated the wrench. The June sun was high and hot, and already his shirt clung to his back. The orchard stretched behind them, the old pear trees heavy with fruit that nobody had properly tended in two years. Some of the branches hung so low they nearly touched the ground, weighted with unpicked abundance. Others, in the sections where the irrigation had failed completely, showed the pale brittleness of drought-stressed wood.

He leaned into the wrench. The valve gave grudgingly, metal groaning against metal. When it finally moved, it did so with a suddenness that made him stumble, his boots sliding in the dust.

"There," David said. "See? You nearly lost it."

"I didn't lose it. It's fine. It's exactly where it needs to be."

They'd installed this line together, Marcus and his father, the summer before their mother died. That was the summer David was still working at the farm, before the scholarship offer came through and everything changed. Marcus could see the two of them, younger versions rendered in memory's uncertain light—his father with the engineer's precision that had never quite left his hands despite thirty years away from his actual profession, and Marcus at thirteen, learning which way the water should flow, learning that his body could do useful things in the world.

David hadn't been there that day. Something about a job interview, or a girl, or both. The details had been lost to time.

"So what's the damage?" David asked. He'd pulled out his phone—not to call anyone, just to check the time, Marcus guessed, or email, or whatever it was he did in the city that made him rich and perpetually distracted. "How much is the whole line going to cost to replace?"

"I don't know yet. I haven't called anyone."

"You should call someone. Get an estimate."

"I'll get an estimate when I'm ready to get an estimate."

They walked the line together, moving north toward where it disappeared into the upper twenty acres. The earth was bone-dry on the surface, with that powdery quality that meant it would require hours of water once they got the line flowing again to really penetrate. David trailed behind, picking his way through the dust in shoes that looked expensive and entirely unsuitable.

The break was where it always was—in the section where the land dipped slightly, where water had a tendency to pool. The line had cracked along its length, a clean fracture that probably dated back weeks, maybe months.

"Jesus," David said, actually looking bothered for the first time. "How long has that been like this?"

"I don't know. A while."

"The trees are dying."

"Yeah," Marcus said. "They are."

It was a bad year, though "year" was generous—it was turning into a bad stretch, two years running of inadequate precipitation and the kind of heat that made the news in the city. David probably saw it there, in whatever climate-controlled office he occupied, a story about drought on a screen. But here it was a different thing. Here it was visual, tangible—trees that had been producing for thirty years beginning to show their age in ways that suggested they wouldn't make it to thirty-one.

"You called Dad, right? About all this?"

"Once or twice. He doesn't really want to talk about it."

"Because he knows the place isn't viable anymore."

Marcus turned on him. "Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Don't come up here for five hours and start making declarations about what's viable and what isn't."

David held up his hands, mock-placating. "I'm just saying what we're both thinking."

"I'm not thinking that."

"Marcus—"

"I'm not."

They stood in the dust, the broken irrigation line between them like a physical argument made manifest. Somewhere beyond the property line, a truck passed on the county road, the sound distant and strange in the afternoon heat.

"It's five years," David said more quietly. "Since I've lived here. And in those five years, I've watched you and Dad pour money into keeping this place going that you don't have. I've watched the trees get older and less productive and harder to manage. And I look at what this land would be worth if we sold it—"

"To developers."

"Maybe. Probably. But it would be worth something. Instead, you're out here patching irrigation lines that were half-installed in 1998."

"1997," Marcus said. "And they work."

"They don't work."

"They worked until three months ago."

"And now they don't, and they'll cost money to fix, and next year something else will break, and it'll cost more money, and the pears will still get smaller and harder to sell, and eventually the math just doesn't work anymore. You know it doesn't. Dad knows it doesn't. Why won't you just say it?"

Marcus crouched beside the broken line, running his fingers along the fracture. The plastic was brittle, degraded by sun and temperature fluctuation. You could see how it had just finally given up, the long slow weakening finally reaching its endpoint.

"Because the moment I say it, it's real," he said. "Right now, if I say the place isn't working, it means I've failed. It means everything Dad did, everything he built, everything he wanted—that was all for nothing."

"That's not true."

"It is. He left engineering to do this. He believed in it. And I'm the one who was supposed to be here, who was supposed to keep it going." He stood, brushed the dust from his hands. "You got to leave. You got to have it be a choice. For me, it was always going to be this."

David put his phone away. For a moment he looked like he had in the old photographs, the ones from before the scholarship—younger, less buttoned-up, less certain of his own trajectory.

"I didn't get to leave," David said. "I had to. If I'd stayed, I would have ended up just like you."

"Like me."

"Yes. Out here in the dust, arguing with an irrigation line about things that have already been decided."

They walked back toward the house, past the orchard proper, past the grove of older trees that had been there when their parents bought the place. The pears hung on every branch, ripening in the heat, thousands and thousands of dollars of fruit that would be smaller and less sweet this year because the trees hadn't had enough water.

The farmhouse sat back from the road, unpainted for five years, looking tired in the way old houses did when they weren't being maintained with the energy they required. Their father was inside—Marcus could see his shape through the kitchen window, sitting at the table where he always sat, probably reading the newspaper or some agricultural digest that came in the mail.

"When was the last time you told him you didn't want to do this anymore?" David asked.

"I haven't."

"Because he'd be relieved."

"He wouldn't."

"He would be. He's tired, Marcus. He looks tired. He's sixty-eight years old and he's tired, and he's been waiting for you to tell him it's okay to stop."

They reached the porch. David stopped before going inside.

"I have an offer," he said. "From a development company. It came through my office three weeks ago. They're interested in the whole property—this land plus the Hendersons' place and maybe the Castillos' if they can work out terms. Residential and commercial mixed use. It's good money, Marcus. It's really good money."

"And you're telling me this now."

"I'm telling you this now because I've already told the developers I'd talk to you. I said I'd come up and see what state things were in, and I'd have a conversation with you about whether it made sense to consider it."

"You want to sell."

"I want us to have a choice about what happens next. Right now we don't have a choice. We're on a treadmill that's speeding up, and one of us is going to fall off eventually. I don't want that person to be you."

"But it's fine if it's Dad."

"Dad knows. He's known for a while. He's the one who—" David stopped, pressed his lips together. "Never mind."

"No, say it. He's the one who what?"

"He's the one who gave permission for the contact. The developers approached him first, actually, about six months ago. He's been thinking about it. He asked me to come up and talk to you."

Marcus turned away from his brother and looked out at the orchard. The light was starting to shift, the sun moving past its apex, the shadows beginning to lengthen. In an hour or two it would be late enough that he could turn the water on—they always irrigated in the evening, letting the water soak in overnight. When the valve worked, anyway. When the lines didn't crack.

"I don't want to sell," he said.

"I know."

"This is the only thing I know how to do."

"That's not true."

"It is. I graduated with a business degree that I never used because I came back here. I've spent thirteen years not using it. I'm thirty-seven years old and I can fix irrigation lines and scout for pests and predict yield based on weather patterns, and that's about it."

David stepped beside him, and for a moment neither of them spoke.

"That's not nothing," David said finally.

"It's something that doesn't matter anymore."

The kitchen door opened and their father came out. He was smaller than Marcus remembered, though he'd seen him just three weeks ago. That was always surprising, the way parents suddenly seemed to shrink, as though the years had a gravity to them that slowly pulled everything downward.

"Boys," he said, with the careful warmth of someone who'd learned that his children didn't always like each other. "You figure out the valve?"

"Valve's fine," Marcus said. "Line's broken, though. Probably needs to be replaced."

Their father nodded, unsurprised. "I was wondering when that would give up. It's been what, twenty-five years?"

"Twenty-six," Marcus said.

"Long enough. Well. Come on inside. I made sandwiches."

He went back in without waiting for an answer. David and Marcus stood in the afternoon heat, the dust thick and hot around them.

"Talk to him," David said quietly. "Tonight or tomorrow, whenever you're ready. But talk to him."

"And say what?"

"The truth. Whatever it is."

The inside of the house was cool and dim, the way farmhouses were in the afternoon. Their father had set out sandwiches and cold tea, and he moved around the kitchen with the practiced efficiency of someone who'd been living alone for a long time, who'd developed routines that required no negotiation.

They ate without much conversation. Marcus could feel the weight of the unspoken thing between them, a kind of pressure that made the sandwich taste like sawdust. Their father ate methodically, the way he did everything—each bite the same size, chewed the same number of times.

"I showed Marcus the line," David said finally.

Their father nodded.

"It's done," Marcus said. "It's just done."

"Yes."

"How long have you known?"

"Since last September, when it first started showing stress. I was going to mention it, but then I thought maybe if I just didn't say anything, it would last another season. Foolish." He took a sip of tea. "The Hendersons had to replace theirs last spring. Cost them eight thousand dollars."

Eight thousand dollars. That was the kind of number that sounded abstract until you tried to pay it.

"David said you've been talking to developers," Marcus said.

His father set down his sandwich and folded his hands on the table, the way he did when he was about to say something important.

"I have," he said. "About six months ago, a fellow came by. Educated fellow, had a whole packet of information, talked about what the property was worth, what it could become. I listened, and then I told him I needed to think about it. And I've been thinking about it."

"And?"

"And I think it's time. For me, anyway. I'm tired, Marcus. I'm tired of worrying about water and disease and whether we're going to get the price we need to make the math work. I'm sixty-eight, and I have an ex-wife I'm not talking to and a son in the city and another son who's worn himself down to a nub trying to keep something alive that maybe wanted to die a long time ago."

"It's not dying," Marcus said.

"It is. It died the year the recession hit and nobody wanted pears anymore. It died the year the water table started dropping. It died a little bit every year after that, and we just kept trying to resuscitate it. But there's a difference between keeping something alive and keeping it living."

Outside, the dust was settling into the long afternoon stillness. On the property line, the broken irrigation line lay in the dirt like something that had tried and failed.

"If we sell," Marcus said, "then what? What happens to me?"

Their father looked at him with an expression Marcus couldn't quite parse—something between sadness and relief.

"You get your life back," he said. "You get to be thirty-seven instead of forty-seven. You get to do something for yourself instead of something for the land."

"I don't know who I am without the land."

"I know. And that frightens you, and I understand that. But I'm telling you as someone on the other side of sixty, when you let something define you that completely, you don't get to find out who you really are. And then one day it's gone, and you're just lost."

Marcus pushed back from the table. He needed to move, needed to do something with the restlessness that had suddenly seized him. He went out onto the porch, and the heat hit him again, the accumulated warmth of the day pressing down like a physical weight.

David joined him after a moment, standing in the way he always stood—present but somehow also distant.

"The money would be significant," David said. "Dad's been looking at the numbers. After taxes and everything, there would be enough for him to be comfortable, and enough for you to... I don't know. Start something new. Go back to school. Travel. Just have some options."

"I don't want options. I want this."

"You want the idea of this. You want to be the person who stuck around, who believed in something. But you don't actually want this anymore. You stopped wanting this about five years ago, and you've just been going through the motions ever since."

"You don't know what I want."

"I know you're not happy. I can see it every time I see you. I could see it before I even got out of the car this morning."

Marcus said nothing. Out in the orchard, the shadows were getting longer, the light starting to turn that particular shade of gold that meant evening was coming on. In another hour or so, if the valve held, if the line didn't have any other cracks, he could turn the water on. The pears would drink for the night. The soil would absorb what it needed. And tomorrow the cycle would start again, the same cycle that had been running for years, the same cycle that was slowly grinding everything to dust.

"What if I don't want to sell?" he said.

"Then you'll keep doing this until the trees die completely, and then you'll sell anyway, and you'll do it from a place of desperation instead of choice. That's not better."

"It's my choice."

"It's not. That's what I'm trying to tell you. The choice was made already, years ago, by drought and time and the fact that nothing stays the same. We're just deciding what we're going to do about it."

Their father appeared in the doorway behind them. He looked even smaller from this angle, diminished by age or time or the simple fact of being on a porch with his sons, trying to guide them toward something they didn't want to accept.

"I love this place," he said quietly. "I have loved it for forty years. But love isn't enough. It's never enough on its own. I learned that from your mother, God rest her. She loved us—she loved me and she loved the land and she loved the idea of staying in one place and building something. But love couldn't save her from getting sick, and it couldn't save the land from the fact that the world had changed while we were busy living in it."

Marcus turned to look at his father. He had known all his life that his father was the kind of man who didn't cry, who'd been raised in a generation and a region where men simply didn't. But he could see the tears now, not falling but present, sitting at the rims of his father's eyes like something that had been waiting a very long time to be released.

"I need to turn the water on," Marcus said.

"I know," his father said. "Go turn the water on. We'll talk more after dinner."

Marcus walked back toward the line, moving through the heat of the late afternoon. The orchard seemed to stretch forever in both directions, the pear trees heavy with their ripening fruit. He could see the pickers' marks on some of them, from the previous years when there had still been enough product worth harvesting. Other sections showed the gaps where trees had simply not come back, where the stress had been too much.

At the valve, he checked the fit of the wrench and slowly, carefully, turned it right. The lever moved smoothly now, and the water began to flow. He could hear it rushing through the lines, a sound like possibility, like something that had been waiting to happen.

He followed the flow north, checking the line visibly for any new cracks, any other compromises. The break was still there, near the dip where the water pooled. He'd need to splice it, or replace that section, or eventually replace the whole thing. But for now, water was flowing past the damage, moving out into the upper acres, beginning the slow work of soaking into the dusty earth.

The sun was lower now, close to the horizon, and the light had that quality of being filtered through time and distance, illuminated from angles that made everything look both more beautiful and more temporary. He stood with the water flowing around his boots, feeling its cool on his skin through the fabric, and thought about choice and consequence and the slow weight of things that couldn't be stopped.

He could feel David watching him from where he stood near the house, and his father inside the kitchen, probably clearing away the sandwich plates, going about the small routines that had defined his days for the past decade. He could feel the weight of their love, which was not nothing, which was perhaps the most important thing of all. But he could also feel, finally, the weight of his own life pressing against the boundaries that he'd accepted so long ago, the press of years and possibility and the simple fact that he was not his father and this was not his mother and the land was not a person and you could not resurrect something that had already decided to die.

The water continued flowing. Somewhere in the darkness of the soil, roots were drinking. Somewhere in the city, the developers were probably sleeping, waiting for morning and the meeting they thought they were going to have. And somewhere in himself, Marcus was beginning to let go of something he'd been holding so tightly for so long that he'd forgotten it wasn't holding him up anymore—it was just holding him in place.

He walked back to the house as the light continued to fade. His father and brother were in the kitchen, clearing up. Nobody said anything when he came in, nobody asked about the water. They simply made space for him at the table, and his father set down a glass of cold tea without being asked, and they sat together in the ordinary quiet of three men who had finally, perhaps, begun to accept what was true.
