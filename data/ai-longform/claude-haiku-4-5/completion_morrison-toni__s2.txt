He kept Regina to a measured pace despite the horse's eagerness, her ears swiveling at sounds the fog had muffled before. The trail narrowed as it wound deeper into growth that predated the tobacco fields and the grand houses built upon them. Jacob's hand rested on his thigh, close enough to the knife there, though he knew a blade would serve him poorly if trouble came. He had seen what violence looked like in this place—had heard stories in taverns from men who carried scars like medals, each one a testimony to survival in a land that seemed determined to teach its lessons in blood.

The afternoon was advancing toward evening when the fog began to lift entirely, burned away by the sun that had been struggling behind it all day. What emerged was a landscape Jacob had glimpsed only in descriptions and rough drawings brought north by traders: vast open fields scored by furrows, with distant figures moving through them like insects across a tabletop. Here and there the great houses rose, their chimneys sending thin smoke upward into the clearing air, their symmetry and whitewash a violence of a different kind against the wilderness they dominated.

He had come south because opportunity had called him, and opportunity had never asked Jacob Vaark to examine his conscience too closely. In the north, he had built something from patience and shrewd trades, had acquired property and the respect that property brought, even to a man whose grandfather had been nobody at all. A man could reinvent himself in new territory. A man could become someone other than what his birth had designated. The colonies offered that promise to those willing to work for it, and Jacob had always been willing.

But the note he carried in his satchel—now drying against his body as the fog dissipated—had mentioned opportunity of a different scale. Wealth. Land. The kind of accumulation that would elevate him beyond the careful calculations he had managed so far. His cousin had written of possibilities in the southern colonies, of ways to multiply one's holdings through ventures that had already proven profitable to others, ventures that required only the right investments and the right connections. Jacob had the former. He was riding toward acquiring the latter.

Regina picked her way down the trail with diminishing caution. The danger of the immediate wilderness was behind them now. What lay ahead was a different kind of danger entirely—the danger of men and their wants, their hungers, their capacity to justify almost anything if the profit was sufficient.

The plantation appeared as the sun was turning the western sky red. It was set back from the road by a quarter mile, approached through an avenue of trees that must have taken decades to grow to such height and spread. The house itself was less grand than the ones Jacob had glimpsed earlier but substantial nonetheless: two stories of pale brick, symmetrical windows watching the approach like eyes.

He slowed Regina further and took in the scene. Tobacco fields stretched on either side of the house's axis, the plants in their mature height, their leaves darkening as evening came. People moved through those fields—he could count at least a dozen—moving in the systematic way of those who had done the same work so many times it required no thought. Some were bending, some straightening, some carrying bundled leaves toward structures at the field's edge.

A boy appeared on the porch—perhaps fourteen or so, dressed in clothes that marked him as something above the field workers but below the class that would occupy the house. He watched Jacob's approach without particular curiosity, the way a person watches a leaf drift past on a current.

"I'm looking for Bartholomew Pennant," Jacob called as he drew near enough to be heard without shouting.

"He's inside," the boy said. "Around back is the stable. Someone will take your horse." The boy went back to whatever he had been doing—it looked like mending something, a bridle perhaps—as though Jacob's arrival was the least remarkable thing to happen all week.

Jacob dismounted and led Regina toward the back of the house. The stable was old but well-maintained, and a man was there almost immediately, a lean figure whose age was impossible to determine but whose movements suggested long experience with horses. They exchanged no words. Jacob handed over the reins, watched long enough to satisfy himself that Regina would be cared for, then made his way back toward the main entrance.

The interior of the house was dim after the outdoor light, and he paused for his eyes to adjust. A hallway stretched ahead of him, lined with closed doors. From somewhere deeper in the house came voices—a man's low rumble and a woman's higher pitch, though he could not make out words. He stood uncertainly, unwilling to move further without invitation.

"You there." It was the boy again, appearing from a side passage. "The study. That way." He pointed.

Jacob followed the direction indicated and found himself before a heavy wooden door that looked as though it had been old when the house was new. He knocked, and a voice told him to enter.

Bartholomew Pennant sat behind a desk that held stacks of papers, ledgers, an inkwell, and little else. He was a broad man somewhere in his fifties, with the kind of face that seemed designed for frowning. He did not stand to greet Jacob, instead studying him as though Jacob were an item in a ledger that did not quite add up.

"You're the Vaark boy," Pennant said finally. It was not a question.

"Jacob Vaark," Jacob confirmed. "Thank you for receiving me."

"I received your cousin easily enough, and he's a fool. But he pays his debts and shows up when he says he will, which puts him ahead of most men." Pennant gestured to a chair. "Sit. I assume you're here about the venture he discussed?"

"In general terms," Jacob said, sitting. "He was deliberately vague in his correspondence."

"As well he should have been. There are things better discussed face to face, not written where servants or curious eyes might see." Pennant leaned back in his chair, which creaked under his weight. "You understand what kind of enterprise we're discussing?"

"Not precisely," Jacob said. "I understand there's profit to be made beyond what the tobacco fields alone produce."

"Obviously," Pennant said. "Everything produces more than one thinks if one is clever about it. The land produces tobacco. The tobacco produces money. But the real money—" He stopped, pressing his fingers together. "Do you know how many workers are in those fields out there?"

Jacob thought of the figures he had counted. "Twelve or so?"

"Twenty-three," Pennant said. "And that's only what's visible at this hour. The fields require more hands each year, more than the indentured system can supply. Men serve their time and move on, hoping to acquire land of their own. They've no loyalty. No long-term commitment. The tobacco lords were looking at a future where there would be no hands to work at all, where the system would collapse under its own demands. Do you understand the problem?"

"Yes," Jacob said. "And I assume you're explaining the solution?"

"The solution," Pennant said, "came when men understood that they didn't need to buy time. They could buy people. Permanence. Not workers who would eventually leave, but workers whose children would be workers, whose children's children would be workers. A system that perpetuated itself."

The words hung in the air between them. Jacob understood what Pennant was saying. He had heard rumors of it already—the shift that was happening in the colonies, the turning away from the temporary servitude of indentured labor toward something far more absolute. He had also understood, in some compartment of his mind he did not examine too closely, that whatever opportunity his cousin had mentioned would likely involve this transition.

"The risk," Pennant continued, "is acquiring them. The slave trade from Africa is already established, but the prices are high and the supply unpredictable. But there are other sources. Closer sources. Cheaper sources. Native people, for instance. People already here, who can be—persuaded—to work. People whose resistance can be managed through separation from their own kind, through the making of new laws that ensure they have no standing to resist." Pennant smiled then, a expression that seemed to require effort. "There are profits to be made, Vaark, in the business of convincing men that their fortunes depend on the absolute subjugation of others. The tobacco itself barely pays anymore. But the investment in human property—that's where the real future lies."

Jacob felt something shift in his chest, some small thing he recognized as what others called conscience. It had troubled him before, at minor junctures, but never enough to stop him. He had learned long ago that the world was constructed in tiers, that some men rose and others fell, and that the rising could be accomplished through knowledge, strategy, and the willingness to do what needed doing. But this—this seemed a crossing of a threshold he had not quite stood before.

"I'm not sure," Jacob began, then stopped.

"You're not sure you want the profit?" Pennant's tone made clear he found this unlikely. "Or you're not sure you want the weight of the knowledge?"

"Both," Jacob said.

Pennant nodded as though this was a reasonable response. "The weight is real. I won't lie to you. But it gets lighter with time. It gets lighter when you see the profits accumulating. It gets lighter when you understand that someone will do this whether you do it or not—that by refusing, you accomplish nothing except your own poverty. The system doesn't pause. It continues. The only choice you have is whether you're inside it, benefiting, or outside it, watching it happen to you anyway."

He leaned forward now, elbows on the desk. "Your cousin understood this. He was uncomfortable at first too. Now he's considering doubling his investment. He's watching his money transform into more money at a rate that would take him decades to achieve through legitimate commerce. That kind of freedom, Vaark—the freedom to stop struggling, to know your line will be secure for generations—that's worth a small discomfort of conscience, isn't it?"

Jacob looked at his hands, which suddenly seemed to belong to a stranger. He had come south believing he was simply seeking greater opportunity, a chance to expand his holdings. He had not imagined—or rather, he had not allowed himself to fully imagine—what that opportunity would require of him. Now, sitting in this dim study, listening to a man explain the machinery of human ownership with the same tone he might use to discuss the cultivation of tobacco, Jacob understood that choices made were choices, regardless of whether one chose consciously or simply by failing to refuse.

"I need to think," he said.

"Of course," Pennant replied. "Thought is appropriate. Thought is responsible. But don't think too long. Opportunities like this—they don't remain open indefinitely. The men who move first accumulate the most. The men who hesitate—" He gestured vaguely. "They accumulate what's left over. It's not my intent to pressure you, you understand. Only to make clear how the mathematics work."

Jacob rose from the chair. "I'll need lodging tonight."

"There's a room upstairs. The boy will show you. We'll speak again in the morning. By then, perhaps your thinking will have clarified itself."

The room was small but clean, with a narrow bed and a window looking out over the fields toward the darkening horizon. Jacob sat on the bed for a long time without moving, listening to the sounds of the house settling around him as evening became night. Somewhere down below, there was the sound of footsteps, a woman's voice calling something, the muted response of servants attending to evening tasks.

He thought of his property in the north, the house he had built with his own labor and shrewd trading. He thought of the woman he had married—a good woman, steady, who had given him two children and expected little beyond security and the knowledge that her name meant something in the community. He thought of his son, growing tall, already learning the accounting that would someday be his inheritance.

What could he offer them if he refused this venture? A comfortable life, certainly. A respectable one. But limited. Always limited. Always at the mercy of men like Pennant, who understood how to pyramid their fortunes while men like Jacob accumulated slowly, carefully, never quite managing to cross into the kind of wealth that would secure everything for generations.

What would it mean to cross that line? To invest in this system? It would mean acknowledging something he had always felt but never fully named: that the boundaries between himself and those below him—the servants, the indentured workers, the field hands—were not natural or inevitable but constructed. It would mean understanding that his prosperity had always rested on others' lack. It would simply be making that relationship explicit and permanent.

The darkness outside the window was complete now. He could hear insects calling from the fields, the distant sound of voices from the slave quarters—for that's what they would be, he understood now, though the word wasn't yet standard. The workers who were not indentured, who had somehow become permanent fixtures, whose status was being transformed into law even as they worked.

He lay back on the narrow bed and stared at the ceiling. Somewhere in this house, Pennant was likely reviewing his ledgers, his records of profit and loss. Somewhere in the fields beyond, people were settling into sleep, preparing for another day of labor that would enrich men like Pennant—and potentially, if Jacob agreed, men like himself.

The choice seemed to narrow as he thought about it. Not because refusing was becoming more appealing, but because accepting was beginning to seem inevitable. This was the direction the colonies were moving. This was where the wealth would accumulate. This was how fortunes would be made in the coming decades.

By morning, he thought, his thinking would clarify itself, just as Pennant had suggested. It would clarify in the direction of profit. It would clarify in the direction of security for his children. It would clarify in the direction that men like Pennant had already traveled, and men like his cousin were now traveling, and men like Jacob would travel next.

He had come south seeking opportunity. Now that he understood what opportunity meant in this place, what it would cost and what it would gain, he found that the choice was already made. The only work remaining was the work of convincing himself that what he was choosing was acceptable. The work of building the arguments that would allow him to sleep at night.

It was work he found, to his shame, that he was quite good at.
