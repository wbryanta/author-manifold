The old man was dead three months when they came to fix the line and it was already the season of heat. The younger brother stood in the orchard with his hands on his hips studying the break in the irrigation pipe where the water had tunneled out a small ravine in the red earth. Dark mud where it pooled. The line stretched away through the trees in both directions, some of the older trees already showing the grayish cast of thirst.

The elder brother came around the line of trees carrying the coupling and he set it down in the dirt without ceremony.

You think we can do it without draining the whole line, he said.

I don't know.

You don't know.

No.

The elder brother was the one who'd stayed. He had hands like the old man's hands, scarred and stained with various substances that wouldn't come clean. He squinted at the sun coming through the canopy.

We could drain it, he said. That'd be the safe way.

That'd take till evening.

We've got till evening.

It was already past noon. The younger brother had driven up from the city that morning in a car that probably cost more than either of them made in a year, and they both knew it. He wore clothes that seemed to require no work from him, pressed and unblemished. His hands were smooth in a way that suggested only theoretical contact with the world.

What's the point in fixing it anyway, the younger brother said.

The point is the trees.

The trees are dying.

They're not dying. They're thirsty.

Same thing out here.

The elder brother didn't answer. He knelt in the dirt and examined the break more closely, running his finger along the edges of the split. The pipe was old, sectional, joined with couplings that had themselves grown unreliable. This wasn't the first break. It wouldn't be the last if they didn't replace the whole system, which they couldn't afford.

If we shut it off at the head, he said, we can change the coupling without draining much. Maybe a gallon or two.

How much pressure is on it.

Forty, fifty pounds. Depends on what the old tank has.

The younger brother looked out through the trees toward where the tank sat on its platform, a wooden structure the old man had built in another decade. It looked like it might fall over if someone pushed on it.

The head valve is at the house, the elder brother said.

I know where it is.

Then go shut it.

The younger brother stood there for a moment, and then he walked back through the trees toward the house. The elder brother watched him go, then looked back at the pipe. He could hear the water moving inside it under pressure, a faint susurration. He took his knife from his pocket and began to scrape away the concrete that had been applied to the joint, a temporary repair from years past.

The orchard was maybe sixty acres, though no one had measured it in recent memory. It had been planted by the grandfather and tended by the father with a kind of mechanical persistence that suggested not joy in the work but rather an absence of the capacity to conceive of anything else. The trees were a mix of apple and pear, some of them already ancient by the standards of commercial fruit. The old man had reduced the number of hired workers year by year as the prices dropped, until in his final years it was just him and the occasional transient who showed up looking for work.

When he died the younger brother had wanted to sell immediately. He'd made calls to a developer from a town twenty miles away, a man with a sunburned neck and a habit of quoting scripture when it served his purposes. The developer had made an offer, not insulting but not generous. Enough to settle the old man's debts and leave a little. Enough to matter.

The elder brother had said no.

Why, the younger brother had asked.

Because it's ours.

It's not doing anything. It's just sitting here being worthless.

It's not worthless.

It's not worth anything either.

But the elder brother had said no and the younger brother had to come back himself to enforce the no, which brought him out to the orchard on a June morning with the heat already building and his hands still soft from a winter of not working.

When he returned the elder brother had finished scraping the coupling. He'd laid out new tools in a line on a piece canvas. A wrench. A compression tool. A new gasket made of rubber that looked supple and good.

You shut the valve.

I shut the valve.

Check it.

What do you mean check it.

Go back and make sure it's all the way shut.

The younger brother stood there. He could see in his brother's face that this was some kind of test, and that he would fail it by refusing. He turned and went back through the trees. The sun was high and the shadows beneath the canopy were dark and complex. He could hear the water in the pipes and he could hear birds moving in the branches, small birds whose names he didn't know. He hadn't been out here in five years, not since their mother died. He'd had reasons for that at the time, though he couldn't remember them now.

The house was old and weathered to a grayish color. It had been painted once in the younger brother's childhood, a white that had faded to something like old bone. The porch sagged. There was equipment stacked around it: ladder, old tire, a piece of pipe that had been rusted so long it had become unidentifiable. The water shut-off was on the eastern wall, a brass valve with a wooden handle.

He turned it. It was already shut. He could feel it had been shut, the resistance of it complete, immovable. He turned it again anyway and then stood there holding it, looking out at the trees that were his in some legal sense but not in any other sense. They would be worth money if they were dead, he thought. Dead land was worth something. Living land that was failing to produce was worth nothing.

When he returned the elder brother was lying on his back with his head under the break in the line, his face turned up as if looking for something in the pipe.

It's shut, the younger brother said.

All right.

The elder brother didn't move. His shirt was wet from the damp ground and from sweat. His arms were thin and there was a long scar on his left arm that had faded to something nearly white, a mark from years back when a broken branch had cut him. He'd bled a lot that day but had simply wrapped it and kept working.

You going to tell me about the developer again, the elder brother said. From under the pipe.

No.

You're going to tell me about it.

I'm not.

He offered more.

How do you know.

I read the letter you left on the kitchen table.

Jesus Christ.

We could do it, the elder brother said. We could sell it. You could take the money and I could... I could do something else. I could find something else to do.

No you couldn't.

I could try.

You've never tried anything else. You don't know how.

The elder brother rolled out from under the pipe and sat up. He wiped his face with his arm. There was dirt on his cheek and when he moved to clear it he only succeeded in spreading it.

What I know is that if we sell it someone's going to cut these trees down, he said. They're going to level the whole place and put houses on it or a shopping center or something. They're going to kill it.

It's already killed.

No it's not.

Look at them.

The elder brother looked. Through the canopy of the orchard you could see where the drought had marked the trees, a browning at the outer edges of the leaf canopy, a thinning. It wasn't dramatic. It was just a slow dying, the kind that could continue for years.

We just need water, the elder brother said.

We don't have water.

The aquifer hasn't gone dry.

It's dropping six feet a year.

Exactly. In ten years we won't be able to reach it. But we're not in ten years. We're in now.

The younger brother didn't answer. He squatted on his heels and looked at the coupling. It was a simple thing, a brass fitting that would cost eight dollars to replace. The old man would have fixed it himself, twenty, thirty years ago. He would have had the part on hand because he was the kind of man who kept parts.

You remember the freeze of '74, the elder brother said.

No.

I do. We thought the whole orchard was going to die. Old man built fires. He ran all night moving those smudge pots around in the dark. Mom made coffee and sandwiches. I must have been fourteen.

I wasn't born yet.

Right. That's right. But he didn't sell then, and the trees came back. They came back strong. We had a good year in '75, had a great year in '76.

And then prices dropped and never recovered, the younger brother said. And now we're here thirty years later wondering why we didn't sell thirty years ago.

Or wondering why we didn't hold on and figure something out.

We can't figure something out. We don't have enough water, we don't have enough labor, and we don't have a market. It's over.

The elder brother stood up. He was a full head taller than the younger brother though they were only two years apart. He looked down at his hands as if they might contain some evidence he needed to examine.

If you want to sell it, we sell it, he said. I'm not going to fight you on it. But I wanted you to see it first. I wanted you to come out here and understand that this isn't nothing. This isn't something you can just throw away.

I'm not throwing it away. I'm accepting reality.

Reality is whatever we make it to be. Right now reality is that the trees are still alive and producing. Not much, but something. Next year it could be different.

Or it could be worse.

It could be.

So we'd be gambling.

Yes.

And the downside is that we lose more money.

Yes.

And there's no upside beyond sentiment.

There is. If the water holds and if the market shifts and if we can hire enough help to actually harvest what we grow. If we can figure out a cooperative thing with some other growers, move the fruit collectively. There's ways. We just haven't tried them yet.

Because the old man didn't try them.

No. Because they're hard and we've been waiting for something to change without doing anything differently.

The younger brother stood. He could feel the argument moving in circles, the way it always did with his brother, an endless circling that never reached a terminus. They were both tired. The heat was making them stupid and angry.

I'll stay for three days, he said. We'll fix the line. We'll get the west section running. But then I'm going back, and I'm going to tell the developer that we'll consider a new offer.

All right.

And you need to think about what you're going to do. Because I can't do this forever.

I don't expect you to.

But I also can't support two people off this place.

I didn't ask you to.

No. But that's what's going to happen if I sell. You're going to have to leave.

I know.

And you're all right with that.

No. But I'm all right with the fact that it's coming.

The elder brother knelt again by the pipe. He maneuvered himself under it and positioned the coupling. From where the younger brother stood he could see his brother's face, concentrated, working through the problem with his hands. After a while the elder brother asked him to hold the pipe steady while he worked the tool. The younger brother crouched and held the pipe and felt the heat of the sun on his back and the dampness of the earth under his feet and he thought about selling the land the way you think about a death that hasn't happened yet. A kind of anticipatory grieving for something that was already in the process of being lost.

It took them an hour to get the coupling seated properly. When they finished the elder brother turned the valve back on and they lay on their bellies watching to see if the water would hold. It held. A small triumphant thing, water moving through pipe under pressure the way it was meant to do.

They went back to the house around four and drank water from the tap in the kitchen. The water was warm from sitting in the pipes. The house smelled like dust and the particular metallic smell of age, a house that hadn't been occupied in earnest in three months. The old man's chair sat in the living room, empty. His books lined the shelves, paperbacks mostly, westerns and mysteries, read and reread until the spines cracked.

What are you going to do, the younger brother said.

About what.

After. When we sell.

I don't know.

You could come to the city.

I wouldn't like the city.

No. I know you wouldn't.

I could find something else out here. Ranch work maybe. Someone's always looking for a hand.

You'd hate it.

Probably.

They sat at the kitchen table. There were dishes in the sink from the old man's last days. No one had washed them. At some point someone would have to wash them and it would be one or both of them and that knowledge hung there between them.

Do you remember the pruning, the elder brother said. When we were kids.

Which time.

All the times. Old man would take us out and he'd show us how to cut the branches. He'd say, You're not cutting the branch off. You're opening the tree up so it can breathe.

I remember.

And he'd let us make mistakes and he wouldn't get angry. He'd just show us again.

He was patient.

He was patient because he believed in it. He believed that it mattered, what we did. That it was worth doing right.

The younger brother understood what his brother was saying and understood that there was no answer to it that would satisfy him. You couldn't argue someone back into belief. You could only wait and see if it came back on its own, or accept that it wouldn't.

We'll fix the east line tomorrow, the elder brother said. And the north tank needs cleaning. The south gate has gotten stuck again.

All right.

It's going to take the three days. To do it right.

I know.

And when you leave, if you leave, these things will get broken again.

Yes.

And I won't be able to fix them fast enough to keep up.

No.

So you might as well sell it, because I can't maintain it alone.

The younger brother said nothing. He got up and walked to the window. Out beyond the house the orchard stretched away in its grid, the trees uniform and patient, waiting. In the failing light they looked almost beautiful, or perhaps not beautiful but something that beauty couldn't touch, something that existed beyond the categories of beautiful and ugly. Just things. Just living things that had been brought into this place by an act of will.

We're not like him, the younger brother said. We don't have what he had.

No. But maybe we could get it. Maybe that's what the three days are about.

Or maybe we just sell it and be done with it.

Maybe.

The elder brother got up and stood beside his brother at the window. They stood there looking out at the dark coming in across the orchard, the heat of the day releasing itself into the sky. In the distance you could hear the irrigation system running, water moving through the repaired line, keeping alive what the old man had planted. A small and temporary victory against the slow tide of everything moving toward failure.

Tomorrow we'll work, the elder brother said.

All right.

And you'll tell me if you change your mind.

I won't change my mind.

But you'll tell me anyway.

Yes. I'll tell you.

They stood there in the growing dark, and neither of them moved, as if by standing still they could hold something in place. But nothing could be held. The orchard would do what orchards do. They would do what men do. And the land would remain behind them, indifferent to their arguments and their failures, patient in a way that suggested it didn't need them at all.
