# The Ferry

The ship cut through darkness with a sound like tearing fabric. Marcus stood on the forward deck, watching the Norwegian coast recede into mist, though he'd arrived only three hours earlier—a flight from London ending in a drive through sleeping towns, and now this, the overnight boat back across the strait to Sweden. His brother's body, or the body of someone, waited in a morgue in Gothenburg. The police had been vague about the details. Identity uncertain. Distinguishing marks unclear. They'd asked if he could come.

Twenty-three years since he'd seen David in person. Twenty years since they'd spoken at all, if you counted that last bitter exchange as speaking. A drunk phone call, wasn't it? David slurring something about their mother, Marcus replying with words he'd later regret but never quite apologized for. Then nothing. Facebook for a while—updates from a distance, then David's account gone dark. The world contracted around their estrangement until it seemed natural, inevitable, the only possible ending.

Now this telephone call from a Swedish detective with perfect English. We found a body, the man had said. Male, mid-forties. Drowned. Caught in nets off the coast. We found some documents, but the identification is unclear. Do you have a brother named David? Do you know if he had any scars?

Marcus had asked the obvious questions. How long dead? Days, the detective said. Perhaps a week. The body was not well preserved. He'd need to come and look, because dental records from England didn't match the condition of the teeth. They wanted to know if he had tattoos, birthmarks, anything that could help.

So now he was here, suspended between continents, between past and present, the dark sea beneath him vast and indifferent. There were perhaps thirty other passengers on the ferry. Most were already in their cabins or the lounge. Marcus had paid for a cabin but couldn't bear the thought of being enclosed. He'd thought the air might help. The night air, the isolation of the deck, the sense of movement—these things might prepare him for what was coming.

He tried to remember David's marks. The scar on his left hand from a childhood accident, a dog bite or a broken bottle, something like that. A small tattoo on his wrist done in secret when he was seventeen, their mother had been furious. What was it? A star? An anchor? Marcus couldn't quite see it in his memory. The scar on his ribs from appendicitis, he remembered that. David had complained about it constantly during that recovery, milking the sympathy. David had been good at that—extracting what he needed from people, leaving them feeling drained.

Or was that fair? Was that memory or was it resentment masquerading as recollection?

The deck was cold and slick with spray. Marcus pulled his coat tighter. He was fifty-two years old, though lately it felt like more. His face in the mirror had started to surprise him—the lines deepening in ways he didn't authorize, his reflection becoming a stranger's. He'd always looked more like their father than David had, or so their mother said. David had her eyes, that particular shade of grey that seemed to contain entire conversations. The eyes that could make you forgive him anything.

That was probably unfair too.

A woman appeared from the stairwell, wrapped in a thick parka, her hair pulled back severely. She was perhaps sixty, Scandinavian in a way Marcus couldn't quite articulate—a straightness to her posture, something reserved in her movements. She lit a cigarette and looked out at the water without seeming to notice him.

"Can't sleep either?" she said, not turning.

Marcus considered not answering. But something in her tone—the assumption of shared solitude rather than the question itself—made him respond. "No."

"Rough crossing tomorrow, they're saying. Storm coming up from the south." She took a long drag on her cigarette. "I don't mind it. I like to feel the ship working. Reminds you that it's actually moving, that you're somewhere between places."

That was what he needed, he thought. That exact thing. She seemed to understand.

"Where are you headed?" she asked.

"Gothenburg. To a hospital."

She didn't ask why, for which he was grateful. She simply nodded, acknowledging that he'd answered, that the answer was his to keep. They stood in silence for a while. The cigarette smoke curled away toward the stern.

"I'm going back home," the woman said eventually. "I visit my daughter in Norway, and then I come back home. I do it twice a year. Have done for five years now, since my daughter moved." She flicked ash into the sea. "Each time I'm sure it will be the last time. That I'm too old, too tired. But then winter comes, or spring, and I book the tickets again."

"What keeps you coming back?" Marcus asked.

"The ferry," she said. "I keep coming back for the ferry. The crossing. The in-between." She smiled slightly. "I tell myself it's the daughter, of course. And I love her very much. But it's really the ferry. The only time I'm not somewhere, you know? Just in transit. Just between."

She finished her cigarette and dropped it into the dark water. Marcus watched the ember arc down, vanishing. 

"Get some rest if you can," the woman said. "The storm will be better if you're rested." She turned and went back inside, leaving him alone again.

He tried to imagine David making this crossing. Had he? The Norwegian police hadn't said where David had come from, where he'd been going. They'd found no identification, no wallet, nothing to say who he was or what he was doing in the water. A body in a net, sent to a morgue, waiting for someone to come and say his name.

If it was him. If it was David.

Marcus had left David a message once, four or five years ago. A birthday greeting sent to an old email address, just a line or two. No response. He'd considered trying to find him after their mother died—they should have shared that, should have grieved together, or separately in the same room at least. But by then David had vanished more completely. No social media, no mutual friends willing to share contact information, nothing. It was like David had stepped out of the world, or the world had stepped around him. Either way, he was gone.

When Marcus finally went to his cabin, he found it small and neat and impersonal. A shelf of dog-eared paperbacks in multiple languages, a bed with clean linens, a porthole showing only blackness. He lay down fully clothed and tried to sleep, but his mind wouldn't settle. It kept returning to David at various ages, as if assembling a composite, trying to build the body that the nets had pulled from the water.

David at twelve, thin and quick-moving, with that way of smiling that made you feel like you were the only person in the world he'd been looking for. David at seventeen, sullen and defiant, fighting with their mother about his choices, his life, his future. David at thirty, the last time Marcus had seen him in person—what had he looked like then? Heavier perhaps. A little tired around the eyes. He'd been working in some kind of hospitality, David had mentioned. He'd looked like someone who'd made different choices than the ones he'd planned.

The ferry heaved gently on the swells. The storm the woman mentioned was building somewhere to the south, not yet upon them but approaching. Marcus found the motion somewhat comforting. It was true what she'd said about the in-between, about floating in the space between one place and another. Here, suspended over deep water in the darkness, he didn't have to be anyone specific. He didn't have to be the successful one, the one who'd made it out, who'd built a career in London and a life that was, from the outside, everything their mother had wanted. He didn't have to be the abandoned brother, either, the one who'd chosen distance over reconciliation, who'd let pride and hurt pile up between them until the gap became unbridgeable.

He was just a man on a ferry, traveling through the night toward something he didn't want to see.

Around three in the morning, a violent pitching woke him. The storm had arrived faster than predicted. The ship climbed each swell like a great beast climbing, then slid down the back, and the motion was enough to send several of the lighter furnishings sliding across the cabin. Marcus wedged himself against the wall and watched the porthole go from black water to black sky to black water again, the rhythm hypnotic and nauseating.

He found himself thinking about their mother—about how she'd loved David with a kind of fierce protectiveness, as if she suspected he might break, or fly away. She'd been easier on him, more forgiving. When David failed his university exams, she'd comforted him. When Marcus worked himself into exhaustion in his first year of law school, she'd suggested he wasn't cut out for it, that he should do something easier. He'd proven her wrong, had built a good life, had become someone. But there was always a tinge of bitterness to it, the knowledge that his success had been built partly in opposition to her low expectations, her faith in David's potential despite his failures.

She'd died believing Marcus had cut David out deliberately, that he'd chosen his own life over his brother. The last time they'd spoken, before the funeral, she'd asked him to reach out, to mend things. He'd said he was too busy. He'd said David had made his choices. He'd said—what had he said? Something cruel. Something about how some people weren't worth the effort.

He'd had three years to regret it. Three years since the funeral, since David's absence from it, since he'd finally tried to track him down and found the trail cold. Three years to wonder where David had gone, what life he was living, if he ever thought about their mother, or Marcus, or anything at all.

By dawn, the storm had passed into something approaching calm. Marcus had slept very little and was conscious of a thick, muzzy exhaustion behind his eyes. He went up on deck and found the sun rising over a sea of torn grey silk, the horizon somewhere between Norway and Sweden, the ferry still suspended between.

The Swedish coast appeared gradually, emerging from the mist like a drawing coming into focus. Forests and rocky shores, small houses painted in bright colors, the whole landscape seeming almost impossibly neat and ordered. Gothenburg announced itself in the distance—buildings, a harbor, signs of human organization. They were almost there.

Marcus stood at the rail, watching the approach. His hands were shaking slightly. He realized he'd been dreading this moment less than the moment after it, when he would have to look at the body, make the identification, and either say "yes, that's my brother" or say "no, it's not." Both answers would change things. Both answers would close a door on something he'd left open.

The ferry maneuvered into the harbor with the slow inevitability of something very large and heavy. People began emerging onto the deck with their luggage, ready to disembark. Marcus saw the woman from the previous evening, the one who came on the ferry twice a year for the in-between. She was looking toward the docks, toward whatever was waiting for her. She saw him watching her and gave a slight nod of acknowledgment.

The gangway was lowered. The passengers began to move down it, becoming real people again, citizens of somewhere rather than travelers between places. Marcus waited until the crowd had thinned before gathering his overnight bag and joining them.

The hospital was modern and efficiently staffed. The detective who'd called him was waiting in the lobby—a middle-aged man with grey at his temples and a tired politeness that suggested he did this sort of thing regularly. He led Marcus through corridors that smelled of antiseptic, down stairs, into the basement where the morgue kept its inhabitants.

"Before we go in," the detective said, "I need to tell you what to expect. The body has been in the water for perhaps a week, maybe longer. The condition is not good. But there are some distinguishing marks. If you can identify any of them, it will help us confirm."

Marcus nodded. He couldn't seem to produce words.

"Take your time," the detective said. "There's no rush."

But that wasn't true. There was rush. There was urgency, the urgent need for the answer so that things could move forward, so that whatever this was could be resolved and filed away.

The room was cold and clinical. A metal table with a shape on it covered by a sheet. The detective stood to the side, letting Marcus approach. Marcus found himself moving slowly, as if the longer it took him to reach the table, the longer he could exist in not knowing.

The detective pulled back the sheet.

It was David. It wasn't David. It was a man the same age David would be, or would have been. It was a body that had been in the water, broken and changed by currents and time and the small organisms that inhabited the sea. But beneath the damage, beneath the ruin of decomposition and salt water—beneath all of that, something of David was still visible.

Or was it? Was Marcus seeing what he expected to see, or what was actually there?

He stared at the body. The left hand bore a scar, pale against what had been the hand's color. The wrist—he leaned closer, needing to see. Yes. A mark there, a tattoo, faded and water-stained but visible. A star, he thought. Or perhaps an anchor. He couldn't quite make it out.

"The ribs," he heard himself say. "Lift the sheet."

The detective did so, carefully. Marcus could see the scar tissue, old and well-healed, the mark of a surgical incision. The appendix. Yes. He was seeing the scars he remembered. Or was he? How reliable was memory? How easily did the mind create what it expected to see?

"Is this your brother?" the detective asked gently.

Marcus felt something crack open inside his chest. Not grief, exactly. Something more complex. A kind of settling, perhaps. An ending. A beginning. A recognition that whatever version of David had lived in his head, this thing on the table was the final answer to the question he'd been avoiding for twenty years.

"Yes," he said. "Yes, that's David. That's my brother."

The detective made a note. He asked a few more questions, things Marcus answered without really hearing himself. Facts. Medical history. Parents' names. Next of kin information. The words came out automatically, as if they were being spoken by someone else, someone more practiced at this.

"How did he die?" Marcus asked.

"The water was in his lungs," the detective said. "So drowning. But there's a head injury. It's possible he fell, hit his head, and then fell into the water. Or it's possible something else happened. Without a witness, it's hard to say."

"Where was he found?"

"In fishing nets, off the coast near Strömstad. About fifteen kilometers from land. It's a busy area for boats. People fish there, pleasure boats pass through. We don't know where he entered the water."

Marcus nodded, as if this information mattered, as if he could construct some kind of narrative that would explain how his estranged brother had ended up in the North Sea. Perhaps David had been living in Norway or Sweden. Perhaps he'd taken a job as a deckhand, or was visiting someone. Perhaps he'd been running from something. Or walking toward it. Perhaps he'd been exactly where he meant to be.

"Is there anything else you need?" the detective asked.

"No," Marcus said. "No, I think... No."

He turned away from the table, away from the body of his brother, and followed the detective back out of that room and up the stairs and through the corridors that smelled of antiseptic and loss.

Later, much later, after statements had been given and arrangements made and all the machinery of death set in motion, Marcus found himself back on the streets of Gothenburg. The sun was high and the day was bright and the city moved around him with the indifferent vitality of people with lives to live.

He found a café and sat by a window overlooking the harbor. He ordered coffee he didn't want and held the cup without drinking it. Across the water, he could see the ferry terminal, the massive bulk of the ship that had carried him here, now being prepared for its return journey. In a few hours it would pull away from the dock and head north, back out into the strait, back toward the in-between.

His phone buzzed. A text from his colleague in London, asking about the case he'd abandoned mid-trial. Could he return tomorrow? The judge was not pleased. Life, demanding itself to be lived.

He texted back that he needed another day. That he would return when he could.

The ferry pulled away from the dock at eight in the evening. Marcus watched it from the terminal, not boarding, just watching it slip away into the darkening strait. Tomorrow he would fly back to London. Tomorrow his life would reassemble itself. Tomorrow he would return to the courtrooms and the offices and the carefully constructed order of his days.

But tonight he had another night in Gothenburg. Another night to exist between who he had been—a man with a living brother, estranged and distant but alive—and who he would become. A man with a dead brother. A man who had never reconciled what needed reconciling. A man who would carry this ending now, this final sealing of a gap that had been open for so long that he'd stopped noticing it.

He left the terminal and walked through the city without particular direction, letting his feet choose the path. The streets were quiet, filled with the soft sound of evening traffic and people moving through their lives. Lights came on in buildings. Windows filled with the warm glow of interior spaces. All those private worlds, lit from within, each one a complete universe.

Somewhere in the darkness, David was gone, and would remain gone, and had been gone—really gone—for far longer than Marcus had understood. All those years of not speaking, not reaching out, were merely the final form of something that had already happened. A prolonged goodbye to someone who had already left.

Marcus thought of the woman on the ferry, moving between the places she loved, sustained by the crossing itself. He understood that now. The spaces between were where you could exist as purely yourself, freed from the demand to be someone specific, to have relationships, to maintain continuity. On the ferry you were just in transit, moving from one state to another, and that movement was its own kind of peace.

He walked until he found a small hotel, a place without pretension or particular comfort, and booked another room. He lay awake again that night, listening to the sounds of the city beyond his window, listening to the rumble of trucks and the distant sound of a train, listening to the small noises of a city at night.

And when he finally slept, he dreamed of David—not the body in the morgue, but David at seventeen, eyes bright with some private knowledge, hand raised in a wave as if to say goodbye. In the dream, Marcus reached out to touch him, and his brother turned and smiled that smile that made you feel chosen, seen, known. Then he dissolved into water, becoming the sea itself, and Marcus was swimming through him, through the place between places, through the dark and endless in-between.
