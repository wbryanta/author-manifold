# The Long Night

The first ewe went into labor just as the wind started, that peculiar sound like something alive trying to get in through the walls. Margaret had learned to read that particular quality of wind in the three weeks since she and Thomas had started staying overnight in the shed, and she knew this one meant snow, real snow, the kind that didn't care that it was late April. She stood in the lambing pen with her hands on the old ewe's sides, feeling the contraction move through her like a wave, and she thought of how her husband would have said something about the animals knowing before we do. James would have said it with that particular tone that wasn't quite a joke but wasn't quite serious either, the way he said most true things.

Thomas was fourteen now, tall and awkward in that way of teenage boys, his voice still catching sometimes when he spoke, and he sat on the bench near the pen with the logbook open on his knees. He'd been taking notes on every ewe, every labor, every birth and every loss. Margaret had asked him to do it, thinking it would give him something to focus on, something methodical in a season that felt anything but. The pen light cast sharp shadows under his jaw and across his forehead, making him look older than he was, making him look almost like his father.

"She's coming," Margaret said, and Thomas closed the logbook and came to help without being asked. This was what they did now. This was what they were.

The ewe labored for another hour before the lamb came, a good-sized ram, healthy from the first breath. Margaret caught him as he slipped out, warm and slick and necessary, and she handed him to Thomas, who rubbed him dry with the clean towel they kept ready. The boy worked with surprising gentleness, his large hands cradling the small wet body, and Margaret could see him concentrating, see him trying not to think about anything else. By morning there would be seven lambs in the shed, she thought. Maybe eight. The animals came when they came, regardless of storms or season or what day it was on the calendar.

By nine o'clock, the power went out.

The shed had lights on a generator, old and temperamental, and when the main line failed, there was that brief terrible moment of complete darkness before the generator wheezed to life. The lights came back on, dimmer than before, and Margaret felt her heart slow its racing. She'd checked the generator fuel that morning, and there was enough. There was always enough, because James had made sure there always was, and she had followed his procedures even though he was three months dead and buried under a headstone in the corner of the parish churchyard.

"We're okay," she said to Thomas, though he hadn't asked. "We have light. We have heat. The generator will hold."

Thomas nodded, but his face was pale in the generator light. He was standing at the open barn door, looking out at the snow, which was already falling so heavily that they could only see a few feet beyond the threshold. The wind sounded angrier now, more insistent.

"Close that," Margaret said, and he did, pushing hard against the wooden door and latching it. The noise of the storm became muffled again, a constant rushing sound like they were underwater.

The second ewe went into trouble just after ten. She was a younger animal, a first-timer, and her lamb was positioned wrong—Margaret could tell as soon as she examined her. The lamb was backward, a breech presentation, and Margaret's hands moved through the familiar steps: the calm voice, the clean intervention, the careful insertion of her arm into the warm interior of the animal's body to shift the lamb into a position where it could be born. Thomas stood beside her, handing her the things she needed before she asked, and she wondered if this was what it would be like now, the two of them in partnership, learning what she had learned only in pieces, over decades, from a man who was gone.

The lamb came free eventually, a ewe this time, small but breathing. The mother began to bleat with that particular sound of maternal concern, and Margaret wiped her arm clean on her apron and let the mother reach her lamb, that crucial first moment of connection.

"Good work," Margaret said to Thomas, and meant it. He didn't smile exactly, but he nodded, and there was a small tightness in his face that eased a little.

By midnight, the wind had taken on a new quality, and even in the shed it was hard to escape the sense that something was wrong about the weather, something dangerous. Margaret had lived through storms before—all her adult life had been lived in the North Country, where bad weather was as common as grass—but there was something about this one that made her understand why animals grew restless, why they moved differently, why they seemed to know things that humans had to be told.

She and Thomas took turns checking on the ewes, and she sent him to the cottage at one in the morning to make tea and bring back the thermos they kept filled with hot water. She watched him through the window as he walked from the shed to the house, a small figure in a pool of light from the torch he carried, and then nothing but darkness and snow around him until he reached the cottage. She found herself holding her breath until she saw the light come on in the kitchen window.

When he came back, his hair was wet with snow and his face was flushed from the cold. He held the thermos out to her like an offering.

"The house is warm," he said. "The boiler's running fine."

"Good," Margaret said. "That's good, Thomas. We did well to have the generator for the essential circuits."

They drank tea sitting on the bench, and for a moment it felt almost peaceful, the warm cup in her hands, the warmth spreading through her chest. She thought about James doing this in seasons past, thought about how he would have known what to do about the storm, how he would have had some quiet confidence in him that came from years of experience and the understanding of how these things worked out. She and Thomas had neither of those things. They had only each other and the things James had left behind in the form of notes and procedures and the deep practical knowledge that had run in his hands.

The fourth ewe went into labor at two in the morning, and Margaret was so tired by then that the world had taken on a dreamlike quality. Her hands moved through the examination, and she found herself thinking about her mother, who had died when Margaret was twelve, who had never lived long enough to see her daughter take up this work. She wondered what her mother would have thought of her now, standing in a lambing shed at two in the morning during a blizzard, with her adolescent son asleep on a pile of hay in the corner.

Except Thomas wasn't asleep. She turned and found him watching her, his eyes alert and serious, and he stood without being asked and came to help. The ewe was laboring hard, and there was something in her eyes that Margaret recognized from long experience as pain and fear. She spoke to the animal in a low, even voice, the same voice she used on all of them, the voice James had taught her to use when she was younger than Thomas was now.

"You're alright," Margaret said, though she didn't know if it was true. "You're alright, and your lamb is coming, and we're here."

Thomas was watching her face as much as he was watching the ewe, and Margaret realized he was learning not just the mechanics of lambing but something else—the tone, the attitude, the way you carried yourself in the presence of an animal in crisis. She was teaching him things she had never explicitly decided to teach him. This had simply become what they did together.

The lamb came easier than the breech had come, and this one was a set of twins, both small, both appearing to be healthy. The ewe turned to them with that primal intensity, and Margaret and Thomas stepped back to give her space. They had learned this too: when to help and when to let nature work alone.

By four in the morning, there were thirteen lambs in the shed, and Margaret's back was screaming and her hands were raw and chapped despite the gloves. The wind seemed to have taken on a new dimension to it, and twice she went to the door and looked out into the darkness, where snow fell so thickly that the beam of her torch disappeared into it within a few feet. She thought about calling the veterinarian, and then remembered that the phone lines were probably down, that even if they weren't, no one would be coming out into this weather.

She turned back to the shed, and Thomas was there, having brought fresh hay for the recovering ewes, having refilled the water basins without being asked. When had he learned to anticipate what needed doing? When had he become this competent, this present? She realized with a small shock that she didn't know anymore where the line was between Thomas the child and Thomas the young man.

"You should rest," she said.

"Not yet," he replied. "You haven't rested."

It was an impasse, the kind that might have become an argument, but instead it became something else—an agreement, unspoken but understood, that they would stay awake together through this night, that they would be present for whatever came.

The fifth ewe delivered a stillborn, and Margaret felt something in herself close off as she handled the dead lamb, felt Thomas's sharp breath beside her as he understood what had happened. She wrapped the body in burlap while Thomas sat heavily on the bench, and she didn't try to tell him it was alright or that this was normal. He was old enough to know that some things died. He had buried his father three months ago.

The sixth ewe had a difficult labor that stretched into two hours, and Margaret's mind began to fracture with exhaustion, to jump around in ways she couldn't quite control. She thought about James's funeral in the rain, about the way Thomas had stood at the graveside without crying, his face set in an expression so controlled it looked like pain. She thought about the lamb born dead and about the eleven that lived and about the mathematics of loss and survival that made up the actual work of farming. She thought about James at forty-seven, still young, dropping a gate that was too heavy and falling backward, hitting his head on the concrete floor of the barn. She thought about how quickly a life could collapse into a before and an after.

The lamb came eventually, and it lived, and Margaret did what needed doing, and the night continued on.

By six in the morning, when the eastern sky began to lighten almost imperceptibly behind the still-falling snow, there were seventeen lambs in the shed. Margaret had lost count of how many ewes had labored, how many were still waiting, how many would come in the day ahead. She and Thomas sat on the bench together, not quite touching, and drank cold tea from the thermos, and neither of them said anything.

The storm showed no sign of breaking. The wind still howled around the shed, and the snow still fell with that relentless, almost personal quality it had taken on. Margaret thought about how storms like this could last for days, could stretch into a week, and she tried not to think about the generator, about how long the fuel would hold, about what would happen if the shed lost light and heat.

"Will Dad have come down to help?" Thomas asked, his voice small in the noise of the wind.

Margaret set down her cup. She turned to look at her son, this boy who was not quite a man, who had helped her all through the night and who was still, somewhere deep underneath, a child waiting for his father to come home.

"No," she said, and she took his hand, which was large and cold and trembling slightly. "No, sweetheart. He won't be coming down. It's just us now. It's just you and me."

She felt him absorb this the way he had absorbed it three months ago, and she wondered how many times in their lives they would have to learn this lesson, this fact, this particular shape of loss.

Another ewe began to bleat, and Margaret stood up, and Thomas stood up with her. They went back to the pen, back to the work. The storm continued. The lambs continued to come, wet and slick and new, each one a small miracle of continuation, of life asserting itself even in the face of wind and snow and the absence of the people we needed most.

When the generator died at half past ten in the morning, they didn't panic. They had oil lamps, and they lit them, and the shed filled with a golden, wavering light that was almost beautiful. Margaret was so tired by then that she felt she was moving through water, but her hands still remembered what to do, still found their way into the work. Thomas worked beside her, learning and learning and learning.

By afternoon, they had lost power to the cottage as well, and the wind had brought down one of the fencing posts near the run. But the shed held. The lambs kept coming. The ewes kept laboring and delivering and beginning the work of motherhood, indifferent to storms, indifferent to loss, driven by nothing but the ancient imperative to continue.

Margaret's hands found a rhythm, and Thomas found his place in that rhythm, and together, in the golden light of oil lamps while the world whited out beyond the wooden walls, they did the work that had to be done. She thought about James sometimes—thought about his hands, his voice, the way he had known things without being told—but mostly she thought about the ewe in front of her and the lamb coming and the next ewe waiting in the pen beyond.

"You're doing well," she told Thomas at one point, and he looked up at her with eyes that were too old for his face, and she realized that this was what it meant to keep going. Not to be okay, not to recover, not to return to any kind of normal. Just to do the next thing, and then the thing after that, and to do it as well as you possibly could, even when your heart was broken and your hands were shaking and the man you had loved was never coming back.

The snow continued to fall. The lambs continued to come. And in the shed, in the golden light, a woman and her son continued the work of survival, together.
