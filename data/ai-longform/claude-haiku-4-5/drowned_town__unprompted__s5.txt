# 

The morning Catherine arrived in Millbrook County, the water was the color of rust. She stood at the overlook above the reservoir, her surveying equipment stacked in the truck behind her, and tried to remember if it had always looked this way or if the drought had stolen something essential. Twenty-eight years was a long time. Long enough to forget the particular shade of water you grew up beside, though she was fairly certain it had been blue.

The county administrator had called her directly, which was unusual. They wanted someone with roots here, someone who understood what they were looking at. The water level had dropped to expose the ghost of Riverside, the original town that had been flooded in 1962 to create the reservoir. No one had mapped it properly on the way down—the surveyors who'd done that work were dead or retired—and now the state wanted documentation before the water inevitably returned. Catherine was forty-six years old, one of the few people still living who had actually walked those streets.

She had left at eighteen and never looked back with any real intention. Her work had taken her to seven states in as many years. She built subdivisions and shopping centers, mapped new highways and parking lots. She documented the world as it was being made, not as it had been unmade.

But the administrator's voice on the phone had been persuasive, almost pleading. There was something about being the only one left who could read the landscape twice.

The surveying equipment felt foreign in her hands after a month away from fieldwork. She'd been in an office in Raleigh, managing other people's measurements, when her mother's heart had finally given out. The funeral had been small. Catherine had not cried then, or in the month since, but she felt the absence of tears the way an amputee feels the absence of a limb—as a phantom pressure, a wrongness she couldn't quite locate.

The truck she'd rented was the wrong kind of vehicle for what she needed to do. She should have asked for something with four-wheel drive, but she hadn't expected the road down to be so badly eroded. Water damage, she realized. The annual cycle of rising and falling had torn the asphalt to shreds. When she finally made it to the shore—what used to be the shore—she had to walk the last quarter mile.

The exposed ground was moonscape alien, a landscape of mud and stone that had been underwater for almost three decades. Weeds grew in patches from cracks in the concrete foundations. She could see the grid of old Riverside laid out before her like a skeleton, the bones of buildings arranged in streets she had walked to school every morning of her childhood. The main street ran perpendicular to the water, sloping gently downward. In her mind's eye, she could place storefronts over the foundations: the hardware store where her father had bought his last package of nails, the diner where she'd worked as a busboy one summer, the library where Mrs. Chen had taught her to use the microfiche machine.

Catherine set up her transit at what used to be the intersection of Main and Oak. The math would be easier if she started at the highest point and worked downslope. She took her first reading with methodical precision, the rubber eyepiece warm against her face. The instrument was an extension of her body at this point, as familiar as breathing. She had spent so much of her life looking at the world through lenses that sometimes she forgot what it looked like to see it direct.

The street grid came back to her gradually. Not as conscious memory but as something deeper, embedded in her muscles and her spatial reasoning. Her feet remembered the distances. The slight dip where the road had subsided near Third Street. The way Main bent almost imperceptibly around the old courthouse. These small deformations, invisible to the casual observer, had been documented in the original survey maps, and she found herself anticipating them before the instruments confirmed them.

By noon, she was standing in what used to be her neighborhood, though she hadn't planned it that way. The coordinates just led her here, to Birch Street, to the grid square where the Finch family had lived in a white colonial with blue shutters and a backyard where her father had built a shed that burned down in the summer of 1990, the summer before her mother left him. The foundation was still there, a rectangular outline of concrete with a slight rise in the center where the floor had buckled. The chimney had fallen inward, creating a small cairn of brick in the northwest corner.

She stood in what used to be her mother's kitchen and tried to feel something. The space was smaller than memory. Memory made everything larger—ceilings higher, rooms wider, the distances between places greater. Now it was just thirty feet by twenty, with appliance hookups still visible on the western wall. She could almost see the refrigerator humming in that corner, could almost smell the particular odor of the house, which had been old even then and carried the smell of age in its walls, a mustiness that no amount of cleaning could eliminate.

Catherine took her readings with professional detachment, but her hands shook slightly as she recorded the numbers. This was a mistake, she thought. Coming back here was a mistake. The town was supposed to stay drowned, was supposed to remain what all drowned things became: impossible, irretrievable, a story you told that grew more untethered from reality with each retelling.

She worked through the afternoon, moving down the slope street by street, building by building. By the time the sun began to lower, she had documented most of the downtown district, and her notebook was filled with coordinates and notations, with the careful language of measurement and distance. The professional language created a buffer between her and what she was actually doing, which was mapping the geography of loss.

It was while she was standing at what used to be the central plaza, the town square where the farmers' market had been held on Saturday mornings, that she saw the boy.

He was maybe twelve, picking through rubble near what had been the gazebo. At first she thought he was a hallucination, brought on by heat and memory and the particular strangeness of walking through a town that was neither alive nor properly dead. But when he looked up at her, he was unmistakably real.

"You're surveying," he said. Not a question.

"I am." Catherine straightened, suddenly aware of how she must look, covered in dust, bent over her instruments like she was peering into the earth's secrets.

"They said someone was coming to map it." He was a local kid, probably. Curious, the way kids were. "Is this your job?"

"It is."

"My grandfather said this used to be a town. A real town, before the water came." The boy turned back to his searching. He was collecting things—a rusted spoon, a piece of blue glass, a nail bent into an L-shape. "He said it had a movie theater. With red velvet seats."

"It did." Catherine found herself walking toward him. "On Oak Street. The Riverside Cinema. They showed double features on Saturdays."

The boy looked up again, and his expression shifted into something like recognition, though she was certain he'd never seen her before. Perhaps it was just the tone of her voice, the fact that she wasn't dismissing his grandfather's story as myth or exaggeration.

"Did you live here?"

"I did. A long time ago."

"Did you go to the movies?"

"Once or twice." This wasn't entirely true, but it was close enough. She had gone to the movies with friends after school sometimes, had sat in those red velvet seats and watched the stories flicker across the screen while the world outside continued its slow decline into irrelevance. "I was very young."

"What was it like?"

Catherine considered the question. She could describe the theater in detail: the entrance with its art deco trim, the ticket booth, the smell of popcorn mixing with old carpet. But that wasn't what he was asking. He wanted to know what it was like to live in a place that people thought was worth flooding. What it was like to lose a town and its stories and its particular geography to something as abstract as a power grid's need for water.

"It was good," she said finally. "It was a good place."

The boy nodded solemnly and returned to his collecting. Catherine left him there and moved back to the plaza's center, but she found it difficult to focus on her work. The boy's presence was a complication she hadn't anticipated. The drowned town had been abstract before, a landscape of facts and figures. But the boy had made it real again, had invested it with narrative and meaning beyond the simple coordinates of location.

She worked until it was nearly dark, then packed her equipment and made her way back to the truck. The kid had disappeared by then, melted back into whatever life he led in the present-day world. She drove to the motel in the county seat, a place that had probably been here when she left, some faceless chain establishment that could be anywhere. She ordered room service, took a shower that was never quite hot enough, and lay in bed studying her notes, cross-referencing them against the original survey maps from 1961.

The grid had held. The town's structure was intact, even submerged all these years. The foundations had settled slightly, but nothing catastrophic had occurred. Concrete was good at surviving when there was nothing to use it for anymore.

The next morning, Catherine went back to the reservoir. The boy was there again, in a different location, sifting through what used to be a residential foundation.

"You came back," he said when he saw her.

"I have more work to do."

"Where did you live?"

Catherine could have lied, but she didn't. She pointed upslope, toward where Birch Street intersected with Elm. "That way. About two blocks."

"Can you show me?"

This was against protocol. She was a professional surveying a disaster zone, not a historian leading a tour. But Catherine found herself nodding, and she found herself walking alongside the boy, telling him things she hadn't thought about in decades. Her father's name. Her mother's maiden name. The route she had walked to school each morning, past the Hansen house with its chain-link fence and aggressive dog, past the Methodist church with its bell that rang at noon every day, past the cluster of birch trees that gave her street its name.

The boy asked questions: What was her favorite thing about living here? Did she have friends? What did she want to be when she grew up? Catherine answered most of them, though some felt too close to the bone. She had wanted to be a surveyor, actually, which she had never told anyone in her childhood. An uncle who visited once had shown her his transit, had let her look through it, and the world had reorganized itself into a series of precise measurements and relationships. Everything had made sense through that lens in a way nothing else ever did.

By the time they reached Birch Street, Catherine's heart was racing. She was afraid of what she would feel seeing her house again with someone else present, someone for whom it was just another abandoned foundation in a catalog of abandoned foundations. But when they arrived at the coordinates, she found she was almost relieved. The house had settled, had collapsed slightly on its northeast corner. The fireplace had fallen through the floor. Nature was reclaiming the foundation, with weeds and mushrooms and the small erosions that time enacted on all human structures.

"This was it," she said quietly.

The boy walked around the perimeter, studying it with the serious attention of an archaeologist. "Do you think they'll rebuild it? When the water comes back?"

"No." Catherine watched a cloud of insects rise from a crack in the concrete. "This town is gone. The water will cover it again, and eventually everyone will forget it was ever here, except as a name in a historical archive somewhere. Riverside, flooded 1962, re-flooded 2050 or whenever the rains return."

"That's sad," the boy said.

"Yes."

"My grandfather still lives in the county. He was born here, too, before it was drowned. He told me that sometimes at night he can still see it, not with his eyes but in his memory. He said the town is more real to him now than it ever was when he lived here, because he can't change it anymore. It's perfect in memory."

Catherine wanted to argue that memory was the opposite of perfect, that it was unreliable and self-serving and constantly revising itself. But she looked at the ruins of her childhood home and understood something of what the grandfather meant. The real house, with its leaks and creaky floors and the smell of age in the walls, was less real to her now than the memory of it. The memory was cleaner, more essential, more truly her house than the house itself had ever been.

"Your grandfather sounds wise," she said.

"He is. He wants to come down here himself, but his legs are bad. I'm documenting it for him." The boy held out his small collection of artifacts: the spoon, the glass, the bent nail, and several other items she hadn't noticed him collecting. "When the water comes back, at least we'll have these."

Catherine looked at the boy and realized she didn't even know his name. She had walked through her drowned town with this stranger for hours now, had shared pieces of her personal history, and she couldn't even identify him beyond his age and his apparent relationship to a grandfather she had never met.

"What's your name?" she asked.

"Daniel." He was waiting for her name in return. It seemed important, this exchange of identities.

"Catherine."

"Thank you for showing me where you lived, Catherine."

She wanted to do something for him, some gesture that acknowledged the kindness of his serious attention. She opened her equipment bag and withdrew her last photograph of the house, taken from the street sometime in the late eighties, before her mother had moved away and the house had begun its long process of becoming what it now was. She had no idea why she still carried it, why it had made its way into her surveying kit at some point in the last decade.

"Keep this," she said, handing it to him. "For your grandfather."

Daniel took it carefully, as if it were a sacred object. In a way, she supposed it was: a document of something that no longer existed, preserved against time and loss and the slow forgetting that claimed all human settlements eventually.

Catherine spent five more days mapping Riverside. She documented downtown, the residential sections, the church and school and library. She recorded everything with professional precision, and her notebooks filled with measurements and coordinates and the careful language of objective description. At night, she would return to her motel room and transcribe her notes into the digital format the county required. At day, she would return to the drowned town and map another section of its grid.

Daniel appeared most days, sometimes bringing artifacts, sometimes just watching her work. She learned that he was thirteen, that his grandfather had been a banker in Riverside, that his mother was a nurse and his father was absent. He was a solitary kid, the kind who collected things and paid attention to the details of the world that most people overlooked. Catherine recognized herself in him, the child she had been before she learned to want to leave.

On the sixth day, as she was finishing her survey of the eastern quadrant, Daniel asked her if she was going to stay.

"In the county?"

"In the town. The new town. When you finish your work."

Catherine realized he was asking if she was going to come home. The word hung between them, unspoken, but present.

"I don't know," she said honestly. "I have a life in Raleigh. A job, a house, friends."

"Do you like it?"

She had never asked herself that question directly. She had built a competent life in the years since leaving Riverside, one that accommodated her need for precision and her understanding of how things fit together in space. She had avoided deep entanglements, had kept her relationships light and professional. This had seemed like success to her, like the opposite of what had happened to her parents' marriage, which had foundered on too much intimacy and too few shared visions of the future.

"It's a good life," she said carefully. "Different from what I imagined when I was young."

"My grandfather says the best thing about memory is you can revise it. You can look at your past differently as you get older, and sometimes you realize things you missed the first time."

Catherine was struck by the wisdom of this statement, the way it recognized that our selves change and that this change allows for new interpretation of old events. She thought about her teenage self, desperate to escape a town she had found stifling and ordinary. She thought about the woman she had become, who had escaped but had never quite stopped running.

The final coordinates led her back to the town square, to the plaza where she had first seen Daniel. She set up her instrument at the center point and took her last reading, and when she looked through the lens, she saw the town not as it was now but as it had been: intact, busy, full of the particular energy of a place where people still believed in themselves and their future. The image lasted only as long as she held her eye to the lens, but it felt important—a glimpse of something true and irretrievable.

That evening, she sat down and wrote a comprehensive report for the county administrator. The documentation was complete. Riverside, as it existed in the moment of exposure, had been measured and recorded. It was a good report, thorough and professional, and it would go into whatever archives the county maintained for the eventual return of the water.

She left two days later. Daniel came to see her off at the motel, bringing his grandfather with him. The old man moved slowly, supported by a cane, but his eyes were sharp and interested. He thanked Catherine for preserving his town, and she realized that this was exactly what she had done. Not just mapped it, but preserved it—first in her measurements and her notes, and more importantly, in the act of paying attention, of bearing witness to what had been lost.

"He would have liked you," the grandfather said. "My father. The one who built the bank building where you took your first reading."

Catherine drove away from Millbrook County in the late afternoon, watching the landscape transform in her rearview mirror from the eroded slopes of the reservoir to the green rising hills of the present. She thought about her mother, about her father, about the particular geography of her childhood that had never really drowned at all—it had simply moved into the past, where all the places we leave eventually reside.

She would go back to Raleigh and her job and her careful, competent life. But she knew now that some part of her had never left Riverside. It had been underwater all this time, preserved in the dark and the silence, waiting for her to remember that it was real and that it mattered. The photographs in her surveying kit were proof of that—evidence that she had actually lived in a place, that she had been a child once, that some things in life were worth leaving and some things were worth returning to, even if only in memory, even if only to take their measure one final time before they disappeared forever.
