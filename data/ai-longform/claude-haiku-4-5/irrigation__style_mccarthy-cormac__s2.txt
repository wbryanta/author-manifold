The elder brother stood in the dry grass with a length of pipe resting across his shoulders like some instrument of penance and he studied the break in the line where the water had ceased its ancient labor three days prior. The younger brother crouched in the dust some yards distant with a wrench in his fist and he did not look up when Thomas spoke.

It's shot through. Corroded clear through.

I can see it.

There's no patching this one.

I know.

Thomas lowered the pipe and set it carefully in the grass and he wiped his brow with the back of a weathered hand. The orchard stretched away behind them in the heat shimmer, the trees hunched and pale as if diseased, their fruit small and hard and worthless. The sun was high and without mercy and the sky held no mercy in it either. It was the kind of June that presaged drought and ruin.

Your man from the bank called again yesterday, Thomas said.

I don't want to hear about it.

He wants an answer. He said they'll take thirty-eight thousand if we sign by the end of the month.

I said I don't want to hear about it.

Thomas picked up the pipe again and he held it before him like evidence in some trial. The metal was eaten through in three separate places and the corrosion had a quality almost of violence about it, as if something had gnawed there with intent. Around them the orchard was entirely still. No wind moved in the leaves. The irrigation system sat dormant and dying, a map of obsolescence written in copper and rust across twenty acres that their grandfather had planted in another century altogether, one that seemed now impossibly distant.

We can't keep pumping money into this, James said. He rose from his crouch and he stood with the wrench loose in his hand and looked at his brother with something that might have been pity or might have been contempt.

That's what you think?

That's what I know.

You know what you know because you've already decided. You made up your mind last fall when the crop came in at half of what it should have been.

And what did you do last fall, James? What did you do when the crop came in? You sat in the house and drank coffee and talked about the weather and pretended that it was temporary. That it was some passing thing that would correct itself if we just waited long enough.

We've been here a hundred years.

We've been here bankrupt for the last five of them.

Thomas set the pipe down again and he walked toward the break in the line where the earth was still wet and darkened. He crouched and examined the rupture and he could see the mineral deposits layered there, the testimony of decades of water passing through these channels. They had built this line together, he and James, nearly thirty years ago. They had laid it out in a summer when they were young and strong and certain that they understood something true about the world and their place in it. It seemed to him now that they had understood nothing at all.

What would you do? Thomas said. If we sold. What would you do with your half?

I don't know yet.

You don't know.

Not yet.

You'd leave. That's what you'd do. You'd take your share and you'd leave and you'd never come back to this place except perhaps to drive past it and see what someone else has made of it.

And what would you do, Thomas? Would you stay?

His brother was silent for a long time. He remained crouched in the wet earth and he did not turn to look at James. The question hung between them like something visible, something with weight and dimension.

I don't know, Thomas said at last.

Then we're equal in our ignorance.

The younger brother walked back toward the truck where they had laid out the new sections of pipe. It was expensive pipe, the kind they should have had ten years ago, the kind that cost more than they could reasonably spend on a failing enterprise. But he had bought it anyway because there seemed no point in buying anything at all if you bought it in half-measures. He carried two sections back and set them carefully in the grass beside the break.

Measure it, he said.

Thomas took up a tape measure and he ran it along the damaged section and he called out numbers. James wrote them on a piece of cardboard with a stub of pencil and then he selected the appropriate lengths of new pipe and began the work of disconnection. His hands were sure and practiced and he did not need to think about what he was doing. The wrenches turned in his hands like things alive and the bolts came free and the old pipe fell away into the grass like the exoskeleton of something ancient.

They had been doing this work for forty-two years, the two of them, though not always together. There had been years when James was away, years when he worked in the valley towns and came back only in the seasons that required his presence. Thomas had managed alone during those years. He had managed but something had been lost in the managing, some essential thing that you could not name but that you felt in the work itself, the sense that the work meant something because another person stood beside you and understood that meaning.

You know what the land's worth now, James said. He was connecting the new sections with a methodical precision that seemed almost meditative. He did not look at his brother as he spoke. The money that's being offered, it's not a gift. It's not even really a fair price. It's the price you get when you have no other choice. When everyone knows you need to sell and they're willing to wait you out.

Then we wait.

For what?

For whatever comes.

The same thing always comes, Thomas. Time. Weather. More bills that we can't pay. More years where we harvest less and spend more and the equipment gets older and the trees get older and we get older. That's what comes. That's all that comes anymore.

Thomas did not respond. He stood and he watched his brother work and he thought about the years that were behind them and the years that might be ahead. The orchard had come to him through his father and his father's father and it was possible that he had always known that the chain would break with him. It was possible that this knowledge had been in him since childhood, some dark certainty about the nature of continuity and possession. That things ended. That you could not hold them, only carry them for a time and then pass them on to ruin.

You've already sold it in your mind, James said. He was tightening the final bolts now and his hands moved with the unconscious grace of long practice. You've already let it go. That's why you keep saying these things about staying. Because you know it's impossible and you can't bear to admit it.

That's not true.

It is true. You just don't want to see it.

The younger brother finished the final connection and he came upright and he stretched his back. He looked at the new pipe lying there in the grass, gleaming dully in the afternoon light, and he thought about the invisible future that moved toward them at the speed of time itself, indifferent and inexorable. Then he turned to his brother and he spoke with a kind of weary compassion that seemed to come from some well of understanding that he had not known he possessed.

Go turn on the pump, he said.

Thomas did not move.

Go on. Let's see if it holds.

The elder brother walked toward the pump house, a small structure of corrugated metal that stood at the far end of the orchard. He could hear his brother behind him moving slowly, as if the work had exhausted him, though the sun had barely begun its descent toward evening. The pump house door was warped in its frame and it took both hands to force it open. Inside, the air was thick and metallic and the pump itself sat like some great inert creature, waiting for purpose to be restored to it.

He threw the switch and the pump shuddered to life with a sound like a throat clearing. The pressure in the line was immediate and he could feel it in the vibration of the machinery, in the subtle shift in the quality of sound that emerged from the iron. He stood in the doorway and waited, listening. There was no spout of water erupting from the newly mended section. There was no sound of rupture or failure. The water was moving through the line invisibly, as it had for decades, as perhaps it would continue to move until the end of things.

When he emerged from the pump house James was already walking back toward the break in the line. He was moving with purpose now, with something that resembled urgency. Thomas followed him and when they reached the site of the repair the water had already begun to flow, the earth darkening in a spreading stain around the new connections. They stood together and watched it spread and neither of them spoke.

It's holding, James said.

It is.

It might hold for another season. Maybe two.

Or it might hold longer.

But eventually it won't. That's the thing you don't seem to understand. Everything that holds, eventually doesn't. Everything that works, eventually fails. It's not some personal tragedy, Thomas. It's just the nature of what we're dealing with.

I understand that.

Do you? Do you really? Because it seems to me that you've spent the last five years pretending that time doesn't move. That if you just keep doing the same work and making the same repairs and holding on to the same land, then somehow you can stop history. You can just freeze it all in place and nothing will change.

Thomas was silent. He watched the water spreading through the soil and he thought about all the seasons that this water had nourished, all the harvests that it had made possible. He thought about the taste of fruit that had grown here in his childhood, how perfect it had seemed, how inexplicable in its sweetness. He thought about his father standing in this orchard in the early morning light, touching the bark of the trees with a kind of reverence, as if he were reading something written there in a language that only he understood.

I never believed time didn't move, Thomas said. I always knew it was moving. I've felt it moving every day that I've been here. But there's a difference between watching time move and helping it along. There's a difference between accepting what comes and inviting it into your house.

Is there? I don't know anymore. I think maybe it's just the same thing with different names.

They stood in silence. The evening was beginning to establish itself on the horizon now, a deepening of color in the west that suggested fire or violence or some transformative process that was already underway. The water continued to spread and somewhere in the distance a truck moved along the highway, headed for some place that James would perhaps never see and Thomas would never have any reason to visit.

We could stay for one more season, Thomas said. We could try for one more harvest. If it comes in better than the last one then we don't sell. If it comes in worse, we sell in the fall and take whatever we can get.

That's not a plan. That's just postponement.

Then what is a plan?

A plan is accepting what is and deciding what comes next. A plan is not waiting for something that won't come.

Thomas looked at his brother. He saw in James's face something of his own features reflected as if in distorting glass, the family resemblance filtered through thirty years of divergence and separate choices. He saw his brother's fatigue and his brother's certainty and underneath both of those things he saw something that might have been love, that might have been the last attenuated strand of whatever bond had held them together since childhood.

If we sell, what happens to the house?

It comes with the land. Whoever buys it will probably knock it down. It's not worth saving.

Then where do you go?

I go where people go who don't have land anymore. I go to town. I get a job of some kind. I live a quiet life. It's not so terrible, Thomas. It's what most people do.

It's what the people who had no choice ever did. We had a choice.

And we still do. We can choose to stop bleeding money into something that's already dead. We can choose to survive.

Thomas turned away from his brother. He walked back toward the truck where they had piled the old sections of pipe, the corroded and useless metal that would be sold for scrap and turned into something else entirely. He sat on the edge of the truck bed and he looked back toward the house, that old structure that held decades of accumulated life within its weathered boards. His mother had died in that house. His father had lived nearly all of his seventy-three years within its walls. It was possible that Thomas himself would die there, if he could keep it long enough. It was also possible that he would not.

The pump was still running. He could hear it from this distance, that steady thudding that was the sound of work continuing, of water moving through channels in the earth, of the old mechanisms of agriculture still turning because no one had yet found the will to stop them. He thought about his grandfather planting these trees, about the vision that had animated that work, the belief that he was creating something that would outlast him, that would flower for generations and provide both sustenance and continuity. And then he thought about himself and James, the last generation that would know this orchard as something other than property, other than commodity, other than a story that they would tell themselves about what had been.

You should turn off the pump, James said. He had come to stand beside his brother without Thomas noticing. They were both tired now, tired in the way that the day's work produced, but also tired in some deeper way that had nothing to do with physical exertion. He's wasting water if there's no one to use it.

Let it run a while.

Why?

Because it's working. Because it's moving water through land that we still own. Because tomorrow it might break again and we'll have to fix it or we won't and either way something will have changed.

James did not argue. He sat beside his brother on the truck bed and they watched the pump house in the middle distance, that small structure of corrugated metal that contained the simple machinery of their lives. The sun was falling now, dropping toward the mountains in the west, and the light took on that quality of golden suspension that occurred only in the last hour before darkness began to establish its reign.

You know he's right, Thomas said at last. The man from the bank. You know that he's right when he says it won't get better.

I know.

So why do you keep fighting?

Because someone should. Because if no one fights then everything just collapses faster. Because I don't know how to do anything else.

James nodded slowly. He seemed to be considering something, some question that was older and deeper than the matter of selling or not selling the land. He picked up a piece of the old corroded pipe that had fallen into the truck bed and he examined it in his hands, turning it slowly as if it might reveal some hidden meaning in its pattern of decay.

What if I'm not your brother anymore, he said. What if I leave and I don't come back and you never see me again except maybe at a funeral years from now. What if that's what happens. Can you do this alone.

Thomas did not answer immediately. He was thinking of all the work that lay ahead, all the seasons that would need to be maintained if the orchard was to survive. It was too much work for one person. It had been too much work for two people for the last decade. To do it alone would be to slowly fail, to watch the whole thing diminish incrementally until there was nothing left but an old man on an old property, waiting for time to finish what circumstance had begun.

No, Thomas said. I can't.

Then you know what that means.

Yes.

They sat in silence as the light continued to change around them. The orchard was beginning to assume the quality of something dreamed, something that existed on the boundary between memory and present reality. Thomas thought about the choices that had brought them to this moment, all the small decisions that had accumulated into inevitability. He thought about his brother's life in the towns, about whatever it was that drew him away from this place and kept him moving forward toward some future that had no room in it for orchards or the past.

I'll tell him yes, Thomas said. I'll call him tomorrow and tell him we accept the offer.

Will you?

Yes. But not until the season's over. Not until we know what the harvest is.

He might not wait.

Then we'll know it was never really his offer at all. That it was just a story he told us about what was possible.

James set down the piece of pipe and he rose from the truck bed. He stretched his back and his shoulders and he looked toward the house where their lives had taken all the forms that they would take. The pump was still running. The water was still moving through the earth. The old machines were still working, for now, in that fragile and temporary way that everything works.

I'm going inside, James said. I'm going to make some coffee.

All right.

You should come in when you get cold.

I will.

Thomas remained there after his brother had gone, watching the light die in the west. He could see the pump house and the line of irrigation that ran toward the house and beyond it the trees in their patient arrangement, waiting for whatever would come next. The water was moving through the earth. The seasons were turning. And somewhere in the distance, though he could not hear it, time was moving at its inexorable pace, carrying forward into some future that no one could see and no one could stop.

He rose at last and walked toward the house where his brother was making coffee, where the light was beginning to emerge in the windows like something breaking through darkness. The pump was still running. It would run until someone turned it off, and then it would stop, and that stopping would be the beginning of something else entirely, some new phase of their lives that they could imagine but not yet occupy. He climbed the steps to the porch and opened the door and went inside.
