The old man had been dead two years when the pump began to fail. It sat in the west field like some rusted oracle dispensing its diminished wisdom in half-hearted spasms until the day it quit altogether and did not start again. The lines that fed it, those great arterial PVC conduits that had carried water to the trees since before the brothers were born, had begun to crack in the summer heat, weeping small tributaries into the hard ground until the earth there grew dark and strange and nothing would grow in it at all.

James had driven up from the city on a Saturday morning in June with tools in the back of his truck and a prospectus in his jacket pocket. He found his brother already in the field, standing over a section of pipe that lay exposed like something exhumed, its white surface filmed with dust and the mineral deposits of forty years. Thomas looked up as James approached, his face reddened from the sun and his shirt already soaked through at the shoulders.

You're late, Thomas said.

I came as soon as I could get away.

The old pump's shot. Has been for a week. I've been trying to run water from the north line but you can see where it's split. Somewhere between the second row and the irrigation access. Could be thirty feet of bad pipe. Could be fifty.

James set down his tools and knelt to examine the split. It ran along the length of the exposed section like a crack in a dried riverbed, and when he pressed his thumb against it the material felt fragile, barely holding its shape. The sun beat down on them both and neither spoke for a time.

How much water are we losing? James asked.

Enough that the trees are starting to show stress. Look at the leaves on the Valencia block.

They walked together through the orchard, moving between the trees in that particular way that men who have done this since childhood do, with a kind of unconscious grace despite the heat and their mutual wariness. The Valencia block lay on the eastern slope where the ground rose slightly and the water pressure was always poorest. The leaves were beginning to curl at their edges, a dull condition James recognized from his youth, from other years when drought or neglect had stretched the reserves of the land to their limit. Not dead yet but dying. Making their slow way toward death like something bearing a heavy burden.

It'll kill them in another week if we don't get water moving, Thomas said. The whole block. Maybe more than that.

Then we'd better fix it.

They spent the morning uncovering more of the line, using a shovel and eventually an excavator that Thomas had rented, scraping back the soil to expose the extent of the damage. It was worse than they'd feared. Two major splits and a section of approximately forty feet where the material had begun to separate at the joints, the metal couplings corroded into something barely functional. The sun rose higher and hotter and they worked in a kind of silence broken only by the sounds of their labor, the grunt of exertion and the scrape of metal on earth.

By noon they had the damaged section fully exposed. James sat in the scant shade of one of the trees—a Valencia, Thomas noted—and drank water from a jug. His hands were raw from the shovel work and his back was beginning to complain with the particular eloquence of middle age. He was forty-three years old and had spent the last fifteen years in an office where the most strenuous thing he did was walk to a car park. The distance between that life and this one seemed to him suddenly immeasurable.

You've thought about what needs to happen here, Thomas said. It wasn't really a question.

I've thought about it.

And?

And I've talked to the developer. The man who was here last month.

I know who you mean. I told you not to do that.

You told me a lot of things. Most of it was the same thing, really. Just different versions of the same thing.

Thomas had moved into the shade as well, sitting on a section of the broken pipe with his hands on his knees. He was forty-seven, five years older than James, and those five years showed in the weathering of his face and the gray that had come into his hair in the last year especially, since their father died. Looking at him James felt something that might have been pity or might have been contempt—he couldn't quite separate the two.

The land is worth more than you think, Thomas said. If we could get the groves back to producing, we could sell it for considerably more than—

The groves aren't coming back. They might have ten years ago. They might have come back five years ago. But they're not coming back now. Every year they deteriorate. Every year we spend money we don't have trying to maintain something that wants to die.

It doesn't want anything. It's land. Land doesn't have desires.

You know what I mean.

I do know what you mean. And it's foolish. It's a foolish way of thinking. The trees produce if you maintain them. They'll produce for another thirty years if we do this right.

This right. Christ. There is no right, Thomas. There's only this, and then there's moving on.

Thomas stood and walked to the edge of the exposed pipe, looking down at it as though it might speak to him, offer some counsel from the depths of its damaged materiality. The sun had begun to move westward and the shadows of the trees fell across the field in long dark bands, like the bars of some celestial prison.

You know what Father paid for this place? Thomas said. Do you remember that conversation?

I was too young.

He paid fourteen thousand dollars. For all of it. The house, the equipment, the trees. Fourteen thousand dollars in 1968. Adjusted for inflation that's less than a hundred thousand in today's money. He built this from nothing. He and Mother both. They worked eighteen-hour days for twenty years so that we would have something. And now you want to turn around and sell it to some developer so he can build houses on it. So people can move here and complain about the smell of fertilizer and the early-morning irrigation noise.

I want to let go of something that's killing us. I want to have a life that isn't about this.

You have a life. You have a different life than this, which is what you chose. But choosing that didn't mean you got to kill this.

James stood. His back protested but he ignored it. He was angry now in a way he hadn't expected to be, a cold and thorough anger that seemed to come from somewhere beneath his current life, from some younger version of himself he'd thought he'd successfully abandoned.

Don't talk to me about Father, he said. Father's dead. He doesn't get to dictate how we live anymore. And frankly, if he could see how much you've let this place deteriorate, he'd be ashamed. He would. You know he would.

Thomas turned and looked at him for a long moment without speaking. There was something in his expression that James couldn't read, something closed off and private. When he finally spoke his voice was quiet.

He knew it was failing. He knew what was coming. He told me six months before he died. He said the land would be gone within a generation if we didn't figure out something. And he said—he specifically said—that I shouldn't let you sell it just because you'd moved on and didn't care anymore.

Then he shouldn't have let it fail. He should have fixed it.

He was seventy-four years old and he had emphysema. He was dying. I don't know what you wanted him to do.

The afternoon wore on and neither of them returned to the broken pipe until late in the day, when the sun had begun to lose some of its ferocity. James drove into town and returned with supplies: thirty feet of new PVC, new couplings, primer and cement. He'd worked in construction for two years after high school before going to college, and though nearly three decades had passed since then he remembered the rhythms of the work, the way the materials fit together, the patience required to do it correctly.

They worked in the cooling hours before evening, taking apart the damaged section piece by piece, cutting out the bad couplings, preparing the new pipe. Thomas's anger seemed to have burned away and left behind something more like resignation. He worked efficiently, following James's lead without comment, handing him tools and materials as they were needed. There was a kind of grace in this division of labor, a rhythm that their bodies remembered even if their minds had forgotten it.

By the time the sun touched the western hills they had completed the repairs and sealed the joints with the new couplings, testing the fit with careful precision, making sure each connection was sound. James stood and looked at the repaired section, turning it over in his mind to ensure they hadn't missed anything. The new white pipe gleamed in the dying light, a stark contrast to the aged and weathered material that surrounded it.

We'll need to bleed the air out of the line, he said. Tomorrow, once we've connected everything. And we'll need to replace the pump eventually. This one's done.

I know it is.

The developer is offering three point five million. The market appraisal came in at three point two. That's actually generous.

I don't care what he's offering.

You should care. That's enough to retire on. For both of us. Or nearly enough. We could split it and we'd never have to worry about money again. We could actually live.

We live now. This is living.

No. This is the opposite of living. This is dying slowly while you pretend you're doing something noble. This is spending every cent we make on upkeep and equipment and replacement parts, watching the returns diminish year after year, knowing that eventually you're going to have to give up anyway because the land won't support us anymore. I know how this ends, Thomas. I've seen it happen a thousand times to farmers like Father.

Thomas had begun gathering the tools, placing them carefully in the toolbox as though each one were a relic of some previous world that was rapidly vanishing. When he spoke his voice was different, softer and more distant than before.

He left me the house and twenty acres. Did you know that? In the will. He gave you the trust fund for your education and some money for getting started, but he left me this. The house and the land and the responsibility. He didn't leave it to you.

He left it to you because he knew you'd stay. He left it to you because he knew I'd run, and I did. But that doesn't mean I'm wrong about what needs to happen.

Maybe not. But it means you don't get to make the decision about it.

The sun was nearly gone now, only the faintest light remaining in the western sky, a band of orange and purple that seemed to hang suspended above the mountains. The trees around them had darkened to shadows, indistinct shapes that might have been anything, any landscape, any haunted place from any story ever told. The broken pipe lay behind them in the pale starlight that was just beginning to emerge, looking small and insignificant now, a temporary wound in the face of something much larger and older than either of them understood.

James picked up his jacket and moved toward the truck. Thomas followed him, the toolbox in his hands. Neither of them had spoken about what would happen next, what the next step would be, and neither of them spoke about it now. There seemed to be an agreement, some wordless consensus, that they would continue as they were for the moment. That tomorrow they would return here and complete the work. That they would get water flowing to the trees again. That they would not discuss the future beyond the immediate necessity of what their hands could do.

At the truck James turned to his brother. In the fading light Thomas looked exactly as he had in photographs from twenty years ago, before their mother had gotten sick, before their father had begun his slow decline. It was startling and painful, this ghost of who he'd been.

I'll be back at seven, James said.

I'll be here.

James drove down the dirt road that connected the property to the highway, his tires raising dust that hung in the still air like something alive, some particulate ghost of the day's labor. In the rearview mirror he could see the dark bulk of the property receding, the cluster of trees at the center that marked the house and the immediate yard. Somewhere behind them Thomas was likely walking the fields, doing whatever it was he did in the evening hours when the work was finished. Checking on things. Listening to the land. Communing with ghosts.

The town was small and hadn't changed much since James had left it. The main street was lined with businesses that had failed or transformed themselves into something unrecognizable. The hardware store that his father had patronized for fifty years was now a yoga studio. The feed store was a antique mall. The bank had been converted into a brewery. In some ways the changes made him feel less guilty about leaving, as though the place itself was committing suicide in slow motion, and his departure was simply the beginning of a process that was larger and more comprehensive than any single person's choices.

He ate dinner alone in a Mexican restaurant that occupied the same physical space as a Mexican restaurant from his childhood but operated under a different name and aesthetic, a trendy updated version of something that had been adequate before. The food was somehow worse, though he couldn't have articulated exactly why. He ordered a second beer and sat watching the few patrons, mostly young people he didn't recognize, people from somewhere else, passing through or staying temporarily before the restlessness that infected everyone would carry them away.

The motel where he'd booked a room was only slightly less depressing than he remembered it being. The carpet smelled of decades of cigarette smoke despite the no-smoking policy, and the air conditioner ran at a volume that suggested it was working hard to accomplish very little. He lay in the dark and thought about the pipe, about the water that would flow through it once they'd finished the repairs. He thought about the trees, which were still living in the way that trees lived—without complaint, without expectation, without any sense of their own mortality beyond the slow cellular knowledge of growth and decline.

In the morning he returned to the property to find Thomas already awake and working. He'd run new line from the pump location to the repaired section and was now in the process of installing a new pump, one of the smaller models that they'd ordered online and had shipped to a local hardware store. It was a beautiful piece of equipment, James had to admit, assembled with Japanese precision and efficiency, all the parts fitting together in logical sequence like the components of some elegant argument.

They worked through the morning completing the installation and running water through the newly repaired sections, checking for leaks, making adjustments where necessary. By noon they had completed the work. The water flowed clear and steady through the repaired pipe, dividing at the junction boxes and flowing out toward the various irrigation lines that served the different sections of the orchard. The pressure gauge on the new pump read steady at forty-five PSI, exactly what the system required.

They stood together watching the water emerge from the irrigation heads, seeing the first rows of trees receive the water they'd been denied for a week. The relief was visible almost immediately—the way the leaves seemed to lift, to turn their faces back toward the sun. James understood that this was probably his imagination, that plants didn't respond this quickly to water, that any visual improvement would take days. But he imagined it anyway, and Thomas seemed to be imagining it too.

There's a meeting with the developer next week, James said. The man wants to know about our timeline. He's apparently interested in beginning site work before the end of summer.

Tell him we'll let him know.

You understand I'm going to sell eventually.

Yes.

And you understand why.

I understand why you think you need to. I understand that you believe there's no other option. I don't agree with you, but I understand.

They walked back toward the house in the heat of early afternoon. The sky was a clear and absolute blue, the kind of blue that suggested clarity and truth and the possibility that some things, at least, might be simple and comprehensible. The shadow of Thomas's body moved alongside James's own on the ground, and for a moment they seemed like a single figure viewed from two different angles, two different versions of the same essential person.

At the house, Thomas produced a bottle of beer from the refrigerator and they sat on the porch that faced out toward the Valencia block. The trees were already looking better, James thought, though he knew this was probably impossible. The work they'd done had been merely mechanical, a temporary solution to a larger problem that would not remain solved. The land was failing and would continue to fail. The decision about what to do with it had already been made long before today, in the moment when their father had first realized he couldn't maintain it alone, or perhaps even before that, in the moment of his death, or perhaps in the moment of his birth, every life containing within it the seeds of its own ending, every beginning the commencement of a particular kind of decline.

Next week I'll call the developer, James said. I'll tell him we need another month. That we need time to think about it.

That's not true.

It's not, but it's what I'm going to tell him anyway.

Thomas nodded and finished his beer. He looked older in the afternoon light, James thought. Or perhaps he simply looked more like himself, the self that this land and this life had made him become. Neither of them spoke about what would happen after the month was through, what the eventual outcome would be. The water continued to flow through the irrigation system, the pump running steady and sure, the sound of it a kind of constant companion to their silence, the sound of necessity, the sound of maintenance, the sound of trying, however briefly or temporarily, to keep something from failing.
