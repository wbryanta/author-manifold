# The Crossing

The ferry smelled of diesel and salt and something else he couldn't name—the accumulated breath of a thousand travelers perhaps, or the slow decay of the ship itself. Michael stood at the rail on the starboard side, watching the lights of the city recede, and tried to remember the last time he had seen his brother alive.

It was 1997. He was certain of the year because he'd just bought his first decent camera, a Pentax that David had made fun of, calling it a tourist's toy. That memory felt thin now, worn transparent from handling. Whether it had been summer or winter, whether they'd been meeting by accident or design—these details had evaporated. Only the shape of the feeling remained: the two of them standing somewhere, and the particular quality of David's disdain, which had always been more intimate than affection.

Michael went inside. The passenger lounge was fluorescent-bright and nearly empty this late in the season. An old woman with a wheeled walker sat near the windows, a paperback open on her lap. Three teenagers occupied a booth near the cafeteria, speaking Norwegian in the rapid, musical way of the young. A man in a high-visibility vest was wiping tables with systematic precision, moving from one to the next with the kind of practiced efficiency that suggested he'd been doing this for decades.

The last time Michael had heard from David directly was 2003. An email, unsigned, containing only a URL. Michael had clicked it once, seen that it led nowhere, and never tried again. Then, for twenty years, silence that had gradually solidified into something like fact—the fact of David's absence becoming indistinguishable from the fact of his existence elsewhere, in some other life that had no intersection with Michael's own.

He bought a coffee from the cafeteria that tasted like warm aluminum and found a seat by a window. His phone buzzed. A text from Annika: *Safe travel. Call when you arrive.* He'd told her it was probably nothing, that the police were likely mistaken, that the body washed up on the shore of an island in northern Norway almost certainly belonged to someone else. Still, she'd driven him to the ferry terminal in Tromsø and waited until he'd checked in, her hand briefly touching his shoulder as he stood to leave—a gesture of such restrained tenderness that it had nearly undone him.

They'd met two years ago at a conference in Stockholm. She was a conservator of medieval manuscripts, precise in her movements, with a way of listening that made you feel as though you were the only interesting thing happening in the world. She worked in Copenhagen mostly, and Michael in Stockholm, and they'd fallen into a pattern of careful, measured togetherness that suited them both. Neither of them spoke of the future or made plans that extended beyond the next month. It was, he'd come to understand, a kind of devotion—this refusal to pretend either of them could promise more than the present moment.

He slept fitfully in a small cabin on Deck C, waking multiple times to the sound of the engines and the subtle motion of the sea. Around three in the morning he got up and went back outside. The sky had cleared, and the stars were extraordinary—not the thin, distant stars visible from the cities of southern Scandinavia, but vast and pressing, as though the darkness itself had weight.

The strait they were crossing had a name he'd looked up: Tyrifjorden. Or was that something else? He couldn't remember. The navigation apps on his phone showed them as a small white dot moving through a pale blue void, though they were actually surrounded by land—the northern coast of Norway on one side, the island where David's body (if it was David) had washed ashore on the other. But the maps flattened everything, rendered the vast comprehensible, turned terror into mere topography.

By daylight, Michael could see the rocky outcroppings and twisted pine forests of the islands they passed. A fishing village with red and yellow houses clustered at the water's edge. A lighthouse on a headland, white and red striped, starkly beautiful in its isolation. He found himself thinking about David as a boy, perhaps eleven or twelve, crouched by a stream with a camera Michael had given him for his birthday—one of the few gifts between them that hadn't been met with immediate scorn. David had been focused, almost meditative in his attention. He'd photographed the same patch of moss and water for an hour, changing positions infinitesimally, adjusting for light. Michael had asked him what he was doing. David had said he was trying to photograph the moment just before things change, and then he'd looked at Michael with an expression of such concentrated intensity that Michael had felt seen in a way he usually didn't.

That was before David got angry at the world. Or before the anger that had always been there became something he couldn't contain.

Michael was called to the reception desk as they pulled into the ferry terminal. The woman there, blonde and efficient, told him his name in a way that wasn't quite a question and handed him a business card. Inspector Soren Roth. A phone number. A small note written in precise, slanting handwriting: *Come directly to the station. We have a small room prepared. Thank you for coming so quickly.*

The police station was a modern building of pale wood and glass, all Scandinavian restraint. Inspector Roth was younger than Michael expected, perhaps in his mid-forties, with the calm, measured demeanor of someone who dealt regularly with small tragedies—the manageable crises of a town where such things were still notable enough to command the personal attention of senior police.

"Thank you for coming," he said again, as though Michael had had a choice in the matter. He had a slight accent that suggested northern Norwegian. "I know this must be difficult. We've had the body stored in a cold facility, and we've attempted to preserve it as well as possible given its condition. The remains were found three days ago washed up on the shore near Honningsvaag. We identified him through dental records—there was some damage, but enough to work with. The Norwegian Dental Registry showed a match to a David Henrik Johansson, who we understand was your brother."

Michael nodded. He'd provided David's dental records himself, dug through old files until he found the name of a dentist David had gone to in Oslo in the late nineties.

"He'd been in the water quite some time," Roth continued. "The pathologist estimates between six and ten months. There was significant decomposition, obviously, but the cold water and the time of year he went in—most likely late February or March—would have slowed certain processes. The cause of death is unclear. There's no obvious trauma that we can identify, but the damage from submersion makes it difficult to be conclusive. There was nothing in his pockets—nothing that would indicate how he came to be in the water."

They walked down a hallway that smelled of disinfectant and something vaguely organic. Roth was still talking, explaining protocols and procedures, filling the silence with information that didn't quite matter. The cold room was just that—a small space where a covered form lay on a metal table. The form was human-shaped and no larger than Michael's memory of David, which was probably inaccurate. Everything was a lie you told yourself about the past.

"We'll keep the cover on," Roth said. "It's mostly dental identification anyway. You're just confirming that you recognize any distinguishing marks or features that might help us. Any scars, tattoos, birthmarks."

Michael stepped forward. Roth pulled the sheet back to the shoulders, revealing a face that was and wasn't David's face. It was the right shape, perhaps. The bone structure seemed correct. But it was also something else—something other than human, or rather something reduced from humanity to its basic architecture. The eyes were gone, replaced by emptiness that felt obscene to look at. The skin had the texture and color of old leather. The hair—which was definitely David's hair, dark and fine—was plastered to the skull in a way that made Michael dizzy.

There was a birthmark on the left shoulder. Small, brown, shaped vaguely like a continent. Michael remembered this mark from when they were children, when they'd changed clothes in the same room before going swimming. David had hated it and wore shirts that covered it whenever possible.

"Yes," Michael said. "That's him. That's David."

He said it quietly, as though the thing on the table might overhear and object.

Roth nodded, not unkindly. "Thank you. I'm very sorry." He pulled the sheet back up, a small mercy. "I know you would have liked more answers. Unfortunately, in cases where there's been extended submersion, we're often limited in what we can determine. If there's anything you can tell us about his circumstances—whether he was suicidal, or depressed, or troubled in any way—it might help us provide some closure. The prosecutor may still open an investigation, but without clear evidence of foul play..."

Michael wasn't listening anymore. He was thinking about the last conversation he'd had with David, which had been on the telephone sometime in the early 2000s. David had called—this itself was unusual, as David never called—and their mother had put him on. David had said, "Are you still taking pictures?" and Michael had said yes, and David had said, "Still pretending to understand the world through a lens?" and Michael had said, "Maybe," and there had been a long silence, and then David had hung up.

Michael had always assumed it was a rejection. Now he wondered if it had been something else. A question, perhaps. A reaching out phrased as contempt because David had no other language for it.

They gave him David's belongings in a plastic bag, though there was very little: a watch that had stopped, a wool sweater that disintegrated slightly when they tried to remove it from him, some buttons, some indeterminate objects that had been fabric or leather once. Nothing that told a story. Nothing that explained anything.

Michael went back to the hotel they'd booked for him—a small place near the harbor—and sat on the bed in the dark, holding the plastic bag. Through the window he could see the strait where David had been, suspended in the cold and the darkness, for six to ten months. He could imagine it so clearly: his brother's body moving with the tides, wearing away, becoming less and less David with each passing day until he was just matter, just meat, until the essential David—whatever that had been—was long gone.

He called Annika. It was early evening in Copenhagen.

"Is it him?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Oh, Michael."

He didn't respond. There wasn't anything to respond to. They sat in silence for a moment, the kind of companionable silence that was only possible between people who understood that not all conversations required words.

"When are you coming home?" she asked.

"Tomorrow. The morning ferry."

"I'll pick you up."

After he hung up, Michael opened his laptop and found the photographs he'd taken over the years. Not of David—he'd taken very few pictures of his brother, and David had hated being photographed. Rather, the other photographs, the ones he'd taken because David had said they were all pretense. The bridge in Stockholm, photographed a thousand ways. The forests around the lake where he'd grown up. The faces of people he didn't know, caught in moments they couldn't control. All the evidence of his attention, his desire to understand the world through the frame.

He wondered what David had wanted to photograph in those final years. He wondered what David had seen in the water, in the moment before the cold took him completely. He wondered if his brother had been angry right up until the end, or if the anger had finally worn away, leaving something else underneath—something like peace, or forgetting, or simply the acceptance that the world was as hard as it had always been, and there was no point in fighting it anymore.

In the morning, the sky was clouded over, and the strait was the color of lead. Michael stood on the ferry's upper deck, watching the town recede, and realized he was crying. He hadn't cried since he was a child. He'd thought himself incapable of it by now, burned out by the ordinary disappointments of living. But the grief was there, it turned out, alive and present, waiting all these years. It wasn't grief for the thing on the metal table—that thing was already too distant from the David he'd known, or thought he'd known. It was grief for the living David, the one who had called him on the phone and asked if he was still taking pictures. It was grief for all the years between them, the ones they'd spent in the same country and even the same city, separated by something they'd never been able to name, let alone bridge.

He put his phone away and simply stood at the rail, watching the landscape pass. The islands and headlands, the small red houses in the valleys, the vast and indifferent sky. It was beautiful, he thought. It was all so beautiful and so arbitrary. And somewhere in that beauty and arbitrariness, his brother had lived and died, and Michael would return to Copenhagen and resume his careful, measured life with Annika, and the world would continue exactly as it had before, indifferent to the fact that there had been a David in it and now there wasn't.

He thought again of his brother crouched by the stream, trying to photograph the moment just before things change. It seemed now that David had been wise to attempt it—that moment between before and after, between the living world and whatever came next. It was the only moment worth understanding, the only moment that contained any truth. Everything else was just narrative, the story you told yourself to make sense of what was senseless.

The ferry cut through the grey water, carrying Michael south, toward the lights of the cities, toward Annika waiting at the terminal, toward the continuation of a life that would now and forever be divided into before David died and after. The strait widened behind them. The northern coast receded. And somewhere beneath those cold, indifferent waters, the last of David dissolved into the vast and patient machinery of the world, becoming stone, becoming salt, becoming nothing, becoming at last the answer to a question no one had known to ask.
