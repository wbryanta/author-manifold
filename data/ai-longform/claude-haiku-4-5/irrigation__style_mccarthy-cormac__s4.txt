# 

The old man was dead three months when they came to fix the line that ran through the north section. June heat lay across the land like something cast and hardened and neither brother spoke much as they worked in the morning before it got too bad. The water had stopped coming through somewhere past the third row of trees and the trees themselves had begun to show the first pale signals of thirst, the leaves taking on a quality of wax or old parchment.

They started at the pump house where the iron pipe rose from the concrete pad like a relic from some earlier geological age. Tom opened the valve and there was a sound of water moving in the lines and then nothing. He stood with his hand on the warm metal and listened to it settle.

That don't sound right, he said.

It ain't right, Dale said. He was the younger by four years and he sat on an overturned bucket in the shade of the pump house, drinking water from a plastic jug. There's a leak somewhere. There's been a leak for a month or better.

Why didn't you say something?

I'm saying it now.

Tom looked at him. The pump house threw its shadow across half his body and his face was striped with dark and light like some creature of two temperaments. He had their father's thin mouth and his eyes held that same quality of calculation, as if everything he saw was being priced against something else.

The old man knew about it, Dale said.

He did.

Yes.

And he didn't fix it.

No. He was sick.

He was sick before he was sick, Tom said. He was sick the day he was born. Being sick don't explain much.

Dale didn't answer. He drank from the jug and set it down between his feet. Beyond the pump house the orchard spread out in its geometry of rows, the trees planted in 1957 by their grandfather, their trunks gnarled and thick, the canopy a green so dark it appeared almost black at a distance. Two hundred and forty acres of Valencia oranges and one section of lemons that had never produced worth a damn.

They followed the main line north using a map the old man had drawn in a ledger some twenty years back, the ink faded to a brown that matched the paper itself. The line ran under the access road and then branched into three smaller laterals that served the different sections. They walked through the heat with their tools and the map and a roll of patching material that might be worthless depending on what they found.

You could just sell it, Dale said after they'd walked for a while.

Sell what.

The place. All of it.

Tom didn't respond. He was sweating through his shirt already, the dark stain spreading down his back. His boots were old and dusty and small pebbles had gotten into his right sock.

You're thinking about it, Dale said. I know you are.

I'm thinking about finding the leak.

It's the same thing.

It ain't.

Dale wiped his forehead with the back of his hand. He was younger and his body hadn't yet accumulated the weight and damage that had begun to settle into Tom's frame, but he had the same restless quality in his movements, the same sense of being always at odds with whatever situation contained him.

The developer called me last week, Dale said. Made a formal offer. Three point two million. That's three million each after taxes and expenses.

Tom said nothing.

You don't have to stay, Dale said. You could buy that place up in the Sierra you were looking at. You could fish. You could do something that don't have to do with killing yourself every day from March to October.

You fish?

I could learn to fish.

Tom stopped and turned to look at his brother. The sun bore down on them both and the sky above was a pale blue that spoke of no mercy. In the distance a valley breeze moved through the canopy and made it shimmer like water itself.

The old man planted this place when he was younger than I am now, Tom said. He came here with nothing and he made something. He made it with his hands and with his mind and with his will. And it wasn't easy and it wasn't pleasant and he wasn't a good man and he wasn't kind but he made it. And now you're talking about selling it for money so you can learn to fish.

He's dead, Dale said. He don't care what we do with it.

That's the point.

What's the point?

That whether he cares is not the relevant fact. The relevant fact is what he did. The relevant fact is what he left.

He left us debt. He left us equipment that don't work and trees that are dying and a line that's broken and a system that has been broken probably for longer than it's worked. He left us a lot of work for very little return. That's what he left us.

They found the leak at the junction of the main line and the first lateral, half buried under a stand of dead manzanita brush that had grown up around the concrete collar. The pipe had separated from the fitting and water had been running out of that seam for long enough that it had created a small ravine in the earth and the ground around it had turned to a kind of paste.

Tom crouched down and studied the connection. The bolts were corroded and the gasket had likely perished years ago. He looked at the patching material in his hand and set it aside.

We'll have to replace the whole collar, he said. We'll have to drain the system and get a wrench on these bolts. This isn't a patch job.

How long?

The rest of the day. Maybe into tomorrow.

We don't have the parts.

Tom was already unfastening the bolts. Two of them came free but the third was seized and he had to work it with penetrating oil and a breaker bar.

We have parts, he said. There's a box of fittings in the barn that old man collected over the years.

They're probably twenty years old.

They're brass. They don't go bad.

He worked in silence, focused on the corroded bolt. Sweat dripped from his nose onto the pipe and he didn't wipe it away. Dale sat on a rock and watched him work. There was something in his brother's posture that excluded him, a kind of closure that made conversation impossible. He'd grown used to this over the years, this willingness of Tom's to simply absent himself even while his body remained present.

I can't do this anymore, Dale said.

Tom didn't look up from the bolt. He turned the breaker bar another quarter turn and the bolt began to move, came free with a sound like something breaking in the earth.

You can't do what.

This. Any of it. The heat and the work and the endless fixing of broken things. I'm forty-three years old and I feel like I'm eighty. My back is ruined. My knees don't work. I think about the future and I see myself still doing this, still fixing pipes, still trying to coax production out of trees that want to die, and I can't see a point to it anymore.

Tom had the fitting loose now and he set the bolts carefully on a cloth so they wouldn't be lost. He looked at the gasket, or what remained of it, a crumbled gray material that came away in fragments when he touched it.

The point is that someone has to do it, Tom said. If we don't do it, it doesn't get done. If it doesn't get done, the trees die. If the trees die, two hundred and forty acres of good land gets sold to someone who'll put up houses or something worse. That's the point.

That's a point, Dale said. It's not my point.

What's your point?

My point is that I want to be alive while I'm still alive. That I want to do something that doesn't remind me every single day that I'm going to end up in the ground next to our mother.

You'll end up there anyway.

Exactly. So why should I spend the time before that happens doing work that I hate with a man who barely speaks to me?

Tom had the old collar removed. He set it aside and looked at the pipe itself. There was corrosion here too, orange deposits that flaked away under his fingernail.

I speak to you, Tom said.

You speak at me. It's different.

Tom stood and wiped his hands on his jeans, leaving dark streaks. He was quiet for a long moment, looking out toward the far edge of the orchard where the land rose toward the foothills. The light there was already beginning to shift, the angle changing as the sun moved toward its meridian.

You remember when you were a kid, Tom said. You remember the old man building this system. You remember him out here with his plans and his dreams of what he could do with that water.

I remember him being angry about it. I remember him hitting someone because they didn't do something right.

He hit me too.

I know.

But he was trying to build something. He was trying to make something good on this land. And he did. Whatever else he was, he did that.

Dale stood and dusted off his pants. He walked over to stand beside his brother. From here they could see a long distance down the row, the trees diminishing in perspective, the spaces between them regular and mathematical and somehow sad in their perfection.

That was his life, Dale said. That was what he needed to do to feel like his life mattered. I don't need that. I don't need to leave something behind that will outlive me. I just need to not be miserable while I'm here.

Tom looked at his brother. His face was weathered and lined and his eyes held that same calculation that Dale remembered from their father, a constant assessment of value and weight and cost. But there was something else too, something that had no name, some quality of refusal or resignation or perhaps simply the weight of having decided something long ago and having to live with it now.

You'll sell it to the developer, Tom said. Not this year maybe. But you'll sell it.

You could come with me. We could sell it together and both walk away.

No.

Why not?

Because then I'd be him.

Who?

The old man. I'd be someone with nothing and nowhere and no reason to believe that I ever mattered at all.

Tom turned back to the fitting. He carried the old collar over to a pile of discarded equipment, rusted and broken things that had accumulated over decades. He set it down with the others, another addition to the graveyard of tools and parts and abandoned intentions.

They drove to town in Tom's truck. The air conditioning didn't work and they rode with the windows down, the wind taking the heat out of the cab. The road was straight and empty and the land on either side showed the first real signs of summer stress, the grass turned the color of old straw, the scattered oak trees dark with dust.

At the hardware store Tom found brass fittings that would work. They weren't exactly the same as the originals but they were compatible and new and they would hold. He also picked up a new gasket and some additional bolts. The young man at the counter, someone Tom didn't recognize, rang them up without comment. There had been a time when he knew everyone in town but that time had passed and he'd let it pass without particular regret.

At the diner they ate lunch at the counter. The waitress was a woman their mother had known, old now but still working, still on her feet for eight hours at a stretch. She knew who they were and she was careful in her politeness, the politeness of someone who'd been present at the old man's funeral and had seen something in his sons' faces that made her cautious about speaking to them at all.

Tom ordered the special. Dale got a sandwich and picked at it without appetite. The radio behind the counter played country music, some song about loss and distance, the lyrics worn smooth by time and repetition.

You know Jim Carver? Dale asked.

The Carvers up on Lost Creek road?

Yeah. He sold his place last year. Three hundred acres. Sold it to a vineyard operation from Napa.

Tom didn't respond.

He's living in Santa Rosa now, Dale continued. He works in real estate himself. Apparently there's good money in it. Apparently he's never been happier.

Tom ate his food. It was good food and it was hot and it required a kind of attention that left no room for commentary on the choices other men had made.

That don't mean anything to me, Tom said when he was finished.

It means something. It means there's a way out. It means other people have taken it and survived.

Surviving ain't the same as living.

That's something you say, Dale said. That's something you've decided. But it's not universal. Most people would say that surviving and living are pretty much the same thing.

The waitress came and took their plates. She poured more coffee without asking and moved on to other tables. Outside the window the town went on with its business, people coming and going, the grocery store, the post office, the bank where their father had renegotiated loans more than once, trying to make the numbers work, always trying to make the numbers work.

When they got back to the orchard the sun was beginning its long descent but it would be high for several more hours. They drained the system by opening the lowest valve and letting the water run out onto the land. It was wasteful and necessary, a contradiction they didn't bother to reconcile. While the lines drained Tom worked on cleaning the corroded bolts, using a wire brush and oil until they were restored to a kind of functionality if not their original state.

The new fitting went in without much trouble. Tom took his time and did it right, checking everything twice before he closed the bolts down tight. He worked with an efficiency that came from having done this work his entire adult life, his hands knowing what to do without much guidance from his mind. Dale helped when help was needed and otherwise stayed out of the way.

By the time they opened the valve again and water began to flow through the restored connection the light was going orange and red and the temperature had dropped enough that the work felt almost pleasant. They followed the line back toward the pump house, listening to the water moving in the pipes, checking for leaks at the joints.

At the third lateral they found water coming out of a pinhole in the main line itself. Tom stopped and crouched down and looked at the corrosion that had created the hole. It was small but it was growing and there were already smaller holes beginning to form on either side of it. He pressed his finger against it and water came out around it in a small spray.

The pipe's shot, he said. The whole section.

How much?

Five hundred feet maybe. Maybe more.

Can you patch it?

Not permanently. You could wrap it, slow it down. But it'll need to be replaced.

Dale sat down in the dirt. The sun was low now and his shadow stretched out long behind him. He looked at the failing pipe and he looked at his brother and his expression was something between despair and resignation.

How much will that cost?

To do it right? Four or five thousand. Maybe more if we have to dig it out. And we do have to dig it out.

Do we have that?

Not right now. We'd have to pull it out of operating capital.

Which we don't have.

No. We don't.

Tom sat down beside him. They were both quiet for a while, watching the sun move toward the mountains. The land around them was their land and it was good land and it was failing and they were failing with it, working against a tide of entropy and age and the simple unwillingness of the world to provide what they needed from it.

I saw the papers, Dale said. The developer's offer. There was more to it than price. There was a timeline. They want a decision in thirty days.

Thirty days.

That's what they said.

Tom nodded. He understood the economics of it. He understood that there was a calculation being made somewhere, a weighing of options, and that the option involving him and his brother standing in the dirt watching their land fail was not likely to come out on top.

If you want to do it, Tom said, I won't stop you. You could sell your half.

I could.

Then do it.

I don't want to do it without you.

They sat in silence. The light was fading fast now and soon they would need to leave the orchard, drive back to the houses they lived in separately, the old farmhouse for Tom, a smaller place closer to town for Dale. They would return tomorrow and there would be more work and more failure and the same argument in a different form and it would continue until something broke.

I'm not going to leave, Tom said. I'm going to stay here and do this work for as long as I can do it. And when I can't do it anymore I'll find someone else to do it. And if there's no one else then the trees will die and the land will be sold and something will be built here that has nothing to do with what the old man did. But that will be after I'm gone. I won't see it.

The sun had touched the mountains now and the light in the orchard had turned to something close to gold. The leaves were illuminated from behind and they seemed to glow with their own light, a quality of transcendence that was purely visual and wholly misleading about the state of things.

Dale stood and brushed the dirt from his pants. He looked at his brother and he looked at the failing pipe and he looked back at his brother again and what passed between them in that moment was something like forgiveness but not quite, something like understanding but not quite, some recognition that they had become the people they were always going to become and that there was nothing to be done about it now.

All right, Dale said. We'll finish it tomorrow.

We will.

But I'm still going to tell them yes. I'm going to sell my half.

Okay.

I thought you should know.

I appreciate that.

They walked back toward the truck in the failing light. Behind them the orchard stretched out in all its geometry and failure, the irrigation line now fixed at the point of breaking, water moving through it in the dark. The trees themselves were silhouetted against the last of the sun, a perfect representation of something that was dying by degrees and had been dying for longer than either of them had been alive.

When they reached the truck Tom stopped and looked back. From this distance the orchard appeared still intact, whole, something that could persist. But he knew what he knew and he had known it for a long time and he would know it still longer, watching the slow unwinding of something that had taken a lifetime to build and would take only years to fail.

He got in the truck and started the engine. Dale was already in the passenger seat with his eyes closed, his head resting against the window. Tom drove back toward town without speaking, the radio playing songs about loss and distance, the darkness gathering behind them like something inevitable and patient.
