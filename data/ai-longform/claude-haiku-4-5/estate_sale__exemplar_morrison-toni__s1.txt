# CARRYING OUT

The realtor had told her it would be a good day for it, Saturday in September when the light comes clean and the neighbors aren't the type to stand in their yards watching. But there she was, standing, watching, as if her body had made a different arrangement with her mind about what this day required. The house exhaled its contents onto the front lawn like some great animal expelling what it could no longer digest. The sideboard her mother had refinished in 1987, the year it seemed she might remake herself into a woman who did things with her hands. The dining chairs with the rush seats that had held them all for years—birthdays, holidays, ordinary Tuesdays when nothing much happened except that they sat there together and ate. Now they were being carried down the brick walk by a man with a tattoo on his neck and a woman in sneakers who didn't look at the house at all, who looked only at the ground to make sure her feet found purchase on the familiar brick.

Evelyn had come alone because there was no one left to come with her. Her sister had called from Denver, her voice small and efficient over the phone: "You'll do fine, Eve. You've always been the capable one." As if capability were something that could be weaponized, turned into a tool for the work of dismantling. Her daughter had sent flowers. A card. Money. Her son had sent a text message that said only "thinking of you" which was technically the truth but not what anyone meant when they said it.

The realtor, a woman named Patricia whose hair was very blonde and very stiff, had made an inventory. That's what she called it. An inventory. As if her mother's life were goods to be catalogued, assessed for value, distributed to the highest bidder. The china cabinet, for instance. It had come from her grandmother, who had come from somewhere before America decided to spell her name differently. That cabinet had held nothing precious for thirty years, just the heavy dishes they never used and some glasses that had accumulated rings from good intentions. Now it stood on the front lawn with a yellow sticker on its side and a price written in Patricia's neat hand: $275.

Evelyn had asked to keep something small, something that wouldn't take up much room in the car she'd driven from Columbus. Patricia had looked at her the way people looked at her when she made requests that complicated their jobs. But there was a music box on the dresser in her mother's room, something Evelyn had wound up maybe two hundred times in her childhood, had listened to its tinny version of something European and sad. She'd asked for that. Patricia had said she would need to price it, that everything had to go through the system, that there couldn't be exceptions, that it wouldn't be fair to the others, though what others meant in this context—others who had brought her mother casseroles in her final years, others who had stayed through the surgeries and the small deaths that preceded the large one—Evelyn didn't bother asking.

She stood now in the driveway wearing her mother's cardigan, which she'd found still hanging in the coat closet and had slipped on without thinking. It was a pale blue thing, expensive once, from a store that no longer existed. The sleeves came short on Evelyn's wrists. Everything her mother had owned seemed designed for a smaller world, a woman who had taken up less space than her daughter did.

"Ma'am?" The man with the tattoo was pointing at the china cabinet. Patricia had disappeared inside, probably measuring rooms, calculating square footage as if space were the only thing that mattered anymore. "This one got a sticker says you want it?"

Evelyn looked at the cabinet. She looked at his face. She understood that he was asking her something, but not the thing he was actually saying. He was asking her what she was doing here, standing in her own mother's driveway, watching the dismantling with her hands in the pockets of a dead woman's sweater.

"No," she said. "I was mistaken."

He nodded the way people did when someone confirmed what they already suspected about the world, that it was mostly strange, that most of what happened didn't make sense if you looked at it too closely. He and the woman in sneakers picked it up again—they must have set it down when he asked, must have paused in their work for this strange negotiation—and carried it toward the truck. The cabinet caught the light as it moved, and for a moment Evelyn could see all the versions of herself reflected in its glass doors, all the ages at once, the child who had arranged the dishes, the teenager who'd hidden notes inside it, the woman who had looked in this same cabinet while her mother died in the next room, the old house keeping its secrets in its wooden bones.

She should go inside. There were things to check, to sign off on. Patricia had made a list of that too. But instead Evelyn walked toward the side of the house where her mother had planted the garden, back when planting seemed like something worth doing. The tomatoes had gone to seed. The roses her mother had loved had spread themselves thin, their flowers small and numerous, like they'd given up on being impressive and had decided instead just to bloom. She touched one, and it came apart in her fingers. Not yet, it seemed to say. Not quite yet.

Her mother had kept a bench out here, a small stone thing she'd bought at an estate sale—not this one, some other one, some other person's discarded life—and had placed beneath the oak tree. It faced the house. That's what Evelyn remembered most about her mother in her last good years: the way she would sit on this bench and face the house, looking at it as if she were studying it, as if there were something in its architecture she was trying to understand. Evelyn had never asked what she was looking for. There had always been time for that question later, and then there hadn't been.

The bench was still there. They must not have found it valuable enough to move. Evelyn sat on it now. The stone was cold, even in September. Even in the sun that came through the oak leaves in those broken-up pieces of light that oak trees seemed always to provide. She thought about her mother sitting here, every afternoon at three, like an appointment she had made with herself, like there was something she was waiting for that might still arrive if she was patient enough, if she looked long enough.

The man with the tattoo came around the corner of the house pushing a dolly with the hope chest in it, the one from the bedroom that Evelyn had slept in as a child. She'd put things in that chest once—ribbons, letters, the kind of treasures that only mattered to people who still believed the future would be organized in a particular way. Her mother had kept blankets in it. Good blankets. Things to be preserved. He saw her sitting on the bench and adjusted his course slightly, giving her space, giving her a wide berth, the way you might walk around an animal you weren't sure about.

Patricia emerged from the house carrying a lamp. Just a lamp. Nothing special that Evelyn could see, but Patricia held it carefully, turning it to show the realtor's eye for damage, for value, for the price a thing might bring when it was empty of context, when it had become only an object, a commodity, a problem to be solved by placement and correct pricing.

"We're making good progress," Patricia called. She had a voice that carried. Probably necessary in her profession. "Should be done by three."

Three. Three o'clock. Three in the afternoon. Evelyn's mother had sat on this bench every day at three o'clock. She wondered if Patricia had planned the schedule intentionally, if there was something in her realtor's intuition that understood the importance of the hour, or if it was coincidence, the kind that felt like evidence of something larger at work even though it almost certainly wasn't.

The house was being hollowed out. Evelyn could feel it the way you might feel an emptying in yourself, some essential fluid draining away slowly enough that you didn't notice until you tried to stand and found yourself lighter, stranger, less present in the world. She thought about what it meant to be the last person in a house, the last one to look at the walls from the inside before they became walls that belonged to someone else, to a person who would paint them a different color, who would hang different pictures, who would exist in these rooms without knowing any of the ghosts that had existed here before them.

Her mother had never believed in ghosts. "When you're dead, you're done," she'd said. Not unkindly. Just factually. As if it were settled, as if it required no further discussion. And yet her mother had sat on this bench every day at three o'clock looking at a house that had watched her grow old, that had held her in its rooms like a vessel holds something precious or something dangerous, depending on who was measuring and what they were measuring for.

The woman in sneakers emerged now, carrying one of the kitchen chairs. They were moving faster, Evelyn noticed. They'd found a rhythm. The work had become efficient. Soon there would be nothing left but the empty rooms and the faint shadows on the carpet where furniture had lived, the imprints of a life arranged and then rearranged and finally removed.

"Mrs. Simmons?" Patricia was back, clipboard in hand. Always the clipboard. "I need you to sign off on the bedroom set. They're about to take that next, and once it's out, I need confirmation that nothing was damaged."

Evelyn stood. The bench was cold still, even where she'd been sitting. The sun hadn't warmed it. She walked back toward the house, back toward Patricia and her clipboard and the business of ending things properly, correctly, with the appropriate signatures.

The bedroom set was the same one her mother had slept in for forty years. Evelyn could remember moving it in, remember being a teenager and watching the men position it against the wall, remember asking her mother if she liked it and her mother saying, "It'll do," which was the closest she came to satisfaction with most things in her life. The mattress had a depression in it, a permanent bowl in the center where her mother's weight had lived for decades. Dust motes floated above it now, suspended in a shaft of light. They looked like they were dancing, and for a moment Evelyn wanted to ask Patricia to wait, to let her stand here for a little while longer with the light and the floating dust and the impression of a body that had lain here every night for forty years.

"All looks fine," Evelyn told her. She signed the paper. Her handwriting looked strange to her, the letters smaller than she remembered them. When had that happened? When had she started writing so small?

The man with the tattoo and the woman in sneakers came in then with their dolly, already practiced in the angle of approach, the way to tilt the frame so it would clear the doorway. They worked with the kind of grace that comes from repetition, from having done the same action so many times that the body could do it without consulting the mind. The bed came up easy, came out quick. In less than a minute, all that was left was the impression on the carpet, the shape of where it had been, the negative space of something solid and present made into something remembered and gone.

Evelyn stood in the empty room. The walls were a pale yellow, a color her mother had chosen fifteen years ago, saying only that it made her feel hopeful, which had struck Evelyn at the time as a strange desire to experience in a bedroom, and stranger still that her mother would articulate it. She thought about her mother lying in this bed in this yellow room, feeling hopeful or trying to, or perhaps just lying in the yellow and letting it work on her the way colors worked on people, the way certain shades of light could make you feel like the world still had possibilities in it, still contained the potential for things to turn out differently than they were turning out.

By two-thirty, the house was empty. Just the floors and the walls and the windows that let the light in. Just Evelyn and Patricia and the ghost of arrangement, the ghost of living. She walked through the rooms, each one cleaner than she'd ever seen it, each one more obviously a room now that all the things that had made it a bedroom or a kitchen or a living room had been removed.

In the hallway, she found the music box. It sat on a small shelf that had been too high for Patricia to notice, that had been too small for the realtor to bother with. It was exactly where it had always been. Evelyn picked it up. The bottom was dusty. She wound it once, gently, and listened to the familiar song emerge, that European sadness, that old translation of longing into mechanical music. It played the same as it always had, unchanged, indifferent to the decades that had passed, to the child who had wound it and the woman who had become and now the old woman she was, standing alone in an empty house listening to a song that would outlast her.

She put it in her pocket. Not the pocket of her mother's cardigan, but the inner pocket of the jacket she wore beneath it, close to her own body. Patricia was in the driveway, doing final calculations, making notes on her clipboard. She didn't ask about the music box. Probably she'd already assigned a price to it in her mind, had already decided it was worth the loss if it meant Evelyn would sign off on everything without complication, without asking for exceptions or favors.

At three o'clock, Evelyn sat on the bench one more time. The house was empty now. The sun had moved slightly, and now the stone was warm. She sat and looked at the place where her mother had sat, at the bricks and the oak tree and the roses that had learned to bloom without arrangement or constraint. She thought about the difference between carrying something out and leaving something behind, about the way objects moved through the world taking on and shedding meaning, about the way a house could hold a person for forty years and then, in the space of a single afternoon, forget them entirely.

The music box played its song in her pocket, or she imagined it did. The melody lived in her body now, in the place where she kept the things she couldn't bear to let go of but didn't know how to keep. She sat and watched the empty house and understood, finally, what her mother had been looking for all those afternoons at three o'clock. Not something outside herself. Not something that might arrive. Just a moment when she could be still in front of the life she'd made and know that it had been made, that it had existed, that it had been real and present and held weight in the world, if only for a little while, if only in the eyes of someone sitting on a bench and remembering how a house could be full.
