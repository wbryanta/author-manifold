# The Dry Below

The morning Margaret arrived, the water was the color of rust. She stood at the overlook above the reservoir, her boots planted in the cracked earth of what used to be a parking area, and tried to square this landscape with the one she carried in her memory—that glittering expanse that had seemed, when she was young, to stretch to the horizon like a second sky.

The drought had been absolute. In the fourteen months since the levels began their unprecedented drop, the county had issued water restrictions that tightened month by month until they became almost philosophical in their severity, asking people to reconsider whether bathing and drinking could not somehow be condensed into a single, efficient act. Now, at the nadir, the reservoir sat at thirty-two percent capacity—a statistic Margaret had read in the county newsletter, a number that meant nothing until she saw it. The water line had retreated so far that the old town was beginning to surface again, piece by piece, like something being exhaled after a very long sleep.

She had not been back since 1987, when she was nine years old and still called Maggie, and the water was still rising in her consciousness—or so it had seemed. But of course the water wasn't rising; she was being lifted away from it. Her parents had moved to Sacramento for her father's job, and by the time she was old enough to make her own decisions, there seemed no compelling reason to return. She had become a surveyor, which her guidance counselor had suggested with the tone of someone offering a practical alternative to ambition. But Margaret had discovered she liked the work. She liked standing at the edges of things, measuring the distance between what was and what could be mapped. She liked the way a survey established facts that could not be disputed by feeling or nostalgia.

Which was why the county had called her. The Cascade Valley project, the planning director had explained over the phone, needed preliminary documentation of the exposed areas. Developers were already circling, sensing opportunity in catastrophe. Before anything could be planned, before arguments could be made about what should happen to the revealed ground, someone needed to record it. Someone needed to draw the lines.

Margaret checked her equipment—the GPS unit, the measuring wheel, the tablet computer that would translate her notations into digital maps. Everything was in order, which was how she preferred to find the world. She descended the path toward the water, which was less a path than a scar in the exposed earth, and tried not to think about the figure she had been the last time she made this journey in reverse.

The old town emerged gradually as she walked. First came the familiar pilings—concrete posts that had once secured docks and boathouses—jutting from the cracked lakebed like the broken teeth of some ancient creature. Then, as she moved deeper into what had been the central basin, the streets themselves began to appear. Not all of them: the water still covered the western quarter, lapping at the edges of what Margaret knew had been the residential zones. But here, in the downtown area, the grid was reasserting itself. She could trace it with her eye alone: Main Street, Oak Avenue, the little Market Cross that intersected them at an awkward angle because it followed the path of an old cattle trail and the founders had been sentimental about such things.

The paving was still there, cracked and stained with mineral deposits that looked like blood. As Margaret walked across it, her footsteps echoed differently than they did on normal ground—there was a hollow quality to the sound, as if the earth beneath had not forgotten how to be water. She marked the intersection of Main and Oak with her GPS, waiting for the device to calculate coordinates with the precision that would have seemed like magic to the people who had walked these streets before they were drowned.

The buildings came into view slowly. Their foundations were intact, she saw with something that might have been relief. The stone and concrete had withstood the water, though the mineral deposits had left them looking leprous, bleached and stained by turns. She could see where doorways had been cut, where windows had framed views of the mountains beyond. One storefront still had part of its sign affixed to the lintel: "PEARL'S—" the rest had either fallen away or been worn smooth by years of submersion. Margaret photographed it with her phone, then made a note in her tablet for the official record.

As she worked, moving methodically down the street, she began to see the ghost of the town she had known. Not clearly—too much had changed, too much had been taken by time and pressure—but well enough that her mind began to populate the ruins with specificity. There was the drugstore where old Mr. Hendricks had let her sit at the lunch counter and drink chocolate milk while her mother was at the bank. There was the post office, smaller than she remembered, with its brass mailboxes still visible inside the corroded frames of the interior door. There was the Methodist church, its cross still mounted on the roof, tarnished but intact, pointing upward at a sky it no longer seemed to believe in.

The strange thing was how intimate the scale was. Margaret had not realized, when she was a child, how small the town actually was. In her memory, it had possessed the vastness that all childhood locations seemed to possess—streets that stretched forever, buildings that touched the sky. But now, mapping it foot by foot, she could see that everything here had been very compact, very knowable. The entire downtown would fit into a city block in Sacramento. The fact that it had been important to anyone seemed almost touching, almost absurd.

She stopped at the corner of Elm and Second, where a structure she recognized as the old library stood. Only three walls remained; the fourth had collapsed inward, exposing the interior to the air for the first time in decades. Margaret approached it carefully, half expecting the remaining walls to give way under her weight, but the stone was solid. She could see the remnants of shelving inside, the curved edge of what might have been a circulation desk. Water had bleached everything to the same shade of driftwood gray.

She had spent the summer before they moved reading in that library, hiding from the heat by burying herself in a corner where the afternoon sun came through the western window. She had read fairy tales and adventure novels and a biography of Eleanor Roosevelt that she hadn't understood but had felt she ought to read. The head librarian had been a woman named Mrs. Chen, who wore cat-eye glasses and knew everyone's name and would sometimes bring her chocolate chip cookies that she kept in a tin under her desk.

Margaret stood in the ruined doorway, not quite stepping inside, as if the threshold still maintained some boundary between what was and what had been. She tried to reconstruct the interior—the arrangement of the stacks, the way light had fallen through the windows, the precise location of the desk where Mrs. Chen had checked out her books. But the image wouldn't hold. Memory was proving unreliable here, contaminated by too much time and too many maps of other towns that had overwritten her understanding of this one.

After several minutes, she made her notations and moved on.

The afternoon sun was moving toward the western peaks when she reached the residential section, where the water still covered perhaps a third of the streets. Here, the houses emerged like swimmers coming up for breath—the peaked roofs of ranch homes, the brick chimneys of older structures, dormer windows that watched the empty sky. Many of them were still largely intact, preserved by the water that had drowned them. Margaret could see into some of them where the walls had partially collapsed. She could see kitchen counters, the frame of a bed, a bathtub tilted at an impossible angle.

She forced herself to be systematic. She marked the position of each structure, recorded its relative state of preservation, made notes about visible damage and degradation. She was a surveyor; she would not allow herself to be a archaeologist of her own past. And yet, as she worked, she found herself slowing, lingering at certain sites longer than professional necessity required.

The Kessler house was on Prospect Street, a two-story colonial with white painted brick and dark green shutters—or it had been. The color had faded to a ghastly shade, and the shutters hung at angles like broken limbs. Margaret recognized it because her best friend David Kessler had lived there, and they had played in the backyard during the final summer, running through sprinklers while their parents sat on deck chairs and drank iced tea. She remembered David pushing her into the shallow end of their above-ground pool—not maliciously, just the casual cruelty of childhood—and her choking on chlorinated water until his mother had hauled her out.

She didn't go inside the Kessler house. Instead, she stood at the property line and documented it from outside: the stone foundation, the extent of weathering, the damage to the roof. She made herself do it the way she would have documented any structure, and only when she had finished did she allow herself to think about David. He was in Sacramento now, she knew—his mother had mentioned it at the grocery store once, running into her mother in the checkout line. He was a lawyer. He had never married. She had not thought about him in years, and yet standing outside his house, she found she could conjure the exact pitch of his childhood laugh, the way he had pronounced her name with a particular inflection that made it sound like a question.

The sun was approaching the horizon when Margaret forced herself to move on. She had other houses to document, other streets to map. She had work enough to justify several more days here, which the county had approved and which she had agreed to. She pushed down the discomfort that was beginning to bloom in her chest and returned to her systematic survey, clipboard in hand, GPS unit tracking her position with the benign precision of a satellite that did not care about ghosts.

By the time she reached the area where she thought her own house had been, twilight was gathering in the valleys. The light had taken on that particular quality it possessed in late afternoon, where everything seemed to be glowing from within—the exposed stone of the old foundations, the mineral-stained earth, the residual water that pooled in the lowest depressions of the cracked lakebed.

The house was gone. Or rather, the house was still there—she could see the outline of the foundation, the remains of what had been the back porch. But most of the structure had collapsed or been carried away, leaving only the concrete base and a few fragments of wall that rose no higher than her knees. Whatever had been inside the house—the furniture, the photographs, the accumulated evidence of occupancy—was long since disintegrated or scattered. 

She sat down on the edge of the foundation, her surveying equipment beside her, and felt something that was not quite grief and not quite memory resolve in her chest. She had not allowed herself to expect the house to still be standing. That would have been sentimentality of a kind her profession did not encourage. And yet some part of her must have hoped it, must have imagined that she might walk through the rooms where she had slept and played and eaten breakfast. She must have imagined that the geography of her childhood would still be inscribed in the physical world, that she could map it the way she mapped other things.

The sun finished its descent, and the sky filled with the particular darkness that came in places far from electric light. Margaret pulled out her phone and activated its flashlight, turned it toward the ruins. In the artificial brightness, the foundation looked even more desolate—not ruined but erased, carefully removed from history the way a typist might correct a mistake. She thought about the people who had lived here: herself and her parents, and for one year a golden retriever named Sam who had suffered from anxiety and eaten the insulation out of the walls. She thought about the morning they had left, how she had cried in the back seat because her best friend couldn't come with her, how her father had said something kind to try to comfort her, something about how the world was full of best friends if you looked for them. He had been right, in his way, but the friendship of the world was not the same as the friendship of David Kessler, and she had learned that lesson slowly and incompletely over the intervening decades.

She stood up and brushed dust from her clothes. The light was almost completely gone now. She should head back to the overlook, to her truck, to the motel room in the county seat where she had booked three nights with the understanding that the work might take longer. She should eat something and review her notes and prepare for the next day's surveying. She was, in her way, a disciplined person.

But before she left, she walked to what she estimated had been the front of the house. She tried to remember which room faced which direction, whether the living room had had a bay window or if that was another house entirely. She could not quite reconstruct it. The spaces inside her memory had become too unreliable, contaminated by photographs from other houses, by the generic furniture of other people's childhoods that she had observed over the years. But she could remember standing at a particular window in early evening, and she could remember the light coming through the glass in a particular way, and she could remember feeling completely certain that she understood how the world worked.

She knelt at what might have been the threshold and placed her hand on the concrete. It was cold. It was rough. It was not at all what she expected, though what she had expected was not clear to her. She felt for something—some continuity between the child she had been and the adult she had become—and found nothing but the physical fact of aged stone and the fading light of evening.

When she finally stood and turned to leave, she marked the location with her GPS one final time. She recorded the data in her tablet: coordinates, condition assessment, notes for the record. She was careful and thorough, the way she was careful and thorough with everything. And if, as she was entering the information, she felt tears on her face, she did not remark on them to anyone, and she did not allow them to interfere with her documentation. The map would be accurate. The map would show what was there. Everything else—the ghost of the girl who had read in the library, the boy who had pushed her into the pool, the parents who had driven away in a station wagon on a August morning—would have to find some other way to be recorded.

By the time she reached her truck, full dark had settled completely. Margaret drove slowly back toward town, her headlights illuminating the empty landscape of the dry lakebed. Behind her, the drowned town retreated back into darkness, its foundations invisible again under the starless sky. Tomorrow she would return with her equipment and her discipline and her maps. She would continue the work of documentation, the work of establishing facts that could not be disputed by feeling or nostalgia. And when the work was finished, she would move on to the next contract, to the next town that needed measuring.

But tonight, driving through the darkness with both hands on the wheel, Margaret allowed herself to remember what it had felt like to be nine years old and completely certain of the world's arrangement. She allowed herself to feel the loss of it without naming the loss or quantifying it or mapping it to any coordinate system. She allowed this brief rebellion against her professional nature, this small act of unmeasured grief, to persist until she reached the town and the lights of the motel began to appear on the horizon, and the map of her life reasserted itself once again, as accurate and as empty as all maps finally are.
