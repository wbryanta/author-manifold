# 

The ferry arrives in darkness, which is to say it arrives as it always does in November at this latitude, the light abandoned hours before, the stars still holding their distance. He stands on the upper deck with his collar turned against the wind and watches the lights of the port assemble themselves like a city being remembered rather than approached. His hands grip the railing. Somewhere in that arrangement of sodium vapor and harbor-work illumination is a body. Somewhere is a name that may or may not belong to his brother.

The crossing has taken fourteen hours. He spent most of it in the cabin with a book he didn't read, watching the water state its argument against the windows. A woman with gray in her hair had nodded at him in the corridor near the dining hall, a gesture of acknowledgment that felt like pity, though he knew this was projection, that she was merely being civil to another traveler who looked as unmade as the weather. He had not slept. Sleep would have required a kind of surrender he wasn't prepared to offer.

The voice on the phone had been official and apologetic in equal measure. A man had been found. Personal effects suggested a connection to his address, an old address, one he'd given up five years ago when the separation became official in everything but name. The police had tried the address first, reached his mother, who had wept in a way that seemed practiced, as if she had been rehearsing this moment since the day his brother disappeared into the prairies. She had called him at his apartment in the city where he'd built a life of deliberate smallness—a job that asked nothing but competence, a studio that barely contained his sleeping, books he borrowed and returned.

"You should go," his mother had said. "You should see."

"Why?" he'd asked, and the question hung there between them like something that might never be answered.

"Because he's your brother."

The word *is*. Present tense. As if the years of silence, the postcards that came less and less frequently, the phone calls that had dwindled to nothing by the time their father died—as if none of that had altered the basic fact of kinship. As if blood memory were a currency that never depleted, no matter how profligately you spent it.

He descends the gangway with the other passengers, all of them moving with the stiffness of people who have been held in suspension. The terminal smells of diesel fuel and coffee made hours ago. A man in a police uniform stands near the information desk, and though the man is not looking at him, he knows he is the one. There is something in the set of that man's shoulders that speaks to expectation. There is a way the police have of standing that suggests they are always waiting for someone to arrive.

"Mr. Thomas Keller?" The officer's name tag reads MARSHAL. He is younger than expected, maybe forty, with the kind of face that has been weathered by wind rather than age, as if he's spent a life outdoors. "I'm grateful you could come on such short notice."

The language of gratitude feels obscene, but he nods anyway. He learns that the body was found three days ago in a ravine some thirty kilometers north, that identification has been difficult due to the state of decomposition, that they have been waiting for someone who can testify to physical specifics—a scar, a dental record, a birthmark that might stand as irrefutable proof.

"How long?" he asks.

"We're still determining that. The coroner thinks maybe six months. Maybe longer. The cold would have preserved certain things."

Six months. He calculates backward. Six months ago he was reading about war in distant countries, drinking coffee at the same café every morning, speaking to no one about anything that mattered. Six months ago his brother might have been dying, and he would not have known. He would not have felt the severance the way certain people claim to feel the death of identical twins across oceans.

They drive in silence through streets he doesn't recognize, though he supposes his brother might have. How long had he been here? What had he been doing in this small port city at the edge of the known world? The questions are too numerous and too strange. It's easier to say nothing.

The medical examiner's office is housed in a building that was once something else—a hospital perhaps, or a school. The hallways are too wide, too resonant with footsteps. Marshal escorts him past doors with opaque windows and signs he tries not to read too carefully. At the end of a long corridor is a room that is too cold and smells of chemicals that make his eyes water.

The body is covered. Of course it's covered. He has known it would be covered.

"Are you ready?" the examiner asks. She is a woman with careful hands and a face that has learned not to expect anything from the living. "We'll start with the general build, then move to specific identifiers if necessary."

She folds back the sheet. What is exposed is neither person nor corpse but something in between, something that has been altered by time in ways that seem to violate the memory of human form. The face is not a face. The hands are skeletal, the nails still present like small dark stones. There is a tattoo on the left shoulder blade that he cannot quite make out through the damage.

He stands there. He stands there and says nothing because nothing can be said.

"Do you recognize anything?" the examiner asks.

There is a scar on the left knee. He remembers this scar. He remembers the day it was made—a bicycle accident when his brother was seven and he was nine. Their mother had poured hydrogen peroxide over the wound, and his brother had watched it foam with the fascination of someone discovering that his own body could generate mystery. There had been stitches. There had been a limp for several weeks that his brother had exaggerated for sympathy.

"The knee," he says. His voice sounds strange to him, as if it belongs to someone else who is being forced to speak through his mouth. "The scar on the knee."

The examiner moves closer, examines the area with professional attention. "That could be consistent," she says. "With what we see here, it's difficult to be more specific. Do you know what caused it?"

"An accident. When we were children."

"I see." She makes a note. "We have dental records from a dentist in the city. We could use those for confirmation if you're uncertain."

But he is not uncertain. He is certain in a way that bypasses reason, that operates at some level of knowing that exists beneath language. This is his brother. This body, ruined and strange as it is, contains the facts of his brother's existence. The scar is proof. The scar is a kind of language that cannot be questioned.

"Yes," he says. "Yes, that's him."

He doesn't cry. He expects to cry and is almost offended when the tears don't come. He stands there while the examiner continues her documentation, while Marshal asks questions about next of kin and arrangements, while the world continues its ordinary work of rotation around a sun he can no longer remember.

It is only later, in the hotel room they've booked him in (because of course they've booked him somewhere, because they understand that people need shelter after these things), that something begins to move through him. He sits on the bed and looks at his hands as if they belong to someone he's never met. His hands that are not skeletal. His hands that still have all their function. His hands that are alive in a way his brother's hands no longer are.

He picks up the phone and calls his mother. She answers on the second ring, as if she's been holding the receiver in her lap.

"It's him," he says.

He hears her breath. He hears what might be a sound escape her throat, though she tries to contain it. When she speaks, her voice is small. "I knew it would be."

"How could you know?"

"A mother knows when her child stops living in the world."

He wants to argue with this. He wants to say that knowing and not knowing are different things, that she couldn't possibly have understood the shape of his brother's ending. But he is tired in a way that runs deeper than sleep, and so he says nothing.

"Where will you have him?" she asks.

"I haven't thought about it yet."

"We should bury him here. Where the family is buried."

The family cemetery is in a small town in Manitoba where neither of them has lived for more than twenty years. Their father is there. Their grandparents. People he has never met and has no wish to know. The idea of his brother's body being placed in the earth of a place he abandoned seems both terrible and inevitable.

"I'll arrange it," he says.

After they hang up, he opens the window. The harbor is visible from the fourth floor, dark water reflecting darker sky. Somewhere out there is the ravine where his brother was found. Somewhere out there are the final hours or weeks or months of his brother's life—the moments that led to the falling, or the jumping, or whatever sequence of decisions and accidents culminated in that ending. The body has been preserved but the knowledge of it has been lost. Whatever story led here is gone.

He thinks about the postcards. They had come sporadically over the years, each one containing a single sentence or sometimes just a word. *Fine here. Snow. Work is hard. The light is different.* They had come from different places—Alberta, then British Columbia, then north, always north, until the final one had arrived from this very city, the port town that now contains him as well.

He had kept them for a while, then lost them in a move. Or thrown them away. He can't remember which, but the loss feels significant now, as if he has destroyed evidence of something important. The words on those postcards had been his brother's last attempts at communication, and he had not understood them as such. He had filed them away with the casual indifference with which one receives news of distant weather.

There is a knock at the door. It's Marshal, holding a folder.

"We found some things," the officer says. "Among his effects. I thought you might want to see them before we file everything."

The folder contains a wallet (empty of identification, nearly empty of anything), a wristwatch (stopped at 2:47, though there is no way of knowing if that was morning or afternoon, or how many times the battery had died since the moment of stopping), a small notebook filled with what appears to be the same phrase repeated over dozens of pages: *I will not return.* The words are written in his brother's handwriting, which has become angular and strange, as if written by someone learning language for the first time.

"I'll need these," he says.

"Of course. We can keep them as long as you need."

After the officer leaves, he sits on the bed with the notebook in his lap. *I will not return.* Page after page of it. A declaration or a prediction or possibly a command directed at himself. He tries to imagine the state of mind that produces such work. He tries to imagine sitting in a room—a rental, perhaps, or a shelter, or the crude structure of someone trying to disappear—and writing the same words over and over until the meaning dissolves and becomes pure form. 

He understands suddenly that he never knew his brother. That the boy with the scarred knee, the teenager who left home too early and with too much anger, the man who sent postcards from the edge of nowhere—these were all people he had observed from a distance, had failed to recognize as fully alive and therefore fully deserving of attention. He had been too occupied with his own construction, his own careful assembly of a life that asked nothing from him but competence. While his brother was falling into ravines, he was reading novels in a studio apartment, believing himself somehow better for his restraint.

He places the notebook on the nightstand. He lies down on the bed fully clothed and stares at the ceiling. The city outside continues its work. Ships arrive and depart. People cross the water. Somewhere in the arctic dark, the body of his brother lies in a drawer, waiting for the next step in a process that will return him to the earth from which he came. And he, the one who remains, who has been chosen to testify, to say *yes, this is the person I once knew*, lies in a hotel bed and tries to remember what it felt like when the separation had not yet begun.

The morning brings a kind of grey light that filters through the hotel window. He is not sure he slept. His mind seems to have continued its work through the night, turning over facts that will no longer change. He showers in the small bathroom and watches the steam collect on the mirror. When it clears, his face appears—older than he remembered, marked by something that might be sorrow or might simply be the exhaustion of being alive.

He has three days before he needs to return. The police have said they can release the body for transport. He has said he will handle the arrangements, though he hasn't begun to think about what that means. There is an order to these things, he supposes. There are people whose work consists of managing the passage from living to dead, from presence to absence.

He decides to walk. The city is smaller than he expected, with streets that end in water or industrial buildings. There are seagulls everywhere, harsh and insistent, demanding entry into a world that has shown them only enough to make them hungry. He passes a bar that is open despite the early hour, its interior dark and promising nothing. He passes a store that sells maritime equipment—ropes and lights and devices whose purposes he can only guess at. He walks without direction, without purpose beyond the fact of movement itself.

On the third block, he finds a café. Inside, the walls are covered with photographs of ships and fishing boats and men in various configurations of water rescue. The woman behind the counter is perhaps sixty, with the weathered look of someone who has spent a life on or near the ocean. She pours his coffee without asking how he takes it and he doesn't correct her preference. It arrives black and hot and perfect.

"You're the one," the woman says. It's not a question.

"I don't know what you mean."

"The police came here asking about a man. Came with a picture. Said they were trying to identify someone. I said, yes, I remember him. He came in here sometimes. He would sit in that corner and drink coffee just like you're drinking now."

He looks at the corner she's indicated. There is nothing there except empty space and the promise of occupation.

"What was he like?" he asks.

The woman considers this with the seriousness it deserves. "Sad," she says finally. "Not angry. I've known angry people. Anger has a different shape. This was sadness that had nowhere to go. Like it had been sitting in him so long it had become hard, had become part of his structure."

"Did he ever say anything?"

"Not much. He would come in, drink his coffee, write in that little book of his. Sometimes I would try to talk to him, but he didn't want to talk. I could tell. So I stopped trying. I just left him alone."

He finishes his coffee. He wants to ask more questions—what his brother had been doing here, what had brought him to this city, what final thoughts had preceded the fall into the ravine. But he understands now that the woman had given him everything she had to give. Everything else is gone, absorbed into the body that has been preserved and catalogued and stored. Everything else is silence.

The café fills with other people as the morning progresses. They seem to know the woman. They call out greetings. They settle into chairs with the ease of repetition. He sits in the corner where his brother had sat and thinks about all the mornings his brother had spent here, perhaps wishing to be known, perhaps wishing to disappear. The distinction between these two desires is not as clear as he had once believed.

He leaves money on the table and walks back to the hotel. The phone is ringing when he enters the room.

"We have the preliminary dental confirmation," the examiner says. "It's definitely your brother. I wanted you to know before you heard it through official channels."

"Thank you," he says, though he's not sure what he's thanking her for. The confirmation seems unnecessary. He already knew. Some part of him had known the moment the ferry arrived in the dark.

He spends the rest of the day making phone calls to funeral homes and burial societies. He learns the costs of things. He learns that his brother's journey will require a box and a plot and a stone with a name carved into it. He learns that the world has systematic ways of handling its dead, that there are professionals who do nothing but facilitate this work.

At one point during the afternoon, he finds himself crying. It happens without warning while he is speaking to a man about the dimensions of caskets. The tears come and he tries to stop them, but they continue regardless, moving down his face with a kind of determination that seems separate from his will. The man on the phone becomes uncomfortable and offers to call back at a better time, but he insists on finishing the conversation. He doesn't want to stop. He wants to speak through the tears, to let them be part of what he says, to make them audible to this stranger. This small assertion of witness.

That night he dreams of water. In the dream he is crossing the strait again, but this time the water is rising, filling the ferry, and he is alone on the upper deck watching it rise with something like recognition. The water is cold but not painful. It has the quality of something inevitable, something that should have happened long ago.

He wakes at three in the morning. The city is silent outside his window. He stands and looks out at the harbor, at the dark water that has brought him here and will take him away. Somewhere in that water are currents and pressures and a cold that would stop a heart in minutes. Somewhere in the ravine north of here is the place where his brother ended. Somewhere in the ground of Manitoba is the place where both of them will eventually lie.

He thinks of the postcards. *I will not return.* Perhaps his brother had been trying to tell him something. Perhaps he had been asking him to understand that some separations are permanent, that some distances cannot be crossed even by the living. He had not understood. He had read the words and felt nothing, and this failure feels like his greatest sin.

He leaves the hotel in the early morning and walks to the cemetery. It takes him an hour, following streets until they become roads, roads until they become rural paths. The cemetery is small and old, filled with graves from the nineteenth century, with names he cannot read and lives he will never know. He finds a bench near the fence and sits.

The sun rises slowly, reluctantly, as if it too is not convinced the day should arrive. Somewhere in the city, his brother is still in the drawer. Somewhere in Manitoba, the earth is frozen hard, waiting to accept him in spring. Somewhere his mother is waiting for him to return and tell her what he has seen, though no words he possesses could adequately convey it.

He sits on the bench and watches the cemetery fill with light. The graves contain their secrets. The stones contain their names. The earth contains everything that has ever been lost. And he, the living one, the one who has been chosen to bear witness, sits and does not move, because there is nowhere else to go and nothing left to do but wait for the day to reveal itself completely, and for whatever comes next to arrive with its own terrible patience.
