He paused. He took a drink of something, she couldn't see what, the light in the room was low and she'd learned not to ask unnecessary questions. Jack Lovett didn't like unnecessary questions. What he liked was to talk, to lay it all out there in the dark, and she'd learned to listen, to sit very still and let him talk himself into whatever it was he was trying to say or not say or work through.

He said: The point I'm making is that it all seemed important at the time. Consequential. Like we were doing something that mattered, you understand? National security. Defense of the realm. All that. We believed in it. I believed in it. Most of us did. There was a clarity to it then, before things got muddled, before the questions started getting louder than the answers. Before people started asking why we needed to keep testing when we already had enough to destroy everything several times over. Before the radiation got into the water supply and the fish died in a particular way that made you wonder if you'd made a mistake.

The thing about those islands was that they belonged to people. Not Americans. Not anyone in the briefing rooms in Washington or the technical facilities in Nevada and California. The people who lived there, the Marshallese, they had names for those islands. They had stories. They knew which ones had the best coconuts and which ones had the cleanest water and which ones their ancestors were buried on. And we just rolled in with our equipment and our calculations and our pink sky and we blew it all to hell. Literally. We turned islands into craters. We turned reefs into rubble. We evacuated people and told them it was temporary and most of them never went back because there was nothing to go back to.

I remember one time, must have been 1956, I was standing on the dock at Eniwetok and there was this old man, Marshallese, watching us load equipment onto one of the transport ships. He just stood there watching. He didn't say anything. He didn't have to. You could read everything in his face—the loss, the anger, the helplessness. We had all the power. We had the bombs and the government and the money and the technology. He had nothing. He had a memory of what used to be there. That was the currency he was working with, and it wasn't worth anything to anyone who mattered.

But you couldn't really think about that while you were down there. You couldn't afford to. If you started thinking about what you were really doing, about whose world you were unmmaking, you'd go crazy. So you didn't think about it. You focused on the technical aspects. You thought about the spectacle. You thought about the pink sky and the perfect execution and whether the yield would match the predictions. You talked about everything else—your wives, your mistresses, your problems back home, the price of gas, the new cars coming out—anything to keep your mind away from what you were actually part of.

That was the real lesson of those islands, Inez. Not the technical data, though God knows we collected enough of that. Not the strategic implications, though the military-industrial complex certainly got its money's worth. The real lesson was about how easy it is to do terrible things when you're part of a system that's designed to insulate you from the moral weight of what you're doing. How easy it is to compartmentalize. How easy it is to become a technician of death instead of a human being making choices.

He stopped talking for a while. She could hear him breathing in the dark. She could hear the sound of the city outside—Los Angeles, they were in a hotel in Los Angeles, or maybe it was San Francisco, she'd lost track of which city they were in. Jack Lovett moved through cities like other men moved through rooms. He had business everywhere. He had connections everywhere. He had secrets everywhere.

She said: Is that why you quit?

He laughed, not kindly. He said: I didn't quit. I'm still in the game, Inez. I never quit. I just stopped going to the islands. I found other ways to be useful. Other ways to participate in the grand design without having to witness the pink sky. Without having to look into the faces of the people whose world was being unmade.

The thing about this business is that there are always new frontiers. New enemies. New threats that need to be assessed and calculated and managed. You think it's all about the Soviets, about this arms race, about whether we have enough destructive capacity to deter them from destroying us? That's part of it, sure. But that's not really what it's about. What it's about is control. Control of information. Control of technology. Control of people. Control of outcomes. And the islands were just the beginning. The islands were the experimental phase. The real work is happening now, in places you've never heard of, with people you'll never meet, dealing with questions that don't have easy answers.

I got into this line of work because I believed I was smart enough to make a difference. Smart enough to steer things in the right direction. Smart enough to be on the side of the angels, or at least on the side of the Americans, which at that time seemed like the same thing. I was young and I had a government job and a security clearance and access to information that most people would never see. I thought that made me special. Chosen. I thought it meant something.

But what you learn, if you're paying attention, is that the people running the show aren't that smart. Not really. They're smart in narrow ways, in technical ways, in ways that let them build things and destroy things and calculate probabilities. But they're not smart about the big picture. They're not wise. And there's a difference between being smart and being wise. Smart lets you split the atom. Wise tells you whether you should. Smart lets you calculate the yield of a thermonuclear device. Wise tells you whether you should detonate it. Nobody I worked with was wise. I don't think I'm wise either, if I'm being honest with you. I think I'm just a guy who got in over his head and made some decisions I can't undo and now I'm trying to figure out what to do with the rest of my life without going completely insane.

He took another drink. She heard the glass hit the table.

He said: You want to know something funny? The people who were against the testing, the ones who protested and marched and wrote letters to Congress, they were right. They were right about all of it. The radiation. The contamination. The violation of indigenous peoples. All of it. But they were right for reasons that were too simple. They thought if you could just stop the tests, if you could just get people to understand the danger, then everything would be okay. They didn't understand that the tests were a symptom, not the disease. The disease is the assumption that we have the right to do anything as long as we can technically do it. The disease is the belief that might makes right. The disease is the idea that human beings are just variables in an equation, to be managed and manipulated according to whatever strategy seems most advantageous at the moment.

The tests are over now, you know. They stopped them. Got some kind of test ban treaty, at least a partial one. I remember when that came through, I thought maybe something had actually changed. Maybe people had learned something. But they hadn't, not really. They just found other ways to do the same things. Other ways to pursue the same agenda. The tests might be over but the mindset that created them, that's still running the show. That's still determining policy. That's still deciding which countries get nuclear weapons and which ones don't, which leaders get supported and which ones get overthrown, which populations get protected and which ones get sacrificed for the greater good.

I'm tired, Inez. I'm tired of being part of it. But I don't know how to not be part of it. That's the trap, you see. Once you're in, there's no exit. You know too much. You've done too much. You've made decisions that other people made based on information you provided. You can't just walk away and pretend it didn't happen. You can't just become an ordinary citizen and live an ordinary life. You're marked. You're compromised. You're part of the machinery and the machinery doesn't let go.

So what do you do? You tell yourself it's for the best. You tell yourself that if you didn't do it, someone else would do it worse. You tell yourself that you're one of the good guys, that at least you're trying to keep things from getting out of hand. You find ways to rationalize. Ways to make peace with yourself. Ways to sleep at night even though you know what you are.

The pink sky, though. That stays with you. That stays in your mind. I can close my eyes right now and see it exactly the way it was. That particular shade of pink that no painter could approximate. The moisture in the air. The smell of gardenias. The weight of knowing that in a few minutes, in a few seconds really, that whole world was going to be unmade. All that beauty, all that order, all that delicate balance that had existed for thousands of years, was going to be replaced by heat and blast and radiation and death.

That's the image that comes to me when I'm alone. That's the image that makes me question everything. That's the image that tells me maybe I wasn't on the side of the angels after all. Maybe I was just another technician in service of forces I didn't fully understand. Another smart guy without wisdom. Another man doing terrible things in service of a larger design that I couldn't quite see.

He stopped talking. The silence stretched out between them like something solid, something you could almost touch. Outside, the city continued its relentless hum. Traffic. Neon. The machinery of modern life grinding on, indifferent to the presence of one man trying to make sense of what he'd done and what it meant and whether any of it had been worth it.

Inez Victor, who was born Inez Christian, who had married a man named Victor and divorced him and kept the name because it was good for politics, who knew Jack Lovett in ways that most people wouldn't understand, sat in the dark and listened. She didn't offer comfort. She didn't offer absolution. She didn't offer any of the easy platitudes that people usually offered when confronted with this kind of confession. She just sat there and let him sit with his ghosts. She understood, in a way that few people did, that some things couldn't be fixed or forgiven or explained away. Some things just had to be carried forward, like luggage you can't put down, like a weight you learn to balance. Some things were the price you paid for having been alive during a particular time, for having had access to particular information, for having made particular choices.

She said: You should sleep.

He said: I never sleep well. I haven't slept well in years.

She said: Then you should try. You have that meeting in the morning with the senator's office.

He said: The senator's office. Right. More of the same. More strategy and calculation and careful positioning. More people trying to figure out how to use information to maintain power. More of everything I'm trying to get away from.

But he would go to the meeting. He would sit in some sterile government office and provide whatever expertise they wanted. He would use his knowledge and his connections and his understanding of how the system worked to help them achieve whatever objectives they'd set for themselves. That was what he did. That was what he'd been doing for twenty years. And he would probably keep doing it for another twenty if he lived that long, because he genuinely didn't know how to stop. The machine had him. The machinery of power and influence and national security and all the rest of it. He was part of it and the part couldn't remove itself from the whole without ceasing to exist.

So he would sleep, or try to. And tomorrow he would wake and shower and put on a suit and go to his meeting. And Inez would do whatever it was she was doing, building her political career, making her own compromises, living her own complicated life in the service of systems she also didn't fully control. They would exist in parallel for a while. They would meet in hotel rooms in different cities. They would talk about the past and the things that haunted them and the questions that couldn't be answered. And then they would part ways again, each returning to their respective duties, each carrying forward the weight of history and complicity and hard choices made in circumstances that offered no good options.

The pink sky would remain. The image of those islands would persist. The knowledge of what had been done would linger. But life would continue. The machinery would continue. The world would continue. And Jack Lovett, and people like him, would continue doing what they'd always done—managing outcomes, controlling information, making decisions that affected millions of people they would never meet, in service of a vision of national interest that they themselves weren't entirely sure they believed in anymore.

He said: Goodnight, Inez.

She said: Goodnight, Jack.

And in the dark of that hotel room in whatever city they were in, they settled into their separate silences and waited for morning to come.
