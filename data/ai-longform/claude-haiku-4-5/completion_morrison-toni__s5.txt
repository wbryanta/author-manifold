He had been on the road for three weeks, and the weight of his purpose sat heavier than the coin in his satchel. Jacob had left his northern territories on business—ostensibly to survey new lands, to calculate what profits might be wrung from the Virginia holdings he had recently acquired through a combination of shrewd negotiation and outright purchase. But the true reason for his journey lay in the letter folded inside his shirt, close to his ribs, written in a hand he had not seen in twenty years.

The letter had arrived in autumn, brought by a trader who knew his name and little else. It contained no greeting, no pleasantries. It simply stated: *Come if you can. If you cannot, send word. I will understand. —E*

E. There was only one E who would write to him in such a manner, and the knowledge of it had unsettled his entire household. His wife, Rebekka, had not asked him about it directly, but he knew she understood from the way her lips thinned when she saw him reading by candlelight, from the particular attention she paid to the packing of his supplies.

Regina's hooves fell into a steady rhythm on the old trail, and Jacob began to relax slightly. The forest here was dense but navigable, the path worn smooth by generations of traffic. He had traveled worse roads in worse country. Still, prudence dictated that he avoid the settlements proper, at least until he reached his destination. The plantation he sought lay perhaps thirty miles inland, a property of modest size but considerable influence, owned by a man named Sorrow—though whether that was a Christian name or something else, Jacob could not say. The letter had been postmarked from that vicinity.

As the afternoon wore on and the fog continued to burn away, Jacob found himself watching the tree line with renewed alertness. The political upheaval of recent years had left the region unsettled in ways both obvious and subtle. A man traveling alone, regardless of the color of his skin or the fineness of his horse, was fair game for any number of predations. Bandits, militia, local authorities with questionable legitimacy—all were possibilities.

He had seen the aftermath of the rebellion the letter had alluded to. Towns burned. Bodies hanging from trees. The swift, brutal consolidation of power by those who had always held it, merely sharpening their grip. And now, with the new laws in place, the chaos had been given legal sanction. Jacob understood economics, the mathematics of supply and cost and profit. What troubled him about Virginia was that the gentry had found a way to make inhumanity profitable, to enshrine cruelty in statute. It was efficient in a way that disgusted him, though not—if he was honest with himself—in a way that prevented him from participating in it.

He had profited from slaves. His northern holdings included indentured servants and people who were not servants by choice. He had not thought much about it in recent years, had rationalized it as he rationalized most things, as the necessary machinery of commerce. But the letter had stirred something in him that he had thought long dead.

The trail widened as the afternoon light began to slant sideways through the canopy. Jacob could see evidence of recent passage—hoofprints, wagon ruts. He passed a small clearing where someone had built a shelter of branches and bark, now abandoned. A fire pit, cold for weeks or months. He wondered who had camped here, and whether they had been fleeing or pursuing something.

By evening, he had reached the outskirts of a settlement—not the main village, but a scatter of buildings that seemed to serve as a waystation for travelers. There was a tavern of sorts, identified by a faded sign depicting a stag of improbable dimensions. Jacob considered stopping to gather information but decided against it. The fewer people who noticed him, the better. Instead, he rode past and found a clearing suitable for camping, where he could see anyone approaching and could hear the movements of the forest around him.

He built no fire. He ate cold provisions from his satchel—hard bread, cheese, dried meat—and watered Regina at a stream that ran nearby. The horse was nervous, her ears pricked forward, and Jacob understood that she sensed something in the forest that he could not. He stood with his hand on her neck, offering what comfort he could, though he knew she would be reassured only by distance from whatever unseen presence troubled her.

It was a long night. Jacob slept little, dozing in intervals and starting awake at every sound. Toward dawn, he heard voices—men on horseback, riding hard along the trail he had traveled. He counted them by sound: at least six, perhaps more. They passed without slowing, heading deeper into the forest. Militia, probably, or enforcers of whatever local authority claimed jurisdiction. The new laws gave them broad latitude.

When the sky began to lighten, Jacob rose, shook the stiffness from his joints, and prepared to ride on. The letter had named no specific meeting place, which seemed both deliberate and tormenting. He would have to find the plantation, make inquiries, discover what E wanted from him after so many years of silence.

The forest began to open as morning advanced, giving way to cultivated fields. Here the transformation that wealth had wrought became visible. Tobacco stretched in neat rows, workers bent in the heat. But even at this early hour, Jacob could see the difference between those who moved with the sluggish weariness of the enslaved and those who worked with some modicum of voluntary pace. The new laws were thorough in their distinctions.

He kept to the boundaries of the fields, riding parallel to the planted rows without entering them directly. A man on a fine horse was conspicuous, and he wanted to know more before he announced himself. The plantation house, when he glimpsed it through a stand of oaks, was substantial but not grand—a two-story structure of brick and wood, with outbuildings arranged with the precision of commerce.

Jacob reined Regina in and sat for a long moment, gathering his nerve for something he could not quite name. Twenty years. The girl—for she had been a girl then, no more than fourteen or fifteen—had become a woman. Her name, as he remembered it, was Emilia. But the letter was signed only with an initial, as though the full revelation of herself was something to be earned or negotiated.

He turned Regina toward the house, riding slowly, deliberately, giving anyone who might be watching time to see him coming. As he drew near, a man emerged from the main structure. He was older, with the bearing of someone accustomed to authority, though his face bore an expression of surprise.

"Your business?" the man called out. His voice was educated, refined, the voice of a gentleman rather than a common laborer or overseer.

"I am Jacob Vaark," Jacob replied, dismounting with practiced ease. "I have come at the invitation of—" He paused, uncertain how to proceed. "I have come in response to a letter."

The man's expression shifted, something passing across his features too quickly for Jacob to read. "A letter. I see. Well, you had best stable your horse. Someone will wish to see you, I think. My name is Sorrow. I oversee this property."

So this was Sorrow—the name from the opening of the letter, the only indication of whom E had become. Jacob tied Regina to a post and followed the man into the cool interior of the house.

The interior was neat, spare even, furnished with good pieces but not lavishly. They passed through a central hall and into a parlor where a woman sat with needlework in her lap. She was perhaps fifty, with graying hair pulled back in a severe style. She looked up as they entered, and her eyes went wide.

"Mr. Vaark," she said, and her voice held a complicated mixture of emotions. "How quickly you came."

"I received a letter," Jacob said carefully. "It was addressed to me. I confess I came somewhat in the dark as to its purpose."

The woman set down her needlework with deliberate care. "Emilia will want to see you. She is resting at present, but if you will wait—" She rose from her chair, and Jacob could see that she was not as composed as her voice suggested. Her hands trembled slightly. "Please, sit. Sorrow will bring you refreshment."

Jacob sat, his mind racing. Emilia was here, then, at this plantation, in the care of this woman—who must be her mother, or perhaps some other relation. He had not dared to hope that she might be alive, much less that she would remember him.

"You have known Emilia long," the woman said. It was not quite a question.

"I knew her," Jacob replied carefully. "A long time ago. In another place."

The woman nodded, as though this confirmed something she had suspected. "She does not speak of those days often. But she has not forgotten you." She paused, choosing her words with visible care. "She asked me to tell you, if ever you came, that the child is your daughter. That she is nearly grown now, and that Emilia wishes you to know it, though she understands if you choose to deny it."

Jacob felt the world tilt slightly beneath him. A daughter. Not merely a ghost of his past, but a living, breathing child—a young woman now, Emilia had said—who carried his blood and his presence into this uncertain world.

"Where is she?" he asked, his voice rough with emotion he could not entirely suppress.

"In the back gardens," the woman said. "She takes the air in the afternoon. She knows you are here. She has been expecting you, in a manner of speaking, ever since she learned to understand the meaning of the word 'father.'"

Jacob stood, moved toward the door the woman indicated, his heart beating in a way that he found both exhilarating and terrifying. This was why he had come, though he had not known it fully until this moment. Not merely to settle an old debt or to answer the call of a woman he had loved in his youth, but to confront the consequences of that love, rendered flesh and history.

The gardens were extensive, laid out with care among fruit trees and flowering shrubs. And there, sitting on a bench beneath a mulberry tree, was a young woman—perhaps eighteen, perhaps twenty—with hair the color of autumn leaves and a face that held something of Emilia's delicate features but also something harder, more defined.

She rose as he approached, and for a long moment they simply regarded each other across the distance of years and circumstance.

"Mr. Vaark," she said finally. Her voice was steady, but Jacob could hear the undercurrent of emotion beneath it. "I am glad you came. I have many questions for you, but I thought it best to ask them face to face."

"Your mother—Emilia—she told me," Jacob said. "I confess I am still attempting to comprehend."

"Yes, well," the young woman said, with something that might have been bitterness or might have been resignation, "it is a great deal to comprehend. I am Flora. That is the name I was given here. Though I gather I had another name once, before circumstances changed."

"I did not know," Jacob said, though even as he spoke, he understood the inadequacy of the words. "When I knew your mother, she did not tell me—"

"She could not tell you," Flora interrupted. "She was owned, Mr. Vaark. She bore me while she was owned. And then, through circumstances that I do not fully understand, she came to this place, and her condition was altered, and we have lived here, mother and child, under the protection of this household. But she has never forgotten you, and neither, I think, has she forgotten what was done to her."

Jacob felt shame rise in him, complex and multilayered. Not shame at Flora's existence—never that—but shame at his own ignorance, his own convenient forgetting of a girl he had known briefly and intensely before his life had taken him in other directions.

"What is it you wish from me?" he asked, because he needed to understand what debt he owed, what recompense might be possible.

"I do not know yet," Flora said honestly. "My mother wished to see you, to know whether you were a man of conscience or merely a man of convenience. I am here to judge that for myself. And perhaps—" She hesitated, and for the first time, her composure cracked slightly. "Perhaps to know what I am, beyond what has been given to me by circumstance."

"You are my daughter," Jacob said, and the words felt true and dangerous and deeply uncertain all at once. "And you are yourself, entirely and completely. Whatever else I might offer you beyond those facts, I do not yet know."

Flora's expression softened imperceptibly. "That is honest, at least. Come. My mother is waiting. We have a great deal to discuss, and the evening is coming on."

As they walked back toward the house together, Jacob felt the weight of the future settling upon him alongside the weight of the past. The letter had called him across distance and time, and now it seemed that his answer to that call would alter the rest of his life in ways he could not yet foresee. Behind them, the gardens grew dim in the gathering dusk, and the great business of consequence—that machinery of human connection and obligation and love—turned inexorably forward.
