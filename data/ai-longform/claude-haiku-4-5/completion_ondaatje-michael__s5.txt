Anil bent forward to examine the bodies more carefully, aware of the woman's presence still lingering at the perimeter of the grave, though she had moved back as promised. The afternoon light had shifted, creating new shadows across the skeletal forms. One of the bodies showed signs of perimortem trauma to the ribs, the kind of damage that spoke of violence in the moments before or after death. She documented this with photographs, her movements precise and unhurried, though her mind was somewhere else entirely—with that shoulder, that particular slope of grief she would carry with her long after this excavation ended.

The other forensic team members worked in concentrated silence. Sarath, who had trained her in these methods years ago in another country, another war, watched her from across the pit. He understood what she had witnessed in that woman's face. He had seen it before, in other excavations, other countries. The universality of loss never diminished its weight.

By late afternoon, they had exposed both bodies sufficiently to begin the careful process of removal. The woman had not returned, though Anil kept half-expecting to see her emerge from the trees that bordered the excavation site. Instead, one of the family members—a younger man, perhaps a nephew—appeared with a bundle wrapped in cloth. He placed it at the edge of the pit without speaking and left again. Inside was food: rice wrapped in leaves, some fruit, a bottle of water.

They ate as the sun began its descent toward the mountains. The landscape around them was beautiful in a way that seemed almost obscene—the green so vivid, the air so clean and fragrant with earth and growing things. Anil had worked in many places where the beauty of the natural world stood in stark contrast to the human violence that had occurred there. She had never gotten used to it. Perhaps she never would.

That night, back at the small hotel in town, Anil sat in her room and began her preliminary notes. She would need to conduct a full skeletal analysis, run tests on the bone samples, compare dental records if they could be found. But already, from what she had observed, she was fairly certain these were the remains of two adult males, likely in their thirties when they died. The positioning of the bodies suggested they had been placed together deliberately, or at least had remained in proximity through decades of decay and displacement in the soil.

She thought about the woman again, about what it meant to find the body of a loved one in such circumstances, or to find what might be the body, only to have it turn out to be a stranger. The families moved from excavation to excavation like pilgrims searching for a shrine that might never exist. Some would find closure. Others would only find more questions.

Her phone rang. It was her contact at the government office in the capital, a man named Gamini who had arranged for her to come to the country in the first place.

"How is the work progressing?" he asked in English, his accent carrying the careful precision of someone who had learned the language formally but had never lived in an English-speaking country.

"We've exposed two bodies at the first site," Anil said. "Initial assessment suggests they've been there for approximately fifteen to twenty years."

There was a pause. "That is consistent with the reports from the communities in that region."

"There will be more sites," Anil said. It was not a question.

"Yes. There are many reports of disappearances in the western highlands. This is why your expertise is so important. We need to establish what happened. We need the evidence to speak."

After they hung up, Anil sat in the darkness of her room. The concept of evidence speaking was one she had once believed in completely. Now she understood that evidence only spoke to those willing to listen. And even then, it could be interpreted in so many different ways. The bones could tell a story, yes—they could indicate violence, they could suggest duration of death, they could provide information about age and health and diet. But they could not say why. They could not say whether the violence was justified or unjustified, whether the death was an accident or a murder, whether the person killed was guilty or innocent of the things they had been accused of.

The next morning, before dawn, Anil prepared the equipment for the exhumation itself. This was always the most delicate part of the work—the removal of the remains from the ground where they had lain for so long. There was a ritualistic quality to it that transcended the scientific procedure. This person, these people, were returning to the world of the living, to the world of testimony and meaning. They were becoming evidence again, becoming witnesses to their own deaths.

The family members began to arrive as the light broke over the eastern hills. More of them this time. An older woman who must have been the mother of the man the younger woman had lost. An elderly man in a faded shirt, probably a father or an uncle. Children, brought to see where their grandfather or uncle or cousin had been found. Anil wondered what it meant to them, this excavation. Did it bring closure? Or did it simply reopen wounds that had begun to scar over?

By mid-morning, they had lifted the first skeleton from the pit. It was fragile, already compromised by time and the acidity of the soil. As they worked, Anil found herself thinking about the history of her own country, the histories of so many countries where this work had become necessary. The bones of the disappeared, the silenced, the eliminated. There was no shortage of sites where she could work. There was an endless supply of grief, waiting to be excavated and documented and given back to families who had been waiting in darkness.

The second skeleton proved more challenging. It had been partially crushed by the weight of soil above it, and the bones were more fragmented. As they carefully cleared the surrounding earth, Anil noticed something near where the skeleton's hands had been. It was small and metallic, corroded with rust. She cleaned it carefully and realized it was a ring—a wedding band, probably. She placed it in a small container to be tested and catalogued.

Anil looked up from her work and saw the mother, the older woman, watching. Their eyes met for a moment. The woman's face was composed, but her hands trembled slightly. Anil did not know what to say, so she said nothing. She simply continued her work, treating the remains with the care she would want shown to her own body, should it ever be lost and found in such a place.

By the late afternoon, both skeletons had been removed and placed in specialized boxes for transport. The family members remained at the pit, as if leaving would mean the bodies would disappear again. Anil understood. She had seen this before. In Guatemala, in El Salvador, in Cambodia. The fear of losing the dead again ran deep in communities where disappearance was a state policy, where the government itself had been the instrument of erasure.

She approached the older woman and, through gestures and Sarath's translation, explained that they would conduct the analysis in the capital and would be in touch within a few weeks. The woman nodded, but her eyes remained fixed on the boxes containing the remains of her son.

That evening, as the sun set and turned the landscape purple and gold, Anil sat alone at the excavation site. The plastic sheeting had been placed back over the pit, weighted down with stones. The work would resume tomorrow at a different location, a few kilometers away, where community members had reported finding bones exposed by recent heavy rains.

She thought about the woman in the grave, crouching between the remains of her husband and her brother. What must it be like to touch those bones, to know that this is all that remained of someone you had loved? To realize that the body—the physical form that had occupied space and time, that had spoken and moved and held you—had been reduced to this, to calcium and phosphate and mystery?

Anil had trained herself to maintain distance from the emotional weight of the work. It was necessary. If she allowed herself to feel the full force of it, she would not be able to function. But the distance was always imperfect. Things leaked through—a shoulder's slope, the way a woman looked at the bones of the man she had loved, the vulnerability of a skeleton exposed to the air after decades of darkness.

She stood up and walked to the edge of the excavation site, looking out toward the mountains in the distance. The first stars were beginning to appear in the darkening sky. Somewhere out there, in villages and towns and cities, there were more families waiting. More people had disappeared in the years since the war had officially ended, though Gamini had said that the war continued in different forms. She knew what that meant. The violence had not stopped; it had simply become less visible, less officially acknowledged.

Her phone buzzed with a message from Sarath: "Rest well. Tomorrow we have three sites to begin."

Three sites. Three sets of families waiting. Three new testimonies to extract from the earth.

Anil returned to her hotel as the night deepened. The streets were quiet, the town shutting down for the evening. She ordered tea in the small restaurant attached to the hotel and sat with her notebook, beginning to write up the formal preliminary report on the two bodies they had exhumed.

She wrote about the positioning, the condition of the bones, the presence of the ring, the evidence of perimortem trauma. She wrote in the neutral, objective language of forensic science, translating the raw reality of death and loss into data points and measurements. It was a kind of violence in itself, she sometimes thought—this reduction of a human life to a skeleton, a skeleton to evidence, evidence to a report that would be filed and studied and used in ways she could not fully control or predict.

But it was necessary. Without this work, without this translation of the physical evidence into language that could be understood and acted upon, there would be no accountability. The disappeared would remain disappeared. The dead would remain voiceless.

As she wrote, she could hear the sounds of the town settling into sleep—the distant bark of a dog, the sound of a radio playing music in another room, the murmur of voices from somewhere down the street. Life continuing, as it always did, in the spaces between the investigations into death.

Anil finished her notes around midnight. She showered and lay in bed, but sleep would not come. Her mind kept returning to that moment in the grave, the woman on her haunches between her dead. The particular way grief had shaped that body, that shoulder, that angle of the spine. The way love could outlast the body itself, could persist in memory and in the fierce attention paid to bone and dust.

She had spent years training in forensic anthropology, had worked on cases in multiple countries, had learned to read the stories that skeletons could tell. But she had never fully learned to protect herself from the emotional resonance of the work. And perhaps, she thought as she lay in the darkness, that was not a failure. Perhaps the ability to be moved by the evidence of suffering was essential to doing this work with any kind of integrity.

The next morning came too early. Anil rose before dawn, as she had the morning before, and began preparing the equipment for the three new sites. Sarath arrived with coffee, and they reviewed the maps and reports that detailed where the excavations would take place. Each site had been located through interviews with community members, through oral histories and survivor accounts, through the careful mapping of disappearances and the places where bodies had been rumored to have been hidden.

As they drove to the first site, Anil found herself thinking about what came after the excavation, after the initial examination and the removal of the remains. There would be the laboratory work, the careful analysis of each bone, the extraction of any possible DNA, the construction of a biological profile that could then be matched against missing person reports and interviews with families. If they were fortunate, they would find matches. They would be able to tell families that they had found their sons, their husbands, their brothers.

But even that would not be complete. The bones could tell what happened to the body, but they could not tell why. They could not provide accountability for the decision to kill, to disappear, to erase. That would require witnesses, records, confessions—the kinds of evidence that governments and the perpetrators of violence were often very good at destroying or hiding.

The first site of the day was in a rural area, far from any town or main road. The family members were already waiting, standing in a loose group near where the earth had been disturbed by recent rains. A young man broke away from the group and approached Sarath, speaking rapidly in a language Anil did not understand. Sarath listened, nodding, and then turned to her.

"They found pieces of bone last week," he said. "They have been waiting for us to come and search properly."

Anil knelt down and examined the exposed earth. She could see fragments of bone, weathered and scattered. This site would be more challenging than the previous one—the remains were more dispersed, more fragmentary. It would take longer to map, to excavate, to understand what had happened and when.

She began to work, establishing a grid system, carefully marking and photographing each piece of bone as it was exposed. Behind her, the family members watched. An older man held a photograph—a young man in military uniform, smiling. Anil did not need Sarath to tell her that this was the person they were searching for, the person they hoped to find in the scattered bone fragments emerging from the earth.

Hours passed. The sun climbed higher, hotter. One of the family members brought water and food. Anil and her team accepted the offerings gratefully. The work of excavation was methodical and slow, and it required sustenance to continue, required focus and care.

By late afternoon, they had exposed what appeared to be the remains of at least two individuals, possibly more. The bones were mixed together, as if several bodies had been disposed of in the same pit, or as if grave robbers or animals had disturbed the original burial, scattering the remains across a larger area.

Anil found herself wondering about the sequence of events that had led to this moment. These people—whoever they had been, whatever they had been accused of or believed to have done—had been killed, had been hidden in this pit in this remote location. And now, years later, their bones were being excavated by a forensic anthropologist from a foreign country, documented and catalogued and analyzed. What did they think about this, she sometimes wondered, if consciousness persisted in some form beyond death? Would they feel gratitude that someone was finally giving them witness? Or would they feel further violation, their very bones being used as evidence in a process whose outcome they could not control?

She was aware that these were not scientific questions, that they had no place in her formal reports and analyses. But they persisted in her mind, especially at moments like this, when she was down in the dirt with the bones of the dead, trying to extract their stories from the calcium and phosphate and the spaces between.

The sun was setting when she finally called a halt to the day's work. The family members, who had remained at the site all day, began to disperse, moving back toward the village from which they had come. The older man with the photograph approached Anil before he left. He said something, his voice low and urgent, looking directly into her eyes. Sarath listened and then turned to her.

"He says he trusts you to find his son," Sarath translated. "He says he has faith that the bones will speak truth."

Anil nodded, accepting the weight of that trust. She wanted to tell him that the bones would speak, but only if someone was willing to listen, and even then their testimony could be disputed and reinterpreted. But she said nothing. She simply watched him leave, moving slowly back toward the path that would take him to the village.

That night, in her room at the hotel, Anil added her notes from the new excavation to her growing file. Three sites examined in two days. Potentially five to seven individuals represented in the remains exhumed so far. Preliminary assessments suggested deaths ranging from ten to twenty years ago. Multiple instances of trauma consistent with violence.

She was tired in a way that went beyond physical exhaustion. It was the kind of tiredness that came from bearing witness to atrocity, from holding in her mind the full weight of human suffering while maintaining the technical detachment necessary to do the work correctly.

Her phone rang. It was a colleague from an international forensic organization, calling from the capital.

"How are you managing?" the colleague asked. "Are you getting the access you need?"

"So far, yes," Anil said. "The families are cooperative. The government has been helpful in providing leads on sites. But there is so much work. So many sites to investigate."

"I know," the colleague said. "That is always the case. The need is endless. But we must prioritize. We must work on the cases most likely to be prosecuted, the ones most likely to lead to accountability."

After they hung up, Anil sat with that word—accountability. In her experience, accountability was rare. Most of the forensic work she did resulted in documentation, in giving families the closure of knowing what had happened to their loved ones. But the perpetrators often went free, protected by amnesty laws or government cover-ups or simply the passage of time and the failure of international will to pursue justice.

Still, she continued the work. Because the alternative was silence, was allowing the disappeared to remain disappeared, was letting the past be erased in the name of peace or reconciliation or moving forward.

The next three days blurred together into a rhythm of excavation and documentation, of careful work in the heat and the dust, of families waiting and watching and hoping. By the end of that week, Anil and her team had examined seven sites and exhumed the remains of what preliminary analysis suggested were at least twenty individuals.

The boxes of remains filled the storage room at the lab where she would conduct her more detailed analysis. The photographs covered the walls, documenting each stage of each excavation. The notebooks filled with observations and measurements and preliminary assessments.

And yet, despite the volume of work, despite the clear evidence of violence and systematic disposal of bodies, Anil could not shake the sense that this was only the smallest fraction of what had actually occurred. The number of officially reported disappearances was in the thousands. The number of sites they had identified was in the dozens. At the current rate of work, it would take years to examine all the potential sites, and there was no guarantee that the government would continue to fund the work or that international attention would remain focused on Sri Lanka when there were so many other crises, other conflicts, other disappeared demanding investigation.

One evening, about ten days into the work, Anil went to a local restaurant and found herself sitting next to Sarath. He was eating alone, and he invited her to join him. They sat in companionable silence for a while, eating rice and curry, before Sarath spoke.

"You are thinking about the scale of the work," he said. It was not a question.

"Yes," Anil said. "There is so much. And I wonder if any of it will matter. If anyone will actually be held accountable."

"That is not your responsibility," Sarath said gently. "Your responsibility is to give the dead a voice. To document what happened to them. What happens with that documentation is a different question, one that involves lawyers and politicians and courts. But at least the bones will have spoken. At least the evidence will exist."

Anil knew he was right, but it provided only limited comfort. She had done this work in other countries, had seen how the documentation could be ignored or disputed or buried in legal proceedings that went nowhere. She had seen how the pursuit of justice could itself become a kind of violence, reopening wounds in families that had begun to scar over.

But she also understood that Sarath's point was valid. Without the documentation, there was nothing. The bones had to speak, had to be given testimony, had to be allowed to enter the historical record. What happened after that was beyond her control.

As they finished their meal and Anil prepared to return to her hotel, Sarath said something else.

"The woman you saw in the grave, the one who was looking at the remains of her husband and brother—her name is Lakshmi. She comes to every excavation in this region. She is looking for them still, even though she has seen their bodies identified and removed. It is as if the excavation itself is a kind of prayer for her, a way of staying connected to them."

Anil thought about this as she walked back to her room. The idea of excavation as prayer, as ritual connection to the dead. It made sense to her, in a way that transcended the scientific framework within which she operated. The bones were not just evidence. They were also sacred, in their own way. They were what remained of people who had lived and loved and existed in the world.

That night, Anil dreamed of the woman in the grave, of the particular angle of that shoulder, of the grief that had shaped her body. And when she woke, she understood that she would carry that image with her forever, that it would be part of the cost of this work, the price she paid for bearing witness.

The excavations would continue. The bones would speak their silent testimony. The families would wait and watch and hope. And Anil would return again and again to the pits, to the earth that held the bones of the disappeared, seeking to give voice to the voiceless, seeking to create a record that might outlast the violence, might speak truth in a world that often seemed eager to forget.
