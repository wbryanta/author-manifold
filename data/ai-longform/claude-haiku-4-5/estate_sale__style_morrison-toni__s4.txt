# 

The particulars of morning had dissolved by the time she stood in the foyer where her mother's shoes had lived for forty years. Not the shoes themselves—those were upstairs in a banker's box, sorted by Margaret according to some principle of degradation that made sense to her but to no one else, certainly not to Diana, who had hired her that way, unsorted and whole, the way things should be but never were. What remained of the shoes was the space, the particular darkness of that corner, the dust risen in a pattern that suggested traffic, suggestion being all that remained now, all that was worth keeping, Margaret having decided otherwise.

The auction house had sent three men. Two of them looked like they'd been designed to carry furniture—wide across the shoulders, their hands already callused before they touched anything belonging to Diana's mother, Helen Reardon, dead now thirty-two days in the careful way old people died in Ohio, which is to say quietly and with apologies to whoever found them. Helen had apologized even to the hospice nurse, the one with the kind face and the hands that worked with the certainty of someone who'd held the dying before. "I'm sorry for the trouble," Helen had whispered, her voice already leaving her, already taking its familiar inflections somewhere beyond retrieval. Diana had been there. She had heard it. And now she was here, in her mother's house, which was not her house, had never quite been her house even when she'd lived in it, even when she'd come down the stairs in the morning and made breakfast in that kitchen where the linoleum was lifting at the corners, a problem Helen had never solved, had only pointed out each time Diana visited, as if the linoleum's failure was somehow a moral failing, a thing that had disappointed her.

The men were taking the dining room table now. She could hear them in the other room, their voices the kind of voices that came from their chests, deep and practical. One of them said, "Her end goes up." The other one, the youngest, said something she didn't catch. They moved through the space with the casual ownership of people who'd done this before, who'd moved other people's lives out of other houses, for other daughters standing in other foyers, waiting for the emptiness to become official. Diana watched them from her position near the stairs, her hands in her cardigan pockets, the October light coming through the oval window in the door, landing on her face in a way that probably made her look sad. She probably looked sad. She should probably feel sad. Instead, she felt the particular numbness that came from watching the familiar become unfamiliar, the way her mother's table—dark wood with carved legs that had always seemed too ornate for Helen's taste, though Helen had never said that, had only allowed it to sit there year after year, gathering a patina of indifference—was now just a table, just an object being moved by men with competent hands.

"You the daughter?" This was the oldest one, the one with the foreman's bearing, the one who'd been waiting at the door at nine o'clock with his clipboard and his assessment of the house the way others assessed horses.

"Yes," Diana said.

"You want to walk through before we take the bedroom suite? Make sure there's nothing you missed?"

She had walked through already. She had touched things. She had found a letter in the nightstand drawer, unsent, addressed to someone named Robert with no last name, dated 1987. She had read it—the first sentence, anyway: "You were right about everything, but I was right about the home." That was all. That was enough. She had put it back in the drawer. She had not told Margaret about it. Margaret was a professional. Margaret would have photographed it for the inventory. Diana was not a professional. She was a daughter, and a daughter, even at fifty-three, could choose not to know things about her mother, could choose to let some small shames move out of the house untracked.

"No," Diana said. "I've seen what I need to see."

The man nodded. He understood. Or he didn't. But he moved away, back toward the staircase, and his footsteps joined the sound of the other footsteps, and the house filled with the noise of its own dismantling, a sound not unlike the sound of rain, if rain were made of grunts and the careful profanity of working men.

Diana moved to the living room. The furniture had been photographed—Margaret had done that, digital images uploaded to the auction house website, where collectors and bargain hunters and people who simply liked the idea of old things could bid on what was now leaving. Most of it was going for less than the reserve. The wingback chairs had failed to meet their minimum. The credenza had sold, but barely. The lamps—a pair of brass things with pleated shades that Diana's mother had loved because they didn't show dust—had been sold as a lot, and then the buyer had canceled, had decided that pleats, after all, were not what he wanted, did not suit his vision of whatever vision collectors had. Everything was worth less than it had been, would be worth less still each day it spent in a warehouse somewhere, each day that made it older, that made it less fashionable, less urgent. This was the trajectory of things. Diana knew this. Helen had not understood this. Helen had believed in value the way she'd believed in other immutable things: God, cleanliness, the proper order of seasons.

The sofa was going next. Diana could hear them estimating its weight, calculating the angle required to get it out the front door without disturbing the painted frame. Helen had bought that sofa in 1994. Diana had been here that day—had she? Or was she remembering something her mother had told her about that day, Helen's story of her own discernment having replaced whatever actual memory Diana might have had? This was the way of it. The way all mothers erased their daughters by containing all the stories. By being the authority on what had happened, when, and what it had meant. Helen had been the keeper of Diana's own history, and now Diana was reduced to wondering if she'd ever actually chosen anything, or if she'd simply inherited preferences, inherited tastes, inherited the whole architecture of her own life from a woman who was no longer here to correct her.

The sofa came past her, tilted at a dangerous angle, the youngest man at its head saying something to her—"Excuse us," probably, or "Coming through," something polite and necessary. Diana moved away. She found herself in the kitchen, where the real work of a life was supposed to happen, according to Helen, though Helen had done very little cooking, had relied instead on the same seven meals rotated endlessly, a formula she'd perfected decades ago and had no interest in varying. Pot roast on Sundays. Chicken and dumplings on Thursdays. Hot dish on Mondays. The other days disappeared from memory, were not worth noting. Diana's own life had been structured around these meals, had taken their shape and their rhythm, and it was only now, in the kitchen where those meals had been assembled with such precision, such lack of joy, that Diana understood she was orphaned not just of a mother but of the entire architecture that the mother had provided, even when that architecture had seemed like a prison, had seemed like a thing to escape.

The kitchen appliances would go separately. The realtor—a man with emphatic teeth and a tendency to use the word "opportunity" more than strictly necessary—had advised against leaving them. "The buyer will want to make choices," he'd said. "Kitchen is where they make their personality happen." Diana had laughed at that. Helen had no personality in her kitchen. Helen had personality nowhere, or personality everywhere, depending on how you measured it. Helen had existed as a series of tasks and small resentments, a woman who'd made clear that she was doing you a favor simply by continuing to live, simply by remaining available to be disappointed by you. This was what Diana had wanted to escape. This was what she had escaped, at great cost, leaving Ohio for graduate school in Michigan, leaving Ohio for jobs in other places, leaving her mother's house and never quite coming back to it in the way Helen had wanted her to come back, which was completely, which was permanently, which was with gratitude.

"The lot's almost done." Margaret was in the doorway now, holding a clipboard, the same clipboard the foreman carried, though hers had more authority. Margaret was one of those capable middle-aged women who'd somehow managed to turn competence into a career, into a suit jacket and a laminated ID and a particular way of moving through other people's tragedies. "The estate sale company's sending a truck for what doesn't sell at auction. Probably thirty percent of the inventory, give or take."

"What will happen to it?" Diana asked.

"Liquidation. They'll take it somewhere—donation centers, discount chains, sometimes overseas. It depends on the item." Margaret consulted her clipboard. "Your mother had a lot of things, but most of it's pretty standard. Not much of a collection. No real antiques."

Diana nodded. She could have told Margaret that Helen believed in disposal, in the regular clearing out of things that had accumulated. Helen had grown up poor, or poorer, and had always been suspicious of accumulation, had always been ready to divest. If the wingback chairs were going for nothing, if the lamps were being rejected, if most of the silverware—Helen's silverware, the good silverware, the silverware Helen had used only for Thanksgiving—was not meeting reserve, then it was all just evidence of what Helen had always suspected: that things didn't matter, that accumulation was a kind of delusion, that everything would eventually be carried out someone's door by strangers with competent hands.

"Is there anything you want to keep?" Margaret was asking. "Sometimes families find it useful to set things aside. Things that have sentimental value."

The letter in the nightstand drawer had sentimental value. Diana's childhood bedroom, upstairs, had sentimental value, or would have, if sentiment were a thing Diana could afford. But sentiment was a luxury, and Diana had been careful, all her life, about luxuries. About needing things. About allowing things to accumulate weight. This was what Helen had taught her, even as Helen had kept things, had allowed weight to accumulate, had lived in a house full of consequences.

"No," Diana said. "I don't think so. Let it all go."

Margaret made a note on her clipboard. She was used to this, Diana thought. Used to daughters who didn't want anything, who wanted only to be free. Margaret probably saw it all the time—the great sorting of lives, the division between what mattered and what didn't, the peculiar mathematics of mourning that determined what you kept and what you released. Everyone wanted release. Everyone was grateful for the chance to be rid of their parents, their childhoods, their past.

The afternoon moved slowly. The men worked with methodical efficiency, and the house grew emptier by degrees, the way anything empties when you're not looking at it directly, when you're not keeping track. Diana found herself upstairs in her mother's bedroom, standing in the space where the bed had been. The mattress had been removed from the will—Helen had, thank God, specified its disposal—and the frame was gone now, and the nightstand. Only the dresser remained, and Helen's mirror on the wall, and the small shelf where Helen kept her bottles of cream, which were supposed to do various things for skin, for health, for the prevention of visible age. None of them had worked. Helen had looked old her whole life, or had wanted to, or had required that everyone acknowledge that she was suffering, was wearing out, was barely making it through.

Diana sat on the floor. The carpet was old, worn through in a path from the bed to the door, a pattern of Helen's routines made visible in fiber. Her mother had walked that same path thousands of times. Had woken in darkness. Had crossed to the mirror. Had assessed what had changed, what had continued its decline. Diana was aware, sitting there, of how much of her own daily life followed the same patterns her mother had established. How much of her own suffering was inherited, was passed down like genes, like a family recipe, like the expectation of disappointment. She was fifty-three years old and she still expected to be disappointed. She still moved through her own house waiting for something to go wrong. She still, even after Helen was dead, was waiting to disappoint.

The men were on the stairs now. Diana could hear them, their voices muffled by the closed door, the distance. They were coming for the dresser. For the mirror. For the last things. Diana stood. She had a moment of panic—a moment in which she wanted to ask them to stop, wanted to ask them to leave something, wanted to ask them to preserve the path through the carpet, that record of her mother's decades of small sufferings. But she didn't. She moved to the wall and unhooked the mirror from its nail. The wall beneath was darker where the mirror had hung, a shape like a life, like the outline of something that had once been there, that had once seemed permanent.

"You want this?" The foreman was in the doorway, his hands empty, his face patient.

"No," Diana said. "I'm just moving it."

She carried it downstairs. The mirror was heavier than she'd expected. It was the kind of mirror that showed you everything, showed you exactly what you looked like, showed you no mercy. She carried it carefully, watching her own face in it as she descended, watching herself become older, become less appealing, become exactly what she deserved to be. At the bottom of the stairs, she set it down gently. She walked outside.

The afternoon light was starting its long descent into evening. October light, the kind that was already mourning the year it was part of. The moving truck was parked in front of the house, and there were smaller items stacked on the driveway—lamps, end tables, the accumulated detritus that hadn't made the auction cut. The young man was smoking a cigarette. The other one was checking his list. And the foreman was inside, probably, waiting for Diana to get out of the way, waiting for the final pieces to be removed.

She stood in the driveway of the house where she'd grown up. She stood among the things that didn't matter. She thought of her mother, who had lived in that house for forty-seven years, who had woken in darkness and crossed to a mirror and assessed what was happening to her, what was being taken away by time, what she could do nothing about. She thought of the unsent letter, the name Robert, the thing Helen had been right about, the thing Diana had been wrong about, the thing Helen had kept to herself, the way she'd kept everything, had never quite told anyone what she needed, what she wanted, what might have made the difference.

The foreman emerged from the house. He was alone. Behind him, the interior of the house looked hollow, looked like the space where a life had been, like the space after loss, like any house, really, like all houses, like the place where everyone ended up if they lived long enough, which was nowhere, which was emptied, which was ready for someone else to fill with their own small sufferings, their own accumulated weight, their own eventual departure.

"That's the lot," he said. He was consulting his clipboard. "You'll want to do a final walk-through, make sure nothing's been damaged."

Diana nodded. She went back inside. The house smelled different now. It smelled of dust and the absence of things. It smelled of space. Of air. Of the particular silence that comes when everything has been removed, when there is no longer any reason for sound. She walked through the rooms. The kitchen was empty except for the appliances. The living room was empty except for the scuff marks left by furniture. The dining room was just a room, just a space, just four walls and a floor. Upstairs, her mother's bedroom was bare. The bedroom that had been Diana's was already bare, had been bare for thirty years, had held nothing of Diana since she'd left, since her mother had cleared it out, had made it into a sewing room, had used Diana's childhood as storage space for patterns and thread.

In the hallway, she found the place where the mirror had hung. The shadow of it. The outline. She could stand there, in that outline, and see herself in her mind, could see herself as she was, could see herself as she had always been in her mother's eyes, which was as someone failing, someone falling short, someone who'd never quite managed to become the person she was supposed to be.

She walked downstairs. She didn't lock the door. The realtor would do that, would put a lockbox on the handle, would wait for the buyer, would hand over the keys. The realtor would be glad to see it sold. The realtor saw these houses as opportunities, saw them as blank spaces waiting to be filled with someone else's life, someone else's history, someone else's small sufferings and accumulated weight.

Diana stood on the front porch. The moving truck was pulling away. The driveway was empty. The house was empty. And she was here, Diana Reardon, fifty-three years old, an orphan in the truest sense, a woman without a mother, without a childhood home, without the architecture that had contained her. She was free. This was what she'd wanted. This was what she'd always wanted. This was the thing Helen had never been able to give her, except in this way, by leaving, by clearing the space, by allowing the erasure that made room for something new.

She turned and looked at the house one more time. It was just a house. It was nothing. It was everything. It was the place where she'd learned that nothing mattered, that everything would be taken, that eventually you learned to want nothing, to keep nothing, to hold nothing so tight that its loss would destroy you. She had learned well. She had become a woman who owned very little, who kept very little, who maintained the kind of freedom that comes from not being attached to anything, anyone. She had become, in her own way, exactly like her mother.

She walked to her car. She got in. She drove away.
