# [Continuous Narrative]

The ferry cut through the dark water with the sound of something patient and inevitable, a knife through cloth that had been waiting a long time to be torn. He stood on the deck in the cold and watched the lights of the southern port slide away behind him, becoming gradually abstract, then remembered, then something that might not have existed at all. The wind came off the water in gusts that seemed to arrive from specific directions in his past, carrying salt and diesel and the smell of rust that he associated with his brother's hands after he had worked in the shipyard, years ago when they still spoke, when speaking was still an option they exercised.

The message had come through a woman named Soren at the hospital in Nordhafen, a place he had never been but which he now understood was situated at the precise intersection of several things he had spent a long time trying not to think about. She had been polite but efficient, the kind of efficiency that suggests a person has delivered similar news many times before and has learned to do it without the weight of emotion that would make the task harder for both parties involved. There was a body. There was a name that might have been his brother's. There were complications, she had said, which was how she phrased the fact that the face was not suitable for identification purposes, that they had found a wallet with cards that matched and mismatched in interesting ways, that they had called the number on a photograph of a woman he no longer recognized, and that woman had given them this number, which was his number, which he had not used in seven years to contact anyone who might have needed to contact him in return.

He had booked the ferry the same evening. It seemed important to move immediately, as if hesitation might allow the body to become someone else, or as if his brother might use the time to slip away again, to prove that he had never been there at all, that the whole thing was an elaborate misunderstanding, which would be just like him. Just like him to be found dead and still somehow elusive, still somehow a matter requiring interpretation. Their mother had died thinking that her younger son was working in Copenhagen, though he had left Copenhagen in 1983 and had not returned except in letters that may or may not have been posted from there, sent to addresses that changed with seasonal regularity. The police had never found him. The police, in the end, had stopped looking.

In his cabin he lay on a narrow bunk that smelled of previous occupants and listened to the specific creaks of the ship's movement, sounds that meant different things at different moments—distress, accommodation, the merely structural groaning of metal that had learned to live with the ocean. He had brought a book that he did not read, a newspaper from three days prior, a photograph of his brother from 1979 in which they were both young and standing in front of their father's house, their father just barely visible in a window behind them as if he were already a ghost, already preparing his exit, which he had made permanent in the winter of 1982.

Sleep came in segments, broken by moments of complete wakefulness in which he was aware of exactly where he was and exactly what he was traveling toward. Around three in the morning he got up and returned to the deck, where he found a woman in a long wool coat standing with her hands on the railing, looking north.

—Can't sleep either, she said without turning.

—No.

—The noise, or something else?

—Something else.

She nodded as if this confirmed something she had suspected. She was perhaps sixty, perhaps younger but weathered by something—sun, worry, the particular exhaustion of a life lived without sufficient mercy. She had silver hair that she wore in a braid down her back and her fingers were stained in a way that suggested either work or art, possibly both.

—My brother died, he said. Not died. Was found dead. Maybe. I'm going to look at a body.

She turned to face him then, and he was surprised by the absence of pity in her expression. Instead she looked at him with what seemed like recognition, as if she had seen this exact scene before, not in fact but in some deeper realm of probability where all such meetings were recorded.

—I'm going to look at a house I haven't entered in thirty-five years, she said. My husband and I lived there for seven years. Then we didn't. Then he died, five years ago, in Gothenburg, in a hospital that smelled like bad breath and floor cleaner. The house had been rented to a family, and now they're gone, and the house is empty, and apparently I have to go and decide what to do with it. Open a window, let the air in. Decide whether the thing still exists, and if it does exist, whether it's still mine.

They stood together in the cold and did not speak for a long time. The water was very dark, and there were no lights anywhere except the stars, which seemed impossibly distant, impossibly indifferent. He understood that she was giving him something, some kind of permission or acknowledgment that the task he was traveling toward was not as strange or singular as it felt.

—What will you do? he asked.

—I have no idea. Walk through it. Look at the kitchen. Look at the bedroom. Try to remember what it felt like to be the person who slept in that room. It's unlikely to feel the same, but one has to check. And you?

—Identify him. If it is him.

—And if it isn't?

—Then I suppose I go back and wait for the next body.

She smiled at this, a small smile that suggested an understanding of how such waits could extend indefinitely, how one could spend a lifetime in the position of not-yet-knowing, which was perhaps more comfortable than knowing, which was perhaps what he had been doing all along.

She left him there and he did not see her again. When he went back to his cabin it was almost dawn, and he found that he could sleep, and he slept without dreaming or with dreams so similar to waking that he could not distinguish between them.

Nordhafen appeared through the morning mist as a gray accumulation of buildings and boats, a place that seemed to have been constructed specifically for the purpose of containing small disappointments. The hospital was on a hill overlooking the harbor, and he took a taxi there directly from the ferry terminal, still carrying his small bag, still wearing the clothes he had slept in on the ship. The woman at the reception desk—not Soren, someone else, someone younger, someone who had perhaps not made this particular call often enough to develop a manner—directed him to a basement corridor that smelled of something between cleaning fluid and death, which he understood were not altogether different things.

Soren was waiting for him. She was perhaps forty-five and had the kind of face that suggested she had learned to keep her feelings in a separate room, locked away, accessible only when necessary.

—You came, she said.

—Yes.

—Your brother, or your possible brother, arrived three days ago. He was found in the strait. In the water. A fishing boat. He had been in the water for some time, and the water here in November is very cold. I want to prepare you for what you will see.

—All right.

—The face cannot be used for identification. The fish, and the cold, and the time. But we have other information. Height, build, a scar on the left collarbone. We have these things. We have his hands, which are still reasonably intact. Do you remember anything about your brother's hands?

He did. His brother's hands had always been too large for the rest of him, large and capable and perpetually stained with whatever he had been working with. He remembered his brother playing a guitar with those hands, badly, enthusiastically, the strings slipping out from under his fingers because they were too broad, too clumsy for the instrument. He remembered those hands pushing him into the snow when they were children. He remembered those hands writing a letter, the grip on the pen so tight that the pen had broken partway through the sentence, spilling ink across his fingers, across the paper, making the rest of the message illegible, which was perhaps intentional.

—I remember them, he said.

Soren led him down the corridor and through a door and into a room that was very cold and very bright and very much as he had expected it to be, which was to say it was nothing like what he had expected, was instead something far more specific and strange and intimate than any anticipation could have prepared him for. The body was on a table and was covered in a sheet except for the hands, which had been positioned with some care, as if Soren or someone like Soren had wanted to present them in the best possible light, which in the light of this room was still very bad light.

The hands were white with the particular whiteness of things long submerged. The nails were intact. There was a small scar on the left hand, between the thumb and forefinger, that he suddenly remembered with extraordinary clarity—his brother falling off a bicycle, his brother's hand coming up to stop the fall, his brother's palm meeting gravel, the blood, the crying, their father saying something that might have been comforting or might have been cruel, it was hard to remember which. He reached out and touched the hand. It was very cold and it was not his brother's hand, exactly, it was what remained of his brother's hand, which was something else entirely.

—Is it him? Soren asked.

—I don't know.

—The scar.

—I see it.

—Your brother. The one you're looking for. He would have had this scar?

—Yes. But many people have scars. Many people are found in the water.

—Yes, he said. Yes, he said. Yes.

He stood there for a longer time and then he asked to see the wallet and the cards and the other objects they had found, and Soren produced them in a plastic bag and laid them out on a second table, small artifacts from a life that may or may not have continued in linear fashion from the life he had once shared with this person. There was a driver's license with a photograph that was not quite his brother but was also not quite not his brother. There was a library card from a place he had never heard of. There were receipts for meals at restaurants with Scandinavian names, crumpled and worn. There was a photograph of a woman who was not his brother's wife, or if she was his wife then he had married someone entirely different from the woman he had married before, which was possible, which was perhaps even likely.

—The woman in the photograph, Soren said. We called her. She was very upset. She said that she knew him as Martin, and that he had been staying with her in Nordhafen for the past eight months, and that he had mentioned a brother, but she said he had said the brother did not want to see him. Did you not want to see him?

—I didn't know how to, he said.

—That is different.

—Is it?

—I think so. To know how to do something and to not want to do something, these are two separate things. I have wanted to do things I did not know how to do. And also the reverse.

She had a gift for stating the obvious in ways that made it seem mysterious, and he was grateful for this, was grateful for her presence as a kind of witness to the strangeness of all of this, the impossibility of it, the way that a person could disappear and then be found and still not be truly found, could be identified and still remain obscure, could be his brother and not his brother simultaneously.

—What will happen? he asked.

—We need your permission to bury him. Or to cremate him. Or to release him to whoever else claims him. If no one claims him, we keep him, and we bury him in the municipal cemetery, in the section for unclaimed bodies. I can take you to see where that is, if you would like.

—I would like that, he said, and he meant it.

They walked through the hospital and back out into the gray morning. Nordhafen was a town built on small hills that descended toward the water, and from certain angles you could see straight through to the strait and to the place where the body had been pulled out, where his brother had been pulled out, or where someone had been pulled out, some version of his brother made strange by time and salt and the intervention of whatever lived in the deep water waiting to be fed.

The cemetery was on the edge of town, a place of stone and wind and flowers that had been dead for a long time and had not yet been removed. The section for unclaimed bodies was smaller, quieter, set apart in a way that was not quite deliberate but was not quite accidental either, the way that such things were handled in all places, a gentle segregation of the truly alone. Soren showed him the plot that had been reserved, showed him the stone that would be placed there if no one came to claim the body, a stone with only a number on it, which seemed to him an appropriate way to remember someone, seemed to him a kind of honesty.

—I'll claim him, he said.

—You're certain?

—No. But I claim him anyway.

He signed the papers that evening, in an office that was very similar to every office he had ever been in, with a woman who was very similar to Soren, or was Soren, he was no longer quite sure. The paperwork required a name and a date of birth and a relationship, and he filled these in with the information that matched the driver's license, the information that the library card confirmed, the information that was his brother's information, or was the information of someone his brother had become, someone he had never known, and would not now know.

He stayed in a hotel near the harbor. From his window he could see the water, the specific water where his brother had been found, and he sat and looked at this water for hours, trying to understand what it meant to find someone who did not want to be found, trying to understand whether there was a difference between not wanting to be found and becoming unfindable through the simple passage of time and the relentless intervention of nature.

On his last day in Nordhafen he walked to the address of the woman whose photograph had been among the papers, whose name was Karin and who had agreed, after some reluctance, to meet him for coffee. She was younger than he expected, perhaps in her thirties, and she had the kind of sadness around her eyes that came from living with someone who was themselves very sad, who was themselves a kind of absence in the shape of a person.

—He was a good man, she said. Or I thought he was. Maybe I was wrong. He never told me much about his family. He said there was a brother, but he said the brother was better off without him. I believed this. I believed this because I was in love with him and when you are in love with someone you believe the things they tell you about themselves, even the bad things, especially the bad things, because you think that your love might be enough to counteract them, might be enough to make them into something other than what they had been. But apparently it was not enough.

—What happened to him? he asked. In the water?

—I don't know. He had been troubled. He had been drinking more. The night he disappeared he said he was going to take a walk, and I said don't go, and he went. And he didn't come back. And I waited, and I waited, and and then after some days I called the police, and the police called the hospital, and the hospital called me to say that a body had been found, and I went to see it, and I could not recognize it, and so they told me to describe him to them and I did, and they said it was probably him, but probably is not the same as certainly, is it?

—No, he said. It is not.

—So I don't know. No one knows. Your brother went into the water and he did not come out the same. Or he came out and he was not the same. It is one of these things.

He left Nordhafen the next morning on the ferry going south. He did not see the woman in the wool coat, and he wondered if she was still there, walking through her old house, or if she had returned to her life elsewhere, or if she had made some third choice that he could not anticipate. He stood on the deck again as they pulled away from the harbor, and he thought about the body in the hospital, the body that was his brother and was not his brother, the body that was now somewhere between the earth and the water, between one kind of being and another.

He had the wallet with him. He had taken it before leaving, had taken the library cards and the photograph of Karin and the receipts for meals at restaurants he would never eat at, and he sat in his cabin with these things laid out before him and he tried to understand what it meant to have found his brother by not finding him, to have received confirmation through doubt, to have been given a body instead of an answer, which was perhaps all that anyone received, was perhaps the only answer there was.
