# 

The Madrona had been rebuilt three stories lower than before, which seemed to Margaret an act of dishonesty more fundamental than the fire itself. She stood in the lobby with her overnight bag, which the bellhop—a boy who could not have been more than nineteen and had no possible way of knowing what she had done here, or what had been done to her—was already reaching for. She gave him the bag. She watched him carry it toward the elevator bank, which was not where the old elevator bank had been. Everything was arranged differently, or perhaps it had simply been rearranged so many times in her mind over the years that the actual layout seemed like a betrayal of what she remembered, which was not really the hotel at all but some other structure that had existed only in the small hours of the morning when she had been moving through it with her cleaning cart, her key ring, her silent knowledge of which rooms held which sleeping people.

The wedding was not until Saturday. It was Thursday. Margaret had arranged her schedule this way deliberately, though she could not entirely articulate why. She had told her daughter she would arrive early to help with preparations, but this was not true. The bride—her daughter's college roommate, a girl named Veronica whose laugh was too loud and whose posture was very poor—had made it clear that all preparations were being handled by a wedding coordinator from San Francisco, a woman with an office and a reputation. Margaret's help was not required. This seemed like mercy, though Margaret had experienced so little mercy in her actual life that she could not quite be sure.

She took the elevator to the fourth floor, which was not where her room had been assigned but seemed like a good place to begin understanding the new architecture. The hallway was wider than it should have been. She walked its length slowly, counting doors. Twelve doors, each painted a color called "coastal white," according to the placard she found in her room describing the hotel's palette and design philosophy. She had not asked for a placard. She had not asked for any of this careful explanation of what had been destroyed and what had been retained and what had been improved. The Madrona's website, which Margaret's daughter had sent her a link to during the months of planning, used the word "reimagined" at least seven times in the first paragraph. Margaret did not use the word reimagined. She used the word rebuilt, or sometimes, in the privacy of her own mind, the word restored, though restored was not quite accurate either.

In her room she unpacked her dress—navy blue, sleeveless, appropriate for a woman of her age and station—and hung it carefully in the closet, which smelled of fresh paint and something floral that was meant to be pleasant but instead made her think of hospitals. She sat on the bed, which was extremely firm, and looked out the window at the bay. It was the same bay. The bay had not changed. It occurred to her that this should have been comforting, but it was not. She had wanted the bay to change. She had wanted something to acknowledge what had happened here, and what she had done, and what she had not been able to prevent, and the bay's indifference to all of these things seemed like a kind of cruelty.

At dinner she went downstairs and ate a salad in the restaurant, which was called something different now—not the Madrona Grill, where she had eaten her meals thirty-eight years ago, but the Bayview Room, though it had no view of anything. A woman at the next table was speaking in a loud voice about the wedding. Margaret listened. The woman was talking about the bride's mother, who had had some kind of falling out with the groom's mother years ago, something about a piece of jewelry or a slight at a party, and now the two women were being seated very far apart, and the centerpieces had been designed to prevent eye contact. Margaret finished her salad. She ordered coffee, which came too hot and in the wrong kind of cup—a shallow cup with a handle too small for her hand. She drank it anyway.

She did not sleep well. She lay in the bed—the extremely firm bed—and listened to the sounds of the hotel. There were fewer sounds than there had been. The old Madrona had been full of noises at night, the sound of plumbing, of footsteps on the floor above, of ice machines and elevator cables and people arguing in muffled voices behind closed doors. This new Madrona was nearly silent. She lay in the silence and thought about what she had been doing on the night of the fire, which had been a Tuesday night, though that detail mattered less than it seemed like it should.

She had been on the sixth floor. She had been moving through rooms with her cart and her key, letting herself in, turning on the lights, seeing what needed to be done. Most of the rooms were empty. It was March, and the hotel was not full. This was true then and it was probably true now, though the website did not say so. The website said the hotel had been "carefully curated to serve the modern traveler," which Margaret understood to mean that the hotel no longer tried to fill its rooms, but instead waited for the sort of traveler who wanted to stay at a hotel that was famous for having had a fire, or famous for having recovered from a fire, or famous for some other reason she did not quite understand.

In room 614 she had found a woman awake, which was unusual. Most of the guests were asleep. Most of the guests never saw her at all. She had a key. She let herself in. The woman was sitting by the window in a silk robe. The woman was perhaps sixty years old. The woman was smoking a cigarette without using an ashtray.

"Oh," the woman had said. "It's quite all right. You can come in. I'm just having a quiet moment."

Margaret had said she could come back. The woman had said no, no, please stay, it was lonely to have a quiet moment alone. So Margaret had stayed. She had stood in the doorway with her cart behind her and her key ring heavy in her pocket. The woman had told her about her daughter, who had gotten engaged to a man the woman did not approve of, a man from back east, a man who reminded her of her own husband, who had been dead for seven years. The woman had cried, very gently, not in a way that required any response. Margaret had said nothing. After a long time the woman had told her she could go, and Margaret had gone.

On Thursday afternoon, while the wedding guests were beginning to arrive, Margaret took a walk along the bay. The path was wider than it had been. Someone had put benches at regular intervals, benches that all faced the water, as if to suggest to people that they should sit and look at the bay. She did not sit. She walked the length of the path and then walked back. She thought about how little she had actually left the hotel, in the years when she had worked here. She had rented a small apartment in a building that no longer existed, and she had commuted to the hotel, and she had worked, and she had gone home. She had not been to the bay. She had cleaned a room on the sixth floor where a woman was sitting by the window, and she had stood in the doorway, and she had said nothing, and nothing had happened, and everything had happened.

She returned to the hotel to find her daughter in the lobby, in a state. The wedding was tomorrow. The caterer had called to say that one of the tables had been measured incorrectly, and there would not be enough space in the ballroom for all the tables and the dance floor. Could the ballroom be extended into the anteroom? No, the anteroom was being used for cocktails. Could the ballroom be extended into the hallway? No, fire code. Could the centerpieces be made smaller? They were already quite small. Could the guest list be reduced? No, the invitations had been sent. Could the dance floor be made smaller? The groom wanted to surprise the bride with a special dance, a choreographed number, and needed a certain amount of space.

"What are you going to do?" Margaret asked.

"I don't know," her daughter said. Her daughter's name was Catherine. Catherine had been born in this city, though not in this hotel, and had grown up not knowing that her mother had once cleaned rooms here, or that something had happened here, something that Margaret had never quite explained and that Catherine had never quite asked about. "Mom," Catherine said. "I don't know what to do."

"You'll figure it out," Margaret said.

"How do you know that?"

"Because it doesn't matter," Margaret said. "Nothing matters as much as you think it does."

Catherine looked at her mother for a long moment. "That's not very comforting," Catherine said finally.

"No," Margaret said. "It's not."

That night, at the rehearsal dinner, Margaret sat at a table with people she did not know. They were friends of the groom's parents, people from Sacramento, people who had opinions about the state of the city and the price of real estate and the unfortunate direction of local politics. One of the men had been a banker. One of the women had been a real estate developer. One of the women had been the wife of a state senator. They asked Margaret what she did, and she told them she was retired, which was true, though it was not the whole truth. She had been a chambermaid, and then she had been a middle school teacher, and then she had been retired. In between these identities, there had been years, and in those years she had not thought about the hotel very much, or the fire, or the woman in room 614.

"And what did you do before you retired?" the banker asked.

"I taught middle school," Margaret said.

"What subject?"

"English. Reading. Writing. I taught them to read, and then I taught them to write about what they had read."

"That's very important work," the state senator's wife said. "So much depends on good teachers."

Margaret did not correct her. She did not say that it had not been important work, or at least not important enough to matter, or important in a way that didn't add up to much in the end. She did not say that she had taught the same grammar lessons to the same kind of students for thirty years, and that nothing had come of it except time passing and paychecks deposited and a pension that was modest but adequate. She did not say these things because she had learned, somewhere along the way, that people did not want to hear them.

After dinner Catherine took her aside. "I solved the centerpiece problem," Catherine said. "We're going to extend the tables into the cocktail area. The caterer said they can serve drinks from a standing bar instead."

"That's good," Margaret said.

"But now people are angry because they wanted to be able to stand and circulate during cocktails."

"People are often angry," Margaret said.

"Is that supposed to help?"

"No," Margaret said. "Nothing helps. But this will pass. By Saturday night it will be over, and by next week no one will remember the centerpieces, or the dance floor, or the problem with the cocktail area."

"They'll remember the fire," Catherine said suddenly.

Margaret was very still. "What do you mean?"

"The wedding planner, the one from San Francisco, she kept saying that the hotel had 'survived' the fire, like that was a selling point. Like people wanted to get married in a place that had suffered. I found it disturbing."

"It was a long time ago," Margaret said.

"It was 1986," Catherine said. "That's not very long ago."

"It was longer ago than you've been alive," Margaret said. This was not quite true—Catherine had been born in 1987—but it was close enough. "And it was not a significant fire. No one died. The damage was repaired."

"You were here," Catherine said. It was not a question.

"Yes," Margaret said. "I was."

"You never told me what happened. Not really."

Margaret looked at her daughter. Catherine was thirty-seven years old. Catherine had a good job in marketing. Catherine was getting married to a man named Richard, who was nice enough, and who Margaret did not particularly dislike, which seemed like enough. Catherine did not need to know what had happened on the sixth floor of the Madrona Hotel in March of 1986. Catherine needed to get married and forget about this hotel and think about other things, better things.

"What happened," Margaret said, "is that I was working. There was a fire. The fire was extinguished. People were evacuated. No one was hurt. The hotel was closed for two years, and then it reopened, and then many years passed, and now we're here for your wedding."

"That's not the whole story," Catherine said.

"No," Margaret said. "It's not. But it's the part of the story that you need."

That night Margaret did not sleep at all. She sat in her chair and looked out the window at the bay, which seemed even blacker than before, which seemed to contain nothing at all, which seemed to be waiting for something. She thought about the woman in room 614. She thought about how long the woman had lived after the night of the fire, whether the woman had ever had a quiet moment alone that was truly quiet, whether the woman had eventually married off her daughter to someone more acceptable, whether the woman had eventually made peace with the fact that you could not prevent people from making bad decisions, you could only prevent yourself from making bad decisions, and even that was often impossible.

She thought about the fire itself. She had caused it—not set it, not intentionally, but caused it nonetheless, the way a person might cause rain by being standing in exactly the right place at exactly the wrong time. She had been smoking a cigarette in room 614. She had placed it on the nightstand. She had left the room, forgetting it. She had been on the third floor before she realized, and she had thought about going back, but the woman in room 614 had seemed to want to be alone, and so Margaret had continued with her work. By the time she remembered, someone had already called the fire department. The fire had been small. The fire had been contained. The fire had not killed anyone.

But it had closed the hotel. And it had prompted an investigation. And it had required that everyone who had been working that night give statements. And it had changed Margaret's life, though no one had ever known that it had, because she had not told anyone what she had actually done.

She had told the investigator that she had been in room 614, yes, but only for a moment, and she had not left anything burning, and she did not know how the fire had started, and she certainly had not been smoking—she did not smoke, she told him, though this was a lie. The investigator had seemed tired. The investigator had seemed to believe her. The investigator had moved on.

She had kept her job for another year, though after that she had left, and she had gone back to school, and she had become a teacher, and she had spent thirty years teaching other people's children to read and write, and she had never said anything about the night of the fire, not to anyone, not even to her husband, who had died in 1998, not even to Catherine, who was her daughter, and who now wanted to know what had happened.

At six in the morning Margaret went downstairs and found the manager, a woman named Sandra, in the back office. "I was wondering," Margaret said, "if you might show me the room where the fire started. I was working here when it happened."

Sandra looked at her with an expression that Margaret could not read. "Of course," Sandra said. "Come with me."

They took the elevator to the fourth floor—not the sixth floor, because the sixth floor had been partially destroyed and had been rebuilt slightly differently. They walked down the hallway, past the coastal white doors, to room 412.

"It would have been 614," Margaret said. "Before."

"Yes," Sandra said. "The entire sixth floor was renovated. The rooms are slightly smaller now. The layout is different."

Sandra opened the room. It was an ordinary hotel room. It was very clean. There was nothing in it to suggest that anything had ever happened here, though of course the thing that had happened here had happened thirty-eight years ago, and even if the room had remained exactly as it had been, there would have been nothing visible to suggest it.

"Thank you," Margaret said.

"Of course," Sandra said. "Is there anything else I can help you with?"

"No," Margaret said. "This was all I needed."

She returned to her room and took a shower and got dressed. She had the whole day to wait before the wedding. She did not leave the hotel. She sat in the bar and drank coffee and watched the staff move through the space, setting up for the reception, arranging flowers, polishing glassware. They had rearranged the ballroom so that it extended slightly into the cocktail area, and the centerpieces had been made smaller, and the dance floor was adequate for a choreographed number. Nothing had been left unsolved. Everything had been made to work.

At five o'clock Catherine came to get her. "How do I look?" Catherine asked. Catherine was wearing her wedding dress, which was white and very simple, and which made her look like a girl, though she was not a girl, and would not be a girl again.

"You look beautiful," Margaret said.

"Are you proud of me?"

This was a question Margaret had not been asked in a very long time. She thought about it. She thought about the fire, and the woman in room 614, and the thirty years of teaching, and the life she had lived quietly, without telling anyone what she knew, without explaining what had happened.

"Yes," she said finally. "I'm proud of you."

Catherine hugged her. Margaret stood very still and allowed herself to be hugged, though it cost her something to do so, though it required her to feel something that she had spent a long time learning not to feel.

The wedding was beautiful. The vows were traditional. The first dance was choreographed, and Richard did not drop Catherine, and no one seemed to notice that it was perhaps a bit longer than necessary, or that Margaret was watching the whole thing from a table in the back of the room, not drinking, not eating, just watching.

The fire had not mattered. This seemed to be the thing that Margaret understood, sitting there, watching her daughter dance in the rebuilt hotel, under the rebuilt roof, in the reimagined space. The fire had happened, and then it had not happened, because people had moved on, and because time had passed, and because no one had died, and because the hotel had been rebuilt, and because everyone involved had learned to carry the weight of it privately, without telling anyone, without explaining what had occurred.

At midnight she left the reception and went upstairs. She packed her bag. Tomorrow she would check out, and she would go home, and she would return to her life, which was a small life, and a quiet life, but it was her life, and she had made it through the fire, and she had made it through the years after the fire, and she had made it to this moment, when her daughter was happy, or appeared to be happy, which was perhaps the same thing.

She stood at the window one more time and looked out at the bay. The bay was black and still. In the morning it would be gray and ordinary again. But for now it seemed to hold all the secrets of all the people who had ever stood in a hotel and looked out at water and kept something to themselves, which was everyone, which was all of us, which was the way the world worked, if you could call it working, if you could call any of this anything at all.
