# 

The ferry departs at midnight from a port whose name he has already forgotten, though he wrote it down somewhere in the documents that sit folded in his coat pocket like evidence of a crime. Water and December. The engines beneath him speak in a language he once understood as a child, when his father took them both across similar channels, similar darknesses. His brother had vomited then, clutching the rail with small hands while their father laughed—not unkindly, but with the particular cruelty of men who do not understand fear because they have never truly experienced it.

He stands now on the deck in weather that wants to be snow but settles for rain, each droplet carrying the mineral taste of something industrial, something distant and burning. The strait is three hours in either direction. The body—if it is a body—has been waiting in a building lit by fluorescents, under sheets that smell of chemicals meant to preserve what has already begun the business of dissolution. He has not seen his brother in eleven years. They did not quarrel. They simply stopped the conversation midway through, as if one of them had stood up from a table without explanation and walked out, and the other had been too proud or too tired to call him back.

The handful of other passengers seem to move through the ship like ghosts of themselves, uncertain about their own substance. An old woman with a suitcase decorated with stickers from ports that probably no longer exist as she remembers them. A man in a suit whose face suggests he has been travelling for so long that arrival has become an abstraction. A younger couple who do not touch but move in concert, synchronized in their alienation from one another. No one speaks. The darkness outside is complete in a way that darkness has become rare, that absolute absence that belongs now mainly to oceans and the gaps between stars.

He purchases coffee from a machine and finds a seat in the lounge where the heating hums with the monotony of something that has been running so long it has become a kind of silence. The coffee is simultaneously too hot and tasteless, as if the temperature has annihilated any possibility of flavor. He drinks it anyway, the way a prisoner might eat, sustaining himself from habit rather than appetite. His hands shake slightly—from the heat of the cup or from something else, he cannot determine. Cold or fear or the particular vertigo that comes from being suspended between two places, belonging fully to neither.

The voice on the phone had been official and distant, a woman's voice trained to deliver difficult information without inflection. A body recovered. Preliminary identification impossible due to the condition of remains. Found in a vehicle. A vehicle registered to someone with his brother's name. But names are common things. Names repeat themselves across continents like echoes that have learned to walk upright.

He closes his eyes and sees the strait as it was when he was young—the water silver under moonlight, his brother's face pale with nausea, their father's voice carrying across the deck in fragments about weather patterns and the foolishness of boy-bodies. His father is dead now, has been dead for six years, and the information arrived via email from a cousin whose name he could barely recall. By the time he received it, the funeral had already passed. He had not mourned, exactly. It was more that he had felt the simple acknowledgment of an absence that had long since become normal.

Time moves differently on the water. Hours dissolve into the vibration of the engines, the minimal shift of the ship through the channel. He opens his eyes and finds that less than half an hour has passed. Beside him, the old woman has fallen asleep, her head resting against the window with the precise angle of someone whose body has learned to sleep in impossible positions. He envies her this capacity—to surrender so completely to the present moment, to trust that the ship will continue its trajectory without her vigilance.

There is a memory he has carried for so long that he no longer knows if it belongs to him or if he has simply inherited it from the story his brother once told him. They are young, perhaps eight and ten. Winter. The lake near their house has frozen solid, and their father stands at the edge, testing the ice with his weight. The ice holds. He gestures for them to come, and they walk out, the surface beneath them creaking with a sound like the ship's engines now, that same metallic complaint. Halfway across, his brother stops. Something in the ice frightens him—a crack spreading like a vein, or perhaps nothing visible at all, simply the fear that lives in the body before the mind can organize it into thought. Their father keeps walking. He follows their father. He does not remember what happened next, which is to say he remembers it differently each time he considers it. Either his brother turned back and ran, or he stood motionless until their father returned and carried him. Either the ice held or it cracked beneath them and they fell, and the falling was so quick and so complete that drowning felt like waking.

The woman at the identification desk had been kind in that professional way, offering him water before he entered the room, warning him in a voice stripped of all personality that sometimes the visual recognition was less reliable than other forms of identification. Dental records. A tattoo, if one existed. A particular scar in a particular location. She had asked if he knew of any distinguishing marks, and he had realized in that moment how little he actually knew his own brother. They had shared a childhood, a house, a father—and yet the specific topography of his brother's body, the small asymmetries and marks that make each body unique, had never been something he had committed to memory. They are not lovers, after all. They are not intimates. They are simply two people who exited the same woman at different moments in time.

The ship's whistle sounds, and he starts in his seat. The old woman does not wake. Outside, he can see the lights of the far shore emerging from the rain, sodium lamps creating an amber haze in the darkness. They are approaching. The crossing that seemed to extend infinitely is ending. Soon he will be required to move, to disembark, to walk into a car that will take him to a building, to a room where someone will draw back a sheet and ask him a question he will have to answer in either words or silence, and both will constitute a form of identification, a declaration about kinship and absence and the stories we tell ourselves about people we have lost long before they die.

He stands and moves to the window. The rain has intensified, and his own reflection stares back at him from the glass—a man whose face he recognizes but does not quite know, older than he expects, marked by something that might be fatigue or might be the accumulated weight of years spent running from or running toward his own history. Behind his reflection, the shore lights multiply and blur, possibilities without resolution.

His father had a way of touching the back of his neck—his sons' necks—with one hand while looking away, as if the gesture required a kind of deliberate inattention to prevent it from becoming something else entirely. His brother, in their last conversation, had mentioned this. They were both adults then. His brother had said: Do you remember? And he had said: Yes. And then his brother had said something else, something important, but he had not been listening fully, had been thinking instead about whether to order another drink or to pay the bill and leave. The moment had passed. They had not attempted to recover it.

The ship shudders as it begins to slow, the engines shifting into a different register, a different language. The old woman's eyes open, and she looks at him without recognition, as if she has just surfaced from a dream in which he did not exist. She gathers her suitcase with its archipelago of stickers, and he moves aside to let her pass.

In the corridor, voices multiply. People who had been invisible now manifest themselves, suddenly present, suddenly real. A family with children who seem bewildered by the lateness of the hour. A man shouting into a phone about connecting flights. Everyone moving toward the same exit, the same shore, drawn by the magnetic force of arrival.

He does not move immediately. He remains at the window, watching the ship dock with its elaborate dance of ropes and pulleys, the crew moving with the practiced efficiency of people who have performed this choreography ten thousand times. When he finally walks toward the exit, he does so slowly, as if each step takes significant effort, as if his body is moving through some medium denser than air.

On the dock, the rain continues, and the wind carries the smell of diesel fuel and brine and something else—salt mixed with the iron smell of things being broken down and reassembled. A taxi waits, its driver already knowing who he is somehow, or perhaps knowing him only as a destination, a problem to be transported. He provides the address from memory, though he had never seen this building before, had only heard its location described in that official voice, matter-of-fact about the matter of death.

The city around him is both familiar and utterly strange—the streets arranged according to logic he does not quite grasp, the buildings speaking a language he has not learned. His brother lived here, apparently. Had lived here. Had driven a vehicle through these streets, had parked it somewhere, had done something that resulted in a body being recovered and stored and waiting for someone to come and answer a question about kinship.

The taxi driver does not speak except to confirm the address, and for this he is grateful. He watches the city slip past—closed shops, empty streets, the occasional figure moving with the hurried determination of someone who has somewhere urgent to be. Who are these people? What are they running from or running toward? At two in the morning, everyone is either returning home from something or escaping toward something else. There is no neutral point in this equation.

The building, when they arrive, is smaller than he expected. Modern but already aging, with the quality of structures built with the assumption that they will not need to last. The windows are dark. Inside, one light burns behind frosted glass. The driver says: Here, and makes no move to help, understanding perhaps that this is a journey one takes alone, one enters into without company, even the company of strangers.

He pays and exits into the rain, which has become something like mist now, less water than atmosphere. The door opens before he can knock. The woman from the phone, perhaps, or someone like her. She does not smile but nods, a gesture of acknowledgment without warmth. Follow me, she says, and he does, walking through corridors that smell of disinfectant and something underneath it, something sweet and wrong. His heart moves in his chest with the irregular rhythm of something trying to escape a closed space.

The room is cold. This is the first thing he notices. The cold is precise and controlled, part of the preservation. A table. A sheet. A shape beneath the sheet that might be anything—a body, an absence, a question posed in flesh and bone. The woman stands at the foot of the table, her hand hovering near the corner of the sheet. She has the courtesy not to rush this moment, understanding perhaps that there are speeds at which revelation can move, and that this is one of them.

When she pulls the sheet back, he sees a face that could be anyone. The damage to it is significant. The features are slack, emptied of the animation that makes a face a face, that transforms bone and skin into expression and identity. This could be his brother. This could be someone else entirely. He has no way of knowing. His brother exists in his memory as a young man, perpetually young, perpetually alive in 1998, suspended in that final moment of their last conversation, whatever he was saying cut off by his own inattention, by the fundamental selfishness that makes all family members strangers to one another.

Is this, the woman says, her voice patient, like she is asking him what color the walls are, whether it rained that day. Is this your brother?

He looks at the hands, the wrists, searches for the tattoo or the scar she mentioned. The left wrist bears a mark—a small, crude symbol that he does not recognize, that could mean anything or nothing. The right wrist is unmarked. He has no memory of his brother having a tattoo, but then again, eleven years is sufficient time for a person to mark their body in ways their family will never know about. Eleven years is sufficient time for becoming someone else entirely.

Yes, he says. Or: No. Or: Perhaps. The words do not leave his mouth. Instead, he nods, a gesture that requires no commitment, that can be read as confirmation or acknowledgment or simply the physical expression of having heard the question. The woman accepts this as sufficient. She moves to pull the sheet back up, and in that moment, as the sheet rises to cover the face again, he realizes he has not actually determined anything. The body—if this is the body of his brother—returns to the status of a question unanswered.

There is paperwork. Of course there is paperwork. Forms that ask him questions he cannot truthfully answer because truth has become a slippery concept, a thing that escapes the moment he reaches for it. He signs what they ask him to sign. His signature looks foreign to him, like something written by another person, someone claiming his name as an alias.

Outside, the rain has stopped, and the darkness is total, absolute, the darkness of a place that exists outside the reach of cities. He stands in the parking lot and breathes. His lungs work. His heart continues its inefficient beat. The body, this body that contains him, persists in its functions, indifferent to whether he has identified his brother or simply identified someone else, someone waiting in another room for another family member to come and nod or shake their head or stand in silence so complete it registers as an answer.

He begins to walk, though he has no destination, though the city around him is dark and empty and speaks no language he understands. Behind him, the building continues its work, its fluorescent lights burning through the night for the next person who will arrive seeking certainty and leave with only the ash-taste of questions.

His brother had said something. In that last conversation, before he stopped listening, before he signaled the waiter for the bill. His brother had said: I don't think I can do this anymore. He had thought it was about the conversation, about the restaurant, about the awkwardness of being two people who shared blood but nothing else. He had thought his brother was simply tired of the performance of kinship. But perhaps his brother meant something larger. Perhaps he meant I cannot continue existing. Perhaps he meant I cannot bear the weight of being a person in a world that makes no provisions for people like me.

The rain has truly stopped now. The street he is walking down is lined with buildings whose windows contain no light. He thinks of his father, who is dead. He thinks of his brother, who may or may not be dead, who exists now in an indeterminate space between living and deceased, between possibility and fact. He thinks of himself, walking through a city at three in the morning, looking for something he cannot name, something that has been absent so long that its absence has taken on the quality of presence.

At some point, the night will end. The sun will rise. He will be required to make decisions about what to do with the body, whether to claim it or leave it, whether to return home or remain in this city that smells of salt and diesel and the particular loneliness of places built without mercy. He will do whatever seems most expedient, whatever requires the least emotional commitment. And this will be the only truthful thing he accomplishes—this act of minimal engagement, this refusal to fully arrive at any conclusion.

But that is later. For now, he walks through the darkness, and the darkness walks with him, and neither of them seems willing to let the other go.
