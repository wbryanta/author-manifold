# [Estate Sale]

The men came at eight in the morning with their dolly and their canvas straps and their eyes that did not see. They saw furniture. Tables and chairs and a bedroom set that had witnessed everything she had done in darkness, everything she had refused to do in light. But they did not see. They came with their efficiency and their clip-boards and their way of moving through a space as though emptying it was no different than filling it, and maybe they were right. Maybe there was no difference at all if you did not care about what had occupied the space before.

She had come early, before they arrived, and sat in the kitchen where her mother had spent the better part of fifty years. Not cooking—though there had been cooking, Lord knew there had been cooking, biscuits and greens and the kind of chicken that made a man forgive you almost anything. But sitting. Waiting. Her hands folded or moving through the small tasks that women her mother's age seemed to understand as the whole point of living. Straightening a dish towel. Wiping a counter that was already clean. Looking out the window at the street that had never changed, not really, not in any way that mattered.

The refrigerator hummed its old song. It had hummed that song for thirty-seven years. She knew because she had been here when they brought it, when her father (who had opinions about appliances the way other men had opinions about baseball) had supervised its placement with the seriousness of a man installing a monument. It was harvest gold. Avocado green had come and gone, and stainless steel had arrived like the future itself, but her mother had kept the harvest gold refrigerator, had kept it clean, had kept it running. That refrigerator knew things. It held the containers in which her mother's life had been preserved—the casseroles that had fed the church, the potato salad that had appeared at every family gathering, the leftover pork chops that her mother could make into something new, something that did not taste like leftovers at all.

Now it would go. The men had it on their list. Lot 47. Harvest gold refrigerator, good working condition. Sold to a woman in Columbus who probably wanted it for irony. For a vintage kitchen. For the nostalgia of something she had never actually experienced.

She had not cried yet. She was waiting to do that, the same way her mother had waited for everything—for the right moment, the right circumstances, the right audience or, more likely, no audience at all. Her mother had wept only in bathrooms with the water running. This was something she had learned as a child, pressing her ear to the door, listening to her mother's shoulders shaking beneath the sound of the faucet. Even then she had understood: some things were not meant to be witnessed. Some griefs were private property.

But this grief was not hers alone anymore. She had called the estate sale company three months after the funeral, when the sympathy had curdled into something else—obligation, maybe, or the simple fact that the house would not empty itself and she had a job in Cincinnati and a daughter in Atlanta and no real claim to stay. The house had to be dealt with. That was how it had been phrased in the meeting with the realtor, a young man whose name she had forgotten before he finished saying it. "We'll need to deal with the house." As though it were a problem. As though her entire childhood, pressed between these walls like flowers in a book, were an inconvenience.

The men started with the dining room. She heard them, even though she stayed in the kitchen. The scraping of chair legs across the hardwood. The muffled impacts as they wrapped the table in moving blankets like it was a body being prepared for something. One of them swore, quietly, and the other laughed. She could hear the companionship in their laughter, the easy way they moved through the world together, accumulating and removing, never stopping long enough to be changed by any of it.

She had brought her daughter pictures to choose from, but her daughter had said, "Mom, I don't have room. You know I don't have room." Her daughter lived in an apartment that was barely large enough for her own life, let alone the artifacts of someone else's. This was what happened when you raised a child to look forward instead of backward. This was the success you could be proud of: a daughter who had no use for the past.

There was a coffee table that she wanted. It was walnut, or something meant to look like walnut, with a glass top and underneath, displayed like specimens in a museum, were magazines from the 1970s and a few scattered paperback novels with their spines cracked from repeated reading. Her mother had read those books. She had read them while her daughter did homework at this very table, while her husband was at work, while the afternoon light made geometric shapes on the floor and seemed to stop time for a few hours. Those books had been her mother's door out. She had seen her mother disappear into them the way other women disappeared into sleep or religion or the small madnesses that kept them functioning.

The estate sale appraiser had looked at the coffee table and written something on her form. "Glass top," she had said, not looking at it really, just cataloging. Lot 23.

She went to the appraiser and asked how much.

"Hard to say. Depends on the market. Could go for fifty. Could go for a hundred and fifty."

"I want to buy it."

The appraiser looked at her, and for a moment something human passed between them. For a moment the appraiser understood that this was not just stuff, that this was a woman trying to purchase back some piece of her own existence.

"You have to bid like everybody else," the appraiser said, not unkindly. "Fair market. That's how it works."

So she would bid. She would stand in this living room tomorrow afternoon with the rest of the vultures and the treasure hunters and the people looking for bargains, and she would try to win back her own past. She might lose. A young couple from the neighborhood might outbid her, wanting it for their farmhouse renovation. A dealer might claim it for inventory. The universe might decide that the coffee table belonged to someone else now. That was how the world worked. That was how things passed from hand to hand until nobody remembered who had held them first.

In the hallway, she heard the men positioning themselves for the bedroom set. Her parents' bedroom set. The bed where she had been conceived, almost certainly, though this was not something a person thought about when they were arranging the estate sale of their mother's house. The bed where her father had died, in the middle of the night, quietly, the way he had lived. Her mother had been asleep beside him. She had woken to find him cold. She had not said much about it, but her daughter had learned later, from an aunt, that her mother had simply turned over and gone back to sleep. Had not called for help. Had not panicked. Had simply accepted that this was the way things were now.

There was a sound like thunder, and then swearing, more emphatic this time.

"Watch the door frame!" one of the men shouted.

She went into the hallway. The bed frame had caught the molding and scraped white wood from the painted surface. A wound in the house. The men were trying to angle it differently, trying to make the bed fit through a doorway it had fit through plenty of times before, but they were doing it differently, with less care perhaps, or simply with the kind of brutal efficiency that was the only tool they possessed. The bed would fit. The house would be emptied. And her parents' sleep, all those nights of it, all those mornings of waking beside each other or waking beside a corpse, would be translated into the neutral language of the market. Good condition. Some wear. As-is.

She went back to the kitchen and poured herself coffee from the pot she had made before dawn. It was cold now. She drank it anyway. It tasted like her mother's kitchen, which was to say it tasted like waiting.

By noon, the living room was gone. The couch where she had watched television as a child, pressed against her mother's side, her mother's arm around her or her mother's hand in her hair. The side table where her mother had set down her coffee cup, always leaving a ring, always forgetting a coaster. The lamp with the pleated shade that her father had given her mother for Christmas in 1982. All of it gone. All of it reduced to a set of numbers and descriptions that made it impossible to remember that any of it had mattered.

The men took a lunch break at noon, sitting in their truck in the driveway, eating sandwiches from a cooler. She could see them through the window. They had removed their shirts. It was hot. Even this clearing of a dead woman's possessions could not stop them from being hungry, from needing rest, from moving through the world as though it would continue to hold them. She envied them their simplicity.

She had thought about going through her mother's drawers, finding letters or journals or some explanation for why her mother had been the way she was—distant, careful, cautious with her love as though it might run out if she spent it too freely. But she had not done it. Some doors, once opened, could not be closed. Some mysteries were better preserved as mysteries. What did it matter now, anyway? Her mother was dead. Whatever reasons she had had were dead with her. The world did not require explanation. The world required only that you accept its indifference and move through it with whatever grace you could manage.

At two o'clock, the men came back to the bedrooms. She stayed downstairs, listening to the violence of it—the bed being disassembled, the dresser being emptied of the last of her mother's things, the nightstands (hers and her father's, placed on opposite sides of the bed, as though they were not quite comfortable sharing the same sleep) being carried out to the truck. Her mother had kept nothing personal in these drawers. No diaries. No photographs. Just the practical things—a spare set of reading glasses, hand lotion, a paperback that had fallen behind the dresser and been forgotten.

Her mother had lived such a careful life. Such a small life. She had done what was expected. She had been a good wife, a good mother, a good woman in all the ways that mattered to other people. And for what? For a house that would be emptied, a bank account that would be divided, a name that would be forgotten in two generations? She had not asked for this. Her mother had not chosen to die. But the universe did not care about consent. The universe simply went on, taking what it wanted, leaving the rest of us to manage the aftermath.

By four o'clock, the house was nearly empty. What remained were the things that had been appraised at such a low value that no one expected them to sell. A broken lamp. A set of curtain rods. Her mother's sewing basket, filled with thread and thimbles and needles of various sizes. The appraiser had not even bothered to list these items. They would go to the Salvation Army or be thrown away. Her mother's final project, unfinished, would be scattered or destroyed.

She found herself standing in her childhood bedroom, which was also empty now except for the closet, which they would deal with tomorrow. The walls were bare except for the ghosts of posters and pennants and the thumbtacks that had held them. The window seat where she had sat and read books—the same books her mother had been reading downstairs, the same books that allowed a woman to imagine a life other than the one she had been given—was just a nook now. Just wood and light. She could sit there, and no one would stop her. She could sit there forever, and the men would work around her, or they would tell her she had to leave, and either way she would have to go.

The appraiser came to find her at four-thirty. "We're done for today," she said. "The men will pick up the rest of the furniture that didn't sell tomorrow. You'll want to come back before the sale to do a final walk-through, make sure we didn't miss anything."

She nodded.

"It's a good house," the appraiser said. "Good bones. It'll sell quick."

"Yes."

"Your mother took care of it."

"Yes."

She wanted to say something more, something that would communicate what this house had meant, what her mother had meant, what it all meant. But there were no words for it, or if there were, they were not words that could be spoken to a stranger in the hallway while the afternoon light was fading and the work was over. Some things could only be felt, and only alone.

After the appraiser left, she walked through the empty rooms. Her footsteps echoed. The house sounded different now, as though the furniture had been absorbing sound all these years and now that it was gone, her every movement was exposed, audible, undeniable. She was alive in this space. She was the only living thing left in it. Soon the buyers would come and fill it with their own lives, their own furniture, their own silence. They would scrape the walls and paint them colors her mother never would have chosen. They would install new fixtures and tear out the kitchen her mother had kept so carefully. They would make it new in the way that people did when they did not understand that nothing was ever truly new, that all the past lived just underneath the surface, waiting to be remembered or ignored.

She locked the door and got in her car. Tomorrow she would come back. Tomorrow she would bid on the coffee table and lose it or win it, and either way she would have done what needed to be done. Tonight she would stay at the hotel on the edge of town, the same hotel where she had stayed during the funeral. She would eat dinner alone and call her daughter and hear about her daughter's life, which had nothing to do with this house or this town or the woman who was being sold off, piece by piece, into the world's indifference.

But right now, driving through the familiar streets, past the houses where her friends had lived and the school she had attended and the church where she had been baptized, she let herself feel it. Let herself understand that this was the end of something, even if she could not say what. Even if she could not explain it to anyone else. Even if it would not matter, in the long run, whether the coffee table sold for fifty dollars or a hundred and fifty.

She was a woman who had grown up in a house. And now that house was being unmade. And she was driving away from it in the dark, toward whatever came next.
