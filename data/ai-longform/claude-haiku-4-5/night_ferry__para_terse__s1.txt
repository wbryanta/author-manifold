# The Crossing

The overnight ferry to Shetland left at half past nine, which gave him two hours to reach the terminal in Lerwick, a journey that seemed deliberately designed to test a man's commitment to the uncertain. He drove north through the Scottish Highlands with the radio off and the windows cracked against the November cold, letting the wind carry away whatever steadiness he'd assembled during the drive from Edinburgh. The call had come three days ago—a body found in a stone cottage on the western edge of Mainland, unclaimed and unidentified, matching in broad strokes the description of a man who'd vanished twelve years prior from this same island.

The sergeant had been careful with his words, professional in the way of people who deliver bad news regularly enough to have developed a technique. Male, approximately sixty, found by a walker on private land. Some time had passed. Dental records would help, they'd said, if he could provide dental records, but first they needed confirmation of identity, and did he have family who might come? His mother had been dead five years. His father before that. It was only him, the younger brother, the one who'd stayed in the south while the other brother had gone north and disappeared into the kind of silence that eventually becomes permanent.

Lerwick materialized through the early evening darkness like a place that had slowly accepted its own obscurity. He found a parking space near the terminal and checked in, watching the other passengers with the vague paranoia of someone undertaking something he'd spent twelve years avoiding. There was a couple arguing quietly, a woman with headphones and a child asleep against her shoulder, an old man in a wool coat who looked as though he'd made this crossing hundreds of times and expected nothing from it but the displacement of water and the passage of hours.

He boarded at twenty past nine and climbed to the upper deck, away from the vehicular level where his car sat in its assigned space like a claim he might not be able to back. The sea was dark and textured, the kind of black-green that suggested depths that mattered. He stood at the rail and watched Lerwick recede, the lights becoming abstract and then meaning something less definite than lights. A man came to stand beside him, older, red-faced, exhaling the fumes of several pints.

"First time?" the man asked.

"No," he said, which was true, though the last time had been with his brother, in summer, when the crossing was trivial and the sea had seemed more like a visual effect than an obstacle. That had been the summer before his brother stopped returning emails, stopped taking calls, stopped being a person you could maintain continuity with. He'd been younger then, and the disappearance had seemed temporary, the kind of thing people did to mark transitions, gather themselves, return from looking changed but fundamentally themselves.

"Gets worse in winter," the red-faced man said. "The sea doesn't care much about the ferry schedule, does it?"

He didn't answer, and after a moment the man moved away, back inside to where there was warmth and the muted bustle of people managing the night in unfamiliar vessels. He stayed outside even as the cold settled into his bones and the spray began to wet his jacket. He thought about the body they'd found, the eleven months or so that might have passed since death, the ways that time works on the physical form. He thought about his brother as he'd been, which was not as a body at all but as a presence—opinionated, restless, given to explaining things in ways that made you feel stupid for not having understood them already.

They'd never fought, not seriously. There hadn't been the dramatic severance that made for comprehensible tragedy. Their father had died, their mother had died, and somehow in the space opened by those deaths, his brother had simply opted out of the shared narrative. The last email had been professional in tone, explaining that he needed to step back, that he couldn't maintain the rhythm of contact anymore, that nothing was wrong exactly, only that continuing felt false. He'd been living on Shetland by then, in this cottage that someone had now found him dead in, years after everyone who might have known he was even there had stopped looking.

Around midnight, he went to his cabin, a narrow space with a porthole that revealed nothing but darkness and the occasional reflection of light. He lay in the bunk without undressing and listened to the sound of the sea against the hull, a repetitive, indifferent percussion that seemed to question why he'd come at all. Identification could be arranged by others. There was no particular reason this had to be his disaster, his confirmation, his moment of standing in some basement or cold room and nodding at something that might or might not wear his brother's face. But the sergeant had asked, and the asking had somehow made it possible that not coming would constitute a different kind of abandonment, a final refusal to acknowledge what he'd already refused to acknowledge for more than a decade.

Sleep came in fragments, interrupted by the motion of the ship and the conviction that he was making a terrible mistake. At four in the morning, he gave up the pretense and went back up on deck, where the wind had become articulate and angry, tearing at his jacket with fingers that wanted him on the ground. The sea was a chaos of phosphorescence and black, each wave a temporary architecture. He stayed until dawn began its pale insistence on the eastern horizon, bringing with it the shape of something that might have been land.

They docked at six-fifteen. He'd forgotten how small the terminal was, how it seemed almost insulting in its brevity, as though Shetland couldn't quite bring itself to welcome the ferry at all. He collected his car and drove into the morning, away from Lerwick, north and then west, following the route the sergeant had emailed him. The landscape was empty in the way of the far north in late autumn, all the colors bled to grey and brown, the trees stunted and twisted away from the prevailing wind.

The cottage appeared suddenly, unmarked and unremarkable, the kind of building you could pass a hundred times and never register. Police tape hung listless from the gate, though there seemed no longer to be an active investigation, only the dead machinery of procedure. The sergeant was waiting for him, a man in his fifties with the kind of face that had been weathered into a permanent expression of measured concern. They shook hands formally, as though they were meeting at a funeral rather than in advance of one.

"Thank you for coming," the sergeant said. "I know it's not an easy journey."

"Is it him?" he asked, though he'd meant to approach this more gradually, to gather information first, to maintain some separation between the question and the answer.

"We'll need your confirmation for the record," the sergeant said, "but yes. The dental records your mother's dentist provided match. And there's personal effects—items in the cottage that we've been hoping you could help us understand."

They walked through the cottage in a pattern that felt choreographed, the sergeant pointing out the ordinary infrastructure of his brother's final years. A bed, unmade in the way that suggested it had been that way for a long time. A kitchen with cans and jars arranged with something approaching obsession. Books everywhere, piled on shelves and in corners, their spines cracked and annotated with notes in a hand he recognized immediately. A small desk with a journal, which the sergeant had kept sealed until such time as family might be consulted.

"We found him in the back room," the sergeant said. "He'd been there some time. The postmortem suggested cardiac event, probably sudden. The local doctor thinks he'd been managing an undiagnosed condition—there were no medical records for him on the island, but there were medications in the house. Not prescribed. Acquired, apparently, through means that are no longer terribly important."

He wasn't listening anymore, only moving through the space like a man under water, everything slowed and filtered. The body itself he didn't see—that had already been transported, photographed, processed into the language of evidence. What remained was the architecture of his brother's privacy, the careful construction of a life built on the principle of as little contact as possible with the living world.

"The identification room is at the station," the sergeant said. "They've prepared everything. It shouldn't take long."

The drive back to Lerwick felt longer than it should have. He followed the sergeant's car through the grey morning, watching the back of the vehicle ahead of him as though it contained answers to questions he hadn't formulated. The identification was perfunctory—a body on a table that had once been a person, the face preserved well enough by cold and accident that he could confirm it was his brother without any dramatic wavering of certainty. He'd thought he might cry or react in some way that would mark the occasion, but instead he felt only a kind of emptiness, the realization that whatever had ended when his brother stopped returning emails had already been completed, and this was only the paperwork.

After, the sergeant took him to an office and explained things he barely absorbed—the timeline, the probable cause of death, the question of what happened to the body, the practical matters of notification and disposal. They offered tea, which he declined, and then asked if he wanted to see the journal.

"That might be valuable for your understanding," the sergeant said. "Obviously, it's yours now. Everything in the cottage is yours. You'll need to arrange for its disposition, or disposal, or whatever you'd like to do. There's no rush. The rent is paid through December."

He took the journal back to the waiting area and sat in a plastic chair that seemed designed to discourage lingering. His brother's handwriting filled the pages, dense and small, a kind of visible anxiety rendered in graphite. The dates began six months after his final email and continued, nearly daily, until a gap of several weeks at the end. He read at random, catching phrases: "The light today is unbearable in its honesty." "Received a letter forwarded from Edinburgh—destroyed without opening." "The silence has begun to sound like breathing." "I don't think I was ever equipped for the world that didn't end when it should have."

The ferry back wasn't until that evening. He drove aimlessly through Lerwick, waiting for the afternoon to exhaust itself, eventually finding a pub where he ordered whisky and sat at a table overlooking the harbor. The same water he'd crossed that morning was back to being simply water, grey and ordinary, dividing places that were perhaps not as different as people wanted to believe. He thought about his brother choosing this, choosing the end of it, choosing a cottage on an island where it was easy to become invisible and where dying alone was perhaps not even unusual.

He should have fought harder. He should have come sooner. He should have understood that the silence was never actually what it claimed to be—not rest, not privacy, but a kind of drowning conducted in daylight. These were the thoughts that moved through him like boats through his chest, necessary and useless in equal measure.

At five o'clock, he boarded the ferry again, retracing the journey in reverse. The cabin was the same, or seemed to be, and the sea was the same restless darkness it had always been, indifferent to meaning or consequence. He stood on deck again as the ship pulled away from the island, watching the lights of Lerwick diminish into the growing night. The journal was in his bag, unfinished, the last entry dated seventy-three days before the body was discovered.

The crossing took nine hours in either direction. You went north, and then you came back. You stood in the spray and the wind and you let the distance open like a mouth, and when you returned you were different or not different, but either way you were changed, because time and water worked on everything eventually, wore it down, rendered it smooth and featureless and finally, mercifully, unrecognizable.

When he arrived back in Lerwick—in Edinburgh, in the south, in the place where he'd been living his small life in parallel to his brother's disappearance—he would arrange for the cottage to be cleared. He would read the journal all the way through and wish he hadn't. He would live with the knowledge that his brother had thought of him, had written letters he'd never sent, had maintained a conversation with a person who wasn't there to answer. He would wonder, in the way of people who are left behind, what he might have done differently, and he would accept that the answer was: everything, nothing, something in between, and it wouldn't have mattered because his brother had already decided, had written the ending in that small dense handwriting, had signed off from the world without fanfare or explosion, simply by stopping.

But that was all later, after the crossing, after the confirmation, after the terrible knowledge had settled into the space where he'd been carrying the possibility of being wrong. For now, he sat in the lounge with a cup of tea that grew cold beside him and watched the other passengers arrange themselves into the temporary communities of vessels, and he felt the ferry cutting through the water that separated him from everything he'd abandoned and everything that had abandoned him, and he waited for morning, for the return, for the resumption of the life that continued on regardless.
