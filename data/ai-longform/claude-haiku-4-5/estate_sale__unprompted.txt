# The Selling of a Life

The woman who had once lived here—Margaret Chen, though nobody in Mount Vernon used her married name anymore, they called her just Margaret or sometimes "the doctor's wife" though the doctor had been dead for thirteen years—stood in the hallway watching strangers maneuver her mother's wingback chair through the front door. It was the chair her mother had occupied every evening after dinner, the one with the faded chintz fabric printed with cabbage roses that Margaret's father had brought home in 1987 because her mother had mentioned it in passing at a furniture store. The man carrying the back end was young, maybe thirty, with elaborate tattoos climbing his neck, and he navigated the frame with the casual competence of someone who had moved a thousand chairs through a thousand doorways.

"Easy," called the other man, his partner, from the front. They rotated it at an angle Margaret had not thought possible, and then it was gone, carried out to the truck waiting in the driveway with its hydraulic lift and its clouded windshield and its promise to deliver the chair to someone in Columbus, some estate sale showroom where it would sit among other people's childhoods, waiting to be purchased by someone Margaret would never meet.

It was two o'clock on a Tuesday in October. The afternoon light was that particular shade of amber it became in Ohio in the fall, thick and nostalgic, the kind of light that seemed to be remembering something on your behalf. Margaret had taken the day off from her practice—she was a dermatologist, had been for twenty-seven years, spent her days in a climate-controlled office under fluorescent bulbs examining the skin conditions of people from a three-county radius—and she was beginning to regret this decision. She should have let her sister handle more of it. Her sister Rebecca lived in Columbus now, worked in marketing, had children and a husband and a life that seemed to demand her full attention at all times. But Rebecca had come down last weekend to sort through things, and they had divided the task with the understanding that Margaret would oversee the estate sale company on the day of the sale itself because she was "more local," which was a polite way of saying that Margaret had never really left.

The couch came next. It was the brown sectional, not very old, purchased sometime in the nineties when Margaret's mother had decided that comfort was more important than style. Margaret remembered her mother sitting on this couch while Margaret was home for Thanksgiving in 2003, her mother's legs tucked beneath her, watching a cooking show, and Margaret had thought at that moment—though she had never articulated this to anyone—that her mother looked happy. That was a strange thing to notice about one's mother, that she looked happy, as though happiness in one's parents was an optional state, something they might choose or not choose, rather than a baseline fact of their existence. The couch was also soon gone, and the room that had been full just minutes earlier began to take on the hollow appearance of a space that had been abandoned.

"Mrs. Chen?" A woman approached, perhaps forty-five, with the professional demeanor of someone who had conducted hundreds of these transactions. Her name tag read "Susan," and she held a clipboard. "We're making good time. Looks like we'll be completely cleared out by four, four-thirty at the latest."

Margaret nodded. She had signed the paperwork last week, had verified the inventory list, had watched the appraisers move through her childhood home with practiced eyes, assigning monetary values to objects that had no monetary value at all. The Tiffany-style lamp in the living room: fifty dollars. The china cabinet: two hundred. The wedding china, never used: thirty. The combination, the appraiser had noted, of relative oldness and indifferent condition created a market value that was surprisingly modest. Her mother's life, totaled up, came to approximately $8,400, split between Margaret and Rebecca, though there were costs associated with the sale—the company's commission, delivery, advertising—that would reduce this final number considerably. Margaret would probably net something close to $2,000, which was less than she paid for car insurance.

She walked through the dining room, where the table was now bare and somehow smaller without the place settings and the fruit bowl and the table runner that her mother refreshed seasonally. Margaret's mother had been what people called "house-proud," a term that Margaret had always found slightly pejorative, as though taking pride in one's home was a minor character flaw. But it was true: her mother had cared about domestic order in the way that some people cared about professional achievement. When Margaret had called to tell her mother about winning the grant to study at Johns Hopkins for her dermatology fellowship, her mother had listened politely and then pivoted the conversation to the new wallpaper she was considering for the kitchen.

The China cabinet itself was being unbolted from the wall, the men working with careful attention to the trim around it, and Margaret found herself moving toward the door to step outside. She needed air. The house smelled wrong—too much activity, too many strangers—and the autumn light had shifted into late-afternoon brightness that made the empty spaces seem more empty.

Outside, the neighborhood was quiet in the way that suburban Ohio neighborhoods are quiet on a Tuesday afternoon. The houses across the street presented their blank windows and closed doors. She knew three of these families, had played with their children in the 1980s, had watched those children grow and leave for college and settle elsewhere, had seen the houses change hands, watched small renovations appear. Mrs. Kowalski, who had lived next door, had died two years ago, and the house had been bought by a younger couple from Cincinnati who commuted to the city. They had painted the shutters a trendy blue-gray, and Margaret's mother had made a disapproving comment about it at Easter.

The truck was larger than Margaret had expected, and she could see now that it was nearly full. There were other sales happening today—she had learned this during her conversations with Susan. The company conducted estate sales throughout central Ohio, sometimes three or four a day. This meant that the items from her mother's house would be transported to a large warehouse where they would be consolidated with furniture from other people's mothers, other people's houses, and then displayed in rows, tagged with prices, waiting to be claimed by strangers.

She thought about her mother, Patricia, who had died in June at the age of eighty-nine. Patricia had been in decline for some time—her memory had become unreliable, her anger had increased, her conversations had become repetitive. But she had lived alone in this house until she fell in the hallway and broke her hip, and after that there had been the hospital and the rehabilitation facility and finally the nursing home where she had spent her last three weeks becoming progressively less herself until she had ceased to be herself entirely. Margaret had been there when her mother died, had been holding her hand, and had felt nothing in that moment but relief, which had immediately filled her with guilt, which had become a permanent feature of the weeks that followed.

"Everything okay?" Susan had come back out, sensing perhaps that Margaret needed something, or needed to not be inside the house.

"Fine," Margaret said. "I was just thinking. Was she... the appraiser... was she thorough? I mean, did she actually look at everything?"

Susan's expression shifted to something more human, less professional. "They usually are. Is there something you were concerned about? Something of value that might have been missed?"

Margaret shook her head. She wasn't concerned about value. She had gone through the house herself, had found her mother's important documents, had called her mother's lawyer and her mother's banker and her mother's doctor's office. She had also found, in her mother's nightstand, a bundle of letters from a man named Richard, dated from 1961 to 1962, before her mother had married Margaret's father. The letters were passionate and raw in a way that had made Margaret stop reading them after the second one, had made her feel like she was violating something fundamental. She had not shown them to Rebecca. They were in a manila envelope in her car now, and she was not sure what she intended to do with them. Burn them, probably, at some point. Or maybe just keep them in her car indefinitely, the way one keeps receipts and napkins and ticket stubs from movies one no longer remembers.

The couch had come to rest with its arms protruding slightly beyond the edge of the truck. One of the men began securing it with straps while the other went back into the house. Margaret could see through the open front door that the living room was now nearly empty. Soon the lamps would go, then the end tables, then the artwork. Her mother had been partial to watercolor paintings of nature scenes—mountains and streams and forests—the kind of art that was pleasant to look at but registered as essentially impersonal. Margaret had spent her childhood in a home decorated by someone with excellent taste but no strong opinions about anything. It was this absence of opinion that Margaret remembered now, the careful neutrality of her mother's choices.

"She was a quiet person," Rebecca had said to her last weekend, standing in the kitchen while they organized plates and glasses into boxes marked for donation. "I don't think I really knew her."

Margaret had wanted to argue with this, to defend her mother's right to privacy, to internal life, to boundaries. But she had found instead that she couldn't. Her mother had been a stranger to her, a kind and competent stranger, but someone whose interior experience remained, after fifty-four years, fundamentally inaccessible. When had this become clear? Margaret couldn't say. Probably always, probably from the beginning, probably it was something she had been avoiding understanding for decades.

The last large piece of furniture—her mother's bedroom dresser, a piece of dark wood from perhaps the 1960s, utterly without distinction—came out at three forty-five. After that it was smaller items, the men moving more quickly, their work now fragmentary and efficient. A bookshelf. A nightstand. The miscellaneous wooden chairs from the kitchen that Margaret's mother had accumulated over the years, never throwing anything away, always imagining that someday she might need extra seating.

By four-fifteen, the house was empty. Not just empty of furniture, but empty in the way that a human body becomes empty after the soul departs—it was the same shell but without any sense that anything of significance had ever inhabited it. The walls showed ghost marks where pictures had hung, the carpet showed darker rectangles where furniture had rested. The kitchen was especially terrible, stripped of the appliances that had been excluded from the sale, the counters bearing only their utilitarian self, the cabinets opened to reveal the empty spaces within.

Margaret sat on the floor in what had been her mother's bedroom, in the room where her mother had slept every night for the last twenty-seven years, and where before that she had slept alongside Margaret's father until he died. She sat with her back against the wall and looked out the window at the oak tree in the backyard, which was beginning to turn, its leaves shifting from deep green to yellow-bronze. She and Rebecca had climbed this tree as children. They had built a fort in its lower branches, had scraped their knees, had fought about who got to sit on the highest stable limb. Their mother had called them to come inside for dinner, and they had ignored her, had pretended they couldn't hear her, had been in that state of childhood where the external world could be denied entirely if you had access to a tree and your own imagination.

She became aware that Susan was standing in the doorway, looking uncomfortable. "We're all loaded up," Susan said quietly. "The inventory checklist matches what we came to get. Is there anything else you need us to do?"

Margaret rose, her knees protesting, and walked through the empty house one final time. The hardwood floors echoed with her footsteps. The light from the windows fell on bare walls and bare ceilings. It looked like a house that had never been lived in at all, which of course was not true, but it was becoming true, was transforming into the past tense, the house already becoming memory rather than place. She stood at the front door and looked out at the driveway, where the truck was now pulling away, headed toward the warehouse in Columbus, toward the consolidation with other people's lives, toward the slow dispersal of her mother's earthly possessions among strangers.

"We're good," she told Susan. "Thank you for your work."

She locked the back door, then the front door, then tested each lock to make sure they caught properly. The realtor would be coming next week to list the house, which would be sold, probably within three months given the current market and the modest but respectable condition of the property. Margaret and Rebecca would split the proceeds—minus the realtor's commission, minus the cost of repairs that the inspector would undoubtedly recommend. Margaret calculated quickly in her head that each sister would probably net around $85,000, which was enough to notice but not enough to change anything, not enough to alter the trajectory of either woman's life.

She pulled her car out of the driveway and parked across the street, looking at the house as though it belonged to someone else. It was a ranch-style house, built in 1972, with a shallow roof and modest proportions. The landscaping was mature but somewhat overgrown. The siding was a soft gray, and the shutters were the traditional green that her mother had chosen forty years ago and had never changed. It was the kind of house that would be photographed for a real estate listing, would be described as "charming" or "full of potential," would be bought by a young family who would paint the inside a trendy color and renovate the kitchen and eventually forget that anyone else had ever lived there.

Margaret thought about her mother as a young woman, before the marriage, receiving letters from Richard, whoever Richard had been, a man who had written about love and future and possibility in a way that suggested he had not yet learned that the future has a way of diminishing hope rather than fulfilling it. She thought about her mother choosing the wallpaper for the kitchen over the years, replacing it when it became dated, never quite satisfied but never quite bothered enough to really do anything about it. She thought about her mother sitting on the brown couch, watching someone prepare food on television, seemingly content in a way that Margaret had found both touching and incomprehensible.

The light was beginning to fail now, the amber glow transitioning to the blue of early evening. A teenager biked past on the street, heading toward some destination that seemed to require speed. A car pulled into the Kowalskis' driveway—the new owners, Margaret thought, the young couple from Cincinnati—and a woman got out carrying groceries. She was probably Margaret's age, or younger, one of those people who had escaped the Midwest and returned to it later, perhaps for family or work or the mysterious logic that sometimes drives people home.

Margaret sat in her car for another twenty minutes, until the cold became noticeable through the windows and the evening had settled into something genuinely dark. Then she started the engine and drove back toward the highway that would take her to Columbus, to her apartment, to her life as it had been configured in the years since her marriage had ended. She thought about calling Rebecca, but Rebecca would only make practical noises about the sale price and the realtor and the next steps, and Margaret was too tired for practicality. Instead she thought about the letters in the manila envelope, about Richard and her mother and the possibility that had at some point transformed into the quiet satisfaction of a life shaped not by passion but by small daily decisions, each one barely noticeable in the moment.

At a red light, she called her office and asked her nurse to schedule a patient for Friday morning, someone with a seborrheic keratosis that had been bothering them. The nurse confirmed that the patient was available, and Margaret said that was fine, that everything was fine, that she would see them then. When the light changed, she continued driving, the darkness pressing in on the headlights as the houses of Mount Vernon receded behind her, already becoming the places where other people lived, the childhood receding into its proper place as something that had happened to a different person, in a different time, when the world was still full of possibility and people cared about wallpaper and building forts in trees and writing letters about love to people they hoped would save them.
