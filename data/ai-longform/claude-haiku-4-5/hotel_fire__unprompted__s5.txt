# The Return

The lobby smelled of fresh paint and something else—a synthetic vanilla that seemed designed to erase memory. Margaret stood in the threshold of the Grandview Hotel, her suitcase small and worn beside her, and felt the architecture of the place rearrange itself in her mind like a magic trick she couldn't quite follow. The grand staircase was new. The exposed brick where the old registration desk had been was now smooth plaster. Even the proportions seemed different, as though the fire had compressed the building into something smaller, and they'd had to reconstruct it from faulty recollection.

Twenty-three years. She had not set foot in this place since the afternoon she walked out with her hair singed short and the smell of smoke embedded so deeply in her clothes that it took weeks to wash out. She had not wanted to come back even now, but her daughter Sarah had gotten married—had gotten engaged, had planned, had asked—in that order—and Margaret could not think of a good reason to refuse that didn't sound like cowardice to her own ears.

"Welcome to the Grandview," the young man at the desk said, his smile professional and unbothered. He typed something into his computer and slid a key card across the marble countertop. Room 412. Margaret picked it up and noticed her hands were trembling slightly. She put them in her coat pockets.

The elevators were new too. The old ones had been small, temperamental things with brass gates that required a particular shove to close properly. She had known the sound of those gates like she knew her own heartbeat—the metallic screech, the resistance of old machinery. These elevators were silent and swift. When the doors opened on the fourth floor, she stood there for a moment before stepping out, as though the hallway might still contain some version of herself that she wasn't ready to meet.

Her room faced the courtyard. She had known this before she opened the door, had somehow remembered which rooms faced which direction despite nearly a quarter-century passing. Room 412 had a queen bed with a white duvet, a television mounted on the wall, a bathroom with marble counters. Nothing like the way it had been when she worked here—the old furniture, the water-stained ceiling tiles, the carpet that was always slightly damp in the corners. They had gutted the place and rebuilt it to the specifications of modern hospitality, which meant erasing every sign of what had happened here.

Margaret sat on the edge of the bed and found that she was holding her breath. She forced herself to exhale.

The fire had started in the kitchen—that much had been in the news reports, in the investigations, in the depositions she'd given to insurance companies and lawyers. A combination of old wiring and fryer oil and human negligence. It had been a Tuesday, mid-afternoon, not a busy time. She had been on the fourth floor, making up rooms, when the alarm began to sound. She remembered the sound very clearly: three short bursts, then silence, then three short bursts again. Like Morse code for SOS, she'd thought then, though she hadn't known what SOS was, not really.

The stairwell had filled with smoke quickly. She had made it down two flights before the lights went out. She remembered the feeling of the bannister under her palms, the specific texture of the metal, the way her fingers had followed its curve. She remembered thinking about her daughter, who was four years old then, who was asleep at home with Margaret's mother, who had no idea that her mother was moving through darkness.

She had emerged from the stairwell on the second floor and found an open hallway, found a window, found the fire escape. Three other people had come down with her, guests she didn't recognize and would never see again after that day. The fire department had arrived within minutes. No one had died. The news reports had emphasized this fact repeatedly, as though it might compensate for the fact that the building had been nearly destroyed.

Margaret had not returned to work after the evacuation. She had taken a job at a different hotel across town and never told them why she'd left so abruptly. Her references from the Grandview had been provided by mail after some phone calls. She had spent the next two decades slowly building a career in hotel management, moving to different cities, accumulating qualifications and titles, eventually becoming the operations manager for a mid-sized chain. She had been successful. She had moved on. This was what she told herself.

She lay back on the bed and stared at the ceiling. There were no water stains here. The ceiling was smooth and white and aggressively pristine. She closed her eyes.

When she opened them again, it was nearly seven o'clock, and the sky visible from her window had turned the particular shade of blue that occurs just before dark. Sarah's rehearsal dinner was in an hour. Margaret showered and changed into the navy dress she'd brought for the evening's events, applied makeup carefully, arranged her hair. She was sixty-two years old now, with gray at her temples and laugh lines that seemed to deepen every year. She had not become the sort of person who looked young, but she had become the sort of person who looked competent, settled, trustworthy. This had seemed important.

The rehearsal dinner was being held in the Grandview's banquet room, which was on the second floor. Margaret took the stairs instead of the elevator, needing the time to prepare herself. The stairwell looked nothing like the old one. It was wide and well-lit and decorated with photos of the hotel through the decades—the original building from 1952, the renovation of 1987, the modern refresh of 2015. She stopped in front of one of the photos, taken in the 1970s, showing the lobby as it had been when she worked here. She could see the old registration desk, the worn carpet, the inexplicable teal color that had seemed fashionable at the time. In the background, out of focus, there was a person in a chambermaid's uniform, pushing a cart of linens. Margaret could not see the face, could not tell if it was her, but something in the posture, the angle of the body, felt familiar.

The banquet room looked nothing like she remembered. The old room had been dim, with wood paneling and a ceiling held up by exposed beams. This new space was bright and open, with clean lines and the kind of neutral aesthetic that could be transformed into whatever was needed. Long tables were arranged in a U-shape, and the room was filled with Sarah's fiancé's family on one side and Margaret's family on the other. Sarah stood when she saw Margaret and came toward her with her face lit up, and Margaret thought about how her daughter would never have to know what it felt like to stand in a dark stairwell with smoke filling your lungs, would never have to spend twenty-three years pretending it didn't matter.

"Mom," Sarah said, and hugged her. "You made it. How's your room?"

"Very nice," Margaret said. "Very comfortable."

"That's good. I'm so glad you could come back here. I know it's not..." Sarah hesitated, searching for words. "I know this place has complicated feelings for you."

Margaret had never told her daughter the details. She had told her that there had been a fire, that it had been frightening, that she had chosen to work elsewhere afterward. She had not told her about the stairwell, the smoke, the fingers following the bannister, the way her hands had shaken for weeks afterward. She had not told her about the dreams, which had faded but never quite disappeared, in which she was climbing and climbing and there was no way out.

"It's all right," Margaret said. "It's a new hotel, really. They rebuilt it."

This seemed to satisfy Sarah, who squeezed her hand and led her to her seat. Margaret found herself between Sarah's father-in-law-to-be and her own brother Michael, who she hadn't seen in person in nearly three years. Michael hugged her and asked about her work, and she asked about his kids, and they fell into the comfortable pattern of people who had known each other a long time but didn't necessarily know each other well.

Dinner was served. The food was good—better than the old hotel would have offered, Margaret thought, though she didn't actually know what the standards had been back then. She had eaten the staff meals, which were usually simple and occasionally inedible. She had been hungry most of the time, working full-time and trying to raise a daughter on a housekeeper's salary. The hunger had made everything taste all right.

She watched Sarah during dinner, watched the way her daughter's new husband looked at her, watched the joy on her face as she talked with family. Sarah had always been resilient, good-natured, forgiving. Margaret had never understood why, because she'd done nothing to teach her these things. The resilience must have come from genetics, from some strain in the family that Margaret couldn't trace. Or from the fact that Margaret had tried so hard to protect her from difficulty that Sarah had never learned to be anything but optimistic.

After dinner, people began to disperse. Some headed to the bar, others to their rooms. Margaret found herself walking slowly through the lobby, ostensibly heading back to her room but actually just... looking. The lobby was the heart of the building, the part that had always felt most real to her. It was where guests first entered, where they felt the hotel's personality most acutely. This new lobby was sterile by comparison. It was efficient and clean and utterly without character.

She sat in one of the leather chairs near the window and watched people move through the space. A couple checking in, a businessman taking a call, a family with children asking about the pool. None of them seemed to be aware that they were standing in a place that had burned, that had been destroyed and rebuilt, that carried the weight of that history beneath its fresh paint and new carpet.

She was pulled from her thoughts by a voice. "Margaret?"

She looked up to see a man in his mid-sixties, with gray hair and a face that was familiar in a way she couldn't immediately place. He was looking at her with an expression of tentative recognition.

"I'm sorry," she said. "I don't—"

"It's Tom. Tom Hendricks. I worked in maintenance here, back in the eighties."

Understanding came to her slowly. Tom had been younger then, of course, with dark hair and more defined features. But the eyes were the same—a particular shade of blue-gray that she remembered from morning staff meetings and chance encounters in the hallways.

"Tom," she said, and found that she was standing, that he was reaching toward her, and then they were shaking hands. "How strange. What are you doing here?"

"I work here," he said. "Have for about six years now. Facilities manager. They rebuilt the whole place, but I came back." He was watching her carefully. "What about you? I remember you left pretty suddenly, back in... when was it?"

"1987," she said. "After the fire."

"Ah," he said. "Right. A lot of people didn't come back after that." He paused. "I came back about five years after it happened. Needed the job, and it seemed like the building had earned a second chance, so maybe I deserved one too." He smiled a little. "That probably sounds philosophical for a facilities manager."

Margaret didn't say anything for a moment. She was thinking about second chances, about the way places and people could be burned away and rebuilt, about whether the rebuilt version was actually the same thing or something entirely new.

"My daughter's getting married tomorrow," she said finally. "The rehearsal dinner was tonight."

"That's wonderful," Tom said. "Is she marrying one of the guests, or—"

"No, she was just married from the church. The wedding tomorrow is the reception here."

"Ah," he said. "That must be strange, coming back here."

"It is," Margaret admitted. She found that she was tired, suddenly, a bone-deep fatigue that had nothing to do with the day's travel.

Tom was called away by a guest a few minutes later, and Margaret made her way back to her room. She took the elevator this time, too tired for the stairs. In her room, she sat at the window and looked out at the dark courtyard. Tomorrow, Sarah would stand in this hotel and marry a man she loved, surrounded by people who cared about her. The hotel would serve its purpose. It would hold the celebration, contain the joy, facilitate the beginning of something new. This was what rebuilt things were meant to do—not to erase what had come before, but to build something better in its place.

Margaret thought about the girl she had been, standing in that dark stairwell with her fingers on the bannister. She thought about the fear, the smoke, the terrible isolation of being alone in the dark. She thought about the woman she had become—competent and trustworthy and afraid of nothing that couldn't be managed through diligence and control. Two different people, really, connected only by a line of time and the fact of memory.

She wondered if the girl would recognize the woman, if they were ever to meet. She wondered if the girl would approve of the choices the woman had made, or if she would judge her for building a life so carefully that it seemed, sometimes, to contain no actual living.

She was still sitting by the window when sleep came over her, that soft edge of exhaustion that precedes the genuine surrender of slumber. Her dreams that night were not about the fire, about staircases or smoke or darkness. Instead, she dreamed of Sarah as a child—four years old, the morning of the fire, still asleep when Margaret had left for work. In the dream, Sarah woke up and called for her mother, and Margaret was there, stepping out of the morning light to say: I'm here, I'm here, I'm not going anywhere.

She woke to sunlight and the sound of the hotel coming alive—deliveries arriving, staff moving through hallways, the sounds of a place that had learned, through catastrophe and reconstruction, how to exist again. She lay still for a moment, letting the dream fade, and then she got out of bed and began to prepare for her daughter's wedding.
