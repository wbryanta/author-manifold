# Excerpt

The main line had burst somewhere between the apricot grove and the northern fence, and Marcus knew it the moment he saw the way the pressure gauge was behaving. It dipped and held, dipped and held, like something was bleeding out into the soil. He'd been checking it every morning for three weeks now, watching it slip away in increments, and he'd finally called his brother.

Thomas arrived at seven, which was early enough that the sun hadn't yet burned off the quality of the air. He was still in good shape for a man pushing fifty, though he carried himself differently than he used to—shoulders rounded inward slightly, as if perpetually braced against something. He'd driven out from the city, and he looked like it: pressed shirt, clean jeans, shoes that had never touched irrigation mud.

"The main line?" Thomas asked, not bothering with a greeting.

"Between the apricots and the fence. Somewhere."

"And you haven't found it yet?"

Marcus was already moving toward the shed. "Not exactly easy to find a burst in two hundred feet of buried pipe."

"You could call someone."

"I am someone."

The dig began methodically. Marcus had marked out a grid using stakes and twine, dividing the search area into sections. Thomas held the shovel first, and his brother watched to see if he'd complain about the heat or his back or the general senselessness of the task. For a while, Thomas was quiet, settling into the rhythm of it. The ground was harder than it should have been—another sign of how little water was getting through—and it took real effort to break into it.

By nine o'clock, they'd opened three test holes and found nothing. Thomas had switched to water duty, walking the line with a listening rod, though they both knew the odds of that actually working were slim. Marcus kept digging. The sun had begun its serious work by then, and sweat had soaked through his shirt.

"How long has it been like this?" Thomas asked.

"Three weeks that I've noticed it clearly. Probably longer."

"And you're just dealing with it now?"

"I'm dealing with it now." Marcus threw another shovel of dirt to the side. "I've been dealing with other things."

Thomas was quiet for a moment. "The water bill came to the house. The one Mom left to both of us. It was three hundred and forty dollars for the month."

"The line's burst."

"I know. You just told me. I'm saying maybe we should talk about whether we're still watering three hundred acres."

Marcus stopped digging. He rested the shovel against his leg. "Not today."

"It's kind of relevant today, given that we're out here in ninety-degree heat looking for a break in a pipe from 1987."

"It's from 1992."

"That doesn't actually help your argument, Marcus."

The brothers had been having some version of this conversation for about eight months, ever since Thomas had moved back from Sacramento to help sort through their mother's affairs. He hadn't stayed in the city—he'd come back to the valley three years before that, which he claimed was about something other than money, though Marcus had never quite believed him. But the timing of his return and his push toward a sale had a correlation that felt too neat to ignore.

"You want to dig," Marcus said, "or you want to talk about selling the place?"

"I can do both."

"No, you really can't. One requires digging. The other requires thinking, and you've already decided."

"I haven't decided anything."

Marcus resumed digging. The shovel hit something solid about four feet down in the second test hole of the afternoon. His heart did a small lift before he realized it was probably just a rock, but he cleared around it anyway, working carefully. It was rectangular, definitely buried deliberately, and when he'd exposed enough of it to be sure, he found the edge of a concrete pad.

"Well," he said. "That's probably not the line."

Thomas came over to look. "What is that?"

"No idea."

They dug down another foot, exposing maybe a quarter of whatever it was. It was definitely concrete, several inches thick, and it ran at least six feet in the direction they hadn't excavated.

"Dad's?" Thomas ventured.

"Maybe."

Their father had done a lot of things to the property over the years, most of them documented in sporadic notes in a filing cabinet that their mother had kept. Not all of them. He'd been dead for sixteen years, and there was probably a whole history of the place that had died with him—modifications, experiments, ideas that had seemed urgent at the time but mattered to no one after.

"Keep digging?" Thomas asked.

"Not unless you want to spend the whole day on this."

"The line, you mean."

"Yeah. The line."

But they'd lost something of the purpose now. They kept working, moving east along the marked grid, opening holes, finding nothing. By early afternoon, they'd established that the break was either past their search area or hadn't been where Marcus thought it was. They took a break under the canopy of the oldest apricot tree, the one their mother had planted thirty-five years ago from a cutting her own mother had brought from Armenia. It had never produced fruit the way it was supposed to, which their mother always attributed to the valley's heat and the irrigation water's salinity, and which their father had blamed on her refusal to let him prune it properly.

Thomas had brought cold water in a cooler, at least, and Marcus drank it gratefully. His hands were already blistering.

"So," Thomas said. "The concrete thing."

"Probably nothing."

"Probably not. If Dad did it, it was probably something. He didn't waste materials on nothing."

Marcus leaned his head back against the tree trunk. The light filtering through the leaves was a particular shade of green he associated with only this time of day and this specific tree. "Are you going to keep bringing up the sale?"

"Probably."

"Today?"

"I don't know. Are you going to keep avoiding it?"

"I'm not avoiding it. I'm just not interested in having that argument right now."

Thomas was quiet for a minute. He'd brought a sandwich, and he unwrapped it methodically. "It doesn't make sense anymore," he said finally. "The economics don't work. You know they don't."

"They work if you're patient."

"With what? Apricots? Marcus, apricots are worth nothing right now. Almonds, maybe, but we'd have to rip out half of what we have. And the water situation is only going to get worse. You know that."

"The water was fine until the line burst."

"The water has been an issue for four years. We've discussed this. Multiple times. We agreed—"

"No. You agreed. I agreed to listen to you."

Thomas set down his sandwich. "Okay. What's the plan? You fix the line, we get through the summer, and then what? We're back here next year having the same conversation, except the line breaks again, or something else fails, or the county water allocation drops another ten percent."

"The plan is to keep the orchard going. Like our family did. Like Mom wanted."

"Mom wanted us to be happy. She also wanted us to be realistic."

"Did she say that?"

"She didn't have to. She left the property to both of us. Do you know what that means? It means she wanted us both to have agency in the decision."

Marcus closed his eyes. "It means she couldn't decide."

"It means—" Thomas stopped. "Yeah, okay. It probably means she couldn't decide. Which makes it our job to decide. Together."

"I've decided. I'm keeping it."

"You can't keep it. I own half."

"Then sell your half."

Thomas laughed, a short, bitter sound. "You know I can't do that. The property is entangled. You can't split it up. And even if you could, neither of us could afford to buy the other out. Do the math. You make money from the orchard—barely, some years. I make money as a consultant, which is fine, but not fine enough to buy you out, and you don't make enough to buy me out. So we're stuck. Together."

Marcus opened his eyes and looked at his brother. Thomas had his father's sharp features, the kind that looked distinguished in the city and worn out in the country. He'd always been the one who left, who moved away, who came back to visit with stories about restaurants and gallery openings and the general sophistication of urban life. It had irritated Marcus when they were younger. Now, mostly, he felt a sort of distant pity.

"You could move back," Marcus said. "Really move back. Not just visit, not just consult long-distance. Live here. Work the place with me."

"Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Don't try to rope me in with nostalgia. You know that's not my life."

"It was your life."

"For about eighteen years. And then I grew out of it."

"You didn't grow out of it. You ran away from it."

Thomas stood up abruptly. "I'm going to look at the eastern section. Maybe the break is further out than you thought."

Marcus watched him go, hiking back toward the fence line with a determination that suggested he had no intention of finding anything. The sun was high and brutal now, and the trees cast very little shade. Marcus could see the way the leaves on some of the lower branches had started to curl despite the morning watering, which meant the irrigation issue was more serious than even he had wanted to admit.

He thought about calling someone—a contractor, someone professional. But there was a stubbornness in him that Thomas probably wouldn't understand, a sense that calling for help on something like this was a kind of surrender. If you couldn't maintain your own property, what right did you have to own it? This was the sort of thinking that had kept his father up at night worrying about costs, and his mother up at night worrying about his father's worrying. Marcus had watched them both age into the land, watched the orchard age them back.

Maybe Thomas was right. Maybe the mathematics of it had simply shifted under them, the way soil shifts under a building if the foundation isn't properly maintained. Their parents had worked out of a different kind of economics—one where land was wealth just by existing, where orchards were legacy as much as livelihood. The world had rewritten those equations while they weren't paying attention.

Marcus got up and walked the line himself, not with the listening rod but just with his eyes, looking for depressions in the earth, for spots where the soil looked darker or damper. He was halfway between where they'd been digging and the northern fence when he found it: a slight settling, barely visible, running roughly perpendicular to the pipe line. He marked it with a rock and went back to get the shovel.

The break was maybe thirty feet from where Thomas had been looking, which seemed to confirm some suspicion in Marcus's mind about his brother's actual commitment to finding it. The pipe had ruptured along a seam, and the break was clean—a failure that happened suddenly, probably in response to some shifting in the soil or some weakness that had been accumulating for years. There was enough of the line exposed that he could see the problem clearly, though not enough yet to repair it.

He was standing over the hole, calculating what he'd need, when Thomas reappeared.

"Found it," Marcus said.

His brother came over and looked down at the break. It was anticlimactic somehow, just a split in old plastic, the soil around it stained darker where water had been seeping out for weeks. You could stand over a crack like that and let an entire future drain away through it, and the crack wouldn't care. The crack wasn't malicious or sad. It was just a crack.

"Can you fix it?" Thomas asked.

"Yeah. I'll need to replace maybe six feet of line. I have stock in the shed from when we did the southern section two years ago."

"How long will it take?"

"Few hours. I'll do it tomorrow, probably. I want to make sure the ground sets a bit."

They walked back toward the house slowly, not talking, following the path they'd worn over decades of walking it. The orchard stretched out around them on all sides, row after row of trees that someone—their great-grandfather, probably, or some generation before that—had planted because they believed in permanence. It was a kind of faith, Marcus thought. An absolute conviction that the future would validate the work. Their ancestors were wrong about most things, and they were right about this one, and neither fact changed the reality of the present.

"I'm going back to the city tomorrow," Thomas said as they reached the house.

"You just got here."

"I know. But I have a job interview on Thursday."

"You have a consulting contract."

"I do, but the contract ends next month. I've been looking for something permanent. Something with a salary and benefits and a future that doesn't depend on my parents' property."

Marcus understood then what the real argument had been about, and what all the arguments would continue to be about. Thomas wasn't trying to convince him to sell. Thomas was trying to convince himself. He needed the sale to mean that leaving had been the right choice, that the future he'd built elsewhere was more solid than the past he'd left behind. And if Marcus wouldn't agree to sell, then that future stayed uncertain. That past stayed a door that could theoretically still be opened.

"You should go," Marcus said.

"I'm not selling."

"I know."

They stood in the driveway, between the house and the shed, in the space where their father had kept tools and where their mother had kept a garden that slowly overran its designated beds until it became almost wild. Both of them were gone now, and the space had become neither tool storage nor garden. It was just a space where two brothers stood in the heat, unable to quite leave and unable to quite stay.

"I'd like to help you with the repair," Thomas said finally. "If you don't mind. Tomorrow."

Marcus considered this. It was not a capitulation. It was not even agreement on the larger question. It was just his brother saying he wasn't ready to leave yet, that there was still something here that required his presence, even if that presence was temporary.

"Early," Marcus said. "Before it gets hot. Probably six."

"Okay."

Thomas went back inside to gather his things. Marcus stayed in the driveway, looking at the property as if he might be able to see into it, to understand what it actually was beyond the simple fact of its existing. The trees needed water. The lines needed maintenance. The soil needed management. These were the realities, vast and practical and not subject to the emotional weight that either brother wanted to load onto them.

But that was the thing about land—it made everything emotional, because you couldn't separate the work from the inheritance, couldn't extract the mathematics from the memory. You were always farming two things at once: the present and the past, the actual and the possible. And some days, like today, those two things were in such direct conflict that you couldn't move forward on either front.

The sun was starting its descent now, though the heat would last for hours yet. Marcus walked over to check the pressure gauge on the main line, more out of habit than hope. It was still dropping, but more slowly now, and he thought about the concrete pad they'd found earlier, about all the buried things beneath the orchard that neither brother knew about and neither brother would ever fully understand. Maybe that was the real inheritance—not the land itself but the infinite accumulation of mysteries beneath it, the layers of decision and intention and failure that no amount of examination could quite penetrate.
