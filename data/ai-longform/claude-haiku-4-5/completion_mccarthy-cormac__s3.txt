They bore him down among them and he could feel the press of their bodies and the rake of their nails and he cried out in the dream and she shook him again and he woke with his heart hammering in his chest and sweat cold on his skin and he was gasping like a man pulled from water.

Easy, she said. Easy now.

He sat breathing heavily in the dark and did not answer. After a time his breathing slowed and he lay back against the pillow and stared at nothing.

What were you dreaming? she said.

Nothing. Just foolishness.

It sounded like more than that.

He didn't answer. In the dark he could hear her breathing beside him and the small sounds of the house settling around them. Somewhere distant a dog was barking and he lay there listening to it fade and die away into the night and he thought about the prophet in his dream and the sun that would not return and he wondered what it meant or if it meant anything at all.

By morning he had half forgotten it or convinced himself he had. He rose before dawn and dressed in the dark and went downstairs and built up the fire in the kitchen stove and put the kettle on and stood with his hands in his pockets looking out the window at the gray world emerging from shadow. The land lay still and colorless and the trees were just dark shapes against a slightly lighter sky. He thought about the three men from the passage, the bearded one and his two companions, and he wondered where they were going and what drove them forward into that western wilderness with nothing but what they could carry and no word between them. There seemed something in them of a purpose that admitted no question, as if they moved toward some inevitable conclusion that was already written in the stars.

The kettle began to whistle and he turned and poured the hot water into the pot and set it aside to steep. He found cups in the cabinet and spoons in the drawer and when the tea was ready he poured a cup and sat at the table and drank it black and hot and bitter. The kitchen was warm now and golden in the growing light and he sat there with his hands wrapped around the cup and thought about nothing in particular, letting his mind drift from one thing to another like a man watching clouds move across a sky.

When she came down he was on his second cup and had laid out bread and cheese and a plate of cold meat on the table. She was wrapped in her robe and her hair was loose about her shoulders and she moved carefully down the stairs as if the weight she carried was always threatening to drag her off balance. He rose and pulled out a chair for her and she settled herself with a small grunt of effort.

How do you feel? he said.

Tired. The same as yesterday and the day before.

The doctor said it would pass.

The doctor doesn't have to carry it.

He poured her a cup of tea and she wrapped her hands around it and sipped it carefully and looked out the window just as he had done. The sun was rising now and the land was taking on color and shape and there were birds calling from the trees and somewhere a rooster was crowing in the distance.

I want you to take me to the city, she said.

What?

The city. I want to go there.

For what?

For the doctor. A real doctor, not that quack we have here.

He didn't say anything. He broke off a piece of bread and began to eat it slowly.

Did you hear me? she said.

I heard you. We can't afford it.

We can if you find work.

There's no work. You know that.

There's work in the city.

And I would leave you here alone?

I'm not asking you to leave me. I'm asking you to take me there. We could stay with my sister.

Her sister was married to a man who had made money in trade and lived in a large house on a good street and had never much cared for her marriage to him or for him. He said nothing and continued eating and she watched him with an expression of stubborn determination on her face.

My sister would help us, she said. She would. All you have to do is ask.

I won't ask her for anything.

Then I'll ask her myself.

You'll do no such thing.

And why not? Why shouldn't I? This is your child, is it not? Your responsibility?

He set down the bread and looked at her and her eyes were bright with something like tears but not quite tears and there was color in her cheeks that hadn't been there before.

It's my responsibility, he said. And I'll meet it.

How? By sitting here in this house doing nothing? By dreaming like you were dreaming last night? By waiting for something that will never come?

Something will come.

What? Tell me. What will come?

He couldn't answer because he didn't know. He rose from the table and went to the window and looked out at the morning and the land beyond and he felt the weight of something bearing down on him, some vast and inexorable thing that was moving toward him whether he was ready or not.

You're just like my father, she said behind him. Do you know that? Always waiting for luck to find him. Always sure that tomorrow would be better than today.

What happened to your father? he said.

He drank himself to death waiting.

He turned from the window and looked at her and she was staring down into her tea as if the answers to all the questions in the world were written in its depths.

I'm not your father, he said.

Aren't you? Sometimes I wonder.

He wanted to say something to that but he couldn't think of anything that would help and so he said nothing and went to the door and took his coat from the peg and put it on.

Where are you going?

Out.

When will you be back?

I don't know.

He left her there at the table with her tea and went out into the morning and closed the door behind him. The sun was warm on his face and the air smelled of growing things and turned earth and he walked without knowing where he was going, letting his feet carry him. He thought about the three men from his dream or the passage or wherever they had come from, and he thought about the way they moved with such certainty and such disregard for everything around them. There was something in that kind of movement that called to him, some echo of a freedom that he had perhaps never known but somehow remembered anyway.

He found himself on the road that led toward the town and he continued on it, passing the occasional farm and the occasional person traveling in the opposite direction, and no one paid him any mind. The sun climbed higher and the shadows shortened and the heat began to build and still he walked on. He thought about the prophet in his dream and the sun that would not return and he wondered if perhaps that was what the future held, not the future but the present, a sun that had already failed to return and a darkness that would go on forever whether anyone was ready for it or not.

By noon he had reached the town and he walked through the main street with its shops and offices and people moving about on their business. He passed the bank and the feed store and the doctor's office where old Hendricks hung out his shingle and claimed expertise in matters medical. He passed the church and the schoolhouse and the tavern. He didn't stop at any of these places but continued on through the town and out the other side and found himself on a road that led into country again, country that grew wilder and more broken the farther he went.

Here the land began to change. The gentle slopes gave way to deeper valleys and the forests grew thicker and older. He began to see signs of things that had passed through, hoofprints in the mud and the remains of fires and places where the brush had been trampled down. He thought of the three men again and wondered if perhaps he was following some trace of them, following them backward through time into their history. There was something attractive in that thought, the idea that he might come to some place where they had been and perhaps learn something of where they were going or why.

He walked until the sun began to sink toward the horizon again and he found himself in a narrow valley with a stream running through it and on either side the ground rose up dark with pine and hemlock. He was tired now and his legs ached and he sat down beside the stream and drank from it, cupping the water in his hands, and it was cold and clear and tasted of stone. Around him the shadows were lengthening and the light was turning amber and the place had a quality of great age and silence, as if no one had been here for a long time and perhaps no one was meant to be.

He thought about turning back, about retracing his steps and returning to the house and to her and to the life that was waiting for him there. But something in him resisted that thought. Something in him wanted to keep going, to walk farther into that wilderness and to see what was on the other side of the next ridge and the ridge after that. There was a pull in that direction like the pull of a great gravity and he felt himself being drawn toward it even as he sat there by the stream knowing that he should turn back.

As darkness fell he made a small camp in a sheltered place and built a fire and sat beside it and watched the flames. He had no food with him and no water except what he could get from the stream and no blanket to cover himself. He thought about the three men and whether they were comfortable wherever they were or whether they too sat beside fires in the darkness with hunger gnawing at their bellies and cold settling into their bones.

In the night he heard sounds that might have been animals or might have been the wind in the trees and he lay awake listening to them and not being afraid exactly but being aware of something, some presence in the dark that was neither friendly nor hostile but simply indifferent. Toward dawn he fell into a troubled sleep and dreamed again but this time it was not the prophet and the sun that would not return but rather a long road stretching out ahead of him and at the far end of it a figure that was always just out of reach no matter how far he walked.

When he woke it was full light and the fire had burned down to ash. He rose and shook off the stiffness in his joints and drank again from the stream and began walking again. The road or the path or whatever it was continued on deeper into the wilderness and the trees grew thicker and taller and the light that filtered down through them was green and dim. He began to wonder if he had been walking in circles, if perhaps the land was leading him back toward where he had come from, but he continued on anyway because the alternative was to stop and he couldn't bring himself to do that.

By midday he had come to a place where the trees opened up and he could see a wider view of the land. It fell away before him in great folded valleys and ridges and in the far distance he could see mountains rising up blue against the sky. It was beautiful and terrible and he stood there for a long time just looking at it and feeling the vastness of it and the smallness of his own place in it.

He continued on and as he walked he began to think less about her and the house and his responsibilities and more about simply the act of walking, the placing of one foot before the other, the rhythm of it, the way it quieted his mind and made him aware only of his body and the land beneath his feet and the sky above him. It was almost peaceful in a way, almost like prayer, and he found that he wanted nothing more than to continue on like this, walking deeper into the wilderness, letting the world he had come from fade away behind him until it was nothing but a dream that he was gradually forgetting.

But as evening approached again he began to notice something that pulled him back from that reverie. There were signs of habitation, old signs but signs nonetheless. A chimney that had fallen into ruin. The overgrown remains of a clearing where someone had once tried to live. A graveyard with stones so old that the names on them had been worn away by wind and rain. He stood among those graves for a long time and read the dates that he could still make out, dates from a century before, people who had come to this place and tried to make a life and had failed or died or simply given up and moved on.

He thought about what it meant to put down roots in a place and to try to make something permanent when everything was temporary and fleeting. He thought about the child that was growing in her belly and the responsibility that that represented and the way that responsibility was pulling him back toward the house like a rope around his neck. He sat down among the graves as darkness fell and he wept, though he didn't know why, and when he was done weeping he rose and began to walk back the way he had come.

It took him all night to retrace his steps and it was dawn again when he reached the town and the streets were just beginning to come alive with people going about their business. He walked through the town without seeing anyone he knew and without anyone seeming to notice him. He was aware of how he must look, covered in mud and dust, with his clothes torn and his face unshaven and his eyes wild. He was aware of the gulf that had opened between him and the world of the living, the distance that had been created by his absence and his walking and his sitting among the graves.

When he reached the house it was midday and the sun was high and white and hot. He could see her through the window, sitting in a chair by the window, and she was looking out at the road as if she had been waiting for him. When she saw him she rose and came to the door and opened it and looked at him with an expression that contained both anger and relief and something else that he couldn't name.

Where have you been? she said.

Walking.

For how long?

A day. Two days. I don't know. I lost track.

She took his arm and pulled him inside and closed the door. In the light of the house he could see the strain on her face, the way the worry had etched lines around her eyes and mouth. She led him upstairs and helped him wash and change his clothes and then brought him food and he ate without tasting it. When he was done she sat on the edge of the bed and looked at him.

You can't do that again, she said.

I know.

I can't do this alone.

I know that too.

So what are we going to do?

He looked at her and he thought about the graves and the ruins and the mountains in the distance and all of it seemed very far away now, belonging to another person, another life that he had touched briefly and then left behind. He took her hand and held it and felt the slight trembling of it, the fragility and the strength both at once.

We'll go to the city, he said. We'll go to your sister.

You'll ask her?

I'll ask her.

She closed her eyes and he could see the relief washing over her face like a tide and he held her hand and sat with her in the gathering darkness and he thought about the three men and the bearded one and the river and all of it was receding now like something from a dream, becoming more and more indistinct until it was nothing but a memory of something that perhaps had never happened at all.
