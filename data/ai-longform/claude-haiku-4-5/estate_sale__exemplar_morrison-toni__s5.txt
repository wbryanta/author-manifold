# 

The living room smells like other people now. That's what strikes her first when she lets herself in—not the absence of her mother, which she'd prepared for in the way you prepare for rain by checking the forecast while the sky remains stubbornly clear—but the presence of strangers' anticipation hanging in the air like humidity before a storm. The sectional sofa, the one her mother bought in 1987 and covered with a quilted throw because the upholstery was a shade of mauve that showed everything, has been tagged with a red sticker that reads "SOLD" in aggressive capital letters. Someone will sit on it. Someone whose life has nothing to do with her mother will place their body in the exact shape her mother's body made, year after year, for thirty-six years.

The estate sale company had arrived on Saturday to catalog everything. They were efficient, those young people with their tablets and their practiced smiles. The woman who led them through the house—her name was Crystal, which struck Dorothy as both generous and slightly cruel, as if the universe had a sense of humor about diminishment—had examined the mahogany dining table her mother inherited from her own mother and written something on her screen. Dorothy wanted to ask what she was writing. Was it worth something? Was it nothing? Was it the kind of thing people wanted, or the kind of thing that filled up storage units alongside exercise bikes no one used and bread makers still in their boxes?

She'd decided not to ask. The not-asking had seemed like the right choice at the time, some kind of dignity preserved through ignorance. Now, standing in her mother's living room at two o'clock on a Tuesday, watching two men with work gloves carry out the coffee table—the specific coffee table where her mother set down her coffee every morning for forty years, the table that had absorbed the rings of approximately fourteen thousand cups despite the coasters her mother kept fanned across its surface like a broken hand—Dorothy wondered if she'd made a mistake. Maybe knowing would have been better. Maybe numbers attached to things made them more bearable.

"Excuse me, ma'am, we need to get the sofa through here."

The man addressing her was young, early twenties perhaps, with the kind of earnest expression that suggested he took his work seriously, which was either admirable or heartbreaking depending on how she wanted to think about it. Dorothy stepped aside. He and another man she hadn't noticed until that moment—they seemed to multiply when you looked away—began wrestling the sectional toward the doorway. It didn't want to go. The corner piece caught on the door frame and one of them cursed softly.

"It went in," Dorothy said. "So it should come out."

"Things are funny that way," the earnest one said, breathing hard. "Went in one way, comes out another. Physics."

He said it like he'd just discovered physics himself, like it was a thing he'd noticed and wanted to share. Dorothy almost smiled. Almost. The smile got stuck somewhere in her chest like a swallowed word. Her mother would have smiled at him. Her mother smiled at young people and gave them iced tea in summer even when she didn't know them, which was often because her mother had a way of attracting strays, both human and otherwise. There had been a period, the early 1990s, when her mother's house had functioned as an unofficial halfway house for people between lives. Dorothy's father had called it a quality and a liability in the same tone of voice, which was the tone he used for most things her mother did.

The sofa came free suddenly, and the men maneuvered it through the door with the awkward grace of people learning something in real time. Dorothy watched it disappear out into the afternoon. Gone. Just like that. No ceremony, no moment of transition. One moment it was where it had always been, and the next it existed somewhere else, or nowhere yet, suspended in the moment between houses, between lives.

She'd taken a day off work for this. She'd told her boss she had a personal matter to attend to, which was true in the way that lies are true—accurate in the facts but misleading in the emphasis. A personal matter. Her mother had died three weeks ago, and today was the day they were selling her things. That seemed like more than a personal matter. That seemed like the kind of thing that should cancel everything, stop time, make the world pause and acknowledge. But the world didn't work that way. The world kept moving. Her job was still waiting for her. Her apartment in Columbus was still waiting. Her life—the one she'd built at a careful distance from this town, this house, this specific configuration of objects and memory—was still waiting, patient and unchanged.

The dining room table went next. This took longer. It was large, solid oak, and it required four men. Dorothy watched from the hallway, leaning against the wall in the exact spot where she'd stood countless times as a child, waiting for her mother to finish breakfast so they could go somewhere, or waiting for her mother to come home so they could start waiting for dinner. The men carried the table horizontally, like pallbearers with their burden. One of them—the earnest one—looked back at her as they maneuvered it through the doorway.

"This one's beautiful," he said. "Real wood. These are getting rare."

"It's old," Dorothy said. She meant it as a statement of fact, but it came out sounding like an accusation. The man nodded anyway, undeterred by her tone in the way young people often are, as if rudeness were just another environmental factor to work around.

Her mother had hosted Thanksgiving dinner at that table for forty-one years. Dorothy had a sudden, specific memory of being seven years old, watching her mother arrange dishes on the table's surface, rotating them slightly to catch the light better, adjusting the placement of the salt shaker with her thumb and forefinger until it was precisely where she wanted it. The memory was so clear it was almost painful. She could see her mother's hands—which had been young then, unmarked by age—and she could smell the turkey cooking, that particular smell that meant the year was ending and the family was coming.

Except the family wasn't a family anymore. It was scattered people who sent holiday cards, and sometimes they showed up and sometimes they didn't. Her mother had continued the dinners anyway, even when it was just the two of them, her and Dorothy's father in his last years, sitting at that enormous table like a couple on a ship that had lost most of its crew. After her father died, her mother had still set the table properly, with the good plates and the cloth napkins folded into triangles.

"All right, the bedroom set next," a voice called from upstairs.

Dorothy hadn't realized the sale company had begun upstairs. The thought of strangers carrying her mother's bedroom down the stairs—the bed where she'd slept for thirty-five years, the dresser with its collection of small objects, the mirror—sent something through Dorothy that wasn't quite panic but was anxiety's close cousin. She stayed in the hallway. She didn't go up. She'd already said goodbye to those things in the way that mattered, which was to say she hadn't said goodbye at all, had simply avoided thinking about them.

The afternoon was getting on toward four o'clock. The men were moving faster now, falling into a rhythm. In and out, in and out. The house was becoming empty. You could see it happening. The rooms were changing size as the furniture left them, becoming cavernous and strange, like caves or the inside of a mouth. Light moved across the walls differently. The shadows fell in new places. The afternoon sun came through the window at an angle it had never quite been able to reach before, and it illuminated dust motes in the shape of a pattern that might have meant something or nothing.

Dorothy's mother had believed in patterns. She'd read tarot cards—not seriously, not with the sense that the universe was actually communicating through cardboard rectangles, but with the sense that patterns were how the human mind made meaning, and meaning was a reasonable thing to be looking for. She'd had a deck that she kept in a silk pouch in the nightstand. Dorothy had found it when she was sorting through her mother's things, and she'd almost thrown it away but hadn't. It was in a box now, one of the boxes that would come with her when she closed up the house.

"We need to move the bedroom furniture through the master bath," the earnest man said, suddenly appearing in the hallway. "Is that okay? The staircase is too tight going the other way."

"Do whatever you need to do," Dorothy said. She meant it to sound permissive, but it came out sounding resigned, which was closer to the truth anyway. "This isn't my house anymore."

He looked at her with an expression that might have been sympathy, and she turned away before he could offer anything—a comment, a platitude, a smile. She went instead into the kitchen, the one room the sale company hadn't touched yet. The kitchen things weren't being sold. Her mother's will had stipulated that the kitchen items be given to Dorothy's brother, who apparently wanted his mother's measuring cups and mixing bowls and the cast-iron skillet she'd been seasoning since the 1970s. Dorothy didn't want these things. Didn't want to keep them in her apartment in Columbus, didn't want to look at them and think about her mother's hands using them, didn't want to inherit the weight of all those Sunday dinners and Thursday night casseroles and breakfast pancakes made on Saturday mornings when Dorothy was young enough to think pancakes were a sufficient reason for the day to exist.

But her brother lived in California and had said he'd figure out how to get the things shipped, and Dorothy had said okay, and now they were wrapped in newspaper and stacked in boxes on the kitchen counter. Dorothy opened one. A blue bowl. A wooden spoon. A measuring cup with faded red numbers on its side. She closed the box again without touching the objects. Touching them would make them real, and real was a state she was trying to avoid as much as possible.

Through the kitchen window, she could see the front yard. The estate sale company had set up tables outside with items for browsing—books, lamps, picture frames. A woman was looking at the frames, holding them up to the light. She had gray hair, Dorothy's age, maybe older. She was examining a particular frame with the kind of attention that suggested she was considering whether to buy it. In the frame—Dorothy could see this from the window—was a photograph. She couldn't make out the details from this distance, but she knew what it was. It was her mother with her three children, taken in the front yard of this very house sometime in the early 1980s. They were all young in it. All unaware of how things would go.

The woman bought the frame. Dorothy watched her put it in a canvas bag and move on to look at other things. That photograph—the evidence of a moment that had seemed ordinary at the time but was now irrevocably past, now owned by a stranger who might hang it on her wall or might decide it wasn't what she wanted and donate it somewhere, or throw it away—that photograph was now property. It had been transferred. It had moved from Dorothy's mother's dresser to the wall of a woman she would never meet.

The back door opened and the earnest man came through, followed by two others carrying her mother's bed frame. The mattress was wrapped in plastic. The headboard had been dismantled into pieces. It was all very efficient. Her mother would have appreciated the efficiency. Her mother had been an organized person, had labeled things and kept systems. Dorothy had inherited some of this trait but had mostly rejected it as a form of denial, a way of pretending that if you could organize your physical space you could also organize the messier parts of living. She'd spent her adult life in a kind of deliberate chaos that was really just stubbornness wearing a different name.

"We'll be out of your hair in about an hour," the earnest man said. "Got a few more pieces from upstairs and then we'll call it. The company will probably send someone by tomorrow to do the final walkthrough, make sure we didn't miss anything."

Dorothy nodded. She should probably offer them water. That's what her mother would have done. She opened the refrigerator—still stocked with things her mother had bought in the final week of her life, the week before she went to the hospital and didn't come back. Orange juice. Butter. Milk that was probably bad by now. Dorothy grabbed a pitcher and filled it with water from the tap. She got glasses from the cabinet—not her mother's good glasses, the everyday ones—and held them out.

"We appreciate that," one of the other men said. He drank his water quickly and efficiently, like a man who had learned not to waste time. "Big house. Big sale. Lot of emotional weight in that."

Dorothy hadn't asked him to comment on the emotional weight. She hadn't asked him to psychoanalyze her mother's belongings or her relationship to them. But here he was, offering his observation like a gift. She took it the way she'd taken his water-drinking—with a nod that committed her to nothing.

By five o'clock, the house was functionally empty. The major pieces of furniture were gone. What remained was the accumulated debris of a life—the things that hadn't sold, the things that weren't worth selling, the things that Crystal the young woman with the tablet had decided belonged to the category of "miscellaneous" or "unsaleable" or "better as donation." Some of these things had been piled in the garage. Others were already at the Goodwill. And some, a small stack of boxes, were still in the living room waiting to be sorted.

Dorothy stood in that living room as the sun moved toward evening, and she could hear the absence the way you could hear a sound. The house sounded different without furniture. It sounded cavernous and strange. It sounded like it didn't belong to anyone. It sounded like the kind of place that would soon be sold—the house itself, the brick and mortar and the yard with its old oak tree—and would become someone else's memory. Someone else's childhood. Someone else's afternoon light moving across the walls in patterns that meant nothing and everything.

She sat down on the floor, right there in the middle of the living room where the sectional had been. The carpet was still there—the sale company wasn't touching the house itself, just its contents—and it was worn in the place where her mother had sat, where all that weight had rested for all those years. Dorothy lay back on the carpet. It smelled like her mother. It smelled like dust and time and all the afternoons her mother had spent in this room, waiting for something or someone, or maybe not waiting at all but just existing, just being the kind of person who sits in a living room and watches the light change.

The boxes were waiting. The house was waiting. Her life in Columbus was waiting. But for this one moment, Dorothy let herself remain where she was—on the floor of her mother's empty house, surrounded by absence, surrounded by the space where things used to be, breathing in the dust of all the years that had come and gone without her being quite ready for them to leave.

When she finally stood up, her knees creaked in a way that reminded her she was no longer young, which was another way of saying she was becoming her mother, which was another way of saying time was doing what time had always done: moving forward with the kind of inevitability that made all resistance seem not just futile but almost charming in its naïveté. She picked up the boxes. She locked the front door behind her. She drove back to Columbus through the evening, listening to the radio, not thinking about anything, or thinking about everything, or thinking about the space between those two states where most of life actually happened.
