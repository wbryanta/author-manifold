# The Operator

The voice on the line belonged to a woman who always called on Thursday nights at eight o'clock, and there was something in the particular timbre of her hello—a slight catch, as if she had been waiting by the phone all day—that made Margaret pause for just a fraction of a second before connecting her through to the man in Colorado. After three months of facilitating these calls, Margaret had begun to anticipate the moment when the switchboard would light up with that familiar number from the house outside Asheville, North Carolina. She would set down her coffee, straighten in her chair, and prepare her fingers on the plug board with the attentiveness of a concert pianist.

The woman always asked Margaret to connect her to the same number, speaking politely, almost apologetically, as if she understood the minor inconvenience she was causing. "Five-oh-three area code, please. Boulder. The number is 442-7861." Margaret had long ago stopped needing to write these numbers down. They had become as familiar to her as her own telephone extension, as deeply lodged in her consciousness as the route she walked to work each morning.

The man—she had never heard his voice give a name, though the woman always spoke to him as if he were somehow infinitely dear—answered quickly. Never busy. Never unavailable. As though he too was waiting by the phone, watching the clock, counting the hours between one Thursday and the next. Margaret would watch the red light blink on her board, indicating an active connection, and she would not disconnect. This was against regulation. She knew this. Her supervisor, Mrs. Halstead, had made the rules abundantly clear on Margaret's first day: complete the connection, disconnect the call once confirmed, move on to the next. Efficient. Professional. No lingering.

But Margaret had lingered. She had lingered for nearly fourteen weeks now.

The conversations were never long—rarely more than ten or fifteen minutes—but they were dense with a kind of aching tenderness that made Margaret feel like an eavesdropper at a sacred ceremony. The woman would ask about his work, his mother's health, whether he had finally gone to see that film he wanted to see. The man would respond with questions of his own, his voice deepening with genuine concern, and there would be silences between them—comfortable silences, Margaret understood—in which nothing was said but everything seemed to be communicated. Once, the woman had laughed at something he said, and the sound had been so pure, so unguarded, that Margaret had felt tears prick the corners of her eyes.

She had never married. She was forty-three years old and had lived alone in the same small apartment for twelve years. Her mother, before she died, had suggested that Margaret was too particular, too inclined to observe the world rather than participate in it. "You would make a fine wife for the right man," her mother had said, "if you would only look up from your book long enough to see him." But Margaret had never been able to explain that she preferred the lives she observed to any life she might construct for herself. Books, music, and overheard conversations—these were the textures from which she had woven her inner life, rich and complete in their incompleteness.

Until the Thursday evening calls began.

She did not know the names of either of these voices. The man never identified himself, and the woman never used his name on the phone. Yet Margaret had constructed elaborate narratives around them. She imagined he was a mathematics professor, perhaps, or an engineer—something cerebral and precise. She imagined the woman was an artist of some kind, or a musician. She imagined them separated by circumstance rather than inclination, that they had once been together and could not be again, and that these weekly calls were a kind of lifeline between two drowning people.

Or perhaps they were not separated at all. Perhaps he was married to someone else, and she was married to someone else, and these calls were stolen moments of truth in an otherwise false existence. The thought made Margaret uncomfortable when it crossed her mind, and she pushed it away.

What mattered was the sound of his voice saying her name—she learned that his name was Helen; he called her Helen, sometimes just Helen, sometimes "my Helen"—with such profound affection that Margaret understood, in her bones, what it meant to be loved. She had never experienced it herself, this particular quality of being valued so completely by another person, but she could hear it in the spaces between their words.

The weeks passed. Spring became summer. Margaret began to dread her days off, the silence of her apartment, the absence of the switchboard and its constant small dramas of connection and disconnection. She arrived at work early on Thursday afternoons and left late, lingering at her station even after her shift ended, hoping that her replacement would be someone with a generous interpretation of the rules. She started to feel that the calls sustained something in her that she had not realized was depleting. She felt purposeful when Helen called. She felt necessary.

In July, Mrs. Halstead called Margaret into her office. Margaret's hands trembled as she knocked on the door. She was certain that she had been discovered, that Mrs. Halstead had been monitoring her work, that she was about to be fired for violations of protocol. But Mrs. Halstead only complimented her on her efficiency and asked whether she would be willing to take on a training responsibility with some of the newer operators. Margaret accepted, relieved and deflated in equal measure. She had not been caught. There would be no intervention. She was free to continue.

The Thursday calls became the architecture around which Margaret organized her entire existence. She made plans that never interfered with eight o'clock. She declined invitations to dinner with her colleague Ruth, who had begun to express concern about Margaret's social isolation. "You spend too much time listening to other people," Ruth said one afternoon, smoking a cigarette with the intensity of someone conducting an argument. "You need to live your own life." Margaret had not known how to explain that she felt she was living a life, that these voices had become more real to her than any of her own direct experiences.

At home, she began to keep a notebook. Not recordings—she would never do anything so invasive—but observations. The way Helen's breathing changed when she was emotional. The small hum the man made while he was thinking before responding to a question. In November, Helen mentioned a book she was reading, and Margaret went to the library and took it out, reading it with the sense that she was reading a message sent directly to her. It was a collection of poems by Mary Oliver, and she underlined passages that seemed to speak to what she imagined was the nature of Helen and her beloved's connection.

"Tell me, what is it you plan to do with your one wild and precious life?" she read, and she thought of the man in Colorado, and the woman in North Carolina, throwing lifelines to each other across the vast distance that separated them.

Winter arrived early that year. By December, Margaret's personal life had contracted to nearly nothing. She saw her sister twice—obligatory family occasions that she endured with impatience. She stopped attending the book club that met on Thursday nights. She called in sick to work only once, and that was for genuine illness; she could not afford to miss a Thursday call, could not bear the thought of not knowing what Helen would say, what news she would bring from her life. The man's life remained more mysterious—he revealed less, seemed more guarded—but Margaret felt that she understood the shape of his tenderness from his silences, the way he asked questions and truly waited for the answers.

January came cold and without snow. February was lonely in the way that February always is. And then March arrived, and with it, the first Thursday evening that Margaret had to acknowledge something was shifting. Helen called at eight o'clock as always, and Margaret connected her through, listening with an intensity that had by now become almost unbearable in its focus. But the conversation was different. Helen's voice was strained. She was crying, though she tried to hide it. The man's voice, when he spoke, had taken on a note of urgency, almost desperation.

"I know," Helen said. "I know. But we don't have another choice."

Margaret's hands grew cold on the switchboard. Whatever narrative she had constructed, whatever story she had been telling herself about their connection, suddenly felt fragile and temporary. She thought of her supervisor's rules, the regulation against lingering, and understood for the first time that she should have followed them. The knowledge that she could not fully access what was being conveyed, that she was hearing only half of a goodbye, perhaps, or half of a beginning, made her feel suddenly and acutely the invasion of her own attention.

The call ended after sixteen minutes. Margaret disconnected, her hands shaking slightly, and sat without moving for several minutes. She did not go home that night. She stayed at her station until midnight, though no calls came that required her attention.

The following Thursday arrived as Thursdays do, inexorable and expected. Margaret was at her post at 7:55. She had brought a cup of tea to help settle her nerves, though she did not drink it. At 8:00, the lights on the switchboard remained dark. At 8:05, still nothing. At 8:15, Margaret began to feel something constricting in her chest, a physical sensation of loss that surprised her with its violence.

At 8:22, the familiar number lit up.

Margaret's pulse quickened. She answered professionally, waiting for Helen's voice. But it was not Helen's voice that came through. It was a man's voice—not the man in Colorado, but someone different, older, with a note of formal distress in his words.

"I'm calling to reach James Morrison," the voice said. "This is his brother. There's been an accident. I need him to call home immediately."

Margaret's mouth had gone dry. She transferred the call without speaking, hands moving on the switchboard with the mechanical efficiency of someone sleepwalking. It was only after she had disconnected that she realized the voice had used a name. James Morrison. The man in Colorado was named James Morrison.

She did not know why this knowledge hurt so much. She had always known that he was someone with a name, a real person with a history and a life and, apparently, a family. But knowing his name made him suddenly solid in a way that her invented narrative could not accommodate. James Morrison. Helen. Two people with actual lives existing somewhere beyond the reach of her attention.

The following Thursday, Margaret called in sick. She could not face the switchboard, could not bear to wait and listen for a call that might not come. She told herself that James Morrison had probably returned to North Carolina. That Helen had probably taken him home. That whatever had been happening in their stolen moments had resolved into something concrete and real, something that no longer required the services of a telephone operator to facilitate.

But she was not foolish enough to believe this entirely. She knew only that the world had shifted, and she could not predict what would emerge from the rupture.

The next Thursday, she returned to work. She had revised her expectations. She was prepared for the absence, steeled herself for it. But at 8:00, the familiar number lit up again.

This time, it was Helen's voice, but it sounded different. Quieter. Smaller.

"Good evening," Helen said. "I'd like to place a call to Boulder, Colorado. The number is 442-7861."

Margaret connected her automatically, and waited for the man's voice to answer. But the line rang and rang, unanswered. After six rings, the operator on the other end came back on the line.

"I'm sorry, ma'am. There's no answer."

"Perhaps if you try again?" Helen's voice had taken on a desperate quality.

Margaret, listening against regulation, heard the click and the dial tone, heard the phone ring again on the other end. Still no answer. She imagined James Morrison gone from that apartment, from that phone, from whatever life had existed before whatever had happened in March.

"I'm afraid there's still no answer," the operator said.

"Thank you," Helen whispered. "Thank you for trying."

The call disconnected. Margaret sat with her hands on the plug board, staring at nothing.

She told herself that she would not listen for the call again next Thursday. She would not anticipate it. She would treat it as any other call, a moment of connection to be facilitated and forgotten. She would return to the rules that Mrs. Halstead had outlined on her first day. Professional. Efficient. Disconnected.

But at 7:55 the following Thursday, Margaret's hands were already moving on the switchboard. At 8:00, the familiar number lit up. And when Helen asked for the same number in Boulder, Margaret connected her through, and waited—in violation of every rule, in violation of her own better judgment—to hear what would happen next.

The phone rang. Four times. Five.

On the sixth ring, someone answered.

It was not James Morrison. It was a woman's voice, unfamiliar, with a note of curiosity in it. "Hello?"

There was a silence. Margaret imagined Helen on the other end, trying to decide whether to speak, whether to explain.

"I'm sorry," Helen said finally. "I must have reached a wrong number."

"Wait," the other woman said. "Wait, who is this?"

But Helen had already disconnected.

Margaret sat in the aftermath of the call, her eyes burning with unshed tears. She did not know what had happened in Colorado. She did not know if James Morrison had found a new life, a new woman, a new name for his Thursday nights. She did not know if Helen had been calling to reach a ghost or to inflict pain or to finally, fully, let go.

She knew only that the architecture of her week had collapsed entirely, and that she would have to find another way to organize her time, another set of voices to listen to, another story to invent around the incomplete knowledge that her work as an operator allowed her to access.

But in the meantime, she did not disconnect. She held the line open, listening to the silence that followed Helen's voice, understanding finally that some connections, once made, were never truly severed. They simply changed shape. They became memory. They became the space between one moment and the next, between one person's life and another's, the vast and unbridgeable distance that made the act of connection—temporary, fragile, and imperfect as it was—the only testimony to the fact that anyone had ever lived at all.
