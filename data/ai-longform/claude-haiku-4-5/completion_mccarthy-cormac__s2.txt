They tore at his clothes and his flesh and he was borne down under their weight and he cried out until he had no voice left to cry with and when at last they released him he lay broken and bleeding in that absolute darkness and the stars that hung above had moved in their courses and were the stars of yet another season altogether and he could not say how long he had lain there or whether he was alive or dead.

He woke gasping and she was standing over him in the darkness of their room and he could see the pale oval of her face above him and her hand on her swollen belly and she was looking at him with something like pity in her eyes.

You was dreaming again, she said.

He sat up slowly and wiped his face with the back of his arm. His shirt was soaked with sweat and he could taste blood in his mouth though he had no wound that he could find.

What time is it?

She didn't answer. She moved to the window and looked out at the street below where a few lamps burned and a cart rattled past pulled by an old horse. The night was nearly gone.

You need to stop drinking, she said.

I ain't been drinking.

You been drinking every night. I can smell it on you. It gives you these nightmares.

He lay back on the bed and stared at the ceiling where the plaster was water-stained and cracked like the dried bed of some ancient river. Somewhere in the building a child was crying. Dogs barked in the distance. The whole city seemed to be made of voices and noise that would never cease.

When did you come to bed? he said.

An hour ago. Maybe more. You didn't notice me come in. You was already sleeping but thrashing around like a man possessed.

She came back to the bed and lay down beside him but not touching. He could feel the heat of her body and smell the soap she used and underneath it something else, something he couldn't name. She breathed slowly and he thought she might go back to sleep but then she spoke again.

My mother says I shouldn't have married you.

He didn't respond. This was an old conversation and it had nowhere new to go.

She says you got a devil in you. That it runs in your blood.

My mother's dead, he said.

I know that. She's been dead these fifteen years. But that don't change what's true about you.

He turned away from her and looked toward the window where the sky was beginning to lighten at its edge, a thin line of gray above the rooftops and chimneys of the city. Somewhere a church bell was ringing, marking the hour though he had lost count of what hour it might be.

By and by she slept and he lay awake waiting for the full light of day to come and wondering what had brought him to this place, to this bed in this rented room above a butcher's shop, with a woman he had married in haste and a child coming that he had not asked for and did not want. He thought of the three figures he had seen in his dream, moving single file across the burnt earth, and he wondered where they were going and whether they would ever arrive at wherever it was they sought or whether they would walk forever into an emptiness that had no end to it.

When the sun finally rose it was a weak and bloodless thing, filtered through the coal smoke that hung over the city like a curse. He rose and dressed himself in the clothes from the day before and went downstairs to the street. The butcher was already at his work, hacking at a carcass hung from a hook, and he nodded at him without speaking. The sidewalk was crowded with people hurrying in all directions, each of them intent upon their own errands and destinations, and he moved among them like a man in a fever, not knowing where he was going but needing to move nonetheless.

He walked for hours. The city sprawled before him in all directions, a great gray organism of brick and stone and iron. He passed factories with their chimneys belching black smoke and warehouses with their doors standing open and workers moving in and out like ants attending to some incomprehensible labor. He passed tenements where women hung laundry from the windows and children played in the streets, their games played out against a landscape of desolation and decay. He passed churches where the devout were gathering for morning services and saloons where men stood at the bar with their glasses raised to their lips and their eyes fixed on nothing.

By afternoon he found himself at the docks where the river ran thick and brown and slow, heavy with silt and refuse. Great ships were moored there, their hulls black and barnacled, and men were working to load and unload them, hauling cargo in nets and on their backs. The air was thick with the smell of salt and rot and something else, something fecal and ancient. He stood at the railing and watched the water move past and thought about stepping into it, about letting himself sink down into its murk and darkness and oblivion.

A hand touched his shoulder and he turned to find an old man standing there, dressed in rags and with a face like weathered leather.

You thinking about it? the old man said.

Thinking about what?

The old man gestured with a twisted hand at the water. About going in.

No, he said, though it was a lie.

I seen plenty of men stand at this rail and think about it. Some of them go through with it and some of them don't. The ones that do, they don't know what they're doing. The water don't kill you as quick as you think. It takes a long time and it's full of pain. Better to find another way.

He didn't ask what other way the old man meant. He turned back to look at the water and when he turned again the old man was gone.

He made his way back toward the boarding house as the sun was setting, painting the sky in shades of red and orange that looked unnatural, like a fire burning somewhere beyond the edge of the world. The streets were crowded now with people coming from their work, women with shopping bags and men with their caps pulled low and children running between the legs of the adults like rats in a grain store. A woman bumped into him and he steadied her and she looked at him with eyes full of something he couldn't quite read and then hurried on.

When he reached the boarding house the butcher was closing up his shop below, pulling down the metal gate across the windows. He climbed the narrow stairs to their room and found her sitting in the darkness by the window, her hands folded on her belly.

You been gone all day, she said.

I needed to walk.

You needed to drink, you mean. I can smell it on you.

It was true. He had stopped at three saloons at least, maybe more. The days seemed to run together now, indistinguishable from one another, marked only by the rising and setting of that pale sun.

I'm leaving, she said.

He looked at her but couldn't see her face in the darkness. The shape of her was there, large and strange with the weight of the child, but her features were obscured.

Where would you go?

Back to my mother's. She's made a place for me. She says I can stay as long as I need to.

And the child?

It's mine, she said. Not yours. You never wanted it anyway.

He didn't argue. It was true. He had never wanted the child. He had never wanted her either, though in the early days he had convinced himself he did, or that he might, given time. But time had not made it better. Time had only made it worse, had filled the days with a kind of suffocation and dread.

When are you going?

Tomorrow. My father's sending a cart.

He nodded slowly. He should feel something about this, he thought. Relief perhaps, or grief, or some mixture of the two. But he felt nothing at all, only a kind of numbness that seemed to have spread through him like a cancer.

You can keep the room, she said. The landlord knows you're good for the rent.

He almost laughed at that. He was not good for the rent. He had not been good for anything for a long time. But he didn't say this. He sat down on the bed and she remained at the window, not looking at him, and they sat together in silence as the city darkened around them and the stars began to appear in the gaps between the buildings.

In the morning she was gone. He woke to find the space beside him cold and empty. There was a note on the table by the window, written in her careful hand, saying only that she had gone early to catch the cart and that there was money in the jar on the mantle for the rent. He crumpled the note and threw it out the window and watched it fall through the gray light to the street below where it landed in the gutter and was immediately trodden upon by a passing pedestrian.

He lay in bed for three days. He did not eat. He did not rise except to use the chamber pot. The landlord came once and he sent him away with promises he knew were lies. The sun rose and fell and rose again and still he lay there, staring at the ceiling, feeling his body grow thin and weak. He thought about the dream and about the prophet and about the sun that had not returned. He thought about the three men moving through the burnt landscape. He thought about stepping into the river.

On the fourth day hunger drove him up and out. He shaved with a blade so dull it scraped at his face like a rough stone and dressed in his cleanest clothes and went out into the world again. The city seemed different to him, or perhaps he was different to the city. The people moved around him as if he were a ghost, as if they could pass through him without seeing him. He made his way to a restaurant where the food was cheap and the men who worked there did not ask questions. He ate a bowl of stew that contained things he could not identify and drank three cups of black coffee and felt almost human again.

From there he found himself moving through the streets without any clear purpose, following the turns and angles of the city as if they held some message he was meant to read. He passed the place where he had first met her, a church social where she had been serving refreshments and he had taken a cup of punch from her hands. She had looked at him with an openness that he had later learned was merely her nature, not anything specific to him. He had mistaken it for something else and she had mistaken his silence for depth and they had married and here they were, or rather, here he was, alone.

The afternoon wore on. He found himself in a part of the city he did not recognize, streets that twisted back on themselves and dead-ended in blank walls. The buildings here were older, brick that had been blackened by decades of coal smoke, windows that were dark and empty. He passed a church that looked as if it had been abandoned, its doors chained shut and its windows boarded over. Beyond it was a cemetery with stones that leaned at odd angles, carved with the names of the dead.

He wandered among the graves for an hour or more. Many of the stones were so old that the inscriptions had worn away to nothing. Others were recent, the marble still white and unweathed. He stopped at one that bore the name of a child who had lived only three days. The date was recent, only a year or two past. He stood there wondering about the parents of that child, about what it must have been like to carry such a small thing for such a short time and then to put it in the ground.

When he finally left the cemetery the sun was setting again, painting the sky in those unnatural colors. He retraced his steps through the twisted streets and found himself back at the docks. The ships were lit now with lanterns, and the work had ceased for the day. The water was black and moving with a thickness that suggested depths below depths.

He stood at the railing and thought about the old man and what he had said about the water and the pain. He thought about how long it would take and whether he would have time to regret it before the darkness took him. He thought about his wife and her mother and the child that might even now be arriving into the world, without his presence, without his name. He thought about the three men in his dream and wondered if they had ever found what they were seeking or if they too were still walking, still moving west along a river that had no end.

A voice called out behind him and he turned to find a man in the uniform of the harbor master.

Can't be standing around here, the man said. This is private property.

He nodded and walked away from the railing. He did not know where he would go. The boarding house seemed impossible, full of the ghost of the woman and the child he had never wanted. The saloons were there if he needed them, and the river was always there, waiting with its patient darkness.

He walked for hours as the night deepened. The city transformed itself under the cover of darkness, became something more sinister and strange. The people he passed seemed less real, or more real perhaps, stripped of their daytime pretense. He saw men who looked like thieves and women who looked like they had given up everything. He saw children sleeping in doorways and old men with the hunted look of those who have nowhere to rest their heads.

By the time the sun began to lighten the sky again he had walked to the edge of the city, to the place where the buildings grew smaller and scattered and the land opened out into fields and marshes. He could see birds rising in the distance, and beyond them hills that seemed to suggest the existence of some country beyond this one. He thought about walking out into those fields and never coming back, about disappearing into whatever lay beyond the reach of the city.

But he turned back instead and made his way toward the boarding house. The sun was fully up now and the city was stirring to life again. The butcher was opening his shop, hosing down the sidewalk with red water. He climbed the stairs and entered the room and lay down on the bed and closed his eyes. Outside the window the city carried on with its business, indifferent to him and his suffering, and within him something twisted and turned like a creature trapped in a cage.

He lay there for a long time, listening to the sounds of the city, to the voices and the carts and the bells, and he wondered if he would ever be free of this place or if he would remain here forever, suspended between the waking world and the dreams that came in the darkness. And if he remained, he thought, perhaps eventually he would forget what it was like to want something else, and in the forgetting he might find a kind of peace.
