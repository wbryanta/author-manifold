# IRRIGATION

The oldest brother came down from the house carrying the wrench and a length of pipe and he set these things in the dirt beside the concrete basin where the younger man was already bent to his work. The day was hot and without wind and the light lay upon the valley floor like something poured. Grasshoppers rose before them in small panicked clouds as they moved through the dried weeds alongside the ditch.

What's the break? the older said.

Quarter mile up. Where that old cottonwood leans across. I could see the dirt all washed out from here.

You sure it's the main line?

Where else would it be?

The older man did not answer. He looked out over the orchard where the trees stood in their rows like something barely animate, their leaves hung gray with dust. The irrigation system his father had laid down forty years ago, PVC that had hardened and cracked in the sun, had been failing in sections for the past five years. Each spring they'd repair it. Each spring the breaks came more frequent.

You going to help or just stand there?

I'm coming.

They walked up through the orchard toward the cottonwood, the older brother leading with the pipe and wrench. The rows stretched away on either side and the peaches hung small and hard on the branches. An old irrigation valve stood beside one tree with its handle broken off at the stem. They passed it without comment though the younger brother looked at it.

When did you last see him? the older said.

Who?

Paulson.

Two weeks ago. Maybe three. He came by looking for work.

What did you tell him?

That we didn't have nothing to give him. He looked like he understood that.

They reached the cottonwood and found the break immediately. The line had split along a seam and water pulsed from the rupture in thin jets, darkening the earth. The older brother studied the break with his hands on his hips. The younger brother was already kneeling, running his fingers along the fracture.

How long has it been like this?

I don't know. Since yesterday maybe. I came up this morning and saw the water.

The older man crouched beside him. The pipe was brittle and the fracture was clean, as if it had simply given up. He pressed his thumb against the opening and felt the water run past his nail.

We're going to need more than a patch, he said.

I know.

He stood and looked back toward the house, invisible now behind the rise and the trees. The walk had taken twenty minutes. He looked at his watch. It was eight-thirty in the morning and already the heat was considerable.

I'll go back and get the coupling, he said. There's one in the shed.

The younger brother rose as well. I could go.

No. Stay here and start digging out around it. We'll need to work clean.

The older brother went back the way they'd come, walking steadily through the orchard. The peaches seemed to watch him with their small hard eyes. Two of the trees were dead, their limbs white and bare. He'd been meaning to cut them down for months. The whole western section, probably another acre, was dying the same slow death. The older the trees got, the more water they wanted and the less water there was to give them.

He found the coupling in the shed, a brass fitting that would couple two lengths of pipe. He collected also a hacksaw and a tube of PVC cement. He put these in a burlap sack and started back toward the cottonwood. Halfway there he stopped and stood among the trees and looked at the property spreading out before him. His grandfather had planted this orchard in nineteen-thirty-two, had hand-dug much of the original irrigation ditch. His father had expanded it, brought it into production. Now his father was dead three years and his brother wanted to sell and subdivide. Every month the assessor's value went up. Every month the fruit yield went down.

When he returned the younger brother had cleared an area of earth around the break, exposing the full length of the damaged section. He'd also stopped the flow by pinching the line at a point upstream, holding it kinked with a piece of two-by-four wedged under a root of the cottonwood. It was a good solution, makeshift but effective. The water had stopped running.

That's good, the older said.

Yeah, well. It won't hold forever.

It'll hold long enough.

They worked silently for a time, the older brother using the hacksaw to cut the pipe on either side of the break, the younger brother clearing away more dirt. When the cuts were made the older brother held the coupling up to the gap.

About right, he said.

He applied the cement carefully, working it into the joints with a small brush. The smell of it was sharp and chemical. They waited while it set, neither of them speaking. A raven called somewhere in the distance. The cottonwood leaves moved slightly though the older brother had said there was no wind, and he watched them move and wondered if he'd been wrong.

How long do you think? the younger said.

Ten minutes. Fifteen to be safe.

The younger brother sat in the dirt. How much did he say he'd give us?

Paulson?

No. The developer.

The older brother did not answer immediately. He set the brush down and looked at his hands, which were stained with cement. He looked at his brother. A million three hundred thousand, he said.

That's more than last time.

Yes.

How much more?

Two hundred thousand.

The younger brother whistled softly. That's something.

It is.

We could pay off the mortgage. We could do a lot of things.

We could.

So why are you still saying no?

The older brother looked back at the pipe. The cement was starting to set, turning opaque at the edges of the joint. I never said no. I said I wanted to think about it.

You've been thinking about it for two months.

I know how long I've been thinking about it.

And?

And what?

Have you thought about it?

The older brother rose and walked to the edge of the ditch, where the water sat in a still pool, thick with algae. He could see his reflection there, distorted and broken. I think about it all the time. I think about it when I wake up. I think about it when I'm working. I think about it at night.

That's not an answer.

No. It's not.

The younger brother came to stand beside him. He was three years younger, thirty-one years old. He'd lived in town for eight years now, working as a mechanic, coming back only when things needed fixing. When his wife left him he'd come back for almost a year. Now he came back only occasionally, and when he did his father's absence seemed to offend him, as if the old man had been inconsiderate in dying.

You want me to say it, the younger said.

Say what?

That the old man's gone. That there's nothing keeping us here anymore.

I know he's gone.

Do you? Because you act like you're waiting for him to come back, like he's going to come up from the house and tell you what to do.

The older brother said nothing. He was watching the water.

I've got a life in town, the younger continued. I've got people depending on me. I've got rent to pay and a truck payment and I'm tired, you understand? I'm tired of coming back here and pretending like this matters.

It does matter.

How? How does it matter? We're losing money. We lose money every year. The equipment's falling apart. The house needs a new roof. The pump's from nineteen sixty-something. When it dies we won't be able to replace it. It's just entropy, man. It's just a thing falling apart and we're supposed to be grateful for the privilege of watching it happen.

The older brother turned to face him. You want me to tell you that you're right? I'll tell you. You're right. You're absolutely right about all of it. The place is falling apart. We're losing money. Nobody's doing this anymore. The economics don't make sense. All of that is true. And I still don't want to sell.

Why?

Because my father worked this place and his father worked it and their fathers before them worked it and I'm not going to be the one who stops.

That's not a reason. That's just stubbornness. That's just ego.

Maybe. But it's the reason I have.

The younger brother shook his head. He turned back toward the pipe. We should check the coupling.

The older brother checked it. It had set properly, the joint clean and tight. He ran water through from upstream, releasing the makeshift dam. The water ran clear without leaking. He turned back to the younger brother.

It's good, he said.

They made their way back toward the house, moving slowly through the heat. The older brother thought about what his brother had said and found that he had no answer for it. The stubbornness was real. The ego was real. But there was something else too, something he couldn't articulate, something that had to do with continuity and meaning and the ways that a man was bound to the things that came before him.

They reached the house at noon. The younger brother's truck was parked in the drive, red and gleaming. The house was the original structure, built in nineteen-twenty-eight, expanded and modified over the decades but essentially unchanged. The kitchen window faced the orchard. From his bed upstairs you could see to the far mountains if you looked east.

You want something to eat? the older said.

I should get back.

It's only noon.

I know. But I told Ramirez I'd be back by two. He's got some transmissions to do.

The older brother nodded. He didn't try to keep his brother. They walked to the truck and the younger brother climbed into the cab.

I didn't mean what I said, the younger said. About the old man.

Yes you did.

No, I did. But I shouldn't have said it like that.

The older brother put his hand on the window frame. It's all right.

I'm going to think about what you said too. About the continuity thing. I don't know if I believe in it but I'll think about it.

That's all I ask.

The younger brother started the truck. The engine turned over and caught. Through the windshield the older brother could see his brother's face, thoughtful, a little sad. They'd had this conversation in various forms for two months now and would have it again, the older thought. Probably many times before this was resolved.

The truck pulled onto the road and the older brother stood watching it go. When it was out of sight he turned back toward the orchard. The day was still hot and the work was still undone. There were other breaks that he knew of, places where the pipe was thinning, other repairs that couldn't wait. There was the roof. There was the pump. There was all of it, the endless list of things that required attention and resources and time.

He went to the shed and put the tools away. He looked at the coupling lying on the bench, still slick with cement. His father's tools were here too, organized on the walls with the precision his father had always brought to material things. The older brother picked up a hammer and set it down. He did this several times, picking things up and setting them down, as if by handling them he might understand what his father would have done, what his father would have said.

When he came back out of the shed the sun was high and white and the orchard lay still under its weight. He moved through the trees toward the western section, where the dying trees stood among the living. He would cut down the two dead ones. It would take an hour, maybe more. It would accomplish nothing, change nothing, but it was work that needed doing and he would do it. He would do it and then he would come back to the house and he would eat something and he would think about his brother's words and the developer's offer and what it meant to be bound to a place and a history.

The chainsaw was in the shed. He went back and retrieved it. He checked the oil and the fuel. The blade was sharp. He carried it back to the dead trees and stood for a moment looking at them, at their white limbs reaching toward the sky like something in supplication. Then he started the saw and began to cut.
