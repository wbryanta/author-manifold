# Irrigation

The morning hadn't yet turned hot when Marcus arrived at the north pasture with the truck bed loaded with PVC pipe and coupling fittings. He'd called ahead, left a message on Tom's phone saying he'd be there at six, before the sun got high enough to make the work genuinely unbearable. Tom's truck was already parked near the gate, which meant his brother had either been up since before dawn or had never gone to bed at all. Marcus suspected the latter. Tom kept the kind of hours that suggested he was running from something, though neither of them had ever said so directly.

The break in the line was exactly where the surveyor's report had indicated it would be, about two hundred yards east of the old stone well, in a section of irrigation that hadn't been serviced in at least three years. The earth had subsided around the fracture, creating a small depression that had collected rainwater and insects. Marcus knelt beside it and could see the split in the pipe clearly—a jagged rupture, the kind that came from freezing and thawing cycles, from the ground shifting, from simple age.

"That's worse than I thought," Tom said. He was standing about ten feet back, coffee cup in hand, wearing the same flannel shirt he'd worn the day before when they'd surveyed the line together. "I was hoping it might just be a crack."

"It's a crack," Marcus said, running his finger along the split. "Just a very decisive one." He stood up, brushing dirt from his hands. "We'll need to cut out a section, probably two feet to be safe. Maybe two and a half."

"That'll be forty minutes of work, minimum."

"Maybe. If the soil cooperates." Marcus looked at his brother. Tom's face had the particular exhaustion of someone who'd been awake through the change of night to morning, that hollow look around the eyes. "You sleep?"

"Some. I was thinking about the numbers."

"Don't."

"Hear me out," Tom said, though his voice had already taken on the quality of an argument they'd had many times before. "Millbrook gave us a new appraisal. The whole property, as it sits right now—"

"I know what Millbrook said. You texted me the figure."

"Then you know we could clear two point three million after the real estate fees. Maybe more, depending on how the market moves. Two point three million, Marcus. That's not nothing."

Marcus opened the truck bed and began pulling out the tools. Shovel, pickaxe, the PVC cutter, lengths of new pipe. He arranged them with more deliberate care than the task required. "We've had this land for seventy years."

"Ninety-two," Tom corrected. "Grandfather bought it in 1932."

"Exactly. It's family land."

"It's also family debt," Tom said quietly. "Have you looked at what we owe on the equipment loans? Have you seen what the water rights are going to cost next year if they keep increasing allocation costs the way they have been?"

Marcus didn't answer. He took the shovel and began to dig around the fractured pipe, loosening the soil to expose more of the line. The earth was harder than he'd expected, compacted and root-bound. Each thrust of the shovel required real effort. Behind him, Tom moved to the truck and pulled out the second shovel.

They worked in silence for fifteen minutes, the kind of silence that could mean many things and usually meant the worst. The sun climbed. The temperature rose in small increments that accumulated into genuine heat, the kind that made your shirt stick to your back and dried your mouth between swallows. A red-tailed hawk circled somewhere above the orchard, too distant to hear but visible against the pale sky, and Marcus found himself watching it for a moment while his arms worked the shovel automatically.

"I never wanted to sell," Tom said finally. "You know that."

"I know."

"But I can't spend another year pouring money into this place and watching it fail in slow motion. I can't." Tom's voice had taken on a different tone, less argumentative than exhausted. "Every time something breaks now, I have to wonder if it's worth fixing. And that's not how you're supposed to think about your family's land, but that's where I am. That's where we are."

Marcus drove the shovel into the earth with more force than necessary. "Then we find investors. We bring in capital, restructure the orchard into a premium operation. There are models for that. Agritourism operations, farm-to-table operations, CSA programs—"

"Those take years to establish. Years we don't have, given the debt service."

"So we take a loan against the land—"

"Against the land we're talking about selling? Marcus, listen to yourself."

They had exposed enough of the pipe now to work with it properly. Marcus set down the shovel and picked up the PVC cutter, a long two-handled tool that required both precision and strength. He positioned it against the pipe, a foot to the left of the fracture, and began to tighten the handles. The tool bit into the plastic slowly, the pipe beginning to crack under the increasing pressure.

"Dad wanted me to keep it," Marcus said. The words came out quieter than he'd intended. "I was eighteen when he died. He called me into his office—do you remember? This was after the stroke, when he could barely move his right side. He told me I was the one who needed to keep the land going. Not because I was the oldest, but because he said I was the only one stubborn enough to do it."

"I remember," Tom said.

"He didn't say that to you."

"No, he didn't."

The pipe fractured, and the tool released its pressure with a sudden give. Marcus removed the cutter and examined the two pieces. The cut was clean, perpendicular to the line, exactly what he'd wanted. He set the pieces aside and measured out the new section of pipe he would need, marking it with a pencil.

"He also told me to be kind to you," Marcus said. "He said you'd need looking after. He said you wanted things from life that the land couldn't give you, and that I should understand that and not hold it against you. Do you remember that part?"

Tom was quiet for a long moment. "No."

"He told me a lot of things that day. Most of it contradicted itself, which I guess makes sense when you've just had a stroke and you're scared." Marcus cut the new pipe section, the cutter hissing as it worked through the plastic. "He wanted me to keep the land. But he also wanted me to be happy. And he knew those things might not be compatible."

"Are they?" Tom asked.

Marcus set down the tools and looked at his brother. Tom was standing with his hands hanging at his sides, his coffee long gone cold, his face marked with dust and the particular kind of vulnerability that came from having a conversation that had been deferred too long. He looked, Marcus thought, the way people looked when they'd been carrying something difficult alone for months and suddenly had the option of putting it down.

"I don't know," Marcus said truthfully. "I think I've been trying to answer that for twenty years. I think the real answer might be that it doesn't matter what I want, because the land doesn't actually care about our preferences. It just needs what it needs. Water, nutrients, management, attention. Dad knew that, I think. That's why he was so worried about what would happen to this place."

Tom sat down on the edge of the truck bed, which groaned slightly under his weight. "The offers aren't going to get better. Millbrook said the development interest is high right now because the county is putting in the new highway interchange, but in five years, ten years, the real estate market will stabilize or tank, and then nobody will want our land except other farmers, and our land isn't farmable enough for most operations."

"I know."

"And it's going to take more and more capital to maintain it. Every year. The well is going to go eventually. The perimeter fencing is shot. The packing house roof is—"

"I know all of it," Marcus interrupted. "I've been here, Tom. I've been here the whole time you've been away working in the city, making your nice salary and your retirement plan. I've been here keeping track of what needs fixing and making the decision every quarter about what I can afford to address and what I need to let go another season. I know exactly how bad it is."

The words came out harder than he meant them to, and he heard them echo back at him in the silence that followed. The hawk had circled away, and there was only the sound of the wind moving through the orchard trees, a whisper across a thousand leaves that sounded like the breath of something vast and patient.

"I'm sorry," Tom said.

"I'm not trying to be an asshole."

"You're not. I know. I know what you've done. I know I left, and I know why you stayed, and I know that my leaving meant you had to stay. And I've spent the last fifteen years feeling guilty about that, which doesn't actually help anyone." Tom looked up at Marcus. "But we have to make a choice now. We actually have to make a choice instead of just letting the land make it for us."

Marcus picked up the coupling fittings and began to assemble them with careful attention to each step. Align the pieces. Check the threading. Seal the joints with the adhesive. The process was meditative, each step simple and precise. He'd done this hundreds of times. He could do it in his sleep.

"What do you want to do?" he asked. "If you're being honest. Not what you think we should do. Not what you think would be financially prudent. What do you actually want?"

Tom was quiet for a long time. The sun had climbed high enough now that it had burned off the softness of morning. It was genuinely hot, the kind of heat that made the air shimmer slightly if you looked at it from the right angle. The trees behind them were fully leafed out, heavy with green, beginning to show the first hints of the stress that came with mid-summer drought even though they'd had a decent amount of rain in April and May.

"I want not to have to think about this anymore," Tom said finally. "I want someone to make the decision for me so I don't have to wonder if I made the wrong choice. I want to not be guilty anymore. I want to not feel like I abandoned this place or like I owe it something I can't pay. I want..." He trailed off, then started again. "I want to go back to being a kid when Dad was alive and this was just home. Not a business. Not a failing asset that I have to decide the fate of. Just home."

"I know," Marcus said softly.

"But we can't have that."

"No."

Marcus finished the first coupling joint and moved to the second. The adhesive smelled sharp and chemical, a smell that had been part of his life for decades. When they'd been younger, before the land became a burden, he and Tom would sometimes run together through the irrigation lines at dusk, following the water as it traveled from the well across the property. They'd been maybe eight and six, or ten and eight, old enough to have the run of the place but young enough that running through water seemed like play rather than work. He couldn't remember the last time he'd thought about that.

"You know what would happen if we sold," Marcus said, not as a question but as a statement. "The developers would come in. They'd tear out the trees, fill in the irrigation lines. It would be a subdivisions probably, or a strip mall, or maybe they'd let it sit empty and bankrupt until the next development boom. In five years, there won't be an orchard here anymore. In ten years, there won't be any evidence that an orchard ever existed."

"I know that."

"I'm not sure you do. Not really. Because that's the choice we'd be making. We'd be making the choice to erase something that Grandfather built, that Dad maintained, that I've been holding onto. We'd be the generation that let it go."

Tom looked out across the orchard. From where they were standing, you could see about forty rows in each direction, the trees older now but still producing, still strong enough to make it through the season if the irrigation held. Beyond that, the land rose slightly toward the county road, and beyond that, you could see the development that had happened in the last decade—new subdivisions, a shopping center, the infrastructure of a place that was growing around them, encroaching.

"We're not the first generation to let something go," Tom said quietly. "Grandfather had a dry goods business before he bought this land. He let that go. There were family farms that got sold for highway construction, for railways, for development. Letting things go is part of how you move forward."

"Or it's part of how you disappear," Marcus said.

He finished the second coupling and began to fit the new section of pipe into place, aligning it with the existing lines before securing the final joint. The fit was perfect. The adhesive held the pieces together as it cured, creating a seal that would keep the water moving through the line without loss, without waste. Behind him, Tom watched the work with the attention of someone who had learned the basics but never internalized them, never made them part of his body's knowledge the way Marcus had.

"If we keep it," Tom said carefully, "what does that look like? Realistically. Five years from now, what does this operation look like?"

Marcus sat back on his heels, surveying the repair. The adhesive would need a few hours to fully cure, but the structural work was done. The water would flow again. "I don't know," he admitted. "I've been assuming we'd figure it out year by year. That something would change, or that I'd find a way to make it work. But you're right. That's not a plan. That's just hope."

"So maybe we need a plan."

"Maybe."

They were quiet again, but this time the quality of silence had shifted. It wasn't the comfortable silence of shared work, but it wasn't the brittle silence of argument either. It was something in between, the silence of two people who were beginning to understand that they might actually need to make a decision together, and that neither of them knew how to do that.

"If we sold," Tom said, "where would you go? What would you do?"

It was a question Marcus had never let himself seriously entertain. He'd always pushed it away, assumed it was moot, assumed the land would keep him. But now, sitting in the heat with the repaired irrigation line between them and the vast green growth of the orchard around them, he found himself actually considering it.

"I don't know," he said. "I've only ever done this. I've only ever known how to be here."

"You could do other things."

"Maybe. Or maybe I'm too old to learn."

"You're forty-five."

"Exactly."

Tom smiled slightly at that, though it was a sad smile. "Dad would probably tell you that you're being dramatic. He'd probably say something like, 'The land doesn't own you. You own the land.' Or maybe he'd say the opposite. I can't quite remember which way he leaned on that question."

"He leaned both ways depending on the day."

"Yeah, he did."

Marcus stood up and wiped his hands on his jeans. The morning was well advanced now, the heat building toward the midday intensity that would make any further work on the line genuinely dangerous. They could wait until evening, work until the sun started to set, get another few hours of labor in before the temperature became prohibitive. Or they could stop here, let the adhesive cure, come back tomorrow to test the line and make sure the repair had held.

"We should go back to the house," Marcus said. "Let this set. We can test it in the morning, make sure everything's holding."

Tom stood as well, moving with the slight stiffness of someone who'd been crouched or sitting in an awkward position. "We're not done talking about this."

"I know."

"I'm going to push," Tom said. "I'm going to keep trying to make the case for selling. Because I think I have to. I think if I don't push, I'll spend the rest of my life wondering if we should have."

"I know."

"But I'm also ready to listen to an alternative if you have one. If you have a real plan, a real way forward that doesn't just rely on hope and stubbornness."

Marcus began to load the tools back into the truck bed. The work wasn't done, but the active labor for now was finished. He thought about his father in that office, his right side slack, his voice still strong, trying to pass along wisdom or obligation or both. He thought about his grandfather buying this land in 1932, during the Depression, buying something that was supposed to be stable and reliable and eternal. He thought about what it meant that he was standing in 2024 with the question of whether it would exist past this generation.

"Let me think," he said finally. "Give me a week. Let me actually sit down and work through the numbers, not the emotional weight of the numbers, but the actual viability. If I can't come up with something that works, then we sell. Fair?"

Tom considered this. "Fair," he said. "But a real plan, Marcus. Not wishful thinking."

"A real plan," Marcus agreed. "No wishful thinking."

They drove back to the house in their separate trucks, taking the old dirt road that wound through the property, the same road they'd driven countless times. At the house, a structure that had been built in the 1950s and updated multiple times since, Tom pulled up beside Marcus's truck and they both sat for a moment in the heat, not moving.

"I'm not trying to hurt you," Tom said through his window.

"I know. I'm not trying to be stubborn just for the sake of it. I'm trying to figure out what to do with what we were given."

"I know," Tom said. "That's what makes this so hard."
