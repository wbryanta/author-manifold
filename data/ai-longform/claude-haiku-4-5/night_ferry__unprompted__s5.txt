# The Crossing

The ferry groaned against the dock with a sound like something wounded. Magnus stood at the rail with his ticket folded twice in his breast pocket, watching the crew secure the lines with a methodical violence that suggested they had done this ten thousand times. The sun hung low over the water, that strange northern light that never quite left, turning the surface to hammered silver. It was June, almost midsummer, and the sky would remain luminous through the night. He had never understood how people slept on ships during these months.

He was the last to board, or so it seemed. The other passengers had already dispersed into the vessel's depths—families retreating to cabins, solitary travelers claiming seats in the lounge, the occasional drunk heading straight for the bar. Magnus waited until the crowd had thinned, then picked up his single bag and made his way inside, down corridors that smelled of machine oil and something else, something organic and slightly wrong. Fish, perhaps, or the accumulated breath of a thousand previous journeys.

His cabin was small, barely larger than the narrow bunk it contained. A porthole window looked out onto the darkening dock and the mountains beyond. He set his bag on the bed without unpacking it and sat beside it, hands on his knees, listening to the ship's engines rumble to life beneath him. The walls vibrated with a low frequency that he felt in his teeth.

The call had come three days ago, on a Tuesday morning when he was standing in his kitchen watching the neighbors' cat walk across his fence. A policeman from somewhere north—he had written the name down but already forgotten it—speaking with that cautious tone people used when delivering unlikely news.

"We're trying to locate next of kin," the policeman had said. "The deceased was carrying an ID. But the body has been in the water for some time. We're asking family members to come and make a visual identification."

Magnus had asked how long. The policeman had equivocated. Not short, he'd said. Not days. Possibly weeks, though the cold water had slowed decomposition. Possibly longer. They couldn't be certain until the autopsy was completed.

"You think it might be him?" Magnus had asked.

"We have reason to believe it could be. But we need you to come and confirm."

The pronoun was still strange. *Him*. It had been seven years since Magnus had seen his brother, and in that time Vidar had become mostly theoretical—a figure referenced occasionally when Magnus's mother called, a set of worries in the background of their conversations. He had not thought of Vidar as a *him* in the concrete sense for a very long time.

The ship lurched beneath him as they pulled away from the dock. Magnus pressed his face to the porthole and watched the land recede. A few scattered houses clung to the shore, their windows already lit against the gathering dusk. A church spire. A lighthouse in the distance. Then the strait widened, and they were alone in open water, the mainland disappearing into haze.

He lay on the bunk fully clothed and tried to sleep, but his mind circled. He tried to picture what Vidar might look like now, and found he could not quite manage it. In his memory, Vidar was still thirty-one, still had the face of a man in his thirties, though logically he would be thirty-eight now if he was still alive. If he had been alive. The mathematics were confusing.

There had been a time, years ago, when they had been close. Magnus was five years older, and there had been that window—roughly from the ages of twelve to nineteen for Vidar, seventeen to twenty-four for Magnus—when they had been something like friends. They had drunk beer on the rocks in summer, played cards, told jokes that their mother didn't understand. Then something had shifted. Vidar had discovered drugs or philosophy or both, had become unreliable and then angry, had alienated himself with a kind of deliberate precision that suggested he was working toward some specific goal. By the time he was twenty-five, he wasn't welcome in their mother's house anymore. By thirty, he had essentially vanished, appearing occasionally to borrow money, to apologize, to disappear again.

Magnus had not tried very hard to maintain contact. This was a fact he had trained himself not to feel guilt about, mostly successfully.

Around midnight, he gave up on sleep and went to the lounge. It was nearly empty, just a few determined drinkers and one woman reading a book in a language Magnus didn't recognize. The bar was still open. He ordered a whisky he didn't particularly want and sat by a window. The sky had lightened somewhat—it was never fully dark anymore—and he could see the water stretching away, ruffled and black.

A man sat down across from him without being invited. He was older, perhaps sixty, with the weathered face of someone who spent time outdoors. A fisherman, probably, or a captain.

"Crossing?" the man asked.

"Yes."

"North?"

"Yes."

The man nodded, satisfied, and sipped from his own drink. "Not many people coming north in June. Usually it's south. People going home."

Magnus said nothing. The man seemed comfortable with silence.

"You from up there?" the man asked.

"No. My mother is. I haven't been back in a long time."

"Ah." The man nodded again. "That's why people cross. Either they're going home or they're running away. Sometimes both."

Magnus didn't correct him, though it wasn't quite either. He was going to identify a body. He was being pulled north by gravity, by obligation, by the peculiar weight of shared blood.

"The water's cold up there," the man continued. "People don't understand how cold. They think they can swim it, think they'll have time. They don't have time. The body shuts down in minutes. Minutes."

"Is that so," Magnus said.

"Found a body once. Or not found—came across it. Floating. I've never forgotten the way it looked. Like a doll. All bloated and strange. The fish had already—" He stopped. "Are you all right?"

Magnus realized he had gone pale. "I'm fine," he said. "I'm just tired."

The man studied him for a moment, then nodded and returned to his drink. After a while, he got up and left. Magnus sat alone with the empty glass, watching the water.

He must have dozed off in the chair, because he woke to sunlight and the ship's horn and a disorienting sense of having lost time. The sun was already high, throwing sharp shadows across the lounge. He checked his watch. Five in the morning. They would arrive in a few hours.

He went back to his cabin and changed his shirt, an absurd gesture toward respectability. He combed his hair. He looked at himself in the small mirror mounted above the sink—a man of forty-three who looked older, whose face had settled into a kind of tired resignation. He wondered what Vidar had looked like at the end. He wondered if recognition would matter, if he would know his brother by some essential quality that survived death and decomposition, or if he would stand there and say yes or no to a complete stranger wearing a dead man's face.

The coastline appeared gradually as they approached port, a rise of grey rock and sparse vegetation. The town was small—a few hundred buildings scattered along the shore, a harbor with working boats and fishing equipment. A place that had never quite decided whether it wanted to be industrial or picturesque. Magnus had never been here before, though it was not far from where his mother had grown up.

He disembarked with a dozen other passengers and followed the signs to the police station, a small brick building two blocks inland. A young officer with red hair and careful eyes helped him through the process. There were forms to fill out, questions about Vidar's distinctive marks. A scar on his left shin from a motorcycle accident when he was nineteen. A tattoo on his right shoulder—a bird, Magnus thought, though he wasn't entirely sure. The officer wrote everything down with meticulous attention.

"We'll need to prepare the body," the officer said. "It will take an hour or so. Can we offer you some coffee?"

Magnus said yes, and they sat in a break room that smelled of heating oil and instant coffee. The officer, whose name tag read *Sørensen*, talked about inconsequential things—the weather, the fishing season, the difficulties of a small community with a limited police force. Magnus understood that this was kindness, that the officer was trying to make the experience less terrible by flooding it with normalcy.

After an hour, they went downstairs.

The morgue was cold, sterile, lit by harsh fluorescent lights. Another officer was waiting, a woman with grey hair pulled back severely. She explained the process in a professional voice. They would show him the body. He should take his time. There was no rush.

The sheet covered everything except the face. Magnus stepped close and looked down at what remained of his brother.

It was Vidar, and it was not. The face was bloated, the skin discolored, the features arranged in a slack mask that bore only a passing resemblance to the man Magnus remembered. And yet. And yet there was something in the structure of the bone, in the particular shape of the forehead, that was unmistakably familiar. The scar on the temple—Magnus remembered that scar, remembered the story of how Vidar had gotten it, a stupid accident involving a glass door and teenage carelessness.

"Is it him?" Officer Sørensen asked quietly.

Magnus opened his mouth. Closed it. The thing on the table was and was not his brother. This was Vidar stripped of personality, of intention, of the particular animating force that had made him difficult and unreliable and strange. This was what was left when all of that was subtracted.

"Yes," Magnus said. "That's him."

They brought him upstairs and had him sign more papers. The officer asked about arrangements. Did he want to contact a funeral home? There would need to be a service of some kind. Did he know if Vidar had any insurance, any wishes regarding cremation or burial? Magnus answered what he could and deflected the rest. He would need to call his mother. He would need to think about these things.

"We'll want to do an autopsy," the officer said. "To determine cause of death. The body was found in the water, but we need to know if he went in alive, if there were injuries prior to that. It will take a few weeks."

Cause of death. Magnus had not asked this, and was not sure he wanted to know. Suicide seemed likely—this was the dread that had hovered around Vidar for years. But it could have been an accident, a slip, a moment of inattention. It could have been something else entirely.

"Do you know when?" he asked. "When it happened?"

"Not precisely. The coroner estimates somewhere between four and eight weeks ago. Early to mid-May, possibly earlier."

Magnus did the calculation. Early May. He had been in Copenhagen at that time, working on a renovation project, unaware that his brother was drowning or had drowned or had put himself in the water deliberately. His mother had not called. No one had called. Vidar had simply ceased to exist in any way that mattered to Magnus, and Magnus had not noticed.

The officer drove him to a hotel—a modest place on the main street that seemed to cater mostly to traveling salesmen and the occasional tourist. He checked in and went to his room and sat on the bed looking at his phone. He should call his mother. He should do it immediately, should do it now. Instead, he lay back and stared at the ceiling.

The light outside remained steady and strong. It was afternoon, but the sun was high and clear, and there was no hint of darkness coming. He thought about closing the curtains and trying to sleep, but the effort seemed enormous.

Around six o'clock, he forced himself to make the call.

His mother answered on the second ring, which meant she had been waiting for it, which meant word had already reached her somehow. The police must have contacted her directly, or someone in the community had called. News traveled.

"They said it was him," his mother said. It was not a question.

"Yes. I identified him this morning."

There was a long silence. Magnus could hear her breathing, careful and controlled.

"I want to come up there," she said. "I want to see him."

"You should wait. They're going to do an autopsy. There will be more waiting."

"I want to see my son."

Magnus closed his eyes. He understood this impulse, the need to look upon the dead in order to make them real, to make their absence official. But he also understood that seeing would only make it worse, that his mother's grief was still theoretical and that seeing Vidar's ruined face would turn it into something concrete and permanent.

"I know," he said. "We'll arrange it. I'll come back down and we'll come together."

"When?"

"Soon. A few days, maybe. I need to... I need to handle some things here first."

After he hung up, he sat in silence for a long time. The light outside remained unchanged, that eerie perpetual daylight of the north, and he realized that he would not sleep tonight either, that sleep might not be possible in this season, in this light, in this place where his brother had died and been pulled from the water and brought to a cold room where Magnus had looked at him and said yes, that's him, as if he had been confirming a reservation or identifying a piece of luggage.

He went to the window and looked out at the town. The street was quiet, a few people moving with purpose, a car or two passing. A perfectly ordinary place where a man had ended his life, or lost it, or simply stopped being careful enough. Magnus tried to imagine Vidar here, tried to picture what circumstances might have brought him to this small northern coast. He had no connections here that Magnus knew of. No friends, no work, no reason to be.

Unless he was running. The old fisherman had said that people crossed the water either to go home or to run away. Perhaps Vidar had been running from something, and this cold shore had seemed like the edge of the world, a place where you could go and not come back.

Magnus turned from the window and lay back down on the bed. The light continued unchanged. Somewhere in the building, he heard a television, a woman's laughter. Ordinary sounds from ordinary lives, lived by people who had not spent years not knowing their brothers. He closed his eyes and tried to think of nothing, tried to exist in the space between one moment and the next, suspended like his brother in water, weightless and unmoored.

Outside, the sun began its slow arc toward the horizon, a journey it would not complete for several hours yet. The town continued on, indifferent, eternal, waiting for the next ferry to arrive with the next person who had reasons to come north—to grieve, to remember, to stand in cold rooms and identify the dead and then return, if they were lucky, to the south and the life they had not yet managed to escape.
