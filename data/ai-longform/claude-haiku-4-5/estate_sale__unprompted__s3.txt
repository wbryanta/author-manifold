# 

The cardboard box marked "KITCHEN—MISC" weighed more than Margaret expected, and she adjusted her grip on it as she stood in the doorway between the dining room and the front hall. Through the open door she could see the driveway, where a young couple in their thirties were loading a bentwood chair into the back of a pickup truck. The woman was laughing about something, her hand resting on the man's shoulder. Margaret recognized the chair as the one her mother had bought in 1987, the year Margaret's father had his first stroke. She had sat in that chair while her mother made soup in the kitchen, back when Margaret still lived here, back when this house had been full of the sounds of ordinary life rather than the sound of strangers' footsteps on the hardwood floors.

She turned away from the door and made her way to the kitchen, setting the box down on the counter. Her realtor, Dennis, had assured her that the estate sale company would handle everything professionally, that all she needed to do was sign some papers and let them manage the process. But Margaret had wanted to be here anyway, had taken the afternoon off from her position at the university library in Columbus, had driven the hour back to Millbrook to oversee the dismantling of her childhood home. She told herself it was practical—someone should verify that nothing was stolen, that the house itself wasn't damaged. But if she were honest, she simply couldn't bear the thought of strangers moving through her mother's rooms while she sat in her condo ninety miles away.

The woman from the estate sale company, whose name was Brenda and who wore her blonde hair in a severe bun and carried a clipboard everywhere, had explained the logistics that morning. The sale would run from noon to four. She'd set up pricing sheets and signs throughout the house. The most valuable items—her mother's china cabinet, the mahogany dining table, a few decent oil paintings—had already been tagged with higher prices. Everything else was marked down to move quickly. Brenda had been brisk, efficient, kind in that distant professional way that made Margaret feel foolish for tearing up when handing over her mother's reading glasses to be catalogued and sold.

Her mother had died three weeks ago. A stroke, like the one that had weakened her husband decades before, though hers had been more decisive. Helen Cabot had collapsed in the grocery store on a Tuesday afternoon and was gone by Thursday morning, leaving behind a house full of accumulated life—seventy-three years of objects, relationships, choices materialized into furniture and books and kitchen utensils.

Margaret pulled open the box and found herself looking at her mother's measuring spoons, her mother's potato masher, her mother's collection of margarine containers saved for leftover storage. She picked up one of the margarine containers, a faded blue plastic box that had probably held butter for some Weight Watchers project sometime in the eighties. Her mother had been compulsively practical, saving things that almost no one saved anymore. Margaret set the container back in the box and leaned against the counter, feeling the weight of the kitchen around her—the avocado refrigerator that still worked perfectly, the harvest gold stove that had roasted countless Sunday chickens, the linoleum that her mother had waxed herself every other month for decades.

She heard footsteps on the stairs and straightened up, suddenly self-conscious about standing idle in the kitchen. A man in his sixties descended, carrying a cardboard box of books. He was the type of collector who haunted estate sales in small towns—Margaret had seen several like him already, prowling through the house with practiced eyes, looking for first editions or vintage paperbacks worth more than the quarter or dollar they'd been priced at. This man had the look of someone who'd built a library on the backs of other people's disposals.

"Your mother read quite a bit," he said to Margaret as he passed through the kitchen doorway, as though they were simply two people making conversation, not strangers engaged in the ritual consumption of a dead woman's life.

"She did," Margaret said. "Mostly mysteries. She belonged to a book club."

"Ah, that explains the Agatha Christies. Most of them are first editions, you know. She took good care of them."

Margaret hadn't known this. She hadn't known many things about her mother's reading habits, come to think of it. She'd been too busy with her own life in Columbus—her job, her marriage before it ended, her small circle of friends and colleagues. She'd called her mother once a week, dutiful Sunday-night calls where they discussed the weather and local gossip and Margaret's work. Her mother never complained, never asked for much, never suggested that Margaret should visit more often than the monthly Saturday afternoons she managed to drive back.

The man disappeared toward the front door, and Margaret heard him loading his books into what she imagined was a carefully maintained vehicle, organized by category and condition.

She turned back to the miscellaneous kitchen box and continued unpacking it, removing items one by one and examining them as though they might tell her something she needed to know. A set of measuring cups. A wooden spoon with a burn mark on the handle. A meat thermometer. A set of cloth napkins that looked hand-embroidered, possibly made by her mother's mother decades ago. Margaret had never seen these napkins used. They'd been stored in a drawer, kept for some occasion important enough that ordinary days didn't qualify.

The afternoon light coming through the kitchen window was that particular Ohio autumn light—generous but not quite warm, the kind of light that made everything look temporary, as though the world was already practicing for winter. Margaret had grown up in this light, had done her homework at this kitchen table while her mother prepared dinner, had learned to make biscuits and pot roast in this very kitchen, knowledge that had largely leeched away over her fifty-five years. She'd never been particularly domestic. Her mother had never seemed to mind, though Margaret sometimes wondered if that lack of caring had been acceptance or simply resignation.

She heard more footsteps, this time a couple—a man and woman who looked to be in their forties, moving through the house with the determined focus of bargain hunters. They were examining the china cabinet now, Margaret could hear them murmuring to each other. The cabinet was an impressive piece of Victorian mahogany, and Margaret had worried briefly about its fate, whether it would go to someone who appreciated it or simply be gutted for parts.

Margaret finished unpacking the box and folded it flat, leaning it against the wall. She should check on things, make sure Brenda was managing the flow of traffic, ensure that nothing was being damaged. But first she moved to the small desk in the corner of the kitchen, the place where her mother had paid bills and written correspondence and kept the records of her life. Margaret opened the top drawer and found her mother's address book, several decades of friendships and family connections written in her neat, careful handwriting. Many of the entries had been crossed out over the years—people who had moved away, or died, or simply drifted out of her mother's life. Margaret recognized several names: her mother's cousin in Delaware, a friend from college named Patricia who had always sent a Christmas card, the Mitchells who had lived next door for forty years before selling their house to a family with three children who lasted only seven years in Millbrook before deciding they needed to be closer to a city.

She picked up her mother's reading glasses from the counter—she'd retrieved them from Brenda, unable to let them go—and held them up to the light. The lenses had her mother's specific prescription, ground to correct the particular failing of her particular eyes. Without these glasses, her mother would have been lost in the world, unable to read the newspaper or recognize faces at a distance or see the television clearly. Margaret wondered if her mother had felt that same sense of dependence on small objects, that vulnerability to the particular requirements of her own body.

"Excuse me?" A woman appeared in the kitchen doorway, fiftyish, with the kind of careful appearance that suggested she spent time on maintenance and upkeep. "Are any of these items still available? The glasses, I mean." She pointed to Margaret's mother's reading glasses, still held up to the afternoon light.

Margaret lowered the glasses slowly. "No," she said. "No, these aren't for sale."

The woman nodded, unbothered, and moved on.

Margaret slipped the glasses into her pocket and walked to the window, where she could see the driveway again. The pickup truck from this morning was gone, replaced by a minivan. A woman was carefully loading a lamp—not one of the valuable ones, just an ordinary brass floor lamp with a pleated beige shade. Margaret remembered that lamp in the living room, remembered reading by it, remembered her mother sitting beside it in the evening with her needlework.

The house was being evacuated. That was the only word for it. Stripped. Her mother's life was being distributed across the greater Midwest, ending up in living rooms and dens and bedrooms that had nothing to do with her mother's own intentions. A stranger would sit under that lamp and not think about Helen Cabot reading by it. A stranger would open the china cabinet and serve food on dishes that had held her mother's carefully planned meals. A stranger would sit in the bentwood chair.

Margaret had known this would happen, had known it intellectually. But knowing and experiencing were different things entirely. She had been braced for sadness, but what she felt was something stranger—a kind of dislocation, as though her own past were being systematically liquidated. Which, of course, it was.

Her phone buzzed. A text from her ex-husband, Gary, who had somehow remained on her phone's contact list despite their divorce five years ago. "Heard about your mom. I'm sorry. Let me know if you need anything." It was kind, and exactly the sort of thing people said, and completely inadequate to the situation. What did she need? She didn't know. A way to unsee the afternoon? A way to undo the estate sale? A way to resurrect her mother and do better, visit more, pay more attention? None of those things was available.

She texted back a simple thank you and returned the phone to her pocket.

By two o'clock, the house had taken on the stripped-down quality of a stage set after the play is over. The living room was nearly empty now—the good furniture sold, the mediocre pieces still waiting in corners for someone to decide they were worth the space. Margaret walked through the rooms like a ghost in her own history, noticing the rectangles of unfaded paint where pictures and furniture had stood for decades. The wall in the dining room where her parents' wedding photo had hung now showed a perfect square of cleaner plaster. The corner where the Christmas tree always went every December was bare and somehow already looked abandoned.

She climbed the stairs, her hand trailing along the banister her father had installed when Margaret was in high school. Her parents' bedroom was locked—Brenda had made a business decision to leave it out of the sale, and Dennis the realtor would handle its contents after the sale closed—but Margaret stood in the hallway and could picture it anyway. The bed with the quilted headboard. The dresser where her mother kept her jewelry. The small television they'd gotten in the nineties.

Margaret's own childhood bedroom was down the hall. She stood in the doorway, though it wasn't really her room anymore. Her mother had converted it into a craft room sometime in the nineties—a place for sewing projects and her knitting and the supplies for various hobbies she pursued in the methodical way she approached everything. But Margaret could still remember it as it had been: the twin bed with the white eyelet comforter, the desk where she'd done her homework, the window seat where she'd sat and read and dreamed about leaving Millbrook.

She'd left at eighteen and rarely looked back. She'd gone to Ohio State, then to graduate school, then to Columbus, where she'd built a life that had nothing to do with her small hometown. She'd made the migration that millions of Midwestern children made, the move away from the places where they grew up, toward cities with more opportunities and less judgment and the feeling of possibility. She'd been successful at it. She had a good job, a nice condo, a social circle, a life that interested her. Her mother had never made her feel bad about it, never suggested that Margaret owed her more frequent visits or more attention. And that grace, Margaret realized now, had made everything worse. She should have been fighting against her mother's expectations, rebelling against guilt. Instead she'd simply... drifted.

She turned away from the bedroom and went downstairs, where Brenda was checking her clipboard and a young couple was examining the silver-plated flatware in its original box.

"How's it going?" Margaret asked.

"Great sales," Brenda said, not looking up from her list. "Really moving well. I'd say by four o'clock we'll have maybe thirty, forty percent of the merchandise left. The smaller items have been particularly popular. People love scoring deals on kitchen things and collectibles."

Margaret nodded. People loved other people's castoffs. It was a fundamental human pleasure, the discovery of treasure in someone else's discard pile. Which was to say that Margaret loved it too. She thought of her own condo, furnished largely with things she'd found at estate sales and antique shops. A Victorian settee. A set of hand-painted dishes. A mirror with a gilded frame. All purchased from the estates of dead people, all carrying the ghost of other lives into her own.

"I'm going to step outside for a bit," Margaret said.

The afternoon air was cool and crisp, and she walked to the edge of the front lawn where she could see the street. Millbrook was quiet, as it always was on a weekday afternoon. A few cars passed. Mrs. Henderson's house across the street had been repainted since Margaret's last visit—the trim was now a darker blue. The Mitchells' old house, where the young family had lived before decamping for the city, had a new family again. Margaret could see toys in the driveway.

She pulled out her mother's reading glasses and held them up again, looking through the lenses at the street beyond. Everything was slightly magnified, slightly clearer. She could see the details of things that her own vision missed. This was what her mother had seen. This was the world as it had appeared to Helen Cabot, slightly altered, slightly enhanced by the particular prescription of her failing eyes.

Margaret thought about her mother in this town her entire life, leaving only once—for college in Columbus, where she'd met Margaret's father—and then coming back, settling into this house, this street, this community, where she'd spent seventy-three years making a life that didn't look like much from the outside but that must have meant something to her. She'd had her book club and her church and her neighbors and her routines. She'd read mysteries and done needlework and maintained her house and made Sunday dinners. She'd lived quietly, without drama, without ambition, without the sense that there might be something better somewhere else.

Margaret had always seen this as a kind of failure, a lack of imagination or courage. But standing in her mother's driveway, watching strangers carry her mother's possessions away, Margaret wondered if she'd been wrong. Her mother had wanted less than Margaret wanted. But she'd also seemed less lonely, less restless, less consumed by the sense that her life should be other than what it was.

A car pulled into the driveway—a woman Margaret didn't recognize, coming to shop at what remained of her mother's estate sale. The woman got out and walked past Margaret without a glance, heading into the house.

Margaret stayed where she was, holding her mother's glasses, watching the afternoon light change over Millbrook. By four o'clock, the sale would be over. The house would be quiet. The removal company would come next week to take away whatever hadn't sold. And Margaret would drive back to Columbus, back to her condo and her job and her life, and her mother would be officially gone.

She put the glasses back in her pocket and went inside, where Brenda was explaining to a customer the provenance of a Pyrex bowl, where strangers moved through her childhood carrying pieces of her mother away, where the rooms grew emptier and the afternoon grew later and time, as it always did, moved forward into whatever came next.
