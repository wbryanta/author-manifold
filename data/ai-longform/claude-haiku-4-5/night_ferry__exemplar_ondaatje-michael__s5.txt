# 

He bought his ticket at the terminal in the late afternoon when the light had begun its premature descent, the northern sky already darkening toward indigo though the watch on his wrist insisted it was only four o'clock. The woman behind the counter barely looked at him, sliding the ticket across the laminate surface with the practiced efficiency of someone who had done this ten thousand times and expected to do it ten thousand more. He held the thin cardboard in his hand, feeling its weightlessness, the way it might drift away if he didn't maintain his grip. The ferry would depart at eight. It would arrive at six in the morning. He had fourteen hours to reconsider, but he knew he would not.

The waiting area smelled of diesel fuel and the particular staleness of enclosed spaces where many people had sat waiting. A television mounted in the corner played the news with the sound turned low, the screen illuminating the face of an anchor whose mouth moved soundlessly, delivering catastrophes to an indifferent audience. He sat on a plastic chair that was bolted to the floor, as if people here were prone to stealing furniture, as if that small act of theft was the final surrender he should expect from travelers in transit. His brother's name was Martin. Martin had left seven years ago, had gone north for reasons that were never fully articulated, had sent three postcards and then nothing. The first postcard was from Thunder Bay. The second from Yellowknife. The third from a place called Aklavik, which Martin had written was so far north that the sun did not set in summer and did not rise in winter, and that this was the most honest geography he had ever encountered—a place where darkness and light simply took turns telling the truth.

The body had been found in the water near a fishing village three days ago. The police report was vague, describing only that identification was difficult, that dental records did not match any missing persons they had in their system, but that the body had been discovered with a wallet containing an expired Ontario driver's license bearing the name Martin Cavanagh. The photograph on the license was from twelve years ago, a younger version of his brother with hair that still held its color, before the weight had settled differently on his face. The constable on the phone—a man named Vance with a voice like gravel—had asked him to come up, to see if he could confirm whether it was his brother. "We'll cover the expense," Vance had said, though he hadn't explained why a small fishing village in the Canadian north would spend money on a positive identification when they already had a driver's license and a body.

He had left his job without explanation, had simply not returned after the weekend, and had not yet checked his email to see what his supervisor had written. He worked in a bank, in an office with views of another office building across the street, and he had performed this work with such consistent mediocrity that no one noticed when he was there and no one remarked upon his absence. He had not called his ex-wife, had not seen his daughter in three months before this, and so departing suddenly seemed merely the logical extension of a life already in progress toward its own dissolution.

The ferry, when they boarded at seven-thirty, was smaller than he expected. He had imagined a great vessel, something that suggested permanence and authority, but instead found himself ascending a narrow gangway into what felt like a converted fishing boat, retrofitted with passenger cabins that smelled of paint and industrial cleaner. He was given a room on the lower deck, a space barely larger than a closet with a single bunk and a small porthole window. He lay on the bunk fully clothed and heard the engines begin to rumble, felt the subtle shift as the ferry pulled away from the dock. Through the porthole he watched the lights of the terminal recede, and then the lights of the town, and then there was only darkness and the sound of water against the hull.

He did not sleep. Instead he lay in the dark and thought about the last time he had seen Martin, which had been at their father's funeral six months before Martin left. Martin had worn a suit that didn't fit him properly, the jacket bunching at the shoulders, and he had stood through the service with the expression of someone attending an event whose significance he did not fully grasp. Afterward, at the reception in the church basement, Martin had eaten three sandwiches and had left early, saying something about having an early shift at the warehouse where he worked. But he had left his job at the warehouse the next week. He had given notice, had collected what severance he could, and had begun driving north. Their mother was still alive then, still in the house in Oshawa, and she had begged him not to go, had called their oldest sister in Toronto and asked her to intervene, but Martin had been implacable in a way that suggested the decision had been made long before he announced it, perhaps even before the funeral. Perhaps, he thought now, watching the darkness outside the porthole, Martin had made this decision the moment their father died, seeing in that death some kind of permission to disappear.

Around midnight he went up to the deck and stood at the railing, looking out at water that could not be distinguished from sky. The air was cold and carried the smell of salt and something else, something organic and faintly decaying, as if the northern waters carried within them the remains of everything that had sunk into them over the centuries. There were other people on deck, silhouettes against the deeper darkness, and he wondered if they too were fleeing toward something or away from something, or whether they had simply purchased tickets like him and boarded a ferry like him and were now suspended between two fixed points, waiting for the morning.

A woman stood near him, leaning against the railing in a posture of such relaxed exhaustion that he wondered how many times she had made this journey, how many times she had stood in this exact location and watched the black water roll past. She was perhaps in her sixties, with hair pulled back severely from her face, and she wore a quilted jacket that had been patched in several places.

"First time?" she asked, not looking at him.

"Yes," he said.

"You can't see anything at night," she continued. "You could be anywhere. You could be nowhere. In the morning you'll see the islands and then you'll remember where you are." She paused, as if considering whether to elaborate. "Though remembering doesn't always help. Sometimes knowing where you are just makes the journey feel longer."

She left him there and went back inside, and he stood alone at the railing for another hour, until the cold had worked through his jacket and into his bones, until he could no longer feel his fingertips. Then he went back down to his cabin and lay on the bunk and waited for morning, listening to the engines and the water and the groaning of the ferry as it moved through the night.

He must have slept because he woke, startled, to gray light coming through the porthole. They were moving through a passage between two islands, low dark shapes that seemed to emerge from the water like the backs of sleeping animals. The sky was the color of old concrete, and the water had taken on a slightly less absolute darkness. He looked at his watch and saw that it was quarter past five. He rose and washed his face in the small sink and changed into a fresh shirt, though he could not have articulated why it mattered whether he arrived wearing a clean shirt. He packed his small duffel bag and went up on deck.

The islands were gradually becoming visible in the increasing light, and he understood that he was approaching land, that his waiting was nearly over. The ferry slowed as it entered the harbor, and he saw the town gradually reveal itself: a handful of buildings clustered near the water, a dock that looked more permanent than he expected, a road that led upward away from the harbor and disappeared into forest. People were moving on the dock, preparing to receive the ferry, and he understood that this was real now, that his brother was somewhere in this landscape, or what remained of his brother was, and that soon he would have to look at that remains and make a judgment about whether it was true or false, whether the body in the water had been Martin or someone else, whether his brother was dead or simply gone in a more absolute way than before.

He waited until all the other passengers had disembarked before he descended the gangway. A man in a RCMP uniform was waiting at the dock, and he recognized this man from a description Vance had given over the phone—tall, with a scar that ran from his left eye to his jaw. This was Constable Davies. The constable shook his hand with a grip that was firm but brief, and did not offer condolences because the situation was too ambiguous yet for condolences.

"We'll go to the medical examiner's office first," Davies said, leading him toward a truck parked near the dock. "She'll walk you through what we have. Then you can see the body."

The drive through the small town took perhaps five minutes. He saw a post office, a general store, a building that might have been a bar or a community center. He saw a few people moving along the sidewalk, and he wondered if any of them knew why he was here, if word had traveled through this small place about the dead man pulled from the water. They pulled up in front of a building that looked like it had once been a private house and had been repurposed into municipal offices. Inside it smelled of linoleum and old wood and something medicinal.

The medical examiner was a woman named Dr. Reeves, perhaps fifty, with reading glasses hanging from a cord around her neck. She had arranged the file on her desk in a neat progression and gestured for him to sit.

"The body was discovered four days ago," she began, in a voice that was quiet but thorough. "It had been in the water for approximately two to three weeks, we estimate. The condition is consistent with that timeline. We don't have the wallet anymore—that was taken into evidence—but the identification it contained indicated the name Martin Cavanagh. The date of birth on the license was March 14, 1967. Do you have a brother matching that description?"

He said that he did.

She removed her glasses and looked at him directly. "The body is badly decomposed. The water up here is cold, which actually slows some processes, but fish and other aquatic life have done significant damage. I'm not going to ask you to make an identification based on general physical characteristics. I want you to look for something specific. Did your brother have any distinctive markings? Scars, tattoos, birthmarks?"

He thought of his brother as he had last seen him, in the ill-fitting suit. He tried to remember his brother's hands, his neck, his arms. He remembered that Martin had a scar on his left palm from a childhood accident involving broken glass, a thin white line that Martin said he could still feel sometimes, phantom pain from something that had healed twenty years ago. He remembered that Martin had a small dark mole on his right shoulder blade, visible when he wore a t-shirt in summer. He did not remember whether Martin had any tattoos. They had not been close enough in recent years for him to know the details of his brother's body.

"A scar," he said. "Left palm. From a childhood injury."

Dr. Reeves nodded and wrote something in her file. "We'll look at that carefully. Come with me."

The body was in a room that was very cold, kept cold deliberately, he understood, for preservation purposes. It lay on a metal table and was covered with a white sheet. Dr. Reeves approached the table with a professional calm and folded back the sheet just far enough to expose the left hand. The hand was bloated and discolored, the fingers swollen to nearly twice their normal size, but when he looked carefully at the palm, he could see it—the faint white line, barely visible, but there.

He made a sound that might have been recognition or might have been grief or might have been simply the sound of breath leaving his body. He could not distinguish between these possibilities.

"That matches?" Dr. Reeves asked.

"Yes," he said. "Yes, that's his scar."

She covered the hand again and then folded the sheet back further, exposing the right shoulder. There, too, he could see the mole, darkened perhaps by the water and the time, but unmistakably present. He had not expected to feel anything when he confirmed these identifications, had thought he might be able to view the body with clinical detachment, but instead he found himself standing in the cold room with his hands shaking, understanding that his brother was dead, that the possibility of reunion, which he had perhaps not fully acknowledged he was holding, was now impossible.

"I'm sorry," Dr. Reeves said, and she sounded as though she meant it, as though she had stood in this room many times with people making this same discovery and had not become immune to it.

He could not speak. She gave him a tissue box and guided him to a chair and left him there for a while. He sat in the cold room and tried to think about what this meant, what he should feel, what he should do now. He had come north to identify a body, and he had done so, and now the reason for his journey no longer existed. He was free to leave, to go back south, to return to the bank and his supervisor's angry emails and his life as he had been living it. He was also now the person who had seen his brother's body, who had stood in a cold room and confirmed that his brother was dead. This fact would not change regardless of what he did next.

After some time that might have been minutes or might have been longer, he rose and went back out to find Davies. The constable was waiting in the hallway, looking at his phone.

"You were right," he said. "It was my brother."

"I'm very sorry," Davies said, and again the condolences seemed genuine. "We'll need you to sign some paperwork, officially confirming the identification. And we'll need information about next of kin, about what you want done with the body."

He had not thought about this. He had not considered what happened after confirmation, what the procedures were for someone found in the water far from home. "I don't know," he said. "What are the options?"

"The province will cover burial if there's no family able to do so," Davies said. "Or you could arrange to have him transported south. There are funeral homes that specialize in this kind of situation."

He thought about Martin in a coffin, thought about attempting to explain to his mother what had happened, thought about a funeral service that he would have to attend because he was the brother who had come north and confirmed the death. He thought about his ex-wife and his daughter and what he would have to tell them. He thought about his job and the fact that he had simply abandoned it.

"I need to think about it," he said.

"Of course. You can stay in town if you want. There's a small hotel—the Kelp House. It's the only one we have. I'll give you my card. Call me if you need anything, or if you decide about arrangements."

He took the card and walked back outside. The morning had progressed further while he was inside, and the sky had become slightly less gray, though the sun was still hidden behind cloud cover. He walked through the town without any particular destination, and found himself eventually at the edge of the water, looking out at the same harbor where his brother had been pulled out of the sea. He tried to imagine what Martin had been doing in the water, whether he had jumped or slipped or been pushed, whether he had struggled or simply accepted it. He had no way of knowing these things. Davies had not mentioned whether the death was ruled as accidental or undetermined or anything else. Perhaps they had no way of knowing either.

He turned and walked back toward the hotel. A woman sweeping the front step of the post office glanced at him, and he wondered if she could tell that he was the man who had come to identify a body, if such knowledge was visible on his face. He felt that something must have changed in him during the last hour, that he should look different now that he carried the fact of his brother's death inside him.

The Kelp House was a small building painted dark green, with a crooked sign that appeared to have been hand-painted many years ago. Inside it smelled of coffee and furniture polish. The woman at the desk—young, perhaps twenty-five, with something guarded in her expression—seemed to know without being told why he was there.

"You're the one who came about the body," she said. Not a question.

"Yes."

She checked him in without comment and showed him to a room on the second floor. It was clean and sparse, with a narrow bed and a small desk and a window that looked out toward the harbor. He lay on the bed and stared at the ceiling and tried to think about what to do next.

After an hour, he rose and went downstairs and asked if there was any place where he could get something to eat. The woman directed him to the building that might have been a bar or community center, though it turned out to be a small café. Inside, an older man was cooking behind a counter, and a few other people were seated at tables. He ordered soup and tea and sat by a window, watching the street.

The man at the next table had a newspaper folded in front of him, and he was staring out the window in the same direction that he was staring. They were both looking at the harbor, at the water where his brother had been found. After some time the man turned to him.

"You just arrive?" the man asked.

"This morning," he said.

"From down south?"

"Yes."

The man nodded, as if this confirmed something he had suspected. "It's different up here," the man said. "The light, the water, the way time moves. People don't understand when they come from the south. They think they're still in the same country, but they're not. You cross certain lines and you're somewhere else entirely. It's like another world up here."

He said nothing, and the man seemed to accept this silence as agreement. He returned to his newspaper, and they sat together in that café, two strangers occupying space and watching water and waiting for something neither of them could name.

That night, alone in the Kelp House, he wrote a letter to his mother. He did not know what to say, so he said that Martin was dead, that he had confirmed it, that she would need to be informed by the police. He said that he would call her in the morning. He did not say that he was sorry, though he was, not because he grieved for Martin in any simple way, but because the world had closed around him in a way that made closure impossible. The world still had not explained why his brother had come north, had not explained what he had been running toward or what he had been running from. The body was dead, but the questions remained alive.

He sealed the letter and placed it on the desk and looked out the window at the dark harbor, at the water that had taken his brother and held him and finally, reluctantly, released him.
