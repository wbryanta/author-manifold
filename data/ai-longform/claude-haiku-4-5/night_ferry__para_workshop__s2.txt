# 

The ferry's horn sounded at eleven, a long, mournful note that Marcus had heard before, years ago, crossing to see his mother when she was still alive. He stood on the open deck despite the cold, watching the lights of the port city recede into darkness. November in the north was all darkness anyway. The water below was black and restless, stippled with foam where the bow cut through.

He'd received the call that morning. A body had been found three days earlier, caught in the rocks near Halberstram—a small town on the eastern shore that he'd never heard of until the police officer explained, carefully, where it was. The dental records didn't match the missing person they'd initially thought it might be. No identification in the pockets. Male, late forties, dark hair turning gray. That was all they had.

"Your brother's been missing since July," the officer had said. It wasn't a question.

"Three months," Marcus said.

"You haven't reported it."

"No."

The officer waited, then told him they'd like him to come identify the body, if he thought he was able. A visual identification, pending further confirmation. Marcus had said yes without thinking, without considering that he didn't actually know if he wanted to identify his brother—if the body was his brother. The relief that came with saying yes surprised him. Perhaps he'd been waiting for this call all along. Permission to act, to decide something.

He bought a ticket for the overnight ferry. Eight hours across the strait. The cabin below deck smelled of diesel and old upholstery. He sat on the narrow bunk without turning on the light and listened to the engines thrum through the hull. Outside his small window, the world was nothing but black. He'd packed a single bag. The drive to the port had taken six hours. He'd thought about not going. He'd thought about it intensely for the first four hours, then stopped thinking and simply drove.

David had been thirty-one the last time Marcus saw him. That was fifteen years ago, which made him forty-six now. The right age for the body. The right description. Marcus didn't know if his brother's hair had turned gray. They hadn't spoken since 1999, and before that only occasionally, phone calls that David usually initiated, his voice bright with something that Marcus had learned to recognize as desperation masquerading as cheer. By 1998, Marcus had stopped taking the calls. By 1999, David had stopped making them.

The officer had said nothing about how David died. Drowned, presumably, since he'd been found in the water. Or pushed in after. Or thrown in. Marcus didn't ask.

He dozed on and off through the night, never quite sleeping. Around two in the morning he got up and made his way back to the deck. The wind had picked up. The ferry pitched and rolled with a kind of steady violence. He was alone out there except for a woman near the bow, smoking a cigarette despite the cold. She didn't look at him.

He thought about David as a child, before everything went wrong—before anything was wrong, or before Marcus was old enough to perceive that it was. David at seven or eight, building with Lego in the basement, humming to himself. David asking Marcus for help, and Marcus, at fourteen or fifteen, too full of contempt for such things, turning away. It seemed important now that he had turned away. It seemed like it had mattered.

David at eighteen, home from his first year of college, talking too much at dinner, then not talking at all. His eyes had already taken on that peculiar quality—not quite focused, as if he were watching something beyond the room. Their father had noticed it first. Marcus remembered his father saying to his mother, "Something's wrong with David," and his mother saying, "He's just adjusting." But their father had been right. Something was wrong. Something had started wrong, or started then, or became visible then. For the next two decades—before David disappeared—he'd been wrong in a way that never fully clarified itself.

The smoking woman flicked her cigarette into the dark water. She pulled her coat tighter and went inside. Marcus stayed on deck.

He'd been living in Vancouver when David disappeared. The police had called him there, the first he'd heard from David in years except for a single postcard, sent from some small town in British Columbia that he'd left shortly after arriving. The card had shown a lake. On the back David had written, "Beautiful here. Thinking of better days." Marcus had not called him back. He'd put the card on the refrigerator for a month, then thrown it away.

When the police called about the disappearance, Marcus had expected to feel something—guilt, probably, or an obligation mixed with resentment. Instead he'd felt a kind of flat confirmation. Yes, of course David had disappeared. It was exactly the kind of thing David would do, had seemed to be building toward doing. It wasn't surprising, which made it somehow worse than surprising would have been.

The shore was visible now, pale gray on the horizon. The sun wouldn't rise for another hour, but the darkness was thinning. Marcus went back to his cabin and forced himself to shower in the tiny stall, the water ice cold and the boat still rocking. He stood under the spray and tried to prepare himself for the identification, but there was nothing to prepare. Either it was his brother or it wasn't. Either he would know or he wouldn't.

The town of Halberstram had one main street and perhaps three hundred people. The police station was small, heated very warm, and smelled like coffee and paper. The officer who'd called him—Brenner, his name was—was younger than Marcus had expected, probably not yet forty. He shook Marcus's hand and asked how his trip was, as if this were a social visit.

"Fine," Marcus said. "How do we do this?"

Brenner walked him down a hallway. "I want to prepare you," he said. "The body's been in the water. It's been three days since recovery, though the medical examiner believes he was in the water longer than that. The condition is not good."

"Okay."

"You may not be able to make an identification."

"I understand."

They entered a small room with a draped table. A woman in scrubs stood near a metal desk with a clipboard. She was older, competent-looking. She didn't introduce herself. Brenner nodded at her and she pulled back the sheet.

The face was swollen and discolored, the lips dark. The hair was indeed turning gray, matted against the skull. There was a tattoo on the visible shoulder, a symbol Marcus didn't recognize. The smell was faint but present—something chemical underneath something else, something botanical and wrong.

Marcus stared at the face. He'd been prepared for not recognizing it, but he also hadn't been prepared for the possibility of genuinely not knowing. The face was human and devastated and could have been anyone. Could have been his brother. Could have not been.

"I don't know," he said.

"It's all right if you need more time," Brenner said.

"No, I mean—I don't know if that's him. I haven't seen him in a long time. The condition..." He trailed off.

"Can you tell us about any distinguishing marks? Scars, tattoos, broken bones?"

Marcus looked again. The tattoo. "He had a tattoo?"

"On the right shoulder. Can you identify it?"

Marcus had never seen his brother with a tattoo. But that was fifteen years ago. People changed. People acquired tattoos. "I don't know."

He realized he was shaking. The woman in scrubs was watching him with a kind of professional sympathy. Brenner was waiting. Marcus closed his eyes and tried to think about David, really think about him, tried to construct him from memory, but the figure on the table kept imposing itself, erasing the internal image with its actual detail. This body. This specific ruined body. He couldn't reconcile them.

"I think it might be," he said finally. "I think it probably is. But I can't be certain."

Brenner nodded. "We'll proceed with the assumption that this is your brother, pending confirmation through dental records and DNA analysis. The medical examiner will have more information for you in a few days. I'm very sorry for your loss, Mr. Chen."

The casually formal phrase—your loss—seemed absurd. He hadn't lost anything. He'd been relieved of something. Relieved of the possibility of encountering David again, of having to decide how to feel about him. Relieved of the uncertainty. Or perhaps he had lost something, and it was only now becoming apparent what it was.

"How did he die?" Marcus asked.

"We don't yet know for certain. The body was in the water, but there were some injuries that we're examining. He may have struck his head when he entered the water. Or the injuries may have occurred post-mortem, from rocks, current, et cetera. The examiner will be more conclusive once the full autopsy is complete."

"In other words, he might have jumped."

Brenner was quiet for a moment. "We're keeping all possibilities open."

Marcus was given the address of a motel. Brenner offered to arrange a social worker, grief counseling, next steps. Marcus declined. He asked about David's effects, and was told they'd been catalogued and were minimal—clothing, nothing else. No identification, no wallet, no keys. Just clothing and the body. Just David, boiled down to those components.

The motel was the kind of place that still rented rooms by the night despite having permanent residents who'd long ago stopped leaving. The proprietor, a woman in her sixties, was kind in a brusque way and asked no questions. The room smelled like the cleaning products used to mask other smells. Marcus sat on the bed and looked at the television without turning it on.

He could call his father. His father was still alive, living in a care facility in Victoria. David's disappearance would have caused him real grief. His father had loved David in a way that Marcus had never quite understood—a desperate love, as if David were something his father was responsible for fixing and had failed to fix. It had made the house unbearable to be in, that kind of love and failure mixed together.

He didn't call his father.

Instead he walked the main street of Halberstram. The town was quiet, almost depopulated. A bakery, closed. A bar with three cars parked outside. A grocery that looked like it hadn't been renovated since the 1980s. He tried to imagine David here, walking this street, buying food, sleeping. Had David lived here? The police hadn't said. Or perhaps they had and he hadn't been listening.

Near the northern edge of town, he found a small park with a view of the water. The strait was gray and rough, the far shore barely visible. This was where the body had been found, he assumed, or near here. This was where it had washed up. He stood looking at the water for a long time. The cold was very deep, he thought. People drowned in water like this. People threw themselves into it, or were thrown. People who were tired enough or desperate enough or angry enough.

He returned to the motel as the light was failing. In the room, he found himself looking at his phone, at the old postcards from David that he'd kept, scanned to digital. There were only three. The first was from 2003, from Vancouver: "Beautiful here. Thinking of better days." The second from 2005, from some place in Alberta: "Miss you. Hope you're well." The third from 2007: no message, just a photo of mountains.

He'd never responded to any of them. He'd let his brother exist in one direction only, sending his quiet messages into an indifference that Marcus had cultivated like a garden. He'd been very careful about it, very deliberate. He'd wanted nothing to do with David's suffering.

The medical examiner called the next morning. The preliminary results of the autopsy indicated the body had been in the water for approximately two weeks. The cause of death was blunt force trauma to the head, consistent with either a fall, an accident, or an assault. There was no water in the lungs, which suggested the head trauma had occurred before entering the water. There were contusions on the wrists and hands. The toxicology results would take longer.

"Could he have been in a fight?" Marcus asked.

"Possibly. Or he could have struggled. We really can't say at this point."

"And the identification?"

"The dental records came back. It's a match. This is definitely your brother."

Marcus felt it then—a kind of settling, as if something had finally been resolved. It was his brother. This fact was now certain. David was dead. The uncertainty he'd been holding onto, the possibility of not knowing, had been removed from him. He should feel sad. He wasn't sure if he was sad. He felt something else, something like the absence of pressure.

He thanked the examiner and ended the call. He looked out the window at the street below. It was afternoon, and a few people were walking past the stores. Regular people, living regular lives, carrying forward into the future with the momentum of their own existence.

He tried calling his father. The facility answered; his father was napping. Did he want to leave a message? Marcus said he'd call back.

That evening he drove out to where the body had been found, following the address Brenner had given him. It was a rocky outcropping north of town, accessed by a narrow gravel road. The rocks were large and dark, smoothed by water and time. The water was loud here, crashing and receding. He stood at the edge and looked out at the expanse of gray.

Had David come here deliberately? Had he known this place, or had he simply found himself here by accident, by some chance movement of current? Had he looked out at this water and thought it was beautiful? Or had he just been tired, just wanted to stop, just wanted the noise to stop.

"I'm sorry," Marcus said to the water. He wasn't sure what he was apologizing for.

He left the next morning without calling his father. He told himself he would call when he got back to Vancouver, when he could think more clearly. But he knew he probably wouldn't call. Some patterns are too old, too worn into the shape of your life to reverse.

On the return ferry, he stood at the rail watching the town recede. The water was gray and restless, the same as it had been coming over. There was no difference in the world. His brother was still dead. He was still alive. These facts had weight, but the world remained indifferent to them.

A man came to stand next to him, an older man in a wool coat. They didn't acknowledge each other. They stood together for a long time, watching the town disappear into the distance.

"Beautiful," the man said finally. "In a grim sort of way."

Marcus nodded.

"You from around there?" the man asked.

"No."

"Me neither. Family matter?"

Marcus didn't answer. He watched the water.

"I lost my son," the man said, "about five years ago. Not to death. Just to life. He moved to Toronto with his boyfriend, and we didn't really stay in touch. It's strange, isn't it? To lose someone who's still alive."

Marcus looked at him. The man was looking out at the water, his face weathered and kind.

"Yes," Marcus said. "It is."

He didn't know if that was true. He didn't know what he had lost or when he had lost it. He didn't know if his brother had jumped or fallen or been thrown. He didn't know if David had loved him or hated him or thought about him at all in those fifteen years. He would never know these things. This seemed, in some strange way, like a kind of freedom. To be released from the possibility of knowing, released from the obligation to understand.

The ferry carried them north across the gray water. Behind them, the town had completely vanished. There was nothing but the open strait, the enormous indifferent motion of it, the cold wind bringing the smell of salt and depth. Marcus held onto the rail and let himself be carried forward. The man in the coat had gone inside. He was alone on the deck.

He would go home to Vancouver. He would arrange for his brother's funeral, though he didn't know who, besides himself, would attend. He would perhaps call his father and tell him his son was dead—dead twice over, once in life and now in fact. He would continue forward into whatever was coming next. And sometimes, on cold mornings, crossing water, he would think about his brother and the rocks below and the question of why anyone stays in a place long enough to know it.

But that was later. For now, he simply stood at the rail and let the wind push through him, carrying the salt deep into his lungs, the ferry cutting through the darkness toward the other shore.
