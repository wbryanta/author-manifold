# Ferry Crossing

The announcement came through speakers as gray water swallowed the harbor. Daniel stood at the rail watching the city recede, becoming first a collection of lights and then a thin luminescence against the darker mass of the hills behind it. November in the north: the ferry groaned through swells that seemed to come from nowhere, born of wind rather than tide, and the few other passengers had already retreated inside. He remained outside, his collar turned up, salt spray occasionally reaching him where he stood.

The body had been in the water for six days. They knew this from the police report, though the language they'd used was more clinical: *discovered November 14th, preliminary assessment indicates time in water approximately one hundred forty-four hours.* A fisherman had found it near the rocks on the eastern shore of Malmö, caught in kelp, and it had no identification. The photographs didn't help. They rarely did, Daniel thought. The face was slack, the features distorted by bloating and the work of the sea. It could have been anyone.

But it could have been Marcus.

It had been seven years since Daniel had last spoken to his brother. Not the kind of distance that could be measured in miles—though there had been that too—but the kind created by a single argument that neither of them had known how to reverse. Their mother was alive then. Marcus had come home for Christmas with a woman Daniel had never heard of, and when their mother asked the inevitable questions about marriage and children and the future, Marcus had laughed and said there was no point making plans, wasn't that clear? Daniel had said something he regretted. Marcus had said something worse. Their mother had cried quietly. By Boxing Day, Marcus was gone, and he stayed gone even after she died two years later.

Daniel had tried, after the funeral. He'd sent letters that went unanswered. He'd called the number he had, listened to the impersonal recording. He'd eventually stopped, understanding that the effort had become a kind of torture for both of them. Time had a way of making silence seem permanent.

Then came the call from Swedish police, and suddenly the silence seemed like something else entirely.

The crossing took nine hours, and Daniel spent most of them in the cabin he'd been assigned, a narrow box with a porthole that showed nothing but dark water and the occasional reflection of the ship's lights. He tried to read but couldn't concentrate. He tried to sleep but managed only a kind of drifting half-consciousness, where he was neither awake nor asleep but trapped in some muddled state between. In this state, he found himself remembering things he'd spent years not thinking about.

Marcus at fifteen, teaching him to ride a motorcycle in an empty parking lot behind the shopping center, hands gripping the seat and shouting instructions that were drowned out by the engine. Marcus at twenty-two, calling from Berlin with a kind of breathless excitement in his voice, saying he'd never realized how small their country was until he left it. Marcus at twenty-seven, the last time they'd been in the same room, and the particular way he'd looked at Daniel when Daniel had used the word *wasted*, as if Daniel had just confirmed something Marcus had suspected for a long time.

The ferry arrived at dawn, pulling into a dock that seemed carved out of the gray landscape rather than part of it. The town beyond—Malmö—revealed itself gradually as the mist lifted. Modest buildings, steep roofs weighted down with tiles, a sense of being balanced on the edge of something larger and colder than itself. Daniel disembarked with the small crowd of other passengers, each of them moving with the particular purposefulness of people who had a reason to be here.

The police station was a short taxi ride from the docks. Daniel gave the driver the address and watched the town pass, noting nothing in particular. A café with outdoor chairs facing the harbor. A bookstore with a display of Swedish mysteries in the window. A woman buying flowers from a stall, and something about her movements made him think of his mother, though he couldn't say exactly what.

The officer who met him was a woman in her fifties with lines around her eyes that spoke of long experience with difficult things. Her name was Inspector Bergström, and she shook his hand with a firmness that was meant to be reassuring.

"I appreciate you coming so quickly," she said in English. "It can be difficult, this kind of identification. But it is important that we know who the person was. The family should have answers."

Daniel nodded, not trusting himself to speak.

"I should tell you," she continued, leading him down a corridor that smelled of floor cleaner and something else, something older underneath, "that we are not certain this is your brother. The body has been in the water. It is difficult to be precise. But there are things that match your description. The age, approximately. The build. And there were some items with the body—clothing, a watch. We will show you these things."

She brought him to a small room with a table and chairs, and she laid out the items carefully, as if they were precious things rather than the fragments of a life interrupted. A wool sweater, dark blue, still damp despite the time that had passed. A watch with a broken face, the hands stopped at a time that no longer meant anything. A leather jacket, creased and worn, salt-stained. Socks. Underwear. The small detritus of existence.

Daniel stared at these things for a long moment without touching them.

"Do you recognize these?" Bergström asked.

"The watch," he said. His voice sounded strange to him, as if it were coming from another room. "Marcus had a watch like that. It was their father's. But a lot of people have watches like that."

"Yes," Bergström agreed. "That is true."

She showed him a photograph of the jacket, spread flat on a gurney. He had the sudden terrible image of what had been inside the jacket, and he pushed the image away.

"I think he had a jacket like this," Daniel said. "But I'm not certain. It was a long time ago. Seven years since I've seen him."

"And before that?"

"We were close when we were young. But we had differences."

It was an inadequate word. They had had more than differences; they had had a fundamental incompatibility that had grown more obvious as they aged, a sense that they were traveling in opposite directions and had simply been waiting for the distance to become official.

"The body," Bergström said, and Daniel understood that this was the moment he'd come for, "is in the morgue. The medical examiner has done the preliminary examination. If you think it might be your brother, you should see it. For your own certainty, if nothing else."

Daniel followed her through a different set of corridors, these cooler, the air tasting metallic. The morgue was a long room with steel cabinets lining the walls, and it took all his willpower not to close his eyes as they approached the drawer. The medical examiner, a man younger than Daniel would have expected, pulled it open with a soft hiss.

The body was covered with a white sheet, and only the head was visible. The face was bloated and discolored, the features distorted into something that only approximated a human face. The eyes were closed, and Daniel was grateful for that small mercy.

He stood there for what felt like a long time, though it was probably only seconds. Bergström and the medical examiner stood quietly, waiting for him to speak.

"I don't know," Daniel said finally. "I can't tell. The face is too damaged. Could it be him? Yes. Could it be someone else? Yes. That too."

"The dental records," Bergström said. "We are still trying to obtain the dental records from Sweden. Your brother was a resident here?"

"I don't know where Marcus lived. We didn't speak. After our mother died, I tried to find him, but I couldn't. I don't know if he was in Sweden or Norway or anywhere at all. I don't know anything about his life except that he wasn't in mine."

He heard the bitterness in his voice and regretted it immediately. It wasn't Bergström's fault that his own failure lay so bare before him.

"We have a mobile phone that we found with the body," the medical examiner said, his accent making the words sound careful and precise. "It is damaged, but perhaps it can be recovered. The SIM card may still work. If we can access the messages or the contacts, we will know the identity for certain."

"How long will that take?"

"Perhaps one week. Perhaps longer. The phone was in the water for several days. The damage is significant."

One week. Daniel tried to imagine returning home and waiting a week, tried to imagine the weight of this uncertainty settling over everything he did. He tried to imagine that it might not be Marcus, that his brother was somewhere alive, perhaps, in Berlin or Vienna or some other city where the language was different and the past was far away. Or he tried to imagine that it was Marcus, and in imagining it, he felt something that he wasn't sure was grief or relief or simply the exhaustion of finally having to confront a distance that had existed for so long.

"Is there a hotel nearby?" Daniel asked.

Bergström nodded. "Several. I can provide recommendations. It may take a few days for the phone to be examined. You should not stay only for a few hours. This is not efficient. You should stay, rest, and we will contact you when we have more information."

He booked a room at a hotel overlooking the harbor, a small place with old-fashioned furniture and a breakfast that was served in a room lined with windows. He sat there the next morning, drinking coffee and watching the water, and he thought about his brother as a boy, before the distance had been established, before they had learned to be separate people.

Marcus had wanted to be a musician once. Not seriously, perhaps, but he'd played guitar and had a pleasant voice, and he used to sit on the porch in summer and play old folk songs that their father had taught him. Daniel had listened sometimes, pretending he wasn't, and he'd felt something watching his brother in those moments—a connection that was pure and uncomplicated by everything they would later say to each other.

What had happened between them was not dramatic. There was no single act of betrayal, no great transgression. It was simply that they'd become different people, and they'd become frustrated with the ways they were different, and they'd expressed that frustration in words that couldn't be taken back. And then they'd been too proud or too hurt or too tired to try.

On the third day, Bergström called.

"We have been able to recover some data from the phone," she said. "There are photographs. There are messages. The identity is now confirmed. The body is that of Marcus Henrik Schatz."

Daniel didn't speak for a moment. Even though this was what he had come here to discover, even though part of him had been expecting it all along, the confirmation still landed like a physical blow.

"There is more," Bergström continued. "The messages on the phone, some of them are recent. Only days before the body was discovered. They suggest that your brother was not in a good place mentally. There are messages to a woman, someone named Kristina, and they are confused, they are desperate. And then they stop."

"Do you think he killed himself?" Daniel asked. The words seemed very large in his mouth.

"We cannot know for certain," Bergström said. "The body was in the water. There is no indication of trauma, but the water can erase many things. It is possible. It is also possible that it was an accident. We may never know."

After he hung up, Daniel sat in his hotel room and watched the harbor. The water was gray and empty, and he thought about Marcus somewhere down in its depths, about all the years that had passed in silence, and about the fact that he would never know what his brother had been thinking in those final days. He would never get to apologize. He would never get to explain or to make peace or to do any of the things that people imagined they would do if they were given a second chance.

Instead, he had this: the knowledge that his brother was dead, and the terrible, absolute certainty that some distances could never be crossed, no matter how long you lived or how much you wanted to.

He spent another week in Malmö, waiting for the paperwork, making arrangements for the cremation. He visited the spot where Marcus had been found, a rocky shore north of the town where the land descended sharply into the sea. He stood there in the wind and the spray, and he said things aloud that he had never said before, apologies and regrets and things that were half-formed and inchoate, things that the water swallowed immediately.

On the day before his departure, he returned to the police station to collect Marcus's belongings. The watch was in a plastic bag, and he held it for a long moment before putting it in his pocket. The clothes had been cleaned and dried, and they felt insubstantial now, as if they'd been hollowed out by what they'd lost.

"I am sorry for your loss," Bergström said as he was leaving.

"Thank you," Daniel replied, though he wasn't sure what, exactly, he was grieving. The brother he had known, or the brother he might have known if things had been different? The past, or the future that would never come?

He boarded the ferry the following morning and stood at the rail as the town receded into mist. In his pocket, he carried his father's watch, which had belonged to his brother, and in it he carried also all the years that had passed, all the silence, all the words that had gone unsaid. The water that separated him from the town was the same water that had held his brother's body for six days. In a strange way, this felt like the closest he had come to understanding where Marcus had gone, and why he had stayed away for so long. Some people, Daniel thought, simply needed to put distance between themselves and everything they knew. And sometimes that distance became permanent.

The ferry turned north, into deeper water, and Daniel went inside.
