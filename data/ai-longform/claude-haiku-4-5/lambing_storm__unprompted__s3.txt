The wind had teeth. Margaret knew this not as metaphor but as fact, the way she knew the difference between a breech presentation and a normal one, the way she could read the distress in a ewe's eye before the animal even cried out. The wind came down from the north across the open moorland and found every gap in the lambing shed's weathered timber, every space where the old wood had warped and pulled away from its nails. It came through like something hungry.

She pushed her sleeve up higher on her forearm, the better to feel for the lamb's legs in the darkness of the ewe's body. Outside, the world had disappeared into white. The snow had started around three in the afternoon, innocent enough then, just a thickness to the air and a few drifting flakes that caught the light. Now at half past nine it was a wall of it, visible only in the cone of the electric light, and even that light seemed weaker than it should be, as if the storm was drinking it.

"How much longer?" Her son's voice came from behind her, young and trying not to sound afraid.

"As long as it takes," Margaret said. She found the first foreleg, slippery in her palm, the tiny hoof like a hard marble. "Bring the bucket closer, Tom. I need the warm water."

He was seventeen, tall enough now that he had to duck slightly under the low beam of the shed, and he moved with the careful precision of someone still learning to trust his own size. The bucket scraped against the concrete floor as he positioned it within her reach. She could feel him watching, could feel the weight of his attention like a physical thing.

The lamb came free in a sudden slide of fluids and effort, the ewe giving a deep bleat of something between pain and relief. Margaret caught the wet body before it hit the straw, checked quickly for the second twin she'd felt earlier, and set the first one down to dry. The lamb was small, no bigger than her hand stretched out, but breathing. Its chest heaved. Its eyes opened. It was alive.

"Good," Margaret said, though to whom she wasn't certain. She looked at the ewe's straining side, the contractions still coming. "There's another one."

Tom had already learned not to stand too close during a delivery. He was at the shelf now, gathering the towels they'd stacked there an hour ago when the snow was just a rumor and his father's absence was the usual kind of absence, the absence of someone who had died three months before spring really arrived. In April, Margaret kept finding tasks that would have been his. Checking the north fence line. Fixing the hinge on the feed store. Calling down the vet when a ewe looked off.

The second lamb took longer. There was resistance in the contractions, a sense that the ewe's body was fighting something it couldn't quite expel. Margaret could feel the wrong angle of this one, a front leg crossed over the back of its head. She'd dealt with worse presentations, but not often, and not alone.

"Tom, I need you to come back."

He was there instantly, his body already moving toward hers before his mind could catch up. That was something, at least. His reflexes were good. His father had taught him that much, though Margaret suspected that when people remembered her husband—and they would, for a while yet, for a year or two—they would remember his steadiness, his quiet way of knowing what to do, and they would forget that Tom had learned most of it already, that he was capable of more than anyone had yet asked of him.

"I'm going to need you to hold her still," Margaret said. "Not rough. Just steady. Put your weight on her chest and keep her from thrashing. Can you do that?"

Tom crouched down, his movements careful, and positioned himself. The ewe was wild-eyed now, panting hard. He laid his hands on her wool and leaned in, not with force but with a kind of calm insistence. The animal stopped fighting him almost immediately. Animals were like that with people who believed they belonged there.

Margaret worked her hand deeper, found the crossed leg and straightened it. There was sweat on her forehead despite the cold; the wind had knocked the power out twenty minutes ago, and the electric heaters had died mid-hum. The shed was cooling fast, and her body was running hot from the work and the fear, the constant low-level fear that had started three months ago and had never quite gone away.

"Easy, girl," she murmured to the ewe. "Easy now."

The second lamb came free, and this one was stuck slightly, the umbilical cord wrapped around its body like a constraint. Margaret worked quickly to unwrap it, her fingers moving by instinct and experience. For just a moment—a long moment—there was no movement in the small body, no reflex, no gasp.

She rubbed it, hard and thorough, and something in her chest seized with a terror so sharp it cut off her breath.

"Come on," she whispered. "Come on, lamb."

It gasped. Coughed. Began to breathe.

Tom's face was white, she saw. He was staring at the small body as if he could will it to live through the sheer force of his attention. When the lamb coughed again, his eyes went shiny with tears that he immediately blinked away. Margaret pretended not to notice.

"Put her in the pen with the first one," she said, keeping her voice level. "There's the heat lamp above that corner. Make sure it's shining on them both."

Tom gathered the second lamb carefully, his face still white but his hands steady. Margaret turned back to the ewe, checking for a third lamb and finding nothing, checking her for bleeding and finding the normal amount, checking everything that experience had taught her to check. The ewe was exhausted, her breathing still rapid but her body already beginning the slow release of labor. She would be all right. She would live.

Margaret stood slowly, her back protesting. She was forty-three years old. She felt older. She felt like she was carrying her husband in her shoulders, in the place where he used to stand beside her and just know what to do without being told.

"How many is that?" Tom asked from across the shed, where the twin lambs were stumbling toward the heat lamp with the blind determination of newborns.

"Twenty-eight. With these two."

"Of how many?"

"Eighty-three ewes this year."

He sat back on his heels, looking at the lambs in the orange glow. "We've got more than half to go."

"I know."

"We should have brought someone in. Mrs. Keene's son, or someone from—"

"We can do it," Margaret said. "We will do it."

It came out sharper than she intended. Tom flinched slightly, and Margaret felt the familiar guilt that had become her constant companion, the guilt of being alive, of having to be strong for both of them, of not having the luxury of breaking down. She'd had one day to break down, the day after the funeral. She'd done it in the feed store where Tom wouldn't find her, and she'd given herself one hour before she washed her face and came back out and asked him to help her check on the ewes.

"I know we can," Tom said quietly. "I didn't mean—"

Another ewe bellowed from further down the shed, a sound that cut through the wind's howling like something that knew it had limited time. They both turned automatically.

"That's the Shetland cross," Margaret said. "She'll be fast. She always is."

They moved into a rhythm then, the hours blurring into one another. Labor and birth and the constant checking of animals and newborns and the examination of every pregnant body that came into the shed trailing anxiety like a visible thing. The wind didn't decrease; if anything, it grew stronger. The snow continued its assault on the old timber walls, and Margaret found herself noting with the purely practical part of her mind that there was a new gap forming in the northwest corner, a space where she could see the swirl of white beyond the wool.

Around midnight, Tom found the camp lantern in the tack room. It was an old thing, burning butane, and it gave off a light that was yellower and stranger than the electric lamps but didn't require electricity. He hung it from the beam above their main work area, and the shed took on the quality of a medieval painting—animal shadows jumping and dancing on the walls, the long shapes of the pregnant ewes rendered strange and mythological by the uneven light.

"Better?" he asked.

Margaret looked at his face, rendered unfamiliar by the yellow glow. He looked older than seventeen. He looked like he might look at thirty, at forty. He looked like he looked like his father, which was something she hadn't quite admitted to herself until this moment.

"Better," she said.

One of the ewes—a young Texel with narrow hips—needed assistance. The lamb was too large and the pelvis was small, and Margaret had to make a decision that she would not normally have made at home: she did what she had to do to save the lamb, which meant she was not as gentle as she might have been, and the ewe screamed, a sound so primal and full of pain that Tom left the shed entirely, ostensibly to check on the newborns warming by the electrical heater in the corner that ran on the battery system.

Margaret did what she had to do, and the lamb came free, and it was alive, and she set it down and stood up and realized that she was shaking.

When Tom came back, he didn't ask questions. He just took the lamb and moved to settle it with its mother, and his silence felt like forgiveness, or at least like understanding. This was the thing about having a son who was becoming a man: he was learning to recognize that sometimes there were no good choices, only choices that were less bad than the alternatives.

Around two in the morning, Margaret lost count. She knew they'd passed thirty, then forty, then somewhere toward fifty, but after a while the numbers stopped mattering. There was only the work and the wind and the constant warm bodies of the ewes and the small desperate gasping breaths of the lambs being born into a shed with no electricity and a blizzard that showed no sign of stopping.

Tom had wrapped himself in one of the heavy blankets they kept for cleaning up and was curled in the corner, but he wasn't asleep. She could see his eyes tracking her movements as she moved from pen to pen, checking, assisting, monitoring. He would sleep in shifts, ten minutes here, five minutes there, but whenever she moved toward a ewe in labor, those eyes opened immediately.

The wind found another gap. Then another. The shed was cooling steadily, and Margaret was grateful for the heat lamps, grateful that they'd set them up on the battery system months ago, grateful for systems and backup plans and the paranoia that came with loving something so completely that its survival felt personal. She thought about her husband, lying in a bed at the Royal Infirmary in Edinburgh, the cancer having settled into his bones like an unwanted houseguest, and she did not let herself think about how he'd looked at the end, how he'd looked at Tom and then at her, how his hand had gripped hers with a strength that surprise him because everything else in his body had already left.

A young ewe came into labor just after three, and Margaret recognized her as one they'd hand-reared as a lamb, one that had been bottle-fed by Tom when she was orphaned. The ewe went into the small pen where they did difficult deliveries—the ewe that could be easily handled—and Margaret settled down to wait for the contractions to deepen.

Tom came over without being asked. He brought the bucket of warm water, the clean towels, the small spray bottle of antiseptic. He knew the procedures now. He moved with a confidence that came from repetition and necessity. Margaret looked at him in the yellow lantern light and thought: this is what it takes. It takes the kind of loss that you never recover from but that you learn to carry, and it takes a seventeen-year-old boy, and it takes a blizzard, and it takes the understanding that nothing is certain except that the ewes will lamb and the lambs must be born and the living must keep doing the work of living.

The ewe labored steadily. The lamb came easily, a small ram lamb with perfect markings, and it gasped its first breath and cried out with a sound like outrage at the world's coldness. Margaret caught it, checked it, dried it, and placed it in the clean straw beside its mother. The ewe immediately began the work of licking it clean, and the lamb began the blind search for food.

"That's fifty-six," Tom said quietly.

Margaret nodded. She didn't correct him even though she thought it might be fifty-eight. Numbers seemed less important in the depths of the night, in the cold, in the yellow lantern light with the wind howling around them. What mattered was the living—the ewes and the lambs, the small boy who had curled up in the corner and was sleeping now, really sleeping, his face peaceful in a way it rarely was awake.

She checked on him once, pulled the blanket up higher around his shoulders. His face was young again in sleep, and she saw the ghost of the five-year-old who had followed his father everywhere, asking endless questions. That boy was still in there somewhere, underneath the growing man. She hoped she wasn't crushing him under the weight of expectation and necessity. She hoped she was just teaching him what needed to be taught.

When dawn came, it came slowly, as if the day itself was uncertain about breaking through the blizzard. The snow continued, but the quality of the white changed, became less absolute. She could see the outline of the fence now, and the edge of the tree line, and the first hint of the world beyond the shed's small circle of existence.

Tom woke as the light was growing, and he sat up immediately, as if his body had been waiting for this signal. They moved through the shed together, checking all the pens, counting heads, verifying that every ewe had been delivered and every lamb was either with its mother or dried and warmed by the heat lamps.

The final count was sixty-three.

Tom stood in the pale light and looked at the numbers on the board, and Margaret watched his face transform from exhaustion into something like pride.

"We did it," he said.

"We did," Margaret agreed.

The power came back on at quarter past seven. The lights flickered once, twice, and then steadied, and suddenly the shed was bathed in electric light that seemed harsh and strange after the long night. The heaters hummed back to life. The electric lights pushed back the shadows. The world outside began to properly exist again, and with it came the knowledge that other people existed too, that they would come to help, that this nightmare of isolation and responsibility was ending.

Margaret stood still for a moment in the sudden brightness and felt something in her chest unwind, just slightly. Not break, not crack. Just unwind.

"Help me clean up," she said to her son. "There's still work to do."

Tom nodded and moved toward the water bucket, already rolling up his sleeves, and Margaret looked at the peaceful faces of the newborn lambs, their small bodies rising and falling with breath, their existence purchased at the cost of a night of wind and cold and the kind of work that changes a person.

The blizzard had passed. The living remained. Spring had come, even if it came with snow and cold and the knowledge of loss. The ewes would heal. The lambs would grow. And her son would grow too, into a man shaped by this night, by the work and the watching, by the knowledge that some things depend on your hands and your will to survive.

Margaret picked up the soiled towel from the floor and began the work of putting the shed in order, and Tom worked beside her, and outside the wind continued to die, and the snow began to lighten, and the world slowly returned.
