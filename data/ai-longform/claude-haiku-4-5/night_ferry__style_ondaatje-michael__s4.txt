# 

The ferry pushes north through water that has forgotten what warmth is. Marcus stands on deck in the dark, watching the wake dissolve into itself, phosphorescence that blooms and dies in patterns he cannot predict. The ferry is called the *Laurentian*. He learned this from the ticket agent who did not look at him. Everything about the crossing seems determined to erase itself—the lights of the southern port already swallowed by distance, the northern shore still hours away and invisible. He is alone on the deck because it is three in the morning and because the wind carries a particular kind of cold that suggests no living thing should be awake to experience it.

He has not seen his brother in seventeen years. He last saw him in a kitchen in Thunder Bay where their mother was still alive, where she stood between them in a way that was both literal and otherwise, her small hands moving from counter to sink in the mechanical rhythms of a woman whose sons had become strangers to her. The argument—and Marcus can no longer clearly remember what sparked it, what words were exchanged—had the quality of something performative, as though they were both acting the part of men too proud to forgive. His brother left first. Marcus left a week later. By the time their mother died, seven years into this silence, neither of them had a clear path back to the other.

The body was pulled from the water near Fort Severn. A fisherman found it. The police contacted a woman named Sarah Chen who said she had known the deceased, and Sarah Chen called Marcus's number, a number he had not expected to be in anyone's recent memory, and said: *I think you should know. I think it might be him.* She was precise about what she didn't know. Age made identification difficult. The body had been in the water. There were dental records, she said. There were other methods. But she thought perhaps Marcus should come anyway. She thought perhaps he would want to.

The cabin he was assigned smells of industrial cleanser and the phantom odor of hundreds of people. He sits on the lower bunk, his bag unopened on the upper one. He has brought clothes he will not change into. He has brought a book he will not read. He has brought nothing that will help him prepare for the face he may or may not recognize. Outside the small porthole, the darkness is absolute. The ferry cuts through it with the confidence of something that has made this crossing ten thousand times.

He thinks about his brother's face as it was then. The particular way James had of looking at you while deciding whether to trust what you were saying. The small scar on his left eyebrow from a bicycle accident when they were children. The way he used to laugh—not often, but when he did, something in the room would shift, would become briefly less weighted by consequence. Marcus holds these images carefully, aware that memory is a kind of corruption, that his brother's actual face is likely completely different now, altered by seventeen years of living that Marcus knows nothing about. The body pulled from the water would be unrecognizable not just by decay but by time itself.

He gets up and goes back outside.

The water at night looks solid, a black plane extending endlessly. The ferry moves through it without apparent effort, as though the sea is something the ship simply agrees with, a compact neither party needs to examine. The rail is cold under his palms. His fingers are numb in seconds. He leaves them there anyway, watching the wake pattern, the way it rises and falls in the ship's passage. Somewhere ahead is the northern shore. Somewhere ahead is a body in a facility he does not yet know the location of. Somewhere ahead is the possibility that his brother has been dead the entire time Marcus has been living his life—marriage, divorce, a career in architectural surveying that took him to the west coast, to Japan for three years, to places where the language was not his and the sky was a different color. All of that time, his brother might have been lying at the bottom of a bay, becoming something else entirely.

It was not the silence that ended things between them. The silence was only the visible symptom. Before that was the argument, but before the argument was something larger—a divergence that had been happening for years. James had stayed in the north. After high school, he had taken a job with a mining company. He had bought a truck. He had married a woman named Rebecca who, Marcus understood, was no longer married to him, though Marcus's information came only through their mother and was therefore fragmented, editorialized by her attempt to present the facts in a way that would upset him least. His brother had made choices that Marcus, even then, was judging silently. And his brother, it seemed, had known this and resented it. When the words finally came—and Marcus still cannot recall whether he spoke first or simply allowed his expression to say what his brother had already inferred—they had the quality of things that had been building for so long they barely needed to be articulated. Just a name James called him, and just a silence Marcus maintained in response, and the rest was already decided.

The *Laurentian* is perhaps three-quarters full. At breakfast, which Marcus attends because sleep will not come and the cabin feels too small, he sees the other passengers: a woman with a child who will not eat, a man in a suit who reads a newspaper from the day before yesterday, an elderly couple who do not look at anything in particular. Marcus drinks coffee that tastes like it has been brewed to no specific standard, designed only to be hot and brown. He eats toast that has been wrapped in plastic. Around him, the ship's gentle motion continues. He thinks about how many people are crossing this water because they need to, because there is no other route, because some necessity has arranged itself in their lives that requires them to be on the other side.

The child drops a spoon. It falls from the breakfast table to the floor with a sound that seems to travel through the entire dining room. The mother retrieves it without comment. The child—perhaps five years old—looks at Marcus with the unselfconscious gaze of someone not yet fully human, not yet entirely burdened by the knowledge of what others think. Marcus looks away.

He goes to the purser's office and asks what time they arrive. Eight-thirty in the morning, he is told. The officer gives him no particular reason for this precision. He will arrive in a town he has never visited, in a building whose address he has memorized but never seen. Sarah Chen will meet him there. They will look at the body together, or separately—he is not clear on the protocol of this. They will either know or not know, and then the question that has structured his entire crossing will be answered, and he will have to construct some new organizing principle for whatever comes after.

He spends the afternoon in his cabin, lying on the bunk and staring at the ceiling. The ship's movement is gentle enough that he barely feels it, but it is there—a steady rise and fall like being inside something's breathing. He thinks about calling someone. His ex-wife. A friend. But there is nothing to report yet, and reporting nothing would be a kind of torture for them both, a demand for emotional engagement that cannot yet be specified. Instead he takes a shower in a bathroom so small he cannot extend both arms at the same time. The water comes in two temperatures: scalding and off. He chooses scalding and lets it burn his shoulders until they turn red.

By evening, he has settled into a kind of stillness. The fear and anticipation have become ambient—no longer acute but simply the atmosphere he is moving through. He eats dinner alone. He goes to the observation lounge and sits in a chair designed to accommodate someone smaller than he is. Through the windows, the darkness presses against the glass. There is nothing to see. There is only the reflection of the lounge's interior—the fluorescent lights, the other passenger, a man smoking despite the posted prohibition, the carpeted floor with its pattern of intertwined leaves.

The man smoking sees Marcus looking and nods as though they have reached an understanding. Marcus nods back. This is the extent of their connection. After a while, the man leaves. Marcus continues to sit. He is thinking about James's truck, which James had kept meticulously clean, and about how this had seemed to Marcus, at the time, like a kind of affectation, a performance of being a man of a certain type. Now, seventeen years later, Marcus wonders if he was wrong about this. He wonders if there is a kind of grace in maintaining something—a truck, a relationship, a connection to your own brother—even when it seems it might be futile. He wonders if his brother thought of him with contempt or if he simply did not think of him at all.

The night passes. The ferry continues northward. Marcus sleeps in fragments. At one point he wakes with the sense that he has been crying in his sleep, but his face is dry. At another point he realizes he has been lying in absolute darkness, that he has no memory of closing the porthole cover, and he gets up and closes it now, which is perhaps pointless since the darkness outside is the same as the darkness inside, separated only by glass.

At six in the morning he is awake and dressed. He eats nothing. He stands on deck as the darkness thins, as the world becomes gradually visible. The sky goes from black to deep blue to something approaching gray. And then, very slowly, a shoreline emerges. Trees. Stone. A kind of landscape that he recognizes from childhood but that seems foreign to him now, as though he has dreamed it rather than lived it. The water becomes less black. Details assert themselves. Far ahead, though still distant, he can see the shapes of buildings.

The docking takes a long time. The ferry approaches the port with the deliberation of something far heavier than it is, and the actual moment of arrival—when the ship settles against the dock with a sound like a sigh—is anticlimactic. Around him, people begin gathering their things. The woman with the child is suddenly efficient, focused. The elderly couple rises with practiced synchronicity. Everyone moves with a kind of purpose that Marcus lacks.

He stands on the dock with his bag, watching the other passengers move toward the terminal. The air is cold enough that he can see his own breath. There are no other ships. There is no one waiting. The dock is industrial and unpicturesque, devoted entirely to the function of moving people and cargo. Beyond it, the town begins—a few streets of low buildings, a church steeple, the general impression of a place that has given up on impressing anyone.

Sarah Chen texts him the address. It is ten minutes by car. He finds a taxi operated by a driver who recognizes the destination immediately and says nothing about it. This suggests it is not an unusual place to go. The facility, Marcus thinks. The place where they keep bodies that need to be identified. The driver has likely made this drive many times.

The building, when they arrive, looks like a medical office or perhaps a small clinic. It is painted beige. It has a parking lot with five spaces, three of which are occupied. Sarah Chen is waiting outside, and Marcus recognizes her not because he has ever seen her before, but because she is standing in a way that suggests she is waiting for someone, and he is the only person getting out of the taxi.

She is younger than he expected. Forty, perhaps, or younger. She has dark hair pulled back. She is wearing a heavy coat and an expression of professional sympathy that has likely taken her years to perfect. When she introduces herself, she does not attempt to hug him, and he is grateful for this.

"Are you ready?" she asks.

He is not ready. He will never be ready. But he says yes, and she nods, and she leads him into the building.

The interior smells like cold and chemicals and something else underneath—something organic that no amount of cleaning can entirely eliminate. They pass through a hallway. Sarah Chen speaks briefly with someone at a desk. Papers are signed. Marcus does not read what he is signing. A door opens. They descend a short staircase.

"The body was in the water for approximately four weeks," Sarah Chen says. She says this in the way that doctors do, providing information as a kind of preparation, a way of framing what comes next. "Identification has been difficult."

"But you think," Marcus says. He cannot finish the sentence.

"I think," she says, "that there are similarities. The dental records that we have match. His age was consistent. The location is consistent with places where he was known to spend time."

They have stopped at a door. Beyond this door is a body. Beyond this door is either James or not-James, and either way, everything changes. Sarah Chen puts her hand on the handle.

"Your brother," she says, "was a good man. I want you to know that before you go in. I didn't know him for very long, but I knew him well enough to know that."

Marcus wants to ask her what she means by this, why someone he hasn't spoken to in seventeen years deserves this testimonial. But he understands that it is kindness, and he accepts it as such.

The body is covered with a sheet. It is smaller than Marcus expected. The face is visible, and yes, it is James, and no, it is not James, because this thing has been transformed by water and time into something that is only technically his brother. The scar on the eyebrow is still there. The shape of the face is still there. But the specific expression that Marcus has carried in his memory—the particular way James had of looking at you—this is gone, replaced by the neutral mask of someone who has been suspended in the present moment for a very long time.

Marcus stands there. He does not cry. He does not move. He is aware of Sarah Chen to his left, giving him space, allowing him whatever reaction he needs to have. He is aware of the fluorescent light and the cold and the sound of some machinery running elsewhere in the building. He is aware, above all, of the profound and unexpected fact of his brother's absence—not the absence of a living man, but the absence of anything that might bridge the gap that had grown between them.

"Yes," he says. He says this so quietly that Sarah Chen has to lean in to hear. "It's him."

She nods. She does not offer sympathy, which he appreciates. She allows him to stand there for as long as he needs. When he finally turns away, it is without ceremony. He walks back toward the door. He does not look back.
