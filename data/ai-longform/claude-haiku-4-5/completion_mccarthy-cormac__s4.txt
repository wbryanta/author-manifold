They tore at him with a fury that seemed to come from some deep well of ancestral rage and he fought back silently without voice or cry as if the very act of utterance might somehow confirm his guilt in whatever transgression he'd committed by existing among them. He felt his clothes rent and his skin laid open and he drove his fists into the formless mass that surrounded him but there was no substance to them, they were like shadows themselves and when his hands passed through them they offered no resistance yet their claws found purchase in his flesh readily enough. He woke gasping and thrashing and she had him pinned with her hands on his shoulders, her face close to his in the darkness, her breath sour with sleep.

Hush now, she said again. Hush.

He lay there heaving and she released him and rolled away to her side of the bed and he sat there in the dark with his heart hammering in his chest like something trapped and trying to escape its cage. The room smelled of old wood and older sweat and something else, something medicinal that she used for the pain. Through the window he could see the false dawn, that peculiar lightening that comes before the true light breaks, and he rose and pulled on his pants and made his way downstairs.

The kitchen was cold and he built a fire in the stove and stood before it warming his hands though they were already warm from the fever that seemed never to leave him now. He looked out at the yard slowly filling with grey light and at the oak tree where they'd hanged the boy in the summer of his fourteenth year. Or rather where they'd found him hanged, swinging from a rope thrown over the lowest branch, his feet not three feet from the ground. Whether by his own hand or another's had never been determined and he'd not pressed the matter. The boy had always been peculiar, had always borne some weight that seemed not suited to his years, and perhaps he'd simply decided that the carrying of it was no longer worth the trouble.

He heard her moving about upstairs and then descending and she came into the kitchen wrapped in a shawl that had been her mother's, a thing of grey wool and faded pattern, and she moved heavily and he watched her with a mixture of pity and contempt that he couldn't quite separate into its component parts. She sat at the table without speaking and he poured water into a kettle and set it on the fire.

They're saying on the road that there's been another one, she said.

He said nothing.

Down near the river crossings. A man found shot and left in a ditch. They're saying it was done during the night and that whoever done it took his horse and what money he had.

He set out the cups and when the water came to a boil he poured it out and added the bitter root that she favored and pushed the cup across the table toward her. She wrapped her hands around it and blew across the surface.

They say there's a band of them, she continued. Men on horseback moving through the country taking what they please from whoever they encounter. They say they're heading north toward the mountains.

He turned away and looked out the window at the lightening sky. Somewhere in that direction a man lay in a ditch with a bullet in him, and somewhere else other men rode on horses they didn't own with money that wasn't theirs, and all of it seemed to him equally inconsequential, all of it part of some vast machinery of plunder and violence that had been grinding on since long before he was born and would continue grinding long after he was gone.

You should arm yourself, she said. Bar the doors at night.

He said nothing to this either. They both knew that there was no arming against what was coming, that the doors of the world stood open and there was no bar strong enough to hold back the tide of human misery and desperation that flowed through it. The boy had understood this. Perhaps that's why he'd chosen the rope.

He went out into the yard when the light had come fully and he worked through the morning mending fence and cutting wood and doing the small tasks that were required to maintain the illusion that the place could be maintained, that it would not simply fall into ruin like everything else left to the natural processes of decay and entropy. By midday the sun was hot and he sat in the shade of the oak tree with his back against the trunk and he closed his eyes and tried not to think about anything at all, but the mind was not an instrument that could be simply switched off and it turned over instead on the dream and on the terrible certainty that had come to him in the moment when the sun buckled and dark fell like a shout, that certainty being that the sun would not return, that somewhere in the mechanics of the world's operation a gear had slipped or a crucial part had broken and all of this, the light and the heat and the cycling of days and seasons, was nothing more than memory, that they were all already in darkness and what they saw and felt was only the ghost of the world as it had been, that they were haunting the earth rather than living upon it.

He opened his eyes and looked out across the fields toward the road and he saw dust rising there and he watched it for some time before he rose and went back to the house. She was resting and he didn't wake her. He took down the rifle from above the doorway and he checked it though he had no ammunition and he leaned it against the wall again. Then he sat in the chair by the window and he waited.

The riders came into view in the late afternoon, four of them moving without haste along the road toward the house. They were ragged and lean and armed in various ways with rifles and pistols and one of them had a knife the size of a short sword hanging from his belt. They did not look like men to whom the distinction between taking and stealing meant much of anything. They turned into the yard and dismounted and the tallest of them, a man with a scar that split his face from temple to jaw, approached the door and knocked.

He rose and opened it.

We're looking for shelter, the man said. And food and water for the animals.

There's not much here.

We're not asking for much.

He looked past them at the horses standing in the yard with their ribs visible and their coats dark with sweat and he knew they'd ridden hard and far. He looked at their faces and he saw in them the same vacancy he'd seen in so many other faces in these latter days, as if the world's violence had hollowed them out and left them as nothing more than vessels for appetites they didn't themselves understand.

The woman came down the stairs and appeared behind him in the doorway and she put her hand on his shoulder and he felt her trembling slightly.

We have some bread and some beans, she said. And there's hay in the barn for the animals.

The scar-faced man studied her for a moment and then he smiled, and it was not a pleasant expression.

We'll take that, he said.

They went through the house with the casual ownership of men who had decided that everything they saw belonged to them, and they sat at the table and ate what she'd prepared and they drank the water he brought them and they studied the small room as if cataloguing it, though there was little enough to catalogue. Old furniture and older pictures on the walls and shelves with a few books and some dishes and a clock that had stopped working years ago and that he'd never bothered to repair because he no longer had any use for knowing the time.

When they'd finished eating the smallest of them, a wiry man with a face like something carved from wood, rose and went to the window and looked out at the darkening yard.

Nice place, he said. Peaceful. Quiet. A man could rest here.

They plan to stay? she asked, and he heard the strain in her voice, the careful control with which she spoke.

The scar-faced man looked at her.

We'll stay the night, he said. We'll move on in the morning.

There's only two rooms upstairs, she said.

Then we'll make do, he said. We've made do with less.

When darkness had fallen fully and she had shown them where they could sleep and come back downstairs she sat at the table with her hands folded and she looked at him across the space between them and he could see the fear working in her, the terrible mathematics of what might happen before the sun came up again.

There's a rifle, she said quietly.

He knew what she was suggesting.

It won't matter, he said.

It might.

There's four of them and they're armed. The rifle's not loaded.

She was quiet for a long moment.

Then we should leave, she said. We should take what we can carry and leave in the dark.

And go where?

I don't know. Anywhere. North toward the mountains like they said.

He looked at her hands, at the way they lay on the table as if they were something foreign to her body, something she'd found there and didn't quite know what to do with.

You could go, he said. You and the child. It's not too far to the Hendersons. They might take you in.

I'm not leaving you here.

There's no point in both of us dying.

I'm not dying, she said. And neither are you.

But even as she said it she didn't believe it and he could see that disbelief written clear across her face, and he wondered at what point the human will to persist in the face of overwhelming odds tipped over into something like insanity, what force it was that kept people alive and moving through the world when all the evidence suggested that death was the only reasonable outcome.

She rose and climbed the stairs again and he heard her moving about and then silence. He sat alone in the kitchen with the fire dying in the stove and the darkness pressing at the windows and he felt the peculiar peace that comes sometimes before violence, that moment of suspension when the world holds its breath and everything is possible and nothing has yet occurred and the future remains still unmade.

He must have slept because he woke to the sound of screaming.

It was her voice and it came from upstairs and he was moving before he'd fully woken, moving without thought or plan, just the body responding to the sound in the way bodies do. He took the stairs two at a time and he found the door to their bedroom open and light from a lamp spilling out across the floor and he went through it into a scene that would mark him as surely as the prophet's sun had marked the dreamer.

The scar-faced man was on the bed and she was beneath him and her arms were thrashing and she was screaming and the other man, the wiry one, was standing by the window with his pistol in his hand and he turned when he heard the footsteps and he raised the weapon but in the moment of that raising the knowledge came to him, the terrible knowledge that he'd been waiting for all along, the knowledge that the sun was never coming back, that the light had failed finally and completely and all that remained was this darkness and these acts of violence and degradation occurring within it, and that to resist was meaningless, was perhaps even less than meaningless, was a kind of obscene assertion of the self in a world where the self no longer had any value at all.

The gun fired and he felt the impact but it was distant somehow, as if it had happened to someone else, and he continued forward and he saw the wiry man's eyes widen in the moment before his hands found the man's throat. The scar-faced man was off the bed now and moving toward him and he felt the blade of the great knife slide in through his ribs but he didn't let go and the three of them were tangled together in a strange dance that might have been a waltz or a wrestling match or simply the final convulsive movements of something dying.

There was blood everywhere, on the walls and the floor and the bed, and he could smell it, that metallic salt that was the signature of violence, and somewhere underneath it he could still smell her, could still smell the lavender soap she used though he hadn't known she used it until that moment when he smelled it mixed with blood and death and the acrid stench of fear.

Then the other two men were there, the ones he'd forgotten about, and they were pulling him away and bearing him to the ground and one of them, the one whose face he never saw clearly, was hammering at him with the butt of his pistol, and he covered his face with his arms and he thought about the boy hanging from the oak tree and he thought about the sun buckled and dark falling like a shout and he thought about the prophet looking down at him with surprise and something that might have been pity.

When it was over and the men had finished with him they tied his hands and they tied hers and they gathered up what valuables the house contained and they set a fire in the kitchen and they rode away into the night and left the two of them there on the bed with the smoke rising around them and the flames creeping toward them like something alive and reaching.

He didn't move. She was whimpering but he didn't move. The heat grew more intense and he could feel his hair beginning to curl and singe and still he didn't move because to move would be to acknowledge that there was still something worth saving, some possibility of continuation, and he had moved beyond that now into a place where nothing mattered, where even pain and death were simply variations on the same theme of meaninglessness that had been playing since the beginning of time.

But she moved. Of course she moved. She twisted and she strained at the ropes and she called out to him and when he didn't respond she called out to anyone, to God or the devil or whatever force might be listening in the darkness beyond the flames, and at last he moved too, not to escape but to reach across and find her hand in the dark and to hold it as the fire consumed them both.
