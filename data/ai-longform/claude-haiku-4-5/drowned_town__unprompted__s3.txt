The surveying equipment weighed less than she remembered from training, though maybe that was only because she had grown stronger, or perhaps because dread was a lightness she'd learned to carry. Margaret stood at the reservoir's edge with her transit case at her feet, looking out at the exposed basin where Millbrook used to be. The water line had retreated so far it seemed almost shy about what it had uncovered—a quarter mile back, leaving behind a margin of pale rock and silt the color of old bone.

She'd been nine years old when they flooded the town. That was thirty-four years ago. She remembered standing on the bridge with her father as the water rose, watching it creep across the street where the Hendersons' grocery had been, where her mother bought flour in paper sacks that left white ghosts on her fingers. The water had looked like mercy then, like a kind of forgetting. Now it retreated and revealed, and Margaret had come back to measure what had been lost, what was being found.

The county assessor had called her specifically for this job. "You know the area," he'd said over the phone, his voice thin and apologetic. "You grew up there. We need someone who understands what we're looking at." What Margaret understood was that nostalgia and professional obligation had been weaponized against her, that some part of her—some stupid, tender part—wanted to know if her childhood was still down there, perfectly preserved, waiting.

She set up the transit at the first coordinate point, a spot marked on the old maps that corresponded to what had been the intersection of Elm and Main. The transit legs sank slightly into the silt as she positioned it. Everything about this landscape was unstable. The exposed ground was still wet in places, muddy in others, and the smell rising from it was something between rot and rust, ancient and fresh at once.

Margaret worked the transit with mechanical precision, recording angles and distances. The math was clean. The math didn't require her to remember anything. But her eyes couldn't help themselves. They wandered as she worked, picking out shapes in the silt—the remains of a foundation here, what might have been a doorway there. She recognized the geometric outline of a cellar, its stone walls still largely intact. She knew which house that was. The Carmichaels. They'd had a collie named Scout that used to chase her bicycle down this street.

As the morning progressed, she moved through the drowned grid of streets, setting up her transit at each marked point, recording bearings and elevations. The work was methodical and clean, which made the intrusions of memory more jarring. A rusted mailbox jutting from the silt. A child's tin toy, a circus wagon, half-buried and cadmium-yellow paint still bright. The scattered bricks of what had been someone's chimney.

She found herself standing at what she was certain had been the corner of Birch Street, where she'd lived. The numbers on her map said so. The configuration of the land said so. But there was nothing visible—no foundation, no basement, no clear sign of habitation. Either the house had been built on soil that washed away more easily, or the memory of where it stood had become less fixed than she'd thought. That possibility unsettled her more than finding it would have.

Margaret sat down on a ridge of higher ground and opened her lunch—a sandwich she'd made that morning in her motel room, wrapped in wax paper. She ate mechanically and tried not to think about walking down this street on the way to school, tried not to think about the particular quality of light that came through the maple trees in summer, tried not to think about her mother's voice calling her in for supper or the sound of the screen door slapping shut.

A vehicle came down the access road to the reservoir—a truck, moving slowly. It parked a quarter mile away, and a man got out, older, with gray at his temples. He stood looking at the exposed basin with his hands on his hips. Margaret recognized the stance of someone looking at a grave they'd opened deliberately. The man noticed her eventually and walked over.

"You're doing the survey," he said. Not a question.

"County work." Margaret stood, brushing crumbs from her pants. "Are you—were you from here?"

"Born here. Lived here till the water came." He was looking at her more closely now, squinting. "You're Margaret Ashford. You're Tom and Helen's daughter."

She nodded. She didn't ask his name. She could see it in his face—he'd been older than her, high school probably while she was in elementary. She wouldn't have known him.

"I come back sometimes," he said. "Usually when the water's high. When you can't see anything. It's strange to see it like this. It's like the town's a skeleton now instead of just being dead." He paused. "That came out wrong."

"No," Margaret said. "It's accurate."

"Were your parents still here when they flooded it?"

"They moved before. My father took a job in the county seat. We went with him." She could have added that her mother had cried about it, had driven back to Millbrook the week before the water came and walked through their house in silence, touching walls. She could have said that her father had forbidden her from coming with her mother on that day, that he'd said it was better to let things go cleanly. But none of that was relevant to the survey work.

"My parents stayed," the man said. "Got relocated money. Didn't matter. They spent the next ten years trying to rebuild somewhere else and it never felt right. My mother said it was like losing a limb but the limb kept hurting." He looked at Margaret directly. "You didn't come back, though. Not till now."

"I didn't have reason to."

He nodded as if that made sense, as if people didn't need reasons to return to the places that had shaped them. He gave her a name—David Reeves—and told her he'd been the postmaster's son, which triggered something in Margaret, a vague image of sorting mail behind a counter. Then he wished her luck with the survey and walked back to his truck, moving slowly across the silt-covered ground. Margaret watched him drive away.

By late afternoon, she'd completed the survey of the residential blocks that had occupied the town's western edge. She marked her final points and began the careful work of packing her equipment. The sun was moving lower, pulling long shadows from the exposed foundations. The exposed town was becoming something else as the light changed—less a grave, more a ruin. More like something ancient, something that deserved to be studied and recorded precisely because it was gone.

She was fastening the transit case when she saw what she recognized as the front steps of the school building. Three steps of stone, leading up to nothing now. She walked toward them without meaning to, measuring the distance with her body, counting off the feet automatically. The steps had been painted red when she was a student. She could see the ghost of that red in the stone, faded almost to white. They'd climbed these steps thousands of times, her and the other children from town. They'd spit from the top of them into the grass during hot summers. They'd sat on them during recess, carving initials with stolen tools.

Margaret climbed the three steps and turned to look back across what had been the town center. From this vantage point, she could see the layout more clearly—the pattern of streets, the distribution of buildings, the way the town had organized itself around this civic center. How small it all was. How comprehensible from a height. The church had been two blocks that way. The library one block over. The train station at the far edge. All of it still there, all of it still here, just rendered into stone and foundation and absence.

She sat down on the third step, the one at the top, and let herself be where she was for the first time all day. The silt around the school building was still wet. It would dry, she knew. Probably within days, depending on the temperature. The county would compile her survey data and make reports and decisions about whether the exposed area needed structural assessment, whether anything could or should be salvaged. The reservoir would fill again eventually, or maybe it wouldn't—drought was becoming the permanent condition. Either way, Millbrook would be gone again, or already gone, the tense becoming unclear when you were dealing with something in the process of disappearing for the second time.

Her phone buzzed in her pocket. She ignored it.

As a child, she'd read books about lost cities. Atlantis and Pompeii and the mythical towns of the Old Testament. She'd wondered what it would be like to stand in a place after it had been erased and then revealed, to know both the before and after, to witness resurrection. Now she understood that it was lonelier than that, emptier. The places themselves weren't waiting to be found again. They were simply dust made visible. The people who had inhabited them—they were the only loss that mattered, and they were gone absolutely.

The sun was nearly touching the ridge west of the basin. Margaret stood and made her way back down the steps, then set about the business of closing up her work site for the day. She secured the transit case in her truck, checked her data sheets, confirmed her coordinates. Everything was in order. Tomorrow she would survey the eastern blocks, the commercial district. The day after that, if the weather held, the residential blocks on the northern slope. By the end of the week, she'd have mapped everything that had been visible below the waterline.

She was driving back toward the access road when she saw it—or when she understood what she was seeing. A foundation she recognized immediately. The pattern of the walls was unmistakable. This was the bank, the brick building where her father had kept his accounts, where he'd taken her once in the summer to show her the safe room, let her touch the smooth metal of the door. This foundation was remarkably intact. The walls had collapsed inward, but they hadn't scattered. You could trace the architecture from the ruins, could understand the intention of the original structure.

Margaret pulled her truck to the side of the road and got out. She had the transit case with her almost before she'd consciously decided to measure this building. She set it up at what would have been the front corner of the structure, looked through the sight glass, and recorded angles. She mapped the interior space, marked where the supporting walls had been, indicated the dimensions of the safe room in the back. All of this was unnecessary—the foundation wasn't on the route she'd been assigned to survey. But she did the work anyway, carefully, with precision, because precision was what she had.

When she finished, the light was nearly gone. She stood in the ruins of the bank and thought about the way her father's hands had smelled like the leather of his wallet. She thought about how he'd never come back here either, after they'd left. He'd died eleven years ago, in the county seat, having never again set foot in Millbrook.

The drive back to town—to the other town, the new one, the one that had absorbed the refugees from Millbrook and kept living—took forty minutes. Margaret rented a room in a motel off the highway, one of those thin-walled places with fluorescent lighting and a bed that sagged in the center. She ate dinner at a diner that had a pie menu laminated to the inside of the cover, and she sat in a booth watching the darkness gather outside the window. A couple sat across the diner, elderly, sharing a piece of pecan pie with two forks. They didn't speak to each other, but they seemed entirely comfortable in that silence, entirely certain of the other's presence.

Back in her room, Margaret transcribed her notes and organized her data. She marked the bank foundation on her map with red ink, knowing she shouldn't have, knowing it wasn't part of her assignment. She drew precise lines indicating its geometry and tried not to think of it as a memorial.

She dreamed that night of the water rising. But in the dream, it rose silently, without rushing, a gentle tide that submerged everything with grace. People stood watching from boats, accepting it. There was no panic, no scrambling. It was a kind of baptism, a resetting. When the water reached the top of the school steps where Margaret stood, she didn't climb higher. She let it come, felt it cool against her skin, felt it lifting her up—

She woke to gray light and the sound of traffic from the highway. Her heart was beating slowly, calmly. For a moment, she lay in the sagging bed and tried to hold onto the feeling of weightlessness the dream had left her with.

The next day's work took her into the commercial district. She set up her transit at marked coordinates and measured the foundations of the bank again—the real bank, the sunken one—and the grocery store and the pharmacy and the hardware store. Each foundation told her something about how the town had been built, how it had organized its commerce and its values. The hardware store had been larger than she remembered, its footprint spreading across nearly a quarter block. The pharmacy had been smaller, which surprised her. She'd remembered it as imposing, a place of adult importance.

Around midday, she stopped to eat and found herself driving to the edge of the exposed basin, parking at a different spot than the day before. She walked out into the silt, following what she realized had been the main road leading out of town. The asphalt was long gone, eroded by water and time, but the grade remained. You could walk along the line it had traced through the landscape, could feel yourself following a path that thousands of people had followed during the last years before the water came.

She walked this path for nearly a mile before turning back. Her boots were caked with pale mud, and her legs ached in a pleasant way, a kind of tiredness that felt earned. As she approached her truck, she noticed another vehicle parked nearby—a sedan, older, with out-of-state plates. A woman stood beside it, looking at the basin. She was Margaret's age, roughly, with long gray hair and hiking boots. When she noticed Margaret, she seemed to consider whether to speak.

"I grew up here," the woman said finally. "I come back every few years to see if anything's changed. Usually the water's too high to see anything. This is remarkable. Terrible, but remarkable."

"You were from Millbrook," Margaret said.

"For the first sixteen years of my life. Then I lived everywhere else." The woman smiled slightly. "I don't know why I keep coming back. There's nothing here anymore. But I think maybe that's why I keep coming back—because there's nothing here anymore. It matters more somehow."

Margaret understood this perfectly. She understood that the woman was expressing something she'd been unable to articulate since arriving. The emptiness wasn't a lack. It was a specific thing, a presence made visible by its negation.

"I'm surveying it," Margaret said. "Mapping it. The county sent me because I grew up here."

"That seems like it would be difficult."

"It's better this way," Margaret said. "The numbers help. The precision."

The woman nodded. They stood together looking at the exposed streets, two women who had learned to live elsewhere, who had built entire lives in the absence of this place. The sun was warm on Margaret's face. She could smell the mineral-rich earth, the old decay, the ghost of water. Somewhere in that exposed ground were the details of her childhood—not metaphorically, but actually. The dirt was made of that place. The stones had supported people she'd known.

"I think about my mother," the woman said quietly. "She was pregnant when we left. My sister was born in the new town, in a hospital instead of at home, which was how it would have been here. And I always think—my sister doesn't exist in this place, you know? Not even as a memory. She only exists in the context of absence." The woman turned to look at Margaret. "Does that make sense?"

"Yes," Margaret said. "It does."

She returned to her methodical work that afternoon and the next day and the day after that. By Friday, she'd completed the survey of the entire mapped area. She'd recorded hundreds of data points, measured the footprints of the buildings and the heights of the remaining walls, indicated where the streets had been and how they'd aligned with the natural contours of the land. She'd created a comprehensive map of Millbrook as it existed now—not as it had been, but as it was: a record of erasure, a monument to absence.

On her last afternoon, Margaret found herself at the school building again. She climbed the three red-painted steps and sat where she'd sat before. The silt had dried some in the interval of days. The ground was becoming more solid, more itself, a place where new growth might eventually take hold. The wildflowers were already beginning to appear—small purple flowers, delicate, pushing up through the exposed earth. They were quick colonizers, the first plants to move into disturbed ground.

She had the complete survey data with her, printed and bound. The county would use it to make their reports and decisions. Someone would file it away. Maybe someday someone else would look at it, would want to understand how Millbrook had been laid out, what it had occupied. The data would outlast her, outlast the people who'd lived here. It was a strange kind of permanence, a preservation made of numbers and angles.

Margaret opened the bound report and looked at her own work. The maps were clean and precise. You could see the logic of the town in them. You could understand how people had moved through these streets, how they'd organized their lives around these intersections. It was beautiful work, she thought. Necessary work. The kind of work that honored what had been lost by refusing to forget it, by insisting on its reality through measurement and record.

She closed the report and held it against her chest. The sun was beginning to move toward the western ridge. Tomorrow morning she would pack her equipment and drive back to the county seat. She would file her survey data and write her report. She would return to her small apartment and her careful life, to the distance she'd created between herself and this place.

But for now, she sat on the school steps as the light changed and the shadows grew longer. She let herself feel the weight of the time that had passed, the strangeness of being here again, the oddness of her own persistence. She was alive. The town was not. They occupied the same space for this moment, and that moment was enough. The numbers would hold it. The maps would preserve it. The absence itself was something real, something that could be measured and recorded, something that had weight and dimension and worth.

The wildflowers quivered slightly in a breeze Margaret couldn't quite feel. She watched them and thought of nothing in particular, which was its own kind of peace. Eventually, the light would fail completely. Eventually, she would leave. But not yet. For now, she could sit here with the exposed earth and the ghost of the town and the careful record she'd made, and that was enough. That was everything. That was the whole point.
