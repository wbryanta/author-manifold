They tore at his clothes and his flesh and he raised his arms against them but there was no stopping them. He cried out but his voice was lost in their howling and cursing. He fell beneath them into the mud of that dark square and they were upon him like dogs on a carcass when she shook him again, harder this time, and he woke gasping and flailing and nearly struck her before he knew where he was.

She grabbed his wrist. It's alright, she said. It's alright. He was drenched in sweat and his heart hammered in his chest like something trying to break free of a cage. He sat gasping in the dark, the dream still clinging to him like grave cloth. Gradually the shapes of the room resolved themselves—the window, the chair, the wardrobe against the far wall. Outside the night was absolute.

What time is it? he said.

Late, she said. Or early. I don't know. You were screaming.

I wasn't screaming.

You were. You woke me from a sound sleep. You were calling out like something possessed.

He swung his legs over the side of the bed and sat with his head in his hands. The dream had begun to fade but its residue remained, a psychic scar tissue of dread. He could not remember what he had been dreaming about but he could remember the feeling of it, the sense of inevitability, of deserved punishment.

I'm going downstairs, he said.

It's the middle of the night.

I don't care.

He rose and felt his way into his trousers and pulled a shirt over his head and moved barefoot down the stairs in darkness. The house around him was silent and heavy with sleep. When he reached the kitchen he lit the lamp and stood looking at nothing in particular, his arms crossed over his chest.

The kitchen was cold. The fire in the stove had burned down to embers. He opened the grate and began to add wood, methodical and deliberate, as if the task required his full attention. The wood caught and flames began to lick up through the iron grate. He stood warming his hands, watching the fire grow, thinking of nothing at all.

His wife came down in her robe, her hair unbound and her face swollen with sleep. She looked at him with concern.

You'll start a fire, she said, meaning the stove.

I'm cold.

Come back to bed.

No.

You're troubled about something. You have been for weeks now. Since before Christmas.

He did not answer her. He turned back to the fire and continued to watch the flames.

Is it about the land? she said. The creditors?

It's nothing.

It's not nothing. You lie awake half the night. You start at sounds. You barely speak to anyone. You're like a man being hunted.

I'm not being hunted.

Then what is it?

He turned to face her. Her expression was plaintive, full of a concern that he could not bear to see. He looked away again.

I had a dream, he said.

A dream. Just a dream then. Come to bed.

No. You wouldn't understand.

Try me.

He shook his head. How could he explain it to her? How could he explain to this woman, heavy with his child, that he had dreamed of a multitude of the afflicted and that he had been set apart from them, marked, judged, and found wanting? How could he tell her that in the dream he had deserved their violence, had in fact invited it?

Go back upstairs, he said.

I won't. Not until you tell me what's the matter.

He turned away from her and stared into the fire. Somewhere in the house, a clock ticked away the hours. Outside, the winter night pressed against the windows like something alive and searching.

There was a man, he said finally, his voice low. Years ago, before I knew you. Before I was any better than I had any right to be.

She waited.

He owed me money. A gambling debt. It was a considerable sum. He came to me with a proposal—he would work it off. He proposed to steal from his employer. I didn't refuse him. I didn't tell him to find another way. I let him go ahead with it.

And did he?

Yes. He took the money and gave it to me. But he was caught. They arrested him. He was tried and convicted and hanged.

She said nothing. He could see her hand upon the back of a chair, gripping it.

It was not my doing, he said. I didn't force him to steal. I didn't turn him in. Those were his choices.

But you accepted the money.

Yes.

And you never went to the authorities. You never spoke in his behalf.

No.

And he was hanged.

Yes. He was hanged and buried and that was the end of it. Or so I thought. But I've been thinking lately that the end of it was no such thing. That the debt is still outstanding, that some account remains unpaid.

She moved to sit at the table. She drew her robe about her more tightly.

What was his name? she said.

Hardin. His name was Robert Hardin.

And his family? Did he have family?

A wife and children. Three children I believe. I never inquired too closely.

She looked at him with an expression he could not read. After a long moment she spoke.

That was not your sin, she said. What he did he did of his own will. You cannot take that onto yourself.

Can't I? Why not? I profited from his ruin. I took his money and I let him hang.

Because he made his choice. And you made yours. And you cannot unwind it now no matter how much you suffer over it. You cannot bring him back. You cannot restore what was taken from him.

She was right, of course. She was nearly always right. But knowing that something was true and feeling it to be true were two different things. He could know that he was not responsible for Hardin's death, but he could not feel it. He could not shake the sense that he was complicit, that he bore some portion of the guilt.

He sat down across from her at the table. The lamp cast their shadows on the wall, elongated and strange.

When I was a boy, she said, there was a woman in our village who had gone mad. Everyone said it was because of something she had done, some sin she had committed. Some said she had killed her own child. Some said she had killed a man in his sleep. No one really knew. But she wandered the streets and the children would throw stones at her and she would wail and tear at her hair and call out to God for forgiveness. And one day she disappeared. They found her three days later in the river. And when they pulled her out she was dead and at peace for the first time in years. And I remember my mother saying that she had finally paid the price of whatever it was she had done. But I never believed that. I never believed that she had done anything at all. I believed that she had simply convinced herself that she was guilty of something, and that the belief had driven her mad.

He looked at his wife. She was looking past him at something beyond the wall of the kitchen, beyond the house itself.

I don't know what you want me to say, he said.

I want you to say that you won't let that happen to you. That you won't let the ghost of a dead man you barely knew destroy what you have built here, with me, in this place.

He reached across the table and took her hand. It was warm from the robe, small and capable.

I don't know if I can promise that, he said.

Then you have to try. You have to try harder than you have been trying.

They sat in silence for a long time after that, watching the fire burn down again in the stove. Gradually the false light of predawn began to seep into the kitchen through the east window, and with it came a sense that the night was truly over, that whatever darkness had held dominion in that hour was being dispelled.

She rose and went upstairs and he heard her moving about above him, preparing for the day. After a while he rose as well and dressed properly and began to make breakfast. By the time she came back down the kitchen was warm and there was food ready on the table and the day had begun in earnest.

But he could not shake the dream. Over the following weeks it returned to him in various forms. Sometimes he was the prophet and sometimes he was the afflicted multitude and sometimes he was the crowd that turned against him in the darkness. He would wake gasping and drenched in sweat and his wife would hold him and he would lie awake until dawn, staring at the ceiling and wondering if she was right, if he was simply manufacturing guilt, or if he was perceiving some deeper truth about the nature of his sin.

Winter deepened around them. Snow fell and lay thick on the fields and roads. The river froze over. He went about his business as usual—attending to the property, meeting with his creditors, attempting to negotiate some arrangement that would keep the farm solvent. But there was a distraction in him now, a divided attention.

One day in late January he received a letter. He did not recognize the handwriting. He opened it with some trepidation and found it to be from a lawyer in the city, inquiring about his whereabouts and requesting a meeting regarding a matter of inheritance. The letter was dated several months prior and had clearly taken a long time to find him.

He sat with the letter in his hands for a long time before showing it to his wife.

It must be some mistake, she said. What inheritance could you possibly have?

Perhaps it's from someone I knew. Someone who died.

But you're not expecting anyone to die. You're not expecting anything.

No, he said. I'm not.

The meeting was arranged for a week hence. He rode into the city on a grey morning, the fields around him all white and empty. The lawyer's office was on the third floor of a brick building on the main street. A clerk showed him in and he stood looking about at the mahogany furniture and the law books and the portrait of a stern man on the wall, presumably the lawyer himself.

The lawyer proved to be a small man with steel-rimmed glasses and a precise manner of speech. He gestured for his visitor to sit.

I'm grateful you received my letter, the lawyer said. I've been trying to locate you for some time. Your last address was outdated.

What is this about? he said.

About an inheritance, as I stated. Or rather, about the execution of a will. A man named Robert Hardin, who died some twenty years ago, left instructions in his will that a sum of money be held in trust and paid out upon certain conditions. Those conditions have now been met. The money is to go to you.

He felt something in his chest contract. He couldn't speak.

The amount is substantial, the lawyer continued. Some fifteen hundred dollars. It's been accruing interest for two decades. The interest alone constitutes a considerable sum.

I don't understand, he said.

The will states that the money is to be paid to you in recompense for monies provided by you at a time of financial difficulty. There's a letter as well, from Mr. Hardin, to be delivered with the payment. I have it here.

The lawyer produced a yellowed envelope and slid it across the desk. The writing on the envelope was shaky and irregular, as if written by someone in distress.

He took the envelope but did not open it. He sat holding it in his hands as if it might explode.

The conditions, the lawyer said, were that the money should only be paid if you had not gone to the authorities regarding Mr. Hardin's crime, and if you had not otherwise profited from his conviction. Both of those conditions appear to have been satisfied.

How could you possibly know that? he said.

There was a clause requiring investigation. We employed a detective to look into the matter. There's nothing in your record to suggest you were complicit in Mr. Hardin's arrest or prosecution. Indeed, there's nothing to suggest you even knew him particularly well.

He said nothing. He stared at the envelope in his hands.

I should tell you also, the lawyer said, that Mr. Hardin's family has been provided for under separate arrangements. His widow received an annuity. His children received education funds. He was a man of some principle, despite his unfortunate circumstances.

He opened the envelope and removed the letter. It was dated twenty years prior, from the night before Hardin's execution. The writing was crude but legible.

The letter read: To the man I am obliged to name, though I do so with sorrow and contrition. I write to you on the eve of my death to set certain matters in order. I have made arrangements for my family, which is the first duty of a man, guilty or not. But I write also to you to say this: whatever you may believe about your own complicity in my fate, I want you to know that I do not hold you responsible. I came to you with a desperate scheme and you did not forbid me, but neither did you compel me. The choice was mine and it was a poor choice, but it was mine own. What I regret is that I could not provide for the man who, however inadvertently, gave me the means to try. Therefore I have made this arrangement, so that you might know that your conscience is clear. Spend this money and know yourself absolved. Spend it and forget me, for I am no more than a shadow now and a shadow has no power to harm the living. Yours in this final matter, Robert Hardin.

He read the letter twice and then three times, each time feeling something unwind in his chest, some knot that had been tied there for twenty years. When he was finished he folded it carefully and placed it back in the envelope and looked up at the lawyer.

When can I have the money? he said.

Immediately, if you wish. The funds are in escrow. I can have a draft prepared within the hour.

He nodded. He had no use for the money—it would solve none of his problems, which were ongoing and intractable. But he would take it anyway. He would take it and he would know what Hardin's forgiveness felt like, at least in the material sense.

He rode back to the farm in darkness, the draft folded carefully in his coat. The snow had begun to fall again and the world was muffled and strange. When he reached home his wife was waiting for him, and he told her everything—the lawyer, the money, the letter, all of it.

She wept when he showed her Hardin's words.

You see? she said. You see? There was no debt. There was nothing to pay.

No, he said. There was nothing to pay.

But he would still carry it with him, he knew. The memory of Hardin would still haunt him, even if the guilt had been lifted. Some stains could be forgiven but not removed, could be absolved but not forgotten. And perhaps that was as it should be. Perhaps a man was meant to carry such things with him all his days, as a reminder of the fragility of his virtue and the ease with which he might slip into complicity with another's suffering.

That night he slept without dreaming, and in the morning the snow had covered everything, and he rose and went about his work as if he had been born again into a world made new.
