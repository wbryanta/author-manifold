# The Smoke

The radio died on a Tuesday in June, which was worse than any other day it could have chosen because Tuesday was when Marcus usually checked in from the ranger station, and when he didn't hear from her by evening, he would worry in that particular way men worry—not frantically, but with a kind of persistent, methodical concern that eventually translates into action. She knew this about Marcus the way she knew the exact angle of light that meant three o'clock, the way she knew which board on the north platform would creak if you stepped on it without distributing your weight properly.

The radio had been coughing for weeks. Sarah had radioed down twice about the replacement, and both times Marcus had said the same thing: *We'll get to it.* Which meant they wouldn't, not before August, not before she left. After fourteen summers in the tower, after fourteen summers of perfect season records and no false alarms and thank-you letters from the Forest Service that came on letterhead that felt too official for what amounted to watching, she understood the economics of decommissioning. The lookout tower at Kemp's Ridge had been losing relevance for five years, ever since the satellite detection system improved, ever since the drones started their systematic sweeps at dawn. This was her final season because there was no season after it. The tower would be stripped—the radio equipment, the desk, the chair she'd had reupholstered in 1998—and the whole structure would either be demolished or abandoned in that efficient government way that required no ceremony.

She'd accepted this with the equanimity of someone who had spent fourteen summers alone at eight thousand feet, where acceptance was simply another word for survival. The loneliness didn't bother her anymore. It had, once, in the first summer when she'd been twenty-eight and newly graduated from a marriage that had ended not with conflict but with the slow, mutual understanding that they'd been mistaking proximity for connection. The lookout had seemed like punishment then, a consequence of her own poor judgment. But punishment, she'd learned, was only as severe as the narrative you constructed around it. After you stopped thinking of solitude as exile and started thinking of it as parameter, as boundary, as the thing that let you see the whole landscape without the distortion of human voices, it became something else. It became clarity.

The mountains that surrounded her were visible for sixty miles on a clear day. She could see the town of Mercy to the southeast, the sprawl of the valley where civilization accumulated in that haphazard way it did, and in the far distance, the ridgeline that marked the reservation boundary. To the west, the terrain broke into a series of drainage systems, steep-sided and forested, each one a fractal echo of the larger geography. The Kemp Drainage was there, the one everyone simply called the Drainage, where the old logging roads had deteriorated to barely passable and where, three miles in, her sister had built a cabin that no one else seemed to know about.

Claire. She hadn't said her sister's name aloud in four years, though they lived sixty miles apart, which was far enough to constitute different worlds. They'd been close once, the way sisters could be, with that particular intensity of people who'd learned early that their parents were unreliable narrators of their own lives. But closeness was like the season itself—it required conditions. Remove the conditions and it didn't persist; it didn't linger like smoke. It just dissipated.

The radio was definitely dead. Sarah held the transmit button, heard nothing but the faint rush of static she'd learned to interpret the way other people read body language. She tried again. No response. She checked the battery connections, which were fine, then the antenna wire, which was also fine. Some things were beyond troubleshooting. She noted the time in the log—4:47 p.m.—and wrote "*Radio inoperable*" in her neat, meticulous handwriting, the same handwriting she used for everything because consistency created a kind of order that mattered when you were alone.

The sun was beginning its long descent toward the western ridgeline. At this time of year, the descent took hours, an architectural event rather than a simple transition. She stood on the north platform, where she could see into the Drainage, and watched the way the light changed the colors of the forest. The standing dead from the 2003 fire had mostly fallen by now, or been removed by crews, but you could still trace the burn boundary if you knew what to look for—a slight difference in tree height, a tonal shift in the green. 

The smoke was almost invisible against the darkening sky, which was why she nearly missed it. It was thin, a mere definition of smoke, barely there, but it was rising from a specific point, and the specific point was the Drainage. Not the cabin—she was good at angles and distance—but near it, within perhaps a quarter mile.

Sarah stood for a long moment, watching. Smoke in August would have meant fire season, would have meant protocol and radio calls and the specific adrenaline that came from knowing her observations mattered. But this was June. June was wet. The fuel moisture content was still high; she had the weather service printouts to prove it. A fire now would be unusual enough to be notable, which meant it probably wasn't a fire at all. More likely a deliberate burn—someone clearing deadfall or doing controlled management on private property. Claire's property. It had to be Claire's property.

She'd never actually been to the cabin, though she'd seen it once from a distance, three summers ago, when a wildfire to the north had required her to scout the Drainage for smoke sources. She'd mapped the cabin's location mentally, added it to the internal geography of her summer, and then filed it away. Claire had bought the place in secret, and it had taken Sarah four months to find out she owned it at all. Their mother had mentioned it in an email—their mother who didn't seem to understand that casually referencing things was a way of enforcing connections that no longer existed. *Your sister is doing quite well, I think. That cabin must have cost her something.*

The smoke continued to rise. Sarah checked her watch: 5:23. The sunset would occur around 8:45; she had three hours before full darkness. She tried the radio again, holding down the transmit button and articulating clearly into the microphone: "*Kemp's Ridge to Ranger Station. Kemp's Ridge to Ranger Station. Smoke sighting, Kemp Drainage, coordinates approximately—*" She stopped. Gave the coordinates. Waited. Nothing. She tried once more, repeating the information, then gave up.

The road down from the tower wasn't really a road anymore, though it had been, decades ago, when the lookout had been staffed year-round and supply trucks came up twice a month. Now it was something between a trail and a memory of infrastructure—eight miles of loose scree and switchbacks that required four-wheel drive in the best conditions. Her truck, a 2003 Chevy with personality and no other choice, could make it, but it was a project, a full-commitment thing that left no room for hesitation or distraction.

She stood on the platform for another fifteen minutes, watching the smoke, watching the light change, watching the landscape make its slow transformation from day to dusk. The smoke seemed steady, neither increasing nor decreasing. Not a crown fire, anyway. Not a running fire. Whatever was burning, it was controlled or contained or not yet ambitious in its hunger.

She went inside. The cabin—she thought of it as the cabin rather than the tower, though it was technically a tower—was eighteen feet square, built of wood in 1931, and had been continuously occupied during fire season since 1935. That was eighty-nine years of summers, eighty-nine fire seasons, eighty-nine iterations of someone sitting where she was sitting, watching the same mountains, waiting for the same signals. She wondered how many of those eighty-nine summers had been lonely. How many of those lookouts had actually wanted to be here, and how many had been hiding from something the way she had been, eventually, even if she'd stopped thinking of it as hiding.

The cabin had a propane stove, a sink with water from a spring-fed tank, a narrow bed, a desk, a chair, and a small bookshelf. Sarah had brought up three boxes of books in her first summer and replaced them periodically as she re-read them. The same books, again and again, had been a form of comfort. She knew exactly where the sad parts occurred and could brace herself. She knew which characters would betray each other and which would survive. Prediction wasn't the same as spoiling; it was more like the difference between reading a map and being lost.

She made coffee on the propane stove, a ritual that existed outside of utility. The coffee wouldn't help anything. The decision had already been made by the smoke and by the radio being dead and by the fact that eight miles downhill was what stood between her and whatever was happening in the Drainage.

Actually, she didn't make the decision at all. She simply found herself doing the things that led to the truck being started, her jacket being found, her boots being laced. The cabin was locked—not because she expected thieves at eight thousand feet, but because procedures existed for a reason, because systems of behavior created systems of thinking, and if you maintained the structure, the structure maintained you.

The truck took three attempts to start, which was normal for June when the temperature dropped below freezing at night and didn't climb that high during the day. Once it did start, it coughed and spat in a way that suggested it was arguing with her about the whole project. She let it warm up for two minutes, then shifted into first gear and began the descent.

The road was worse than it had been in May. Winter had cracked the asphalt further—though there was barely any asphalt left, just the ghost of asphalt, the memory of what the road had been. She drove slowly, which meant slowly by any normal standard was glacial, which meant she had time to think about what she was doing and why.

She hadn't seen Claire in four years. They'd had a fight—not a dramatic thing, not the kind of fight people talked about later as the moment everything changed. It had been a small fight, a disagreement about their mother, about what they each owed her, about what distance meant in a family where distance had always been the family's primary language. They'd exchanged some emails afterward, emails that got progressively cooler, progressively more formal, until eventually one of them (Sarah still wasn't sure who) had simply stopped responding, and that was that. That was the end of it. That was the structure holding.

The road flattened out at the three-thousand-foot elevation mark, which meant she was roughly halfway down, which meant she'd been driving for forty minutes. The light was different here, lower on the horizon, and she could no longer see the smoke column. This was the thing about descending—you lost elevation and with it the vantage point. You became subject again to the limitations of ground-level perspective, to the way the world closed in and obscured itself.

The Drainage turnoff came after another fifteen minutes. It was marked with a metal sign that had been placed there in 1987 according to her records, and it pointed down a road that was barely a road, barely a suggestion of human passage. She shifted into low gear and began the final descent into the Drainage itself.

The canyon walls closed around her. The creek ran alongside the road—the Kemp Creek, which fed into the larger river system downstream—and she could hear it even over the engine noise. The forest was different down here, older-growth, less touched by fire. Massive Douglas firs and hemlocks, some of them surely predating the fire lookout itself. The light was strange and green, filtered through old growth, and very low on the horizon. She was losing the day.

The cabin appeared suddenly, as things do when you're driving in a canyon, when you're not expecting the world to open up but it does. It was small, well-built, the kind of cabin that suggested serious money and serious intention. Smoke rose from a fire pit perhaps fifty yards away, toward the creek, and Sarah could see a figure moving near it. As she approached, cutting the engine so the sound wouldn't carry, she recognized the figure as Claire.

Her sister had always been beautiful in a way that didn't seem to require maintenance. She'd been beautiful at nineteen and beautiful at thirty and apparently was still beautiful at forty-three, though she'd acquired some lines around her eyes and a kind of weathered quality that suggested the cabin was not merely a retreat but a genuine attempt at living differently. She was wearing work clothes—jeans and a flannel shirt—and she was tending what looked like a controlled burn in a fire pit made of stone. There were branches piled near the pit, burn pile debris, and the deliberate, careful way she was feeding the fire suggested she knew what she was doing.

Sarah turned off the engine and sat in the truck for a moment, watching. Claire didn't notice her; she was focused entirely on the fire, on keeping it at a manageable level, on the specific task at hand. She had the same focused quality she'd always had, the ability to diminish the world to whatever was immediately in front of her. Sarah had envied this about her, once, and then had learned to cultivate it as well, up in the tower, where the world was always immediately in front of you and nowhere else.

She got out of the truck.

Claire looked up immediately, which meant she'd known someone was coming, or had sensed the absence of engine noise, or had been expecting someone. The recognition happened in stages. First, the immediate moment of not knowing. Then, the moment of knowing but not believing. Then, finally, the moment of actually seeing.

"Sarah," Claire said. Just her name, neutral, the way one might identify an object. Not *my sister*, which was what it might have been. Just *Sarah*.

"I saw the smoke," Sarah said. "From the tower. The radio died, so I couldn't call it in. I came down to make sure it wasn't a fire."

"It's not a fire." Claire turned back to the fire pit, pushing a partially burned log deeper into the flames with a stick. "It's just a burn pile. I've been clearing dead wood from the property. The county requires permits, but I have them. Everything's legal."

"I wasn't asking if it was legal."

"No," Claire said. "I suppose you weren't."

The fire crackled. The creek made its sound. The light was almost gone now, just a red smear on the horizon where the sun was finishing its descent. In a few minutes, they'd be standing in full darkness, which seemed like a natural place for this conversation to happen, if it was going to happen at all.

"How long have you had this place?" Sarah asked.

"Three years. Almost four now." Claire finally turned around fully, and Sarah could see her clearly—the face she'd known since before she'd known anything else, the face that was more familiar than her own because you never really saw your own face except in reflections. "I didn't tell you because I knew you'd have opinions about it."

"I wouldn't have had opinions."

"You would have. You would have thought it was a mistake, or that I was hiding from something, or that I was wasting money that could go toward something more practical. You would have thought all of that without saying any of it, and I would have felt it anyway."

Sarah didn't deny it, which was the same as confirming it. "Is it?" she asked. "A mistake?"

"No," Claire said. "It's been the best decision I've made in years. I come out here for two or three weeks at a time, usually in summer. I don't have cell service. I don't have internet. I just have the cabin and the creek and the work of maintaining it. I come out here to remember how to be alone without being lonely."

The language hung in the air between them—*alone without being lonely*—and Sarah recognized it as something she'd been trying to articulate to herself for years without quite finding the words.

"The tower's being decommissioned," Sarah said. "This is my last summer."

"I know. Mom told me."

Of course she had. Their mother was a collector of information, dispensing it thoughtfully and carefully, the way one might plant seeds, never quite certain what would grow. She would have known about the decommissioning because the Forest Service would have been required to file paperwork, and papers got filed, and people talked, and somehow information propagated up through channels that had nothing to do with direct communication.

"I didn't know what to do," Sarah said, "when I saw the smoke. The radio was down. I couldn't call it in. I couldn't do my job."

"So you came down to make sure it wasn't a fire."

"So I came down," Sarah acknowledged.

Claire sat down on one of the fire pit's stone seats and gestured for Sarah to join her. Sarah stood for another moment, then sat. The heat from the fire was warm against her face, and the night air was cold, and the temperature gradient between them seemed to contain the entire situation—the gap and the bridge both at once.

"I have something to tell you," Claire said. "I've been planning what I'd say if I ever saw you again. I've practiced it, actually. Out here, where no one would hear me. I've had conversations with you about why things went the way they did, and whether there was a moment where it could have been different, and whether what we did was a choice or just something that happened and we called it a choice retroactively to make ourselves feel better about it."

"What did you conclude?" Sarah asked.

"That it was both. That it was a choice we made and also something that happened. That the distinction is meaningless once enough time has passed." Claire looked into the fire. "I missed you. I want to say that. I know I'm saying it now, at the end of your final summer, which makes me seem late to the party. But I want you to know that I missed you, and I think I was angry at you for not missing me back in the way that would have made it possible for us to stay in touch."

"I missed you," Sarah said. The fire crackled. The statement hung there, inadequate and also somehow complete. "I just didn't know how to say it without undoing the distance. The distance felt important."

"To keep the hurt at a manageable level."

"Yes."

They sat in silence for a long time. The fire burned down. The night became full and complete around them. Overhead, where Sarah was used to looking from her platform, stars began to appear, and more stars, and eventually the familiar constellations that had anchored her summers. She recognized them with the part of herself that lived at eight thousand feet and saw the world clearly, and she also recognized that those same stars were visible from here, in the Drainage, and that they looked no different. The altitude changed the detail, but not the essentials.

"I could stay," Sarah said. "I could spend the last month of the season coming down here, helping with the cabin, spending time with you."

"You could," Claire agreed. "Though that would mean abandoning the tower."

"I don't have to abandon it. I could come down on the weekends. I could make the drive."

"The truck would hate that. It barely makes it now."

"Then we'll deal with it when it breaks."

Claire smiled, and it was the smile she'd had when they were young, before everything, the smile that had been the beginning of their closeness and also its foundation. "I'd like that," she said. "I'd like it a lot."

They sat until the fire was nearly out, then Claire got up and began to contain it properly, burying the coals in sand and silt from the creek bed, making sure there was no possibility of resurrection. Sarah helped, and they worked in the darkness with only the distant light of stars to guide them, and it was the first time they'd worked together in four years, and the silence between them felt like the kind of silence that didn't need to be broken, the kind that was its own form of speech.

When it was done, they stood at the truck, and Claire reached out and hugged her, which was something Sarah had thought might be difficult but wasn't. It was just what it was—an embrace, warm and solid and complete.

"Drive safe," Claire said. "Come back soon."

"I will," Sarah said, and climbed back into the truck, and began the long drive back up to the tower, back to the height where she could see everything, back to the summer that was still happening, that still needed her watching, that would end soon enough anyway.
