# 

We say we forget fires the way we say we forget pain, as though forgetting were something that happened to us rather than something we did, as though time were a solvent instead of a thickening agent. I had not forgotten the Meridian Hotel fire of 1987 in any material sense—I could still see the particular shade of the hallway smoke, a brown that was almost mauve, and I could still hear the sound of water hitting hot surfaces, a sound like screaming—but I had managed, in the way one manages these things, to stop thinking about it. That is to say I had managed to live in the world as a person to whom this had not happened, which is, I suppose, what most people would call recovery.

The invitation had come in March, cream-colored cardstock in a cream-colored envelope, my name written in calligraphy that seemed to require an apology. Margaret Chen and David Rothstein request the honor of your presence at their marriage. I held the card for a long time without opening it, knowing already what the date would tell me. Some knowledge comes to us not as information but as a kind of recognition, the way you recognize a face you have not seen in twenty years and know at once that it is a face you have not seen in twenty years.

I had not seen Margaret in that time either. We had been chambermaid-partners, which is what the hotel called those of us who worked together on the same floors, and we had not been friends in any way that would suggest the word should be saved for something else. We had been civil in the manner of people who share a task and understand that civility is what allows the task to be completed. We had pushed our carts through those hallways together, had knocked on the same doors, had learned each other's rhythms as one learns the rhythms of an appliance. After the fire, after the hospital, after the period of time that the hotel called the closure and I called something else, Margaret had not come back to the Meridian. I had not come back either, at first. Then I had come back.

The letter enclosed with the invitation was handwritten. Dear Claire, it began, as though we had not spent a decade and a half in the kind of proximity that either creates genuine affection or creates nothing at all, and we had created nothing. I skimmed the letter for the information I needed: Margaret had moved to San Francisco, had gotten married once and divorced, had met David at a company retreat. The wedding would take place at the Meridian. They had chosen the Meridian, the letter said, because it was where Margaret's life had changed, where she had learned to understand her own strength.

This was the sort of thing people said now, I knew, the sort of framing that made catastrophe into a kind of gift. I had read the articles in the years after the fire. There had been something in the Times about personal transformation and trauma, about how the mind protects itself by converting suffering into narrative, by finding meaning in the shape of pain. I had read about it with the kind of attention one gives to articles about people in other countries, understanding that this was information about a human tendency rather than a description of my own experience. My own experience, when I examined it at all, seemed to resist this narrative conversion. The fire had happened. I had lived. Margaret had lived. We were living still.

The fire itself had been banal in the way actual disasters are banal. It had started in the sub-basement laundry facility, the official report said, from a fault in a commercial dryer. There had been no warning because there had been no warning. I had been on the eighth floor, turning down beds in a suite that had not been occupied, had heard the alarm as one hears all alarms, as an interruption in the day. The smoke had started before the evacuation announcement, had started in a way that the evacuation announcement seemed to be responding to, rather than preceding. I had been the one to find Mrs. Kolner in room 817, an elderly woman who had not heard the alarm because the door to her room was sealed against the hallway to keep the air conditioning from escaping. I had not been trying to be heroic. I had been trying to complete my work.

Margaret had been somewhere else on the building. We did not see each other during the evacuation, did not see each other in the parking lot or the hospital or the days of interviews that followed. We had been interviewed separately, had given separate statements, had been treated as separate survivors rather than two people who had survived the same event. This seemed important at the time, the fact that we had not been together. It seemed to say something about the nature of tragedy, that even when it was collective, even when it burned down walls that many people had built and many people inhabited, it remained fundamentally solitary.

The city had rebuilt the Meridian, as cities do. They had replaced the burned floors with new materials that met new codes, had installed new safety features and new aesthetic choices. The lobby had been renovated in the manner of contemporary hotel lobbies, all light wood and strategic lighting, the kind of space that looked like a photograph of itself. I had walked through it once, on a day when I had had no particular reason to walk through it, and had not recognized it. This had seemed like it might mean something—the erasure of the past, the triumph of capital, the way we build over our disasters—but by the time I left the building I had decided it meant only that I had not been paying attention the first time. Spaces do not hold our memories. We hold our memories, and we put them in spaces, and then we blame the spaces for keeping them.

I decided to attend the wedding three weeks after receiving the invitation. I made this decision on a Tuesday, which seemed like the kind of day on which one should make a decision. I called Margaret's number, which was listed on the invitation. I did not expect to reach her—I was calling in the late afternoon, when people were at work—but she answered immediately, as though she had been sitting by the phone.

"Claire," she said, and her voice was exactly as I remembered it, which is to say exactly like any other woman's voice, containing no information about who she was or what she meant to me.

"I got your invitation," I said.

"I'm so glad. I wasn't sure... I knew it might be strange. I knew you might not want to come."

"Why would you not be sure?"

There was a pause. I could hear her breathing, or I was imagining I could hear her breathing, and there is no difference between these two things if one truly believes the imaginary sound.

"Because of the fire," she said finally. "Because of what happened here."

I had not been expecting this directness. I had assumed we would proceed as we had always proceeded, in the register of surface politeness and implied understanding. "The fire was a long time ago," I said.

"I know," Margaret said, and something in her voice shifted, became something that might have been genuine. "That's why I'm getting married here. That's the whole point."

I told her I would come. I told her I would come and sit in the ballroom of the Meridian Hotel and watch her marry a man I had never met, and I told myself that this was simply something one did, that this was how one conducted oneself in the world, with grace and without flinching. I did not tell her that the thought of it made me feel as though I were being asked to accomplish something with my body, to move through space in a way that was not natural to me, the way a person feels when they are asked to smile for a photograph at the moment their face is not arranged in that configuration.

The wedding was scheduled for a Saturday in June, which seemed to me an entirely logical choice. I bought a dress, which seemed to be what was expected, a dark blue dress that I had not particularly wanted. I bought shoes that matched. I made these preparations as though I were preparing for a journey, though the journey was only to a building that stood four miles from my apartment. I had not set foot in the Meridian since 1988. I had not had a particular reason to avoid it—I did not work there anymore, and I did not stay in hotels for pleasure—but the avoidance had accumulated over time into something that had the quality of a principle.

On the morning of the wedding, I woke at five o'clock, which was earlier than I needed to wake, and I lay in bed considering the question of whether there was any reason to attend. The reasons I had given myself seemed suddenly thin. Margaret was not my friend. The hotel was not my place. The fact that I had survived something there was not a compelling reason to return to the place I had survived it. People survived things all the time and moved on and did not attend ceremonies in the locations of their survival. I could, theoretically, call Margaret and say that I was ill. I could say that something had come up. I could simply not go, and Margaret would understand, or she would not understand, but either way the wedding would continue without me.

Instead, I got out of bed and made coffee and drank it sitting by the window. Outside, the city was engaged in the process of becoming June. The light had taken on that quality it takes on in late spring, a kind of insistence, as though the sun were determined that we notice it. I drank my coffee and I thought about the morning of the fire, which had also been a morning like other mornings, no different in any meteorological sense than any other morning. I had woken and dressed and gone to work as though the day were going to unfold in the usual way.

At half past one, I began to dress. I put on the dark blue dress, which looked on me the way unfamiliar clothes always look on one, like a costume of oneself. I put on the shoes. I put on the small amount of makeup that seemed to be required, though I was not sure what it was required for, since I did not imagine that my face would be of any particular concern to anyone at the wedding. I looked at myself in the mirror and tried to understand what I was looking at. There was a woman in a dark blue dress. The woman was composed of cells and chemistry and memory. She was the kind of thing that survived fires, that endured after disasters, that showed up at ceremonies.

The drive to the Meridian took fifteen minutes. I drove slowly, the way one does when one is in no particular hurry to arrive at a destination, when one is engaged in the activity of delaying. The city passed by my windows in its usual configuration, indifferent to what I was doing within it. I thought, as I had thought many times before, about the fact that tragedy is quotidian to cities, that they absorb disasters the way they absorb everything else, that the ground upon which a building burned is the same ground upon which other buildings stand.

The parking lot of the Meridian was full. This seemed obvious in retrospect—there was a wedding—but I had somehow imagined the parking lot would be empty, that the hotel would exist in a state of suspension, waiting for me. Instead, the lot was full of cars in the manner of parking lots at weddings, and I parked and sat for a moment in my car, considering that I could still leave. I could drive away from the Meridian and not attend the wedding. There was nothing stopping me except the understanding that one attends things one has said one will attend, that one does the things one has said one will do. This is what civilization is, I had always understood. This is what maintains it.

The lobby was as I remembered it was not, which is to say it was entirely unfamiliar and therefore strangely comfortable. The wedding was signposted toward the ballroom, which was down a hallway that I did not recognize, though of course I had never entered the Meridian as a guest, only as a servant. This seemed relevant, though I could not articulate why. I walked down the hallway and I did not think about the hallway as it had been, filled with smoke that was almost mauve, but I thought about it anyway, the way one does think about things one is not thinking about.

The ballroom was decorated in a way that suggested someone had spent a considerable amount of time thinking about flowers. There were flowers in the colors of sunset, arranged in configurations that seemed to require architectural forethought. The chairs were arranged in the traditional manner, and I took a seat in the back, which seemed to be where I belonged. Around me, other people were settling into their seats, speaking to each other in the voices of people who had met before and had things to discuss. No one attempted to speak to me. I had the sense that I was slightly out of focus, that I had arrived in a slightly different key than the other guests, and though I knew this was a perception unique to me, I did not find this knowledge particularly reassuring.

The ceremony began on time, which seemed like it might be significant. Margaret entered from the back of the room, not from the side as I might have expected, and she walked down the aisle in a way that suggested she had practiced this walk, had wanted to arrive at her destination with grace. She was fifty-three years old, I calculated—we were both fifty-three—and her face had developed the kind of lines that came from being a human for that long, from having a face that moved and changed and recorded things. She looked happy, which is to say she looked like someone who was attempting to look happy, and I wondered if there was a difference.

The ceremony was brief. The man she was marrying was tall and had the kind of face that suggested he had never experienced a serious problem. He looked at Margaret with the expression of someone looking at a painting that he had purchased and was pleased with. They exchanged vows that had been written by someone who believed that love could be expressed in language, that language was adequate to the task. I sat in the back of the ballroom and listened to these vows and thought about the fact that Margaret and I had been in a building together while it burned, and that we had both left the building alive, and that this was the extent of what we had in common.

When the ceremony ended, I stood when other people stood. I applauded when other people applauded. I moved toward the reception area when people began to move toward the reception area. The reception was arranged in another room, with a buffet and tables and a bar. There was wine. There were small foods that had been constructed with care. I took a glass of wine and stood for a while at the edge of the space, as one does at weddings where one does not know many people.

Margaret found me near the window, which I had positioned myself near in order to have a reason for standing alone. She was still in her wedding dress, which seemed like the thing one did at one's wedding, though I had never attended enough weddings to be certain. She hugged me, which I had not expected, and in the moment of being hugged I did not know what to do with my body, where to place my arms.

"You came," she said. "I was worried you wouldn't."

"I said I would come."

"I know, but you've never... You never came back before."

This was true. I had never come back before. I had managed, in twenty-eight years, to avoid the Meridian Hotel, to live my life in the city without ever returning to the place where I had worked and where I had been burned and where I had survived being burned. I had thought this was something I had done deliberately, but sitting there in Margaret's wedding dress, I wondered if it was something that had simply happened, the way many things happen without intention.

"The building is different now," I said.

"I know. They rebuilt everything. But the land is the same. The foundation is the same."

I thought about this, the idea that the foundation remained, that beneath the new materials and new walls and new aesthetic, the original structure persisted. It seemed like the sort of thing people said when they wanted to create continuity between past and present, when they wanted to suggest that some essence remained even as everything else changed. It seemed like what Margaret wanted to believe about herself—that she was fundamentally the same person who had worked as a chambermaid in 1987, but also transformed, improved, made into something new.

"I'm glad you're here," Margaret said. "I wanted you to be here. I wanted to show you that this place isn't just something that happened to us. It's something we got through. It's just a building. It's just a place where a wedding is happening."

I looked at her and I tried to understand if she truly believed this, or if she was a person who had learned to say things with the kind of confidence that made it impossible to know whether belief was involved. "Congratulations," I said, "on your marriage."

She smiled at me in a way that suggested I had said something kind, though I had only said something conventional. She moved away to greet someone else, and I returned my attention to the window. Through the glass, I could see the parking lot, still full of cars, and beyond that the street, still moving, still indifferent to all of this. The sun was beginning to set, which seemed like it might be meaningful, which seemed like the kind of thing that happened at the end of ceremonies. I watched the light change. I watched it become mauve, and I did not flinch.
