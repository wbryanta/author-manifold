# The Return

The letter had arrived on a Tuesday in March, forwarded through two addresses before finding its way to the apartment on Kings Road where Margaret had lived for the better part of twelve years. She held it between her fingers in the kitchen light, noting the weight of the paper, the embossed lettering at the top left corner: The Esplanade Hotel. It was curious that her hands did not shake. She had trained herself over the years to move through the world with the appearance of a woman to whom nothing very much had happened, and the letter seemed to invite this performance, this careful disregard. The wedding invitation inside was printed on an even heavier stock, the kind one saw in the windows of expensive stationery shops on Hollywood Boulevard. Richard Castellano and Patricia Morse were to be married in the garden room of The Esplanade Hotel on the twenty-third of June.

She did not recognize either of the names.

The note beneath the formal announcement was written in an unfamiliar hand, the penmanship of someone unaccustomed to writing by hand: "Margaret—I very much hope you'll come. I have thought of you so many times over the years and wanted you to be here for this. Please telephone if you have questions about accommodations. —Ellen"

Ellen. There had been an Ellen, a housekeeper who had taken her in hand those first difficult weeks, who had shown her how to fold sheets with military precision, how to dust around the objects on a nightstand without moving them, how to make a bed so tightly that one could bounce a coin upon it. Ellen had been kind to her in the way of people who know that kindness is often a luxury neither party can afford. Margaret had not thought of Ellen in a great many years.

The wedding was three months away. That seemed an impossible amount of time to live with the knowledge of it.

She called the hotel's main line on a Thursday afternoon and was transferred through several extensions before reaching Ellen herself, whose voice had aged in ways her handwriting had not. They spoke with the careful politeness of people who had once shared a genuine warmth but could not locate it anymore.

"I've thought of you so many times," Ellen repeated, as if this were a fact Margaret had disputed.

"It's kind of you to think of me," Margaret said. "The invitation is very thoughtful."

"I heard you moved away. I wasn't sure where you'd gone. A woman who worked with you—Sandra, do you remember Sandra?—she mentioned you were in California."

Margaret did remember Sandra, though not fondly. Sandra had a habit of borrowing things from the other girls' lockers and forgetting to return them. "That's right," she said. "California."

"We've rebuilt, you know. You probably saw the articles. It's completely different now. Everything new. The old timbers are gone, all of it. Sometimes I think that was the best thing that could have happened, though I wouldn't have said so at the time."

"I'm glad," Margaret said.

There was a pause then, and in that pause Margaret understood that Ellen had been waiting for her to say something more, something that acknowledged what had happened on that morning in March when the linen closet on the third floor had caught fire, when the building had filled with smoke so quickly that the concept of time became meaningless. Margaret had been seventeen years old. Ellen had pulled her down the hallway, away from the guests who were streaming toward the staircases, through a service corridor that Ellen seemed to know by instinct, the way one knows the layout of one's own home.

"The garden room is beautiful now," Ellen said finally. "Completely rebuilt. New glass, new everything. Patricia wanted to get married outdoors, but her mother insisted on a room with proper climate control and good lighting for photographs. The garden room is the compromise."

"It sounds lovely," Margaret said.

"Will you come?"

"I don't think so," Margaret said. She had not planned to say this. The words seemed to offer themselves up without consulting her will. "I appreciate the invitation very much. It's kind of you to have kept track of me. But I don't think I can do it."

Ellen did not answer immediately. When she did, her voice had taken on the same quality it had possessed on that morning so long ago: practical, accepting, already moving forward. "All right. I understand. I wanted to give you the option, though. In case you felt ready. But I understand."

"Thank you for the invitation," Margaret said.

"You can always change your mind. Until the end of May."

After she hung up the telephone, Margaret stood in her kitchen with the invitation still on the counter, its expensive paper catching the light in a way that seemed deliberately designed to make her uncomfortable. She had never been good at lying to herself. She had survived the fire because of luck and because of Ellen, and she had survived the years afterward by constructing an architecture of avoidance so elaborate that she sometimes lost her way inside it. The letter was a small thing, a courtesy extended by someone Margaret had not thought of in years, and yet it had the force of an interruption, a deliberate breach in the wall she had spent so long building.

She called back the next morning.

"I'll come," she told Ellen, who did not seem surprised, who said only, "Good. I'm so glad. I'll arrange the room for you."

The drive to the coast took five hours. Margaret made it in six, stopping once at a coffee shop in San Luis Obispo, where she sat in a booth and read the same page of her book four times without absorbing a single word. The day was grey and bright, the kind of June day that California did well, when everything seemed possible and nothing seemed permanent. She drove with her hands at ten and two, the way she had been taught in driving school, the way one drove when one wanted to draw no attention to oneself.

The Esplanade Hotel announced itself long before she could see it—first in the change of the wind, which came salted and direct from the ocean, then in the quality of the light, which had taken on a particular color that belonged only to that stretch of coast. The parking attendant who helped her from her car was young and efficient, the kind of person who likely saw no difference between one guest and another, for which she was grateful. The lobby had been entirely rebuilt. There was nothing in it that reminded her of the old hotel, which had been darker, more heavily decorated, the kind of place where it had always seemed to be 1952. This new lobby was open and minimal, with a fountain and some very modern art on the walls, the kind of art that could mean anything or nothing.

The desk clerk was a woman Margaret did not know. This was a relief. She checked in using her own name, which still felt strange in her mouth after all these years. Margaret Thorne. She had changed it legally after moving to California, though she had never entirely committed to it in her own mind. She had been Margaret Sullivan on the morning of the fire.

Her room was on the second floor. She unpacked carefully, hanging her two dresses in the closet, arranging her toiletries on the bathroom counter with the precision of someone who had once made her living from precise arrangements. The room was lovely—clean lines, a soft bed, a window that looked out toward the ocean, though she could not see the water itself. She sat on the bed and tried to understand why she had come, what she had expected to find. The hotel was entirely new. Ellen was older. The people who were getting married were people she did not know. There was nothing here for her but the fact of return, which seemed to be the only thing that had mattered.

She walked down to the dining room for dinner and found it empty except for a few couples scattered at different tables. She ordered fish, which arrived with a sauce she did not particularly enjoy, and tried to eat it as though she were eating it in any other restaurant in any other city. The waiter was attentive without being intrusive. She left a careful tip.

When she returned to her room it was not yet eight o'clock. She drew the curtains and got into bed and lay there in the dark, listening to the sound of the hotel, which was the sound of all hotels—the distant hum of machinery, the occasional voice in the hallway, the settling of the structure itself. Around midnight she heard what sounded like a small party on one of the other floors, people laughing, glasses clinking. Then even that faded, and there was only the sound of the ocean, which was the sound she remembered most clearly, the sound that had been there every day of the two years she had worked in the hotel, so constant that she had learned not to hear it.

The wedding itself was not until the following evening. She spent the day in her room and in the grounds of the hotel, walking through the gardens that had been replanted, looking at the cliffs from various vantage points, eating lunch in the café that had been built where the old kitchen had been. Everything was tasteful and new and utterly devoid of the particular personality of the old hotel. It was as if someone had taken an impression of what a luxury coastal hotel should be and built it directly from the template.

She was in her room getting dressed when there was a knock on the door. She had chosen to wear the blue dress, which was not particularly suited to a wedding but which had always made her feel slightly less vulnerable than her other option. She opened the door to find Ellen standing in the hallway in her housekeeper's uniform, which seemed designed to distress her more than it should have.

"I wanted to see you before the event," Ellen said, and then, after a moment, "It's good to see you, Margaret. You look well."

"So do you," Margaret said, though this was not quite true. Ellen looked tired in the way that people look when they have held the same job for a very long time and have learned to treat it as a form of endurance. She was heavier than she had been, her hair a uniform grey, her face marked with the kind of lines that come from too much exposure to weather and stress.

"I wanted to apologize," Ellen said, "for any difficulty the invitation may have caused."

"There's nothing to apologize for."

"I thought of not sending it. I thought maybe it would be better to leave things as they were. But then I thought—well, I thought that you might want to know that we'd rebuilt it. That we'd made it new. I thought that might matter to you."

"It does," Margaret said, and found that this was true. "It does matter."

Ellen nodded, satisfied by this answer in the way that she had always been satisfied by small offerings of truth. "The ceremony is at six. You're sitting with me and my family, if you don't mind. Your name card is already at the table."

"I don't mind at all."

After Ellen left, Margaret looked at herself in the bathroom mirror for the first time since she had arrived. She looked like a woman attending a wedding at a hotel, which was all anyone would see. This seemed to be enough.

The garden room had indeed been completely rebuilt. It was a space that seemed to exist in suspension between indoors and outdoors, with walls of glass on one side and high windows on the other, the whole thing controlled by some sophisticated system of climate and light. Flowers had been arranged with a professional hand—peonies and roses, everything pale and arranged in tones that would photograph well. There were perhaps eighty people scattered among white chairs, and Margaret understood that she was attending the wedding of people who had money but not so much money that they were required to invite their entire world.

Ellen's family occupied a table near the front, which meant Margaret was forced to stand and greet them, to shake the hand of Ellen's husband (a man she had never met, even during all her years at the hotel), to smile at Ellen's daughter, who was perhaps thirty and carried herself with the particular exhaustion of a woman who was tired of being the subject of maternal hope. There was small talk, the kind of talk that requires no actual engagement, only the appearance of it.

The ceremony itself lasted twenty minutes. The bride was thin and blonde and seemed to be made almost entirely of anxiety—the kind of anxiety that belongs to women who have been told their entire lives that they are the culmination of their parents' hopes and investments. The groom had the satisfied expression of a man who believes he is making a good decision. They exchanged vows that had been written for them by someone with a professional hand, and they kissed, and everyone applauded.

During the cocktail hour, Margaret found herself standing alone near one of the windows, watching the light fade over the ocean in that particular way that it did in June, slowly and then all at once. Ellen found her there.

"It went well, I thought," Ellen said. "Patricia was nervous, but brides usually are."

"She seemed nice," Margaret said, though she had no actual basis for this assessment.

"She is nice. She's kind to her mother, which counts for quite a lot. Not everyone is." Ellen was quiet for a moment, then: "Do you ever think about what happened? The fire?"

Margaret had been wondering when this question would arrive, had been braced for it since she had first read the letter. She had even prepared an answer, something dismissive and final. But looking at Ellen's face, at the genuine concern there, she found herself unwilling to deploy the lie she had been holding in reserve.

"Sometimes," she said. "Not often."

"I've thought about it quite a bit. I had nightmares for years afterward. I would wake up and smell the smoke, even though I was nowhere near here. I don't have the nightmares anymore, but I still have the dreams where I can't find people. Where I'm looking for someone and they keep moving, and I can't catch up to them."

"Did you dream about me?" Margaret asked, though she was not sure she wanted to know the answer.

"Sometimes," Ellen said. "You were very young. I've always thought that you might have had difficulty after. That it might have marked you in some way."

"It did," Margaret said. "But not in ways that are visible."

"I'm glad you came," Ellen said. "I really am. I've often wondered what happened to you. If you were all right."

"I'm fine," Margaret said. "I'm very fine."

This seemed to be what Ellen needed to hear. She patted Margaret's arm in the way of people who have touched base and found everything in order, and then she returned to her family, to the small dramas of their evening, their conversations, their lives that had continued on without interruption.

The reception was held in the dining room where Margaret had eaten her careful dinner the night before. Long tables had been set up, decorated with low arrangements of the same flowers that had graced the ceremony room. Margaret was seated next to Ellen's daughter, whose name was Susan, and who seemed to be laboring under the impression that Margaret was some sort of old friend of the family.

"Mom talks about you sometimes," Susan said. "She always speaks very kindly of you. I think she felt like she helped you through something."

"She did," Margaret said. "She was very kind to me when I was young."

"She's like that," Susan said. "She's always been like that. It can be exhausting, actually, being around someone who is always kind. It creates an expectation that you should be kind as well, and that's not always easy."

Margaret did not know what to say to this, so she drank her water and listened to Susan talk about her own job, which had something to do with real estate in Los Angeles. It turned out that Susan lived not far from Kings Road, though they had never encountered each other, which seemed improbable given the size of the world.

The food was served in courses. There was a salad, then a fish or a chicken, then vegetables. Margaret ate carefully, the way she ate everywhere, as though she were being tested. People gave speeches. The groom's father spoke about his son. Patricia's mother wept while discussing how proud she was. A best man told a story about a time the groom had been drunk and had decided to sleep in a bathtub. Everyone laughed as if this were charming. There was dancing, though Margaret did not dance. Instead she sat and watched, which was what she had always been best at.

Around eleven o'clock, she excused herself and returned to her room. She lay in the bed and listened to the diminishing sounds of the party, the gradual exodus of guests, the return of the hotel to its quiet. She thought about Ellen asking if she had ever thought about the fire, and the answer she had given, which was the truth but not the whole truth. The whole truth was more complicated. The whole truth was that the fire had happened to her, had tried to consume her, and that she had survived it through a combination of luck and Ellen's quick action and the particular mercy of remaining alive. She had survived, and in surviving she had discovered that there was no particular glory in it, no special meaning or lesson. There was only the fact of waking up the next day and the day after that, and learning to move through the world as though the thing that had happened to you had not, in fact, happened.

But it had happened. It had happened to her. And she had come back to this place, to this reconstructed hotel, not to resolve anything or to achieve any particular peace, but simply to verify that the place existed, that the thing she remembered was anchored in something real and not only in her own mind, where it had lived with such persistence.

Before she fell asleep, she thought about the fire one more time, about the smoke and the heat and the moment when Ellen's hand had closed around her wrist, pulling her away from danger. It was a memory she had tried to make small over the years, to contain within some private chamber of her mind where it could not reach out and touch her. But it was still there. It would always be there.

In the morning, she packed her things and left the hotel by the same entrance through which she had arrived. The parking attendant was different—one of the night shift people, probably, heading home after a long night. He brought her car around without comment, and she tipped him the appropriate amount, not more and not less. She drove back toward Los Angeles with the same careful attention to safety that had gotten her there, and by nightfall she was back in her apartment on Kings Road, where she put her clothes back in the closet and her suitcase back under the bed, and settled back into the architecture of her life as though she had never left it at all.
