# Untitled

The first ewe collapsed at three in the afternoon, and by then the snow had already erased the fence line between the north pasture and the county road. Sarah watched from the shed window as the wind rewrote the landscape in white, each gust a sentence that contradicted the one before it. The radio weather service had upgraded from winter weather advisory to blizzard warning an hour ago, which meant nothing practical—it meant the same thing it had always meant: work until it was done, then work some more.

"Mum, she's pushing again," Tommy called from the lambing pen.

Sarah turned from the window. Her son was seventeen, all elbows and knees still, though his hands had learned a competence that belied his angular frame. He knelt beside the ewe with his sleeves rolled, forearms already slick. The animal's sides heaved with contractions that seemed to consume her entire body.

"How long?" Sarah moved toward them, her boots loud on the concrete.

"Fifteen minutes since the last push. Something's not right."

Sarah had brought two lambs into the world during her marriage—watched more than assisted, if she was honest. That had been Michael's skill, the quiet reading of the animals' bodies, the knowledge that lived in his hands. He'd guided her through it twice, patient and steady, his voice never rising above the necessary. The second lamb had come breech, and she remembered the tightness of his jaw as he worked, remembered thinking that this man, this calm man, was also capable of terror if something mattered enough.

She crouched beside the ewe now, placed her palm on the distended belly. The tension there was different from what she'd learned—too hard, too static.

"Go get the OB chains," Sarah said. "And the lubricant."

Tommy hesitated only fractionally—he'd learned not to ask why, learned that delay was a luxury they couldn't afford. He scrambled up and crossed to the equipment wall where everything hung in its place. Michael had organized it five years ago, and she'd maintained the system out of necessity. Necessity and the fact that moving anything felt like moving him, like disrupting the last evidence of his orderliness.

The ewe's eye rolled white as another contraction moved through her. Sarah had learned the language of pain in animals differently than she'd learned anything else—not through instruction but through the long practice of paying attention. This was pain without resolution. This was pain that didn't lead anywhere good.

"Here." Tommy returned with the chains, the bottle of lubricant, the notebook where Michael had written everything in his small, precise handwriting. Sarah had tried to follow his notes during the first lambing season without him, but the words seemed to describe an entirely different profession, one that involved instinct and judgment rather than procedure.

She took the lubricant and worked it into her hand, her arm. The ewe trembled.

"Talk to her," Sarah said, and Tommy moved to the animal's head, placed his hands on either side of her face, began speaking in a low, steady voice. His words were nothing in particular—he was just making sound, presence, the thing that mothers and sons knew how to do for each other. Sarah guided her hand inside the ewe's body, feeling for the lamb, for the reason that nothing was proceeding as it should.

The presentation was wrong. The lamb was folded in on itself, twisted in a way that made passage impossible. Sarah's fingers found the slick warmth of it, the tiny hooves. She worked to understand the configuration, to imagine unfolding it without tearing anything vital, without hurrying into panic.

"What is it?" Tommy's voice cracked.

"Wrong way," Sarah said. "Keep talking."

She manipulated the lamb with her fingers, trying to encourage it to turn, to present its front legs first. The ewe groaned, a sound that held both anguish and resignation. Sarah understood that sound. She'd made that sound herself, at 4 a.m. nine months ago, when the police came to the door with their hats in their hands.

The lamb turned. Just barely, just enough. Sarah withdrew her arm and cleaned it on the towel hanging nearby, then moved back to the ewe's back end and waited for the next contraction. When it came, she guided the first lamb toward the opening—two tiny hooves, then the nose, wet and perfect and still and then suddenly gasping, violent with breath.

Sarah cleared the lamb's nostrils and mouth, felt it contract in her hands with a strength that seemed impossible in something so small. The ewe heaved, and a second lamb slid free almost immediately, as if it had been waiting its turn, as if the first had only needed to break the seal on the possibility.

"Two," Tommy whispered.

"Two," Sarah confirmed. She dried them as much as she could, nudged them toward the ewe's udder. The animal's instinct clicked in—maternal recognition, the ancient machinery that didn't require thought or memory or any of the human functions that seemed to complicate everything. The lambs found their footing, found milk, and the ewe, released from her torment, rested her head and let them feed.

Sarah sat back on her heels, suddenly aware of her own breathing. The adrenaline that had pulled her through the delivery ebbed slightly, leaving her feeling unstrung. Through the window behind the pens, the snow continued its relentless accumulation.

"You did it," Tommy said, and there was something like wonder in his voice—not because the delivery was particularly difficult, she knew, but because she had done it. She had done his father's work.

"We did it," she corrected, though the correction felt important in a way she couldn't quite articulate. "Get her water and hay. Extra nutrition. She'll need it with twins."

The power had gone out during the second ewe's labor. Sarah became aware of it gradually—the absence of the barn heater's hum, the sudden silence that felt thicker than the snow outside. She and Tommy exchanged looks but didn't stop working. The lights had been running on the generator for two years now, the emergency backup that Michael had installed when the main line had first proved unreliable. He'd complained about the noise while servicing it, but he'd serviced it regularly, and the notebook was full of entries in his handwriting about fuel checks and oil changes.

The generator was in the small outbuilding attached to the eastern wall of the barn. Sarah made Tommy stay with the ewes while she suited up—jacket, gloves, the old ski mask that still smelled of Michael's cologne, a scent that had faded to almost nothing but that her brain insisted on detecting anyway. The wind hit her like a physical force when she opened the door, knocked her sideways, made every breath feel like drinking cold water.

The outbuilding was only fifty feet away, but the visibility had contracted to perhaps twenty. She moved along the rope line that Michael had installed years ago—a precaution she'd never understood at the time, a belt-and-suspenders kind of thinking, until the moment she needed it. She held on, moving incrementally, her eyes straining against white that contained no depth, no perspective.

The generator was running, but the fuel tank was nearly empty.

Sarah stood in the small, unheated building staring at the gauge, waiting for the situation to reconfigure itself into something that made sense. The tank was nearly full three days ago when she'd checked it. She was certain of this. She'd stood exactly where she was standing now and noted the level mentally, the way she'd learned to note everything after Michael died—as a potential problem to be addressed or anticipated or solved.

The generator had been running steadily for two days, though, as the storm developed, as the frontal boundary moved in from the northwest, as the first labor pains had started in the ewes that had been bred on schedule. Sarah did the math in her head and found it didn't add up. The fuel consumption wasn't wrong. The previous level had been wrong.

She'd misread it. That was the only explanation. The white plastic of the tank reflected light strangely, and she'd misjudged. Or Tommy had, when he helped her service it a week ago. The important thing now was not how the tank had gotten to this state but what she did next.

The barn had gone cold already. The heating elements that kept the worst of the winter from penetrating had begun their slow surrender to the outside temperature. Sarah felt this in the sudden acuteness of the air when she returned, when she peeled off the mask and gloves with fingers that had stopped feeling like her own.

"The power's out?" Tommy appeared from around the corner of the lambing pens, where he'd been waiting.

"Generator fuel is almost gone."

"How almost?"

"We need to ration it." Sarah's mind was already moving through the problem, dividing it into components. "We can run it in bursts—heat it up when a ewe is in active labor, let it go cold between."

"Mum, it's a blizzard out there. You can't—"

"We won't be able to." She was already moving toward the fuel storage on the far side of the barn, the backup cans that Michael had always kept, always maintained. "Unless there's fuel in storage."

The storage area was part of the secondary shelter, a lean-to structure that had never quite been weatherized properly. Sarah unlatched the door and stepped into a cold that felt like an accusation. The metal cans she was looking for—three of them, each containing twenty-five gallons—were stacked behind the grain bins. She knelt beside them and unscrewed the first cap. The smell hit her immediately, chemical and sharp. The can was full. All three were full.

Michael. The thought arrived with physical force. He had filled these cans last fall, maybe further back than that, the way he filled everything, the way he maintained the systems that would keep his wife and eventually his son alive in the large silences of sheep farming.

Sarah sat back on her heels in that cold building and let herself feel the full weight of the fact that he had done this and he was dead anyway, and all his preparations, all his careful maintenance of the systems meant to protect them, had only meant that she would have to face this particular moment alive.

She filled the generator fuel tank from one of the cans, slopping gasoline onto her hands and her jacket, not bothering to wipe it away. The wind howled through the cracks in the lean-to, and she thought about the power grid, about the lines down somewhere west of them, about the weather system sitting on the region like a lid. They would make it through this. She would make sure they made it. But she also understood, in the way she was understanding a lot of things lately, that the making it through was going to cost her something every time, that each survival was going to require her to be a version of herself that she hadn't particularly agreed to become.

Back inside the barn, Tommy had brought all the heavily pregnant ewes into the main lambing area. There were eight of them, ranged around the pens in various states of unrest. The temperature had dropped perhaps ten degrees already.

"Start the generator," Sarah said, and then added: "Not yet. Let me look at the pens first."

She moved between them, checking for signs of imminent labor, reading the bodies of animals that had been born and raised in this place. One of them—a ewe named Margot, ridiculous name that Michael had given her, who was ridiculously their best producer—was clearly close. Her udder was tight and shiny with readiness. Her pacing had taken on a purposeful quality. The preliminary contractions would start soon if they hadn't already.

"Get the generator running," Sarah said. "Keep it at operational temperature but not higher. We'll reduce to essentials once I have a sense of how many more we're dealing with."

Tommy didn't ask what essentials meant. He'd grown up around this work, and he understood that some decisions weren't really decisions—they were just the next thing that needed doing. Sarah pulled up a plastic milk crate and settled beside Margot, and then, because waiting was a kind of torture that nothing had prepared her for, she pulled out the breeding notebook from two years ago and tried to calculate how many ewes were still to come.

The snow continued outside, visible through the high windows as a kind of visual static. Sarah had moved past fear into a functional clarity that felt almost like peace. The generator hummed steadily, burning through Michael's fuel at a rate she could live with. Margot's labor progressed normally—lamb presenting correctly, arrival easy and quick, a single large ram lamb that he decided to name even though she'd told him not to name them. "That's Asterix," he said, watching the lamb suck, and she let it stand.

Three more ewes came between then and full dark. The third one had complications—another breech, though this time the lamb was smaller, more malleable. Sarah worked with her forearm inside the ewe's body, feeling the tiny hooves, encouraging rotation. This time there was no Tommy sitting with the animal's head, making reassuring sounds. Tommy was managing water and hay and the heat settings on the generator and the stalls of the lambs already born and the profound anxiety that seventeen-year-old boys felt when they understood, suddenly, that their mothers were mortal and flawed and doing their best in the dark.

When the fourth lamb arrived—dead, had been dead, the umbilical cord wrapped around the neck—Sarah cleaned it off anyway and set it aside for burning later, and she did not explain to Tommy why she was doing something for something that couldn't possibly benefit from care. Some gestures were just for the living.

By nine p.m., ten lambs were on the ground. The generator had been running for six hours on supplemental fuel. Sarah's hands were raw and covered in a thin layer of grime that no amount of washing seemed to remove. She'd eaten nothing since breakfast. Tommy had drunk about a gallon of water out of the cold tap—his way of managing fear, she'd learned. Some people got busy, some people drank water, some people named their lambs Asterix.

"How many more?" Tommy asked during a lull between labors.

Sarah consulted the notebook again, doing the arithmetic she'd already done several times. "Three, maybe four more due before the end of April. The rest aren't scheduled until May."

"So maybe we only have a few more tonight?"

"Tonight or tomorrow or whenever it breaks." Sarah looked up from the notebook. Tommy was standing in that angular way of his, weight on one leg, the other cocked at an impossible angle that only teenagers and herons could manage. He looked exhausted and somehow younger than he had that morning. "You should sleep. I'll watch them."

"I'm not sleeping."

"Tommy—"

"I'm not," he repeated, and there was a firmness in his voice that reminded her so sharply of Michael that she stopped arguing. "I'll sit with the ones that are born. You work."

They established a rhythm around this. Tommy moved between the new lambs and their mothers, making sure the connections held, that the nursing was productive, that the warming was adequate. He sat with them in the cold barn, sometimes talking, sometimes just present, doing the thing his father had taught him to do without having to teach it—just by example, just by being the kind of man who sat with animals and kept them alive.

The fifth ewe came into active labor around ten. Sarah recognized the signs now, had watched enough that the pattern had imprinted itself into her muscles as much as her mind. This one was older, had delivered before, and had a confidence about her discomfort that was almost reassuring. Two lambs, both healthy, both correct presentations. The work was becoming easier in the way that any repeated action becomes easier—not easier in its accomplishment, but easier in the rhythm of it, the familiar repetitions.

But something had changed, Sarah realized, between the fifth and the sixth ewe. Some accumulation of time and cold and purpose had shifted. She found herself less aware of Tommy, less aware of the snow outside, less aware even of herself as a separate entity. She was just hands, just attention, just the person whose job it was to move the work forward. This was almost peaceful.

The sixth ewe was smaller, younger, probably a first-timer who'd been bred too early, against better judgment, possibly by Michael before his judgment had been taken out of service by heart attack. The lamb was large for the ewe's frame. The presentation was wrong again, the lamb twisted sideways, its front legs bent back.

Sarah worked with quiet intensity, trying to rotate the lamb, to find the angle that would allow passage. The ewe screamed—actual screams, the kind that couldn't be misinterpreted as anything but agony. Tommy appeared, offered his hands, and they worked together in a new way, Sarah directing and manipulating while Tommy provided pressure, support, an additional set of eyes.

"Is it—" Tommy started.

"Sideways. Little one, too small for this lamb." Sarah kept working. "If we can't turn it—"

She didn't finish the sentence. Neither of them needed her to. If the lamb couldn't be turned and couldn't be delivered as it was, then the choice was between losing the lamb or losing the ewe, and sometimes both.

The lamb's shoulder rotated against the inside of the ewe's pelvis, found a new angle, began to slide. Sarah guided the front legs around, felt the moment when the entire presentation shifted from impossible to difficult to possible. The lamb came in a rush—bloodied and gasping and alive, both its eyes open, tiny and furious.

The ewe collapsed entirely, flat on her side, and for a moment Sarah's heart went with her. But the animal's sides were still moving, still breathing. The immediate threat had passed. The ewe had given everything for this lamb, and the lamb was alive.

Sarah cleaned the lamb, set it at the ewe's udder, and stepped back into the cold barn, where the generator hummed and the wind sang in the rafters and Tommy's face was illuminated by the understanding that he'd just watched his mother do something that his father had probably done hundreds of times and that felt, in this moment, like a kind of magic.

"You should get some air," Sarah said.

"So should you."

"I will." She was already moving toward the supply shelves, already thinking about what came next. "But one of us needs to stay available. Go on. Ten minutes. Walk around. Just—be outside of this for a moment."

Tommy hesitated, and then he understood. He needed to go. He nodded and moved toward the door, pulling on his jacket, his gloves, the mask that still smelled like his father, and Sarah watched him leave, then turned her attention back to the animals.

Seven lambs had been born. That meant the ewes' work was mostly done. That meant hers was mostly done. That meant they'd probably make it through, that the generator fuel would last, that some natural balance had been struck. The barn was still cold, but it was a cold that the animals could tolerate, that the lambs were tolerating, huddled against their mothers in the lamplight that ran off the generator Michael had bought and serviced and left behind.

Sarah sat down on the milk crate and let herself shake, just for a moment, with everything she'd been holding in check. Then she stood up, wiped her face, and moved to the water bucket to clean her hands.

When Tommy returned, his hair was dusted with snow, and his eyes looked clearer than they had. "It's beautiful," he said. "It's terrible and beautiful and everything is just white."

"Yes," Sarah said. "That's what it's like."

They sat together then, not talking, just sitting with the animals as the night deepened and the lambs slept and the wind continued its work outside. Somewhere around midnight, the generator's note changed slightly—a small modulation that probably meant nothing important but that Sarah registered anyway. She'd learned Michael's language after all, his careful attention to systems and their failures, his way of reading the world for what it was trying to tell you.

She didn't get up to check. There were two days' worth of fuel left, maybe more, and other things required her attention. Tomorrow would bring more challenges—the weather might break or might not, the power might return or might not, there might be more ewes in labor or there might be a miraculous calm. But tonight, they had light and heat and living animals. That was enough. That would have to be enough.

Sarah put her hand on Tommy's shoulder, and he leaned his head against her arm like he was still small, like she still had the answers, like the simple contact of hands could communicate what couldn't be said. The barn held them both—the cold, the light, the lambs breathing, the world outside erased to white.
