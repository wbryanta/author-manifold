# The Return

Margaret had not intended to come back to Sacramento. She had made a point of not coming back to Sacramento for twenty-three years, had positioned herself carefully across the country in a way that suggested not evasion exactly but rather the normal drift of a life lived without particular attachment to origins. Her daughter Caroline had gotten married in Portland. Her son Richard had settled in Boston with his wife and their two children, whom Margaret saw at Christmas if the flights were reasonable. She had done the things one does—she had a house, a small house but a house, with a garden that required more attention than she gave it. She had a job at a design firm that paid adequately. She had never married, had never felt the lack of it keenly enough to pursue it, and this too seemed like a choice she had made, or rather a condition she had allowed to become true of her by not resisting it.

When she received the invitation to Richard's cousin Patricia's wedding at the Gramercy Hotel, she had felt a small contraction in her chest, the kind of physical response to an idea that she had learned decades ago to notice and then to set aside. The Gramercy Hotel. It would have been rebuilt by now, of course. Hotels were rebuilt. That was what hotels did. They burned and they were rebuilt, and people went to their weddings there, and someone made the reservations and someone checked the guests in, and no one thought about the maids anymore, the girls who had worked the third floor and the fourth floor and had fled down the back stairs in the dark.

She had called Richard and asked if they might have the reception somewhere else, and Richard had sounded puzzled. "It's a beautiful hotel, Mom," he said. "Patricia chose it specifically. She likes the historical aspect, actually. It's been completely renovated. It's quite elegant." Margaret heard the slight defensiveness in his voice, the way young people got when they thought you were being difficult about something that was not in fact about that thing at all. She did not try to explain. What would she have said? That she had worked there as a girl, that she had seen the smoke begin to seep under the linen closet door on a Tuesday afternoon in March? That she could still remember the smell of it, not just the smoke itself but something else underneath, something chemical and wrong? That she remembered standing in the alley afterward in a borrowed coat, watching the firemen work, feeling nothing at all—not relief, not fear, not gratitude—just a kind of blank incomprehension, as if someone had asked her a question in a language she did not speak?

"I'll be there," she had said to Richard. "Of course I'll be there."

Now she was in the lobby of the rebuilt Gramercy Hotel on a Friday evening in June, and it was not at all as she remembered it. But this, she supposed, was the point. They had renovated. They had taken the bones of the building, which had survived, and they had done something entirely new with it. The lobby was all pale wood and glass now, very modern for its time, or perhaps not modern anymore—it was 2003, and she was not sure what counted as modern now. There was a fountain in the center, a large abstract thing that burbled water over itself in what seemed like an inefficient way. There were plants. Someone had decided that plants improved things.

She checked in at the desk and the young man there was very polite and efficient and had no idea who she was. Of course he had no idea. She looked nothing like she had looked at nineteen. She had a gray streak in her hair that she no longer bothered to dye out. She had the kind of face that women develop after a certain number of years of not being particularly beautiful and not fighting it—a face of repose, her friend Helen had once said, meaning it kindly.

Her room was on the second floor. She had asked for the second floor when she made the reservation, had done it casually enough that the woman taking her reservation had not remarked on it. The third floor and the fourth floor were where the fire had started, where the old wiring had finally given out, where the dry rot in the walls had been waiting for an excuse. She had been on the third floor that afternoon, had been replacing towels in the rooms, had been thinking about what she would eat for dinner when she got off her shift, had been thinking about absolutely nothing of importance when Mrs. Dalton had come hurrying down the corridor saying there was a smell, had anyone else smelled that smell?

She unpacked her things now, hanging the navy dress she had bought specifically for this event in the closet, the one that was supposed to make her look like the mother of the groom at a wedding where she was only the mother of a distant cousin of the groom, a relationship so attenuated that she was surprised to find herself invited at all. Richard had explained it to her—something about Patricia's mother and Margaret's sister-in-law having been close, something about family obligation and tradition. It was the kind of thing that seemed important to people like Richard, people who had grown up thinking that family meant something larger than it had meant in her childhood, something more reliably present.

She lay on the bed for a moment, not resting exactly, just occupying the space. Through the window she could see the street below, the same street it had always been, though the shops along it had changed. There was a Starbucks now where the dry goods store had been. Everything was always becoming something else, and people accepted this as natural, as the correct way of things.

The wedding reception was at seven. Dinner was at seven-thirty. She had hours yet. She got up and went downstairs again, drawn by a feeling she did not quite want to examine. The hallway to the right of the lobby led toward the old restaurant, though it had certainly been reconfigured. She found herself walking there anyway, her feet remembering the path even though the path had been made new. The restaurant was called Meridian now. It had tall windows and very white tablecloths and waiters in subtle uniforms. There was a bar at one end where people were already gathering for drinks before the ceremony. She stood in the entrance and looked at it, this room that had not existed when she last stood in this building.

"Can I help you find something?" A woman in a black dress, young, maybe thirty, was speaking to her with professional politeness.

"I'm looking for the wedding reception," Margaret said. "I'm early."

"Oh, you're a guest of Patricia's? We won't be seating anyone until seven-fifteen. There's a lounge near the main ballroom if you'd like to wait there, have a drink."

Margaret nodded and turned away. She had not meant to come to the restaurant. It was not that she needed to see everything that had changed, exactly. It was more that she needed to confirm something, and she was not sure what that something was, and she suspected that the confirmation would not actually tell her anything.

She found the main ballroom, which was called the Grand Room, and there was Patricia's mother, who Margaret recognized dimly from some previous family event, standing with a clipboard and wearing an expression of controlled panic. The room was full of flowers—white roses and gardenias and pale green hydrangeas—and there was a string quartet warming up at one end. Everything had been made very beautiful and very expensive. Patricia's father must have money, Margaret thought. Or Patricia's mother had married well. One or the other.

The ceremony took place in a small room adjacent to the ballroom, an arrangement that seemed practical. Margaret sat in the second row and listened to Patricia make her vows in a clear, steady voice. She was a thin girl—no, a woman, Margaret reminded herself, she must be thirty-five—in a dress that had clearly cost more than Margaret spent on clothes in a year. She was marrying a man named David who worked in finance and had the kind of pleasant, unremarkable face that seemed to belong to men who worked in finance. They had met at Stanford. They had been dating for three years. All of this information had been relayed to Margaret by Richard, who seemed to believe that background information about distant relatives would interest her.

At the reception, Margaret was seated at a table with people she did not know. They were Patricia's colleagues from the nonprofit where she worked, a foundation that gave money to environmental causes. They were pleasant enough, asking her where she lived and what she did, and she answered them with the mild details of her actual life, and they responded with their own details, and after a while the salad course arrived and the conversation became the kind of conversation that requires minimal engagement.

"How do you know Patricia?" one of them asked, a woman named Jennifer or Jessica, something with a J.

"Through the family," Margaret said. "Very distantly. My son is her cousin."

"Oh nice. Patricia's family seems really spread out. Her parents are wonderful people though. Her mother has been so stressed about this wedding but she's managed it beautifully."

Margaret agreed that it had been managed beautifully, and then she looked across the ballroom toward the kitchen entrance, where staff members were moving in and out with practiced efficiency. Young people mostly, though there was an older man with gray at his temples supervising something, checking something off on a clipboard. The catering company, probably. They contracted out for events now, she was sure. The hotel had reduced overhead, eliminated positions. Progress, that was what people called it.

She excused herself after dinner and walked back toward the lobby. She was not trying to go anywhere in particular. She just needed to be alone for a moment, needed to sit in a chair that was not part of a table of six people trying to make pleasant conversation. There was a lounge near the elevators, a modern lounge, all clean lines and comfortable seating. She chose a chair facing the entrance and sat down.

A man approached her almost immediately, perhaps five minutes later. He was probably seventy, maybe older, with the kind of lean, weathered face that came from years of working outside or in some other condition that damaged skin. He was not a guest—he was wearing a small badge that said "Staff," though it said nothing else. He looked at her for a moment, and Margaret looked back, and then he said, "You shouldn't be here."

It was not a question. It was stated as a fact, matter-of-factly, without any particular rancor. Margaret felt something in her chest contract again, that same feeling from when she had opened the invitation.

"I'm a guest," she said. "My family—"

"I know," he said. "I remember you. You were here. You worked here."

Margaret did not say anything. She had learned long ago that there was no adequate response to recognition of this kind. You could either confirm it or deny it, and neither response gave you what you needed.

"The fire was 1980," he said. He sat down across from her without being invited. "March. You were on the third floor. You came down the back stairs."

"You have a good memory," Margaret said finally.

"I was the manager then," he said. "I was the manager for thirty years. I retired ten years ago. I only come back for events now, special events, to help my nephew. He's the current manager. I told him the other day I'd come back to help with this wedding, and when I was going over the guest list I saw the name. Margaret Stryker. I remembered you because you were very calm about it. After. A lot of the girls who were working that day were hysterical, crying, frightened. You just sat on a bench in the alley and didn't say anything at all."

"I was frightened too," Margaret said. "I just didn't show it."

"I know. That's why I remembered. That kind of thing, you don't forget it. The ones who don't cry are the ones you worry about." He paused. "Twelve people died in this building, you know. Not in the fire itself, but the injuries, the smoke inhalation, the complications. We never made the national news much because of the hotel fire that happened in Las Vegas that same month. But twelve people."

Margaret had not known this. She had not read about it afterward, had not gone looking for information. She had simply moved on, had moved out of Sacramento, had gotten a new job in Oakland, and then a better job in San Francisco, and then had left California entirely. She had let the event fall away from her like something that had happened to someone else.

"I didn't know," she said.

"No. Why would you. You were nineteen years old, just working for the summer before college, or maybe not going to college, I can't remember. You weren't part of the public story. You got away clean. That was the important thing."

"I don't know why you're telling me this," Margaret said.

"Because you came back," he said. "You actually came back to this building. Do you know how many of the people who worked here that day have come back? You're the first one. Everyone else moved away and stayed away. But you came back."

Margaret thought about this. She had come back because Richard was getting married—no, Richard wasn't getting married, Patricia was getting married—and because it had seemed like the kind of thing a mother did, attended these functions. She had not been running toward anything; she had been running away from the perception that she was still running away.

"I didn't want to come," she said. "I called Richard and asked if they could have the reception somewhere else."

"But you came anyway."

"Yes."

"That's the interesting part," he said. "You came anyway." He stood up. "I hope you enjoy the rest of the evening. Patricia is a lovely girl. She used to come to the hotel as a child with her parents. She was about ten when we rebuilt. She watched the building go up again. I think that's why she wanted to get married here. To take something that had been ruined and make it into something beautiful." He paused. "That seems like a good impulse to me."

He walked away then, back toward the ballroom, and Margaret sat alone in the lounge and thought about impulses, about what drove people to come back, about the difference between moving on and actually escaping something. She thought about how you never really escaped; you just changed the way you carried things. The weight stayed the same. It just shifted.

She went back to the reception after a while. The dancing had started. Patricia and David were dancing, and then Patricia was dancing with her father, and then the father of the groom was dancing with his wife, and gradually more people joined in, and the dance floor became a kind of casual celebration of something—joy, maybe, or the performance of joy, or the understanding that the performance was what mattered.

Richard found her near the dessert table and put his arm around her. He was happy. He was the kind of person who was capable of real happiness, and Margaret was grateful for this about him, though she was not sure why. Maybe because it meant that her distance from him did not actually harm him. He would be fine regardless of what she did or did not feel.

"How are you doing?" he asked. "I know this is kind of a lot of relatives you don't see very often."

"I'm fine," she said. "It's a lovely wedding."

"Patricia wanted to get married in this hotel specifically. Something about it being rebuilt after the fire in 1980. She's very into the idea of renewal, second chances, that kind of thing. I told her about how you used to live in Sacramento, and she thought that was a nice connection."

Margaret felt something shift in her. Richard had told Patricia? Richard had mentioned to Patricia that his mother used to live in Sacramento?

"I didn't work here," Margaret said carefully. "I just lived here."

"Oh, I know. But Patricia was interested because of the fire, and I mentioned that you had been a resident at the time. She thought it was a meaningful connection, you being part of the community's history."

Margaret did not correct him again. What would be the point. History was something that happened to groups of people, not individuals. History was always being made and remade into something beautiful and reconciliatory and ultimately meaningless. A girl had worked in a hotel. A fire had happened. The hotel had been rebuilt. A woman came back to it years later for a wedding. These things happened. They happened all the time.

She left the reception at eleven and went back to her room and packed her things, though her flight was not until the next evening. She lay in the dark in the rebuilt hotel and listened to the sounds of the city outside—the traffic, the occasional siren, the ambient noise of Sacramento continuing to exist, continuing to remake itself. In the morning she would have breakfast. She would call Richard and thank him for including her. She would go to the airport early and read a book on the flight home. She would resume her life in Portland, the life she had constructed carefully and well, a life that asked very little of her and in return demanded very little of others.

But tonight she remained in the dark room, suspended between the Margaret who had fled down the back stairs in 1980 and the Margaret who had sat calmly in a chair in the rebuilt lobby and been remembered by a man whose name she had never known. She thought about renewal. She thought about how sometimes the things that destroyed you were also the things that freed you, and how you never knew which until much later, and sometimes never at all. She thought about hotels, about how they remained in place while everything changed around them, how they absorbed the stories of people passing through and then let those stories go, shed them like old paint.

In the morning, it would be a beautiful day. The sun would shine on the rebuilt Gramercy Hotel, and new guests would arrive, and new events would be planned, and the work of forgetting would continue, which was also the work of surviving, which was also, she supposed, the work of coming home.
