The whole thing was theater, really, that's what I'm saying. Theater for a particular audience. Jack Lovett paused here, looked at Inez Victor across whatever distance existed between them, physical or otherwise, because even when they were in the same room there was usually some distance, some careful arrangement of space that had to do with who they were and what they'd been to each other and what they could never quite be again. The thing about those events, he continued, was that they were always being performed for someone else. The scientists wanted the data. The Pentagon wanted the confirmation. The politicians wanted the photo op. But what we wanted, what the people who actually stood out there in the dark waiting for first light wanted, was to see something that the normal world couldn't see. Something that proved we existed in a different order of things.

Inez Victor lit a cigarette. She did this slowly, deliberately, the way she did most things, as if each gesture contained information that should not be rushed. She'd known Jack Lovett for thirty years, had loved him in the way that certain people love each other without ever quite resolving whether that love was the deepest thing between them or a surface beneath which other, less nameable things moved. He was talking about the tests because that's what men like Jack did when they were trying to say something they couldn't say directly. They told stories. They constructed elaborate narratives of peripheral events and tangential details, building a kind of logical scaffolding that would eventually support whatever emotional truth they were trying to convey.

The thing you have to understand about being present at those events, Jack said, is that it changes something in you. It's not the radiation, though of course nobody knew what the radiation was doing, not really, not back then. It's something else. It's the sense of having witnessed the absolute outer limit of human capability. We weren't just observing a test. We were observing the moment when theory became material fact, when something that existed only in equations and calculations suddenly occupied actual space and released actual energy. You could feel the heat on your skin from miles away. You could taste metal in your mouth from the fallout. Your teeth would ache sometimes, days later, and you'd wonder if that was radiation or just the psychological weight of knowing what you'd seen.

He stopped talking and drank whatever was in his glass. Inez noticed that his hands had begun to shake slightly, which they hadn't done before. Or perhaps they had and she'd simply stopped noticing, the way you stop noticing the flaws in someone's face after you've looked at it long enough.

You came back different, he said. Everyone who was there came back different. Some of them couldn't talk about it, not to their wives, not to anyone. Some of them talked about it constantly, the way I'm talking about it now, as if the repetition could somehow integrate the experience into the normal sequence of their lives. It never did, though. It never could. Because you'd seen something that your government, your country, had decided should not exist in the normal world, should only exist in the form of tests conducted on remote islands and in the desert, witnessed by a small group of people sworn to secrecy about the details that actually mattered.

The details that actually mattered. Inez considered this phrase. She'd spent a large portion of her adult life distinguishing between the details that actually mattered and the details that simply appeared to matter, the ones that occupied space in conversations and newspaper columns and the carefully constructed narratives of public life. This was perhaps the central skill required to be married to a senator, to move through the upper reaches of California society, to remain present in the world of power without being entirely consumed by it. You learned to see through the surface structures, the official histories, to the bedrock of actual intention and actual consequence.

What changed, she said, was you. Not the world. You came back and the world was exactly as you'd left it. Your wife was exactly as you'd left her. Your children were exactly as you'd left them. But you were different, and so everything had to be rearranged to accommodate this new version of you, and nobody was ever told why the rearrangement was necessary.

Jack looked at her with something approaching surprise, as if she'd said something he'd been traveling toward without knowing it, something he'd needed to hear from someone else's mouth before it could seem real.

Yes, he said. That's exactly right. That's the thing that nobody talks about. The tests were classified, so you couldn't talk about what you'd actually seen. But the change in you wasn't classified, it was just invisible, something you carried around like a piece of machinery implanted under your skin that nobody could see but that changed the way you moved through the world.

They were sitting in what had once been called a living room, though neither of them lived here anymore. The house belonged to Inez's husband, Senator Harry Victor, though he was rarely in it. He spent most of his time in Washington or at their place in Montecito, conducting the daily business of being a man of power, being served lunch by men who wanted something from him, giving interviews to journalists who were interested in his opinions about matters that he'd learned long ago to have opinions about without actually thinking about them very much. This house, the one where Jack and Inez were sitting, was maintained by a staff that came twice a week, that kept it from descending into the chaos that uninhabited spaces naturally tend toward.

I saw him last week, Jack said. Your husband. We were at some thing, some dinner, some benefit or other. He seemed well. Prosperous. The kind of man who looks like he sleeps without difficulty.

Inez didn't respond to this directly. She finished her cigarette and reached for another one, a gesture that suggested that the conversation was about to become more difficult, that she was preparing herself for something.

He doesn't know about us, she said. Not really. He knows that we know each other. He knows that we've always known each other. But he doesn't know anything beyond the surface of that fact, and I've worked very hard to ensure that he never will.

Jack nodded slowly. This was not news to him, but it seemed to require the kind of acknowledgment that went beyond simple comprehension. It seemed to require recognition of the specific effort that Inez had expended, year after year, to maintain this separation of worlds.

When I was on those islands, he said, waiting for the shots, I used to think about you. Not in a sentimental way, not exactly. But I used to think about the fact that you existed somewhere else in the world, moving through your own version of reality, making decisions about your life that didn't involve me or reference me or take me into account in any way. And that was oddly comforting. It meant that there was a version of the world in which the tests didn't matter, in which the machinery of government and military power and scientific advancement existed only as background noise, something that occasionally appeared in the newspaper but didn't fundamentally alter the day-to-day texture of actual life.

I married Harry because he was the kind of man who could function in the world as it actually was, she said. A man who didn't require the world to be any different than it was. A man who could look at the tests and understand them not as some profound rupture in the order of things but simply as technical exercises conducted by the government, matters to be considered and debated and then filed away in the appropriate cabinet.

But you're not that kind of person, Jack said.

No, Inez said. I'm not.

She stood up and walked to the window. The view from this window showed the street where they lived, a street in a neighborhood that had been fashionable ten years ago and was still, in certain circles, considered desirable. There were large houses set back from the road, trees that had been planted sixty years ago and were now mature enough to provide substantial shade. It looked like the kind of place where nothing difficult ever happened, where the central concerns were the quality of the lawn service and whether the neighbors' children were being admitted to the right schools.

I need to tell you something, she said without turning around. Something that I've been thinking about for several months now, and that I've decided I need to tell you before it becomes something else, before it calcifies into a different shape and becomes impossible to discuss.

Jack remained silent. He'd learned, over the course of their long acquaintance, that Inez needed to be given space when she was trying to articulate something that didn't have a readily available form in language. She would work toward it gradually, circling closer with each sentence until she'd developed a structure stable enough to support what she wanted to say.

I think I've made a mistake, she said. A fundamental mistake. Not with Harry. That's been exactly what it needed to be, exactly the right choice for the person I was when I made it. But a mistake with you. With us. I've been operating on the assumption that we existed in two separate worlds, that what we had could be maintained in a kind of quarantine, separate from the actual machinery of my life with Harry, separate from the actual structure of the life I've built. And I've been very successful at maintaining that quarantine. But the longer I maintain it, the more I understand that what I'm actually doing is preventing myself from understanding what this is. What we are.

She turned to face him. Her face had aged since he'd last looked at it directly, though perhaps that was only because they were now sitting in a room with better light than they usually occupied together. Or perhaps it was simply true that she had aged, that time had moved forward whether they acknowledged it or not.

I don't want this to be something that exists in the margins of my life anymore, she said. I don't want this to be something I do in the way other people might have hobbies or small infidelities or confidential friendships. I want this to be organized, Jack. I want this to be the primary structure around which everything else is arranged.

This was, for Inez Victor, an extraordinary statement. It was the kind of statement that people made when they'd decided that the cost of maintaining a particular fiction had become greater than the cost of destroying it. It was the kind of statement that preceded significant changes in the arrangements of lives.

What are you saying? Jack asked, though he understood perfectly well what she was saying.

I'm saying that I'm going to leave Harry, she said. I'm saying that I'm going to file for divorce and that I'm going to do it in such a way that it creates the minimum possible amount of public disruption, the minimum possible amount of damage to his career. I'm good at managing these things. I've been trained in the art of making difficult transitions appear inevitable in retrospect. But I'm saying that I'm going to do this because the alternative is to spend the rest of my life pretending that a significant portion of my interior life doesn't exist, and I'm no longer willing to do that.

Jack stood up. He walked toward her, moving slowly, as if he was approaching something that might startle if he moved too quickly. He took her face in his hands the way he'd done on various occasions over the past thirty years, occasions that had been scattered through time like islands in an ocean, moments of absolute clarity separated by long periods of ordinary confusion and negotiation.

You don't have to do this, he said. You've already sacrificed enough. You've already built a life. You've already made the arrangements that seemed necessary.

But I'm not sacrificing anything, she said. That's what I'm trying to tell you. I've been telling myself that I was sacrificing something all these years, that I was giving something up in order to maintain the shape of my marriage and my public position. But I don't think that's true anymore. I think I was just afraid. I think I was just using Harry and my position and the whole structure of my life as a way to avoid understanding what I actually wanted, which is you. Which has always been you, since the beginning.

Jack held her against his chest, and Inez closed her eyes, and for a moment the two of them existed in a space that was separate from everything else, separate from the tests and the islands and the pink sky and the machinery of government and the carefully constructed lives they'd built. It was a small space, and it couldn't possibly hold everything that they were trying to put into it, but it was something. It was a beginning.
