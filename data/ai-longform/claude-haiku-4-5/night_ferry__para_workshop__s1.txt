# 

The ferry cuts through black water that doesn't feel like water anymore but like a substance that has forgotten its own name. Marcus stands at the rail watching the mainland recede, though there's nothing to see now except the navigation lights of other ships, small red and green eyes that blink and vanish. The air is cold enough to make his throat tight.

He's been awake for twenty-three hours. The call came at three in the morning from a woman named Constance, who identified herself as working for the Northern Regional Medical Examiner's office. She had a professional way of speaking that made everything sound both urgent and routine, as though unidentified bodies were discovered every morning and most turned out to be nobody's business.

"We believe we may have a match," she said, and proceeded to list characteristics like ingredients: height, approximate age, a distinctive scar on the left shoulder blade. Marcus had sat on the edge of his bed in the dark, phone pressed to his ear, trying to remember if he'd ever seen his brother's shoulder blades. They'd shared a childhood, but that was a long time ago.

"I'll need you to come in person," Constance said. "For a positive identification."

The ferry is half-empty. A few trucks lashed to the deck below, some cars, and maybe thirty passengers scattered across the lounges like objects that have been dropped from a height and have landed in random configurations. Most are sleeping. An old woman with a paperback and a plastic cup of coffee reads in the corner, her eyes moving steadily across the page with the concentration of someone engaged in serious work.

Marcus had booked the only cabin available, a small interior room with a narrow bunk and a porthole that looks onto nothing. He'd sat there for an hour before coming up to the deck, thinking about his brother.

Ten years. That's how long it had been since they'd spoken, and even that last conversation had been short and purposeful, full of the kind of statements that don't require response. His brother, David, had called to say he was moving north and wouldn't be coming back to the city. Marcus had said that was fine. What else was there to say?

There had been a time, years before that, when they'd been close in the way siblings are when they're young and have no choice but to understand each other. They'd shared a bedroom. They'd learned to read each other's moods the way other children learn to read books. But that was a different version of brotherhood, the kind that doesn't require effort because it hasn't yet discovered the possibility of failure.

Their father had died when Marcus was twenty-two and David nineteen. It was a heart attack, sudden and absolute. Their mother had needed them both, and for several months they'd both been there, present in the practical ways that grief demands. But something had shifted in the months afterward. David had developed convictions about how their mother should be cared for, about what she needed and what was good for her, and Marcus had disagreed. The disagreement had seemed large at the time. It had seemed to matter.

A wave hits the side of the ferry with a sound like a massive door slamming. The ship adjusts, and Marcus adjusts with it, his body responding to the movement without conscious instruction. The human body is better at acceptance than the human mind, he thinks. It knows how to accommodate forces larger than itself.

He tries to imagine what it would mean if the body in the medical examiner's office was David. He tries to conjure the appropriate feeling and finds instead a kind of blankness, like a room he's walked into and turned off the light in without knowing it.

The woman with the book looks up at him briefly, perhaps noticing his stillness, then returns to her reading. Marcus wonders what her story is. Everyone on a ferry in the middle of the night has a story. Everyone is going somewhere to meet something they're afraid of or hoping for. The old woman looks calm about whatever it is, turning pages with the quiet certainty of someone who has learned how to occupy time.

He goes back to his cabin and tries to sleep but instead lies in the narrow bunk thinking about the scar. He has never, as far as he can remember, seen a scar on David's shoulder blade. But memory is not reliable, and the years are long. David could have gotten that scar after they'd stopped being the kind of brothers who saw each other without clothes. He could have gotten it through any of a thousand ordinary accidents—a fall, a cut, a moment of carelessness that leaves a mark.

When he does sleep, his dreams are populated with people he doesn't recognize and places that don't quite exist, rooms that are combinations of other rooms, landscapes that shift when he's not looking at them. He wakes once in the middle of the night, disoriented, and can't remember whether he's moving or still.

Morning comes gray and indistinct. The window of his cabin shows him a fog so complete it seems less like weather than like the ordinary condition of the world, water visible only where the foam of the wake breaks through it. He showers using water that comes out hot and uncertain, and he dresses in the clothes he brought, which suddenly feel like clothes he's never worn before.

The cafeteria on the ferry serves eggs that taste like they were cooked yesterday and coffee that tastes like it was cooked a week ago. He eats them standing at a window, watching the fog, thinking about the particular quality of northern light. His brother had chosen to live in a place where the sun barely rose in winter and never set in summer, a place of extremes. That seemed like the kind of choice David would make, though Marcus wasn't sure why. Had David always been like that, drawn to absoluteness? Or had he become that way in the years after they'd stopped talking?

A man sits down across from him with a breakfast tray. He's in his fifties, unshaven, with the look of someone who has been traveling for a long time. He nods at Marcus without speaking and begins eating with a kind of grim concentration.

"Rough crossing," the man says after a while, not looking up from his eggs.

"I didn't notice," Marcus says, which is true enough.

"You wouldn't if you slept through it." The man finally looks at him. "You sleeping?"

"Not really."

The man nods, as though this confirms something he suspected. "First time up here?"

"Yes."

"I come five, six times a year for work," the man says. "You get used to the ferry. It becomes invisible after a while. Your body stops being aware of the motion." He sips his coffee with a grimace. "The coffee never becomes invisible. The coffee is always terrible."

Marcus almost smiles. It seems important, somehow, to maintain a normal interaction, to prove that he can sit across from a stranger and exchange the small courtesies that humans exchange. It's a form of practice.

"What's your business up there?" the man asks.

Marcus considers lying. He considers inventing a job, a reason, something ordinary. Instead he says, "I'm going to identify a body."

The man sets down his coffee cup carefully. "Ah," he says. "I'm sorry to hear that."

"I don't know if it's someone I know," Marcus says. "That's why I'm going."

The man nods and returns to his eggs. He seems to understand that there's nothing more to say about it, that the topic has been exhausted. After a while, he finishes eating and stands up, collecting his tray.

"Try not to hope too hard either way," he says. "It doesn't matter what you want to be true. Reality's going to be what it is." He walks away before Marcus can respond.

By the time the ferry docks, Marcus's hands are shaking. It's a physical response he hadn't anticipated, a betrayal by his own nervous system. He grips the ferry railing as the ship bumps against the dock and holds on until his hands steady.

The town is small, the kind of place that seems to exist more out of necessity than any other reason. There's a main street with shops that look like they haven't been remodeled since the seventies, a hotel that appears to be entirely empty, and various institutional buildings that house the functions of government. The medical examiner's office is in a gray concrete structure that also contains the police station. Constance is waiting for him there.

She's younger than her voice suggested, perhaps early forties, with the precise manner of someone who works with facts and only with facts. She shakes his hand and leads him through a hallway that smells like institutional cleaner and something underneath it, something he tries not to think about.

"I should tell you some things before we go in," she says, stopping in front of a door marked with a small placard. "The body has been in the water. There's been some decomposition. We're going to have you look at it, but we also have fingerprint records if you want to—"

"I should look at it first," Marcus says.

She nods, as though this is what she expected. She puts her hand on the door. "Are you ready?"

He is not ready. He will never be ready. "Yes," he says.

The room is colder than the hallway, artificially cold, and it smells like that smell, the one he was trying not to think about. There's a table with a shape on it covered by a white sheet, and the shape is wrong in the way that covered shapes are always wrong—too long or too short, the proportions somehow inexplicable.

Constance pulls the sheet back to the shoulders.

The face is not a face, not exactly. It's what a face becomes after being in water for an indeterminate amount of time. The features are distended, the skin is discolored, there's something wrong with the eyes that Marcus's mind refuses to process fully.

"I don't know," he says. "I can't tell."

"Take your time," Constance says. She's looking at the body, not at him. "Is there anything distinctive you can compare? Dental work? Any marks or scars?"

The body's shoulder is visible. There is a scar there, pale and slightly raised against the bloated skin. The scar of what. A childhood accident. A teenage mistake. An adult moment of carelessness. Any of those things.

"I don't know," Marcus says again. "I can't tell if it's my brother. It could be anyone."

"The fingerprints," Constance says gently. "We have records."

"Yes," he says. "Do the fingerprints. Please do the fingerprints."

She covers the body again and leads him out of the room. In her office, which is small and institutional and decorated with nothing personal at all, she opens a file on her computer. She has the fingerprints from a 2007 arrest for breaking and entering. It takes twenty minutes to cross-reference them with the deceased.

"It's not him," Constance says, reading the screen. "The fingerprints don't match. This is not your brother."

Marcus sits in the chair across from her desk and puts his face in his hands. He's not sure what he feels. Relief and something else, something darker. The knowledge that his brother is still out there somewhere, not dead, not at least in this particular way, but also not in his life, not part of his life, still gone.

"Do you want to try to find him?" Constance asks. "Your brother, I mean. He might not be that hard to locate if he lives in the area."

"No," Marcus says quickly. "No, I don't."

She doesn't ask why. She's a professional. She understands that people have their reasons, and the reasons are rarely simple.

The ferry back is crowded, full of people who seem to have actual reasons for traveling, people who belong in the places they're going. Marcus sits in the cafeteria with a cup of coffee and watches the fog roll past. The northern lights are visible tonight, though the word "visible" doesn't quite capture what they do. They don't reveal themselves in the way that light usually does. They seem to emerge from the darkness itself, like the darkness had light trapped inside it all along and is finally allowing it to escape.

He thinks about David standing somewhere in this northern landscape, looking at these lights. He thinks about whether David ever thinks about him. He thinks about the ten years and what they've contained.

His phone buzzes with an email from Constance. She's providing him with the contact information for the actual deceased, information that had been discovered in a jacket pocket, enough to eventually identify him, to contact his own family, to deliver the news to people who were actually waiting for it.

Marcus deletes the email without reading it completely.

By the time the ferry reaches the mainland, night has fallen completely. He drives back to the city, which takes five hours, which takes a lifetime. The highway is empty except for trucks, the same trucks he passed on the way north, it seems, carrying their loads back and forth in a perpetual exchange. He doesn't know what they carry. He doesn't particularly want to know.

His apartment is exactly as he left it. The dishes are still on the counter from the morning he received the call. The light is still on in his bedroom. It's as though he's been gone for minutes instead of days, as though time is a river that has paused waiting for him to decide what to do next.

He throws the dishes away instead of washing them. He turns off the light in the bedroom. He stands in his living room for a long time without moving.

He could find David. It wouldn't be that difficult. A person leaves traces. He could hire someone, or he could do it himself, could follow the threads of his brother's life until they led to wherever his brother is. He could call and say, "Hello, it's Marcus. I was in the north. I thought you might be dead."

Instead, he goes to bed. In the morning, he goes to work. He makes coffee and answers emails. He has lunch with a colleague. He does all the things that people do when they are alive and moving through the world. The knowledge that his brother is somewhere, alive and breathing the same air as everyone else, somewhere north in the land of extremes and insufficient darkness, sits with him like a stone in his stomach.

A month later, he receives a letter. No email, an actual letter, with a stamp and a postmark from the north. The envelope is plain, addressed to him in handwriting he doesn't recognize. Inside is a single page, written by hand.

"Marcus," it says. "I heard you came looking for me. I heard a body was found and my name came up and then they must have found it wasn't me. I don't know how you knew to look. We haven't spoken in ten years. But I want you to know I'm alive. I want you to know I'm okay. And I want you to know that I'm not coming back. This is where I belong now. I don't know if you understand that. Probably you don't. But I needed you to know it. David."

Marcus reads the letter three times. There's no return address, no way to respond. He considers getting in his car and driving north again, considers standing on the ferry deck and trying to reverse time, trying to go back to a version of events where things had been different.

Instead, he puts the letter in an envelope and files it away in a drawer. He doesn't burn it. He doesn't destroy it. He keeps it the way people keep important things—safely, carefully, in a place where it won't be found unless he decides to look for it.

He doesn't decide to look for it.

Years pass. They pass the way years pass for everyone, in the accumulation of small moments that don't mean much individually but that add up to something over time. He develops routines. He builds friendships that never quite become intimacy. He works and sleeps and eats and does the things that people do when they're alive but not entirely present, when they're living in the world but not fully in it.

Sometimes he thinks about David. Sometimes, driving north of the city on some errand, he'll see the sign for the ferry and he'll consider going, just to stand on the deck and look at the water. He never does.

Once, in a restaurant, he sees a man who looks like David might look now, older, weathered by northern light and northern weather. He watches this man for a long time, trying to decide if he should approach him. But the man is eating with someone else, someone who seems to matter to him, someone who makes him laugh. Marcus watches them for a while and then leaves the restaurant without introducing himself.

He doesn't know if it was David. The face could have been anyone's. The resemblance was probably just something he imposed on a stranger because he needed the stranger to be someone.

But he thinks about it. He thinks about the possibility that they had been in the same room, breathing the same air, separated by only a few tables and years and pride and the accumulated weight of all the things they had failed to say to each other. The man he might have seen laughed at something his companion said, and for a moment Marcus had felt something crack open in his chest, a door that had been sealed shut for so long that he'd forgotten it was there.

He never goes back to that restaurant, though it's not far from his apartment. It wouldn't be right to go back, wouldn't be right to try to engineer another meeting. That's not how reunion works. That's not how forgiveness works, if that's even what this is.

The letter remains in the drawer. He never reads it again after the first month, though he sometimes remembers phrases from it, small fragments that surface in his consciousness without warning. "I'm okay." That's what David had said. "I'm okay." As though David knew that Marcus would want to know that, as though David had imagined Marcus standing somewhere in the dark, wondering.

It's a small thing, a letter from a brother he hasn't seen in fifteen years. It changes nothing and everything. It's a bridge that connects them across the distance, but a bridge no one ever crosses. It exists for the knowing, not for the walking.

And Marcus lives with that, the way he lives with everything—carefully, quietly, moving through the world as though it's a place he's learned to navigate without looking up.
