# The Crossing

The ferry cuts through a darkness that is almost absolute. There is the sound of it—the low machinery beneath the deck, the percussion of water against steel—and there is the salt-heavy air that enters through the opened window of his cabin, but for long stretches there is nothing else. He stands at the railing on the starboard side, a man in his fifties now, shaped by the kind of work that bends the spine and hardens the hands. The northern latitudes have stolen the color from his hair. They've given him instead a kind of transparency, as if he's being slowly erased by the long daylight that won't quite break here, even at what passes for night.

The letter came to his workplace. A construction site near Prince Rupert where he'd been hired to work the framing crew. The envelope was official in that way that official things are—government seal, his name spelled correctly, an address that was three years out of date but had somehow found him anyway. Inside, the careful bureaucratic language. The body. The identification. The options. His brother's name appeared only once, in parentheses, as if it were merely supplementary information.

He hasn't seen Michael in fourteen years.

There had been a time when they were close, the way brothers are when they share a room and a particular kind of loneliness, when their parents were still alive and still angry with each other in that quiet, permanent way. They'd slept in the same bed during the coldest months, back-to-back like animals, and Michael had told him things in the dark that he never spoke aloud in daylight. But that was a different world, one in which Michael was still trying to become something, still had plans that involved other people.

The ferry hums around him. He can hear music from somewhere below, something tinny and sad coming through damaged speakers. A few other passengers have ventured onto the deck despite the cold. They stand separately from one another, each one lost in the particular isolation that ferries seem to generate. An older woman smokes with one hand thrust deep into her coat pocket. Two younger men, possibly lovers, possibly not, stand close enough to share warmth. A child leans over the railing with the kind of abandon that children have, as if the ocean might suddenly begin to speak and tell them secrets.

He thinks about what Michael had looked like. Not the recent past, which he can barely imagine, but years ago. Michael had their mother's face—the high cheekbones, the way his eyes seemed perpetually disappointed by what they were seeing. He'd been thinner than their father, more delicate. He'd played the violin badly in high school, with immense seriousness, in a school auditorium that smelled of floor wax and human breath. Their father had attended exactly one concert and afterward told Michael that he had no gift for it, which was true, and then had never spoken of it again.

The letter said the body was found in a fishing village two hundred kilometers north of Vancouver. Winter before last. There had been complications with identification. Dental records had been difficult to locate. A recent dental procedure, done off the books by someone who couldn't be found. The body was in storage. The crematorium was waiting. The government needed closure. That was the word they'd used. Closure.

His cabin is small. A narrow bed with a blanket that smells of diesel and something older, something that might be sleep itself. A porthole that looks out at nothing, just the suggestion of water. He lies there for an hour and doesn't sleep. The ferry rocks in a way that is almost gentle, and he thinks about the last time he saw Michael, which was in a coffee shop in Vancouver, a place with high ceilings and the kind of music that seemed designed to depress people. Michael had looked thinner than he remembered, hollowed out from the inside, as if something fundamental had been removed and not replaced. He'd been living in the Okanagan then, he'd said. Working in a vineyard. Or was it a restaurant? The conversation had the quality of a dream, where the details shift even as they're being spoken.

Michael had told him he was thinking about going north. Thinking about disappearing for a while. There was something he needed to work through. He'd used that phrase specifically: work through. It made no sense at the time. It makes less sense now.

The next morning brings a kind of false dawn—the sky lightening to pewter, the line between night and day becoming negotiable. He showers in the narrow stall and dresses in clothes that have been packed so long they carry the smell of the duffel bag. He shaves carefully, examining himself in the small mirror as if the reflection might contain some clue about what he should do or feel. His face is weather-beaten in a way that makes it difficult to determine his actual age. He could be younger or older than he is. The eyes are competent but tired. The mouth is shaped like someone's mouth, not like anyone's in particular.

The breakfast room smells of burned eggs and burnt coffee. He sits at a table near the window and eats without tasting. An older man across from him reads a newspaper, folding the pages with deliberate care. Through the glass, the coast emerges incrementally from the darkness. Rocky outcroppings. Dense forest coming down to the shoreline. The landscape looks untouched by anything, which he knows is unlikely. Everything has been touched. Everything has been altered.

He thinks about what they will ask him to do. They will bring him into a room, or perhaps a refrigerated room that is made to look less refrigerated. They will show him the body, or part of the body, or a photograph of the body. They will ask him if this is Michael. They will be gentle about it or not gentle about it, depending on who they are. And then what? He will say yes or no. Yes will mean that he acknowledges this transformation—dust into what dust becomes, the body as a kind of terrible punctuation. No will mean that his brother is still out there somewhere, still alive in the way that missing people are alive, as an absence that has its own terrible weight.

The ferry passes through a strait where the water moves in different directions at once, creating patterns that look almost purposeful. The other passengers begin to gather on deck as land becomes more definitively present. A town emerges from the conifer forest—a cluster of buildings that seem to have been set down without much thought to their arrangement. Docks with fishing boats. A church steeple. Houses that face the water as if waiting for something to be returned to them.

He goes down to collect his bag, moving through the interior of the ferry with the shuffling gait of someone disembarking. The corridors smell of industrial cleaner and something less identifiable. A couple is arguing quietly in one of the doorways, their voices carrying the particular pitch of a conversation that has been ongoing for a long time. He passes them and feels nothing. He is becoming more transparent.

The terminal is small and poorly heated. A woman sits behind a counter reading a book, barely looking up as the passengers file past. He steps out into the cold air and sees a man holding a sign with his name on it. The man is perhaps forty, with the weathered face of someone who works outside. His eyes are neutral.

They drive through the town, which is smaller than it appeared from the water. Streets lined with storefronts selling bait and groceries and things that tourists might want if any tourists came here. A few people on the sidewalks, moving with the kind of purpose that comes from having lived in a small place long enough that all routes are familiar.

The man—whose name is Constable Morrison, he's told him—drives slowly and doesn't speak. The radio plays country music at a low volume. On the outskirts of town, they pass a cemetery where the headstones lean at various angles, as if the ground had shifted beneath them. Some of the graves have flowers, many of them plastic and faded by weather.

The hospital is a newer building, incongruously modern in a town where most other structures seem to predate the modern world. Inside, it's warm and fluorescent and smells the way hospitals smell everywhere—a particular combination of disinfectant and something organic that disinfectant cannot completely eliminate. A woman in scrubs approaches them. She introduces herself but he doesn't retain her name. She has kind eyes and he's grateful for that, though it doesn't change anything.

They walk through corridors. They pass rooms where people are lying down, some of them unconscious, others staring at ceilings. They descend a level. The air becomes cooler. They arrive at a door and the woman unlocks it and they enter a room that is painted institutional beige and contains a table with something covered by a sheet.

She begins to explain, but he stops her. He nods toward the table. She and Constable Morrison exchange a glance that suggests they've done this before, have seen the particular ways that grief can demand to be rushed through.

She pulls back the sheet with the kind of grace that might come from experience or from a specific training in how to do this. The face revealed is both familiar and unfamiliar. Time and decay have altered it, but something beneath the alterations speaks of Michael—the shape of the nose, the particular way the cheekbones catch the light. This was a man. This is what remains of a man.

He realizes he is holding his breath.

Michael would be forty-eight now. Would have been. The body looks older, worn by something that wasn't just time. There's a tattoo on the wrist he doesn't remember from before, a small symbol of some kind. The fingernails are broken. The skin has a quality that makes clear that Michael has been nowhere warm for a very long time.

The woman is asking him something. He doesn't understand the question. She asks it again, gently. Is this your brother.

He wants to say that this is not anyone's brother anymore. That whatever existed between them, whatever might be recognized or claimed, ended long before death made the distinction academic. He wants to say that he doesn't know this person. That the person he knew was someone else entirely, someone who played violin badly and wanted to disappear and apparently succeeded more thoroughly than anyone might have predicted.

What he says is: Yes. That's him.

The woman looks relieved and devastated in equal measure. Constable Morrison places a hand on his shoulder, not pressing, just present. Around them the room is neutral, designed to accommodate only the necessary, with no ornament or softness to suggest that any feeling has ever occurred within these walls. And yet all of history is here. All of the histories. Every body that has been brought to this room has carried with it a particular story of ending, of the moment at which one person became no one. His brother is no one now. They have made him official. They have documented him. They have reduced him to the kind of paperwork that can be filed and stored and eventually forgotten.

He signs forms. He answers questions. The woman tells him that Michael had been living in a shelter for part of the time before his death. That he'd been seen at a community center. That someone had reported him missing almost two years before he was found. That he'd been identified by a dental match and by photographs provided by a woman named Sarah Chen, who had known him years ago and had preserved a picture.

Sarah Chen. The name carries something forward. He has no idea who Sarah Chen is, and yet the name suggests that Michael had connected with someone, had been present in the world as more than just a deteriorating person approaching death. This is somehow worse than if he'd been entirely alone.

The constable drives him to a hotel—a small building painted blue, with a neon sign that says Vacancy. He helps with his bag and tells him that the cremation can proceed whenever he wishes. They have his contact information. He nods, says nothing. The constable leaves him alone in a room that smells like industrial carpet and coffee and other people's sleep.

He sits on the bed and looks at his hands. These are the hands of someone who builds things. These are the hands of someone who has measured and cut and fastened. These are the hands of someone who has tried to make things solid in a world that resists solidity. He doesn't recognize them.

Outside, the northern light begins to change again, beginning its slow descent toward what passes for night in these latitudes. Snow had fallen the previous day, he'd been told. It would fall again. The world here is in a state of permanent transition between seasons, never quite committing to any single state. Michael had come here. Michael had chosen to come to a place where nothing seemed permanent. Michael had come to disappear.

He goes down to the small restaurant attached to the hotel and sits in a booth that faces the street. He orders soup and bread and water. The soup is hot and tastes of nothing in particular. The bread is dense. The water is cold and clear. The restaurant has only two other customers—a couple sitting at the counter, not speaking to each other, eating with the focused attention of people for whom food is simply fuel.

When he finishes eating, he doesn't feel any different. He had expected some shift, some rupture in the continuity of himself. Instead, he is simply a man who has eaten soup and now sits in a restaurant in a small town in a northern place, and his brother is downstairs in a hospital, waiting to be rendered into ash.

He thinks about the last time they'd spoken in that coffee shop. He thinks about how Michael had said he needed to work through something, as if life were a problem that could be solved through the application of proper technique. He thinks about how they'd left things. There had been no embrace. There had been a brief awkward conversation about staying in touch that both of them had known would be abandoned. Michael had paid for the coffee, which had surprised him, because Michael never had money. Or rather, Michael had never had enough money to do anything but spend it on things that didn't matter. He'd left the coffee shop and driven away, and that had been the last time—though he hadn't known it was the last time, because no one ever knows in the moment, until much later when knowing becomes the only available option.

He leaves money on the table and goes back to his room. He lies on the bed and waits for sleep that doesn't come. Instead, he lies and watches the light change outside the window, watching the world shift from gray to darker gray to black. Somewhere in this town, in a hospital basement, his brother is waiting to be turned into ash. Tomorrow or the next day, they will put the ash into a container and give it to him. He will have to decide what to do with it. He will have to find some way to hold this remainder, this final weight of what was Michael.

He thinks about the violin. He thinks about Michael playing in that school auditorium, the notes wavering and uncertain, but played with a particular kind of commitment, as if belief might make the music true. He thinks about their father sitting in the audience, his face already composed into disappointment before the first note was played. He thinks about how little it would have taken—a word, a gesture, something to indicate that the effort itself mattered—to change the trajectory of a life. But nothing was offered, and Michael had learned, and their father had gone on to die, and Michael had gone on to disappear, and now he is here in this room in this small town, and it is too late for anything to be changed.

The next morning, he walks through the town. The streets are slick with ice, and he moves carefully, as if any sudden movement might break through the surface and pull him down. He passes the church. He passes the cemetery again. He passes the hospital and doesn't go in. There is nothing to be done yet. The paperwork is being processed. His signature is becoming official. His identification of the body is being recorded in files that will outlast him.

He walks to the water. The strait between this place and the larger world is gray and textured. A fishing boat moves through the distance, heading toward something he can't see. He stands at the edge and feels the cold coming off the water, and he understands, in the way that understanding sometimes comes without explanation, that Michael had come here for the cold. That Michael had come here for the distance. That Michael had come here because coming here meant that everything before no longer mattered.

When he finally returns to the hotel, there is a message waiting. The cremation can proceed. The container, when ready, can be picked up. He calls back and leaves a message saying yes, proceed. The woman on the phone seems relieved, as if waiting had been difficult, though she's done nothing but her job. He thanks her and hangs up and sits in the lobby of the small hotel and waits for what comes next.

What comes next is the slow accumulation of hours. What comes next is the gradual understanding that nothing will change the fundamental fact: his brother is gone, and he is still here, and there is no bridge between these states. What comes next is the ferry ride back, the container sitting on the bed beside him, sealed in a box that gives no indication of its contents. What comes next is the return to the construction site, to the work of building things that will eventually be dismantled, to the rhythm of labor that fills time without filling anything else.

But that is still to come. For now, he sits in the lobby and looks at nothing in particular, and he is present in his body in a way that barely qualifies as presence. The northern light beyond the window begins again its slow cycling, dark to gray to dark, and he doesn't move. He has become something else now—something that contains absence the way other people contain hope.
