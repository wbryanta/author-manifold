# The Mapping

The truck wound down the canyon road in low gear, gravel spinning beneath bald tires that had seen better seasons. Margaret held the wheel steady, watching the lake appear in increments—first as a distant gleam between the pines, then as a blue suggestion, and finally as the thing itself, diminished and strange, the color of weak tea where it should have been deep bottle-green. The exposed ring of white mineral deposits marked the retreated waterline like a bathtub someone had forgotten to clean.

Forty-three years. That was how long it had been since she'd last stood on the actual streets of Millbrook, and even then, she'd been too young to understand that a town could simply disappear. She'd understood houses could burn. She'd understood that people moved away. But a whole town, the actual brick and mortar foundations, submerged under two hundred feet of water—this had seemed to her younger self like a magic trick, the kind that looked impossible until you realized someone had always known the secret.

The county office had called her specifically. Not just any surveyor would do. They wanted someone who might remember the layout, someone whose grandfather had built half the commercial buildings, someone whose family name appeared on property records dating back to 1887. Margaret had said no at first. She had a practice in Reno. She had contracts. She had carefully constructed a life that did not involve returning to the place where her mother had learned to drive on Main Street, where her father had proposed outside the Presbyterian church, where she herself had lost a front tooth during the Fourth of July parade of 1981.

But the county offered enough money to make refusal unreasonable, and her partner had been pushing her toward a sabbatical anyway, saying she'd become difficult, saying she drank too much wine on Wednesday nights, saying that sometimes Margaret would stare out the office window for entire minutes without blinking. So she'd said yes to the reservoir authority. She'd rented the truck. She'd packed her transit, her chains, her notebook, her grandfather's 1952 survey maps of the town, printed on paper so old it felt like papyrus.

The boat launch was crowded with other spectators—tourists mostly, people from the valley who wanted to see the marvel of exposure. Margaret parked at the far end of the lot and sat in the truck for a while, watching families unload kayaks and fishing equipment. A child in a pink life jacket ran toward the water with the reckless joy of someone who didn't know yet that places could vanish.

The surveying work would take weeks. The reservoir board wanted complete documentation of the exposed infrastructure—streets, sidewalks, building foundations, utilities—before the water rose again. There was talk of a small historical museum, maybe a walking tour, though that seemed optimistic to Margaret. Most people didn't want to be reminded that modern infrastructure was temporary, that a town could be drowned as casually as one drowns a mouse.

She found the old county maps on the passenger seat where she'd left them, their edges foxed and soft. She'd studied them for hours before coming, tracing streets with her finger the way she used to trace patterns on her mother's quilts. Maple Street, where she'd lived. Elm Street, where her friend Katie had had a house with a wraparound porch. Main Street, the commercial district with the hardware store, the drugstore with the soda fountain, the movie house where they showed films three nights a week.

The water was lower than she'd expected, even accounting for the drought. The lake had retreated so far that the canyon floor was visible in places—a strange, exposed landscape of smooth stone and exposed earth, already eroding where the former shore had collapsed. But there, emerging from the shallows like the spine of some vast skeleton, was the grid of streets.

Margaret got out of the truck.

The walk down to the waterline took longer than she anticipated. The terrain was treacherous, unstable in the way of newly-exposed earth. She picked her way carefully, her equipment bag slung across her chest. The air smelled wrong—like copper and algae and something vaguely sulfurous that hadn't seen sun in four decades.

The streets themselves appeared in order. First Ash, then Birch, then Cedar—her grandfather had apparently run out of tree-related enthusiasm partway through the alphabet, because after Cedar came numbered streets. The houses that had lined these streets were now just foundations, rectangles of cracked concrete and remnants of basements. The power lines had long since been removed, but the poles still stood, ghostly white with mineral deposits. It was like walking through a diagram of a town, all the essential lines drawn but none of the life filled in.

Margaret knelt beside the first foundation and pulled out her transit. The work was methodical, almost meditative. She took measurements, recorded angles, noted the alignment of utilities. Her hands remembered the motions from hundreds of other sites—schools, roads, subdivisions that would be built and probably demolished before anyone alive remembered her ever having mapped them. But this was different. This work would remain, a document of absence.

It took her three hours to map out the first block. By then her knees were sore and her eyes burned from the glare off the exposed stone. She was standing over the corner lot when she realized where she was. The hardware store. Bruner's Hardware. The storefront had occupied this corner lot with the distinctive copper gutters that had needed constant polishing. Her father had brought her here when she was small, had let her push the creaky door with its bell that chimed whenever someone entered. The owner, Mr. Bruner, had given her lemon drops from a crystal dish.

The foundation was there, just as it should be. But something about seeing it reduced to this—to outlines and angles—made her breath catch. She sat down, right there on the cracked concrete, her equipment beside her.

The sun was moving westward, throwing long shadows across the exposed streets. From this angle, with the shadows falling at a certain pitch, she could almost see the buildings again. Not perfectly—her imagination wasn't that faithful—but enough. The broad front window that had reflected the street. The awning that had shaded the sidewalk. The bell.

A sound made her look up. A figure was walking down the canyon, moving between the exposed foundations with the easy gait of someone familiar with the terrain. As it drew closer, Margaret recognized it as a woman, probably around her own age, with gray threading through dark hair pulled into a braid. She wore hiking boots and a ranger's jacket.

"You're the surveyor," the woman said when she was close enough. Not a question.

"Yes." Margaret got to her feet, brushing dust from her jeans. "County project."

"I thought so. Saw your truck." The woman extended her hand. "Elena Reeves. I work for the reservoir authority. I should have been here to brief you, but I was in town." She smiled, and something in that smile was familiar, though Margaret couldn't place it. "Actually, I live here, so I've been monitoring the exposure level. Checking for ecological damage, unstable shorelines, that sort of thing."

"You live here?" Margaret's surprise must have been evident, because Elena laughed.

"Not in the old town. In Millbrook Flats, the new development. Though I grew up here, in the old town. My father was the bank manager." She gestured toward the streets around them. "Second Avenue, the corner lot with the flagpole. Three stories, Victorian style, needed constant repairs."

"You lived in a bank building?"

"We lived above it. That's where—" Elena stopped, studying Margaret more closely. "What's your name?"

"Margaret Thorne."

Elena's eyes widened. "Margaret Thorne. God. You were maybe two years behind me in school?"

"Maybe."

"Your grandfather built things. My mother used to comment on his craftsmanship." Elena was smiling again, but differently now, with the peculiar intimacy of shared history. "I can't believe you came back. I thought everyone who left had made a clean break. That's what people did."

Margaret didn't know how to explain that she'd thought the same thing about herself, that she'd built her entire adult life on the assumption that Millbrook was the kind of place you left and never returned to, like a childhood fever or an embarrassing haircut. "The money was good. And the county wanted someone local."

"Someone with memory," Elena said. "That's the real commodity they wanted, isn't it? They could hire any surveyor from anywhere. But they wanted someone who could remember where the buildings actually stood, who knew the shape of the town in three dimensions instead of just two."

That was precisely what they wanted. Margaret hadn't articulated it to herself before, but Elena had named it exactly. She felt a strange gratitude for the accuracy of this stranger's—not quite stranger—analysis.

They stood in the copper silence of late afternoon, listening to the small sounds the exposed town made. The wind moving across stone. The occasional settling of unstable ground. In the distance, a kayaker's paddle hit water with a soft splash.

"I'll give you the lay of the land," Elena said. "I've been walking these streets for months now, watching them emerge. It's become—I don't know—part of my job, but also part of something else. Something personal."

They walked together, and Elena narrated the town as it had been, filling in the shadows Margaret's imagination couldn't quite hold. She pointed out where the post office had stood, where the library had occupied a converted mansion, where the school had been built in 1927 and was considered state-of-the-art. She spoke with a kind of tender accuracy that suggested she'd spent considerable time in this reconstruction, walking the streets of memory.

"My mother took me to the diner last," Elena said at one point. They were standing in what had been the heart of the commercial district. "The day before they started the flooding. I remember thinking even then that I should pay attention, that I should remember everything about it. But of course I didn't. I was eleven. What I mostly remember is that they were out of cherry pie and I was furious about that. Furious that I couldn't have cherry pie as some kind of remembrance."

Margaret thought about her last memory of the town. Her mother had taken her to say goodbye to Mrs. Patterson, the neighbor, who wasn't leaving. She was too old to start over, she'd said, though she'd ended up living another thirty years, eventually evacuated to a nursing home in the valley. But young Margaret had stood in Mrs. Patterson's kitchen, drinking lemonade and trying to understand what it meant that this house—this entire street—would soon be underwater. It had seemed impossible, the kind of thing that couldn't actually happen despite all the talk of dams and water projects and Progress.

They worked as the sun descended, Elena pointing out where streets intersected, where significant buildings had stood. Margaret took measurements, recorded angles, updated her maps. The work was easier now, contextualized. When Elena said, "The schoolhouse was just there, where you're setting up the transit," Margaret could visualize the redbrick building, could remember (or thought she could remember, though the memory might have been borrowed from a photograph) the bell tower.

By the time they stopped, full dark had fallen, and the stars were appearing with the clarity that only high desert darkness allowed. Margaret had forgotten about stars like these, stars so numerous they seemed wasteful. In Reno, the sky was perpetually milky with light pollution. This clarity felt almost aggressive, almost accusatory.

"You should come to dinner," Elena said. "Before you leave. My place isn't fancy, but I cook better than I used to. There's a point after you become an adult where cooking becomes interesting instead of a chore."

Margaret didn't commit, but she didn't refuse either. Elena gave her the address and drove away in a dust cloud, leaving Margaret alone with her equipment and the ghost town and the strange sensation of having been recognized after a very long absence.

She worked through the next week in a methodical way, moving through the streets in an established pattern. Ash to Birch to Cedar to numbered streets. Gradually, the town took shape again on her maps—not the lived town of memory, but the town reduced to measurements and geometry. Every street corner documented. Every building foundation recorded. Every utility line mapped.

On the fourth day, she found her own house.

She'd been avoiding it, unconsciously taking routes that skirted Maple Street. But there was work that needed to be done on that block, and she couldn't put it off forever. The house—her childhood home—sat on the eastern corner of Maple and Fifth. It was a modest 1960s ranch-style home, the kind that had been considered modern and desirable and economical all at once. The foundation was intact, though the basement had filled with sediment and algae bloom.

Margaret stood at the front corner of the foundation and tried to align the reality with her memory. The house had felt enormous when she was small, a sprawling palace with endless rooms. The foundation was maybe fifteen hundred square feet. She could see now where the kitchen had been, where the living room with its bay window had looked out onto the street. Her bedroom would have been there, in that corner—the northeast corner with the single high window that looked out onto the neighbors' apple tree.

She'd had nightmares after they moved, about water rising through the window, about being trapped in her sunken bedroom. Her parents had tried to explain that it was a good thing, that the water meant electricity and irrigation and Progress. But young Margaret had only heard that her room was going to be drowned, and no amount of rational explanation addressed the fundamental betrayal of that fact.

The surveying work on this block took longer than others. Margaret found herself repeating measurements, checking angles twice. It was as though her hands resisted the mapping, as though some part of her believed that careful documentation might change the outcome, might somehow prevent the water from returning.

Elena came again on the fifth day, bringing coffee in a thermos and sandwiches wrapped in foil. They ate sitting on the foundation of what had once been the town's water tower, looking out toward the main street.

"You mapped your house," Elena said. It wasn't a question. She'd seen the annotations in the survey map.

"It was necessary."

"Of course it was." Elena poured coffee into the thermos cap, steam rising in the clear air. "I did the same thing, last year. Not officially—I wasn't working for the county. But I came down here with a measuring tape and a notebook and I documented my old house from top to bottom. Or from corner to corner, I suppose, now that it's only a foundation."

"Did it help?"

"No," Elena said. "But it felt important anyway. Felt like some kind of ritual, even if it was useless. Like I was honoring something by taking it seriously enough to record it."

They sat in silence for a while, drinking coffee that was already getting cold.

"I left because I wanted to forget," Margaret said at last. "That was the explicit plan. Get out, get far away, build a life so different that the old one wouldn't matter."

"Did it work?"

"No." Margaret set down her coffee cup on the concrete. "It's exactly as bad as I expected it to be. Coming here. Walking these streets. It's exactly as bad as I thought it would be, and I still came."

"Maybe you needed to come," Elena said. "Maybe you needed to see that the town wasn't haunting you. That it was just a place. A place with a foundation and some memories and a specific geographic location, but ultimately just a place."

"Is that what you needed?"

Elena took a long time answering. "I never left," she said finally. "That's the difference. When they dammed the valley, I was old enough to understand what was happening, but not old enough to leave. My parents got jobs at the new town, we lived there for a while, and then I went to college in the valley. And when I graduated, I came back. Not to the drowned town—there was nothing to come back to. But to the place where it had been. I got work with the reservoir authority. I hiked these canyons. I came back to where my town used to be whenever the water was low enough."

"Why?"

"Because I needed to prove it was real," Elena said. "Because people kept acting like it was just this sad historical thing, and then it was done, and we moved forward. But it wasn't done for me. It was still happening. Every time the water rose, I felt like the town was being drowned again."

Margaret understood this completely. The work she did as a surveyor was, in some sense, an attempt to prove things were real before they disappeared. By mapping them, by documenting them, by creating an official record, she made them matter in a way that mere memory couldn't.

On the last day of the survey work, Margaret completed the final measurements and packed up her equipment. The maps were comprehensive now, detailed and accurate. They documented the town in absentia, recorded the places where people had lived and worked and built lives before the water came.

Elena helped her load the truck. They didn't talk much, just moved around each other with the easy coordination of people who'd been working together for nearly two weeks. When the last box was loaded, Elena said, "You could stay longer. The work could expand."

"No," Margaret said. "The work is done. And I need to go back."

"Will you come again? When the water drops again?"

Margaret thought about this. The drought wouldn't last forever. Eventually the rains would return, the lake would refill, and Millbrook would return to being the underwater town it had become. But there would be other droughts, other low waters, other years when the exposed foundations would be visible again.

"Yes," she said. "I'll come back."

She drove out of the canyon at sunset, watching the town disappear in her rearview mirror. The lake was already beginning to rise—a storm was moving in from the north, clouds gathering with the promise of snow in the mountains. By spring, the water would be higher. By next summer, probably back to near-normal levels.

But not entirely. The drought was a sign, people said, of something larger shifting in the climate. Even when the water rose again, it might not rise as high as it used to. There would be other low years, other exposures. The town under the water might become visible again.

Margaret thought about Elena, living in the new Millbrook, working for the authority that had drowned the old one. There was something almost hopeful in that, something that suggested the possibility of reconciliation. Not forgetting, not moving past, but learning to live alongside absence.

She drove through the night and arrived in Reno just before dawn. Her apartment was exactly as she'd left it, waiting for her like a piece of stage set. She made coffee and spread out her survey maps on the kitchen table. They looked official and final, the kind of documents that would be filed away in county archives, consulted occasionally by historians or curious tourists.

But they were also a record of her return, a map of her own journey back into the place she'd spent forty years trying to forget. The measurements were accurate. The angles were precise. But the real mapping, she understood now, was the interior one—the reorientation of her own internal geography, the understanding that you could leave a place and still carry it with you, that the work of surveying was as much about yourself as it was about the land.

Outside her window, Reno was waking up. Traffic began on the interstate. The sun rose over the mountains to the east. It was a different light than the light that fell on the exposed streets of Millbrook, but it was equally real, equally present, equally true.

Margaret sat with her coffee and her maps and thought about the next drought, the next time the water would drop and the town would emerge. It would happen, eventually. And when it did, she would return. She would bring her transit and her chains and her notebook. And she would walk those streets again, this time not as someone trying to forget but as someone learning, slowly, to remember.
