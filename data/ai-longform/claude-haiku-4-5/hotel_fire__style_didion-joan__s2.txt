The thing about surviving fire is that you never quite believe in the permanence of anything afterward. You understand in a way that other people, people who have not stood in a hallway watching the wallpaper curl like skin, do not understand, that all of this—the walls, the fixtures, the careful arrangements of comfort—is fundamentally temporary, a set of illusions maintained only by the collective agreement that they will hold. Margaret understood this in 1987 when she was twenty-three years old and working the morning shift at the Regency-Pacific, understood it still in 2004 as she stood in the reconstructed lobby, her daughter's wedding invitation crisp in her handbag, examining what they had made from the ruins.

The new Regency-Pacific was, in the way that rebuilt things always are, an approximation of the old one. The bones might have been similar—the same plot of land on Olive Street, the same general footprint, the same brass fixtures around the desk—but the effect was entirely different, which is to say that it had no effect at all, or rather it had the effect of expensive emptiness, the kind of deliberate sterility that hotels aim for now, spaces designed to be interchangeable, to be forgotten. The original had had character, a word people used to describe old things before the fire took character away from us, before we learned that character was just another word for accumulated negligence, for the slow combustion of poor wiring and worn carpets and the kinds of daily small failures that nobody notices until suddenly they notice them all at once.

She had not stayed at a hotel since 1987. This was not, strictly speaking, true—she had stayed at hotels—but she had not stayed at them in the way that normal people stay at hotels, which is to say without constant awareness of the exits, the smoke detectors, the fire suppression systems, the distance from her bed to the nearest window that might open. Her husband, before he was her ex-husband, had found this exhausting, this constant vigilance. He had wanted her to relax. Relaxation, she had tried to explain to him, was a luxury of the unaware.

The lobby smelled like floor wax and something floral that was probably supposed to suggest calm but instead suggested the kind of synthetic peace that made her want to leave immediately. She stood near the entrance, watching people check in, watching them make their transactions with the clerks behind the desk. Young people, mostly, in uniforms that suggested training seminars and employee handbooks. She wondered if any of them knew about the fire, if it was the kind of thing they were told during orientation, or if it was simply history now, something that belonged to the before-time, to the moment before whatever this was, this gleaming and forgettable space.

Her daughter Catherine was upstairs, presumably, in a suite, getting ready. Margaret had been invited to help with this, but she had declined. She had said that Catherine should have the experience of being ready with her friends, with the people who knew her as she was now, not as the daughter of a woman who had survived a fire in the same building thirty-seven years earlier. Margaret was not the type to burden occasions with her history, though it often seemed to her that her history was the only accurate thing about her, the only part that had never been revised or updated or made to fit the narrative that the rest of the world preferred.

She took the elevator to the third floor. This was a test, a small one, the kind of test that people who have not survived fire do not understand they are taking. The elevator was new, all mirrors and chrome, and smelled like the same floral thing as the lobby. She stood very still and did not press the alarm button and did not think about the fire doors that were not, she was certain, as fire-resistant as they claimed to be.

The third floor hallway looked nothing like the third floor hallway from 1987, which was to say it looked exactly like she remembered, which was impossible. She walked toward room 342, where she and her team had found Mrs. Chen in 1987, Mrs. Chen who had not wanted to leave her room because she did not want to be a bother, who had been polite about it in the way that a certain kind of person is polite about asking for help, which is to say not at all, which is to say by accepting whatever happened to her as her due.

She did not go to room 342. She went instead to the suite where her daughter was, room 401, which was larger now, she was sure, than it had been before, or perhaps the before-time was simply compressed in her memory, made smaller by trauma, by the way fear shrinks the world until it is only the size of a hallway and a fire escape that everyone was told not to use because the instructions were unclear and people were, in the end, very good at following contradictory instructions if you said them with enough authority.

Catherine opened the door before she could knock. Her daughter was twenty-eight years old, worked in marketing for a tech company, and had never asked her mother what it was like, the fire. This was not surprising. People generally did not ask, and Margaret generally did not tell them, and between the two of them this arrangement had held for nearly four decades.

"Mom," Catherine said. "You came."

"Of course," Margaret said. "Why would I not come?"

"I don't know. I thought—" Catherine gestured vaguely. "You never talk about this place. I thought maybe you wouldn't want to be here."

This was a perceptive thing for Catherine to say, in the way that children of the traumatized are sometimes perceptive, reading silences the way other people read books. Margaret stepped into the suite. It was very nice. It had a living area and a bedroom beyond, and a view of the city that was probably, she thought, the view from before, or something very close to it. The same buildings. Different light, maybe. Light was always different.

"I wanted to see where you were getting married," Margaret said. "In the place I worked. That seemed important."

Catherine was wearing a pale blue robe. She had her hair in a towel. There were other people in the suite—friends, presumably, bridesmaids—and they were looking at Margaret with the kind of sympathy that people reserve for the mothers of brides, that special pity they extend to women who have raised children and lived through time and are now required to stand on the sidelines while other people's narratives moved forward.

"Can we get you anything?" one of the bridesmaids asked. Her name might have been Jennifer. There were several Jennifers in Margaret's life now, though she had known none when she worked at the Regency-Pacific.

"No," Margaret said. "I'm fine."

This was a lie, but it was a sophisticated lie, the kind of lie that was close enough to truth that it could be told without the speaker feeling she was being dishonest. She was fine. She was not fine. She was standing in a room she had never been in, in a building that was not the building she had worked in, in a moment that was not the moment when everything changed, and in this moment she was as fine as anyone ever is, which is to say not fine at all, only moving forward because moving forward was what you did.

The ceremony was at three o'clock. Margaret sat in the hotel's new ballroom, which had been a parking structure in 1987, or perhaps it had always been here and she had not had access to it, not having been the type of employee who moved through the hotel's glamorous spaces. The ballroom was very white, very clean, very careful in its beauty. There were flowers everywhere—white roses, orchids, the kind of flowers that meant expense and care and the conviction that beauty could be ordered like room service and would arrive exactly as specified.

Margaret sat next to her ex-husband, who was next to his current wife, who was not as bad as Margaret had expected her to be, and who had been kind enough to ask Margaret, before the ceremony, how long she had worked at the Regency-Pacific. Not many people asked this question. Most people were too polite to press on anything that seemed private.

"Six years," Margaret had said. "Nineteen eighty-one to nineteen eighty-seven."

"That's a nice run," the current wife had said. "Good benefits back then?"

The question was, Margaret understood, not hostile. It was only a question, the kind of conversational offering that people made when they did not know what else to say. But it had struck her, standing there in the white ballroom, as so perfectly wrong, so completely missing the point of what those six years had been, that she had excused herself and gone to the bathroom and stood for several minutes in front of the mirror, looking at her face, which was older now, which had earned every line it contained.

The music began, and everyone stood. Catherine appeared at the end of the aisle in the kind of dress that took months to obtain, and Margaret felt the thing that mothers feel, which is not happiness exactly, but a kind of helplessness in the face of time passing. Here was her daughter, an adult woman, getting married in the place where her mother had almost died, in a rebuilt version of the place, in a space that was so carefully constructed that it did not acknowledge the fact of its own history. This seemed important, though Margaret could not have said why.

The ceremony was very nice. The vows were personalized but not too much, careful in their individuality, the kind of vows that suggested the couple had paid for a vows-writing workshop or a therapist or something similar. Margaret found herself thinking about her own wedding, which had taken place in a church, which had seemed like a very large thing at the time, very significant, but which she now understood had been only the beginning of a long process of trying to maintain an arrangement that was fundamentally unstable, the way all arrangements were, the way the Regency-Pacific had been, the way everything was, if you looked at it clearly enough.

At the reception, there was dancing. Margaret danced with her ex-husband because Catherine asked her to, and they moved through the motions of it, and he did not mention his wife, and she did not mention anything at all. They had been together for twenty-three years and had produced one child and had, in the end, discovered that they could not live together, though they had lived together quite well, all things considered. Margaret had not been unhappy in the way that women were supposed to be unhappy before they left their husbands. She had been, instead, exhausted by the constant performance of being fine, which was her default state, which had become such an integral part of who she was that she could no longer distinguish between the performance and the self.

Later, after the ceremony, after the dancing, after she had hugged Catherine and told her she was beautiful and would be very happy, Margaret took the elevator back upstairs. She had a room on the second floor, a room she had booked weeks earlier, though she had not told anyone she would be staying. The plan had been to attend the wedding and drive home, but standing in the lobby earlier, something had shifted, some resistance had softened, and she had decided, without thinking about it too much, that she would stay the night.

The second floor hallway looked like the third floor hallway from 1987, or perhaps that was only what her mind did with space now, compressing it all into a single hallway, a single moment that repeated every time she walked down a corridor in a building like this. Room 247 was her room. She opened the door and stepped inside and did not turn on the lights immediately but instead stood in the dark, letting her eyes adjust, letting her body remember that darkness was not dangerous, that buildings could be dark without burning, that a room could be an empty space without becoming a trap.

She had learned certain things, over the course of thirty-seven years. She had learned that survival was not the opposite of death, but rather a state of constant awareness of death's proximity. She had learned that buildings were always burning, in a way, always in the process of consuming their own structures, always moving toward some eventual collapse. She had learned that you could know these things and still live, still go to work, still raise a daughter, still attend her wedding, still do all the normal things that people did, if you were careful, if you maintained a certain distance, if you did not let yourself believe too thoroughly in the permanence of anything.

She turned on the lights. The room was pleasant, anonymous, exactly what a hotel room should be. There was a view of the city. There was a bed, a television, the usual arrangements. She sat on the edge of the bed and took out her phone and called Catherine.

"Hi, Mom," Catherine said. "Are you okay? What's wrong?"

"Nothing," Margaret said. "I just wanted to say congratulations again. To say that I'm happy."

There was a pause. Then Catherine said, "You're staying at the hotel. You're upstairs."

"Yes," Margaret said.

"Why didn't you tell me?"

"I wasn't sure I would, until I did."

Catherine was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "Do you remember when I was little and you wouldn't go to hotels? When we took that family trip to San Francisco and you made us stay with Aunt Linda instead?"

"Yes," Margaret said.

"Was it because of the fire? The fire at your old job?"

"Yes," Margaret said.

"I wish you had told me," Catherine said. "I wish I had known."

"I know," Margaret said. "But I'm here now. I'm staying at the hotel. I'm fine."

After she hung up, Margaret sat in the dark for a long time, looking out the window at the city lights, which seemed steady but which she knew were not steady, which were produced by electricity, by complex systems of power and distribution that could fail, could catch fire, could consume everything in their path. But the lights stayed on. The city continued. The world persisted in its fragile brightness, which was the only light that was available to her, the only light that had ever been available to anyone.

She thought about Mrs. Chen, who had stayed politely in her room, who had been found and carried out and had survived the fire and had never, as far as Margaret knew, returned to the hotel. She thought about Mr. and Mrs. Vasquez in room 407, who had refused to leave through the window, who had insisted on the elevator, who had not made it out. She thought about all the small moments of choice and circumstance that had resulted in her being the one who got out, the one who lived, the one who became the mother of a daughter who was upstairs in the rebuilt hotel, sleeping next to someone who loved her, who did not know about fire.

In the morning, Margaret woke early. The sun was coming through the window, and the room was very quiet. She lay in bed for a while, not thinking about anything, just existing in the physical fact of being alive in a room in a building. She showered using the hotel's small bottles of shampoo and conditioner. She dressed in the clothes she had brought. She looked at herself in the mirror and saw a woman of sixty years old, a woman who had survived, a woman who had worked in a building that had burned down, a woman who had raised a daughter, a woman who had lived through time.

Before she left, she walked down to the third floor. She did not go to room 342. She simply stood in the hallway, looking at the doors, looking at the fire extinguishers and the exit signs, looking at all the careful preparations against disaster. The hallway was very quiet. It was early, and the guests were sleeping. The building was in that state of peace that buildings sometimes achieved, that state of being empty of human need, of urgency, of the thousand small ways that people disrupted the careful silence of constructed space.

She walked to the stairwell and opened the door and looked down the concrete passage. This was not the original stairwell. The original had had wooden handrails. This one had metal ones. She touched the metal, which was cool, which was solid, which had never been on fire and might never be on fire, though it was always possible. Everything that was not currently on fire was only not on fire for now.

Catherine was waiting for her in the lobby. Her daughter was wearing sunglasses and the pleased exhaustion of someone who had gotten married the day before. Her new husband was with her, holding her hand. They were going to the airport. They were going to Costa Rica for two weeks. They were moving into a new apartment that Catherine had shown Margaret pictures of, a very nice apartment in a building that was very new, that probably had very good fire codes.

"Thank you for staying," Catherine said. "For being here. For all of it."

Margaret hugged her daughter. She held her for a moment longer than necessary, the way mothers did, the way mothers always did, as if they could keep their children safe through the sheer force of proximity, through the conviction that love could prevent all harm, which was, of course, completely untrue, but which was the only lie that made survival bearable.

"Be happy," Margaret said.

"I will," Catherine said. "I am."

Margaret watched them leave, watched them walk toward the entrance, watched them step out into the light. The door closed behind them, and they were gone. The lobby was quiet again. The staff moved through their routines. The building stood, solid and fireproof and temporary, like all buildings, like all things, holding its shape against time and disaster, holding it for as long as it possibly could.
