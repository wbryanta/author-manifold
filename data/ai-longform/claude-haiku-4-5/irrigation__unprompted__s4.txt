# Irrigation

The morning heat was already fierce when Marcus and David reached the break in the line, though the sun had barely cleared the ridge to the east. Their father lay in a hospital bed forty miles west, which meant the work fell to them as it always did when something needed fixing. The water pressure gauge at the property line had been fluctuating for three days, and Marcus had tracked the problem to a section of pipe that ran beneath the oldest row of apricot trees, the ones their grandfather had planted in the forties.

"Here," Marcus said, kneeling beside the shallow trench where the copper pipe lay exposed to the dry air. A hairline fracture caught the light where the metal had split, a fault so delicate it might have been a trick of the eye except for the dark stain in the soil and the grass that had begun to brown in a deliberate line down the row.

David stood with his hands on his hips, surveying the split with the expression of someone witnessing the conclusion of a foregone conclusion. He was three years younger than Marcus but had long ago claimed the manner of someone who understood the inevitable outcome of any situation before it fully arrived. Their mother said this was wisdom. Marcus thought it was the posture of someone waiting to say "I told you so."

"How bad?" David asked, though he could see the fracture as clearly as Marcus could.

"Bad enough. The line's shot through. We'll need to replace at least thirty feet." Marcus stood and walked the trench with his eyes, counting the span between the joints. "Probably forty."

David exhaled slowly, a breath that contained volumes. "That's what, two days of work? Three?"

"If we're lucky. If we don't run into anything else."

"There's always something else."

Marcus said nothing to this. It was true, and there was no profit in arguing with the truth. He walked back to the truck where they'd loaded the new sections of pipe and the coupling supplies, the wrench set, the socket kit, the threadlocker compound that smelled like industrial solvent and regret. They'd brought everything except the will to do it, and even that they'd scraped together from somewhere deep and nearly depleted.

"We could sell," David said quietly, as though the words had simply slipped out despite his best efforts to contain them.

Marcus was pulling on his work gloves, the heavy canvas ones their father had bought two decades ago and which still held the shape of his hands. "Not this again."

"It's not 'again,' Marcus. It's the same conversation we've been having for two years, and every broken pipe is another argument for why we shouldn't be having it."

"The conversation only happens because you keep starting it."

"Because it's the conversation we need to have." David moved closer, and Marcus could see the fine webbing of sun damage around his eyes, the vertical crease between his brows that had deepened over the past eighteen months. David was thirty-four years old and looked like a man who'd been fighting with gravity for longer than gravity had been fighting back. "Look around, Marcus. Really look. This is failing. All of it."

Marcus did look, though he'd been trying not to. The orchard stretched behind them in its orderly grid, two hundred and twelve acres of trees in various states of compromise with the dry climate and their own neglect. Some of the rows were vigorous still, the fruit forming in dense clusters on healthy branches. Others had begun to thin, gaps appearing where trees had died and they'd simply lacked the money to replace them. The irrigation lines, the primary arteries of the whole operation, were from Marcus's childhood. Some of them were older than that. The packing shed needed a new roof. The sorting equipment had been jury-rigged so many times that it more closely resembled an art installation than agricultural machinery.

"Dad's coming home in a week," Marcus said. "Dr. Chen said—"

"Dr. Chen said Dad can't be lifting anything over ten pounds for six months. Dr. Chen said his heart is working at seventy percent capacity, which is another way of saying we're lucky he's alive. And none of that changes the fact that we can't afford to keep this place running on luck."

"We can't afford to sell it either. The land doesn't cover what we owe on equipment, on the house, on the note for the nursery retrofit."

"We could. The developers from Fresno made an offer. Seventy-two million. Seventy-two million, Marcus. That's enough to pay everything and still—"

"To still do what? Live in some suburb an hour away from anything we know? Work some job where we trade our hours for money that doesn't mean anything?" Marcus could hear his voice rising and he forced it back down, tucked it into the cold place where he kept things he couldn't afford to let out. "Dad didn't break his heart out here so we could sell to developers."

"Dad broke his heart out here despite the fact that he should have sold five years ago, or ten years ago, or whenever it became clear that this was never going to be enough." David's voice remained steady, which was somehow worse than if he'd shouted. "I'm not trying to hurt him, Marcus. I'm trying to keep him alive."

The fracture in the pipe caught the light again as the sun moved higher. It was such a small thing, really—just a moment of metal that had grown tired and given up, let go of its structural integrity the way things did when they were asked to hold together for longer than they were designed to last. The water pressure had found that weakness and exploited it, the way water always did, patient and inevitable.

"Let's just fix it," Marcus said. "Let's fix this one and worry about the rest after Dad gets home."

David looked like he wanted to say something else, but he simply nodded and went to get the socket wrench. They worked in silence for the next hour, unscrewing the bolts that held the section of broken pipe in place, careful not to damage the threads on the joints that would remain. The soil had begun to bake in the heat, and the copper was hot enough to require gloves. Marcus worked one end while David worked the other, and the old rhythm of it—knowing without looking where the other's hands would be, anticipating the movements that needed to happen next—almost made it feel like they were working together rather than working in parallel, each alone with his own thoughts.

When they had the broken section fully removed, Marcus measured out the new pipe against the gap. Two inches short. He measured again to be sure, then walked to the truck and came back with another section.

"I didn't bring enough," he said. "I miscounted."

"We can splice in a section and reduce the coupling."

"Yeah. That'll work."

It would add time, which meant it would add frustration, which meant it would give David more time to circle back to the subject of selling. Marcus cut the new section to length with the hacksaw, working the blade in steady strokes that made his shoulders burn. The copper was soft enough to cut easily, and the action was simple enough to be meditative, a thing he could do while his mind wandered to places that had nothing to do with irrigation lines or his father's failing heart or the spreadsheets he'd been staring at for the past six months, spreadsheets that showed with increasing clarity that they were circling the drain.

"I got a call," David said, watching Marcus work. "From the developers. They want to know if we'll take sixty instead of seventy-two."

Marcus stopped sawing. "When?"

"Yesterday. I didn't mention it because I knew you'd react like this."

"Like what?"

"Like I'm betraying something sacred. Like I'm suggesting we burn the place down."

"You are suggesting we burn it down, just slower and more profitably." Marcus resumed cutting, putting more force behind the blade than was necessary. "You're suggesting we take something that's ours and exchange it for money that we'll have spent in five years, and then what? We'll be out of work, out of purpose, and out of family land."

"The land isn't ours, Marcus. We're leasing it from the bank. We own maybe fifteen percent of it, and that fifteen percent is mortgaged against the equipment, which is obsolete, and the house, which is falling apart. What are you actually protecting by refusing to sell?"

"The principle of it."

"The principle of it is bankruptcy, just slower."

Marcus finished cutting through the copper and set down the hacksaw. The sound of the blade stopping created a sudden silence that felt full of weight. He could hear the wind in the palms at the property edge, the distant growl of the highway that cut through their land. He could hear David's breathing, not labored but not easy either.

"Did you tell them you'd consider it?" Marcus asked quietly.

"I said I'd talk to you. That was stupid, apparently, because talking to you is what makes you think this is something we can talk through. But the truth is, there's nothing to talk through, Marcus. There's only the numbers, and the numbers don't lie."

"The numbers are just information. They're not wisdom."

"No, but they're the information that will determine whether we're here in five years or gone. They're the information that will determine whether Dad spends his declining years watching his life's work disappear one foreclosure at a time, or whether he spends it somewhere safe with money in the bank."

Marcus worked the new sections of pipe into place, connecting them with the reducers and coupling hardware. He applied the threadlocker compound with a small brush, the smell of it sharp and chemical. The physics of the thing was simple: two pieces of metal, properly threaded and sealed, could hold back whatever pressure the water wanted to exert. As long as the seal was good, nothing would leak. As long as the metal didn't fracture under stress.

"I'll call them," Marcus said finally. "I'll get their actual offer in writing. But if we're going to make this decision, we make it together. And we run the numbers again first—the real numbers, the ones that include what we could actually make if we brought in the Valencia contract, if we upgraded the equipment to the point where we weren't wasting half our water to evaporation."

"We can't afford to bring in the Valencia contract."

"We can if we sell the eastern forty."

David was quiet for a moment. "That's where the new graft experiment is."

"It's also where three of the eight existing wells are, which we could sell as a package. That water rights alone is worth eight hundred thousand, maybe a million if we play it right. We use that to capitalize the equipment upgrades. The new equipment cuts our water loss by thirty percent. Suddenly we have the margin to take the Valencia contract. Five years later, we're stable. We're profitable. And if at that point you still want out, at least we'll be selling something that's actually solvent."

He could see David working through it, the mathematics of it playing out behind his eyes. It was possible. Marcus had run these numbers enough times to know they weren't fantasy. It was possible, but only if they both believed in it, only if they both worked toward it, only if they accepted a few more years of failure and uncertainty and the kind of exhaustion that came from building something that might never be built.

"That's still five years," David said quietly. "If the offer stays at sixty, with the eastern forty sold off, we're looking at maybe forty-five million net after everything's settled. We could retire. We could actually stop."

"We could also keep going."

"Why would you want to?"

It was such an honest question that Marcus had to take a moment to answer it. He stood up from the trench and looked out over the orchard, really looked the way David had asked him to. The morning heat was fierce but the trees would handle it. They were built for this, bred for this over generations. They would produce their fruit and then next season they would produce again, and the season after that, for decades if tended properly. The work was hard and the margins were thin and the future was always precarious, but it was something. It was tangible in a way that a bank account would never be.

"Because it's ours," he said finally. "Because Dad built this and Grandfather built this before him. Because it's the one thing in my life that means something that isn't just economic value."

"It means something economically too, though. That's the whole point."

"The point is that if you reduce everything to what it's worth, you end up with nothing worth anything."

David didn't argue further, which might have been the kindest thing he could have done. Instead, he helped Marcus finish connecting the new sections, checking the seals, applying the threadlocker to each joint. They worked together without speaking, the way they had learned to work in their early twenties, before the weight of the place had started pressing down on them, before the slow accumulation of difficulty had begun to build walls between them.

By noon, the pipe was sealed and ready to pressurize. Marcus went back to the main line and opened the valve slowly, watching the pressure gauge climb. No leaks appeared in the new section. No water wept from the joints. The repair held.

They sat in the shade of one of the old trees for lunch, eating the sandwiches their mother had made before they left, drinking water from plastic bottles that sweated in the heat. The apricots were just beginning to size up, small green orbs that would fill with sugar and color over the next month. A good fruit year, if the water held. A good year if everything went right.

"Call them," David said eventually. "Get the offer in writing. But Marcus—make sure you're doing this for the right reasons. Not because you're afraid to sell, but because you actually believe we can make it work."

"I do believe it."

"Make sure that's not just what you're telling yourself."

Marcus didn't answer immediately. He could see the horizon, the way the heat made it shimmer and distort, the way it made the far edge of the world look like it might not exist at all. "I'll be honest," he said finally. "I'm afraid to sell. I'm afraid of what comes after, what we become if we're not this anymore. But I'm also afraid of staying. I'm afraid of pouring five more years into something that might still collapse. So yeah, I'm afraid. But the alternative to being afraid is giving up, and I'm not ready to do that yet."

"Okay," David said. "Okay. Let's do it your way for now. Let's see if we can make the numbers work."

They finished eating in silence, listening to the wind in the palms, the distant sound of the highway, the drip and hiss of the irrigation system beginning to do its work again, carrying water out to the trees, sustaining them for another day. Marcus thought about his father, lying in a hospital bed in Visalia, his heart rebuilt with wire and medicine, his mind probably circling the same questions that Marcus and David had been circling. Whether to hold on or let go. Whether the struggle was worth it, or whether surrender might be the wiser choice.

He didn't know the answer, not yet. But as he stood and brushed the dust from his jeans, preparing to move to the next section of line that likely needed attention, he felt something close to resolve settle into him. They would try. They would fix what could be fixed and measure what couldn't. They would see if the numbers could be made to work in their favor. And if they couldn't, if it turned out that holding on was just another form of drowning, then at least they would know they had tried. At least they would be able to tell their father that they'd fought for it, that they'd believed in it enough to spend the summer doing this work, this endless, necessary, never-quite-complete work of keeping something alive.

"Come on," he said to David, offering a hand to pull him up. "There's probably another break somewhere down the line. There's always something else."

David took his hand and let himself be pulled to his feet. Together, they walked back to the truck to gather their tools, two brothers moving through the heat of a June afternoon, away from the shade of the oldest trees, toward whatever needed fixing next.
