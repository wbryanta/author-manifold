# 

The maple dresser goes first, the one with the mirror that made her look taller when she was nine. Margaret watches the two men from Marty's Estate Sales maneuver it through the doorway, and she has to step back into the living room to give them space. The mirror catches the afternoon light as they angle it, throwing a bright flash across her face. She closes her eyes against it.

This was not supposed to happen on a Tuesday. Her mother died on a Thursday—a clean, definable thing—and Margaret took a week off work, which was already running short, and she should have postponed the sale, should have taken more time. But her mother's insurance and the property taxes and the mortgage that still, impossibly, had six years remaining, all of these things had other ideas. The estate sale company had the date locked in since March. To reschedule would cost money they didn't have.

So here she is, watching her mother's furniture leave, which is what you do when you're the executor of an estate and you have a sister in Portland who sent a very nice card and said she couldn't possibly take time off right now, and parents in assisted living in Toledo who can't manage the emotional weight of cataloging the detritus of their daughter's life. That leaves Margaret, who learned long ago that she was the daughter who managed things.

"The desk?" One of the men—the younger one, maybe thirty—has come back inside.

"That stays," Margaret says. It comes out too quickly, too firm. "I'm sorry. Yes, that one stays. There's a tag on the sofa in the den, that's being sold. The desk in here stays."

She realizes she's been standing in front of the desk, shielding it with her body like it might run away. She steps aside, embarrassed, though the men have already turned their attention to the task list on the clipboard. They've probably seen stranger behavior in the past three hours than Margaret could manage in an afternoon.

The house smells like her mother—lavender sachets, old books, the particular funk of aged wood and closed windows. Margaret has opened all the curtains, let in what passes for light on an October day in Marietta, but the smell persists. She wonders if she'll ever be able to close these curtains again without seeing her mother's hands doing it, without hearing her mother's voice commenting on the weather, or the neighbor's new car, or whatever minor observation had occurred to her as evening fell.

Margaret is fifty-four. Her mother was eighty-one. By any reasonable measure, this was not premature. And yet the house still feels like a place where her mother was moments ago, where she might emerge from the kitchen at any moment with a dish towel over her shoulder, surprised to find all these strangers in the living room.

The sofa from the den goes next. This one Margaret remembers well—the family sat on this sofa to watch television in the evenings during the winter of 1987, when her father's brother was dying of something no one in the family discussed directly, and they all pretended to be interested in whatever was on PBS. Margaret sat at one end, her mother at the other, and her sister—young Karen, who was still Karen then and not yet the yoga instructor named Kaia in Portland—sat between them, reading magazines and occasionally laughing at things that were not funny, out of a desperate attempt to fill the silence.

They'd all been waiting for a phone call that came eventually on a Tuesday morning, and by then they'd moved on to other things, other worries, other furniture. The sofa had simply absorbed that winter and stored it in its fibers, and Margaret had forgotten about it entirely until she saw it just now, with the red tag attached to the leg.

"Do you want to sit, ma'am?" The younger man again. He's looking at her with the particular kindness people reserve for the bereaved, and Margaret realizes she's been swaying slightly, holding onto the doorframe.

"I'm fine," she says. "Just warm."

It's fifty-three degrees outside. She's wearing a cardigan over a thin blouse. But they don't contradict her.

The Hummel figurines that her mother had collected—or that had been given to her as gifts and collected by default—are already gone. Margaret had carefully packed them into boxes the night before, and the sale company had taken them to be auctioned off for a combined estimated value of $180-220. Her mother had loved those figurines. She dusted them every other Saturday, and she had opinions about which ones were valuable and which ones were merely charming. Margaret hadn't been able to watch them being loaded into the van.

The piano, though. The piano stays. It's a Steinway, a beautiful upright in dark walnut that her mother had bought in 1978 with money she'd saved from teaching piano lessons. Margaret can still see her mother's hands on the keys, playing Chopin—always Chopin—in the evenings. Margaret had taken lessons for exactly two years, from age eight to ten, and had been uniformly terrible at it. Her mother had never pushed, which somehow made it worse. The piano was simply always there, in the corner of the living room, representing the life her mother might have had if she hadn't married Margaret's father, if she hadn't had children, if she hadn't stayed in Ohio.

Margaret's mother had been, in her own quiet way, a disappointed woman. It was the kind of disappointment that didn't announce itself. It simply sat down at dinner every night and ate what was served and asked polite questions about school and work. It played Chopin on Tuesday evenings. It watered the plants and mended the clothes and wrapped Christmas presents with perfect corners. It was the disappointment of a woman who had understood, at some point in her thirties, that the life she would have was the life she had, and it would have to be enough.

Margaret had spent her adult years making sure not to replicate this scenario. She had married a man she actively liked. She had pursued a career in medical administration that she found cognitively engaging, if not emotionally fulfilling. She had no children. She told herself she was being intentional about this, that she was making a choice rather than accepting a default. Whether or not any of that was true remained an open question.

The bookshelf from the hallway is coming down now. Marty, the owner of the company—a woman in her sixties with efficient hands and the expression of someone who has learned not to develop attachments to objects—is carefully removing each book and placing it in a box labeled "literature" for auction. Margaret watches her mother's reading life being dismantled: the complete Agatha Christies, the Reader's Digest Condensed Books from the seventies, the volumes of poetry that had been birthday gifts from Margaret's father, with his handwriting in the front covers. "For Margaret (the older one)," he'd written, the year Margaret turned thirty. Even then, she was being disambiguated from her sister by her relative age.

"You're not supposed to be sad," Karen had said on the phone, the day after the funeral. "We're supposed to be relieved. She was in pain. She wanted to go."

"I know," Margaret had said.

"So why do you sound like that?"

Margaret hadn't answered because she didn't have an answer, or because the answer was too complicated to explain to her sister in Portland, who had built a new life in a new city and had become someone their mother didn't quite recognize during the annual Christmas visits. She'd gotten a tattoo on her ankle. She'd bought a dog. She'd changed her name to something she liked better. She was living, in other words, a different script entirely.

By two o'clock, the main rooms are mostly empty. The dining room chairs are gone, leaving just the table—a solid oak piece that no one had wanted—and the china cabinet, which had also not sold. Margaret's mother's dishes are still inside: the good china, the everyday china, the travel mugs, the water glasses, the coffee cups. No one wants dishes anymore. They want things that are light, portable, disposable.

Margaret sits at the bare dining table and eats the sandwich she brought. It tastes like cardboard and sadness. The light is coming through the windows at an angle now, and she can see the dust particles that have been disturbed by all the movement. The house is becoming a series of empty rooms. Soon it will belong to someone else, someone who won't know that Margaret learned to ride a bicycle on the front sidewalk, or that she and her sister had a fort in the back bedroom that their mother pretended not to notice, or that their father died in the master bedroom twelve years ago, suddenly, of a heart attack, on an ordinary morning in March.

The piano tuner is coming next week. That's the one thing Margaret has arranged separately. The piano is going to be kept, shipped to her apartment in Columbus where it will absolutely not fit, where she doesn't play, where it will probably fall out of tune within months. She'd paid for the tuning from her own account, supplementing what little money remains after the funeral expenses and back taxes and everything else. It was a stupid decision. It was the only decision she could have made.

At 3:47 p.m., Marty comes to find her.

"We're mostly done," she says. She's holding a wooden box, carved with an intricate pattern. "We found this in the bedroom closet, in the back. There's no tag on it. Should we take it or leave it?"

Margaret takes the box. It's surprisingly heavy. She opens it, and her chest does a strange thing, a catching and dropping simultaneously.

Inside are letters. Dozens of them, maybe a hundred. They're old, the envelopes yellowed, the postmarks from the sixties and seventies. They're addressed to her mother, and they're from someone named Paul.

"I'll keep it," Margaret says. Her voice sounds strange to her, distant. "That one definitely stays."

"Okay." Marty doesn't ask questions. She's used to this, probably. People discovering things about their deceased relatives that rewrite the narrative slightly, or drastically. She simply nods and heads back out to the truck.

Margaret sits with the box in her lap. She doesn't open any of the letters. She knows without reading them what they are: the shape of a different life, the one her mother might have had. She can feel it in the weight of them, in the careful way they've been preserved. Paul, whoever he was, is a ghost in this house as much as Margaret's mother now is. He's a man who wrote letters that were never meant to be discovered, and a woman who kept them, and a daughter who was never supposed to know that her mother's disappointment might have had a name.

By 4:30, the house is empty. The piano remains. The desk remains. The box of letters sits on the dining table, where Margaret has positioned it in the slanting afternoon light. The estate sale company will come back tomorrow to pick up the few pieces that didn't sell—the china cabinet, the oak table, a bookcase. By the end of the week, the house will be clean, cleaned by a company her mother used to pay for, and it will be ready to show to potential buyers.

Margaret walks through the rooms. Her childhood lived in these walls, and her adolescence, and parts of her adulthood that visited on weekends and holidays. Her mother lived here for forty-three years. She died here, in her sleep, in the master bedroom, which now looks smaller without the bed, without the dresser, without the accumulation of her life arranged around her.

The living room is so big now, so empty. The space where the sofa was shows a slightly lighter square of carpet. The space where the bookshelf was is just a blank wall. The piano sits alone in the corner, dark and mute, waiting to be moved. It won't produce any sound for a week, until the tuner comes. Right now it's just an object, a thing, a piece of furniture that has lost its meaning.

Margaret sits on the bench and places her fingers on the keys without pressing down. She doesn't remember enough Chopin to play anything, and even if she did, she wouldn't. The silence feels important. The absence feels appropriate. This is what remains when everything else is taken away—the space between what was and what might have been, and the knowledge that you will never know enough to bridge that distance.

Outside, the sun is dropping toward the horizon. October in Ohio is turning toward November. Winter is coming. The house will sell, or it won't, but either way it will be someone else's house now, someone else's repository for their own small disappointments and quiet compromises. The piano will go to Columbus, where it will sit in Margaret's apartment and slowly fall out of tune. The letters will be read, eventually, probably not tonight, but soon, and they will change nothing and change everything, the way revelations do.

Margaret stands and walks to the front window. The For Sale sign is already in the yard. A realtor sign, which will come down once a buyer is found. In this neighborhood, in this town, houses sell slowly. There's no hurry. Time moves differently here, or maybe it doesn't move at all. Maybe it just sits, like the piano, like the letters, like Margaret herself, waiting for something to require it to continue.

She locks the door at 5:15 and walks to her car. The afternoon is almost done. The sun is almost gone. Tomorrow she'll drive back to Columbus, back to her job, back to her apartment with the piano that's coming to it, back to the life she's constructed out of the pieces that were left to her after she decided not to choose any of the things her mother had chosen. Whether this was wisdom or cowardice, she still doesn't know.

The car is cold when she gets in. She sits for a moment before starting the engine, listening to the silence. Somewhere in Ohio, Paul is dead or alive, probably dead. His letters are in a box in an empty house. Her mother is also dead, and whatever she felt about those letters, whatever she wanted to say and didn't, will remain locked in the place she died, the place that is no longer hers, the place that will soon belong to someone else entirely.

Margaret starts the car and drives away from the house, toward the highway, toward Columbus, toward whatever comes next. The sun sets behind her. The autumn darkness gathers. And the piano, in the empty living room, waits silently for its new life to begin.
