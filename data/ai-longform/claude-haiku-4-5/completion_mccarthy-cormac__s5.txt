They beat him senseless and left him for dead there in that starless dark and when he woke again it was to the grey light of morning and the sound of the river. He lay on his back among the stones with blood dried on his face and his ribs aching with a dull and terrible ache. He did not move for a long time. The sky above was colorless and the wind carried the smell of rain. He turned his head slowly and saw that he was alone on the bar of pale stone at the river's edge, the water running cold and dark before him like something from another world entirely.

He rose eventually, gripping at the stones to pull himself up. His left arm would not work properly. He sat again and looked at it without expression and then he got to his feet once more and stood there swaying, watching the river pass. The morning was still and cold and the birds were not singing. He did not know where he was or how he had come to be there. The dream still clung to him like a second skin but it was receding now into that darkness from which all dreams emerge and he found himself grateful for it. He turned slowly and looked up the riverbank and then down and he chose a direction as a man might choose between two equally dark roads and began to walk.

The stone gave way to muddy earth and then to a kind of scrub growth, low and twisted and ancient-looking in their dwarfed forms. He moved slowly and with the careful deliberation of the newly injured. His shirt was torn and stained and his boots were gone. The stones of the riverbed had cut his feet and he left small dark prints in the dust behind him as he walked. He did not look back.

By midday the pain had become something almost abstract, a constant white noise beneath his consciousness that he had learned to live with in much the same way that the river learned to live with the stones that lay in its path. He sat beside the water and drank from his cupped hands and the water was cold and tasted of earth and minerals. Downstream he could see where the river bent around a wooded point and disappeared. The sky above was still grey and the light had a strange metallic quality to it as though the sun were shining through some vast and corroded lens.

He rose and continued on. The scrubland was growing thicker now and he had to push through it sometimes, feeling the branches catch at his clothes and his torn skin. He did not curse or complain. He simply pushed through it the way a beast might push through, not with any particular effort but as the simple working of muscle and bone in response to the will to move forward.

As the afternoon waned he came upon the remains of what had once been a settlement. The buildings were made of adobe and had largely collapsed into their own dust. Vigas protruded from the crumbling walls like the ribs of some half-buried animal. He stood in what had once been a street and looked at the dead place. A wind moved through the ruins and made a hollow sound. He could see where someone had scratched lines into one of the walls, perhaps tallying days or marking time or simply the restless work of boredom in a place without purpose. The lines made no sense. They could have meant anything.

He moved through the settlement slowly, examining the ruins without any particular curiosity, as though he were cataloging items in some vast and terrible archive. In what had once been a room he found the skeleton of a child curled on the packed earth of the floor. He did not disturb it. He looked at it for a moment and then continued on.

On the other side of the settlement the ground became more broken and he could see where the river had changed course over long years, cutting deeper into the bedrock. There was a trail of sorts that ran along the high ground above the water and he took it, moving now with the sun at his back and the orange light of approaching evening burning the sky. Overhead the birds had begun to move, pushing through the air in great scattered formations as though fleeing from something unseen. He watched them go with an indifference that was almost complete.

He came to a place where the river widened and became shallow and rocky and here he saw the tracks of horses and the remains of a fire that was perhaps a week old. He squatted beside the fire ring and examined the ashes. There were bones there too, the bones of something that had been cooked and eaten. He could not determine what kind of animal. He rose and moved on.

It was full dark when he reached the next settlement and this one was not dead. Light burned in windows and he could hear the sound of voices and the thin cry of an infant. He stopped at the edge of the buildings and looked at the warm squares of light and felt something cold move in his chest. He did not know what to do. He had not thought about what it would mean to encounter people again. He stood there for a long time while the voices continued inside and the light spilled out onto the dust.

Eventually he approached. He moved slowly and with his hands visible and he came to the nearest door and knocked. The voices inside ceased abruptly. He heard footsteps and then the door swung open and a man stood there holding a rifle. The man was middle-aged and weathered and his eyes were flat and empty of all mercy.

What do you want? the man said.

He looked down at himself and saw that he was a creature of blood and dust and torn cloth and he understood what the man was seeing. He looked up again into the flat eyes.

I've been injured, he said. I'm looking for shelter.

The man said nothing. He simply stood there with the rifle held loosely in his hands and his eyes fixed on the stranger's face.

There's nothing here for the likes of you, the man said finally.

I don't want anything, he said. I just need to rest.

You need to keep moving, the man said. There's nothing here.

He could hear other people moving in the darkness behind the man. Women perhaps. Children certainly.

All right, he said.

He turned and walked away. He did not look back. He moved past the other buildings with their warm light and he continued on into the darkness beyond until the settlement was behind him and there was nothing but the sound of the river and the wind in the sparse trees. He did not feel anything about being turned away. It seemed to him now that the world was a place of simple and irreducible facts and that feeling was something that belonged to other people, people who lived in the light and had not been beaten down into the starless dark where all the rules changed.

He walked through the night. The moon rose eventually, nearly full, and it cast a silver light across the landscape that was somehow worse than darkness because it illuminated things—the bones of dead animals, the twisted forms of ancient trees, the empty eye sockets of a skull half-buried in sand—that might have been better left obscured. He walked steadily and the moon tracked him across the desert and he did not rest until the first grey light of morning showed the landscape in its true colors again.

He found water at a spring that bubbled up from the rocks and he drank deeply and washed the blood from his face and hands. The water was ice cold and hurt his teeth. His reflection stared up at him from the still surface of the pool and he did not recognize himself. The face was something from a nightmare, bruised and swollen and strange. He turned away from it and sat on the rocks and waited for the sun to rise fully.

By the time it did he was asleep. He woke to the heat of noon pressing down on him like a physical thing. His mouth was dry and he went back to the spring and drank again. He could feel the fever taking hold now, a kind of distant heat working its way through his body from some deep interior place. He sat in the shade of the rocks and tried to think but his thoughts would not cohere. They scattered like birds before a gun and he could not catch them and hold them long enough to make sense of them.

He slept through the afternoon in a fitful way, plagued by dreams that were not quite dreams but rather fragments of memory that had broken loose and reformed in strange configurations. He saw the woman who had shaken him awake but her face kept changing and becoming the faces of other women he had known or imagined. He saw the river flowing backward. He saw the sun crumble to ash in the sky. He saw his own hands move in the darkness like the hands of a stranger.

When evening came he rose again and continued walking. The fever was stronger now and his vision had taken on a strange prismatic quality so that everything seemed to shimmer and separate into its constituent parts. The rocks became geometrical. The plants became mathematical equations. The river was a music that he could almost hear but not quite.

He did not know how long he walked in this state. It might have been hours or it might have been days. Time had become unstable, a thing that could expand or contract at will. Once he came upon a road, a real road with wagon ruts and horse droppings, and he followed it for a distance before leaving it again for reasons he could not articulate. Once he saw a figure on horseback in the distance and he hid himself in the brush until the rider had passed, though he did not know why he hid or what he was hiding from.

The landscape was changing gradually, becoming more broken and rocky, with higher ground visible in the distance and the river cutting deeper into a canyon. The vegetation was becoming sparser and more desperate, the plants twisted and thorny and hostile. He passed through areas that seemed almost lunar in their barrenness, places where nothing grew and the rocks were exposed and raw.

It was in one of these places that he encountered the man. He came around a bend in the river and saw him sitting on a rock, a figure in drab canvas with a wide-brimmed hat and a beard so matted and tangled that it looked almost like an external organ. The man was fishing, his line stretching out into the water, and he did not look up as the stranger approached.

He stopped at a distance and waited to see if the man would acknowledge him. The man continued to fish. The sun was setting and painted everything in shades of red and orange.

You been following the river? the man said finally. His voice was rough and scraped, as though he did not use it often.

Yes, he said.

It's a long river. Takes you a lot of places if you follow it long enough. The man reeled in his line slowly. He had not caught anything. He cast again. It leads to a town eventually. Town called Nacimiento. They got a doctor there. Fellow named Sawyer. He's drunk half the time but he's better than nothing if you're hurt bad.

The man looked at him for the first time and his eyes were a pale blue and seemed to contain an infinite sadness.

You're hurt bad, the man said. I can see that.

I'm all right, he said.

No you're not. You're burning up with fever and you got the look of a man that's been to places he didn't want to go. The man turned back to his fishing. You follow the river from here, you'll reach Nacimiento in two days if you can walk that far. Maybe three. You'll know it when you see it. It's a sad little place but it's got walls and a roof and people who won't kill you for sport.

Why are you telling me this? he asked.

The man shrugged. I don't know. I'm a fool sometimes. Or maybe it's because I figure we're all traveling the same road and helping another traveler don't hurt nothing. The man reeled in again and this time there was a small fish on the line, a thin silver thing that thrashed and tried to escape. The man held it up to the failing light and looked at it with something like pity and then he removed the hook and tossed it back into the water. He watched it sink. There's things alive in this world that's trying their best just like us, he said. Don't seem right to eat them.

He stood there watching the man fish until the light was nearly gone and then he thanked him, though the man did not acknowledge the thanks, and continued down the river. Behind him he could hear the man casting again, the gentle sound of the line hitting the water, and it seemed to him like the most peaceful sound he had heard in a long time.

He did not stop that night or the next day. The fever had him in its grip now and he moved through the landscape in a kind of waking dream, aware that he was walking but not entirely present in his own body. The canyon grew deeper and the walls rose up around him, vast slabs of red and orange rock that seemed to hold the heat of the day long after the sun had set. The river roared and tumbled below him and sometimes he could see the mist rising from its rapids.

He passed other travelers but did not speak to them. An old woman leading a burro laden with firewood. A group of children gathering something from the rocks. A solitary man on horseback who did not look at him. The world had become a tapestry of brief encounters that meant nothing, connections that were made and broken in the space of seconds.

And then on the morning of what he believed was the third day—though it could have been the second or the fourth, he had lost track—he came around a bend and saw the town. It was small and made of adobe and it clung to the river like an animal seeking water, the buildings clustered together in a rough semblance of streets. Smoke rose from chimneys. People moved in the streets. Animals stood tied to posts. It was real in a way that nothing had been real for a long time.

He moved toward it slowly, aware now that he was staggering, that his body was barely obeying his will. His vision was still strange and shimmering but underneath the fever he could see the town clearly enough. He could see figures stopping to watch him approach. He could see the doors of buildings opening. He could feel the weight of their eyes upon him.

At the entrance to the town—if such a place could be said to have an entrance—a man stepped forward. He was younger than most and he carried himself with a kind of authority that came from neither size nor weapons but from something internal, something that told him he belonged here in a way that the stranger did not.

I'm looking for a doctor, he said. A man named Sawyer.

The man looked him over without expression.

You look like death, the man said.

I'm ill, he said.

I can see that. Follow me.

The man led him through the streets and people watched from doorways and windows. A child pointed and an older woman pulled it back inside. A dog barked and was silenced. The town seemed to close around him like a trap, not threatening but merely indifferent to his presence, the way a trap is indifferent to the animal that enters it.

They came to a building that was slightly larger than the others and the man knocked and waited. An old woman answered and spoke to the man in Spanish and then she looked at the stranger and nodded and stepped aside.

Inside it was cool and dim and he could smell medicines and something else, something that might have been death or might have simply been the smell of a place where many people had suffered. The woman pointed to a room and he entered and found a cot and lay down on it without being asked. The fever took him then completely and he descended into darkness like a man descending into a well, the light growing distant above him and then disappearing altogether.
