The harbor was a slow clock that ticked in tides. In winter it kept a particular kind of time, the kind Silas could read in the way the light stayed low and the gulls moved like punctuation marks against a sky that had forgotten how to be blue. He sat most mornings on the worn planks of the western pier, back against the piling his grandfather had nailed into that same rotten wood, boots tucked under him like two small damp islands, breath stealing in pale clouds. Behind him, the cannery—long, boxy, and lit with a permanent lamplight—hissed steam into the cold and smelled of salt and fish oil and the quick, metallic tang of work. The cannery had always been the town’s breath; in the last year it had the laborious wheeze of someone trying to finish a sentence they'd been saying for fifty years.

This was the winter the cannery would close. Everyone knew it the way they knew where the nearest eddy lived: by memory, by the way mouths tightened when they spoke of it. Men stood at the dock like weathered markers and talked in that low voice made for bad news, and women ran hands along the seams of their coats as if patching might keep the building warm for another season. For his part, Silas had been saying goodbye to lines and knots and the bar for a decade. He had let the lighter of his pilot’s license go to moths in the bottom drawer, but the sea kept speaking to him, and his body would not unlearn the language.

Tess came to him on the damp mornings when the world looked like a ledger of grey. She had a roundness to the face that would, over the next years, become angles; she wore her hair in a tangle because there was no point in taming it against the wind. She was his daughter's child, and her mother—Ruth—worked the conveyor lines in the cannery, wrists strong, hair always bound and flecked in the seasons when they cued of fish. The child had been raised on this industry, on canned dinners and cardboard boxes and the distant orchestra of knives and machinery, but where Ruth had learned the cannery's rhythm through repetition, Tess wanted to know the deeper things—the ones that did not hum but existed by a grammar of their own. She wanted to learn to read the water.

"Come here," Silas said the first morning she asked him. He patted the plank beside him, and she perched like a small gull. Her coat smelled of wool and school, and when she leaned in the world tightened to the span between them and the sea.

"What does it say?" she asked.

"Depends," he told her. "You don't just do one thing. You ask. You ask and you watch. The water's not a book you read a page out of; it's a mouth you listen to. It tells you where it came from, where it's going, who's been through it. It tells you what's hiding under it and what will wreck you if you don't listen."

She tilted her head as if she were hearing her first foreign sentence. "Teach me."

He took her hands in his. They were warm, younger, the knuckles unspotted. "First thing," he said. "Color." He pointed with a careful finger toward the channel. "See that line? The water isn’t a single color. It writes in bands. That dark green, that's deep water with kelp. That milky strip where the wakes meet, that's fresh water spilling out from the creeks. When the harbor wears a long ribbon of gunmetal blue and there's a thin silver line at the base of it, that's tide coming in slow and steady. When it's glass—no one thing moving—it's waiting. When the surface breaks like something breathing, it's restless."

She watched the surface, trying to sort the names into the motion. Her eyes tracked the lines of light sliding on the water like the stitches of some enormous garment. "How do you tell apart a wave from a swell?"

"Feel," he said, because sometimes the short answers were the clearest. He lifted his hand and let it hover above the surface without touching. "Waves are wind. Swells are memory. A swell comes from far away storms and washes into the harbor like an old man who still carries the scent of his ship. Wind-driven waves will have edges and teeth, bursts of white. You will know them when they scold you."

She smiled, then tried to make the sound of disapproval she imagined waves making. Silas let her, the sound curdling the morning air into two kinds of laughter.

What he taught her was less a set of rules than a set of practices: how to look where others did not, how to fold attention down to the crease of a current. They learned the poissonier markers—stones like teeth, the way two currents meeting painted a seam of oil on the surface. He showed her the buoys as if they were the town's very own punctuation, each color and number a sentence ending: red cans, green nuns, the big floating bell that tolled to scrub away any notion of calm. He taught her to sense the difference between a tide that would leave a beach propped high and dry and one that would take a rowboat and leave its oars laughing on the sand.

In a small wooden skiff Silas had kept tied to the inner wharf, they went into the harbor on days when frost chipped at their jackets and the engines of the larger boats muttered like animals. The skiff was called Bellamy, a leftover name from a woman who had once given him her carriage horse or something as sentimental and impossible. Silas liked to say the boat answered to the shape of his hands. It bobbed gently, and when he started the little motor in the winter mornings the wake it made trailed off into the bigger patterns of the sea like a child's drawing linking to an older map.

"Close your eyes," he told Tess on one such morning. "Not because I trust you to steer but because I want you to learn to hear where we are."

She scrubbed them shut and listened—the motor's tick, the slap of the hull, the birds complaining ahead near a folding shoal. "I hear the gulls," she said.

"Good. Gulls mean three things. Fish, scavenged things, and mistakes. If they're circling in a tight white ring and diving like knives, there's fish. If they're racing the ferry's wake picking up what people throw, that's town. If there are crows with them, that's inland smell. But listen to the gulls' rhythm. Slow deep cries mean wind from the sea, high staccato calls mean the weather's nervous."

She listened until the gulls blurred into a band of sound and the motor's hum became a background hum for the sea's first language. He pointed, hand small and resigned into the cold. "There," he murmured. "Do you see the blues turning inside out like fabric? That's where currents are undercutting each other. There's a tail of green—herring. The trawlers' nets will be near. See the foam? On the rock there—"

Her body leapt slightly at the sight of the foam; her insides rearranged themselves around learning something that felt dangerous but was merely knowledge.

It wasn't all technical. Silas had the pilot's stories, the ones his job demanded as if ships were confessors and pilots keepers of secrets. Some were small—of captains who had forgotten the way into harbor, of a boy who thought a tide was a toy and learned otherwise. Others were monumentally quiet: the time a bar ran like a hand across his boat and the fog banks took all the world, or the spring when the cannery had run so hot and busy that everyone smelled of steam and fish for a month, and he had walked home after his shift with his pockets full of cigarette butts and the weight of someone else's tired. Those stories were not meant as entertainment; they were bedside manners for a job that took a measure of your spine and borrowed it.

One afternoon they found, in the shallow water behind the cannery, a small handline boat struggling to keep its nose against the wash. It belonged to Marino, a man who had been fishing these waters since before Tess was born. The outboard sputtered and died, and he was a figure hunched against the cold, hat pulled down, fingers in the water near the propeller as if tinkering with some small, mechanical wound. Silas and Tess pulled alongside. Marino waved them off with a hand that told nobody to come.

"No, thank you," Marino rasped.

"Let the old man have a rest then," Silas said, and slid an arm over the gunwale, lifting the boat free of the kelp that had stuck like hair. He had the way of doing old favors—the kind that were both prideful and necessary. Tess watched his hands, the way knuckles flexed, thumbs like the tip of a clock. She felt a strange intimacy with the boat, as if in that act the harbor had allowed them a private answer to a long-asked question.

"How do you know when a line's about to part?" she asked while Marino cursed at his motor and scrubbed his hands on his trousers.

"You feel it in your teeth first, then in your legs," Silas said. "Your body learns to predict what the sea will do. You can't hurry that. You earn it by noticing. Always notice. And when you stop noticing, you lose a little more of yourself to the water."

He taught her how to look at foam lines to read where back-currents lay, how the water near the docks would turn a particular hue when the steam condensate washed through and mixed with the tannins from the river. He had charts of the harbor that had been annotated in the careful slanted script of a man who had long ago turned his handwriting into an instrument as precise as a sextant: little arrows in red showing the way the ebb latched onto a shoal, a cross near a submerged rock, a note in pencil that read DOCK TRESPASS—WATCH TIDE if you came at low slack.

On those charts he had marked stations—places where a pilot watched the water and, in the passing, learned a life's worth of small evils and favors. One such place was the breakwall, where the tide danced a complicated square with itself, and the water came and left like a heartbeat. Another was the bar at the harbor mouth, a ridge of sand that moved like a child rearranging a toy collection and could claim a boat in the span of a confused afternoon. When he had still been young enough to be needed on the ships, he had run pilothouses with a surety that made others rely on him. He guided oil tankers with the same tenderness he used to pick her up when she’d skinned her knee on the wharf.

Silas liked teaching her the practical magic that was still possible in a port so small the big maps had the same curvature as small lives. He told her about the way light fell on a hull when a storm had shifted; he taught her to read the smell of the air, because sometimes the water betrayed itself in smell—an estuary's sweetness, the sour drag of rotting kelp, the iron-bite that meant something had leaked. "Nose-reading," he called it, and she laughed like a bird that had found a new kind of flight.

The town watched them with something like approval. People like to see a tradition that looks like it might be handed on; it gives them the comforting idea that at least in one small corner something endures when everything else is being boxed and shipped to some farther shore. Ruth came with her own opinions, sometimes scolding about Tess's coat or whether she had remembered to pack a thermos. She smelled filtered by work—cordite and steam and the detachment of having to count each can entering the system, coupling the day's worth of life into neat stacks. She did not quite trust the romance of the harbor; she'd learned, early, how easily romance could rust like nets left in the weather. But she also appreciated Silas's hands around her child, the slow patience of a man who had forever been on watch.

"She can learn more than lines," Ruth told him once, stepping out of the cannery in a swirl of steam. The winter had a way of making voices small and urgent. "She needs to know how to make money off a skill if she's going to stay."

"She doesn't have to stay," Silas said. He watched his granddaughter braid kelp with a stick and pretend the strip was a fishing line. "That's the thing. The sea teaches you how to leave. It teaches you timing. If she wants to go she'll know when. If she wants to come back, she'll know when."

Ruth's hands folded into each other the way work hands do. "She could do worse than know when to leave."

The cannery's closure made their lessons smell like a child's bedtime story told while a house was being packed. The last winter's work was frantic and careful in the same breath. Men tightened belts; women traded shifts. The town seemed to be doing everything at once: laying off workers, hiring part-time, asking for loans, and, in corners where the light did not reach, beginning to whisper of packing cars and driving inland to jobs that paid nothing like the cannery but paid in steadier slices. Barges came and took away last loads; the noise of canning that used to be the town's heartbeat became patchy, like a radio with the dial half-turned.

There were small symbols of grief. The sign in front of the cannery read, in a hand that once had the same steadiness of a man used to steering, LAST CATCHES — SUPPORT OUR WORKERS. The town paper posted a headline about a meeting at the firehouse that read like a list of absences: "Union Fails to Reach Deal." Neighbors brought casseroles to one another as if adding food to the pantry would somehow anchor the building's heartbeat. Silas listened to the talk and read the water, which now made visible what the town's human speech could not: the harbor would be less busy. Nets would be rarer. The boats that still came in seemed solitary for the first time, like someone visiting an old neighbor.

There was a day when the weather smelled of a storm long gone, the sort that looked lovely only from a safe distance. The sky lowered until it was the color of a sheet of lead and the cannery's lights shone like a promise tied to a dying balloon. A rower, young and new to the town, pulled a boat too close to the bar as if the ocean had been misread. A line of breakers sheared there, not thin, not polite. Silas saw the pattern. The water had turned a particular shape—a crescent of churn where two currents met and decided to twist—and he didn't need to think, only to act.

"Tess," he said. "Look."

She was with him on the pier, hair banded, cheeks flushed by the cold. She followed his eye and saw the breaks, the way the water folded itself as if looking for pockets to collect. Her small hands tightened on the rail.

"That boat's too close," she said. It was a sentence he'd said a thousand times, but now it left her lips.

"Call out," Silas told her.

She did, because the job of being taught is often to be brave in public. Her voice climbed up the air, raw and precise. She warned the young rower whose head snapped like a startled bird. He looked around, then, because the town was still small and the sound of any one person could be heard across a bay when the world had decided to listen. He turned the blade of the oar as if he had never been taught where the line of safety lay, and the boat angled away from the bar.

The breakers took him for a breath, and then passed by. There were consequences possible that day that didn't happen, and the saving of a thing—human, or a boat, or a moment—felt to Silas like a communion. The town's habits were to aggregate small, discrete helpings into an idea of community. Sometimes the help was only a shouted warning across cold water, and it was enough.

After, they walked along the cannery's dock and watched the last of the catch come ashore. Men who had worked there for forty years moved like ghosts in the lamplight, their shoulders stooped as if stacked with canned food. They cheered thinly for the boats, but the cheer was a practiced measure. Tess had a thermos of cocoa slung in the crook of her arm; she fed a gull a bit of stale bread and watched it float down like a small white paper. The cannery exhaled steam into the night like a tired engine, and somewhere inside the building an old record player cracked from the same needle—someone had brought a radio in to keep the workers company. There was a bandage place on Silas's wrist, a white square of plaster where he'd pricked himself the day before while tightening the rope to the dinghy. He had the feeling, sometimes, of being a living artifact in a museum of things that used to be alive.

On quieter afternoons he let Tess sit with his logbook. The book was the size of a Bible and thick with entries—sketches of harbor features, short notes on wind direction, times when things had gone wrong. The handwriting changed in ways he could see: the early pages bold and deliberate, later ones smaller and more cramped, like someone writing in a storm. He handed it to her and watched the way her small fingers traced the pencil marks. "This is what I kept," he said. "Not for anyone else but me. A man always writes to steady himself. You're taking notes now. You'll make your own."

She did. She wrote the kind of things a child notices—how the light slants in from the east like a knife and makes the water look like cut glass, how the gulls line themselves like white pebbles on an invisible string, how the cannery's smell changed when the tide turned. Her notes were sometimes in the margins, sometimes on the back of a receipt, sometimes in her head. Often she would copy one of his notes in a miniature hand and try to make the letters look like his, as if by matching his handwriting she could catch his life.

There were evenings when Silas would talk to her of the days that had made him want to be a pilot. Childhood was a shoreline, he said—always there, always changing. He had grown into the job because he liked being in the moment heavy with responsibility, the kind that made a person small and careful. He loved the silence a ship makes by keeping its bow from smashing into invisible teeth. He had been on freighters big enough to swallow islands and on little lobster boats that smelled like lemon and tar. He carried in him a particular modesty—that the sea was the teacher and the man must listen.

Sometimes his voice would break on a name: a captain he had known, a ship that had taken on water and left him with the taste of salt and diesel for a week. The sea had given him these memories as a gift and a bill. He had lost a friend to the bar once, a young man who had misread the tide, the sort of accident that lived in a town like a candle kept on a mantle—always burning a little less bright with each telling. He told that story often enough that Tess had it memorized and could close her eyes and see the night he described: ropes screaming under tension, the bar like an animal testing jaws, the boat’s bulbous nose inching until it found the wrong sand.

"I keep thinking sometimes," he said one day, rubbing a knuckle against the wood so hard the skin reddened, "that the only thing that makes the sea fair is attention. You give it attention and it gives you back your shape."

Tess nodded, and there was a resolution in her face that comforted him. Young people often had that. Their faith in things being amendable was cheap in the best way—affordable and bright.

In late January the cannery held its last all-hands meeting. The foreman read the same lines they'd heard in town meetings, the numbers like cold mathematics. Men and women who had put their lives to the rhythm of the cannery wept in places their hands would not quite reach. There was a slow, private exodus of tools: a knife in a pocket, a bench left in a corner, an apron hung like the coat of a soldier who would not, perhaps, march again. At the end of the meeting, the workers formed a ring outside and sang a song—something old and gaunt, sung in rough voices bound together by work. Silas and Tess listened from the pier.

"It's like watching a ship go," Silas said. "You can see it sail, but the way it leaves water behind feels like peeling a skin off the world. The town will have to remember how to breathe again."

"Will it?" Tess asked. "Will it forget how?"

"No," he said. "But it will learn a new way to be small and brave. Towns are like fishermen: they change their nets when the fish change."

She wrote that down, the metaphor a tiny banner in her log. It pleased her to attach similes to the world and make sense with them. Sometimes their conversation was in metaphors, as if the sea required them to speak not directly but in language washed raw by salt.

On the last night of the cannery, the town made a thing of it. They loaded the final pallets like making a bed for someone who had gone away—careful, not wanting to wrinkle the sheets. They had an after-hours party, modest and warm, smelling of fried fish and beer and the lap of people trying to do ritual. Silas and Tess drifted through the crowd. People shook hands, hugged, left a palm on someone's arm that tightened and then let go. There was music, tinny and cheap and very welcome.

When the lights went out in the cannery that night, they did not go out with the clang of a final factory whistle. They dimmed in stages, as if the building were too tired to die all at once. The big doors closed, and the last of the forklifts rumbled out into the cold like beetles retreating. Then there was a silence the town had not known in living memory. It was not empty; it was simply new, a quietness like thin new ice on a pond—possible to walk on but uncertain.

Silas and Tess stood on the pier until early morning, when the sky was the color of the inside of a shell and the water held the last light like a coin. He had given her, impulsively, his pilot's cap that evening. It was soft leather and the band had been smoothed by the shape of his head. He had no need of it now; it would have fit too loosely and left him feeling even older. He set it on her head as if placing a crown on a child. Tess accepted it with the solemnity of a person inheriting something that mattered less for its value than for the intent.

"What do I do with it?" she asked.

"Keep your head warm," he said, and then, because he could not let the moment end without a small ordinance, "and read the water."

"Will you go with me?" she asked.

"I have a chart to mend," he said, half in jest and half in truth. "And sometimes the body doesn't like cold quite as much as it used to. But I'll be near." He reached out and squeezed her hand. The contact was small and sure, linking a past to a future neither of them could fully name.

The next morning, in a harbor that had less purpose lit into it but a peculiar kind of freedom, Tess rowed with Silas in Bellamy one last time before the spring thaw. The water was a mirror most of the way, the sky a high ceiling with the cannery like a collection of boxes under it. They moved slow, like people trying not to leave marks they had to clean up later.

"Do you know why pilots sometimes refuse to take a captain's command?" Silas asked, just to speak. The question floated between them.

"No," she said.

"Because even experienced men can forget their humility. A captain can own a ship and still not know the sea. The pilot's job is to remind you of the ocean's will. You can't own the water. You can only be its guest."

Tess kept her hands on the oars and thought about being a guest. She felt a giddiness like being allowed to cross a border with the wrong passport and not be stopped. She had learned something practical in the last months, but what pleased her most was the moral shape of the thing—the courtesy required to be in the world.

They came upon the bar, and there it was again, a living seam of light and dark and movement. A small skiff approaching from shore misjudged the line and found itself at that invisible junction. The engine coughed. The skiff's father leaned into his oars with the sour face of someone surprised by miscalculation.

Tess's hands tightened. "He's too far in," she said before thinking. Her voice alone did not do it, but she had learned the rhythm of the warning. The skiff corrected.

Later, when they were back on the pier, warm with cocoa and the small, securing heat of being in a building with work moving around them, Silas placed the logbook on Tess's knees. "Keep it," he said. "Put in what you find. Not for me, not for anyone. For you. It doesn't have to be pretty—notes are ugly things. They are the truth."

She opened it and found pages of other people's names, small sketches of currents, handwritten fragments: RED BOUY—WATCH WEST SLIDE; TIDE X: 2.5 FT; LOST: RUFUS, 1973. She slipped a pencil behind her ear like a tool-belt and made a mark: TESS—LEARNS WATER. She felt as if a new muscle had begun to grow in her chest, the sort of muscle you earned by paying attention.

The town adjusted. Some people left to find work in cities with lights too bright, some stayed and learned new trades—roofing, carpentry, things that would make money without the clean smell of fish. The big empty cannery building remained a shell for a while, like a tooth with no root, waiting for someone to pull it up by the jaw. Sometimes kids dared one another to touch the walls at night and claim a story. The town mustered reserve kindnesses: soup kitchens, small markets that sold things on tick. There was a peculiar blooming of lawns, as if people had to fill the domestic time the cannery had once taken up. None of it erased the finality, but it rearranged grief into old furniture.

Silas grew gradually quieter. The sea kept him company, and he let a kind of grammar of retirement settle in him. He would stumble on small things—forget a phrase, misplace his keys—and his laugh had a new softness, like ocean foam. He taught Tess until he could not keep up the long hours, until the cold put a thoughtful ache into his bones. But she had grown heavy with skill; she could look at water and read it as if it were a face she knew. She could weather a tide and translate the restlessness into practical commands. The town found reasons to ask her to help, to reach things, to find things that were being misread.

On an evening when the frost had come abrupt and hard and the cannery was a dark rectangle under stars, Silas and Tess sat together on the pier in the gunmetal sky and watched the harbor settle. He had been quiet for a long time, and she counted the minutes to trace them on the back of her hand.

"You won't always be here," she said, because the thing people think inwardly often must be said aloud to sound like permission.

"No," he answered. "I won't."

"Will you be sad?"

"Sadness is a kind of tide," he said. "It comes in, leaves patterns. Sometimes it takes you with it a little. But mostly it brings things to the shore. You have to look at what it leaves you and decide if it will be useful."

She considered that. The sea had been good to them: it had taught them skills, given them stories, offered work. It had also taken; it had taken hands in times it was violent and neighbors when it was careless. But even in loss there was a ledger. The harbor kept things like a ledger, and he had been a patient accountant.

"Promise me you'll keep teaching people?" she asked.

He smiled. It would be easier, perhaps, to refuse a promise than to make one. "I can promise that I'll come down in the mornings as long as my legs will take me, and if you need an old man to look at water with you, I'll be the man who does it."

"Will you teach me how to leave?" she asked. She was young and full of a very old hunger to run far.

"That's the last and the first lesson," he said. "You read the water not to trap you but to free you. A good pilot reminds you the world is larger than your harbor. You can go if the sea tells you it's the time. You can stay otherwise. Either way, you will know."

She caught his hand, their palms joining like two halves of a map. The harbor in winter was a slowing clock and in its slow beating it had taught them. They had been refined by cold, by absence, by the long smells of fish and machine. They made a covenant of sorts not to be swallowed by the town's grief but to carry on the small practice of reading the water.

When spring came, the cannery's buildings drew lines against the sky empty of the hum they had once known. New uses were suggested in meetings that were part hope and part audition. People came to see Silas and Tess sometimes like they were curiosities, living artifacts of a trade the town felt it had lost. But in small ways the harbor's grammar remained—the current behind the rock, the slip of a color in the slewing, the way gulls argued with one another over the last of a scrap—and the learning went on.

Tess would leave, likely, in a year. She would travel inland to a college with books that had air-conditioning and professors who loved elaborate metaphors and probably too little wind. She liked that thought and feared it. The harbor would shrink, in some ways, and swell in others. She would come back to visit and would, on each return, line her childhood against something new she'd learned in the wider world. The cannery would be a memory and perhaps, if the town got clever, something new altogether.

Silas stayed a little while longer. He began to talk about being as nice to his back as he was to the world. He mended the logbook more carefully, adding notes in a small hand that was starting to tremble. The harbor's lessons had become his bones; he had no other schooling.

The last entry he made in the book he handed to Tess a winter after the cannery closed was not a technical note. It was a line in the margin, a small sentence with an ink blot. She found it and read it aloud—his handwriting a whisper.

"Read the water," she said, and then smiled. "And the rest will show itself."

Outside, beyond the porch light and the dim stretch of the empty wharf, the harbor rolled in to meet the night like a chorus leaning in to speak. The water seemed, in that moment, to be reading them back. It told them not the future but the possibility of weather: a drift of fog to come, perhaps, or a calm that would let a boat lay down. It told them of change as it always had, in small, patient sentences that required no apology.

Silas put his hand over Tess’s on the logbook as if closing a pact. The town, the cannery, the season—they were all in the past now and also in the grain of the world. A small child, when first learning, misreads things for beauty and for danger and then learns to tell the difference. Tess had learned. The harbor would take and return. The last winter the cannery had been open had taught the town that endings were not a single catastrophic thing but a long, layered knowledge.

Tess looked at the water. It was a ledger and a species of friend. She had read it until she could hear the shape of its sentences like a mother’s lullaby. The thing she had learned, finally, was not how to make a living—though that matter would come—but how to be in the world without being consumed by the noise around it.

When Silas died, months later, it was under a different sky, in a house where his hands had the smell of charted paper. The town mourned in the ways small towns mourn: they told stories of him in the grocery store and on old benches; they put flowers at the pier; they had a small ceremony on a day that was not gentle. Tess read from the logbook at his funeral, not a technical note but one of his stories—about a fog and a man and a simple act of attention that saved a life. It felt, in that hour, like a prayer.

Afterwards, the harbor did what harbors do. The water kept moving, indifferent and precise. It turned its surfaces into a thousand different languages and, to those who would learn, remained generous. Tess would continue to be a student of the tides. She would, when necessary, leave the town and return, and each time she would recognize the difference. People would forget and remember in cycles; buildings would be repurposed or demolished; new children would find old men on piers and hear the same call to attention.

On the pier, two generations of attention had been passed on like a coin. The sea had been the bearer. It continued to speak. Tess, cap on her head, logbook in her lap, watched the winter water as if reading a familiar face. When a gust came in and the harbor announced itself in a chorus of sounds, she could hear her grandfather’s voice in the way the gulls cried, and it seemed to her that everything he had not said was there, waiting in the tide.

She lifted the logbook to write a note: the kind of small, honest mark that meant someone had been paying attention. The harbor, cold and clear, accepted the new writing without protest, and as she closed the book she felt the reassuring weight of being in the right place with the right knowledge to meet whatever weather might come next.
