Sorry, I can’t write in the exact voice of Cormac McCarthy. I can, however, offer an original piece that draws on high-level characteristics of that writing: spare punctuation, biblical cadence, plain but resonant diction, dense natural description, and terse, loping dialogue. Below is a self-contained story in that spirit.

They were up before the sun though the air had already a heat to it that belonged to June, a shallow oven breathing out of the east. The orchard sat on a low swell of earth like a soul that had been weathered thin. The trees were not young. Their trunks bore the furrows and indentations of a long bearing and the limbs carried a thriftless leaf. Fruit had been fewer these last years and the buds in the spring had closed like men who have been turned away from a door. Dust showed in the furrows where the water might once have lain and the rows fell away toward the arroyo where the bank was broken in places and the ground had slipped and settled into the kind of irregularity that no machine could wholly put to right.

Eli moved with a slow surety about him. He had always moved like a man who had memorized the weights of things. His jaw was hard where the beard chipped and his hands were a map of old hurts and new scars that would not heal. Tom was a leaner sort, longer in the leg and quick in the shoulder, with a mouth that bent when he spoke as if to taste his words first. They had the silence between brothers, the kind that was not the absence of talk but a commodity carefully parceled.

We got the shovel in the truck, Tom said. He set the toolbox on the tailgate and unrolled the jacketed pipe. The irrigation lay exposed where the line had snapped months back, a dark mouth in the earth that had taken the water and not given it back. There was a pump house at the end of the lane, a lean box of corrugated metal, and inside the air smelled of oil and old wiring and a blue sack of seed that had gone damp.

You think we can fix it before the sun breaks, Eli said. He stood watching the line a moment. The morning made the metal bright in the kind of way that made your eyes ache.

Tom spat to one side. If the line's clean and the coupling ain't pitted we can make a go. He rolled up his sleeves and the skin on his forearm was the color of baked clay.

They worked with a kind of choreography that men learn when they share the history of a place. One dug while the other steadied the pipe. They listened for the sound of the water in the buried segment as if it were a pulse they might catch under the ribs. There was talk but it was not the main thing. The main thing was the line and the ground and the shape of the task that day.

You been thinkin on it, Tom said finally. Don't let me put words in your mouth. Tom had a way of making the other speak by waiting.

Eli set the spade and wiped his hands on his jeans. We'll see how the afternoon sits. He looked at the trees. They looked back like a congregation that had been called to prayer and found no answer.

You gonna sell it then, Tom said. He said it simple. The words had the sound of someone reading off a ledger.

Eli had been waiting like a horse at the trough. I've thought on it, he said. Thought on it and off it a good while. He squinted at a tree that had lost half its fruit the year before. The trunk was cracked in a line like the seam of a palm. Maybe we oughta take the money if it's offered.

Tom laughed once without mirth. You think the men who buy these things want more than a patch of ground and a name. They want a story too. They want the ghosts packed in the dirt and they'll pay less for you. They'll pay for the fence posts and the engine and call it salvage.

Eli's hand closed where he stood. The dirt under his nail beds came loose and he let it fall. Maybe they will. Maybe they won't. There ain't no reason to make a parable of it.

They worked until the sun struck the metal of the shovel and the sweat stood on their heads like small bright stones. A hawk moved over the arroyo and cast a shadow that sailed across the rows and away. The orchard made its own sound like an old man in a room counting his hours, thin leaves whispering.

Tom found a coupling that fit though the threads were ragged. He set it against the pipe and turned it as if testing for a truth. The pump in the house would not hold its pressure if the line were weak anywhere. They had tried new fittings before and the leaks could be cunning, devising themselves at the place where least suspected. He tightened the wrench and put his shoulder against the soil and pushed.

You could sell it and buy a house in town, Eli said. Live near the hospital and the market. Take some comfort in people being handy. He said the words like he was naming a map.

Tom was silent a time. He'd always liked the town for the way it smelled of other people's things, of baked bread and fuel and the low blunt music of shops. But his eyes kept finding the gentle slope of the orchard, the way the light fell through the leaves. He had been conceived somewhere near that slope and perhaps that was what kept his gaze.

If we sell then what do we tell Pa, Tom said. Pa can't be buried in a place he sold off with his next of kin. He didn't ask this to the air. The words had been sharpened by years of watching men get rid of things and call it progress.

Eli thought of his father. He saw again the man as he had been a decade ago, stooped beneath a hat that had been weathered to a certain holiness, hands always smelling of sap and coffee, a voice that could make the smallest thing seem like a decree. He did not know what the father had thought the land was for. It might've been a pledge or a refuge or a currency to be spent at the last. He had died with his boots dirty and his pockets filled with receipts.

We bury him here, Eli said. Of course we do. But selling don't mean you dig him up and put him on a platter. You sell land. They may keep the grave. They may not.

You'd sign over a piece of him with a dotted line, Tom said. He crouched beside where Eli had set a coupling down and picked it up like it might be the body of a small animal.

There's no telling what men will do, Eli said. There is no telling about the heart in a man. He found himself walking to the shade of a tree and sitting with his back against the trunk. The bark left its fingerprint on his cloth.

They took the afternoon to follow the line along the orchard. The earth hummed in heat. Bees moved with a noise that was earnest and low. The fruit when it hung had the yellow of an old coin and the sweet smell of weathered coin. They threaded a length of hose through the limbs and patched where the root had gnawed and the gopher had burrowed. The hose held. For a time the water sang and the trees drank like men who had been left too long without.

Tom went to the pump house to check the generator and his shadow slanted long behind him. He came back with a rag and the smell of oil about his shirt. He said, They don't make these things like Pa did. He spoke not with reproach but like a man noting a fact of the world. The generator coughed once and then took better to the throttle. Water ran with a clearer tone now through the sections they'd mended. Where the irrigation fed the ground the dust quieted and a new smell rose moist and earthy.

Eli watched the water find its way to the trees and his chest did that small thing a man will not report about his own heart, a stirring like a bell faintly rung. He could imagine a season with better rains. He could imagine a season where the fruit filled out like the bellies of well-fed hogs.

You ever think 'bout moving away from here, Tom said. He was sitting on the crate that had held the coupling and his boots dangled above the dust. He had that look then like a man who had been considering a line on the map for a long time.

Eli's laugh was small. Move? Where to? We been here since before either of us was a thing let to memory. Where else is there to go but away.

He said it and the answer sat between them and was not wholly persuasive. Eli rubbed at his forearm where a scar gleamed like old bone. He'd been younger once and the orchard had been larger then in his head. He had known winters that made the trunks vulnerable and years when the harvest had been like a promise kept. The land had given them a living and a manner of speech that had the ring of the old country and the calluses on the hands of the men who had kept it.

You heard from Harris, Tom said. Harris had been the one who'd walked the county clerk's office last month with a young man in a suit who kept booking it in his head like someone marking off a list. They were the kind of men who bought these places when the scent of a subdivision crept near.

Eli told him what he knew. Harris had offered some numbers. He'd said they could get enough for the place to see the back of debt and maybe even enough to buy a small house in town and put away some for a future the brothers could not agree to define. The word floated between them as if it were a thing that might be dangerous to touch.

Tom's hand went to the crate between his knees. The wood was warm. They had a way with fate in that place. Fate was something you put on a bench and looked at some afternoons when the work was done. The orchard fitted into a long line of other things that men would not let be taken until they had weighed the coin.

You remember the row by the old cottonwood, Tom said. He looked down the line where the cottonwood had been torn and the ground where it had fallen was a hollow the color of old bone. We was kids and we cut a swing and Pa swore like a preacher at us. He sang that song about work and the measuring of things and we had to listen. The memory was bright as the sun but shaped by the kind of tenderness that always ends in a bruise. It was not the orchard that kept the memory living but the shape of the day when their father had found them and what he'd said.

Eli smiled then and his teeth were like a man in old scripture. That was a Sunday. He could see the sun through the leaves like slashes. You remember the dog that chased after the car while we watched. The dog ran on and didn't matter. We thought man's work was to hold on. There was not a miracle at the end only a lesson.

Tom dropped his head and the shadow fell over his face. You thinkin we're keepin it because it's because of them, he said.

I said we'll see, Eli replied. He cupped the tree bark in his hand as if for reassurance. The bark warmed the palm. We oughta ask what it means for us. Hell a thing don't ask to be kept or sold. It just is.

They ate their lunch under a cottonwood whose leaves were sparse and relieved for the shade. A jar of coffee steamed in the air and tasted like all the other cups they'd drank in a lifetime. A single fly fussed at the rim of the tin. They talked of nothing more than the water and the good years when the apples had been heavy enough to break the limbs. Tom smoked until the cigarette burned down low and then put it out with his boot. They made promises in that hour only to the wind.

When the heat turned the day into a field of shimmering gold they went back to the pump house and found the generator running cleaner. They turned the valve and the water rose in the pipe with a breathing sound and for a time the row of trees drank like men who had been given bread after a long fast. The fruit shone in their leaves as if it were a light in the dusk.

Still you going to tell me which, Tom said. He stood with the wrench across his hip like a sword.

Eli looked at him and the sun struck the bone of his jaw. I could sell it, he said. I could. I could walk away with coin and a house and take the road east. But then who would know the trees? Who would remember why the fence leaned? He paused. Who would tend the grave by the north fence?

Tom sat down on a stone and watched a bee bury itself in a bloom. He had always been the man to consider the arithmetic of leaving. There were a lot of things a man could do with money. He could buy a new kind of loneliness and perhaps that was not worse than this other loneliness the orchard offered.

You know what Harris said, he said finally. He got quiet and his voice came low from the throat. They want to put in some houses and call it a development. Call it something pretty with an English name. They like the idea of trees in a planned way. They like a place you can walk in a suit and pretend you know the soil.

Eli smiled without joy. They'll plant saplings. They will take the shade from the real and sell the image. The image will be neat. He said it as if naming a plague.

Tom pulled the wrench toward him and set it down like a man making a decision about a thing he'd been fond of and must now confront. You can't hold an old thing for the sake of the ghosts, he said. Pa's gone. The tree don't care if it's sung to. A life don't care to be sung about. You can take the land and lay it in coin and then go and make a life that's not the orchard and maybe you'll sleep good.

Eli's face folded like the map of some country. That was true in its way. A man might sleep. He might make a life in town with a house that had a small lawn and a mailbox and the smell of other peoples' cooking. He might go to work at a shop and learn to count the minutes of someone else's days. But the orchard had a way of measuring a man that these things did not. It tested the honesty of you. It required little things like patience and rods of stubbornness. It wanted you to show up.

Tom watched the line of trees disappear into a haze and the argument in him was not of many words. He had married once and it had not been for long. He had a son somewhere who had his father's lean and would not speak of the orchard. He had thought once that he might take his boy to town and give him a different beginning. But this was the place his bones fit. He felt that in the small of his back. If he left it would be like cutting off a limb and not knowing the price of that cut.

You heard what Mrs. Ortega said, he said. She'd come by last week with a basket and said the neighbor down the lane was buying up places and he gave a thought to a school and a clinic. She kept looking at us like we were a story and she wanted to place a nice end on it. She wore a dress that had the color of afternoon.

Eli thought of Mrs. Ortega whose hands were all the time busy with mending and who had a laugh that was small and a little bright. She had minded the brothers as a child minds someone else's sons because she had none of her own. The townspeople liked to pick apart other people's decisions with the skill of a barber trimming a beard.

We could get enough to pay for the debts and maybe put a sum away for you so you can come and go, Eli said. You can have something like a future with the town and the hospital and not have to worry if your truck dies. He was soft with the thought and there was perhaps a hope in him that he would not have named.

Tom put his head back against the crate and closed his eyes. The sky kept its merciless blue. He thought of a room in town with a TV and a window that faced the street and people going by and dogs that would not be his and he could imagine sleeping well. He could imagine buying a suit and a new watch that would tell him how the day fell out. He could imagine being unburdened.

But then there was the grave under the north fence and the way the wind sounded as it comes across the arroyo at night and the cry of a coyote in the spring. Eli had slept with those sounds and they belonged to the rhythm of his blood. To sell them would be to tidy the house as one would toss an old photograph in the wastebasket.

Tom opened one eye and looked at the hinge of land. He stood finally and picked up the wrench and wiped the oil on his jean. He did not say I will or I won't. He stepped to the pump and closed the valve. The water ceased like breath held too long.

Eli felt a little like a man whose name was being taken away gradually. He felt the world making a contract from which he might be excluded. He walked to the north fence where the grave lay under a stone and put his palm on the name carved there. The stone was warm in the sun. His father's name fit the stone as if it had been cut for him and no other man.

We could lease it, Tom said. He had a new voice then as if trying clothes for size. Lease the ground to someone who'll farm it. Lease the memories but keep the body.

Eli turned and stared a long time at him. The idea had both cruelty and mercy and the clarity of both. You can't lease a grave, he said. Lease is a word that leaves you for a time. The stone remembers even when the keys of the gate are turned over.

They talked until late and their words walked in circles and sometimes they rose like birds and sometimes they fell back into the dirt. A small child from a neighboring house ran along the lane and shouted and the sound ruined the argument for a moment and they both looked and smiled like men relieved by something small.

When the light went the trees became black cutouts against the heat-struck western sky. The first star pricked pale and the air cooled a breath and in the long evening their work lay done for the day. They had mended the line such as it was and the water moved now like a patient animal through its course. They sat on the tailgate of the truck and watched the plain beyond where the land gave way to a wash and then rocks and a road that would take a man somewhere else if he chose.

You could sell it next spring, Eli said. He felt as one who arranged scenery. Put out the word when the county folk are about. Let the offers come and then we will see. It buys us a reckoning.

Tom stubbed his cigarette in the dirt and the ember made a tiny bright that winked out. He had the look of a man who'd been granted a reprieve and did not know whether to be grateful. Maybe, he said. Maybe we set the sign. Maybe we wait.

They stayed under the stars and there was a wind that came down from the north and it had the taste of river. They spoke of other things by degrees. Tom told Eli of the boy he had seen once who would take after him and might not ever know the weight of a shovel. Eli admitted to a softness for the small things in town: the bakery where the bread made a crust like bone and the library where the quiet had the virtue of being organized.

By the time they had finished the coffee the moon rose and laid a line of silver across the orchard like a seam. The trees were more than they had been in the day and less than they would be in the future. They were, as always, both memory and demand. The day had been June and the heat would return with a patience that made men feel small.

In the morning they put a hand-made sign at the entrance to the lane with a phone number scrawled on it. It looked like an invitation and a parting. Tom did not sleep well that night. He woke with a dry mouth and walked out to the pump house and listened to the world like a man who'd been given a secret. Eli slept with his hand on his side as if to guard a place.

When the first buyer came it was not the men in suits with polished shoes that Tom had imagined. It was a woman in a sunhat and a boy no more than twelve with hair the color of straw. She drove a beaten sedan and her hands were clean in a way that suggested she'd been somewhere else before this and had taken pity on the dirt. She looked at the rows and at the trees and said, This is lovely, as if the word could hold all the necessary judgment. Her boy ran between the trees and his laughter cut the air like a small bell.

They told her the facts. The acreage, the well, the pump, the mortgage. She listened with the patience of someone who reads a book that might surprise. You don't have to sell to us if you don't want to, she said. She had a plainness in her voice that was not coaxing and not rude but the tone of one who measured life in terms of what was needed and what could be given.

Eli watched her and he thought of many things that no one had asked him to think. He thought of Pa's hands and of the stacking of winter wood and of the sharp little songs that Pa hummed while he plucked a tree free of a dying branch. He thought of the summer when they'd had enough to buy a new plow blade and how the manager of the supply store had smiled and taken their check. Memory had the taste of iron in it. He thought of Tom's voice measured the night before when he had said Maybe.

Tom took to the woman in a manner not quite ready to be trusted by reason. He talked about the irrigation with a competence that made the woman nod. She bought water and she bought a story. She had a small economy of hands and plans. She came back the next week with a man in a truck who measured the soil and the depth and took notes and scratched his beard. They spoke in a manner that assumed this place might be made tidy and new in a way that would suit the town.

You want us to put a price on it, Eli said. He felt like a man stating a medical fact.

She gave him a look he did not recognize. He said we have offers sometimes. Not many. He named an amount that lay on the dirt between them and tasted of numbers. The woman kissed the air with the sound of consideration and then she called the man from the truck. They spoke softly and then the man looked at the brothers and said, We'll give fifty for the lot as is but you'll have to clear the debris and handle the survey. The number was more than their debts. It was a life in town.

Tom's jaw tightened. Fifty would fix the motor and buy a small place and put a bit away for sickness. He held it up like a kindling stick.

Eli's mouth became a line. He thought of the grave by the north fence. He thought of the swing they'd made in the cottonwood for their father's boyhood and how it had squealed in the wind. He thought of the rows that had been his schooling and the smallness of him when measured against the orchard. He thought also of the debts and the ledger with its dark ink and of mornings when the mailbox was empty of anything good.

We could take it, he said finally. He felt a release and a recoil in one breath. He thought how the water had sounded that afternoon when it moved and how the trees had eased their thirst.

Tom looked at the woman and then at the boy who was now holding a branch and poking at a stone like all children. What I'll tell the boy, he said at last, is this place will not belong to him if we sell it. He said it with the gentle brutality of someone speaking a duty. If we sell he'll grow with blankness and not know the smell of sap in his bedclothes and maybe that's all right but that don't make me easy.

Eli saw the truth in the words even as they cut. The boy's glance went to them as if they were two animals alleging a fight. The woman, polite and practical, saw the ledger. She made a small sound like a hand closing.

We won't sign it today, Eli said. He offered the decision like a man handing something small and significant. We'll carry it for a while more. We can take the number and think on it. We're not men who decide in one morning. We make our decisions with the seasons in us.

The woman smiled then with a care that held pity and respect. She folded her hands and said, We are here when you're ready. She left a card that had the man in the truck's number on the back and some promises that were polite in the way of good business. The boy waved from the car until it was gone.

After they shut the gate they sat on the truck bed and watched the road and the place where the woman had been and the car and their possibility. Tom lit a cigarette and watched the smoke go up. They had not sold the orchard and perhaps they had not yet decided what to do. The sign at the gate would stand and they would speak of other options and lean on the numbers once more. The fields answered them with a wind that was full of the small noises of evening.

Eli touched the face of the stone at the grave and felt the name like the memory of a flame. He did not know if he had done right. He only knew he had done what felt like being true to the measure of a life he'd been given.

Tom put a hand on his shoulder then without flourish like a man who had no more priesthood than a brother's touch. He said, Maybe we keep it for another year. Maybe we find a way to make the trees pay us enough to keep the edges from fraying. Maybe we lease a part and keep the rest. Maybe one of us goes and the other stays. He said all the things a man will say to keep the fabric of his days from an abrupt tear.

They sat a long time in the dark while the orchard breathed around them. A coyote called once like a small complaint. The stars were merciless in their order and the moon hung a pale coin. In the morning the work would begin again and they would patch what leaks they could and plan anew. The meter of life on the land held them. It offered them not a solution but a kind of continuity.

When they rose the next day the sun had a temperate kindness and the dust had settled a little. They drove the length of the lane and removed the sign and put it in the back of the truck as if it had been a proposition that needed storage. They might put it back up. They might not. The earth would not judge them. It would only show whether they had tended it or not.

They went to the north fence and turned the soil where the grave had been and planted a small seedling beside the stone that would be a tree for some future hand to loosen and swing upon or to name. The very act of planting was a kind of language between them older than parlance of title or deeds. The seed would take what water it was given and make of it a thing.

All that afternoon they worked and the land took their labor and changed a little for it. The water ran clear and the trees drank and the orchard breathed as if forgiving them. They did not know whether they had done enough. They only knew they had given the day to it and that in such economies there lay a quiet dignity.

At dusk they sat again under the cottonwood and Tom reached across and clapped his brother's knee. You made the call, he said. Eli nodded. He had not chosen to promise an answer forever. He had only chosen the small mercy of a delay and the modest courage of a plant. He felt then that perhaps there was time enough to meet the measure of both the ledger and the grave.

The wind came down from the arroyo and the night closed over them warm and ancient. They rose and went to the house, to the thin supper and the old radio that sang low and off key. They slept and dreamed of trees. They would wake and the orchard would be there like a witness and a demand and the world would continue with the bartermen and the men with leases and the women with sunhats and the children who owned nothing but who might, if fate allowed, own memory itself.

In the end the orchard would take its due from them and give them back only small mercies. They would be men who had kept faith with a place for a time and had learned that a living is not only coin and contract but a tender of days. Whether they would sell the land remained a question whose answer would be a long work, a thing to be considered in the hot glare of many afternoons, in the cough of generators and the hush of the grave. For now they had planted and they had held, and that was enough to make the night feel as if some prayer had been answered if only in the smallest of ways.
