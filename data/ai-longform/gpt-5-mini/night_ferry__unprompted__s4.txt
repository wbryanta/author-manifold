The ferry left with all the small theatrical finalities of a thing that had done this many times—horn, a long slow reverse of engine, a creak that sounded like an old man waking. I had gone up on deck because the ferry was itself part of the memory: the taste of salt in a throat, the way the air takes your face and smooths against it. The night sea was a field of oil under a low cloud. Light from the ship flared on the water and turned it into quick, bright insects. Some of the cars on the stern still had their headlights on, ghosts of journeys that were already ended; people were leaning against railings or wrapped in blankets, their breath small and white. There was no one whose face I registered. Faces in that light all tended toward the private, pulled tight by wind and some ongoing calculation about what to leave behind.

I had my hands in my pockets and the paper envelope in my breast, warm and pliant through the fabric. The envelope had had no return address, only a case number on a small, typed sticker—C1768—folded into the corner like a marginal note. Inside, the coroner had included a photograph, like the light from a distant shore. There had been one of those portrait snapshots with the background of an apartment, a grinning man with an old sweater over his shoulder, and another, a closer, harsher photo of a face half-eaten by the sun and wind. Someone had written Einar on the back in blocky, official letters. There was also a note, brief as an order: Released to next of kin for identification. The rest of the sentence had been clipped—Identification required within 24 hours—and below it a name and a telephone number.

I had dialed the number in a waiting room full of the smell of syrup. The voice that had answered was careful and small, like someone covering a thing with a hand so it would sleep. "We can keep him, if you can't get here in time," she said. "There are procedures." She told me how his body had been found on the rocky shore beneath the old lighthouse, his clothes tattered, the tide having touched him like a final, formal greeting. He had been in his fifties. He had been alone. The police had not found any identification on him. The official voice arranged me a place with the single, economical compassion that would permit someone, anyone, to do the naming.

It had been a ferry ride in the middle of the night between two places whose names had lost their heat. I had booked the last cabin because I wanted some private airing of the heart that had been collecting. I hardly slept. The hum of the engines was a distant animal. Occasionally the ferry would cut its speed and the drop in vibration would produce an almost ceremonial silence that smelled of metal and old coffee. I thought of how long it had been since I had seen Einar. Ten years, I guessed. Perhaps more. The calculations of time on your own side can be pitiless because you stack them against what you think you owe someone.

We had grown up with the sea as a kind of sibling that would always be there, restless but predictable. There had been summer houses with flaking white paint, a boathouse that father had built with the wrong nails and forgiven in the next season with a pot of tea. We had been taught to watch the weather by the color of the water. In winter the surface of the strait turned to a smoke color and the gulls were beads strung at the edge of the world. Einar left and kept leaving. He took small excitements: a job on a fishing trawler up north, then a bus ticket to a town that had words for its isolation built into them, then finally, a life that was like a series of accidental harborings. He called sometimes, then stopped. We argued once outside the pub about a woman and a coin and a foolish, sentimental promise he had made and then broken; the argument unspooled into unsaid things, which are the most durable. I stayed home and watched the gulls and worked as a mechanic in a garage that smelled of oil and conversation snipped short by an early closing. That was my life, the one that fit my hands.

We had shared the house with our father until he died. He died the way men of his generation die, with the small files of their suffering smoothed into a polite paper list of conditions. Einar came back then, briefly, his hair longer, his voice soft around the edges. He slept on the cot and cooked instant coffee and sat with father for a week. They spoke of work and places and the names of people whose faces they had been unable to summon. On his last night in the house he left without a word and later, in a letter that arrived folded like a fortune, wrote that he had gone to a town where the harbor kept secrets and the air was clean. There were photos of a small boat, a dog, a cigarette butt on a ledge. He signed it in his usual way, with a thin, flinty humor: SEE YOU WHEN THE SEA TAKES US. It was either a joke or a vow.

The ferry changed speed; the hull kissed and left the waves in bright ribbons. I thought about the last time I had seen him, which I had sealed in a small, dishonoring place in my memory along with unpaid bills and the small religious gestures one makes to avoid feeling badly. It had been at the boatyard, behind the shed where the winter boats were stored. We had been younger, the kind of young where the future looked like a map that might be redrawn. He had been drinking cheap beer and laughing at bad things. He offered me a cigarette and then took it back, more sober than the sound of his laugh. "Be the one to break it," he said, meaning our father, or the house, or the thing between us. I had been too angry to be the breaker. I had wanted to keep what had been given to me even if it meant shutting a door. I had watched him go then, sleeves rolled down and hands like pale maps. I watched him go in a way that was a refusal to beg.

When the ferry docked, the sky was a long, apathetic gray. The passengers moved like a single thought made of many hands. At the ferry terminal I bought a coffee I could not taste and a cigarette for ritual and asked directions to the funeral home. The ferry's light receded from the window like a moon sliding backward. The town where the coroner's office sat had that compressed flavor of weather-beaten places—piers leaning like old people, a bakery with a glass clouded by butter. The main street had throngs of closed shopfronts; the funeral home was unpretentious, a house with a blue door and plastic chairs on the porch. There were a pair of white birches in the yard, their bark peeling like old paper. A nurse opened the door. She was less an employee than a fixture of the house; she wore a cardigan and the tired look of someone who had been asked to witness too much.

He sat me in a room with linoleum and light that had no particular mood. The coroner was a compact man in a suit that wanted to suggest severity. He had the fatigue of those who move in between things, the tenor of "Sorry." He had kindly eyes that did not do sentiment. There was paperwork, a legal vocabulary of death that folded things into categories: natural, accidental, violent, undetermined. He explained that the tide had taken his body and that the blunt instrument of sea and rock did most of the work of disappearance. He showed me the photograph they had taken on the shore: a man on his back, hair spread like comic ink, one shoe off. The face had the soft, indistinct features the sea produces when it wants to make us guess. It could have been anyone. It could have been Einar. It could have been someone else entirely.

"Do you know him well?" the coroner asked. "Would you be able to identify him?"

"Yes," I said, and my voice folded over the word the way a garment folds over skin. It was not an honest answer. To say "No" would have been to prolong a process of naming that I could not afford. To say "Yes" was to confirm something—identity, ownership, the last small legal thing to be asked of a person. I realized then I had come because there are obligations that feel like bones in your chest; you can ignore them for a long time until they itch the wrong way.

There was a small ritual about viewing a body. The coroner walked me to a room that smelled of disinfectant and cold linen. The hall outside the door had paintings of vessels on stormy seas, oils with faces in the foam. When he opened the door there was a rectangle of bright blue light on the floor, the kind that refrigeration units throw like a confession. A man lay on a stainless steel table under blankets. He was not wrapped in a sheet; his fingers were bare and showed the livid, translucent color of someone who had been under water for many hours. A small tag had been tied delicately to one wrist; my name was not on it. My name would only go on the release forms if I said what needed to be said.

The body was smaller than I expected. Time does strange spatial tricks. Our father had been a large man; Einar had always been medium-sized, wiry, the kind of man who wore his life in folds of light. The face was swollen, the mouth partly open and the lips a gray color. I blinked because looking feels like a motion that has to be pulled. The hair was salt and short. It was only when I moved closer that something like recognition slipped between my ribs—an old scar above one eyebrow, the faint lattice of lines you get from laughing too often. I had once given him a playfully hard punch there for stealing a comic book when we were twelve; the mark had never entirely vanished. I saw it now like a punctuation mark, small and unwanted. My fingers wanted to touch; the impulse felt as old as the bones. I did not lay my hand on his face. There was a distance of rules and water and a small, stubborn decorum that kept me.

There was a dent in his coat pocket that I recognized—a knock of the pocket where he kept a cheap lighter with a ship stamped into its brass casing. He was the kind of man who would carry a trinket in the same place whether he was alive or dead. I asked the coroner to open the pocket and he found it there, damp but intact, the lighter with paint rubbed away as if it had been cradled a lot. My mouth made the shape of his name and it felt like a hole had been opened in my chest and some things were pouring out: stories, small grievances, years of silence. I thought of the small lighthouse where he had tended the bulb for a winter once, a job that kept him anonymous to everyone but the sea.

"You can identify the face," said the coroner, low and practical. "Or the personal effects. It is your choice."

It seems obscene—the act of making a decision of identity like choosing which of two coats is yours. I had left a lot of flags unfurled in the wind of our argument. There had been a time I judged him harshly and wanted him small for the offense of living a life different from mine. He had been free in ways I could not understand and unfaithful in little domestic quarrels; he had been a traveler who came home with hands smelling of other men's cigarettes. A kind of contempt had crept where family should have been; it had been easier to draw a border than to be interested in the ways he might have been changing. I had called him extravagant things when I was small and angry, which is nearly the same as being cruel. There was a ledger of grievance that had grown a crust.

When they unzipped the coat, my chest tightened at the sight of a small piece of folded paper, a grocery list maybe, a receipt. The coroner unfolded it with the mechanical politeness of someone who is inclined to give the dead the benefit of privacy. The paper said, in a handwriting that could only be his, a line like a joke: BUY: TEA, BREAD, LIGHTER FLUID. In the bottom corner, a smudge like a fingerprint. That ticket, mundane, immediate, had been for me the final evidence, accidental as the sea. I found myself leaning in and then, because the environment of the morgue has laws about contagion and grief, I bent only my elbows and made the decision with a dry, juridical "Yes."

The coroner nodded and the paper forms were ceremonially brought out. He told me I would need to sign. My signature felt like an instrument I had not used. We sat at a narrow table and filled out the columns: name, relationship, address. I wrote my name and the old house at the harbor, which I still owned because I could never bear to sell it despite its slowly failing roof. There was a long row labeled "Cause of Death." I left it blank because no one knew. There was also a place for a statement—Do you wish an autopsy?—and I checked no because what good is an autopsy to the person who is gone? There was a box that suggested whether you wanted the body released for burial or cremation. I checked burial, not because burial was what he would have wanted but because it was a conservative word that felt like anchorage. The woman behind the counter thanked me and gave me a small pamphlet about grief that suggested people to call. Grief felt like a foreign country. The pamphlet sat and rustled next to a stack of forms as faintly present as a rumor.

Later, after forms had been stamped and the coroner had asked whether I wanted anything removed from the pocket or if there were items to be kept, I asked about the watch. Einar had always worn a watch, cheap but with the kind of face that makes a man animalistically proud. Military. It had been his way of saying he would be somewhere on time to someone who didn't care. The coroner produced it: waterlogged, glass cracked but small numerals still visible. The strap had been lopped at one edge by some careless blade of the sea. I put it in my palm like a stone and felt the coiled history of him in my hand. The whole identification had felt like an act of gathering flotsam that designated ownership.

"It's not certain," the coroner said, gently, as if he were correcting the pronunciation of a name someone long dead hadn't used. "But it's close."

There was a moment in the quiet of the office where the world withdrew to the size of my hands. The name on the forms could be changed; in some country, somewhere, wrong names had been attached to raincoats and the rain had not cared. What holds is not always proof. I thought of calling home, of how father used to say that proper ceremonies are for the living, to keep themselves from dissolving. There is a gain in saying goodbye aloud. I said the name—Einar—in a low voice, which was closer to a prayer than a fact. There is such smallness in the way grief asks us to make statements because there is a need for a thing to be held by language.

They wrapped him in a sheet for the transport. Even wrapped, he held a kind of presence that made the table look like a stage on which a scene had been finished. The attendants moved with a reverence that belonged to people who had to greet every day with the knowledge of bodies and their silent habits. They took him out to the vehicle and installed him there carefully as if he were a fragile instrument. I followed to the edge of the coroner's yard because following is what we are taught to do, even when we are unsure where the road leads.

Outside, the air had the particular metallic taste that comes before a storm. The harbor had a grayness that felt almost religious. The morgue's van slid away and I stood and watched until it was a shape on the road then faded. A woman with a dog passed me, her steps brisk, the dog tugging toward promises I could not see. I wanted to be somewhere else that I could not name. The electric sky held out a flat sheet of light, not consoling but steady.

I had to tell someone. I called my sister—she lived inland with two kids and a husband who kept his hands warm by mechanical means—and she answered with the kind of calm that means an adult has already sorted the world into things one can manage. "Do you want me to come?" she asked. Her tone was the one you use when someone else has been injured and you need to decide whether to administer bandage or merely hold a hand. I told her to come if she wanted and then I told her to wait and not to swallow the ferry's schedule. In the half-hour that followed my mind produced stories that might explain everything and some that could explain nothing. Perhaps he had tried to cross a rock to fetch driftwood. Perhaps he had sat on the edge and the water decided, with its patient cruelty. I realized then that whatever the cause, the water had the final vote.

I had imagined a funeral in the small chapel by the harbor with a few chairs and wind in the rafters. I had imagined the attendance of the kinds of people who mattered because fishers, mechanics, and old teachers have a way of summoning themselves for these things. I had imagined saying words that would make sense. Instead, there was the unsettling business of forms and the hungry bureaucratic time of the coroner. The law was a compass placing your grief on the ledgers of the state. I felt like a man with a pen between his fingers, consenting to the language.

That night I slept in the house I had kept because it was easier than selling a house that contained your father's voice. The radiators clicked like the heart trying to find a rhythm. I had precise dreams: a small boy carrying a lantern across the harbor, a village made of paper, a face in the face of a wave. I woke at four and found myself on the shore, though I could not say whether I had walked there; memory sometimes owns its own bodies. The lighthouse stood like a watchman with its lamp unloaded. The town's lights were embers. I sat on a rock and the cold went under the hem of my coat. The tide had rubbed itself away into the sand. The sea spoke in the vernacular it had and the gulls had folded into the early.

My sister arrived with coffee that tasted like the kitchen of a rented room. She held her hands around the cup like old ritual. "He was our brother," she said before asking his name. The truth traces itself in small, unexpected gestures. There are sudden, intimate recognitions in the way a sibling uses a phrase your parents used, or the way a person folds a napkin. When she said he was our brother she made a claim that had been denied by time and by silence. We went to the chapel because sometimes the formal architecture of grief gives your body something to attach itself to; its wood is honest and does not try to be weatherproof to your feelings.

At the cemetery I noticed the wedges cut into paths by the wheels of the hearse. The monument stones had names like thin doors. I had never seen Einar in a suit; the idea of dressing him seemed to me at once theatrically wrong and the only possible kindness. We had chosen a modest plot, facing the little sea. The priest—small and more of a navigator than a holder of dogma—spoke of mercy and the impossibility of explanation. There was the usual array of condolences, the small exactness of who had come to be witness. The air smelled like damp wool. I looked at the folded flags and at the faces assembled like a congregation of people who had chosen to be present.

They lowered the coffin and the sound was a kind of finality. The ground swallowed sound as if ashamed of having to be so practical. People said the things people say about the dead: "He was a good man," "He had his faults," "He loved the sea." The words stirred in the belly like a spoon in shallow soup. The priest's voice went on, and it was as if the world had contracted to the circumference of the grave and the sky above it. I thought and thought. Grief is not a single emotion. It is a house made of many rooms; in each room you feel something different. Sometimes you're furious; sometimes relieved; sometimes merely blank.

There was an older man there I had never met who said, when the formalities were done, "He saved me once," and described how Einar had pulled him from a slip, how he had left him a coat in payment. People with stories find their way to the podium; they had been held. There was a woman who said he had given her a dog and in turn had been given one back. The world rearranged him like a scrapbook and pressed him between memories. I felt at once like an intruder and a steward. The truth is, I had wanted to be more than a signature on a form. I wanted to be the person who could say yes and mean it and not have the old ledger still thick on my conscience.

After the graveside, people drifted toward coffee and small sandwiches, a ritual of returning to the ordinary. My sister sat very still, her hands in her lap. I learned then that she had kept a diary when she was sixteen and had a page full of Einar in it: the way he balanced a glass on his forehead and let a record scratch in the background like proof of the way life should be messy and irreverent. She read a few lines from the diary as if reading a charm. It was like watching a small memory become set. I had a need to tell people what we had been and then to confess we had not been.

After everyone left, after the chairs had been stacked and the smell of coffee had evaporated, I walked down to the water. The harbor was quiet, a piano of small waves. All night the sea keeps its own counsel. I had identified him, and in that identification something inside me shed its armor and became small and soft. You can, in the span of an hour, perform acts which have a centuries-long tail of meaning. Identification was one of those acts. It made legal what was already true in one sense and false in another. The thing that lived inside us as a private ledger had been folded into a public act.

I thought about the littleness of documents. In an office, people who had never met him would carefully archive his name under a file number. They would keep his photograph, his lighter. The coroner's hands were efficient, and the world would go on. I realized I had thought identification might undo a grief I had not fully recognized. But grief is elastic. It will not be conformed to a single event. In my pocket, where I kept the watch, my fingers found the dent of his lighter. It lay warm against the heart of my jeans.

At home, the house where father had sat with his pipe and the radio playing the way a flame keeps a kettle, the rooms retained small evidence of the lives that had walked through them. There were a pair of boots by the back door that had been in the house too long to belong to anyone but us. I brewed coffee and sat at the table and put the lighter on the wood like an island. The morning percolated in a rhythm that approximated ordinary mercy. The lighter's brass was no longer touched in the same way. Still, to have done the necessary thing felt like a wound sewn with a slow, heavy needle.

A week after, I received a call from the coroner. "We had the autopsy," he said. "The results are negative on any foul play. Cause is undetermined with an overlay of seawater exposure." He was apologetic about the vagueness of the state, which is the soft way officials explain the steadfast silences of the world. There was no evidence he had been harmed. There was no obvious sign that he had attempted suicide. The sea remained itself—ambivalent, implacable. It had swallowed him and given him back with a precision that was partial and teasing.

Sometimes I think about the small things that become our proofs. A lighter. A scar. A crooked tooth. They are like coins stamped at the mint of memory. The emotional economy of identification has many currencies. You can weigh the heart against an object and find the object a little too cold. But we did what families do; we found the thing that would anchor him. People asked me later if I had any regrets. Regret is a kind of acid that flares in the throat when you hold your life up to the light and see its fractures. Mine is small, mostly composed of single unsaid apologies that seemed impossible to fit into windows of time. "I'm sorry," is a note that sometimes does not have a receiver. I kept those unsaid because they were private and because at the time I thought them not worth the risk of demanding more of Einar than I had ever given him.

On the anniversary of his death, I went down to the point where the rocks kissed at the sea, with the lighter in my pocket and a small bouquet of thrift-store flowers. The lighthouse blinked in its slow way. I thought of the men who go out and break themselves against the world and of the ways in which a brother is at once the easiest and the hardest thing to love. I placed the flowers on a stone and the lighter beside them like a small, sacramental object. The water licked the base of the rock as if to test the world for tenderness. It was cold. It moved with the kind of disinterested affection a tide has for history.

The thing about identity is that it asks us to tell a story that can be believed by those who have no other interest in the person except what they can record. In my hands, his life had been reduced to signatures, to choices about burial, to a little slit of cash for a service. And yet the truth of him—the way his laughter had slid into the air and made people tilt their heads—was unpurchaseable. That is the small scandal at the heart of grief: you sign papers and the soul goes away.

Once, years before, Einar had called me at two in the morning from a bus heading nowhere, his voice thin with laughter and loneliness, and asked if I would come and get him. I had told him no. That answer had been petty and absolute and I kept it like a coin in some jar where grudges live. I replayed it and refused to be absolved by the years that would pass and unmake things. When would a man have a chance to come back and fix things? The question has no calendar. I had said yes when the coroner asked me to identify what was left of the map of him. It was a yes that had as much to do with the economy of the living as with a filial duty.

At the funeral, when the priest said "May he rest," a little part of me wanted to say "Or may he roam," because some lives do not settle even in the earth. They become a constellation, a pattern on the water. People who loved him told stories and those told stories in turn until he was at once smaller and somehow more generous than in life. I think this is what family does; it adds ornaments to the memory, it translates actions into meanings. I did not say many things at the chapel; I had no capacity for the eloquence I wanted. Instead, later, sitting at the kitchen table, I wrote a letter I never sent. I told him small things—about father, the house, the leaky sink—and about the ways the gulls had changed their routes. I placed the letter with the lighter in a drawer. The act was one of private liturgy.

The sea keeps really what it wants to keep. Sometimes it returns a thing and sometimes it takes it. Identification is an attempt to exert civil order on an animal that is indifferent. In the end, the thing that felt most true was not that I had recognized him on a table under a cold light, but that I had been willing to say his name aloud to the world. Saying it did not make the missing years forgiving. It did not bring back missed meals, conversations, or simple, drunken confessions. What it did do was ask me to be present in the way the world counted presence: paperwork, signatures, attendance.

Years later, when strangers walked past the pier and asked directions because their phone had died and sun had come up just left of the lighthouse, I would sometimes watch for a profile that resembled his in the faces of passing men. Sometimes a shadow would cross and I would feel a small, piercing not-unpleasant ache. People die who feel like a half-finished story; you go on the map of them with little finger-paintings. You put their lighter on the mantle and it begins to patina. You might forget it for a while and then catch the scent of brass and remember.

At night, the sound of an engine and the smell of diesel will still make me think of him. The ferry runs again and again along its same slow route. Sometimes I stand on the deck like a man who feels the sea's breath on his face, and all the decisions one has made in the various rooms of life—half-justifications, small cruelties, acts of overdue mercy—are a gentle turbulence under the surface. I watch the wake like a ledger undone, a white ribbon that tells me that things move forward whether or not they are finished.

I cannot say whether what we did was right. I think there are no right answers in these places. We do what we can, and if the sea is a judge, it rarely speaks. It only shapes. In the end, I kept the watch on my wrist for a long time and then, years later, pushed it into a drawer with the lighter. I would pull the drawer open sometimes and rub the brass like a rosary bead. The act was private. It was a confession more than an absolution. The identification had been a doorway through which I had walked, and beyond it the world remained ambiguous and vast. The sea closed and opened its hands, and I listened.
