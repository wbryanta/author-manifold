But the thing about all that, I told her, was not what anyone imagined. People think brightness equals revelation, you know, they think when the sky goes white or pink or whatever color God decides to put on the ceiling it means something holy or decisive, like a verdict has finally been read; but most of the time it was just a clever trick of distance and dust and weather, a trick that made you catch your breath long enough to forget the rest of the day. It was theater. It was a very expensive theater. And when the curtains dropped, you went back to the same nickel-and-dime life you'd been living before the curtain was even pulled.

Inez didn't say anything for a while. She sat there with the teacup balanced on the saucer like it was a little weather station and she was judging the day by the way the steam moved. She'd been doing that for years—the little assessments—checking my shoes, the shape of my hands, the way my laugh had gotten less like a laugh and more like a grunt. I could tell from her face she was letting me have the story without interrupting. That's probably why I kept talking; not many people get that kind of audience anymore—people who will let you wander off on a subject and wander back without thinking you've deserted them.

You remember Johnston, I said, more to fill the air than to confirm to her. You remember me telling you about the souvenir? The little brass thing with the enamel map on it? I had it once, tucked away in a bureau drawer, next to an old deck of cards and a wedding band that wasn't even mine. I don't know what happened to it. Sometimes I think I left it on a plane. Sometimes I think I sold it to a boy in a bar who liked that sort of thing. Maybe I told you that story already, too. Memory is economical, I told her—takes the things it likes and tosses the rest by the side of the road.

She smiled then, and the smile had the same effect it always used to—immediately brought down the volume of the room. That's what she did; she could take the air pressure out of any high-flying anecdote. 'You and your souvenirs,' she said, though I know she meant everything else. She had this way of saying things where she didn't have to raise her voice to make you feel small or beloved. We had been through that geography a thousand times: for me the Pacific, for her whatever small town set of public schools and church socials that had produced her maiden name with the dignity of a baptismal certificate. I liked to think the names made us fit into our skins.

Anyway, there were these times, and then other times. You'd go to one of those little atolls and think, this is the end of the world, and then two weeks later you'd be back in town trying to find a shoeshine who would still remember you. You meet people on those islands who work in the rhythms of weather and maintenance and bookkeeping and they have this look like their souls have been ironed flat by monotony; then they watch a sky and suddenly they're something else again—children, or priests, or fools in love. You didn't have any illusions that we were changing anyone's mind, though. The tests were for the maps and the math and the newspaper headlines, not for the hearts.

I remember one night in Saipan, after a shot that someone later put in the files as 'successful with anomalous propagation,' we sat on the roof of the officers' quarters and opened cans of peaches because the chow line had decided peaches were a luxury for the postshot morale. There was a cool wind, and we were six or seven men with the kind of faces that never felt like family to each other but for that one night they were close enough to pass cigarettes without looking. An old sergeant started telling us about catching eels in a lagoon when he was ten and how his mother used to slap his hand with a wooden spoon if he didn't come in when she called. There is always some human story like that, a small domesticity that makes you realize everyone who stands there with a Geiger counter or a clipboard or a camera is someone’s kid. Makes you want to cry sometimes—real silly, useless crying. We didn't, though. We smoked. We ate peaches. We watched the horizon until the sky did that trick and we were all, for one ordered second, children again.

Inez reached across the table and brushed a crumb from my sleeve like she was brushing lint from a uniform. 'You always were a sentimental cuss,' she said. The way she used the word 'cuss'—not mean but affectionate—made me answer in kind. 'Sentimental's better than a thief,' I told her. We laughed, quick and small. It was the kind of laugh that means we were both keeping certain things back. You learn the economy of silence when you've done the things we have.

Because, see, that's another thing people don't understand. The test shots, the islands, the towers, all the theatricality of it—that's just the outer layer. Underneath were the memos, the orders, the evenings in hotel lobbies with men in brown shoes and little folds around their mouths, plotting things that would be—well, not pretty. You read a paper from that era—if you can find it—and it sounds like science fiction but with the appetite of an accountant. The men in charge had handwriting like librarians but ideas like conquerors. It's a bad mix, if you ask me. All the more reason to keep a deck of cards and a little brass map charm. A man needs something small to hold that's not the future.

There was one fellow down at Eniwetok—good hands, I liked him—who used to take pictures with a little Kodak and then make these meticulous albums of the sunsets. He'd paste them in, neat as a thing, and every now and then he'd lose himself in those pages like he was taking a long walk through his childhood. He didn't survive the afterward. Not everyone who watches the sky ends up leaving their child at the door and whispers 'I'll be right back.' Some of them have the luck of getting a long slow life and some of them get no luck at all. When the wash came, it was not like water. It was like a rumor that gets in your lungs.

We do a lot of euphemizing. I mean, we had official language for everything. Instead of saying someone was contaminated you wrote 'exposed to extended radiation events' which sounds sterile and purposeful until you realize it implies a life permanently rewritten. Nobody wrote poems about that. Our poets were busy with rations. Nobody sang lullabies about fallout. We did what we had to do. The phrase 'no detectable residuals' was a bureaucratic exorcism. We assured the public the world was fine so effectively that most of them believed it until their teeth started falling out—metaphorically speaking—until their local fishery declined or their kids had strange rashes. I don't want to get morbid, she said once, when I was going off on this subject, but I've never been great at toeing the line between morbid and accurate.

Inez reached for the sugar bowl and her wrist is thin; the veins were like little waterways. She's older than me now by a margin that used to be laughable and is now an afternoon, and there are things that happen you can't negotiate. We have both been done wrong by the clock. We joke about the timer on the stove like it's a calendar. Sometimes at night I wake up and can't remember where I am and I hold my hand out to find the lamp and then realize I've been living in two places—one is this little house with old curtains that smell faintly of mothballs and lemon oil, and the other is the Pacific, which is bigger and meaner and has a smell of gardenias that don't belong there.

There was a time I thought the gardenia scene was ridiculous—us pinning cut flowers into hair and driving out to Schofield; but then I thought, maybe that's precisely why we did it. You put beauty in unlikely places and it announces your stubbornness to history. You can take a grenade and bury it under concrete, but you can't bury the habit of putting something pretty on your lapel when the world demands ugliness. Inez used to say I romanticized things—the sky, the islands, the men we met—but I say you have to make romance out of what you have because sometimes that's all that keeps you going.

There's something else I ought to tell her because she deserves it and because these stories like to take advantage of me when I'm not paying attention. The last field day I had on a testing site—there are so many ways to say 'last' that I keep inventing new ones—I was alone walking back from the pad. Most of the men had already been bussed back to the barracks and the cameras were being turned in. The air still smelled faintly of burnt sugar and ozone. I could see where the sand had turned a grey that was not a natural color, like someone had dipped it into a pot of steam. There was a sea breeze carrying a bouquet of something that might have been gardenias; maybe my senses were making connections they had no right to. I found a child's shoe, the sort the little island kids might have worn—the canvas, a faded blue. It was badly singed, and one eye blinked at me like a question. For a minute it felt like the world had shrunk to that shoe. I thought of sliding it into my pocket like a charm. Instead, I kicked it up into the surf where it snagged on a scrap of driftwood. I walked away. It is the only time in my life I did not take something that struck me as a souvenir. Maybe I felt guilty. Maybe I felt tired. Maybe I thought that small thing belonged to someone else.

You can guess the rest, the way coincidences line up and make their own sentences. Years later, when a woman in the mainland called to ask about a missing shoe, I mentioned having seen a shoe. I don't know why her voice went quiet the way it did when she said, 'I lost my boy there.' You hear those confessions over the phone and you can't do anything with them except listen. Photography does many things but it can't stitch a life back together. The tests left a lot of things unstitched. That is why people with ghosts tend to get very good at certain kinds of small domestic projects; you lean into what you can control.

I told Inez once that I don't like parades anymore. Parades are a display of rarefied cheer where everyone is contained and proud and none of the messy things get shown. Even in Nevada where the oddness was supposed to be more formal and industrial, men with medals would sit on folding chairs and clap like their hands were being paid. They clap because you clap in those circumstances, because applause fills the air and lets you think someone else is keeping the score. I don't know—maybe I'm jealous of applause. Maybe I've had enough of it that it doesn't taste like anything.

Do you want to know about the people I liked? There was an electronics tech named Murray who had an encyclopedic distrust of governments and a gentle way with cats. He would sneak stray cats into the compound and feed them canned sardines. There was a physicist from Princeton who wrote sonnets to his graduate students in the margins of his lab notes; he had a mild face and a way of fixing his glasses that made him look like he could command an army or make a souffle, I'm not sure which. There was a cook named Rosa who could make anything taste like home, which is a strange talent for a woman who had never left the state line. You collect these characters like one might collect stamps, pressing them into albums. Later, when you leaf back through, you realize your life has been made up of a small set of energies that—if you had been a better man—you might have turned into something richer than the reports and the briefings.

'Turned into something richer,' Inez said at that point, and the way she said it made me remember the time I was young and thought changing the world was something you could do in a week if you had a sufficiently large slide rule. What do you want to be, she had asked once, and I told her I wanted to be the man who made water boil faster. She laughed at that for days. Later I discovered you can't make water boil faster without making some people very unhappy. There's an arithmetic to consequences—one plus one is a threat these days.

We had nowhere to be that afternoon. The town next to ours had a bridge that was patched so often you couldn't tell whether it was holding the river up or the river was holding the bridge. A boy went missing there once and everyone decided the river had won. Sometimes it seems like you come home from doing these extraordinary things and the only fight left is to keep the shrubs trimmed. I keep thinking about that shoe by the sea. You live in two climates—one is the very specific care for the body, the other is the metaphysics and the way we talk about it. I got very good at one and very bad at the other. She was better at both than she lets on.

You'd think after standing in the presence of that sudden light a few dozen times you'd get reconciled. You'd think you'd have higher tolerance for wonder. I did for a while. Then you start to notice the pattern. Everything is a repeat. The sky makes its theater act; the bureaucrats file the forms; the papers make their little headlines and forget them. It's like a set of rules someone wrote and published as a manual for dealing with the world. You begin to loathe manuals because they're written by people who want to keep their distance from the consequences of okay, let's call it policy.

There was a man I worked with who believed in policy as if it were a relic to be worshipped. He was a tall fellow, and he wore shoes that were always a little too shiny. I remember once he told me—right in a briefing, right in front of a number of men—that any fallout would be minimal and within acceptable levels. He said it with the kind of voice I hate—equal parts sermon and contractual clause. After a while you notice the pattern; men who say 'acceptable' have a pathology for cost-benefit. The calculus for twenty lives in an awkward place is easier to reconcile than the calculus for the ruin of a river. This is not a mathematical quandary; this is moral laziness.

She tapped the spoon against the inside of her cup and I watched the sound ripple across the porcelain, a tiny witness to patient listening. There are moments I think she knows the exact moment I will wander into some topic like a drunk through an open door and she is already standing in the hallway waiting for me. She does it with a sigh that isn't irritation. It's not pity, either. It's more like a tolerance. Tolerance is an underrated virtue in the aged. It keeps households together.

We had a child once. I'm telling you this like I'm giving you detail out of an archive because we used to treat child-rearing the way we treated minor maintenance: do it on a schedule, don't get emotionally involved. But then you find yourself very emotionally involved. The child was beautiful in the ordinary way children are beautiful, rounded and insistent. For reasons that were at once our own and the world's, we did not keep him. It's a more awkward sentence in the retelling than I'd like. The truth of it sits in my chest like a stone I don't want to tug out. Maybe I should be more precise: we were not permitted to keep that child by forces that began in small administrative misunderstandings and finished in a tribunal office with a ceiling that had seen too much. Or maybe I'm inventing the tribunal to make myself righteous. Either way, the hole exists. When you have a hole you learn to sew patches quickly.

I told Inez that day about seeing a child's shoe because it's a way to tell the world that something indelible happened in a place where indelible things are meant to be ephemeral. The islands are a clean place to practice forgetting: sand covers footprints, tides erase wills, wind makes tombstones illegible. But memory doesn't obey geology. You can build a test site with neat hedges and manicured lawns and still leave a residual in a person's life that won't wash out with sea salts. You learn that your heart is not a government property. It won't be scheduled and labeled and filed. It remains messy.

Years pile up and the paperwork multiplies like lichens on stone. I filled out forms about personnel, about yields, about safety protocols. I signed my name in a dozen fonts and contexts. I have to say the government's ledger is very good at making people feel like cogs. A cog is neat because it fits; it's a small part of a machine that hums along and makes money. People who like machines love cogs. I like living things better. A living thing is messy. A living thing looks you in the eye and asks for explanations you don't always have. Inez doesn't do well with the word 'explain' when it comes to matters of the heart. She prefers 'behave' or 'stay.'

Do you remember the movie houses in those island towns? They had wooden benches and a can of popcorn that someone forgot to change out for months. We used to sit in those theaters and watch disasters upstairs on the newsreel while the local kids did their exercises on the beach. Somewhere between one reel and the next they would run a training film about fallout patterns and how to dig yourself a temporary shelter in a trench. It all had a neat, almost comedy timing to it. The training films were less dramatic than the newsreels and more prescriptive. You watched them and suddenly felt like your life could be bullet-pointed. This might be the reason some men went into policy. Bullet points are easier to keep than lives.

There was an old man on one occasion, a fisherman, who came to the bar while we were waiting for the weather. He had a face like a map made before maps, all curls of age around the mouth and wind lines. He asked me some question about the shot without naming it. Men who have lived with the elements a long time develop a vocabulary for things the rest of us call 'tests'—they call them seasons, like you might call snow a season in upstate New York. We get a little arrogant with our language because we think it belongs to us, the educated vocabulary, the instruments, the math. The fisherman looked at me and said, 'You boys bring us a lot of noise.' He wasn't angry. He was practical. It sounded more like a diagnosis than a complaint. I told him we tried to be careful. He smiled a small smile and said that's the trouble with you boys—you try. There are some things you cannot mitigate with a nice phrase.

In all this, you carve out small, private rebellions. Mine was to learn the names of birds on each island. There was a kind of mockingbird on one atoll that had the most absurd call; it sounded like a telephone with a bad connection. I used to whistle back at it and sometimes it would answer me. Birds don't care about policies. They care about worms and the rhythm of tides. You keep bird lists like you keep anniversaries because both are an insistence that there is continuity beyond forms and briefings.

We had an algae bloom one summer in a lagoon which they blamed on climate cycles and then other things, and watchful men in suits took samples and argued about pH levels. Meanwhile the kids who lived nearby had to stop swimming there. The men in suits convened a conference and there were graphs and coffee. I tried to remember the fisherman's face because it's one of the few honest faces I recall. This is where the story becomes less theatrical and more domestic: a woman from the island came to me with her hands cupped like a bowl and said, 'Are my children's futures acceptable?' The question was the epitome of the infernal arithmetic. You don't have a ready answer for a human's hope budget. It isn't in any manual.

The nights after shots are the ones that stay with me. Not necessarily the brilliance of the event but the long unspooling you get after when the work is mostly done and the men wander back with small predations in their pockets and larger ones in their brains. People drink and tell stories that have low fidelity to truth but high fidelity to feeling. There was a man in Nevada who would tell a story about a hat that once flew off someone's head and circled the blast radius like a frantic insect. He told it so often it stopped being a hat and became an omen. We used to laugh, but the laughter had edges. Laughter is a proxy in that business. You lean on it when you can't lean on anything else.

When the lights finally dim in a testing theater, when the engineers have filed their stamps and the accountants have tallied yields, there's a private administration of grief. You walk among your own people and you feel how thin the skin is between 'we did a job' and 'we made trouble.' There are always veterans' groups and friendship associations and lunches where a man can pass a napkin to another with a look and say nothing at all and yet say everything. That's how you patch a life sometimes, with cheap linen and a sympathetic nod.

I told Inez about the time I almost quit. It was at night, after a test that the papers said was a 'clear success.' I walked away because I couldn't stand the cheer. People were clapping in the mess hall, the way men clap when they want to make noise to keep the quiet at bay. I sat down on a crate outside the main building and the waves were a soft thunder in the distance. I thought at that moment: what if I just go, get on a freighter and not come back? I imagined a life of small tasks, of washing one dish at a time and making the bed without thinking of anything that might ripple beyond my courtyard. I was almost at the end of my rope until that damn deckhand with the missing teeth came up and sat beside me and asked for a cigarette. We shared one, and he told me about a woman in Papeete who could fix radios with ribbons and swear like a navvy. He described her hands with such admiration that I remember thinking maybe my leaving would be an unkind thing. You leave sometimes because you're dramatic and you want to make a point. Sometimes you stay because you understand a small kindness will be the only thing that will outlast the generators.

She stirred her tea and said, 'You never told me about Papeete.' I gave a half laugh, a sign that maybe I had been pruning my past to make it fit. She knew enough to let me be deliberately incomplete. There are some confessional acts you only commit once.

We have a habit of chopping our lives into stories to live better with them. When you have a string of jobs built around explosions and blueprints, you begin to appreciate a narrative that ends with a punchline rather than with an accounting. I like to end things with a laugh because a laugh is a kind of mercy for everyone. Maybe that's why I tell these Pacific stories as if they were funny—if you put on a decent cadence people will listen. The truth is rarer: everything had its price. The sky looked like God and sometimes that was the closest thing you'd have to a revelation, except revelation is too religious a word and what we practiced was more a mechanical seeing. We saw and recorded. We measured and then moved on.

The other morning she found an old envelope under the armchair. You know the kind; envelopes survive like cockroaches. Inside was a note I had written once and never given to anyone. I must have been feeling dramatic, because I wrote in the way men sometimes write when they think honesty will pin them like butterflies. It said, 'If I disappear someday in a puff of bureaucracy, know that I loved you like a gardenia—fragile, fragrant, and learned in salt.' She read it aloud and then laughed and then cried the same way she had when we were young and I'd come home smelling of diesel and sacrificial heat. She called those years 'our postmarked life.' It seemed to suit us.

We make our apologies in small gestures now. She buys extra lemons when they are cheap; I keep the porch light on when she goes out to pick up the paper. We have rites. Rites are what hold you together when the big things fall away. Sometimes she says, 'You could have quit then. We would have done a different life.' And other times I say, 'Don't tempt me with a life having been different. You wouldn't like the man who would have been one who left.' Both statements are modest evasions.

At night I watch the horizon from our kitchen window and try to remember exactly how pink the sky really was on Johnston. Memory softens like old fabric. You don't recall the ferocity of a color so much as the way it made your chest compress with the scale of it. Maybe that's what God feels like to a man who has seen too much light—an emphatic smallness. It makes you humble if you can stand it. Humility is underrated. It doesn't make books or generals, but it keeps you awake and kinder at small hours.

Sometimes I have dreams now of ladders descending into water. I haven't braved the psychology of ladders yet; it's a symbol I avoid. In the dream there's always that pink sky at dawn, and a voice—maybe mine—saying, 'You don't need to go down.' I wake up angry because I like to think I earn my rest. But Inez is there and she pats the space beside her and I realize the rest has always been less earned and more parceled out by someone else's willingness to stay.

We do better now. Not in a heroic sense but in the practical one. We have a small yard and a lemon tree that gives stubborn fruits like it is keeping us honest. I try to keep a list of bird calls and fix whatever the lawnmower refuses to accept. She brings me soup and remembers things about my family that I have already forgotten. She'll correct me on the dates of things, and I let her because the dates don't stir me like the images do. When I close my eyes at night I still see the men forming a line like dominoes in the light; I also see a pair of hands that know how to sew a button back on without making the cloth pucker. You can hold two images in your mind at once. It's called living with complexity.

There are moments of regret that smell like burnt sugar and other times that have the dull metallic odor of a hospital. We are not melodramatic, we are not theatrical about our ailments. We have practical receipts for them. If someone asks what you want to be remembered for, it feels silly to say 'I made the world a better place.' Most of us will be remembered for small things: a recipe, a call, a song hummed badly. I would like to be remembered as someone who kept his hands to himself when the world demanded more, and who learned to give up a little grandeur in exchange for staying at a kitchen table and learning how to butter toast the right way.

I said this all to Inez partly because the younger men at the reserve—whenever I talk to them they have this urgent clarity like the sun—they asked me once if the light was 'like God.' They didn't expect an answer other than a weatherman's. I told them: it is an impressive thing to behold, but the real gods are the small ones who mend shoes and open the jars when the lid is tight. They didn't go for that, but some of them later told me it stayed with them anyway. Maybe it's the only theology I can offer and it was learned on the lap of a life that smelled like engine grease and talc.

She put down her teacup and folded her hands like a woman who knows prayer is more practical than idea. She said, 'Promise me one thing.' If you ask a question with the weight of 'promise' in it you better be prepared to be sincere because promises like that are small monuments. I asked, 'What's that?' I think I already knew. 'When you tell those stories, don't leave out the people.' She had that look again, the look that can rearrange the furniture of an argument with a single, perfectly placed comment. 'Tell them about the kids on the beach,' she said. 'Tell them about Rosa's soups. Tell them about the birds.' She wanted me to remember the things that didn't fit in the budgets.

I promised. I can't say promises always hold—people are porous—but I promised then because it felt like the right thing to do. Later that night I went to the fifth drawer of my desk, the one with the card pockets and an old pencil that once belonged to a man named Murray, the cat-man. I took out my camera negatives and packed them into an envelope. I wrote on the envelope with a pen that bled a little and labeled it, 'For when someone asks.' That way, if I ever go to a rest home or disappear into the folds of my own making, there will be something left behind that isn't a press briefing or yield table. It will be ugly and sentimental; it will smell of fish and sweat and lemon, and maybe a bird call or two. It will belong exactly where it should.

We finish our tea then, and the steam curls like small parentheses over the table. There's a quiet that doesn't feel like absence; it feels like a room with all the windows open to the same breeze that used to smell like gardenias on the islands. I don't know if the breeze is the same—perhaps it's just the memory working overtime—but I lean back in my chair and feel like the world is the size of a card table and also the size of an ocean. I suppose that's acceptable.

'You always go on,' Inez said, smiling in a way that insisted upon no more. 'That's because you keep finding more to say,' I told her. We did not speak for a while. The kind of silences that can hold the weight of entire nations are the ones we practice. Then she hummed a line of something I had not heard in years and it turned the evening into a different kind of theater. There's no curtain now, only the steady sound of the road and the light from the kitchen that makes the spoon look like a moon.

When the sky outside finally dimmed into that predictable domestic blue I was not thinking of policies or bulletins. I thought of spoon divots in ceramic, of the way bunions can be a map to a life of walking, of how small revenues of mercy accumulate into days. If there is a summation, it's this: you can see God in a bright explosion, if you must. Or you can see him in the way a woman will slide your worn sock into the washer without complaint. Either way, you're probably going to end up making tea and sweeping the porch. That is the work. That is the reckoning. We do it badly sometimes and well other times. And no amount of pink sky will change how human you end up being when the lights go down.
