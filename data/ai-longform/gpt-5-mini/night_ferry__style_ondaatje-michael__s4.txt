I learned the shape of the strait by listening to it. At night it spoke in a low continuous language, a single vowel that moved under the ferry like a great turning of pages. The boat's engines hummed in my teeth. I had a cup of bad coffee, a paper cup, and the deck was a ring of indifferent lights. The air tasted of iron and salt and a faint diesel that made everything feel embalmed, neat. There were other passengers—two figures wrapped in blankets, a woman in a fur hat who read by a small light, a man in rubber boots who smoked as if each cigarette needed to be finished before arrival—but mostly there was the sound of water and the long slow shudder of the ship as it crossed into the other province, into the other time of a place that had been waiting for a body.

The call came in the small hours, as calls that change you always do, when the world is asleep and you are a vessel with one thing going through you. I answered because my hand trembled on the bedside table and the phone slid into my palm like a living thing. It was a nurse, or a woman who used the word "coroner" as if that gave her authority. "We have someone," she said. "A body. Possibly yours." The pronoun held like a bait. I sat, and the light from the kitchen clock made a small cold square on the floor. It was not my body. I said nothing. I thought of the brother I had not been able to find for years, the brother who drifted out of everything, and I told the woman I would come.

There are practical things you learn when someone asks you to identify a body. You pack a bag with the casual ritual of leaving—toothbrush, a clean shirt, a pen. I packed a paper photograph in a waterproof sleeve, an old Polaroid of two boys on a dock, their hair blistered by salt, smiles that had nothing to do with the men we became. They were sitting on the same planks, knees touching, one leaning into the other. It is ridiculous what a photograph can do; it draws you toward what it remembers, fixes you with the accuracy of a mirror. I folded the photograph inside a shirt. I tucked my hands over it as if it could be warmed.

The strait is a country of misprisions, a place of half-truths and accepted losses. There are fishermen who won't tell you the depth of the channels, and there are tides that run like secret trains. On this crossing, the ferry's wake laced the water with a silver thread that unraveled slowly in the dark. I watched the line recede and thought of the lines we leave in other people's lives—less visible, though sometimes more monstrous. The horizon was a band of near-black. Clouds, if they made sense at all, were like torn paper glued to a ceiling. I kept remembering how, when we were boys, we used to watch the horizon for ships as if any arrival might change us.

I had not seen him in five years. Once, in the late afternoons of our childhood, we believed that absence could be measured like a distance on a map: an ocean, two towns, a place called performance or failure. He left because he wanted to be elsewhere; he left because there was a woman; he left because he took a job on a trawler and it swallowed him. He left without a final word, and what remained were the small gestures that that absence exaggerated into guilt. When you have an estrangement, you find ways to keep it polished: you place blame as if it were a coin under a glass. I had told myself the reasons why, like an inventory. There was a fight, yes. There was the woman he loved and I didn't approve of, or perhaps he married her and I didn't approve of the wedding. There was an inheritance that failed to manifest. There was the terrible coin of cowardice. But when the possibility of seeing him in a body was placed before me like a clinical test, my reasons became private objects that did not quite hold.

On the ferry there was a small library of things I had been told about death: "You should come," the nurse had said, "even if you doubt it. The family wants to be sure." The coroner's office used the word "family" with the solicitousness of someone adjusting a blanket. In small towns the town and the family are nearly the same thing; they are networks, rope ladders, gossip. I thought of my mother, who had been dead ten years. She had once said that men who go out to sea go away in pieces. She said it as though it were a blessing. She thought of the sea as a cleanser, perhaps because it could remove blood and hair and other inconvenient things. I imagined her sitting very straight in a kitchen in which the only light was the weak winter sun. She would have lifted her hands and folded them as if showing me how to wait.

I spent the crossing in little rituals—taking my watch off and then putting it on again, as if to check that the strap still fit, as if to know time could be measured; laughing with a man from the ferry office about the fare; watching a television in the cafeteria that played a documentary with subtitles I could not read. The ship had a smell of old fries and plastic and someone else's perfume. It was comforting. The night pressed against the windows, a thick black cloth. Occasionally another ferry would cross us, a mirror image with its own small history of cargo and silence.

At some point I found my way to the rail. The wind cut at my face and made my eyes water. In the breath between one wave and the next I heard the precise memory of the brother's voice saying a single sentence—something ordinary he must have said the last time we spoke—and it was delivered in a tone I had not expected, which made me realize how much of his tone I had kept folded in my mind like a shirt in a suitcase. The ferry's horn spoke at long spacing. The voice of the ocean answered.

When I landed it was nearly dawn. The smell of the town clung like a damp shawl—tar, baking bread, woodsmoke. Small lights winked in rows. The harbor was a crush of dark shapes—masts like broken spines. A man from the coroner's office—thin, with a scarf tied so tightly it seemed to be part of his face—waited on the quay. He had the patient's economy of speech. He drove through streets that I remembered only as an outline, as if memory had rusted the signs and left only jars of light.

The hospital and the coroner's office sat back from the water as if they were cautiously avoiding it. The morgue smelled faintly of antiseptic and flowers. There were two wreaths at the door as if some small bureaucratic compassion had been practiced on behalf of the unknown. The nurse I had spoken to smiled in a way that was meant to be comforting and failed. Her name badge said "Lena." She held the necessary cruel things like a caretaker in a chapel: the forms, the thin clipboard, the list of possible relatives. I signed things with the same flatness one uses for a receipt. On the table next to the clipboard was a package of cigarettes someone had left, still warm with the idea of hands that had just existed.

They brought the body into a small room with a single bed under a lamp. The lamp made a halo on the sheet like the last light of an altar. The room was bare except for a chair, a small table, and a basin. The coroner stood to the side with porcelain patience. She had a woman's hands—short, precise. She had the look of someone who had learned that grief is not always what you expect: sometimes it is a succession of small acts—opening a drawer, picking up a watch, turning a ring.

"Are you ready?" she asked.

I said that I was. I think I said it because I wanted to prove something to myself: that the man who had walked away could be confronted by me, that I could find the horizon again and name it. The sheet was folded very carefully. There was a taste of formal ritual in the way the flannel was laid back: like opening a present that you did not want to find, or closing a book you had not finished reading.

The face was not the face I remembered. It had been softened by sleep or by something the coroner used—apparatus that made the skin more generous. There were changes that horror stories never prepare you for: the small collapsing of features, the way the mouth parted slightly as though the person had been taken mid-breath. The hair had receded around the temples in a way I had not expected. There were bruises, an absence of color like a kind of map. On the chest there was a jacket—a dark, worn jacket with a cigarette burn near the collar. I touched the fabric with a gentleness that felt ridiculous, as if the jacket might scold me for being too clean-handed. It smelled faintly of Old Spice and smoke. It smelled, unpredictably, like my childhood summers.

I remembered a jacket like that, or perhaps it was only an idea of a jacket in my head. We all carry prototypes of our people's clothing. My brother had once worn a jacket the color of midnight and he had a patch sewn on the elbow, a small replacement for what he had lost at school. He used to wear it in winter, leaning into the wind on the boarding house porch, his cigarette glowing in the dark like a single star. He smoked too much because it gave him an excuse to pause. He paused in the middle of conversation. He paused in the middle of leaving.

"I don't know," I said, which is another way of saying I was trying to be honest and failing. I had expected that seeing him would either break me open or calm me down. Instead it placed a small, blistering question over my chest: Who had I loved—my brother or my idea of him?

The coroner told me about the wound in the language of someone who has learned to wrap violence in thin gloves. "He had been found on the rocks two days ago," she said. "There was severe exposure. It's likely he went into the water. There were public calls. Someone phoned when tides turned." The public are patient with bodies; they use the sea as if there were a trash box they could fling things into. There were small ripples of gossip through the hospital. Lena had been an instrument, she said, of a person who had recognized him by a detail: the ring on the index finger, a plain band. "It had engraving," she said. "Two initials."

I leaned forward as if the initials might be scratched into the skin, as if the ring might still heat to the touch. I wondered if the ring belonged to my brother he had previously removed it, or given it to someone else in a momentary burst of affection that had nothing to do with me. Maybe it had been from an old lover, from a wife who had left, from a child he had never told me about. Identification in small towns has a way of folding in other people: other lovers, other debts.

His hands were folded over his stomach. They were small and veined as if they had held nets. The skin was like maps of rivers under tracing paper. One knuckle bore a white crescent where an old scar had healed—something I knew from a childhood injury where one had stood and watched the other bleed and no one had called a doctor. We had been improvised surgeons then, who used grit and whispers. I touched the knuckle cautiously and felt the solidity of bone. It was as if the hands could tell me more about him than the face could.

Objects do the remembering that people avoid. The coroner unfolded a wallet: a bus pass, two photographs of a woman with a cigarette and more photographs of the man himself, younger, and one with him sitting on a small motorbike. There was a small card—"Joe's Café"—and a receipt for a motel across the strait. On the back of the receipt someone had scribbled a telephone number with a name I did not recognize. There was a matchbook with a logo from a bar where he liked to drink—a place I had not been to in years. Each object introduced a new corridor of memory, like opening a door and smelling the kitchen of a life you had not been allowed into.

I could not decide how I felt. It was indecisive grief that felt like the weather—cold, not yet rain. There is a particular weight to the task of identification: it is a litmus test for all the failures that come before. If you can say his name, you have reclaimed the past; if you cannot, you might be condemned to tidy regret. Recognize him. Admit that he belongs somewhere in your map. These are the small demands of the living.

"Would you like to see more?" the coroner asked. She wore a blue coat with a badge that said "CORONER" in small white letters. The badge was the size of a coin, the size of a promise. She had the efficient patience of someone who had seen this question posed by many shapes of grief. "You can take something. You can take a garment. You can take a finger print. You can take a photograph."

I asked if I could take his watch. It seemed then the right thing to do, ridiculous or sanctified. Watches in our family had always been dangerous. Our father had a watch that he kept on a chain and only removed for special occasions. My brother had taken that watch once and kept it for a summer. Watches tell stories about how people are shaped by time. When the coroner rolled back the sleeve, the watch lay against a white wrist, its face scratched but intact. It was a heavy thing with a leather band, its hands stubborn, one of them stuck at a quarter past three. I had no idea whether that was the time he died, or the time he last felt the urge to check it. I lifted it with fingers that trembled and felt the lock of a tiny hinge, the small dignity of clasps.

"You're sure?" the coroner asked.

"I'm not sure of anything," I said. That was closer to the truth. I wanted to collect something that might belong to him, to have an object like a ledger with his name in a handwriting I could hold. I wanted a thing that made him more solid. Or maybe I wanted to hold the watch because it had belonged to our father, and taking it felt like reclaiming a small trophy of family history.

When I walked back to the small room they led me to, with humming fluorescent lights and a mandated courtly silence, I could feel the town pressing against me new. Outside the window the mornings opened like a small gull's wings. People were arriving—mourners, workers, the fisherfolk who knew the sea as a second skin. I drank a coffee that tasted of pause. I looked at the photograph in my pocket and discovered that when you look at a boy in an image you have the quick, selfish power of choosing what he was. The Polaroid was saturated now with the event I had just witnessed. I compared the face of the boy to the man and found the distances from one to the other where they had shifted.

There is something about brotherhood that resists closure. Brothers are, in the old stories, thieves of identity. They borrow faces, trade insults, repair each other's clothes. We had been thieves and builders in equal measure. When we were children we made a boat out of boards that were not ours and painted it blue. We called it freedom. It lasted a summer. The boat would not keep us safe but it taught us how to fall into the water and climb back. It taught us that the shore remained patient even when you made mistakes.

The man who had been my brother had been a different kind of builder. He built a life that refused our mother and refused the town, a life of sudden addresses and names of streets that were always a little beyond what I could find on a map. Over the years there were letters that grew thin with neglect and telephone calls that landed like stones in a pond. The long silence made the sound of his voice a memory that could be turned to any tune. I had every reason to be angry and many reasons to be forgiving. In the end, these moral inventories get filed under "insufficient funds."

They allowed me to sit with the watch in my palm. It felt reassuring, as if somehow time could be kept in one place. I placed it on the table and watched the hands. The hour hand moved as if sampled from an older tempo. There was a small smear on the glass, perhaps where his thumb had rested. I rubbed it carefully with the sleeve of my shirt and felt shame for this simple, inadequate act of cleaning an absence.

There were small faces in the corridor—people who knew the brother I had not known, who had a kindness that assumed he belonged to them now. A woman I later learned was a neighbor brought a box of knitted socks and left them on a chair with a note in curling handwriting. There were men in oilskins who had been dragged, once a week, by the hard work of nets, and who had the curt manner of men who had no time for elegies. They nodded and said things like "Been a rough season," as if the seasons could explain everything.

When it came to identifying him officially, there was the required formality: name, date of birth, relationship. I wrote what I knew, naming him in full like a litany, as if naming could be a kind of permission to grieve. The paper felt small and inadequate, and I thought perhaps this was what formal grief required: a signature to make it real. I signed my name, my full name, and in doing so admitted the thing we both had been too stubborn to say: that he existed.

Later, in the narrow car park outside the hospital, a man came up to me who had watched me go in and out. He introduced himself as someone who had sometimes seen my brother at the bar. "He had a laugh," he said. "A laugh like a crow." He was trying to make me remember the laugh and did it in the present tense as if to summon the sound into the day. He told me that the brother had been there the week before and told stories about a place he had been. "You look like you're carrying something." He smiled, but his smile had the guardedness of someone who had known the brother had been a secret.

It's strange how strangers can be more intimate than family. The coroner, for instance, with her measured speech, had known a lot about him: his financial troubles, the time he had broken a broom across a barroom table, the call he had made from a hospital two towns away to ask if his mother was still alive. Sometimes those third-party accounts are the truest things, because they are not mired in the private mythologies we erect to justify absence.

The funeral was a small, practical thing that the town arranged without fuss. There was the music you expect—a hymn that had little to do with the man but large with the community—and a short reading by a woman who had once worked with him at a fish-processing plant. There was a vase with flowers that were not particularly original. There were the usual speeches: recollections of kindness, a dry insertion of humor. People said the right words. I sat, and somewhere under my ribs, a small object that might have been a heart or a stone shifted.

I thought of the night we sat on our roof as boys and watched a meteor streak through their sky. We made wishes, but we never spoke them aloud. There was that unsaid contract between us then, which was a promise to protect each other even if the shape of protection changed into something else. There had been a betrayal later, hard and hot like a matchstick dropped on oil. But the old scaffolding remained. There are loyalties in men that you can't reason away. You will not find them in logic. You feel them like hunger.

After the funeral, a woman approached me quietly. Her name was Carol. She had been his partner, if partner in the way that small towns can render enormous intimacies into the format of "partner" or "friend." She had a necklace she had never taken off and an old coat stitched at the collar. She told me, in a voice that had been smoothed by weeks of crying, that he had left a note in a coat pocket. "I didn't look at it," she said. She only said that because she thought I might read it more kindly than she could. "I thought it might be his to give." She brought out a scrap of paper and drove a fingernail along the line where the ink had bled.

The note said very little. It was the kind of illicit thing one writes rather than speaks. "Forgive me," it said—but there was no signature. The smallest thing in the whole world is a word that belongs to no one. I kept the scrap folded between pages in a book I had brought, a book of poems I hardly remembered. The scrap was the most solid confession and the least; it held the appearance of contrition without explaining.

In the days that followed I walked the water's edge a lot. There is a particular sound the tide makes when it finds the rocks: a small tremor in the chest. I would place my palm on the stone and imagine the brother's hand in the place of mine. These are silly things to narrate, but human beings are small machines of ritual. You walk the shore, you look for injury, you map the landscape of the person you have lost. I imagined him alive, tied to someone else's story, holding a pen to make grocery lists, trading barbs, falling asleep in chairs. I also imagined him out on the water at night, as the tide took him, thinking of something that had nothing to do with me.

The coroner told me afterward that sometimes identification is not about certainty but about reconciliation. "You don't need to know everything to know him," she said, in a tone that suggested she had been married once. She meant in the way a priest might mean. Sometimes faith substitutes for knowledge. You lean on the little pieces you have and build a shelter.

Some nights, years ago, we had both re-enacted the other. In the way you can act, we had been roleplayers. I would pretend to be him to understand how he moved through the world. He would do the same. We were trying on each other's skins. It is a dangerous game because you may find you prefer the other's fit. In later life, we had stopped playing. This was maybe where it began to fray, where small pretenses harden into reasons.

I kept the watch for months. It sat on my bedside table like an accusation and a comfort. The hands did not move, and I liked that because it was a small memorial to a pause. Sometimes I would wake and expect the wrist to be warm. Sometimes I would hold it and press it to my chest as if by leaning on it I could receive a pulse. At night I would index the hours against the sound of the wind. Time keeps you honest. Time also allows for the rewiring of guilt.

One afternoon I received an envelope with a photograph in it. The photographer was someone who had been at the funeral. He said he had been taking pictures of the town and thought the lights on the ferry at night were beautiful. In the photograph my brother was inside the bar, his face half-obscured by smoke, a laugh in his eyes. The camera had a kindness to it. I could see the way the light bent around the jaw, the crease by the ear where he had once pressed his cheek against mine in a moment so small and tender that it registered like a medical procedure: you know you will remember it for the rest of your life once you have been inoculated by it.

People kept giving me things: a scarf, a letter, a small book of recipes he had loved. They were ways of making the life be more than its end. The town had an economy of gifts. You showed yourself by giving something back. Somewhere a ledger was being balanced. My name appeared in it as one of the living who would now be responsible for this man's memory.

In the quiet places of my days afterward, I would think about what I did not say. There are stacks of words I never used: apologies, questions, small directions like "take care." I had built a life that allowed me to avoid asking for forgiveness. To reach for him after he was dead felt like cheating and like mercy. I would go to the place in the harbor where we had left the little boat and look at the water. The tide had long since reclaimed it.

One morning in late spring I took the watch down to the water's edge. I held it over the strait and stared at the blank face. Somewhere beneath the glass the two hands seemed to agree on a time and refuse to move. I left it in the palm of a wall and went to the edge. The wind was cold and fierce. The watch felt small in my hand, like a relic. I considered tossing it into the sea as if to locate my brother there once more—give him the watch for better company. The sea is unfair in its generosity sometimes; it returns things, sometimes, in unexpected ways. But I kept the watch.

There is a certain cruelty to endings. A body is a last authority on a life. It cannot bear the nuance. It cannot hold a history that says "I regret" without becoming an argument. It is, in some ways, the last stubborn denial of narrative. It refuses to explain itself. You stand before it and you must give it a name or let it remain the anonymous thing that the town contemplates and then forgets. I named him. I said, in the small office where I had been led months ago, "Yes, it's him." The word was a key and a blade. It opened doors and closed others. It allowed the town to shift its attention and my mother in her grave to exhale.

What identification does is make your duty like a bell. It rings and other people come. They bring roses and casseroles and years of hearing our story told in ways they have invented. They retell it as if in doing so they prolong some kind of presence. At night I would lie awake and listen to the house breathe. The watch would sit on the bedside table, a small dry thing that refused to change its mind.

On the ferry the next time I crossed, months afterward, the weather was kinder. The water was a plain that the light had ironed. I watched the line of the wake and thought, as I often did now, of the small things that remain: a watch, a photograph, a scrap of a letter that said "forgive me." Forgiveness is not a single action. It is a muscle you use until it forgets how to ache.

I have imagined him in a hundred small domestic ways: stirring coffee, repairing a net, laughing at a joke. They are fictions, in the way that any form of love is always partly invention. I have also imagined him when the tide took him—how the water could have been kind or merely indifferent. In the end both cruelty and grace sit in the same boat. I told the coroner once that I was grateful for the careful manner in which she spoke, for the way she always used "we" as if the town and I and the dead had the same loyalties. She smiled and said that it was a professional necessity. Perhaps it was also something like pity.

Crossing the strait in the evening, you see a long bright smudge where the city lights smear across the water. It looks like a bruise. When I look at it now, I think of the bruise we left on one another when we were small. I think of how the bruise faded and sometimes reappeared, as memory tends to bruise you anew. There are lines you cannot untie.

I do not know if whatever precise act of identification I performed gave my brother back to me or merely cued the community to perform its necessary rituals. Perhaps both. Perhaps neither. I keep the watch in a box and sometimes wind it to keep the hands alive, like an old mechanism that protests at being still. It keeps a time I invented, which is another way of saying that memory is a clock that we set ourselves.

When you are young you assume you will have many quarrels left to settle. When you are older you see that quarrels are a narrow river and the rest of your life is a slow sea. You learn how to live with the ways people vanish. There is loss and its twin, absence, and somewhere between them the world keeps its great machinery of tide and harvest. You make your choices in the small moments—whether to answer a call in the night, whether to take a ferry, whether to touch a shoulder with a hand that remembers how the world used to be.

I know now that identification is not closure. It is recognition of a shared fact. It does not absolve the present of its loneliness. But it does make space where people can tell stories without interrupting one another. It made space for a woman to say, at the wake, "He once fixed my radio for me," and to receive a chorus of approving nods. It allowed the man in the oilskins to tell the story of how my brother always gave the best of his catch to a stray dog. These small acts accumulate like shells on a beach. They become the loose history of a life.

On my desk is a photograph of two boys on a dock. The sun flattened them into bright cones. Their hair is full and alive. Their hands are on their knees. In the years since I have kept the photograph like a map. I am fond of maps that do not need to be precise, those that insist on showing only the contours that matter. The strait taught me that. It taught me that water is a kind of memory, that boats have names and are sometimes forgotten, that the horizon keeps on moving even when you stand still.

I do not go back to the town much. But sometimes, when the weather is kind and the ferry's wake is a neat seam, I find myself standing on some anonymous deck with a cup of bad coffee and thinking that there are not many things we can recover. We make small economies out of memory. We salvage what we can. We keep an old watch wound, or a ring that will not fit any hand, or a photograph of two boys who have the universe between them.

The sea keeps speaking in its low vowel. The ferry's engines hum. The wake unspools itself as if the world were a spool of thread. There is a narrow pleasure in being permitted to move across it, to carry an object from one shore to another and decide where it will rest. I am allowed to choose who I will believe in, in the small bright decisions of the day. I chose, finally, to say his name. I made that sound the name of the man who had one chest like a boat and another like a house. Saying his name was an act of permission. It did not soften the hard fact that he was gone, but it gave the town something to hold.

Sometimes, in bed, when I wake in the small hours, I listen for the sound of somebody climbing the stairs, for the small scrape that meant he had come home. It never comes. But the watch sits on the bedside table and tells its slow, deliberate lie: that time is a thing we can wind again if we choose.
