The reservoir was a conversation she had not planned to rejoin. For twenty years my job was straight lines and angles, the patient geometry of county plots, a tidy record of what belonged where. We measured roads and argued with developing boots and cut through bureaucratic thickets until things sat on paper as if they'd been always meant to be there. Then the sky changed, the soil went hungry, and the county called me back to a place I had thought I would never need to map again.

When I turned off the highway the air thickened as if someone had closed a door behind me. Heat rose in a quiver over the asphalt and the cottonwoods trembled but did not move; their leaves had already learned to do less and not ask for more. The road to the dam ran straight as a ruler. I kept the truck in low gear, the instrument case safe on the passenger seat like a child, and watched the world shrink: the low bank with its ring of stones, the picnic tables with their backs baked into sugar—these small, honest things that suggested the reservoir had simply been a larger pond until someone decided to make it a lake.

They had made it in sections, I had been told as a child. It ate the low places first, in an appetite as careful as a sculptor's. Houses were moved, some with their foundations jacked up like dentures; some were left with only chimneys to hold witness. The county pamphlets called it "easement" or "reclamation," but the verbs never fully captured the way everything that had been lived in grew small or absent overnight. There were photographs of the day the final sluice gate was opened: men with hard hats and wide eyes at the crest of the dam, water rushing in like applause. At the edges, roofs and steeples stuck out like islands. For years the drowned town lived as a half-story children told one another; in deeper droughts you could walk the streets that had become the lakebed and find a piano or a child's shoe or a sink with rusted taps.

I parked on a stretch of cracked clay and dragged the instrument case to the lip, my boots scuffing paper-thin sediment. I had not been back since I had been small enough to need the hand of whoever held my wrist to cross the kitchen one last time, since anyone measured out the last pieces of a life. As a child I had last stood on the dam with my father, small enough to fit—then, now I would space my steps more carefully. I carried a laptop, a GPS receiver, a total station that could fix a point with numbers that meant nothing to my stomach. I also carried a field notebook, the sort of book overstuffed with little humiliations—pens that ran out and ink smudges and the small corrections that made the bigger plans legible. The notebook belonged to me in the old way, the way a person might keep an animal in a pocket: alive, at hand, a pivot of habit.

It was a precise mission. The county commission had authorized me to chart the old township for a report on submerged property lines—an administrative chore thinly lit by a historical grant that allowed me to call this more than clerical. My task was to map the streets now visible where water had held their names below for sixty years. We needed to know where foundations lay, where the concrete sidewalks had cracked, where an orchard's pattern persisted in ragged trunks. The work required distance and attention. It required walking lines and counting joints. It was the kind of intimacy I had always practiced, though never with a place that had been my child's name.

I stepped onto the exposed bed and the soft dust made a sound like cloth being unrolled. There were lines there I could almost see without the theodolite: parallel seams of old street, paths of packed gravel that refused to be forgotten. Old curbs rose like lip bones from beneath the silt, pale as bones bleached on a shore. Chimneys stuck up in crooked constellations, their bricks worked into quiet obelisks. Once I had read a book that told me how chimneys were the last defenders of old houses, the flues holding the memory of smoke; these chimneys were all that remained of entire wardrobes of rooms. Some were wrapped in lichen, others in algae, like the town had weathered a century of different waters and was still clearing its throat.

There are measures that feel like tenderness, and I treat them as such. I set the tripod on an old place where the street had been called Main, smoothed its prongs into frozen mud, and watched as the total station came to life. Its little screen blinked and began to align with a grid I had not yet mapped. I set my blank field sheet on my knee and began to mark points: “Chimney – 2.5m from curb, 0.9m north of manhole.” The ritual steadied me. I recorded coordinates the way a priest may record names: to declare that something existed even if the rest of the world had gone to sleep.

I had been born in that town, the county's records said. My mother told me this when I was old enough to hold my own bowl of cornmeal at the table and ask whatever came to mind. She told me how the house had a bay window and how she had learned to make bread there, how the pantry was a room that smelled like pockets of late summer. When the notices came about the rising water and the new reservoir, they sorted things in schedules and boxes and dates, and they told people where to be and when. My father drove a pickup and kept his hands in the right places on the wheel. He took our family to the river in the last week before the inundation as an excuse to picnic, to see what would be drowned. I remember a thin wind and an unfamiliar sense that everything we had fit in the bed of a truck could be transferred into the ground: rugs, photographs, a highboy dresser. I remember a neighbor who cried because the apple trees had to stay. My mother cried in the house because you are allowed to cry only once or twice, and it was better that she allowed all of it then, so later there would be none in the eye.

The boy who sold fireworks on County Road 12 had tied his business to the old post office at the edge of town. That post office had been a long rectangle with a flagpole and a floor that creaked in a particular way, and now its foundation lay in a trough of mud, an impression of wooden beams like grooves on a tongue. There was a rusted sign attached to a crooked post that leaned at an angle the town remembered as off-kilter: the letters peeled away, a pale negative of what had been printed. In the silt I found a scattering of things that had been swallowed: the turned hinge of a lock, a battered spoon, a child's marbles transformed into little planetary orbs of dried glass. I lifted a small object shaped like a crescent and wiped the silt with the hem of my shirt. It was a button, mother-of-pearl and cracked, warmed to the touch by the afternoon as if it had swallowed the sun. My fingers left prints in the mud that were soon erased by the wind.

I mapped the schoolhouse with a care that felt like apology. The frame of the building had been wooden, low-ceilinged, with a bell that the boys rang because boys could not help themselves and the sound was big enough to reach the hills. Now the bell's anchor lay exposed, a ring of rust as large as a palm, and the foundation revealed the pattern of where desks had sat. At one threshold the concrete held a child's name, carved in small handset, letters uneven and proud: L. Hoskins, July 1949. I stooped and set my instrument to focus, squared a line with the door-sill of what had been the principal's office, and measured until my numbers sat like small facts in my notebook. The sun moved crosswise to the old blackboard, and I imagined the smug scrawl of chalk and a teacher with her wrists rolled up over an attendance book.

You can arrange a place on paper and call it a map and the map will lie and be true at the same time. In my ledger I could draw the block of Main and perpendicular lines that once held hardware stores and a beauty parlor and a saloon that was all thrift and smell. In my head the streets still had names shouted at their corners. In the mud-still air the wind made a sound like cloth on a line. I could not tell whether the town existed now in the same way I had known it — as a litany of small, certain things — but when I set the tripod down and aligned the optical sight with a chimney, I felt, with a subtle insistence, that I was putting a hand where one had once been and saying, gently, you belong here.

A heron lifted from the skeleton of a willow and circled over the exposed lot with the weary confidence of birds that had seen towns changed before. Its shadow sliced our patch of afternoon. Near where the hardware store had stood I uncovered a length of copper piping and a door hinge that still remembered its screws. There were tiles of the house kitchens—little clay squares, blue and yellow, and one with a cat's head stamped on it. I stopped, crouched, and the cat's face was almost a joke, a grin like something someone had left on purpose for the future. I made a note: “Tile w/cat motif – consider historical preservation.” We preserve what is easy to preserve, I thought. We find objects and make them icons of loss so we can carry grief in a neat case.

I had an engineer's instinct to triangulate. Lines become truth if they cross. I measured the well in the center square that used to be a green for Fourth of July picnics; its rim was half-buried and still ringed with the iron bolt where the pump had been. Along the outline of what had once been the Anderson house, I found the pattern a life leaves on the earth: a small arc of rust that had held a swing, a scatter of ceramic that had been a picnic setting. In the living room there was the outline of a hearth that the water had not wholly surrendered, and in that hearth, embedded in the silt like a fossil, lay something that turned my stomach and tightened my hands into gloves of remembering. It was a small tin locket, its hinge stiff, its surface eaten by time but stubbornly intact. Inside, beneath some powder, there were two black-and-white halves of a photograph: a child's eyes, round as coins; a woman's jaw, defiant; someone who had smiled for the camera at some long-ago fair.

I carried the locket close to my chest like contraband. It did not belong to anyone I immediately recognized, and there is something almost indecent about claiming a stray relic. Yet I must have expected that the past would be a neat parcel: a name, a face, a suitable sorrow. The locket was not mine and yet it was a usability of my own past, an artifact from a time when people arranged heavy things into patterns and believed in their own permanence. I traced the rim with a forefinger until the metal warmed and the little photograph softened under my touch. There were no names writ inside. I put it in my pocket and went on walking, leaving imprints of boot on the old street, the city of my childhood stepping in my tracks as if waiting to be hired again.

The town had been ordinary in its ordinariness. I had always thought ordinary places were the kind no one loved theatrically; they were loved in the slow ways that make grand gestures unnecessary. There was a church that used to do potlucks, and the smell from those dinners was, in my memory, a perfume of cabbage and dense pies. The church foundation sat high where it had been, a good spot to look out over the valley. I measured the pad where the steeple had anchored; the steeple had punctured the sky in my childhood memory like a punctuation mark. On the ledger, I wrote down the angle of the sun and the pitch of the roof as if I might reconstruct the way it had hung against weather. Standing there I felt the tilt of long rows of pews and the weight of a hymn held a little too long, as if someone had been reluctant to let it go.

It is strange how memory will choose the edges to cling to: the chipped china of a grandmother's dish rather than the way she held her hands when she braided my hair. When I stepped into the old school cellar, my soles sank a little and the smell of wet earth and something like dried apples made me lean a little to the left. A beetle skittered across a sliver of sunlight like a note. On the wall, someone had left a scratch—a child's mark where the silt had not covered completely—and the numerals scratched there were a kind of diary. I ran a gloved finger across them. The numbers were shallow and old, a date perhaps: 1957. They were the date of a summer in which everything looked possible and the town had not yet been given away to a new geography.

An old man appeared on the horizon as if the land itself had coughed someone up. He walked with a cane, his frame as thin as a weathered stick, and when I saw him, for a moment I thought he might be one of the ghosts the kids in town used to tell one another about—the salt-skin of a memory come to shore. He had come in a truck with a Mary-Kay umbrella and a can of coffee like an offering. He introduced himself as Mr. Benson and told me, without preamble, that he had been a boy here when the men came with the concrete trucks, when the nailers and the surveyors and the county men drew lines in the dirt and signed their names like verbs. He said the dam had been necessary and that the town had been "in the way" of progress. He coughed and then said that his father had been buried in the cemetery that they had had to move. "They moved the stones," he said, and then corrected himself, "They moved some stones." He looked at me, a small inquest in the way an old weathercock looks for wind.

He asked what I was doing, and I told him I was mapping. He sat down on an upended bit of concrete and watched me hold the prism of the station steady. The old man had lived his whole life as a neighbor of the water and its moods. He told stories that had not been told before and had become electrical when said aloud: of the horse that had been fair but stubborn, of the summer that the blackberries were so plump the mice learned to have their own feasts. When he remembered the flood he did not say water as much as the word that had been used in the papers, "submergence," and he said it with the clinical air of someone observed. He told me the day he decided to go back to the lattice of streets and look for the cellar of the boarding house where his mother had once filed the milk bottles: "I thought I'd find the floor," he said, "and maybe a few jars. I didn't think I'd find the names." He paused as if names were a kind of treasure.

I asked him if he'd been here since the reservoir had been low before, and he said he had. "Every drought's a little kinder than the last," he said. "The silt takes its time settling. It gives you things you can carry." He waved his hand toward a stretch where the river had once done its paces; my mapping had to include him, because the work of this place included the people who could remember a person's best angle. I pointed toward the school and asked if he remembered L. Hoskins, and he closed his eyes as if the name was an old radio. "Sure," he said, and then he smiled in a way that made his face younger than the cane would have suggested. He told me small details I would not have known: the lunch bell was rung by the tallest boy, who liked to make a game of missing certain days. The schoolteacher would fold her letters in perfect squares and slip them into a sock drawer she kept for important things. "None of it came with instructions," Mr. Benson said, "but there was a way the town said how it wanted to be held."

When he got up to leave he did so slowly, and before he walked away he tied a small piece of red tape to a rusted nail on a chimney. "So they'll know someone was here," he said. I watched the tape flapping like a memory. Later, when I drew that spot on my map, I would write a little note: "Resident visit—symbolic marker." It seemed inadequate.

Mapping a drowned town forced me to reckon with what gets to be called public and what gets to be private. Boundaries are not simply lines on paper; they are the contours of what people can touch. Some homesteads were already under the water and would never be recovered. The county wanted exactitude: the exact placement of foundations, coordinates to decimals, latitudes that could later translate to a program on someone's screen. The grant wanted public interest and photographic records. A graduate student might write a paper about the sociological implications of displacement. A museum might hang a few black-and-whites on a wall with a placard that begins, "Before the reservoir..." And me, with a compass and an old locket in my pocket, I wanted to make peace with the way things had been named.

There are moments that fall like currency into your palm: an encounter with the past in a detail so small you could miss it. I found a pair of glasses in what had been a pharmacy's doorframe, corroded but with one lens still whole. Someone had once set them down on their nose and not picked them up again. The pharmacy counter, where the pharmacist had been an authoritative figure with a clean white apron and the slightly haughty air of someone who preferred order, now hummed with the silt's muffled music. Behind it I found, embedded in the mud like a dent in time, a child's placard for a school play: "The Little Engine." I held danger of sentimental seams that might tear under close examination, but I felt something akin to grace—an odd gratitude that these small objects, which had been trivial then and are central to us now because they remain, had not been fully erased.

I marked the saloon's threshold, measured the distance between a lamppost base and the closest chimney, and recorded the way the sediment had scalloped the detail of the sidewalks. Sometimes the numbers became a kind of eulogy: 12.34 m, 7.88 m, coordinates with the gravity of a name. They obliged the town to exist for the sake of being plotted on the county's geometric ledger. I recorded what I could and spoke to the bricks the way one speaks to someone who will not answer. Do you know you are inscribed? I wrote down parameter numbers and then paused. The algebra of the place—the numbers, the lines, the angles—was neat, as if mourning could be handled with the calm of a surveyor's hand. But as the sun slid westward and the shadows lengthened like an apology, the geometry felt brittle.

That night I camped on the high bank and laid my notes across the hood of the truck. The stars were very old and steady. I opened the field notebook and rewrote sections that had been smudged; I drew cross-sections and added the little notations that an instrument could not. Under the flashlight's cone the lines became a restless city. I turned over the little locket and peered at the picture again. I did not know why it had felt requisite to take it; some measure demanded an answer. If a person could carry away a small thing, perhaps the town would forgive the theft. Perhaps my own history wanted to be recognized as part of a long continuum of losses, small and frequent.

When dawn came it came with a certain severity, a light that made the world more honest. I walked the grid in a different order, following an old habit I'd learned with my father: map in the morning where the shadows are shortest, because then nothing is obscured. I came to the place where our house used to be. The foundation remained as a rectangle of darker silt. I had no expectation to find the artifacts of our lives; the county had moved many things, and the movers had had no way to carry the intangible. The oak tree that used to mark our yard had been cut and its stump left to grieve. There was nothing in the foundation but a small pile of bricks arranged in a way that looked deliberate, as if someone had attempted to put the house back together in a child's naive logic.

I stood in the center of what had been my bedroom and took out the locket. It was impossible to know if the child's eyes in the photograph were mine; faces often rearrange to fit memory's needs. I had remembered a girl with my same small jaw, a gap in the teeth, and a certain stubborn look when the teacher asked a question that needed no answer. I opened the locket and looked closely. The photograph shivered in the tin. I could make no claim. Instead, I set it carefully in the place where the hearth had been and then I covered it with a thin dry layer of silt, a small cairn that promised a privacy of its own. I made a note in the fieldbook: "Personal deposit—unidentified locket, human remains no."

There was a quiet absurdity in that act; I had the authority of a surveyor and the small rebellions of a girl with a pocketful of secrets. I walked out of the foundation and measured again, took bearings, and felt the day's slow accumulation of data settle in me like a fine dust. The county map would eventually be digitized and placed in an archive; researchers would make shapes of that which I outlined. None of that would ever capture the sound of the school bell or the smell of bread or the way the town pressed its children against one another like a secret. But it might keep a list of where to find those things if the land ever decided to cough them up again.

I finished my work by aligning an old plat map with my new GPS readings. The two images fit together like hands finding one another after years apart. The old grid's lines dove for the new coordinates and found friends. I traced over the map with a pencil, the soft sound of graphite traveling over paper like a soothing audible. For each notation I made a small explanatory paragraph because maps are not merely coordinates: they are stories told in numbers. I stapled photographs to the packet—chimneys, steps, the schoolhouse threshold—and I wrote little comments in the margins.

Before I packed away my instruments I walked one last time along Main Street, my boots now more confident with the give of dried silt. There was a small group of people gathered near the boat launch—a family with an aged radio playing a country song that our town might once have liked. They were taking pictures with phones as if that could summon what had been lost. A child was holding a stick and pretending it was a sword and the child had not yet been taught how much the world would ask of him. I watched them and felt the strange, steady shrink of a thing people call nostalgia into the more serious complex of memory.

The county office wanted the map, and I would submit the dossier the following week. I would write my recommendations for signage, for a possible interpretive trail, for preservation in the museum of certain artifacts should they be recovered without harm. There was something utterly reasonable about this; it seemed like neatly pressed sorrow. When I closed the fieldbook its pages held the residue of a place and my handwriting made a map of me as well.

I left a marker—a small, rusted tin nailed to the last chimney with my initials and the date. It felt like an oath. I could have left nothing. I could have taken everything. Instead I left a claim that read, quietly, "I was here." Maybe some future person would find it and read the letters and know that someone had stood in these streets and recorded their bones. I drove away with the total station in the back folded and mute, with my notes aligned in a neat packet and the locket sleeping in an inner pocket against the fourth rib of my heart. I did not know if I would bring it to the museum, or if I would send it to a stranger who would say, yes, this was my grandmother's. I did not know if the county would accept my suggestion for a modest memorial plaque or if bureaucrats would prefer the place left unnamed.

On the drive back, the reservoir reflected the sky and looked for all the world like a photograph someone had cut and pasted onto the valley. Ridges of the old town disappeared under water again, and the line was visible on the chimney faces like a tide mark on the arms of a swimmer. The dam kept its degree of indifference. The truck hummed. Behind me the place that had been both my beginning and a body of water moved on in its own cycles.

There is a kind of work that is a polite exorcism; mapping the drowned town felt like that. I had given it coordinates and photographs and an inventory. I had been professional about it; I had been sentimental about it. I had measured chimneys and touched a locket. The map will go into the county's files, and sometime a researcher or a grandchild will open my packet and read the slow, careful lines as if they were a litany.

When I was a child they had said the town would be drowned for the greater good. People said such things only when the bartering for a future was urgent. The future, then, was an act of taking from some in order to give to others. I do not think the world is sensible enough to make these trades fairly. But it does what it will. My job, here, had been to take what the earth would show me and put the truth of it down in numbers and sentences so that future men with pens would understand the shape of what was gone.

I kept my eye on the horizon and thought of a child's chair left at a window, imagined a mother finishing a loaf and setting it aside as if she could preserve a piece of time. I thought of Mr. Benson past the hairline of age, who had tied a bit of remnant tape to a chimney, like a man knitting a memory to a nail. I thought of the locket, and the fact that I had not tried to be holy about it. It was a small responsibility to have taken something that had lain in the earth and to have returned it to the earth in a slightly different order, private and deliberate.

There is a mercy in the simplicity of lines. A map does not rehearse feelings; it keeps the geometry of a place honest. At the county, someone will digitize my map and place it next to a satellite image and a parcel number, and there it will wait, a clean, conditional thing. But when I close the file in my head and let the truck's heater warm my knees, I find that another mapping has been done—one of faint roads in my chest, the kind children learn from running their fingers along the rails of a porch. Those roads are not for the public record. They are for me. They run, in thin, careful pencil-strokes, from the place where my mother learned to bake to the place where the school bell used to hang heavy and ring.

At the end of the day, as I pulled back onto the highway and the reservoir receded behind me into its folded self, I thought of the town's drowned streets as a hand extended in a sleep. I tucked the map into the truck's glove compartment as if one might carry a folded country in the small, private place where one's fingers might find it in a waiting moment. I drove on, and the sky opened up like an unsent letter. The math of the job would be finished soon; the ledger would be closed, the coordinates filed. But maps do not erase the fact of living, nor do they capture everything. They are an invitation to come again. I suppose that is what I had wanted to leave: an address, a way back, some small stick with my initials on it hammered into a chimney that would hold its own against whatever water would come. They call places "drowned" as though water were a verb that steals and goes on its way. But water keeps everything it carries in motion. Sometimes the things it takes come back to sit in sunlight and let us take their measure.

I drove until the black top changed color and the trees closed their ranks. When the county line came up on my schedule that week, I would type up my report, digitize my photos, and attach them to a file like an offering to logic. The next time the reservoir was low, someone would follow my lines and find the chimney I had marked and perhaps find also the little red tape still flapping, the last of Mr. Benson's ribbon. If they did, they would know someone had been there before them and had not let the place go without a name.

I folded the fieldbook and unfurled its pages with my thumb, a small, private ritual. Then I put the locket back into the inner pocket, against the place where my ribs met the map of me, and drove on, carrying the coordinates of a place I had been and was not ashamed to remember. The town remained, in lines and shadows and the memory of those who still liked to touch a brick and say aloud a name. There is a consolation in recording what is left, in insisting that we place our small, sure marks upon the earth: we say, here—this is where something was treasured. We say, too, that we came and saw and measured and loved, in the only ways we could.
