The ferry woke me up like a memory you cannot place: not with suddenness but with the soft insistence of motors and water against hull, the ship chewing through blackness and salt and distance until things that had been separate became merely distant. I had gone to the lounge because cabins felt too much like arrest, because the thin mattress and the hum of the wall behind my head made my thoughts move in circles the way the tide did. Here, in the public part of the boat, there were other sleepers—wrapped in blankets, heads fallen against steaming mugs, a woman with a child's hair plastered to her cheek—people going somewhere or coming back from somewhere. It made me feel less conspicuous, less like a chosen witness.

I had not been on a ferry overnight in years. I knew how the wake looked on dark glass—like a smear of light that the sea could not help making—but I had not remembered how sound criminally softened the world, curling into the small spaces so it could be heard alone. The captain's voice had been a recorded, neutral thing announcing estimated time of arrival. Occasionally a laugh from the passengers dissolved into the engine. I thought of Sam, because that is what happens when you go to identify a body: the present sits in the same chair as the past. Sam became a series of images I kept setting on the table in front of me and then knocking over with my elbow.

We grew up on the cliffs by the strait. When I think of Sam I always imagine him with the crust of salt in his hair, a scab on his chin, one knee perpetually bloody. He could always climb farther than I could. He could always blink hard enough to appear insolent right before falling into something dangerous, like the old railway bridge over the inlet where we used to dare each other to walk the sleepers. I remember a day when he shoved me down the bank to the tannin water and I thought I'd drown and then he reached down and hauled me back by the jacket collar with the entire animal persistence of someone who had never been taught to be careful about bodies. He was six months younger than me and somehow full of fewer anxieties—there were things he didn't measure in his head before doing: he loved first then worried later.

There was a time we were thick as brothers are supposed to be. There was a time our mother would call from the kitchen and both of us would appear at her back like twin shadows, because we were reversible: you could fit one into the other’s slot and the house would hold. Lots of these memories are small and private; the way Sam would whistle when he thought no one was listening, a flat thing like a trout; the way he tucked his ear under the pillow when we let the radio run all night.

We lost that along the rungs like a ladder slowly detached. The fracture didn't announce itself with anything seismic: it was a string of omissions. Missed birthdays, angry text messages, nights when I left the porch light on and he did not come home. Once he stayed gone for six months and when he came back there were new bruises on his forearms and a look like someone had stolen his vocabulary. He began to drink from the filtered bottles of shops rather than from the pub. He took to sleeping in the doorways behind the fishmonger's for warmth and to avoid the friends I had who never forgave him for stealing cigarettes or for something bigger I never proved. He told me once, when he was three quarters drunk and sentimental, that you cannot pick a family like a coat. You wear what comes on you. I retreated back into what was available: employment, the small kitchen with the clock that never changed the batteries, the same alarm I would set for work at five.

Our split became formal the day he broke our mother's trust. It was not a monstrous affair in any legal sense; it was a betrayal measured in private mathematics—money missing from her jar, the old ring off her finger. She accused him with the thin voice of someone in constant disbelief and I stood with her and legalism felt like alliance. Sam said it was a mistake, that he'd pawned some boxes of old tools for a doctor's appointment, for food on bad weeks. I took her side. I suppose I was younger in my reserve then, and also more frightened of chaos than of compassion. The argument that followed—the last real argument we had—ended with words I would spend ten years trying to retrieve: Get out. The door had swung behind him with the finality of a coffined lid, and I felt at once incontestably right and voraciously hollow.

I had not spoken to him properly since. There were messages that felt ceremonial—an inquiry about our mother's health, a blacked-out number phoning once to ask if there were any jobs in the mill—but conversation as comfort did not return. He drifted the way men who have nowhere to go drift: from one couch to another, from train-car to hostel, across the country like a rumor. Sometimes he would phone and hang up. Once, many years ago, he called to tell me he loved me and then said he was going to put his head under the rim of the river and held the receiver out into the bath for me to decide whether to catch a drowning man. I didn't. He didn't drown that day. He went away. He returned months later smelling like other men's habits, and for a while our house looked like shelter until it didn't.

People think estrangement is easy without being in it. It feels like rupture and then tidy disconnection. In reality it is like a clock becoming run-down; you wind it each time you think you heard its tick and then you realize the sound was in the other room. There are griefs you hold because you have to, logistical griefs—papers, insurance, seeds of lineage that require tending. When I was younger the thought of standing before a stranger in a white coat and saying, "Yes, that's my brother," seemed impossible in its intimacy. But the freight the words take has a habit of crowding you from behind. When the police called at three in the morning I thought I would instruct them to send letters, or to do whatever the law requires. It was easier not to speak to them than to go. But then the woman on the phone said, simply, We have a body. There were no further refinements. We found him on the rocks two miles north of the headland.

I had rehearsed other people's opinions in my head throughout the night crossing: the local constable who knew both of us as kids would say that I should come and identify. The clerk at the mortuary might be brisk. My mother, who lived three hours inland, would want to know everything and yet know everything already. The part that reared up like a wild animal was simpler: what if he was not Sam? What if I was the person asked to give a name to a body that was not mine and by saying so I made a dead man into someone he was not? There is an ugliness in giving a name to a thing because you cannot stand the alternative, because you prefer the shape of a story to the raggedness of the world.

At the ticket desk a woman with tired lipstick sold me a coffee that tasted of burnt grounds and novelty. The ferry's desk had one of those scanners that made a brief, obedient sound as my ticket passed by. Outside the air was raw and the deck, which had been wet earlier from spray, kicked cold into the soles of my shoes. The strait’s surface had pins of white where the water showed its teeth. I leaned on the rail and watched the black move and thought how little of a person can be distinguished from the sea when the sea has had its way with you: hair, clothing, those little human ornaments that in life seem to matter and in death come off like props.

The port at the other side looks like a throat where a mouth is closed. There were low buildings, a crane, a truck park with a dog asleep in the belly of it. A police car waited by a blue light that sat like a bruise on the pavement. Behind a chain-link fence a man smoked and jotted something into a notebook. A woman in a high-visibility vest walked with an animal gait, which is how I think all people moving in uniforms appear—decisive. When I told her my name she looked at me for two long seconds as if cataloguing me and then asked me my relation to the deceased. My voice went static when I said Brother, and it sounded foreign in my ears.

The mortuary was not like the ones I had seen in films. It was a long, low building with a sliding door that operated with an apologetic mechanical sigh. Inside, fluorescent lights hummed and a heating system clicked. There was a row of chairs by a desk where a phone kept ringing at intervals. Papers, forms, laminated cards with lists of questions you answer only once. The attendant—Marta, she said when I asked—was young for the tone of her job, perhaps in her thirties, with eyes that had already built a kind of practical armor. She asked for my identification and wrote my name in a book with the sort of script that forgets ornament for necessary clarity. She told me the body had been found in the morning, clothing intact but waterlogged. There was a torn tartan scarf in one pocket, a watch with a cracked face, and a paperback with the spine like a wedge of light—On the Road, the smudged cover as though someone had half-read it in rain.

On the trolley, he looked both like someone I knew and like someone else entirely. The face that had wanted to live in my memory had turned away from me and left me with a rubbed-in version, the features exaggerated by water's bluntness: eyelids swollen, nose flattened, hair like cords. His beard, which Sam always let slide long when he was trying to look older than his years, hung clung to skin. The people on the trolley were discrete: a leather-fingered man with a clipboard who would not meet my eyes, a nurse who left briefly and then returned with a latex glove clamped over something. The room smelled faintly of the disinfectant you could find in school changing rooms—clean, training-worn, and then something else, salt and the dryer smell of river silt.

The first thing I looked for was a scar we had both gotten by accident when we were eleven, carving our initials into a fence post with a penknife: J over S. I put the back of my hand against the trolley rail because I thought it steadied me and then realized the instinct was ridiculous. There were no immediate, obvious points of recognition. The wrist that had once had the slashed knuckle from wrestling showed nothing; the knuckles were swollen, the lines of the palm unreadable. I found myself imagining possibilities: a healed burn behind the right ear where our mother had once pressed a metal iron too long; a thumb scar where he had burned the oven when he was supposed to be making toast. I leaned closer because the face I knew had a certain angle when he smiled, a left eyebrow that bounced, and I wanted to hold that angle against the hulking reality before me and see if they aligned.

Marta asked if I wanted to see the face. My voice was a thing soldered by being asked questions. I said Yes, because there is only one way to learn whether the face of the world you remember still belongs to a man. She turned a knob and a sheet was peeled back, slow as a curtain in church. The face beneath looked firstly ordinary—Pale. Lips torn by cold. A cut above the eyebrow stitchlike and red. But there was something in the mouth that made my stomach drop: a split in the lower lip we used to joke about, the way he had once bitten his lip during a spelling test until it bled; a small crescent of a scar at the eyebrow from a winter when he slipped on ice and I had laughed and then the laugh pealed into real fear. When I saw those things it was like finding the old map under a pile of new maps: you know the lay of the land though it has been overlaid.

I was surprised by how small he seemed, in a way that had nothing to do with the morphing of perception in grief. He was compressed into the world that had been pressed against his body. In life Sam had bulk, not necessarily in muscle but in how he took up space—he had a way of leaning his whole head into a conversation that left the room with a better understanding of air. On the trolly, there was none of that audacity left. He fitted into a geometry of a person who had been washed and measured.

"Would you like to remove personal effects?" Marta asked as if suggesting to have a tea. I said, No, I didn't, and then added because being honest felt like a cheap broom: just the watch if it's not too much trouble. She went through the pockets with a professional gentleness I could not emulate. She extracted a fat wallet with cards bobbing like dull teeth, a set of keys, and then a watch wrapped in a crust of salt. The band was stiff. It had been mine once—no, not mine, but it was a watch like the one I had given Sam on his eighteenth when we pooled money to get him something "decent." I remember the inscription on the back in block handwriting: To Sam, from J, with the proud deception that initials were private. As a child I had thought initials meant ownership; we had both believed that communion could be sealed with letters.

She handed the watch to me. Metal rang against metal somewhere in my chest. For a second I held it like a prayer book; for a second I thought of the man who had stolen and sobbed and kept walking, the man who had once laid his head against my lap in the backseat of a car and promised never to leave again. The watch's face was cracked but the hands were at least intact. I thought if I set it, maybe time would unfurl like a blanket and the ache would lie down. The thing was smaller than memory allowed it to be; I saw that my own recollection inflated objects in the absence of their owners, the way grief tends to enlarge a thing until it presses through the walls.

"Do you want to look at the tattoos?" Marta asked. It was a neutral line, like someone offering a second biscuit. Tattoos are the cemetery that the living make for the living; they are a deliberate attempt to write permanence onto the unstable body. Sam had a swallow on his forearm somewhere, not a grand design but a token of foolish invincibility we both thought we owned. I said yes because the savage mind will request proof. She rolled the sleeve back and there it was, a blotchy blue bird, its paint smudged by water and time, not ornate but its shape recognizable. My eyes found it like someone finding an old house key by the fireplace. Identification in our culture more or less becomes the bouquet of physical assertions: ring, scar, tattoo, watch. They are not full proofs but they are the grammar of memory.

Then the only remarkable thing happened. I found a corner of a paperback, and once you have read On the Road you know that certain marginalia are like fingerprints: the way a man underlines a phrase, a note in a margin in the intimate slant of someone's hand. There was a cheap book, binder-faded, a page with a pencil line that curved like someone had been about to say something and then erased it. There was a note in the margin in an ink I recognized because Sam had the trick of writing like he was always in a hurry and yet taking his time with each turn of letters, as though he had to wrestle their shapes into a final pose. It said, in his irregular scrawl: "For later, when I'm less afraid." I did not see the words so much as I remembered the stroke that began the S that looked like a hook Sam used in his grocery lists. All the things I had suspected in the world that told you what was yours reassembled into a plausible story.

The formal question slid into the room like a professional bird: "Do you wish to make an identification?" The attendant's face had the same quiet gravity. I held that watch like a talisman and thought of my mother's hands in the kitchen, of our house no bigger than a shoebox in my mind's eye. I thought of the way the harbor smells had always meant leaving. I thought of how ashamed I had been at the time of that argument, how pride had seemed more urgent then than kindness. I measured all of these like baggage and realized how light it is to stand up and say something you are not certain of when your heart is a coal fire. I nodded, not because I was sure in the forensic sense but because I was certain of a different thing: the work that comes after telling a story true or false.

"Would you like to sign here?" the man on the clipboard asked. He handed me a thin pen. My hands trembled in a way that belonged to an hour earlier in my life when I had learned to squint and steady. I signed with my full name because it felt like a seal. There is a legality to naming that binds you across a different axis than love does; you become the person who can release the dead. I wrote his name on the line too, as if ink could fix return.

There were obligations. Do you wish to claim the body, do you wish for cremation or burial, do you wish for medical records to be released for coronial review? They asked other things—did he have an executor, did he have debts—and I felt the thrum of the future like a door closing behind me. I signed forms that requested I make choices for a man who, for a long time, had made choice seem unnecessary. My life, I realized, would now have a function that his death required: I would be the person who informed our mother that the boy she had stooped to discipline was no longer a concern. I would be the one who negotiated with undertakers and remembered to return a borrowed book. There is a cruelty in death that requires the living to be rational accountants.

When it was over, Marta wrapped the watch in a sheet of paper and gave it to me with a look that had sympathy but also a boundary; she performed both because she has practiced the difference. I kept it in my pocket like contraband. On my way out, in the corridor by the freezers, the man with the clipboard told me quietly that identification is a human act with legal consequences and that if there is anything I later think I've made in error I should call them. The voice had the weight of someone who sees these things happen all the time, the weight of being witness without hope. I stepped into the morning which seemed, perversely, like a kind of consolation: gulls tied to the waste bins, the port's cranes glittering like a child's recollection of a dream. A sun thin and brave found the edge of the sea.

I did not tell my mother on the phone that day because the sound of her voice would have been too much like a hand closing. Instead I walked along the pier until the ferry I had arrived on had a shape in the distance like a raw white thing and the town resolved into its honest, imperfect self. The smell of diesel and harbor had settled somewhere in my coat. I pulled the watch out of my pocket and looked at the little inscription we had made in the flour of a shared pride. The letters were not explicit—just blocky strokes like someone trying to claim a name—and I traced the J with a thumb I did not know belonged to me anymore. I thought about Sam's handwriting in that book—little gestures of hope and fear—and thought about how easily one can be the sum of objects placed against a body.

On the quay a man with a kettle was making tea and he asked if I wanted one. I said no; a city of small favors was assembling around me and I had nothing to give back. Sitting on a low bench I watched the harbor boats float like anchored secrets. People with tattoos and the practical skin of those who work with ropes loped by and looked at me the way other mortal folk do—briefly and without particular worry. My watch ticked in the pocket more vehemently than it had any right to.

Possibly, I thought, all this was a fiction but a useful one. Perhaps the man on the trolley was a stranger; perhaps the watch would turn out to be a common sort and the handwriting in the book another's flourish. But what is needed sometimes is not irrefutable truth but an acceptance of a shape that lets the living continue. There is a funeral the family must stage, people who will travel, condolences to be managed, and a body in the ground is a social fact that stabilizes behaviour the way fenders keep boats from banging in a storm. We build rituals because otherwise grief becomes a maelstrom without a shore.

When I thought of the alternative—leaving a man unnamed at the edge of the sea, a thing without claim—I felt a sudden, compounding shame. The man might have been Sam, but there was also the possibility that the man had been someone else with his own mother, his own grievings. Yet I imagined a woman somewhere who would wake up in the morning and find the world persistent with neglect and there would be some difference if her boy had a person to address him by name in the newspaper. My decision had nothing to do with the absolute, everything to do with the covenant we make to one another in family: to be there in the mechanical moments even when the feelings have been traded away. If Sam had wronged me, claiming him would in a small way repair what pride had broken. That thought, selfish perhaps, mollified the rest.

I called in at a payphone because the thought of my mother hearing me on a mobile felt somehow anticlimactic. She answered on the second ring. For a moment nothing between us changed: she spoke and I answered because we were part of the same old choreography. I told her we should talk later, that there were things to arrange. After I had hung up, I walked to the edge of the quay and looked down at the water. It was a hole of midnight inside the day. The tide was coming in quick as if the world were trying to erase things it had only just remembered.

On the way back to the ferry I stopped at a secondhand shop that smelled of old paper and forgotten furniture. The owner, an elderly woman with hair like an unbrushed cloud, looked up with the benign suspicion you get from someone whose profession is to value worn things. I thought about many tokens. In the end I bought a small jar of tobacco—a childish affectation that somehow seemed the right weight to leave for a man who had tried to quiet his hands with smoke. She wrapped it in a brown bag and wrote on the receipt in looping script the name of the town. Outside, the sky had the fragile, high quality of morning. I thrust my hand into my pocket and found the watch; it lay there like an animal sleeping. I wondered if, when I returned it to my mother, she would recognize it and hold it close or if she would look at my face first and then the metal and see confirmation that her boy had been found. Either way, I realized, there is charity due in naming; sometimes it is the only kindness left.

Back aboard the ferry for the return crossing, the bunting of the ship made the sky look more festive than it felt. The lounge smelled of tea stronger than on the outward trip and there were new passengers, the small, careful transactions of the world: a woman buying a magazine, a child with sticky fingers, a man asleep with his hat over his face and his mouth forming a tiny O of contentment. I sat near a window and watched the blackness divide again. My hand went to the pocket where the watch slept. It kept a kind of stubborn beat. If I had been asked, before that morning, whether I would sign my name beneath a body that may not have been my brother's, I would have said no. Now I knew I would have said yes because we do things for reasons both noble and self-serving, because in the end being the one to say a name is both a duty and a comfort.

When the ferry reached the bend where the water calmed and the town shone like a smeared coin, I thought of the old rail bridge and of Sam's laughter there. I thought of how the sound of a brother's voice had been absent for a long time and how, when battlefields are behind you, the quiet is an equally legitimate landscape. I did not know whether my identification would be contested or whether it would be a simple administrative fact. All I knew was that I had made a decision in the presence of the person tasked with making practical grief into a manageable object. It was an act that would alter a small number of lives and perhaps spare a stranger's mother the labor of finding a son without a stone to stand upon.

In the night ahead there would be practicalities: notifying the bank, speaking to undertakers, telling an old neighbor. In the core of me there were dull things—shame, relief, love, guilt—composed of the same materials you find on an engine: oil, rust, heat. I thought of Sam's handwriting in the margin and how it read like a plea and a covenant all at once: For later, when I'm less afraid. For later, when we are less afraid together. I put the watch up to my ear like some benighted child and listened to the ticking that kept time on a body that could no longer be harmed by time.

As the ferry pushed into the mouth of the harbor, the lights of the town stretched themselves thin in the water. A man started a guitar in the corner and played a tune that sounded like a memory rewritten. I thought of the body on the trolley and of the small unanswerable mystery of whether the face I recognized belonged to the brother I had loved badly. Perhaps I had been correct. Perhaps I had been mistaken. There is a temper to human life that requires us to act before all reasons are clarified. I had done the human thing: I had named a man and by naming him made him my responsibility.

On the dock they unloaded like they always do: a line of people and belongings, the mundane choreography of arrival. I stepped off with the rest and felt, in the light, how paper-thin most certainties are. The watch was warm in my pocket. I turned my collar up against the wind and walked toward the road that would take me inland and to my mother, to chairs pulled close and the smell of biscuits, to questions that would ask me to do things—sign forms, choose a coffin, read a name out loud—and I knew already that I would do them with the clumsy, necessary attention of someone trying to be small and useful. The day was raw and inevitable. The harbor glowed behind me like a secret that had been given a shape.
