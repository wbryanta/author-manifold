The morning came in hard and white and hung over the orchard like a lid. They moved through it with their shirts stuck to their backs and the dust of the road still in the seams of their shoes. The trees lay in ranks like old men at prayer, trunks cracked, limbs bereft of the green that had once been a spectacle at this time of year. The fruit was small and hard and few. The earth between the rows had a pallor as of old bone. A wind, if one could call it such in the heat, drew a fine powder from the upper crust and the sun rode high and flat and burned the light to a glare that made the world look newly raw.

They spoke little. This was how they had always been together: silence that made work possible. They were brothers with the same hands and different angles to their faces, the elder with the shoulder of a man who had bent under burdens longer and the younger with a look that still slept sometimes in the shape of accusation. They had brought tools, a coil of new black pipe, a wrench, a small gas saw, a tarp and a cooler. The truck sat in the lane, its tailgate down, a dog-eared radio on the dash that they did not use. On the hood a faded photograph of a man in a shirt that had been white once. The photograph had the sameness of relics. They had it for lack of anything else.

The leak was near the center of the stand where the elder had planted the first trees with their father. There was a hiss under the earth wherever the pipe had worn and split. They had found it the week before when the pump ran and the pressure would not hold. The well had been hand-drilled by a contractor whose name the elder could no longer place and the pump had been set when their father had been a man with a further reach than he had now. Things wore out. There is no pleading against the worn.

We ought to sell, the elder said first when they had their boots in the loam and had cleared brush to make a place to work. The words were not meant to be soft.

Sell, said the younger.

He turned the wrench. There was the rhythm of such turning in the bone. He had a watch on his wrist that did not work. His voice was the voice of a man who had lived in town for years and had driven home on the weekend. You can't keep throwing money at land that won't make it back.

The younger laughed. You going to turn the place into a shopping center, he said. He dug with a trowel at the base of a pipe where roots had knotted it like an old wound.

There's money in the trees, the elder said. There always has been.

Not so much now, the younger said.

The elder crouched and looked at the earth as if that would tell him secrets about the future. Between their palms the soil came loose in flinty crumbs. The irrigation pipe lay in its shallow trench, a ribbon of black that had been cut and spliced and taped perhaps more times than either of them remembered. The sun stood up over the ridge and threw long, thin shadows from the trees. A man like their father might have stood in such light and spoken without hurrying. He had not left the farm with anything but a will and an example and an old hat that he wore to Sunday, and that hat had sat on a peg in the house after he died until it was moth-eaten and the younger wrapped it in newsprint and put it in a box.

We can't keep running it like this, the elder said. He ran his finger along the pipe; there was grit under his nail. They had tried a band once, a rubber sleeve clamped on as if that would be enough to bind the past to the present. It had not been enough. The pump would cough and cut and the fruit would wither.

We keep doing what we do, the younger said. You can't sell what you can't find in your sleep.

The elder looked up suddenly. That was the way he did it: a quick lift of his chin as though some passing thought had hit him like a bird. You ever think about that, he said. You ever think about leaving this and not looking back.

I look back for the things I am supposed to, the younger said.

The elder laughed, a dry little noise. Supposed to. He took a length of new pipe from the pile and felt its lightness. They had left the tractor by the road and had come with their hands and their mouths because the money to buy someone else to do it was not there and because the work to be done was small enough for two men and a borrowed pump and the pride that comes from doing what needs to be done without the surveillance of another. They fitted a coupling and tightened the clamp with the wrench until their knuckles whitened. The metal smelled like blood when it was hot in their hands from friction.

You know what they'll do, the younger said. They'll take the clearest piece and call it development. They'll cut a road through the center if you let them. They'll put up houses with bright lights and someone to cut their grass at noon on Sunday. You think Dad wanted that?

Your father didn't get to choose clever, the elder said.

They worked and the day grew as if it had been pushed into being by some slow hand. Birds moved among branches like small furtive spirits. The sun crossed and remade the shadows. They dug out the broken section and set in a new coupling. The black pipe had the smell of old rubber and sun. For a while they paused and mounted the new section and clamped the joints and the elder painted dark primer on the ends as if that were a sacrament, the solvent bringing the black to a wet shine before it dried and bonded. He had done this with their father as a boy, hands smaller, voice lower, learning how to press the elision of two things into a single seam. The younger remembered that, how their father would press the pipe together with a grunt and a curse and the look of great attention as though he were trying to teach them the arc of necessity itself.

You always thought you were smarter than him, the younger said finally. He dug with the trowel and a small vein of sweat made a clean dark line across his cheekbone.

I was younger, the elder said. He set the coupling, then took the saw and cut a clean edge. It's different now.

Different how, said the younger.

You know how, the elder said. You get older and you start to measure in other things. You look at debts the way you once looked at trees.

The younger stopped and wiped his hand across his mouth. For a moment he looked much like the photograph on the hood of the truck. You sound like him, he said.

The elder's face did a small shift, an imperceptible thing. Like who, he said.

Like him, the younger said. Their father. The way he would say a thing that needed saying and then stand back as if it were an ember.

They had not spoken of selling since their father was put in the ground that had fed them and kept their mother alive until the fall that took her. The orchard felt different now that none of them were as it had been when the two of them were small and their father was a man who could take a trunk in both hands and snap it like a branch of elder wood. The house on the rise was empty in the middle days. Sometimes at night they could hear the mice working behind the cupboards and the old clock stopped with a patinaed hand at noon. They had kept the rooms tidy enough for a demon of conscience and no more.

You want to sell and go where? the younger said.

Anywhere with a bank that won't take your breath, the elder said. He worked the coupling until the solvent set and the two sections of pipe would not pull from each other. You can stay here and irrigate a yard and oil a tractor and the world will look at you like you are the last man in a world mostly of ghosts and those ghosts will look back like they want to buy you something.

The younger's hands were fine enough that one could imagine them some other place. He had a scar across his knuckle where a chain had bitten him when he was a boy. He thought in a sort of inward dialect the elder could not enter. There is honor in not leaving, he said. Not every place is a marketplace.

Honor, said the elder. He kicked at a stone and it rolled. If honor paid the taxes they wouldn't be talking about it.

They argued like this until the sun folded down and the day went into a single furnace of air. Passers on the road or the men at the feed store would have said minimal things if they had been asked, which they would not. One of them thought of selling as a kind of legal euthanasia for something that was alive only by memory. The other thought of selling as treachery.

A woman in town had been to see the orchard the spring earlier and had written them both a letter. She had some money and wanted land where she might put her horses and a house with a porch and a view of the water that came in the rainy times. The elder had opened the letter twice and left it on the kitchen table; the younger had said nothing. They had both read the paragraph in her hand about the light and the silence and the possibility of a life there. To the elder it read like potential. To the younger it read like an erasure.

They had shared a father, and in the sharing had learned one inescapable thing: men of a certain sort prefer the dialect of labor to the dialect of talk. This day they talked because there was a wound that asked for operating and the wound was the orchard and the words used to stitch around that wound. They worked in the way of surgeons without anesthesia. The repair was a bandage; it made a leak less. The water came again in a thin strained life like breath in a throat where disease had been.

You can't make it give what it's not, the elder said when the water finally ran and the pressure climbed a shade more. They crouched at the late stage and watched for the little tokens. A drop of green showed at the tips of a leaf where there had been brown a week before. They let that speak for a moment.

We ought to lease it, the younger said. People take land, tend it, leave it to go back.

Lease it to whom, the elder said. There aren't people with the kind of patience that soil wants.

He thought of their father planting a row and cursing the soil that would not take to the stone he had dug with his own hands. He thought of the way the man would stand at the end of the day with the hat low on his face and the earth on his boots. He had built the place the way a man builds a house. It was his measure. The elder had felt the need to get his debt right and he could feel the fissure in the days like a thing in a shell.

You always were better at giving up, the younger said. He reached and turned a valve to test the line and the water came through in a steady, sullen sigh. The sound was a music of sorts and it made the younger's eyes close like one praying.

The elder did not answer except to look at the distance and the way the land fell away, reaching toward a river you could not see from the orchard. To sell would bring them money, some of it at once, and relief for the loans that had circled like falcons at the hem of their finances. To sell would also be to sign away the stories that were anchored in the earth. Which of them was the truer?

The younger had a child once for a while and then the child had been taken by decisions neither brother had the clarity to prevent. The memory of days spent with that child under the trees returned sometimes in summers like a fever. He could stand under an apricot branch and smell the fruit and the childish laughter that had once rung like a bell. He thought of selling as a theft conducted with the consent of the owner.

When the pump would not respond they came up the hill to the old shed where the engine waited. It had been their father's engine once, a machine the father had cussed and petted in equal measure. They lifted the lid and leaned down inside and the smell of old oil and metal rose in a sticky heat. The elder found an old spark plug he had bought when his father still kept lists and the younger passed him a rag. They were two hands learning the same sequence, like the braiding of a rope. There is a small sudden joy in motors when they take life again.

You going to put up with the bank calling, the younger said.

For now, the elder said. We get it going. We put it on for the season and we see.

We always see, the younger said.

The engine coughed. The elder cranked and the engine took and then it stumbled and would not make steady breath. The afternoon fell like heat into a black cup and the light from a distant town made a low holding sway on the horizon. The orchard exhaled; the trees smelled of a wetness that meant some return. The brothers sat on the lip of the truck bed and the elder passed a bottle of water between them.

You remember him, the younger said after a time when the words had been used up.

Remember who, said the elder.

Our mother, he said. The elder's jaw twitched with the effort of recollection. He remembered small things about her, the way she wrapped pies in cloth, the way she moved in the kitchen silent as a prayer. She had died in winter when the house was at its most full of shadow. The younger had been there and the elder had been elsewhere for a while in a place where one kept one's wits about one and came back to learn how to stand in a parlor with a coffin. They did not talk of this for long. Men are not factories of consolation. They are not stores of tenderness that are open on request.

There's a letter from the bank in the house, the younger said. I haven't opened it. He made it a small factual thing and then he held it again with the odd way a man holds a loaded thought.

Do you want to open it, the elder said.

No, said the younger. Let it be.

Let it be, said the elder. He put both hands on the tailgate and leaned forward like a man about to fall from a height and chose not to disclose it.

They ate cold sandwiches and the bread was like paper. Their talk went to small things. At noon the wind came up and then died. An insect with too many wings whined somewhere in the box elder trees. You could tell the day was thin from the body as it was. The brothers moved like two animals that had become used to the fence of habit. There are rituals for keeping a thing alive and rituals for giving it a send. They had known both, even if one had been named differently by each.

The day had in it an old geometry. Lines of sun, lines of shade, the pipe like a black artery threading the soil. A man can mend a small section and think he has done more. It was not merely that. When the water flowed into the hoof marks and into small cups under the rose bushes there was a small reanimation. A leaf found a color it had not had for months and there was a sound like an answered prayer. The brothers stood in that and looked at each other, and each of them read what he wanted. You could not say which was truer.

You should take it to the county, the younger said at one point. What county, the elder said. He had been to the table at the county seat once in another life and the tables had been full of men who made decisions like stone. You try to get a grant or something. They won't write you a check for sentiment but they might for conservation.

Conservation, the elder said. He heard the word as one might hear a name newly given to a thing he had lived with for fifty years. We would need to find the right forms and the right men. They want stories that are neat.

You make it sound like a confession, said the younger. The elder smiled and shook his head.

Which of us does the story fit, he said. The younger bent to his hands and looked at the dirt.

There is a life in the land that some people call by the name of spirit, the younger said finally. Others call it obligation. I do not know a better word.

The elder had a ledger in the glove compartment when his father had been alive and had kept the accounts by hand with a pencil that would not lie. He had the ledger like one keeps a ghost. The columns were neat and the sums staggered under one another like a small testimony. He had kept them in the warm shoulder of his car for months. He opened the ledger now and ran a finger down an old figure. When you do the numbers, the elder said, and do them honestly, there are only a few outcomes. The orchard either will feed you or not. If it will not feed you it will take from you. Take what you can get and you do the good thing for the generation next and you put your name on the line and you buy a home that will not require your back to be the brace.

The younger looked at him with a small, fine fury. The land takes from us all the time, he said. You put your hands on it and it gives you fruit and takes your blood back. The orchard is not an account. It's a place where the dead live.

You think this is the language of the dead, the elder said. He put the ledger back in the glove compartment and closed it with a small click.

If it is, the younger said, we are the ones who make their bed.

They worked until the light thinned like a used coin. The patch they had made held and the water ran with an evenness they had not expected. The trees in the stand drank and the green showed again and there was a small assurance in that. They dragged a hose along one row and the spray came in a fine sheet and the world smelled like wet earth and the two of them felt the pull of satisfaction that belongs to work done. The sounds of the day had narrowed to the hiss of water and the creak of the tailgate and the small breathing of the trees. It was, the younger thought, enough for now.

When the sun dipped at last behind a ridge it shed a coolness like a promise. The sky went thin and then a deep blue like a bruise. They sat on the tailgate and the elder lit a cigarette and passed the pack. The smoke went up and the sound of it was like the smoking of a rite.

You going to sign anything, the younger said.

He looked at him as though this were the same question as asking whether the sky would change. I don't know, he said.

I thought you would be a man of ands, the younger said. Which is to say, he was disappointed by a thing he perhaps had no right to. The elder’s mouth twitched.

Ands, said the elder. Or buts. You pick them as you pick a seed.

You going to put it up, the younger asked again.

Maybe, the elder said.

Maybe, said the younger. It was a small word that held resignation and hope and an assent to the shape of things.

Night came down and the frogs began in the low hollow like a single instrument tuning itself. They watched their breath in the cooler air and the orchard stood in the dim light like a cathedral where there was no longer room for the congregation. They had not settled anything more than the leak. To mend a line is not to mend a life. To add a coupling is to glue a seam on a shirt that will fray in other places. They had done what men working together might do. They had given the place a breath. Whether that breath would sustain them was another calculus.

You could sell, the younger said at last, and mean it. You could sign and the world would move and the land would pass into hands that would make their own story of it.

You could, the elder said. He crushed the butt of his cigarette out in the dust. Or you could keep it until there's nothing left to keep. He watched the smoke go down into the dark.

The younger did not answer for a long time. He had been his father's son in many things and the memory of those things held him like a weight. I can't, he said suddenly. He said it without flourish. It was simply a negation, the single outline you see when a building collapses and there is no more room for argument.

The elder's eyes were small slits in the night. He had thought that the younger might have less to hold him. He had thought that the younger would be more easily bought by the future's promises. This was not so. He had miscounted his brother and the estate of their father and the way that memory lays claim. He had meant to argue. For now he had nothing more to do than to sit with the cool and think himself into a size the world might not take.

We take it day by day, he said at last. We fix a leak and we see. He did not say whether he would sign a deed or not. The word deed seemed to him then like a thing that took breath and swallowed it.

We always did, the younger said. And the way he said it was not ruling but recapitulation. We always did what the day required. If the day required that we sell, we would do it. If the day required that we stayed, we would do that too.

They were men bound by habit who both knew how to honor their father and how to betray him. The land was not an altar to which they could always bring exactly the thing required by the spirits they thought it held. It held them both in the end and would be indifferent to their wants. They had the ledger and the letters and the photograph and the pump and the gnawed pipe and the memory of fruit in their hands. That was their inheritance.

Toward midnight a small cloud came, and a tentative rain began like a rumor. It misted at first and then it gathered and the smell of it rose from the soil in a blessed vapor. In the dark one could not see the exactness of the orchards but one could feel the trees take breath. They sat in the flat bed of the truck and the elder reached across and put a hand on the younger's knee. The younger managed a small hollow laugh at the gesture and then they were both quiet and the rain made a small music on the metal of the hood.

Tomorrow, the younger said.

Tomorrow, the elder said.

They did not define the tomorrow. They had stitched a pipe and revived the pump and the orchard would be breathing for a while. There were bills and letters and men who thought in numbers who would make their proposals. There were decisions at the edge of the light like cliffs. For that night they had, between them, done enough to keep the thing going and had enough of one another to be two people at rest in a precarious place.

When the rain had stopped and the last wet had puddled in tiny mirrors between the tires, they climbed from the truck and walked among the trees with their hands in their pockets. The leaves, having drunk, looked ashamedly renewed. The elder bent and broke a small twig and handed it to his brother like a covenant. There is a small green inside, he said. The younger touched the tip and the green was indeed fresh as a marrow. For one small time the orchard seemed to answer them.

We will see, the elder said.

We will see, the younger echoed.

They walked up together and left, their lights on dim so as not to disturb the field mice and the night birds. Behind them the pipe lay like a sewn vein and the water that ran through it would find its way where it always did and the orchard would drink as it might. The house on the rise would be there in the morning and the ledger and the letter would still be on the table. The world in which decisions are made by men who wear suits and count closed doors would continue to turn. The night held them like a letter in which the last word had not been written. They would come again with the same tools and the same arguments and perhaps with the same outcome or perhaps not. Men understand at last in different measures.

They went back to the truck and the elder started the engine. The light that shone across the bowl of their faces was a small lamp and the road home ate the dark before them in a slow thin mouth. Far off, a dog barked and then was gone. The hills held the last light like a promise they were not in a hurry to break. The brothers rode home in a long thin silence that had all the shape of what had been the day.
