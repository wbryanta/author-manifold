He woke with his back slick with sweat and the taste of iron in his mouth and the shadow of the dream still clinging to the corners of his sight, the echo of the crowd like a bell in some other room. She had not moved. The weight at her belly lay like a stone beneath the thin blanket and her breath was low and even as if none of it had ever happened. He lay a long while listening while the room came back about him in its small, obstinate fact, the rough board, the peg where the kettle hung, the sloping black pane where the night held a stubborn, indifferent piece of the sky. Far off a dog began to bark twice and then to answer to itself. He rolled over and put his head on his arm and cursed softly to himself, a sound half prayer half confession, then pushed back the blanket and swung his legs over the edge of the pallet and sat there in the gloom with his hands braced upon his knees and the room smelling of the susurrus of sleeping breath and the sweet metallic of blood not yet run.

Hush, she said, without waking, the words buoyed on the thin bedair, and he obeyed by instinct and watched the shadow that had been his heart pass slowly out from him. There was no light at the window; the east had not yet begun to worry at the horizon. He stood and crossed to the small stove and set on a scrap of coal and the thing of light that came up over it made a modest day in the room, a day not much to look at. He took down the battered pannikins and listened for a noise from the street beyond and there was only the soft long breathing of other houses in the block and the small shudder of a truck somewhere distant as it took some bridge. He filled water from the kettle and set it to warm and watched the steam lift in a way that made the rest of the dark look like something it had no claim on.

When she stirred and sat up he saw the round of her face in a new kind of way where the light touched it and showed him how thin it had become at the cheeks. Her hair was loose. She looked down at her belly with an animal, private reverence and then at him long and with an unspoken thing that would not be caught into words as easily as the smoke out of a pipe. You poor fool, she said, and there was no pity in it only the hard slice of truth, and he smiled at that and the laugh that came out was small and rusty. He smelled coffee and ate a biscuit she handed him and the silence between them was of the sort that does not bedezzle itself with explanations.

We move, he said at last, we should be gone by light. She nodded and her fingers found the seam of the blanket and she tucked it about the belly and the curve of her hips like some economized armor. He had been thinking the same thing the whole long night. The town had many eyes and some of them were looking for him and that was no place for a woman with the shape of a child beneath her clothes. He dressed quietly, the worn trousers, the boots that had been worked at a hundred times and the jacket that carried the smell of river and old fires, and he fastened the little knife at his belt the way one might tie a talisman. She watched him without speaking until the kettle boiled and he ladled the water into a tin cup and handed it to her warm and that was all the morning needed.

They crossed the street and the air was thin and raw, the ground frost-mottled, the river invisible behind the trees. A gull passed and was gone and a blue haze sat on the far hills like a promise they had never meant to keep. They moved on without haste as one does who has meant to get somewhere for a long while and knows the coming of the moment will have the slow quality of a long-told story. People were up here and there, faces peeking from doors, some bent from age, some that had been freckled by fever and wind, all of them moving with the conserved business of the forlorn who have not yet given up on the small rituals of day. He kept his head down; she kept the small satchel close.

They walked out of the town by a road that had been more a memory of a road than anything else, potholed and grassy at its edges. The river lay on their left like a dull blade. The ground fell away in places to a corridor of marsh that shone black and chewed at the light. Farther on they would find the bluff where the others had camped the night before if the story that passed along the stewhouses and the back porches had said so and he had meant to find them, meaningless though the reason. There are times when a man seeks not a place but the face of some other man who has looked at the same horizon for some small derivable reason. The three that had sat at the fire with the river between the world and them had had a shape to them that stuck in his memory like a bruise. One bearded, big about the chest with a gait like a man that had mastered solitude by force; one small with a face like a knucklebone and eyes that could double back upon silence and a third whose mouth had been always open as if he were expected to swallow what the others threw him.

They had not seen as yet, but the road bent and then another bend and there they were as if fate had pulled aside its curtain for them. The three came up with the sun at their backs, silhouettes long and quick, carrying bundles and a kettle hung from a pole over their shoulders. The bearded one looked up and nodded when he saw them and there was no easy warmth in the gesture. A nod is as full of meaning as any sermon if you read it properly, and the man read it. Morning, he said. Morning. The bearded one spat once, a round little sound, and then lifted a hand in a motion that might have been a greeting if not for the way the fingers remained splayed, as if he had been shaping something.

You got anything to spare, he asked without preface. Nothing, the man said, we have only what is pocketed on us. The bearded one shifted his weight and looked at the woman and his eyes went to the swollen round of her belly as if looking for the seam where she might be sewn around like a pledge. The bearded one is a man of business, the tongue said by the way he set his jaw. They spoke of neutral things then, the location of a ford that had a shallow place and a log that would serve as a road across the marsh, of a man downriver that had a line of tin pots and who might buy the kettle that the bearded one carried. They spoke as men will who have a momentary commerce to keep the day's hunger from insisting upon itself.

The bearded one watched her as one might watch weather shift. What are you running from, he said to the man, and his voice had the thin ring of those who have called out across hollows and been answered by wolves. No answer for long. The man felt the dream like an accusation. He had done nothing and yet everything, and that was the nature of things now. You ever been with the prophet, the bearded one asked suddenly, and the question landed like a stone. The man swallowed. He had been with a prophet once, years gone, in a different life or perhaps none at all, a man who had worn a tatter of linen at his shoulders and who had shouted that the sun would give back what it had taken if the people would only kneel and the miracle would occur before his raised hand. The miracle had not come then either, and it was no small wonder that men remembered such failures as if they were debts to be collected. He said nothing. The bearded one narrowed his eyes as if to read the silence for ink. You got close enough to touch the prophet, he said, then he spat, and the glitter of some old resentment slid from his lips.

They went their ways then, the three to the north and the pair to the south, and the man kept watching them until the shape of them was only a memory of motion on the grass. For a long way no one spoke and the conversation was a steadier companion than dry bread and the woman had put her hand upon his arm and they kept to the path as if the path itself were protecting them. They would come to a place where the ground ran up and the bones of an old bridge lay in the water smelling of iron and rot and they crossed on a log that was forever wise to the tread of small men. On the other side the country opened to another sort of desolation, dry gullies and scrub and trees that were attenuated to the ribs, as if everything that could be taken had been taken and whatever remained was only for the patience of the ruined.

Toward the hour when the sun rides up and makes kings of men for a little and then forgets them the country changed and there were fences and a corral and smoke that rose like a flag over the crest of a low hill and a road that bore the dents of comings and goings. They had come upon a settlement that had something like a market about it, a place where those with the habit of gathering had got the notion to assemble themselves together as if in numbers there might be a safety. And yet beneath that useful fiction lay other modes of commerce that had less to do with bread and more with conviction. Men stood about a wagon that had been hauled into the center of the green and upon it a man had mounted with a voice like a rope pulled tight.

He was a lean man this preacher, if preacher he is to be called, and he had the mouth of a man who could coax a fish from its cave. His skin had the sort of dry tautness that the sun lends in these places and his eyes were deep set to the business of a public that could be stunned by certainty. He wore a jacket patched at the shoulder and a kerchief the color of dried fruit at his throat and he held up a book that had no title upon it and spoke in an easy fashioned cadenced way that made the crowd lean like saplings to the sound of the wind. The talk was of cures and bodies and things to be set right, and men and women came forward with the circle in their eyes and the fold in their hands.

There was a man up near the speaker who had a bowl of flour and a small pile of coins and who kept the accounts of what was given and received and he moved like a priest of accounts, attentive and quick with a hand that could count and a frown that could match the need. The preacher spoke of a darkness that would pass and of a turning of fortunes by the will of a man who had been chosen and a small band cried amen and the sound was like a bowl being set down. The man watched the ritual with a particular sort of weariness and with the old itch of memory for the dream that had visited him that night. The prophet you dreamt of, he thought, is here in some fashion for the world is full of prophets and half-prophets and men who can make of the same word a crown or a cudgel. He turned his face away and thought of the bearded one and the way he had watched the woman and for a split second a certain future unrolled and it was one without them among the living folds.

They regarded the man with an inspection like men choose cats, eyes skimming to find what can be used for warmth. The little congregation hardened its attention when the preacher called for volunteers, for it is the practice of some men to offer to be the sacrifice while others count the receipts. He would be called up, the voice said, for a test, and the man found himself stepping forward as if his legs had their own memory. She pressed the heels of her fingers into his knuckles and did not hold him back.

The preacher held out his hand and took the man's wrist and his fingers were like a net. He put his face near to the man's and looked at him as if looking for a seam. Do you believe, the preacher asked. The man thought of the night and of the sun that had buckled and of the crowd and their howl and the taste of their hands and he said I do not know. The preacher smiled a smile that had been reduced to business. We will see then, he said, and clapped. A little hush spun around like dust.

There was an old woman brought forward then, her face lined with the work of many such days and a marigold about her neck. She sought to be cleansed of a pain and she wept not for the pain but for the feel of being chosen. The preacher took her hands in his and recited a litany that had been learned, it might have been, from the mouths of other men whose names had been forgotten and when he opened his hands there was no obvious motion, only the thing that comes to those who have been prepared to receive. For a time nothing happened and the little crowd shifted like a herd. The preacher's jaw worked. He put his finger to his lips and then brought it down again and the old woman's face relaxed in an odd way and the crowd breathed like a congregation at the close of a prayer.

The man stood on the wagon with the preacher and his skin prickled as if the world were about to be turned inside out. If the sun would not return its favors then men would trample upon the next available miracle like a field of wheat. He felt the eyes of a hundred folk on his head and then a noise came from the crowd, low and threaded with discontent, and a figure stepped out and pushed against the back of another and a voice raised up and the voice was not for the preacher but for some other. He saw the bearded one at that instant in the crowd with his shoulders set like a clamp and his hand had found something that glittered dark in his grip and his mouth moved as if tasting the word of a grievance.

The man on the wagon who kept the accounts opened his hand like a palm and counted the coins aloud and some of those coins were not the sort that a man would like to see exchanged for salvation. The bearded one loosed his grip and a shout came like an old bell. You sold us a lie, he cried and men near him took up the cry. The preacher's voice rose with all the talent of an animal in heat. Who has bought and paid, he called, who has weighed themselves in the scales and found wanting? The bearded one pushed his way forward and a small, yellow boy that sold fruit tripped and fell and there was flour and orange all rolling on the ground and the crowd took it as if it were a sign.

Accusations are soft things that when properly tended swell to a weight all their own. Names were called. The bearded one said the preacher had promised the night would be bright again and that on the third sunset there would be healing and there was none. The preacher stammered and then he spoke again with a new certainty and he said the work had been done but that such things are complex and that men must wait. The bearded one spat and then advanced. I watched you, he said addressing the man on the wagon who kept the accounts and the little shrine that had been built on the wagon, and he went on about details only a witness would know, and his words sank like spearheads. He accused the preacher of having taken spoils and of having given back only promises and of having used the poor to make coin. The crowd, hungry for someone to fix upon that anger, fed upon the accusation and their shape shifted from listeners to jurors.

The man on the wagon held up his hands and spoke into the wind a while as the bearded one recited details and the faces in the circle around them changed and the woman at his side gripped his wrist as if to keep him from moving. Nothing I have, the man said, and the words were small and short, and it seemed to him like a confession grown out of himself rather than for his defense. The preacher turned his face to him and his eyes were like the aperture of a camera. He knew him, the preacher said, and the words settled on him like dust. You have been with the bearded men, he said. You rose with them by the river and you went to their fire. You sold us the faith of a thing that would heal and it did not come.

It was as if a hand had reached through a table and found his throat and he felt the press of that hand and the cold in his limbs. The crowd pressed inward. He thought of the dream and how the faces in it had known him there and had turned on him with such feral speed and he felt the same manual push of inevitability now. He tried to speak and the jostle of the mob swallowed his voice. The bearded one advanced to the wagon and took hold of the book the preacher kept and opened it and the pages were blank save for a few words written in a hand that had been hurried and uncertain. The book had been an imitation; a book with no inside is the stretcher of charlatans.

They seized him then with no grace and no more care than one would use to secure a stolen fork and hands were upon his arms and his feet and his jacket was torn at the seam and the crowd's cry grew. He looked at the woman as they dragged him down the steps of the wagon and he felt something like ice press at his brain and it was not the fear only but the sense that the world had decided upon a narrative and that he was to be the necessary burn. She reached and the bearded one lifted a hand and struck her across the face so that she cried out once and then the world narrowed to the sound of her cry and to his own voice which had not yet been reduced to nothing. There was a small boy who had stood with a basket of pears and who began to cry as if the play of this life had become too great; men kicked at the pears and the juice ran a trail like memory across the dirt.

Do you deny it, the bearded one said to him, and he struck him in the ribs so that he doubled. He had not the strength to fight his way out. He could only answer in the old voice which had shrunk and withered from the business of lying and truth and the voice that came had the flatness of one who had been practiced in saying the wrong thing for the right motives. I did not know, he said. The bearded one spat and the thing that went down his face gleamed. You knew, he said. The preacher, seizing the moment, lifted his hands and began to speak of good order and of the shame of false prophets and of the need to purge the body of those who would taint it with deceit. His voice had that way of making the righteous appear enormous.

The crowd roared now for something clear and simple and the bearded one held him fast like a dog that has caught a rat. They dragged him from the wagon and into the center of the ring and someone fetched a length of rope and a noose was made with fingers that had the knowledge of things they had done before. Her hands were white where she gripped the strap of their bundle and the child beneath her belly had moved as if answering. The rope that they snaked around the tree was thin and had the make of some boaters' line rather than a rope to kill a man with, but it would do as most rope does. They planned to humiliate then to punish. The bearded one had set his teeth about the pleasure of demonstration.

He had but an instant in which to choose where his life hinged and he remembered in that instant a whole ledger of failures and small wrongs that had followed him like a train. He could lie and tell them he was the prophet and that all this was a test and perhaps fools would buy the lie and let them go; he could confess and that would be the end; he could stand and attempt to turn them with a rousing speech; or he could say nothing, the worst move of all but the one which sometimes betrays you into survival. He remembered the face of the woman and how she braced her jaw and revealed no plea. There was a small quiet in her like the waiting bell of a church. He looked at her and he saw in her mouth the raw plan of leaving. If he could give them a story they would not follow, perhaps. If he could be the martyr here, take their anger upon himself, perhaps they would let the woman go. The calculus of such things is never arithmetic but hunger and the clock of the mob had only the simplest algorithm.

The preacher lifted his eyes and called for order and his voice spilled out like oil. Who among you will show mercy by letting truth come forth at this hour, he said, and the crowd turned at the sound of their own rustle as if to see what sort of mercy would appear. The bearded one stepped forward and pointed at the man and said he had been a liar and that there could be no saving such as this. He set his foot upon the man's chest and pushed him down and his breath left him for a moment and the world filled with the old, familiar white glare of a man staring at the underside of the sky.

There was a small boy who tugged at the preacher's sleeve and the boy cried out I saw him with the others at the river, and his words had the truth's small size again. He saw the preacher's mouth form a promise as old as history and he felt the weight of that promise like a debt. If I say I am the prophet, they will tear me for it or make me of gold, and neither are things he could endure. His mouth opened and a voice came, but it was not wholly his, it was something like the reused language of a sermon and it answered for him in a way that startled him.

Listen to me, the voice said, and the crowd leaned closer as if a dog had begun to speak. I have been with the prophets and I have been with the poor and with both I have found the same thing, he said. Not that miracles do not come but that they come in ways you cannot buy or sell. The sound that he made was strange and high and borrowed, the preacher's ear would later record, as if a man were speaking out of his own ruin and the ruin of those about him. The bearded one snorted and drove his knee into the man's side. The man kept talking and his voice grew and was not in tone the same as it had been. There was a cadence to the words, a fall and rise like a harvest wave.

He spoke of nights when the sky had not opened and men had put their faces into the cinders and watched the ash keep them warm and called that a blessing. He spoke of children who were given names by people who had lost their own and who learned to sing in the absence of fathers. He spoke of the river and of the way it keeps its grief to itself. The preacher's face hardened as he listened because the voice sounded like something that did not desire his coin. The crowd at first listened only because when someone begins to speak they will let even the faintest thing have its say as if one must collect points for charitable hearings. Then something happened that had not been expected. The cadence became gospel and the man's words, which he had meant to use as a lie perhaps, found the truth of an invocation and the sound that came out of him was the thing that can seize a small town and rearrange its affections.

The bearded one struck him across the face and blood fountained from under his lip and he tasted the iron and the words did not stop. She waited until the dusk and until the bearded one had gone off with his kettle and then took up the small shawl from where it lay and wrapped it about the child's head as if there were some late midwifery in the air. She made no plan that had the shape of a plan she only rose and took by the road that led back to the ford and the log and the marsh and the river gone on as if it had been nothing more than a mechanism for the world to continue in. She walked with the knife at her belt and the small satchel close and the thing that in my tongue we call courage was there in her at no one's bidding. The wind had teeth and the gulls flew hard against it and night was not far.

When she came to the place by the bluff where three men had camped the night before there was no sign of them, only the faint circle of their fire and where the ground had been warmed. In the cold in the morning she rose and walked west along the river where grass bent and the reedbeds sighed and where now and then a man would look up and nod and go on and there were more and more of those nods for people had begun to live by the contract that said look no deeper than you must. She carried the small bundle and the knife and there was no more to the world but the path and the river and the strange, honest hunger that is the business of a woman with a child to feed and a dead man to remember. She did not know if she would find a town that would take her in for a while or if she would sleep in some barn under the ribs of a cart and for a while it made no difference. That which is required is taken in the step and in the breath and it will do.

Far behind, where the little settlement had been, the preacher climbed upon his wagon and read his book aloud to the men who remained and the bearded one counted his gains and the world continued its commerce of salvage and sermon and the river cut on like a man that will not be reasoned with. The child's face lay warm against the woman's arm and she felt in that small warmth the outline of a future that might be cobbled together from the scraps of what had been left. There was shame and there was love and there were the small instruments of both and the road went on and the sun went on. And that is how it came to be that the dream came round about them and the dream came to life and the living learned from it what the dead had always understood, that men will always find a need to make of someone a god and then to find fault with that god and to throw him down.
