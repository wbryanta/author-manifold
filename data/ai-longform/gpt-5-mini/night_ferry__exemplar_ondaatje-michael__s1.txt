I watch the shoreline diminish until it is a thin knife of lights and the ferry turns its face to open water. There is a box on my lap, a cardboard box with the flaps pushed down so nothing slides out, and inside the box are things I have chosen like fragments of a life that might still belong to someone: a school photograph browned at the edges, a letter folded once, once again, a key with a rusted ring, a small coin with the year my mother died punched into it where the silver has thinned, and a watch with a cracked crystal that wound two summers and then stopped. I had told myself I would not bring anything that could be mistaken for a talisman. I had told myself I would bring only the facts. The facts would be short and sharp. But the things in the box are the facts I know — the way a name sits in the palm of the hand.

There is a cafe up ahead, empty save for a pair of fisherman in green oilskins, their faces hammered and soft like driftwood, and a woman with a blanket around her knees who is reading a book from that kind of library that feels like a language of its own. The engines are steady, a low constant breathing in the belly of the ship. Night has a skin of oil on it here, and the waves lift, let go, lift again. Every so often the hull thumps against the water and the ferry gives a small apology that runs through the soles of my feet.

I had not planned to come. The phone call from the coroner’s office had the thinness of a message left by someone who has refused the luxury of emotion. "Is there anyone to identify?" the woman had asked. There had been a pause, then the name. I said a name out of habit and then the long silence like a coping mechanism. "Not sure," she said. "He had no papers." She read a number off a list in her ledger and then the place: a small town on the other side of the strait, a town I had not visited since I was a child. The voice had the way of people who spend their lives at false dawns — polite and worn. It had something of the ferry itself, said through the box and then hung up.

I remember the strait from boyhood in the way that one remembers a bruise. It was always wider than the map suggested, and the towns on each shore looked like leftover words — truncated, softened at the edges by their distance. We had once thrown a rope from one cliff to another and dared the gulls; we had once hidden in a boathouse until the light cooled to blue and my brother pressed his ear against the hull of an old dory and said very seriously that the sea keeps everything and returns very little. Caleb said that then: the sea keeps everything. He sounded older than his years, as though the words were stitched into his chest. He used to walk the docks barefoot in winter and pretend he had a boat that belonged to him. He left a lot of things on the water. I am here to discover whether a body that was brought from the rocks under a headland is one of those things.

People on ferries are split open in different ways. There are families in small armies with sleeping children; there are men who sleep against the mica of windows and wake up with a face for strangers; there are night workers who move like hands around the waist of the world, making the passage for others. I locate a corner and sit with my box. I have on a jacket that is too warm and shoes that creak just enough to make me feel noticed. A child passes, looking as clean and raw as any country: mouth full of sleep and the smell of fries. The woman with the blanket looks up and gives me an unreadable nod. I hold the box like a small animal that I have come to see for some reason.

The boat's announcements come in a voice that slurs the hours into each other. "We will be making landfall in approximately one hour," says the voice, and the word "landfall" is theatrical enough to make you imagine old sailors with their hats in the wind. I had told myself to be practical: know the marks, the scars, the tattoos; know the way a face ages under salt and sun. But there are holes in the remembering. There are holes where our shared life was burned out by an argument whose date I cannot pin and whose nature I have not allowed myself to own. We always thought of each other as brothers in the way brothers are also strangers who have met too many times to still be strangers. There were things we left unsaid. There were reasons for absence that became long enough to be considered part of the landscape.

On the wall opposite me, an old passenger has folded a map and is tracing, with a thumb the way one traces a route one has taken alone. I have the photographic memory of certain items — the way my brother's teeth curved oddly at the front, the scar along his left thumb where a saw once lacerated the skin in my father's shed, the way he could fit a whole orange into his hand as a joke and then squeeze the juice into the gutter. These are small things called proof in the private law we had together. When the coroner had asked me if I knew of any unique marks I had recited them like a prayer. He had asked if there were any identifying items on the body. I had said there might have been a watch, an old watch that stopped the summer he left, and the woman on the phone had paused and said that there had been something vague and small, as if it had been wrapped in seaweed for weeks. She had said also that it might be not him at all, that sometimes fishermen of the shape of men washed ashore and took on one another's stories.

The ferry becomes, in the night, a place where memory and possibility cross like tides. The engines are the throat that fills the boat with rumor. I open the box, holding the flaps, and select the photograph from ten years ago: my brother glancing sideways, his hair in that particular comb he favoured, laughing in a way that always began in his chest and then spilled into his face. They had taken that photograph in a kitchen with light from a little window high up. I know every shadow on his jaw. I place the photograph on my knee like an offering.

There are things I still want to say to him — to say whether for an apology or for the lazy summation of absence — and yet I have learned the way that saying names can anchor monsters. To call someone by his name is to enforce a life upon his corpse. At the same time, not naming is to let the sea decide. I have been told that the sea is an economy of forgetting; it has its own manners. But I have known instances of its generosity, too. Once, when we were children, we had dropped a string of beads into the river and swam after them until the cold pushed the muscles into slow surrender, and then a week later the necklace washed ashore under a low moon, tangled with seaweed and perfectly intact. My brother had laughed and said the sea had returned its debts. I have not left all of my debts with the water, but some have been so large I can still hear them moving.

The harbour approaches and the lights of the town sharpen like stitches. Fishermen lean over rails and talk in a language of knots. The ferry's ramp breathes open. I have in my pocket the phone number of the coroner's office and also, in a small paper, the name the woman had spoken. I walk with the box as though the cardboard were a casket I had been allowed to carry. Ahead of me, the town is a reduced world: a line of shops closed, a pub with a light and a television set playing some indifferent late match, a bakery where an old man takes a cigarette between his lips and counts nothing. There is a taxi. The driver asks me where I want to go and I say the hospital. He says there is no hospital anymore — just a clinic and a funeral home that does the work courts used to do. He says it as if the rearrangement of towns were a simple matter. We stop in front of a building with a small parking lot. The wind has a taste of iron.

Inside the coroner's office, things smell of bleach and coffee. There is a young man at reception with a tie that refuses to be pulled straight and a woman with tired eyes who checks my name against a list and then hands me a small paper badge to wear on my lapel. The badge is white and has the word "IDENTIFIER" printed on it. I feel ridiculous with the badge. I feel old. The coroner is older than I imagined, a woman with a voice like dry flour but with an attention to the small facts that steadies her. She does not ask for my long history. She asks about marks, then about the time I last saw my brother and whether there was estrangement. There is a part of me that wants to make excuses, to explain the months and the months until the coroner looks bored with personal narrative and wants fact. She is a keeper of edges.

The body is on a gurney in a room where the light is flattened to a single square from a fluorescent fixture. There is an antiseptic smell somewhere under the salt smell. The room is small and smells like a washing machine. I have to sign a paper to be allowed to see. The signature is the thing they want, as if a name on a line is the only way to make a stranger stop being a stranger. I sign. I am shown in. There is someone else in the room: a young nurse who has seen a lot of bodies and is now seeing another and is practicing professional neutrality like the way birds practice flight.

I stand at the side of the gurney. The sheet has been folded back from the face in the careful way of people who have done this many times, like fishermen unrolling nets. The face is not entirely a face I remember. Death rearranges features the way tide rearranges the shore: a rocker of lines where once there had been a laugh. I look for the front teeth that had an odd curve, for the scar on the thumb. The thumb is pale and washed; the teeth are still crooked in the familiar way. For a moment I let myself believe that the body is indeed my brother. Then I think of how the ocean has a habit of giving up look-alikes, men who could be cousins with the same cheekbones.

There is a small mole behind the right ear that I had once kissed because we were foolish boys who joked at each other's vulnerabilities. I don't know if memory can be trusted to hold such a tiny landmark. The coroner is patient, waiting for me to do what I must. There are no dramatic signs. The body has a line of green algae along an eyebrow like seaweed ornament. The hair is thin in places and matted. He looks older. Identification, I learn that day, is like a negotiation between what was and what looks like what was. I run my fingers over the cheek, careful, careful, as if by touching I could convince the living to return.

I have brought, in the box, a ring my mother wore and a cigarette packet with a particular crease that Caleb used to carry as a joke. I take out the watch. It has coral salt under the cracked glass and the hands have stopped. I hold it for a moment and then place it under the sheet, on the chest, because the coroner has said sometimes people bring things to see if the body recognizes them as if the dead remembered their possessions like sleeping things. The nurse looks at me with a kind of gentle bewilderment. "You know," she says, "sometimes people come from cities and other lands and they look for any small thing to anchor a name. It helps us." She has the compassion of practice.

When I draw the sheet back enough to see the left hand, I see that the knuckles are nicked, the nails have the blue lines of someone who worked with rope or with cold, and there is a scar along the forearm that runs like a pale river. I think of the saw he used to use with my father; I think of the fire we had when we were teens, a small blaze in the yard, and the way he had burned his forearm trying to stuff wet wood into it. The memory sharpens into a certainty. It is not proof, but it is enough. I tell the coroner it is him. I tell the coroner his name. My voice wobbles and then steadies. The coroner watches as if she were reading a small script that sometimes works and sometimes doesn't. She asks me how sure I am and I say I am as sure as I can be from a face that the sea has tried on.

There is a paperwork ritual that is businesslike: forms to sign, questions about next of kin, a request for any last contact. They ask whether there were disputes. Were there threats? Debts? A relationship? I have to give addresses. I give the one where our mother used to live, which is now a bare lot with a lone apple tree. I find myself explaining the estrangement in a way that feels like inventory: years of silence, a fight over money, a slip of words at a funeral years ago that had been given too much gravity. The nurse nods with a practiced sorrow. The coroner asks if I want to take a photograph of the face to later compare with other photographs; I say no because the photograph I have is already in my box and because I do not want my last image of him to be the blue of that fluorescent light.

There is a part of me that expects a tectonic shift — an absolution delivered by the sight of the body, as if the sight could stitch together errors of years. But intimacy is not so easily administered. I feel like someone who has been given a letter with a tear in it and asked to reconstruct the final sentences. The coroner hands me a small form that says "Declaration of Identification." There is a place for my name and for the relation. I have to write "brother" on the line and it becomes suddenly a ceremony, as if putting the word down seals not only the documents but the life. I write. The pen makes a small hollow sound on the paper and I imagine the sea hearing it and registering the act.

Outside, the sky is beginning to harden to grey. The town's single road is waking up. I walk slowly back to the ferry, carrying the empty box now, which seems lighter than it ought. There is a weight that remains where the body lay, like a second presence inside my chest, and it travels with me across the parking lot. The taxi driver asks if I will need a ride back and I say no. I want to walk with the happening of things cooling around me. A gull takes a low path and cries.

On the deck the wind is the same as it had been when I left, salt and onion-skin cold. The ship's rail is wet and I lay my hands on it and watch the water break. In the distance the lights of the shore become a smear, and then the darkness reasserts itself. There is a small café on board where the night workers drink tea and the coffee is black enough to make a mark on men who have no expectation of sleep. I sit in a corner and take out something that had been in the box: a note folded small, a child's handwriting that says, "Don't go." It is a joke we had once, a scribble my brother left on the windowsill when he left the house. I read it three times and then fold it and put it back into my pocket. I have not yet decided whether to place something with him, whether to let him carry a token across.

To identify a body is to invent a continuity. This is not a metaphor; it is a law of practical compassion. When you place a name on a corpse, the world responds: death notices are printed, a church will ring a bell, a plot is marked in a cemetery with soil that reads as sanctioned. I had the urge, when I left the room, to go back and ask again if I had imagined the mole, the scar, the smallness of the watch. To ask the coroner if she ever makes mistakes. But those are not the things she will answer. An identification cannot be an academic exercise. It is a kind of coronation. The coroner, in the end, is an oracle. She declares that with what passes for evidence, the name may be fixed.

There are memories I have not told anyone because their truth might make other truths fall. There is a night when we were young and the river ran high and we climbed a pier that kept drifting into the sky, and he said he would leave. I remember his back as he walked away; I remember the exact way his coat swung. We had a last drink in a kitchen and my sister said something cutting and he left because he could not stand the small things that had been said; he loved drama less than he loved freedom. He left without violence, just walked and walked until he wasn't visible. Years later there would be a rumor of a man seen in a harbour café in a city two hundred miles away. Once I received a postcard with an unfamiliar handwriting and no signature, just a fish stamped over a crude map of some place south. I had kept that map in the box for a long time, as a thing to prove that he had existed in more than memory. I had left it hidden because to hold on to the map was to indulge in the fantasy that I might one day find him on a bench somewhere.

On the crossing, people begin to move into their places: a man reads a book whose spine is soft and browned; a woman cries for reasons that are not mine to know; a pair of teenagers laugh heartily enough to make private things public. The sea has a way of making voices small. I watch the dark line of the horizon for a long time until the ferry's pattern of waking starts the lights below. When the stewardess asks if I'm all right I nod and keep my gaze steady on a piece of dark water that looks like a bruise.

The next day, when I get a message on the phone about what must be done — the certificate, the release — the coroner says, "You have done the right thing." She does not say much else. There is a bureaucratic softness to grief that I have not developed a taste for. There is also the way the town treats the returning bodies with a kind of matter-of-factness: they wrap them in linen the way you wrap bread, or perhaps they place them in zinc boxes as if preservation were a tax. My brother's body will be moved. I will be asked about burial. I will be asked if there is anyone else. There are only me and a few names that have been given to me like checkmarks.

Driving back out of the town, I pass the headland where the rescue had found him. The rocks look like teeth after a long examination. I stop and get out of the car and walk to the place where the sea meets the land in a complicated negotiation. There are a few bottles lodged in a crack and an overturned crab pot. The tide is low and the stones are slick with an algae that looks blue like an old bruise. I pick up a smooth pebble and hold it in my hand. The pebble fits the hollow between the joints of my fingers like an old relationship. I think of placing it on the chest when I had seen him, like a primitive currency. Instead, I keep it and put it in my pocket.

It is strange how identification simplifies the world. The rest of life proceeds with a strange new clarity, as if the body having been named frees other things for attention. The arrhythmia of my years settles a little. I make a list of calls to be made. I say goodbye to the idea that we will ever have a long conversation about the things that had gone unsaid. I find myself, oddly, able to recall things I had forgotten, some of them luminous: the pocket-sized atlas we used to study; a joke about a man who kept his socks in the freezer, and the way my brother had played piano badly in the kitchen until the neighbors complained. The catalog of trivialities seems suddenly important.

At the funeral home there are arrangements to select. The director walks me through fabrics and plans like a man who arranges the breathing of others. There is a sense in him of not being allowed to be private for long. He makes jokes to fill the white places. He asks if there will be a service and I say yes, because it feels right to let the town hear a name that has been neglected and then rehearsed. Names are a debt; they must be paid publicly, lest they be repossessed by the sea.

There is a moment before the service when I stand alone in the room where the coffin rests. The room smells of polished wood and candle fat. The coffin is a soft rectangle in a way I had not anticipated. My brother looks like a man asleep in perfect alignment, and I cannot reconcile that image with the idea of him as a man who left the shape of our lives. I place the photograph on the little pillow by his head. I have the watch in my hand and the coin punched with the year of my mother's death. The watch is a lie now. It never measured the hours that counted. I press the coin into his closed palm and let the act be the small scene that ends or begins something. No one sees me and it is not important that they do. The service is simple, with hymn-struck voices like nets. People say their names and say their brief note of condolence. Someone mentions the sea in a sentence like a thing to be both feared and revered.

Afterwards, outside the church, the sky has that leveled gray that makes faces like carved wood. I walk to the vehicle that will take him to the cemetery and I imagine for a second that I might fold all the things of our past into the car, but that is impossible. Names are not enough to reconstruct years. They are only a small place to tie a boat.

On the way back to the ferry, I pass the pier where we had once thrown stones for hours, experimenting with the patterns they made. I pick up something to remember which I have not yet decided. I have been practicing forgiveness like a silence — using it to fill the gap where conversation no longer stands. Some people will say I did not do enough, that I waited too long. Others will say I did what I could. Those assessments, made by those not in my chest, will float along without anchoring.

The ferry takes me back under a sky unstiff with morning. I stand on the deck and watch the town like a scar retreating. I think of the possibility that the body I had identified was not him at all. For a long time this thought lives under the skin of the day. It is not scandalous to admit; our bodies sometimes become placeholders for the lives we invent from absence. Sometimes misidentification anchors a grief that was in any case waiting for a name. I allow myself the room for doubt and then I let it go because, in some deep and private register, doubt is a luxury you cannot afford when you want to create something like belonging.

On the crossing, a woman leans her head on my shoulder — not with intimacy but with the exhausted consent of those who travel often. She sleeps in her coat with a small snore. The coffee in the café tastes like bad news. On the table in front of me is the box now empty and flattened with its flaps open like a mouth. I send a text to a cousin to say that I have done what was necessary. The cousin replies with an old joke about the summer we tried to build a raft. The jokes keep us human in the face of such things.

Back in the city, there are calls to be returned, paper to be filed, and the odd kindness of those who come to say their condolences and ask about the funeral. They tell me the town lined the road; fishermen went to the headland and tossed a simple wreath. Someone brought a small boat with chaperoned oars. They said that the sea had been calm that day as if to honor him with a hollow of its own. I wanted to believe it. I wanted to believe that the strait had performed one last courtesy. It is a comfort to think the sea makes repairs. It is also true that storms arrive without consultation.

At night, when sleep is a commodity I cannot purchase, I take the photograph out and hold it under the lamp. Memory is a kind of laboratory where the scientist is a boy with his finger pressed to a jar. I look for the small mole behind the ear, the scar on the thumb, the way the hair fell across his forehead. I try to measure the distance between the image and what I had seen. The difference is slight and then large. The two do not cancel each other. They exist like river and road, meeting in a ford and then passing on as if they had never known one another.

There are nights when I think of him as a stranger with a name. There are nights when I think of him as a brother I failed. I am aware now of how often people go to the sea to leave, to change their skin with salt and wind. The sea does not care. It consumes, then reveals. It is partial and occasionally tender. It can be a ledger of returns. Sometimes it gives back a necklace intact and sometimes it returns nothing. We are all, in the end, indebted to the elements in one way or another.

Months later, I find myself returning to the harbour in the small way of those who have been given the right to return in memory. I go to the headland and stand with a pebble in my hand, the same pebble perhaps that I had picked up before or a different one; it does not matter. I toss it into the water and watch it sink. My name is not like the sound of the pebble. The pebble makes a small ring and it is gone. I think of the letter I never sent. I think of the map of the places he may have passed. None of these things create the man; they only create the context in which I can say his name without collapsing.

To identify a body is to do something like admit you have been in a story that will never be fully told. I have fixed a name to a face and a place to a passing. I have not resolved the quarrel between us. Perhaps quarrels rarely end cleanly; perhaps they are carried forward by the living for no better reason than to make sense of their own absence. I visit the grave in spring when the grass is starting to stitch itself into green and when the sky, in a way I have come to notice, almost apologizes. There are sea thrift flowers at the margin of the plot like small pink stars. I place the photograph on the stone and sit for a long time. The wind has the scent of farther places. I breathe in the salt and think of all the names the water has swallowed and all the ones it has given back.

When I leave the cemetery I do not feel absolved. I feel consolidated, as if the life had folded into some coherent packet I can now carry. Identification was not the end; it was a way station that made possible the rituals people accept because they crave order when things are chaotic. I walk back to the car with the photograph folded in my coat and feel, for the first time in years, something like a small sorrow that is not jagged. It is a sorrow that will settle in the ribs like a pebble and remain. That, too, is a gift.

At home, I put the watch on the shelf with the other things that have no time left to live. It is a small museum of absence. Sometimes I take the watch down and wind it, knowing it will not move, and listen to the silent mechanics as if they were a friend. There is a comfort in repeating small rituals: a cup made the same way every morning, the photograph turned to face the wall for a while, the box flattened and stored under the bed like a record in waiting. It is not enough to cure the old wounds, but then perhaps nothing is.

There are strange consolations. Time moves unevenly and recollection sifts its own materials. I find myself in the kitchen one evening, washing a plate, and I can suddenly hear his laugh, the exact pitch of it as if recorded on an old tape. It is momentary and it is mine. From somewhere far removed the sea asserts that it is still keeping a ledger and will in time decide whether it keeps him or gives him back to us in a different form. For now, I have his name on a piece of paper and the sensation of having done the necessary. This is not small. It will not soothe everything, but it will be the chorus to which other things might be tuned.

The strait remains the same as it ever was: narrow in places, wide in others, a thin place that divides and also joins. To cross it is to enter those liminal spaces where facts and myth meet. I have learned that the work of identification is not a simple retrieval; it is a remaking. It is also a permission to grieve publicly and to arrange into some order what had been scattered. I do not know, in the end, whether the man in the morgue will be remembered as the same person he was while he was alive. I know only that I have named him and that the sea has been given its last debt. I stand now by the window and watch the light on the water change in a way that is almost kind. I drink a cup of tea that is too hot and I leave the photograph face up on the table, like a small map. The ferry travels its routes whether we cling on to it or not. The strait is still there, patient and indifferent and sometimes generous. I have given the sea one of our names back again and in doing so I have made a place for my brother to be called, and that must be part of what we mean by home.
