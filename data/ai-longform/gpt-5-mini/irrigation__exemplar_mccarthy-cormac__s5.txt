They worked from the hour when the shade still lay long and good beneath the trees and the sun had not yet learned to pick at the skin. The orchard was a thing of rows like ribs and the trees stood bent and patient in it, knotted with scars and bearing what fruit they could muster, small and hard and dim as a child's promise. A line of pipe ran down the valley from the headgate at the ditch, a plait of dark plastic half buried in the bad clay, and it was that pipe that had brought them out in the morning, that and the small stubborn thing in the chest of a man who will not be told the last time has come while breath yet moves him.

Silas found a break near the third row, by the old pestle tree that their father had set there when Abel was small because he said the pestle would bring them luck or at least it would drive the dolt deer off the fruit. The pipe had split at a joint, the soil heaved about it like a wound, and water ran from the break in a pale hissing stream that made the clay slick and black and stank of the dissolved iron in the soil. The trees were lean for water as a town drunk is lean for a man's warmth. Heat rose in clear sheets off the turned earth. Crickets made a high wire of sound from the hedgerow. In the distance a truck moved and its sound was dull in the hot air and then gone.

They set down the auger and the shovel and the lengths of new pipe they had brought in the back of Silas's pickup. The new pipe lay pale and stiff in the shade, still holding factory memory, while the old pipe spat and licked at the dirt. Silas knelt and put his thumbs to the split and felt how cool the water was, the living pulse it had, how it wanted to be returned to its course. He worked the jag of earth away and found the cracked sleeve and pulled it free. Abel stood with his hands on his hips and a rag twisted about his neck, a silence on his face as heavy as a hat. He was younger than Silas by a dozen years and the ridge of his cheek still carried the quickness of a man who had not yet learned to accept the things he might not change.

You want me to dig, Abel said. I got the trench, Silas said. I hadda.

There was a pause as if the heat itself were listening.

Who you writin to, Abel said.

Silas smiled without laughter. Ain't nobody writin me today. You lookin for work?

Abel spat and a brown crescent hit the dirt. Probably be bad damned lucky if I find any, he said.

They worked with a kind of economy of motion. Silas had the ear of a man who had learned the land's languages; his hands knew where roots slept and where the compacted soil would give. He dug and cleaned and set the cuffs and couplings and Abel held the pipe straight while Silas fitted it, tamping the clay around the joint with careful blows so that the fit would take the drag of the dirt and the earth would settle and not pull loose the new union. Once you were down in close to these things you could hear how the orchard was breathing, the soft scratch of a woodpecker in the far cottonwood, the slow clack of a squirrel's tail, a bird that never stopped complaining.

You gonna sell it, Abel said through a mouth half full of dust.

Silas kept working, his hands quick and sure. I been thinkin bout that, he said. Ain't made no step.

You gonna sell it to Harrowton?

He looked up then and the movement was heavy as if a bell had been struck. They'd had a letter once, a letter from a man who wore a pressed shirt and a tie and who wrote about development and potential and improvements and the orchard's suitability for small lot estates and the matter of a clean title. A neighbour had taken less than a mile away and now had a string of houses that would hold a future and a pasture, and Harrowton was the man who had come with glossy plans and the same earnest, smoothed paper voice.

Maybe, Silas said. Maybe it's what a man does. Sell when the coin's offered and the story's ended.

Abel laughed then, but it was a laugh without humor, a short spasm. Ended, he said. Whose story you talkin bout? Ours or some folks in suits?

Silas went on tamping. He had a kind of patience with the earth that gave him a face like a map. You talk just like Pa used to talk, Abel said. He was always sayin if you got it in your hands you better hold on to it cause somethin'll come along that'll want it. But he put his thumb on the fig tree at the corner and said if a man don't have no choice he can learn to choose.

Pa'd be damn near rollin in his grave with the fruit on these trees, Abel said. Ain't nothin left on them but the bones.

You think I don't know that? Silas said. You think I ain't counted every pit and every wire? He set the new section into the socket and turned the coupling and the pipe took the new shape with a dry click. It fit as if it had been made for the place. There was a small rush as the water took the new line and seemed to test it. For a moment the choice of the day lived in that sound.

Abel sat against the trunk of the pestle tree and watched. The skin about his eyes was raw from the sun. He had been in town in the spring, home for a handful of days, and he came back with a stain upon him, a kind of city manner that did not fit against the smudged dignity of the orchard. He had been offered work in a warehouse unloading trucks. He would not take it because it would be a way of high-tide money for some time but then what was left? He'd been seeing women in colors and bottles and he'd been telling himself he might be happy among people who were not afraid of finishing a thing by selling it to somebody who had always wanted it. He had a map of other lives and he traced them on the palm of his hand whenever he slept.

You ever been tempted to go, Abel said, to take your bag and go see what the lights are like? Not like they is here, but like they are in the pictures.

Silas laughed then and it was a sound that bit at itself. You and me and Pa, he said. Pa said the world was only a story told to keep the light on your porch long enough for you to find your way. He had his own idea on things. He'd hole up in that house in winter and he'd bake bread and he'd keep the lantern filled. I remember him settin on the stoop and watchin the moon go by like a coin. He had a way of seein you right through. He could speak to a thing like he could make it stand up.

Nothing left in him to speak with, Abel said. It don't matter what you say if the ground won't answer.

Silas worked his hands until his knuckles paled, and the sweat beaded under his hair. He spoke slow as if working words into the soil. The ground answers if you talk to it in the way it knows, he said. If you get at the root and you lay down and you listen. But you gotta keep at it. You don't get up one mornin and say I done with this and the earth don't remember. She remembers everything. She carries it down in her. She holds like a ledger.

You talk about her like she was a woman, Abel said. Land ain't no woman, Silas. It don't forgive.

There's a way a man can go on that ain't forgive or forget, Silas said. There's a kind of accounting that don't need a judgment. He looked at Abel and there was a thing in his face like an old photograph that had been rubbed while still wet. You sell this place you sell the want for it in your face, he said. You hand it to them and they'll call it progress. They'll plant some green lawns and they'll sell the nights back to the road.

That's just words, Abel said. You always do that, you put it in a book. They sell you a good price and you put it in your pocket and you get that relief. You get away from the rust. You get a bed that don't creak and a woman that don't look at you like she was a question. You'd set us up real good, Silas. You can see it. A little house under a roof, the car in the drive, a store you don't have to push.

Silas finished and pushed the pipe into its bed, and then he stood and stretched and the muscles of his back made lines like the furrows of the field. He looked down the lane where the orchard ran to the river, a pale ribbon to the cottonwoods that kept their heads like a crown. He had been there every year. When the frost came he would go out early with a lamp and lift the branches and shake the ice from the leaves. He had set traps for rabbits in his youth and he had shot one or two coyotes that came too close to the pens. He could name every cinder patch, every stone where a snake had been seen. The orchard was a map in his body.

You seen the notice, Abel said suddenly.

Silas nodded. The men down at the feedstore been talkin. Harrowton's been around again. Talk of a meeting with a man that owns acreage for mills. You been near the house? Ableness, he said. You know what all is in that safe?

Abel looked at him with an expression that was half pity half contempt. We ain't startin on that again, he said. We done took what we need from the house. You sold the old truck last winter. I kept some of Pa's tools. Ain't no paper there more important than a length of pipe.

Silas made a small noise in his throat like a soaked log. There's a letter come for you, he said. It sat on the table when I went by. You said you'd come take care of the lanolin, he said. He reached into his shirt and took out a folded corner of paper, stained at the edges. He held it out and Abel took it with two fingers and unfolded it like a man whose hand remembered the shape of a thing before his eye had.

From Harrowton it read. They offered a solicitor's tone and the cadence of purchase. We would be pleased to submit an offer would you be willing to entertain such a proposition. The paper smelt of someone else's leather and it had an ease to it that suggested money had been handled with a pleasant hand.

Who you gonna listen to? Abel said. You and I both got our own voices.

They finished the repair with the quiet of lovers at the last seam of a dress. Abel held the mended pipe while Silas packed the coupling with clay and the water took again with a surge that made a small hurricane of dust. For a while the orchard's breath seemed stronger and the leaves answered with a color closer to the green of memory. The pests hung from the boughs and ate the fruit still. A fly loped its course across the air. There was a judge of weather watching them, a sky without cloud, and a wind came up from the river that cooled the backs of their necks.

You ever think, Abel said, what Pa'd do if he was alive now?

Silas wiped his forehead. Funny thing, he said. He'd walk around with his hands like he was feelin for the edges. He'd sit by the gate and scratch at his cheek. He'd be thinkin on the river water, how it runs and how it don't run right. He'd talk to the trees. Pa had a strange way with words. He used to say the land's like a ledger and the debts got to be paid.

Paid to who, Abel said. We ain't got no money.

To somebody he said. Sometimes it's just you and the land and the thing you owe is yourself. He looked at Abel. You can't let some man walk up and take the story out of you.

Harrowton don't want our story, Abel said. They want the dirt and the view. They want the right to put a lawn there so the folks on the other side of the valley can look at something that shows they done well.

They made sandwiches then out of bread and cheese and a little dried meat that lived in a bag, and they drank water from a jar that had been kept cool by an old rag. They sat beneath the pestle tree and watched the dust sink like snow into the furrows. A hadstone plane of light lay across the top row and the trunks of the trees looked like vertebrae beneath it. Abel ate half of his sandwich and looked at Silas as if measuring him across.

If you sell it, Abel said, will you take me with you?

Silas blinked. Is that the price? he said. Is that what you want? I can give you enough for a truck and a cheap house and a new shirt. I'll put it in the bank so it don't burn. You been offering me plans like a man offers a horse a carrot, Abel said.

You ain't talkin like you want it to be sold, Silas said.

I want the fruit is what I want, Abel said. I want the trees to be fat again. I want to sit at the table and not hear the bank's voice in the floor. I want Lucy to have a room that don't shake when the wind comes. You wouldn't know that feeling, Silas. You been married once but you don't remember the smell of a woman's hands on the bread like I do.

Silas pressed his lips together and watched a bee work the seam of a blossom. He'd been married a long time ago and the woman had left with a trunk and a look that had been certainly final and left him with the orchard and with a stubbornness like a thing you keep because it was your only marriage. He had another way to pay. He'd been offered menial sums and his jaw had gone hard and he had kept quiet. He had a child, or had had. The child had gone to live with a sister in the city where they would give him a better promise of bread. He would not give up what remained to him. That was his prayer.

There's more to sell than the land, Silas said. There's a thing in the house that might have some value. There's a safe. And the note books.

Abel's teeth clattered a little. The notebooks, he said. You think money comes out of a book? Those books are what Pa kept. He wrote like he was building a house of words. He had lists and prices and something like reckonings he made on the heat of a day. You sell them and what you got is a memory in a paper. You can't pay the steady with a memory.

No, Silas said. But the city man don't buy memory. He buys things they can hang and photos they can put on a wall. He wants the view more than the roots.

They moved then among the trees, their shadows soft as a wash. They patched leaks in other places as the day warmed and the irrigation line showed itself in the open like a snake. They found another seam, a portion that had been gnawed by a root and had split in the heat, and they cut out a length and spliced it with a clamp that made a neat metallic ring. They worked with the shyness of men who understood the ways of their work and of their family. They hadn't spoken of the money in numbers, not yet, but there were things you could see in a man's hands that said as much.

Abel found a small plum that had fallen and he rolled it between his fingers and then ate it. The juice made a small dark trail on his chin. You remember when Ma used to can, he said. She'd sing a little while she did it. She wouldn't never sing in the yard, only in the kitchen. The jars would sit like lanterns on the shelf.

Silas nodded. He had the memory also, a faint light that came to him at odd times like a moth. The sound of a spoon on a jar lid. The smell of vinegar and sugar and the brown paper that they'd wrap the jars in for winter. Ma had been good to them. They'd sat at that table and they had their food and their little reprieves and a small faith that things would come about.

You thinkin bout goin down to the house? Abel said. You gonna see what's what in the study?

Might, Silas said. Might take a look. He stood silent a while as if the planets and the trees were in a discussion. You ever think birds like us, he said. They move off and they don't tend to come back. They don't ask for permission. They don't have the same ledger.

Abel tossed the pit of the plum into the grass and it struck a pebble and hopped away. Birds don't owe. They don't owe a man a thing. They come and they go and they eat and they leave behind other birds. You can't hold 'em.

Silas lifted his face. You think the land owe us? he said.

Abel laughed and it was a wet sound. I ain't sayin owe, he said. I say it ain't got no moral ledger. It's indifferent. People put names on it because they need to talk to something.

Indifferent, Silas repeated. He was thinking. The word was like a penny he turned. Pa used to say the land's a patient witness. It takes the blame for what's done upon it. It can't speak out but it remembers. He reached down and took up a fist of earth and let it sieve through his fingers. Look at it, he said. See those roots? See how they wind. See how they don't die easy.

Abel's voice came soft. Maybe it's better to trade what you've got for something you can hold without it bein weighed with what came before. Maybe you don't remember all the bad. Maybe you make new bads and they weigh less.

Silas closed his hand until the last grain fell. I don't know, he said. I don't want to have to live somewhere where the trees ain't mine.

Why do they have to be yours? Abel said. They ain't breathin with your name on 'em.

Because they was planted by our hands, Silas said. Because we watched the first bloom. Because I was here when he died. Because when the nights get mean down there and there's a cold in the house I go out and stand by the fig and I wait and I talk to it. I know it loves me. That might be foolish but it's what I got.

Abel watched him some time and then he took his hands and put them under his legs and folded them as if in prayer. You got me to think about that now, he said. I don't know if I want to be here when the last tree goes down. I don't know if I want to be here when the bank comes and they put those men in suits in the kitchen. But I don't like the idea of not knowin where my blood come from, he said. I don't like to think that part of me will be sold for a mall.

You like to think that part of you owns you, Silas said. But a land ain't a will. It don't have to obey.

They stopped for a while and sat under the pestle tree and the sun moved a little and the light upon them shifted. A hawk rode up the thermals and circled and then was gone. A dog barked in the distance, old and high. The river had a sound like a low voice without words. There was a particular weight in the air, a kind of pressure that made breathing a small labor. It was as if the world were leaning for a while on the two of them.

You ever think, Abel asked, what we think we'll do if we sell? Will we go someplace and start over? Will the new house have curtains? Will I get used to sleeping without the sound of the fruit in the back? Will you?

Silas looked at his hands. He had callouses that were like ridges on a coin. He had small hairs rubbed away at the base of his thumb by years of gripping. He thought then of the city, of a street where everything was sharp and new. He thought of money which had a sound like a bell and could be measured. He thought of fear also, an animal which had been in him since the night he'd first heard the bankman call and say there were arrears. He had a hunger that was for safety and a hunger that was for memory.

If we sell, he said, we got to be honest. You can't take half and call it whole. You can't walk into a bank and say give me less and expect it to be enough. If we sell we get a clean thing. We leave our debts. We get some comfort that isn't always the thin sort.

Abel nodded. What would you do with the money, he asked. The question was not subtle but it had a soft edge.

I'd give some to you, Silas said. I'd set aside enough to keep Lucy a room for herself. I'd get us both a roof that don't cry. I'd buy a truck that don't sweat. I'd maybe put out some cash for a place where we could keep a small patch of trees if we wanted, even though it'd be smaller and not what we've known.

You don't sound happy, Abel said.

No, Silas said. I ain't happy. I'm pragmatic.

They worked again after that and the day walked with them across the rows. They unbent a washed out trough, mended a gate, wrestled with a stubborn wheelbarrow that had a spoke like the leg of a crooked man. It was the sort of day's repairs that go on and on and do not mark the end. Repairs are the religion of men who cannot afford to worship their losses. As the sun climbed higher the air took on the color of hot iron and the men perspired and their shirts clung. They drank from a cheap thermos and spat and breathed and did not speak so much. The trees overhead creaked in that way a thing complains when burdened with a fruit trying to be born.

At noon they ate wet sandwiches and drank coffee that tasted of varnish. A dog came down from the hill and lay by the stump and put up its head at them as if attendance upon the brothers was part of its day. The dog had eyes like bread crusts and a muzzle grey with dust. It left when they rose and then returned again as if to say he would hold them now if he could.

You met Harrowton, Abel said.

Silas nodded. I seen him at the feed store. Big man, soft hands, he said. Had a way of tellin you what he'd pay and how it would feel to be rid of the worry. He called it vision.

He got a way about him that makes things seem smaller, Abel said. They'd carve up the place for sure. They'd want those houses where the view looks good, maybe set up some streetlights and charge for the water. The river'd be nicer maybe. People'd come out for a weekend and they'd bring their wives and show them things that look like the day has been made neat.

Silas watched the line of trees and the way the sunlight made a lace of the leaves. He thought about the price Harrowton had suggested. It would clear the arrears, it would put the bankman's voice at rest, it would leave a new door with a lock that worked. It would make him move from the house at the end of the lane and find a smaller life. There was comfort in the numbers, in the absoluteness of ink. You could plan in chalk and lay out a life like you plan out a field. Maybe the price was right. Maybe it was the only way to stop the rot. He was tired of the bank's letters.

You can always move, Abel said. We can find another place. Maybe less money but less talk.

Maybe, Silas said.

They went to the house in the afterglow of the sun, because a man cannot live in an orchard without circling back to the house like a godfather. It was an old place with a sag to its hips and a porch that needed some nails. The windows were smeared with dust and the front room had the last of the houseplants curled and brown. On the table lay the correspondence from the town and a small tin of nails and a pair of spectacles. Silas took a slow inventory and found that which was left of their father's care. He pulled open a drawer in the desk and there were the ledger books, his father's hand pressed into the paper, a dense scrawl of prices and measures and a godlike patience. He flicked the pages like a man who had worried at a wound.

You gonna sell them? Abel said.

Silas did not answer at once. He held the books like a rosary and there was a tremor about his mouth. They both knew these things had no currency in a bank unless some dealer found a glint in their margin. Pa's notebooks were more than arithmetic. They were memory. They were the shape of a man's days in ink. He had written of weather and weight and the conversations he had held with the cat. He had written the recipe for syrup that was given out in winter. He had drawn lines like the seams of a map. The man who had these things could make the earth speak.

You can sell them to some collector, Abel said. They got men who trade in that sort of thing. Maybe a man in the city who collects horticulture and old ledgers would give you a wad.

Maybe, Silas said. He sat down heavily and the chair made a complaint. He ran his fingers through his hair and then opened the safe. In the safe there were small things: a watch with a cracked face, a coin, a photograph of his father with a hat on, eyes like iron. He took them out and looked at them as if it was a duty he had been avoiding. The photograph had the back of a hand pressed against the glass where some nail in time had eaten at the surface and the face like a relic.

You ever think they'll be sorry for what they do? Abel asked.

Who? Silas said.

The men with the plans. The ones that sell your land.

They might be, Silas said. Regret is a thing that rolls like a loosened stone. There's men that'll buy these hills for the view and then find their view has been eaten away by traffic and their kids get mean. There's all sorts of people that find regret in a place like a flea. But sorrow doesn't put back a tree.

Abel went out to the porch and lay back and looked up at the rafters. The light came through the slats and made bars across his shirt. He thought of Lucy, how she had been small when they had last seen her and how now she would be full of world and wants. He thought of a bottle of beer he drank in the city and how for one moment it had felt like water from another life. He did not like the idea of selling the past but he did not like the idea of future hunger either.

There's a man come by last summer, Abel said. He was talkin about plantin grapes. He said grapes was where the money was. You seen the land down the other side? You ever smell the air across the river? There's a man that'll put in the vines and the tourists will come. He used to come to that market and he'd show a man pictures.

Silas folded the photograph and put it in his pocket. Grapes. Wine. They made a small sound, words like coins on a table. He thought of grapes on the slope in pictures, of vines pruned like the arteries of some patient thing, of summers that were full of labor and the beauty of a barrel. A man could be tempted by that trade. The river reflected the sky and seemed to say nothing.

You got to be sure, Silas said finally. You got to be careful what you let in. You lose a place and you can't ever put it back the way it was. But sometimes you hold and the thing you are holding chokes you. There's a point where holding is not love but a grave.

They sat late into the afternoon and the light came honeyed and low. They drank coffee from a pot that kept strong and their talk folded the way it did, circling and then moving on. Visitors had come seldom and when they did it was to measure in soft steps the value of their times. Harrowton would not hurry. A place like theirs was patient as a tomb. Men with plans were patient too because money could wait. The bank's letters were a different drum, a consistent soldier that kept time.

Night crept in and the orchard's silhouettes grew heavy. The air cooled and a sound like the thirst of the land lifted from the soil. They sat on the porch and watched the river where the moon had cut a skin into the water. A bat cut a black line across the sky and then vanished. In the far house a light came on and then off and the darkness took it in its hands like a thing being set to sleep.

What you think, Abel said. You gonna do it? He looked at Silas with the small earnestness of someone for whom a single answer could hinge the life he had left.

Silas set down the cup and there was a long time he held it between his palms as if it too were a thing he was choosing between. He remembered his father's hands in other hours, saw them in the lamplight while he counted seeds for winter, saw them when he scolded and praised in the same breath. He thought of the possibility of a small house with a working stove and a man taking his hat off and sitting down and being able to pay the bills. He thought of the rasp in his chest he carried like a secret illness. He thought of the orchard making its slow claim on him and how to be rid of it might be to be rid of the last tether.

I reckon, he said, I reckon we'll see what the fall brings. Harrowton's man'll come and they'll make their offer and we got to decide. We got to do it together. I ain't gonna sell you out. I ain't gonna take you with a lie.

Abel drew in his breath. You gonna take me with you if we sell?

Silas looked at him hard, the light sliding across his brow. I will set it aside and then we'll see, he said. You'll have your choice. But I ain't gonna lie, he said. If it's time to go we'll go. If it's not we'll stay and keep on patching. Life's a long road and sometimes it's a day of the birds and sometimes it's only the buryin. But we'll do it together.

It was an answer that did not make the ache go away, but it was an answer which allowed the night to come with hands newly warmed. Abel let the breath out and the sound was like a blade being put back in its sheath. He believed Silas as a man believes a rock will stay where it is.

They lay down then on the porch steps and watched the stars hang like seed beads in the black. The scent of the orchard rose all about them and made the night feel thick. A train in the distance called like a mourning thing and then was gone. There was a peace that is not the lack of trouble but the grace to hold it near for the time being. They were brothers and they were not simple men. They had argued and they had not come to swords.

Pa had died in the winter and he had left them the orchard with the smell of wood smoke in the corners. He had told them to look to the trees and to the river and to keep the lamp lit. He had given them a map of his life and a few small trinkets wound with love. There was a kind of duty in the old ledger books that was not monetary and the men knew this as if it had been whispered upon their birth.

You know, Silas said at last, I had a dream last night. It was a little thing. I dreamed that the river came up and it took the fence post by the gate. Not the whole orchard, just that one post. And it left the gate open and a fox walked through like he owned the place. He didn't eat the fruit neither. He just walked slow like the world had promised him nothing but what he could take.

Abel smiled in the dark. Maybe that's the world, he said. A fella can't hold all the posts. He'll just hold what he can.

They slept sometimes in the same bed as children had slept, their breathing like the measured walking of a second clock. The next morning the men were up and at their repairs again. Talking had gone on and their words had been kept in small safe places. They would go to the meeting with Harrowton if there was one. They would listen to the figures with their soft hands. They would drink him out like a cup. But the pipe had been mended and the trees had drunk that day and the orchard lay quiet and green in its own tongue. No decision was at once made. That night and the next they patched the leaks the way a man mends his own heart, by returning to the place and by putting his palms to the wound.

When summer grew toward the end of the month and the pears hung like small moons under the leaves and the ground had a blistered look where the sun had spent its hours, a letter came that would change things or not change them. It came in the mail and a man in the feed store had said Harrowton would come by. Harrowton's man came and he had a tie and a smile and he spoke of numbers the way a man draws water from a well and measures out hands of grain. He walked the line and he looked at the trees and he measured the slope and he made no bones. He had a plan with neat lines and artists' renderings.

After he left they sat on the fence and watched the sun go and the shadows lengthen and the orchard become the hush of a thing with fruit. There was a silence between them then like a wound that needed to finish bleeding. Abel rubbed his hands together until the skin was red.

What you think, Abel asked finally.

Silas folded his hands into his lap and the light of the coming dusk took the edges of his face and made him older than his years. I think we let them show their hand, he said. I think we'll ask for something fair and if it ain't fair we'll say no. I think we keep our eyes open. I don't want to live a life of numbers alone and I don't want to die with a house full of dreams no one will buy. He touched the ledger book that lay nearby like a talisman. We'll see what the paper says.

Abel put his head back and laughed a little. You and your paper, he said. You always make a soldier of the ledger. He paused and then his voice was low. I don't want to move away. I don't want Lucy goin to a place where she don't know the sound of a tree. But I don't want us to starve neither. He spat again the way a man clears a throat.

Maybe we don't have to make that choice right now, Silas said. Maybe the land will tell us yet. He took a breath and the orchard's air filled his chest with the smell like fruit and earth. We got time to think and we got time to say what we mean. We will not be tricked into a sellin we've not agreed.

Abel's face softened. He looked younger there in the last light. He's the sort of man who will love the orchard even if he leaves it, who will carry the smell of the sap in his shirts until the bones feel like home. He believed Silas then and the belief made him a better man.

They went in then and made soup from the scraps of the day and they ate under the low hum of the fan and the house seemed to shelter them as it had sheltered others. The night settled around the place like a blanket. They told each other stories of the trees and of their father and of the day the neighbour had put a roof on his barn too quickly and it had collapsed in a hard wind. They told the small histories of the land and of their little selves. When the hour came for sleep they went and lay down and the orchard breathed all around them and it was not lessened by the knowledge that men with paper had plans.

You know, Abel said in the night, that no matter where I go the smell of the orchard will come back maybe. It's like a rope.

Silas made a sound like a conceded thing. If we keep it, he said, we'll have to keep mending it. If we sell it we'll have to keep a new thing called enough. Both are burdens, he said. He was not afraid of the choice. He was afraid of the choice's aftermath, which is the unmooring of a life.

It rained twice in the month and when the orchard drank the leaves floosed with a clean sound and the fruit swelled like a prayer. Men in town talked of houses and of lots and of Harrowton and of how a place like this made a city man think of escape. The newspaper ran a small item about a proposal to put some housing near the river and people sent letters to the council. Men with suits came and they listened like they were at a sermon and then they left.

In the end there is always a thing that tilts a decision. It can be money or a sickness or a marriage or the stone a man kicks out of the road and finds a ring under it. For them it was a Wednesday when the call came from Lucy's aunt who hired them to see about a small boy who had a fever and who needed a place to be. She'd lost her job and she asked if they could take him for a while. It wasn't a push, Abel said. It wasn't an answer. It was a circumstance, a weight set upon the scales.

We can keep him for a spell, Silas said. We'll see how it goes. Money is a thing that will talk to you on the phone. It will make promises and then it will see what else it can buy.

Abel kissed his knuckles at the thought of Lucy and said he'll be back to the house the next morning to fetch the boy and that when he comes he will bring more than a trunk. He hoped for a new patience.

They did not sell that week. They did not sell the next. They took the boy in and he slept with a honesty that makes the heart loosen and the men found themselves listening to a new heartbeat at the foot of their porch. The orchard kept its rows and the pipes took the water. The men repaired a few more leaks and counted the cost. Harrowton wrote again and a man with a softer face came to eat at their table and left a small envelope that had a figure in it to be considered.

The ledger book remained unread by strangers. The safe kept its trinkets and the photograph of Pa sat on the mantle and looked on. The brothers kept at their repairs and sometimes they laughed until the sound passed into something like a blessing. They argued like men who were careful with each other, as if each word might bruise and might also heal. Where some such story might have ended with the sale and the departure there was instead a long slow bargaining with daylight. The orchard in June was patient and it would take its own time.

Listenin to them now, a man might have thought them simple men arguing as men will about trade and kin and the worth of a piece of earth. But underneath the talk there were bones of the world: blood and time, the way a man is taught to keep a promise by the things that outlast him. The orchard had been their father's song and now it was the chorus the two of them would sing in turns until the song's end.

When the summer finally moved to grape ripeness and the pears were heavy and bending, Harrowton's final offer came in. It was polite and it said many things a polite letter says. It put numbers on the table and it put terms: a payment in two installments, the buyers to assume the water rights, and a clause for an easement by the river. The number was less than what the men had hoped if the land could have been a vineyard or if the city men put a higher premium on the view. It was more than what they had right then on their own.

They sat on the porch and they read the letter and the sun was low and the orchard a fever of leaves. Abel put the letter down and then he took it and folded it and unfolded it like a small man going through a last habit. They had been brought to the point of such a choice before and they had never found ease.

We take it, Silas said.

Abel looked up like a man who had been struck by lightning and laughed and then his laugh curdled and he put it back in his pocket. You sure, he said. You sure you want to take it first?

I am, Silas said. We need to lay out our lives in a way not to swallow each other run by debt. We don't get many turns, Abel. He looked at the orchard then and there was something in his face like the horizon, and a look that said either buy them or bury them, he was not sure.

It was not the easy answer. It was a business decision and also the sigh of a man could be heard in it. They sat a long while and counted the reasons for and against as a math of the heart. The night came with slow patience and then they went inside and they took up the ledger and they called a man in town who was honest with a voice like a bell.

Make sure, Silas said. Count the water. Make sure the easement ain't somethin that'll steal the river. He spoke with the steadiness of a man who weighs anchors.

They wrote back to Harrowton and their words were careful. The price was accepted on three conditions. The water rights would be detailed in a way that the bankman would not take. The easement would be limited and the right of way would be paid for. And lastly they asked to be permitted to tend the orchard until the following harvest, to bring the dying things to the last of their fruit and not be cast from the place at the first rooster.

Harrowton answered in a polite haste. He agreed to the price and the terms, and then came to meet them and sign his name with a flourish and a small leather box and a key. He smiled when he signed and he said what good taste it was that had come to bear on him in choosing that place.

When the day came to close the deal Silas put the ledger book and the photograph and a coin in the safe and then he unlocked it and he put the key into the hands of Abel. You hold it until I come back, he said. Abel took the key like a chalice.

They signed their names and the men with the soft faces shook hands. Harrowton spoke about progress and building and how a man's life can better be arranged and how in a new place the children might know a differently made sky. He had an easy tongue and an easy smile. They let him have what he wanted this day and they kept in their pockets the weight of what they'd chosen.

They did not sell the orchard because they were cruel men nor did they sell it because they were simplistic. They sold it because they were tired of the bank's voice and because there was a boy who needed a room and a man who wanted a proper bed and because sometimes the world offers a ledger with a clean line and a man takes it. They set their names down and folded the letter and took the money and then they walked through the trees one last walk while their hands were empty and full at once.

They took the last of the jars down from the shelf and they folded the cloths and they put Pa's tools into a box. They worked through the harvest and they sold what fruit they could and the buyers with their trucks came and they loaded with a kind of silence. The harvest that year seemed to know it was the last under their hand, and the fruit tasted of parting.

When the buyer's men came with the stakes and the surveyors they measured and they told the names of streets they planned and they made marks in the dirt with pink ribbon. The boys of the town came to watch as if a horse was being broken and they became grown-up for a day in their watching. Men with trucks took down the sagging fence and the river took its edge in a new way and the orchard slowly became unstitched from their keeping.

On the last night Silas and Abel sat beneath the pestle tree and the moon was thin as a coin. They drank cold water and they said little. Abel looked over the place and he thought of the future and it unsettled him. Silas thought of a life with hearth and numbers and a roof he could count on. They had done what they said they would, and the last of the ledger was closed. The land would be different but a part of them would remain tethered like a calf to a post.

I reckon, Silas said finally, I reckon we did right.

Abel's voice was small. We done what we could. He put his hand in Silas's and they sat and the night kept them like a vessel on a deep sea. The orchard breathed one last breath under their hands and folded itself into memory.

And the land, when the men with their plans cut the first sod and drove stakes down for a new road, did what the land does. It received the footfall and it remembered both the men who had tended and the men who had paid. It was patient and it kept its ledger in layers beneath the soil. The river went on and the trees left their seeds in the damp and perhaps a volunteer sprig came up in a crack of a sidewalk when the rains were good. Some stories ended by selling themselves and some ended by the slow wearing away of a day's repairs. Both ways the world kept moving.

Silas and Abel left in the truck that had been once their father's and which they had repaired with old patience and a new resolve. They drove slow down the lane as if to memorize the turns and the dips. When they rounded the corner the orchard opened behind them like a hand closed, and they were not sure if what they'd done was a theft or a salvation. They were brothers and they had survived the day. They had mended the pipe in June and patched a life together with the cautious work of men who had to hold together two different kinds of hope.

In town, by a light, they drank and counted and then they went their ways. Abel would sleep in a small room that would smell of new paint and Lucy would sleep at the window with the sound of traffic like a lullaby. Silas would buy a jar of nails and a padlock and they would keep the key between them for a little while like a promise.

Sometimes in the years that followed a man will find himself in a different chair and think of the orchard with a kind of mourning. You never lose that first field you loved. It is kept in you like a ledger that you carry and you make payments upon it in the form of visits and the telling of stories. They would come back later sometimes and stand at the edge of the new road and look at the houses and the way the lights glittered and the lawnmowers made the sound of an order. Sometimes they would not. They would leave it to other hands and to the small shoots that insist on living.

And if a man asks whether they had done right, one might say that rightness is a thing you pay for in different currencies. They had money then and a roof and a key. They had the ledger closed. They had a boy who knew the sound of a tree and a man who had his brother's hand. They had regrets also, and regrets make their own kind of music. It will go on that way. The orchard slept under a skin of new things and the river ran on because it's what rivers do, indifferent and yet carrying every name that ever washed into it.
