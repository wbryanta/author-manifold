He woke with her hands on his shoulders and her breath in his ear and a taste of smoke and river water in his mouth, and for a long instant, while he squinted against the dark and the dry black of the room and tried to bring the dream back into its place in him and make it sensible as yesterday and not as a truth, he thought he lay still and that the mob had only come upon him there in the square and that he had not been beaten and dragged but only had his shirt rumpled and his pride bruised and that they would yet turn and go and that perhaps the sun would stand where it had stood and perhaps nothing at all had been lost. But she held him with an insistence that was not tenderness but the sheer mechanical will of someone who had come awake with news and must set the order of the house and the order of the day by hers, and though she did not speak at once she had the look of one who counts and recounts and will not be content with an uncertain sum, and when she did speak it was only, Hush, quit hollerin, which was not because the neighbors would hear but because she herself feared the momentum of his voice, the way it could gather and take them both into something that would not leave them thereafter.

What? he said. His voice was a raw thing, like a strip of leather that had been pulled tight and left in the sun. He was older than she by some years but he had a child's fear of the dark and of the sounds made by other men in the dark, and in the dim he could see the outline of her belly, the nameless weight that pressed against the bed between them like an accusation and like a promise, and his hands went to it as if to feel the warmth of what was inside, and she pushed them away with a sigh that was almost a laugh and said, Don't you start that again, and then she said, What was it this time? in a voice that had the old impatient tenderness of one who had heard the same fear turned over and knuckled for years until the edges had been blunted and the center had turned to a dull grief.

He told her, because to tell a dream is to make it less dangerous, is to spit out the mote of coal before it sets the mattress on fire. He told her the prophet and the sun and the healed faces and how at the last the sun had gone and how the crowd had turned and fallen upon him with howls of outrage. He said it as a thing that had happened to some other man and not to himself, and yet in saying it he felt the old pucker of terror in him as if the hands had been on his neck still and his feet were still fretting the packed mud. She listened with the bluntness of one whose day is measured in tasks and whose concern for the supernatural and the metaphysical had long ago been worked down into the common denominator of bread and the fire to boil it, and when he finished she said, Well then they had cause, which was the crueler kindness between them because it taught him the scale on which the world measured right and wrong and because it cut off the long expansion of excuses he was always inventing for himself.

They lay quiet and the house around them was like a ribcage closing down; the night had a damp smell like a cloak left to hang in a stable, and there were distant sounds of men moving in the alley and the thump now and then of the river against piling, the raw animal sound of the city that had not yet risen to its own noise of barter and complaint and hoarse greetings. In the dark the shape of the bed seemed longer and more lonely than it had a year before and the thin mattress under them creaked in the way of old wood and old bargains. She said to him at last, You got to go.

He wanted to answer that he had no place to go to that they had no work and no money and that if he left he would leave her with the child to keep and that even the law of the town had become something you could not reckon on—he wanted to make a case and marshal defenses—but his mouth closed and he only said, Where?

She knew where. She had always known; women of their kind have a way of folding maps in their head and carrying them like a rosary. She said, Down river. West. With the men they camped with. She said it with the kind of certainty that makes itself a fact by being named, and he felt the old fear and the old weary hope at once. The band of three men who had moved across the bluff and down into the river had been there the week before, or the week before that; such reckonings were loose now and a man could not hold time as he once had. They had come to the river at dusk, they had made a small fire, they had eaten with their pannikins, and they had lain down with their mouths gaping to the stars, and by morning they had taken up their duffel and gone west along the river as if the river itself had minds and wills and had told them to go. Those men had been silent mostly and feral, and the bearded one had a way of standing before a fire with his legs apart like a man who owned the earth and the sky, and there had been violence in his hands that made other hands draw back from him with an instinctive prudence. They had not asked for alms nor had they been importunate; they had been simply there, like an eddy, and you either got into its pull or you let it go. And she said, We go with them, because when men who move in shadow decide to take on a work they make of it a sort of law, and a law is safer sometimes than the lack of it.

He had never been a leader of men; his talents were of another order—petty things, little ruses and small satisfactions that served the moment. He had been at one time a man in the square, with his hand held up to the prophet, but not as a prophet's acolyte and not as a believer in miracles; the sun that fell away had meant to him the end of a certain toleration, the end of small liberties, the end of those nights when a man could ply a quick trade or a quick lie with the light on and be forgiven by morning. When the sun did not return, there were no laws left to keep the balance between men, and hunger and blame and fear made together a thing that had a head and a tongue and fed on the weak. He had been near enough to the prophet to feel the heat of that man's voice and to catch the drift of his promises, and perhaps for a while he had thought to be of it, to stand in the square and call on men to believe and to take the coin and drink their rations and let the prophet do what prophets do; perhaps he had thought that to stand with that voice would be a way to safety. But the voice was a voice like any other and the promises were small silver things, and when the light buckled and left them it was as if a trapdoor had opened under their feet and the crowd's hunger had the roaring of a machine. He had been pushed forward like a thing on a current and had opened his mouth to call out Me, and Me! and the shout had been in him as much from his own need as from any malice, and perhaps that was why they fell upon him.

The story of the square had become a thing that moved around from mouth to mouth in the market and the alleys, and each teller added or subtracted to fit their own malice or pity. Some said he had been the prophet himself; others said he had been a supplicant and had cried out for help and been denied. Women who watched from the lintels said he had walked away with his face split and his clothes torn, and children made a sport of it and sneered whenever he passed, and there is no greater humiliation than to be the symbol for something larger than you and to feel it pin you like a moth under glass. He had not meant to lead men to ruin; he had not meant the sun to stay away; he had not meant to be a scapegoat or a prophet or a thief; he had only been a man who lived by small delas and who had found himself in a crowd. But the town was a thing that longed for a face on which to lay the blame, and when a face is found the town is a hungry, patient beast.

She said then, And if they come—if the crowd comes here, what will you do? The question had the carefulness of one who has borne a burden and knows that a plan is better than a prayer. He did not answer at once because he saw again the square and the dark face of the prophet and the thin ring of sun and the cough of the crowd's outrage, and he thought of his mother who would speak to him sometimes in that dream voice that made the world simple and ordered. If they come we will be gone, he said finally, and there was a slowness in his speech as if he were not sure he believed his own lie.

She sat up and reached for the tattered lamp on the bureau and blew on it until the blue flicker lifted, and in the light the room took on dull and familiar things—a cracked cup, a pair of patched breeches over the back of a chair, the blank face of the clock whose hands lay misaligned and not moving. She counted their small holdings with her eye—the beltknife his, wrapped in old cloth; the last three coins in the hollow of her blouse; the thin loaf they kept for emergency; the flint in the drawer that had a knack of making a sear of light when struck; the old pannikins dried with many fires; and when she had scanned she said, We could leave before the day gets up. He felt a sudden ease then, not because the path was certain but because action is a kind of belief, and belief steadies a man's chest like a hand on his shoulder. The motion outward was better than the motion inward, and in the face of her crisp practicality he found himself folding to it as one folds a sheet around a sleeping thing.

Before they moved he dressed and stepped out into the alley and the air hit him with a damp chill that made him hitch his shoulders, and the town lay there like a thing half awake, its roofs a line of gray against the early wash of the sky, and already some had begun to set out stalls and to make the daily barter of bread against labor and of soap and of news. He saw the baker who would sell loaf for work, the small boy who bought his bread with foxed advice for a favor to come, the washerwoman who hung the day's linen like a row of whitened flags; the world was made small and battered and uses were being made of it as always. He wrapped the blanket about him and took the duffel from the corner where he had kept it and put in it the things they would need—the last of the beans, two pannikins, the worn spoon, the rag for the child's head, a bottle mended with pitch that had been given to him in trade for a favor he had yet to repay. He was at once relieved and ashamed, ashamed because it was again the way—he left with nothing saved, with the meager and the last, with no plan and no counsel but that which the woman in his bed provided.

They moved down the narrow street with the light loosening into a paler blue and the market beginning to fill with the small calls of sellers. They kept to the walls and the shadows because the town's eyes were many and quick and sharp and because where men gathered there too gathered tongues and accusation. They crossed the bridge and the river was a black smear threaded with the lights of rivercraft and the reflection of the first skerrick of light that the east had to offer, and he felt, as of old, a steady gladness that was separate from his fear. He had always loved the river and its indifferent patience; it made of cities and men and their tragedies mere driftwood, and he had often stood and watched it take and take the flotsam of life and later give some piece back though different and smoothed and small. The men who camped along its banks knew this; they moved with the river's slow, implacable patience.

They found the three men at the place they had been promised, near where a barge lay beached and the reeds tall and whispering like old women. The bearded one recognized them at once but said nothing; he had the look of a judge in a small temporal court who had no need for words because his meaning was in the way he stood. The bearded man spoke once to the others in a low tone and they turned and came toward them and for a long minute there was an appraisement of faces like the turning of a key in a lock. The bearded man looked at the woman and then at him and then at the child's swell and made no show of pity, only a small assent by which they were accepted into their number because the river allows numbers but requires also that the weak be concealed in the strength of the pack. The bearded man's eyes were something like the core of coals and his mouth had a small habit of tightening; he held himself as if he did not want to be spoken to on matters of law and order, which is to say that he led by a rough common sense and by the will to be obeyed.

They walked with the three men up the fold of the bluff where the morning lit sawgrass into a burnished heap and the river below was a band of quicksilver. The larger party kept silence as if they had nothing to say that was not measured and as if speech had a cost and a tariff, and when they camped it was in the manner of men who had been doing this for a long time—quick fires, efficient division of labor, the pooling of scraps. They shared their meager bread and their pannikins; the bearded man cooked with a quick hand and the pair of men who were his companions pitched tents and tended small tools and swept the ring where the fire had been so that it left no sign for those who came after. The woman sat with her knees tucked under her and the man's eyes watched her and the child's swell as if it were a secret treasure that might be plucked by any wind, and all night he lay stomach down and watched the stars until his eyes blurred and the image of the square with its dark rim of sun sank from him like a spent coin.

There was an unspoken pact among those who move downriver: each night is for keeping watch and each day for moving and bartering and making small fortunes which are then measured in bread and in the cleanliness of the child's cradle, and there was also a law among them against talking of past lives which was periodically broken by slow dull people who liked to tell of what they had lost and by dreamers who could not help but spin their stories. The bearded man was taciturn by nature and if you cut him you would not see the fat of idle words but the lean of clenched speech and the small scar of former fights. He watched the man often and once, when the night was deep and the fire a dim red, he said to him: You keep dreaming at night, don't you? and the man's answer was a laugh that was half terror and half a concession.

There was, in that band, a tacit hierarchy that came not of titles but of the ability to endure. When a man cannot endure he is placed where all men who cannot endure are placed—on the margin. The bearded man had the line of a leader; he did not make speeches and he did not kindle a worship but there was in his eyes a prudence that others obeyed because prudence feeds many when word feeds none. Men had come and gone from his band and some had been left at river bends for mischief they had done or for debts they had refused to pay, but others had been taken in and folded into the work of the day and in this way the band was improvised and held together as patched material is held together by a single strong stitch.

They lived aboard the beached barge for a while in the fashion men must when they make of a thin claim a home, and in the mornings they rowed upstream or downstream in search of work, and the work was never moral or noble but it was honest labor for the day—unloading a cargo of coal, patching a net, carrying bundles of board, a short hand of farm labor for a man who had a small field. He traded his skill with words for small favors and his skill with his hands for bread and in this barter he kept his head down. The woman's belly grew and the child would kick at times that caught him and it made him a more simple man to see the life that was not yet born inside a woman he loved and respected, and he found that the dream's hard claws receded when he had work to do because labor is a tough salve.

Yet the dream returned at night with the persistence of a sour tooth and sometimes in the midday heat, when the sun lay like a peeled coin on the river's back, he would see again the prophet and the circle of supplicants and the coil of the crowd and the way it had turned on him, and he would imagine the faces of those who now called him something that he did not deserve and did not want. He had tried to speak at times to the bearded man of leaving, of going further west to towns beyond which men said the land was better and the law was somewhat still like a shield, but the bearded man only spat and said, The farther you go the more you carry of yourself with you, which was a phrase meant to justify a small man's appetite for staying put. What he meant was that the world carried its burdens in its belly and that movement did not erase the weight, and he spoke with want of poetry but with a truth: there are no absolutions in motion, only the changing of scenery. The man's guilt was a thing he carried, and to move did not lighten it unless what you moved toward had a capacity to forgive.

One afternoon, as they waited in a small cove for the tide and the day hung like a thick cloth over them, a woman came toward the camp with a child in her arms and the look of one who has walked a long way. She wore a scarf over her head and her cheeks had been sunburned into lines. She brought with her a piece of news. She sat upon a rock and swallowed and then said, Word has it down the river that the square's crowds are moving again, that they are hunting for a face to lay the failing of things on, and that the name they call is yours, which she pronounced not as a question but as a thing that might be a stone dropped into the river—something that would ripple and pass and then be gone. The man's first thought was to deny it, to say that no such thing had been said and that the square had gone on to other things and other targets, but he saw in the woman's eyes the old hunger for news and the way the town eats rumor for a meal and then wants more. He said nothing at first and then he touched his chest as if to count the beats and said, Who said?

The woman smiled a small sour smile and said, Men who have nothing else to do, who barter terror like a commodity. She said, They say you promised the sun would return. You told them it would. The man's blood froze. He had been careful to keep his mouth stitched when the prophet came through, and he had given nothing to the mob that was not borrowed and half a lie to fit the mood. He had not promised the sun nor had he called himself one chosen, but once a tale begins it makes a body for itself from the small slippage of tongues and the want of people to be told what to hope for. He had heard once, in a tavern, some men joking of his name and their laughter had the prick of a knife, and now it was being carried to him by this new woman as if delivered to his door.

The bearded man listened in his stoic way and then said, We move, which was as much a policy as a command. The days after that were busy and full and the small preparations had the high seriousness of some religious rite. Men came and traded what they had for sharp edge and for one day's ration. The woman packed in a dry bundle the small linens she had; the man took the beltknife and the mended bottle and the ragged shirt he planned to wear until it would weave into itself the soil of many more nights. The town had a hunger for symbols and when it found them it chewed them slowly; it was better to be somewhere else when this process began than to be the meat on the bone.

On the day they left there was a small pale of dawn and the river had a glassy patience and the gulls argued like small priests in the air. They pushed the barge off with a steady shove and there were others in the camp who hugged them and called them names both kind and not, and a child of the band gave the man a small carved wooden horse that had been whittled from a splinter and said, For when you want to remember home, and the man's throat ached and the words he wanted did not come. The woman sat on the prow with her hands upon her belly and her eyes were like two soft coals as she watched the town fall behind them, and for the first time in a long while the man felt a small clear joy that was like a coin in the hand—something he could flip over and regard for a while before he swallowed it.

They floated downriver in the manner of men who do not hurry and who measure their days by the sun's run and the stretch of their hands and the slow rhythm of the oars. The bearded man kept watch because the world is always peopled with men who will take what you have while you sleep and with beasts worse than men for there is in mankind an appetite for the taking of symbols. There was talk sometimes among their number of a place further west where the land was said to take a gentler shape and where the law still kept something of its bark; this talk was always vague and full of the nostalgia of men who have nothing left but maps in their heads. The world they traversed was a narrow one—fields and little towns and abandoned farms where an old woman kept a flock of hens and traded eggs for small favors, and sometimes the men of the band went ashore to barter their labors for a night under a thatch roof or a bowl of soup.

At one of these places, where the banks were cut with the old scars of cattle and where the river broadened into a slow cupped pond, they met a man who had once been a teacher and who now kept a general store that was a temple of small goods—tinned meat, a coil of wire, a few pounds of salt. He had a mild face and a small patience for the world, and when he knew that the man's name was the one the town had given him he did not show surprise. He said only, People will always make monsters of those who are different, which was a wisdom that carried no pity and yet showed the man the shape of things. The teacher traded for a scrap of cloth and some flint and gave them in return a map he had drawn in youth which led to a small hamlet beyond the next bend and said, They have a well there and they're kind to strangers, and at that the bearded man nodded and the woman smiled in a way that was small and grateful. The map was rough and the hamlet he spoke of might be no better than the one they had left, but to have another point on a map made it a thing to steer toward and to say aloud.

Night after night the journey was the same: fire, bread, the small talk of men who make stories and then test them on one another for the pleasure of hearing. The man found himself thinking of the square and of the prophet's voice in a different key; he tried to put distance between what he had been and what he was to become and though distance does not change the stain on a man's hands it does sometimes dull the memory of how bright the stain once was. He slept sometimes and dreamed and sometimes he did not sleep at all and sat by the embers until his bones complained; the bearded man would watch him and then, with a grunt, go at work and the others would do the same and the barge would take them forward in its flat slow way.

Once, at dusk when the sky had the green-blue cold of a bruise, there came to them a party of men who had the look of the court and who carried a kind of warrant. They moved like a thing that had been rehearsed; their boots were of leather once polished and their belts had the small shine of metal that you can only keep when a man has a method of living and some place to return to. The band tensed at their approach because wherever authority comes it brings with it the memory of the past. The leader of that party had a face like a map of small cruelties and he looked at the man with something like curiosity and with something else that was less describable—a hunger for the story of his ruin. He said, We have orders from the square, and the bearded man spat and said, Orders are paper, and if you have to collect honor the river will not help you, and the man with the warrant smiled with a sudden small triumph like a fox before he spoke his reason: we have a list. We have been told to gather names that were said to have led people into false hopes and to measure them, and we have heard that you were one of the faces. For a long breath the river and the quiet held the thing between them and the man's skin crawled. They were hands with precise instruments and they moved like a law.

He thought then of the crowd in the square and the sift of small things by which men make a story, and he thought of the prophet who had held out his hand and how easy it is to take a hand when it is offered, and how hard it is to refuse because refusal is hunger and hunger is a kind of prayer. He opened his mouth to speak and then clamped it shut; the bearded man stepped forward and said only, He was with us. And the man with paper looked at him then and his eyes slotted and he said, You would save him, would you? The bearded man answered, We move, and it was a stony thing and the court man smiled and said, Move. The party took their leave as if their business had been done though nothing had changed, and the man watched them go and felt the iron of an old fear tighten about his chest.

It was the nature of their time that law could be hired and rumor could buy itself an armory, and the party of men who came with paper might have been the instruments of the square or might have been a group of men with private grudges, and who could tell? When they left, the bearded man rubbed his jaw and said, Keep your head low, and the woman tied a scarf about her belly and the child kicked and calmed inside as if content with the rocking of the world and the sound of the river like the beating of a drum.

The days continued in the song of the oar and the smell of the smoke and the barter and the small kindnesses of men who do not ask why the world broke and choose instead to mend what they can. The man felt himself growing harder though not less guilty; the muscle of his body filled and a kind of stupid pride took a residence in his chest that made him think he could beat any small hazard thrown his way. He started to believe in work as a religion and in labor as redemption, and that belief saved him for a time. The dream receded into a thing like a scar you forget to wash until it itches, and sometimes at night he would hold the woman's hand and listen to the rhythm of the child and he felt that some sin could be redeemed by the small acts of himself so that when the child came he could be something other than the rumor that trailed him.

Yet the rumor is an animal that finds ways to track and to find the bedrock scent of what a man is, and one evening as they made camp in a bend that sheltered them like a hand the world broke upon them anew with a noise like a great sieve. They had not been warned nor had they suspected. Men came down the slope in the dark like thieves and their number was such that even the bearded man paused and counted and found his hands small and empty. They had torches and they had the kind of low speech that is the practice of killing something by making it weary, and their leader was a man who had a hat with a feather, which makes him an image and a danger because men like such birdsers hold to old apparatus and to the idea of being a leader. He called them by their names as if naming a death makes the death a thing of business. He came forward with a swagger and said, So at last we find you, and he pronounced the man's name like a thing he had practiced in the dark. They had not come to trade, and the man at the center of it knew he had been found.

They took him into the ring where the torches made ovals of gold and between the parties there was the sound of the river like a donkey and the low breathing of those who watch. The leader jutted forward and said, The square needs a face and it has chosen yours, and the crowd that had gathered there, made of the neighbors of the river and the living dead who follow the dictates of a hungry town, began to chant and to call the name. The man tried then, for his life shrank small to the size of his lungs, to say he had not been the man, that he had been only a supplicant, that he had been pushed, and he said Me! and Me! as if the dream had grown teeth and had come to gnaw him in the waking world. They would not hear him and the leader threw him down and the crowd took up the hate like a labor of love; hands found his hair and his face and his throat and there were cries like the bell of a ship and the man's world went out in a soft blackness like a coal dropped in water.

He woke again at the woman's hand on his shoulder and the taste of river water in his mouth and for a moment he did not know which was dream and which was waking and if the two had any virtue separate from one another. She said to him as she had said before, Go, before they come, and when he picked up the duffel and the beltknife he felt a new cold that had not been in him before. The band shoved their barge off and the river closed about them like a throat, and the town behind them seemed to ricochet with the clamor of something that had found a prey and would not let go. He put his shoulder to the oar and the rhythm took him and he felt a small piece of himself fall away with each stroke as if the act of movement shorn him of the old accusation, and the bearded man, who had little to offer in words, gave him an approving nod, which was in his fashion the strongest speech a man could want.

There are times when a man must do what men who love him do: they make a shelter and they tell themselves a story that will live between them. And so they rowed, and the woman sat between them and her belly rocked in harmony with the oars, and she sometimes cried with a small low noise when she thought of the smallness of their goods and the immensity of the thing they carried, and he pledged to her in a handful of sentences that were simpler than prayers that he would keep her and the child and that if the world forced him to the square he would stand and take whatever they laid upon him and he would not run. Whether he meant these words truly he did not know, but the vow was an instrument that steadied him for the hour.

They lost the town in the long shoulders of the river and the world narrowed to the ripple of water and the whisper of reeds. At night the stars returned and their putrescent, ancient shining was a kind of conspiracy of comfort against the news that had been left behind. The bearded man spoke rarely but when he did it was as if he had always been a man with an opinion that was weighty. He said at last, You cannot live your life by what towns say. You must live it by what your hands will do and what your belly will feed, and though he did not speak of the square he meant that the world takes and takes and sometimes what a man has is only himself and the strength of his arms and that will not be stolen from him unless he lets it.

The river wound like a slow animal and took them into a place where the banks were gentler and where the farms had the scratch of use but not the scar of warfare. They found work again sometimes and they found shelter in a small hamlet where a woman took an interest in the woman's belly and where a man whose trade was of making shoes had a daughter who smiled at the child in the mother's belly and call it a blessing. There were nights when the man felt almost as if the square had been an ugly dream and that the river had washed it clear, and he slept like a man who had used up his terror and was now allowed some rest.

But peace never keeps fast, and the narrative of the world was not yet done with him. One morning the bearded man went ashore and did not return until noon, and when he did his face was like a hammered metal and his coat was dusted with the town's earth. He brought with him a small paper carefully folded and he laid it upon the planks of the barge and it was the sort of thing that read with a formal cruelty and a polite violence: a summons, a list, an order. They had been found out, the paper said, and names had to be collected, and the name at the head of the list shone like a beacon of malcontent. For a while they did not speak and the river took the sound of the oars and the moan of the wind and the paper lay between them like a stone, and then the bearded man bent and broke the paper in half with his fingers and said, We go again, which was all the policy the men needed. His mouth had a slow smile that was perhaps not cruel but which had in it the acceptance of things a man must do when the world calls.

They moved on, and the man felt all the old hunts and the old accusations shadowing them like great birds. He would think of the square as something that sits in the middle of a man's life and that will call for the toll of his being whenever it needs blood. He wondered what he had been in the mind of the prophet and whether he had ever truly believed in what he had uttered. He thought of the prophet once more as a little sham of a man who had sold hope and left men starving for the sale, and he thought of the crowd who had eaten that hope and then turned on its vendor. The dream returned in waking and the square and the prophet became more concrete and the man's guilt grew heavy again, but the woman still steadied him with half a touch and a small mute counsel.

Time flowed on the river and the band grew like a small family made by necessity. The child finally came in a barn lent to them by an old farmer who had heart and little fear, and they named him a small and modest thing that did not speak of majesty or prophecy but of hope and of little survival. When the boy was born he had his mother's hair and his father's stubborn line of mouth, and his first breath seemed to make the bearded man look like a man who feared less. They kept the child wrapped and the nights grew those that will make a man find a peace he had never known: feeding, changing, the small ceremonies of parenthood that reduce the colossal to the domestic and in that reduction make it survivable.

But the rumor is a relentless animal and the square, when it wants meat, can move like a wave and pull what is nearest into its mouth. Word reached them at last that the square's hunt had driven through the towns and culverts and that there were men who had taken the law into their own hands and had made of rumor a weapon. There was talk of trials by fire and of lists posted in the square calling for names. Once more the river, which had been a sanctuary long enough to let the man breathe, became a thing that ran with notes of warning. The bearded man said nothing at first but sat and carved small toys for the child with a pocket knife and the man watched him with the hollow in his chest. The bearded man finally said, We go west, and there was no arguing him then. He had decided the course and whatever the small law of his command it was obeyed because it was necessary.

They took the barge out into a morning so pale the sky was like milk, and as they pulled the oars the man felt, for the first time in months, the old terror of the square in a new shape. He saw in the mind's eye the prophet on his little platform and the thin rim of sun and the faces of those who had come to him for miracle and had received only the small comfort of a burned bread. He thought then of a thing he had not thought before: that perhaps in the end the square wants not to punish a deed but to name an actor and to force all men to stand and confess to some narrative. He knew then that to be a man is sometimes to be at the mercy of stories and that you cannot know which story will find you and make you into its image.

They passed bends and the banks grew less cultivated and more wild. The wind began to have the salt tongue of the sea and the river widened and wanted to be an ocean. The bearded man kept a steady eye on the horizon and the barge cut its slow track into the silver. For once the man felt a small peace, not because he believed the hunt had died but because the child's small fist closed around his finger and in that contact the world narrowed and was bearable. He would go on, he thought, for the child and the woman; he would not run into the square and he would not give himself as meat, and yet a part of him knew that to be hunted is not only to be taken by men but to be taken by the sum of rumor and loss and that no matter how much you row the river may itself deliver you back to your door.

They moved in shadow and light, they moved in the hush of dawn and in the dark of noon, always west, always with the current that the river provides. They were a small bundle of people who had learned to measure their morality by their meals and by the keeping of a child's cry at night. The past, with the square and the prophet and the crowd, was like an illness that sometimes fluttered up inside him and made his tongue silver and his hands tremble. But he lived forward with a new modest hope that perhaps the river had a mercy and would carry them to a town where no one had a memory of his name, where he could be simply a man who kept his hands busy and who fed his family by the plain method of the day. And once, in a dawn that burned with a clean little flame of a sun like a coin newly minted, he thought that maybe the sun would always return after all, because it had returned now for so many mornings that to say it would fail seemed like a superstition burned down by the warmth of many new days. He drilled that thought into himself and held it like a talisman, and he kept his head low and rowed.
