The smoke rose like a thought coming late and suddenly into focus: a single column at first, slender as a pencil line against the blue, then softening and thickening, feathering into a low, billowed hat that made the mountains look as if they'd been fitted for some slow, heavy grief. I watched it with my hands folded around the mug of coffee the way people fold hands around other people's hands in images of consolation; it was a small animal of warmth in the glass box of the tower. The cup steamed. The slipped lid of the radio was closed and mute on the shelf beneath the maps, its face like a small, dead moon. The decommission placard on the door still read the year the government stopped cutting the checks and started calling towers "historic sites." For a living thing it had been put to rest politely: a bench bolted in place, a plaque with the names of men who had liked being high into the air, and a key kept in a file labeled "Master."

It was my final summer up here. The thought had been a slow erosion, like water on stone; I'd told myself I would stay until the last prescribed season had finished, until the government men took the final readings and hammered their little nails into the small printed logbook, until my badge — an old oval with paint gone thin — could be mailed back with a signature and a receipt. That last summer had become a discipline, an inheritance of light and small, certain tasks: sweep the solar panes when the dust got thick, climb the ladder and check the vent on the roof for nests, log every observed plume, no matter how unnoticeable. It had given me a shape to my days; every morning there was the careful ritual of spreading the map on the table, aligning the pencil crosshairs with the cups of road and the forked lines of the rivers, scanning with binoculars until the muscles in my back began to quit their own work and I had to stand and stretch.

The smoke came from the drainage below, the one that carved itself like an old throat through the pine slope, and I knew it by the way the light hit the eastern cliff as certain people are known by the tilt of their shoulders when they walk into the room. In the woods people liked to use geography as a gossip; "the drainage below the old stump" became the shorthand for more than place. The drainage had Margot's cabin in it. Margot, who had the habit of burning things: old papers, small piles of accumulated household refuse, the unspent griefs with which she kept her life tidy and dark and very private.

The radio was dead because, a week earlier, when a thunderstorm had installed itself over the ridge and fiddled with the solar charge, the panel had been struck twice. It was an old set; I've always thought of radios as people that never know what to do but keep trying anyway. That morning I'd unscrewed it, looked at the thin copper, tried to coax the old man who lived in my toolbox into making new friends with the circuit. I soldered a wire. I held up the antenna to the wind and cursed at the universe. Nothing. Dead. The little red light the socket used to flash had given up like a heart that stopped on someone else's good day. There was a handheld, ancient thing with a frayed strap in the cupboard, but its batteries were long dead and even the new pack I had ordered had been delayed by a road closure and a bureaucratic backlog and the last man to visit the tower — an inspector with a gentle badger's moustache — had laughed and told me I'd done enough, that they could see the tower on their satellite maps if anything really happened. They seemed to like maps where the world was flat and tiny.

Eight miles to the road. It sat like a fact in my chest, a small stone that had no need to be said aloud to be real. Eight miles down, single track twisted like string; some of it was old fire road, washed in places down to the bedrock, some of it a footpath only known to deer and the boot heels of those rustic bastards who never left anything be. Down meant heat. Down meant the air would be thicker with the thing that made no good promises. Down meant I would move from my place of seeing into the place of doing, the place where there might be small heroics or very little at all in the end. Down meant reentering a world where I would have to be a daughter, or a sister, or someone split between those two.

Margot had made a life of keeping the cabin between the map's contour lines as if sheltering it would reverse some wrong. She called it "ancestral," and she had a way of pronouncing the word that made it sermon-like. I had come up here because I liked the light and the mathematics of fires; because I liked knowing things before they told themselves; because I'd grown tired of being in rooms where people used me like a mantelpiece. Margot had stayed there because she liked the leaning light of the cabin porch and the sound of rain on the tin roof; because she had more patience than I did with small slow things; because she loved—some heavy and private sentence—that old structure represented.

We hadn't spoken, really, since Mother's funeral. Not that one single day explained everything; the corral of all our old resentments had been a herd for years before it stampeded. There had been the will — there is always a will with its particular ways of cleaving people — and there had been the thing Mother left in an old trunk, which Margot took without asking, and the times I had flirted with sending my money almost exclusively to the tower and the way Margot said I was "choosing to watch the world burn." Sometimes she said it with humor, a skirt of chestnut laughter, and other times like a blade under the dishes.

I put the cup down and the sound it made on the iron table was clear, thin as a clock's answer. I slid the binoculars higher up my nose. The smoke still rose, and now it had a thin orange thread through it, small sparks the size of beetles floating and then surrendered into the column. The cabin's tin roof would shine and show up like a silver tooth if the wind chose to reveal it. A person who spends days in towers becomes adept at reading small things; you learn that smoke that curls is different from smoke that bullets straight upward, that the latter is less worrying. This column bulged and was bloodied with orange veins. It was hard to be precise up here with such sympathy for things.

I thought about the radio again. I thought about the last time we had sat on the cabin porch together and drank beer from the same bottle without speaking religiously. That had been three years ago. Margot smiled then and said, "You're always on the side of the map, June. Not in it." I had thought her kind then; I had thought she was saying something as if it were a fact. I had stood and walked to the edge of the porch and looked over into the drainage where the deer came down at dusk. It had been a warm, glassed day. I had felt the world tilt slightly, like an awkward café table, and then there had been an argument over whether Mother would have wanted the cabin sold. It went from quick words to two years of removal like a river that gets warmer and then changes its course. Words do that; they pick a passage and then become a waterline.

If I went down, I'd be the one to carry the quiet that had grown between us. We had both been good at not carrying things.

The wind changed toward noon. It smelled, faintly and then stronger, of sap and of something else, a dry kind of sweetness that makes people think too quickly of campfires and marshmallows; the smell of old paper burning in a lean, focused gust. I put on my boots, the ones with the laces that never broke, and stuffed the thermos and a length of rope into the pack. I left the key in the file where the master men had asked it to be left. The tower had its authority. Sometimes living in a thing like a tower claims you according to its own terms; when you leave you are not truly yourself until you come back and find the pattern of your absence.

The path down was honest and rude, moving like a patient creditor. The first mile unspooled familiar sentinel pines that had been the same size since I had been twenty and had moved up here to be a single woman without a plan. There were new beetle kill trees where copper-colored needles hung like from dead weddings. The sunlight through the canopy was thin and stitched; it made little windows in the moss. My knees protested the first switchbacks; they reminded me they had lives of their own. Halfway down, a hawk lunged from a dead tree and my heart beat once against my ribs like a small trapped animal and then settled into the rhythm of the trail.

The further I descended, the more the smell became a narrative. It told me this was not a controlled burn. Controlled burns have order to them. They are tidy. They stink in a purposeful way, composed of cut grass and charred undergrowth arranged for the good of future growth. This was ragged fragrance: the fine, immediate, angry sweet of real wood, the smell of houses faintly mingled inside it. It could have been someone burning an old couch or it could have been a house beginning to ache under flame.

When the cabin first came into view, I wanted to think that I had been wrong in my panic. The cabin was a thing of long timber, low-ceilinged, with a porch bowed by time. Margot's garden, which had been a little rectangle of defiant order, was a sprawl of neglected boxes, late-season wildness sticking up like weeds with attitudes. The tin roof glinted; for a moment I saw sunlight and thought I could breathe easy. Then I saw the dark breath curling at one corner and the flame gnawed like a small animal, then a larger one, licking at the eaves. The porch was a wreath of smoke. A blackened edge traced the corner of the cabin. The main column came from the drainage further down, a partial lightning strike of flame that had moved up and then found the dry tinder of a lawn and the leftover trim of a porch.

When she saw me, Margot didn't gasp. She had a loin-length braid the color of dust and a face that had been kept long by solitudes. She was shirtless in an old grey tank and had dirt up to her wrists. She had a metal bucket in each hand and was carrying them like casseroles of peace. Her dog — a mutt who looked as if some unlucky pot of paint had been spilled onto a sheep — was rolling in the dirt and barking at shadows. Margot's eyes met mine with a peculiar expression, less surprise than an expectation that I would arrive somewhere, at some time. There are people who seem to live knowing you will eventually come. When it is your sister, it's something more. There's a folder of small nuclear certainty in how some people expect you. She looked at me in that way: that quiet assessment that had always made me want to both say yes and push away something at the same time.

"Thought that was mostly smoke," she said. Her voice had the same flatness as maple leaf in October. It didn't ask but it didn't refuse. Her face was unchanged in the way faces are unchanged by years of neglect; it had simply built itself more to its features.

"I could call it in but—" I started, and then the thought of the radio being dead stopped me like I had been physically touched. My words were small and useless there between us, frayed as the rope in my pack.

"We're fine," she said. "It's just the back forty. The heart of hell, isn't it." She laughed a small sound that could be shame. She ran a hand over the top of one bucket. "I thought you were only ever going to restrain the world from the map."

"I thought you were only ever going to set it burning to watch it look good. I thought we were both little enough to keystone each other and be done with it," I said. The sentence surprised me; it was more mild than venom and more honest than any apology. The words were shaped like a ladder, testing whether it would hold.

She snorted. "Keystone? You always get Christian metaphors on your tongue when you are trying not to mean something." She used my name then, the one she gave me at the age of two when she decided it was shorter. It felt like an old coin in my mouth. "Take the buckets. Fill them by the creek. Don't stand there. The neighbor's boy saw a flare and drove? No — he thinks it's in the drainage. He was muttering to himself like he was sent to prophecy. I told him to go help. He left."

We moved without the conversation we had been denied for months. We moved the way people move when there's a thing to be done that won't be fixed by talking. The creek ran cold and thin, stuttering over stones with that old water's impatience. We filled the buckets and carried them like prayers. The dog darted between us, a joke of sunlight.

The work demanded that we invent no language beyond what was needed. We stamped at the embers that tried to step beyond their place. We smothered a line of flame with the ends of wet towels and melted the possibility into a sticky, steaming nothing. I was glad that my body remembered longer than my conscience did. A human hands-on the world is a bracing instrument; you take the heat and you put it out. It is simple and mechanical and, in its repetition, sacred. Margot and I did not sing. We were efficient.

Between one bucket and the next we traded short clipped sentences. She was a litany. Once she said, "It started down by the drainage. Old stump got lit." Once she said, "The wood pile at the north side caught because of the wind. I'm sorry about the photos if anything goes. They were in the top box under the quilt. I tried to save them but—"

"You tried to save them," I said.

"I tried. That's doing something," she replied.

The smoke thickened. It wanted to speak. The sky filled with a thatch of small birds winging away. We were trying to smother a place where private fires lie in the roots of things: the box of letters in the attic, the stack of notebooks with lines so small they were like secret wells. It's weird how a small domestic fire reveals private inventories. People save the wrong things. People don't save the right things, either. It's redundant to say this when things burn, because the burning shows you how wrong you were the whole time.

Several times we passed each other and our palms met, accidental and accidental with the weight of a calendar. Each time, there was recognition in the contact that was not the whole of forgiveness but its beginning. It's a slim start. It is like the first time two hands remember each other's calluses. Margot's palm was callused at the base, knotted like an old rope. Mine was smooth and had thin scars like tiny railway lines. We had both been used to other kinds of work. I thought of the time as if it were a seam that had been half sewn and then abandoned.

At the end of three hours, the immediate fire had been sorted into something manageable. Not put out, necessarily, but given to warning lines and patience. I lay on the cabin porch and let the old tin sing as it cooled. The dog heaved himself up and sat between us as if custody were something negotiated in the presence of danger.

Margot had a bandanna around her forehead and dust in it. She had combed the dust into a halo and it suited her. She had that look people get when they are rendered both exhausted and pleased. "You could call them," she said finally, pulling her thumb across her mouth as the bird's wing of an anecdote smoothed into place. "Call them about what? That the roof is smoking and we are hitting it with buckets? They'll send a truck that'll track everything down the wrong road. These men — they want to sell maps to God. They don't want hands."

I understood at once the bitterness that did not become slander but simply an observation. The men with maps liked to watch from a distance. I had been one of them once. That was true. In the months after Mother died, I'd made myself a recorder. I had made a place for myself where my choices could be accounted for with logs and times and coordinates. That life had meant practical things. It had also meant I could leave and be far away when things needed more than seeing.

"Did you call the neighbor then?" I asked. "The neighbor who drove down? Did he call them?"

She chuckled. "He called himself to himself again. First thing you learn about small towns is that everybody's an alarm system of one function. If the alarm chimes low, you don't all come running. You watch the sound and you decide if it's worthy."

She picked up a charred corner of the porch and chewed at it with the edge of a grin. There was a time when one of us took charge and the other conceded. It had not been true for a very long time; maybe it had never been. That was the hard part of siblingness: the way roles circulate like the seasons until you are old enough to see them as patterns.

We sat on the porch and, for the first time in three years, we let our conversation take a bow under the small sun of nothingness. It didn't reach nostalgia. It was rougher, like old meat. We said things we had prepared. We said "Why didn't you answer my calls" and "Why did you leave the letters in that one box" and "You took the thing that wasn't yours." It made a small, private music: the tenderness and the arsenic in the same breath. The way you speak to someone who has been a country, a landscape you know and also refuse to speak to. We argued gently. The heat made our words bend like straw.

At some point, Margot said, "Do you remember, when we were small, how Mother used to have us launch fires?"

"Victories?" I asked. The word came out bitter and I tried to wrap it in a joke.

"Her little bonfires," she corrected. "She'd call them victories and we'd be given a stick and told to stir the embers into a pattern so the smoke would go a certain way. She would say, 'If you can control the smoke, you can control where it tells its stories.'"

The memory arrived, uninvited and clean, like a photograph. Mother standing in the yard like a small priestess, telling us that smoke was the hymn she believed in. My throat clenched then for reasons I could not name: because Mother had used a match and then a sympathy in her voice to make us believe in the correct ordering of flames. She used to teach us how to fold our anger into a marshmallow stick and keep it from catching the hedge. Mother loved control; it had been her religion when she was not otherwise worshiping the idea of a family.

"She thought she could order everything," I said. "She thought she could make a ledger of affections."

"She knew her foes," Margot said. "She knew which ones would take her bills and which would take her heart. I'm half convinced she left some of those alleys on purpose. The trunk, the things in it — they were accusatory."

We were dismantling, piece by piece, our narratives. It was both a doing and a letting be; it made me dizzy in the best possible way. The slope of the argument had been a cartography of our shared life. It now was being unwound like an old spool.

"You took the tea-stained letters," Margot said, softer now. "Not all of them. You took the short ones, the business-like ones. You took the ones that made Mother look like a person who kept a bankbook. I kept the ones with her handwriting that didn't rhyme with bills — the half-poems and the ones that had the small, terrible things in them."

I hadn't known she had those letters. It was silly to be surprised by that. We had always kept things; we had both been pack rats in different ways.

"I had a reason," I said. "I wanted to know things. I thought a map could tell me who she was."

"You thought a map would tell you who you are," she replied. There was a sad, bright humor in that. "You're always about the map."

"Maybe. Or maybe I like being able to point to things and see how they fit," I said, and in saying it I felt the old comfort of being an instrument of order. It sat with me, like a cord.

We were both, in different ways, those women who shape the world into tolerable lines: one by watching, the other by burning away the underbrush. Now here we were, both hands smudged and the cabin in danger, and we had discovered that the world required both kinds of labor to be lived in. It was an insight that neither of us had been keen to accept in the years of our separation.

Night came with a thin, iron-colored dusk. The smoke began to tire and clear; the fires were mainly small now, and had the look of hounds that had been called back. We took our work to the mound of ashes beside the woodpile and kneaded it like a rough bread. Margot fetched a bottle of cool, bitter beer from the cooler under the bench and offered one to me with the same plainness as the buckets. We sat in the tired heat on the porch, on chairs mismatched like unconvincing promises. The dog curled at our feet.

"You could go back up," Margot said at last. "When they come with their forms, they'll find you've logged this. They'll be pleased you were here. They'll tape the report and tell you they saw valor."

"And leave the rest of it to you," I replied.

"We will both be part of it," she said.

I thought about the tower. I thought about returning to my place of sight, of letting the men of maps marshal their data and hang it on the wall like a fossil. It would be easy. There was something in me that wanted the easy completion of everything being put into boxes. But the box I had tried to make for myself while I lived in the tower had kept me insulated from other things — from family, from the complex, messy business of being of use in the world in ways that had nothing to do with forms and ways to send reports. That life had been a logical life. It had its comforts: the certainty of mornings and the reliability of mechanical tasks. But the smell of smoke on my hands made me question the cost.

"We could keep it and be practical," Margot said, tilting her head toward the darkness where the small stars were turning on like precise errors. "We could get it fixed. We could talk and sign things and make tidy what needs to be made tidy."

"It is never tidy," I said.

"No," she said, and her voice had both humor and a surrender. "But maybe it's not always a fight. Maybe it's sometimes a cup of coffee on the porch while the sky goes quiet."

We sat there a long while with the cigarette smoke of other people's campfires in the air and the slight, cleaned smell of sopped embers. We didn't promise anything grand. We exchanged small things, the way one gives someone keys to a car when they have to run an errand and there is trust in the motion of handing it over. I told her about the radio and the solar panel and the inspector's moustache and how the check for the box had been changed to "historic" and then to "abandoned" on a list that had been updated with someone else's handwriting. She laughed, a real sound then, and at once the porch, the cabin, the drainage, small as they were, felt like a meeting place for the kind of truce that didn't require renaming.

When the stars came out in the velvet way of mountain nights and the air cooled, we sat shoulder to shoulder. It was a small act. I put my arm up like a younger woman and let it hover near Margot's shoulders and she did not flinch. It was a movement like the turning of a page. We talked then, not as if to construct anything immovable, but as if to stretch the muscles of speech after disuse. We told each other about small failures: the tower got termites last year and the inspector sent a memo; Margot told me she had been in love with a woman with a laugh like wet coins and then the woman moved away to a city that taught her new ways to be mean. We traded shame for courage and it was the most ordinary thing: the making a bargain without a lawyer.

By the time my head felt light and the work of the day had dribbled like a small wound into something that could be scrubbed, there was a plan that had the ungraceful beauty of people who had lived long enough to know no plan is made in the idle air. We would call in the fire when the wind changed. We would step down to the road when the truck got down and pick up the neighbors who had offered their muscle. We would walk the perimeter and sign the papers that meant we had done everything within our ability. We would, at some point, bring down the box that had been the center of the argument. We would open it in the presence of many birds and less smoke.

The radio, still dead, had become an emblem. It was a machine I'd used to connect the visible world to the logistical world, the world where everything can be translated into a code. I realized in the smallness of my moment that the thing I had been missing all these years wasn't necessarily the ability to send a signal into the bureaucracy, but the ability to be present in the ragged face of events. I had found watching to be comfort enough for a while. Watching is a way to be exact without being messy. But life bends on the messy.

When dawn came, we walked to the road together. The neighbor who had seen the flare came back with a truck and a face that had been softened by two or three failed expectations. There were men from the local volunteer brigade who'd come down by the kindness of their time. They were ham-handed and kind and their faces held the small earnestness of people who do the practical thing because there is a clear advantage to it. They asked us the usual questions about origin and permission and risk. We answered as we had: with truth and with the disclaimers that accompany that.

"We'll document it," said the man with the volunteer badge. He had a face that didn't hide the ability to care and it made me soften in a place I hadn't expected.

"Document whatever you like," Margot said and gave him a sideways look. "If it makes you sleep better."

We stayed while the men gathered up equipment, with their hoses and their gloves and their sensible boots. It was all of the world — the way the human race tries, in the end, to keep the rest of the world in some kind of manageable order. Margot and I had our hands dirty and our sleeves rolled and every minute we spent was a minute of inventory. There were moments when we both wanted to say I'm sorry and neither of us could manage the timing; our apologies waited in their corners like obedient dogs.

Later, when the paperwork made its little circles and signatures and the men had left with the quiet business of ordered men, I found that the radio still did not work. It sat, small and smug, a useless thing in the corner. I felt a silly melancholy about it, like missing something you'd never had. The tower would remain, for now, a place of history and not an instrument of warfare. The world would go on without it.

Margot offered me the key ring to the cabin the way you pass a ball in the park. She had one small key that did not seem like much but meant a lot. It fit in my palm and was warm from the sun. "Keep it," she said. "If for nothing else, you can come and make coffee and tell me about the maps."

I was tempted to make a large promise then, to say "I'll stay," as if that's something anyone could do and know what they meant. Instead I let the smallness of the exchange be enough. "I'll come," I said.

We left the drainage with the quiet satisfaction of rescuers who knew they had only been rescuing what they could touch. On the road, an old man from the volunteer brigade gave me a lift partway. He told me his story of a son and the son who had gone to the city and never been back. He told me also about the way he liked to keep his tools in order. We parted at the last switchback, him with his small paternal words and me with my pack and a key in my pocket.

When I climbed the last slope toward the tower, the place where the men of maps liked to pretend everything was a line, I thought of the way our family had always tried to make fire a thing of language. Our mother believed in ordering the smoke; she believed in knowing the pattern and being able to show it to others. It had been a philosophy that both of us adhered to, in different ways. I had liked lines and points and the ability to call smoke to account on a log sheet. Margot had believed in the sacrament of burning, that to remove what you don't want is to rewild the rest.

The road to the tower was still quiet. It had a beauty to its long, empty stretch. I thought about my final summer and what it meant to be finishing something while starting another. I thought about having a sister who had burned and kept and been as ungovernable as weather. I thought about apology being less like a stone and more like a small relay: you pass it to someone and they pass it back, and the thing runs its electricity.

The smoke in the drainage thinned as the day went on. By the afternoon, it was a faint thing like gray chalk. The cabin's roof, where the flames had worried the tin, looked bruised but intact. Margot had saved more than her photos. She had saved the porch and the smell and the dog and that small, important sense that you could wake up the next morning and not find everything undone. It didn't feel like victory exactly, but it did feel like a reprieve — unromantic and thin and very human.

Up in the tower, I opened the logbook and wrote the thing down as if I were doing something bureaucratic again, because that is what I knew how to do. My hand wanted to be steady; my words wanted to be measured. I wrote: "Observed smoke. Location: drainage below cabin. Action taken: assisted with extinguishing small fires. Evacuation: none necessary. Contact: local volunteer brigade responded." It sounded small. It sounded like reason had a name for everything. Under the log, in a different ink, I wrote: "Also, talked. A small truce." It felt ridiculous to write it down, but I did. The log seemed to approve.

The radio remained dead. I placed my palms on the glass and watched the valley like someone looking at an old photograph. The cabin was there, a small thumbprint of life on a swathe of green, and I felt something like gratitude, not for the government men or the volunteer brigade or the map, but for the way two sisters could still make a day of work into the most radical of acts: the decision to come down the mountain.

In the weeks that followed, the tower's days were full of the ordinary. I would watch, and log, and drink coffee that brewed finally as if it had been waiting its whole life to do so. But when the wind turned and the drainage smelled of snapping wood or the neighbor's boy would come down to tell some small story, I would find a key in my pocket and the memory of Margot's braids. That was enough to make the choice, the shape of the last summer, be more than a tidy exit. It made it an arrival.
