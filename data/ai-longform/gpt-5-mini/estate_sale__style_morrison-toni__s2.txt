The afternoon wore its heat like an old shawl, folded and refolded across the shoulders of the town until the edges frayed. June stood on the narrow strip of sidewalk that belonged to her mother in law and street and memory, watching strangers lift the furniture of her childhood out through the front door as if it were wood and cushion only — as if those things did not carry the weight of an entire life in them, the weight of an ordinary, unforgiving love. They came with boxes and blankets, with their breath short from the climb of the porch steps, with their hands steady because hands have steadied for a living. They came like weather. They did not ask why the house looked tired. They did not ask why the curtains held onto dust in the valley between their folds. They did not know the peculiar architecture of a mother's presence, how it nests in the curve of a stair-worn bannister, in the filigree of a knitting needle, in the place a tin of sewing needles is always kept. They came and they went. June kept an eye on the tagger, the woman with a pad and a voice that could pare the wrong price off a thing, and she kept an eye on the men carrying the couch, and she kept an eye on the town across the street, where people do not pass a house without a look, and the looks of towns are examinations without mercy.

Her mother’s house looked like an old recipe that had been properly observed: measurements precise, oven hot, flavors married. It had no modern trimmings to ease the eye. The lawn held its own bone-structured map of summers and winters — a path to the mailbox, a path to the side gate where the compost smelled, even now, faint and sour like a memory of boiling greens. The porch was a place for visitors and an accusation. The porch steps had a nick in the fourth tread where a stubborn boot once slipped, where a child had landed on a shin and cried until the cry became sleep. June had been that child. So had others. They all had a claim on these steps. The mailman had once left a package on the third step and stuck a hand deep into the mailbox for the muffled paper, and when he told June's mother he was sorry for taking the package out of order, her mother had told him, "No, son, it fits right where it fell." That sentence had been the law of the house — things belonged where they fell, where they were dropped by some finger in the dark.

June's hands were bare but for a watch whose strap had been repaired by a shoemaker who could do such things and make it look like a coin. Her fingers found the grooves of the bannister as if they belonged there still, as if the wood remembered the shape of her palm. The house smelled of dust and lemon oil, of coffee that had sat in a pot too long and of stories that had sat in a throat until they hardened into facts. There was also a thin, bright smell that was always present in the back of the pantry, like a phantom of bleach, and June had known, even before she knew the particulars of grief, that certain smells will remain after a body has left a place because smells are patient and stubborn.

The men carried the sofa out at the beginning. It was a heavy thing with carved arms like the knees of some old animal. June had sat there as a child and watched the television, a small square of static flickering, while her mother pressed a hard hand across a knee and said, "Don't get so close to the set." The sofa had saved knees, had absorbed elbows and afternoons and the weight of three bodies at once. June watched it go, watched the men balance it down the steps as if it might bail water and leak the life out of the room. The sofa left a hollow in the living room air the way a removed tooth leaves a jagged gap in a mouth. The curtains arched to look out after the sofa and then folded back, the way people fold back from their daily routines to see what has been carried away.

Visitors came and talked. They spoke as if they were buying things from a co-op of objects, as if bones had been set out for auction and the community would take turns bringing them home. They smelled like perfumes and cheap cologne, like the outside of other people's lives. A woman — named Mrs. Clapp by the tags and known as Mrs. Clapp by the town, though she had not been born that name — came because she liked the oak dining table. She echoed the price the tagger suggested and lifted a corner of it with a gesture as if it might, by being looked at, become hers. June stood by and watched. The table was a kind of planet in their lives; it had been the stage for homework and for silent meals, for the slow cutting of the meat at Christmas and for the careful folding of the Sunday paper. June's mother had polished it until the finish shone like a promise and then covered it with a crocheted runner, the way one protects a promise with ritual. Now, under the white price tag, it looked raw and candid.

June had not expected to be both the owner and the witness. Ownership had seemed an abstract until paper said it so. She wore the official name of executor like an ill-fitting hat. Her mother's will was small and had the shaky handwriting of someone who had spent a life holding other people's page numbers instead of her own. The lawyer had been patient in his small, gray office and had been kind in a way that cost nothing. "It's best," he had said, "to let things take their market." June liked the way "market" sounded stern and English. It was like the mayor's voice or the bell at high noon. It said things that could be arranged. His phrase had pressed down on the chest like a thumb holding down a map. Things will find their market. And so here they were: the market of neighbors and strangers, the market of hands and trucks and the slow arithmetic of possession.

The kitchen had been mined for years by the mother’s rituals. There the kettle had been a weather-lore instrument. When the whistle sang, neighbors knew to pick their corn at the field's edge because the tea was ready or because a child had called for help. The mother had brewed tea that could staunch arguments and tea that could begin them. June had learned the difference by ear. "Too much sugar leaves a lie behind," her mother said one morning when a young neighbor wanted to borrow money for a new tire. The mother had said a great many things, some of them like the silverware: tucked away, useful, necessary in a way that was taken for granted. Now the silverware lay in a metal tray with a tag that called it "serviceable" and a price that seemed to want to be away from the life it had served.

When June opened the cabinet beneath the sink she found a jar of marbles as if someone had set a child's game beneath the domestic machinery in case a child should appear. There had been children here — plenty of them across the years. They had sat at that table with sticky fingers and bright mouths and the mother had taught them to stack their plates like coins, to line their spoons like soldiers. The marbles were not for sale. They were marked "Keep" by a shaky hand. June did not know whether that hand was her mother's or whether she had written the word in the gray of last night's insomnia. She pressed the glass of the jar like a person checking the pulse of a sleeping animal. The marbles rattled inside like a small confession. June tucked the jar under her arm with the furtiveness of someone carrying a small contraband.

People asked about the piano. They asked about the upright that had sat in the parlor like a monument. Her mother had not been a musician; she had been a keeper of notes, someone who could read a hymn from a line and find the chord the way a cook finds salt. The piano had belonged to a grandmother or was a purchase made on credit. It had kept the house in tune because the mother liked the sound of a key struck by a forgiving hand. June had learned to play only haltingly, the way we learn to apologize. Her little fingers had hovered like hesitant birds over the keys until the right chord came and then the mother would call, "Not so loud. You'll make the neighbors think we are a family with things to hide." The piano had been the boundary and the bridge both. Now two young men with squared shoulders and bright talk were hauling it down the walk and one of them was whistling a tune that fit nothing in the house. The whistling was a wrongness in the room's mouth. It made the rooms bite their tongues.

As the afternoon slid, the sale took on the mood of a funeral without prayer. People drifted in and out, touching, lifting, measuring. They made jokes about price because jokes are the small paper banners people put up when they are uncomfortable with the grander terms: debt, legacy, erasure. A neighbor — Irene, whose widow's hat had become her crown — stood against the sky and watched the men carrying the buffet out. "Mae would have said that piece had more backbone than some of the men she knew," Irene said, and then made a sound like a bird. She was often sentimental and sometimes almost right. June heard the sentence as one of approval and as a threat. Backbones leave a mark, and nothing had been bone in that house that had not been broken and mended, a delicate sort of carpentry.

There were small glitches. A young boy tugged at a moth-eaten shawl and asked for the price and the tagger said, "Ten dollars," and the boy's face went round with hope and the mother of the boy, with the cardigan damp with sweat, said, "You don't need that, baby." June felt something like a parenthesis close inside her — the soft space where a memory slips and then is gone. There was a china cabinet that had been filled with cups for the kind of tea you do not drink alone. June had once stood in front of that cabinet and learned the recipes of women who had walked through the doors of this town carrying little bundles of advice. The cups had a floral print and one was chipped on the rim. The chipped cup had belonged to the mother who set the cup down and never noticed the jaggedness because it did not matter in the way of things that matter: the chip was a place to rest a finger during arguments.

June had not wanted to hold a yard sale. She had wanted instead to pick the house up and run with it like a loyal dog, to carry it to a place where the bad pipes would not leak and the shadow in the back bedroom would be greeted and not ignored. But the house was not portable. It was nailed to the earth by a thousand small absences. And there was the law and there was the practicality of debts paid and there was also that thing that comes to every life: time had moved and shifted the house into someone else's potential. People come to these halves of towns because they want to make them theirs; they are looking for the bones to build a new kind of liturgy.

A man bought the lamp that had stood on the table by the bed, the lamp with the stained glass shade in colors like bruises. He lifted it carefully and thanked June as if she had performed some public service. Thank yous are small coins. June took a coin and did not pocket it. It lay on the surface of her skin and slid, and she felt it for days. The lamp had been used, often, to see into a paper. The mother read until her eyes closed while she read. She read bills, notes from the church, the back of cereal boxes. She read to keep the dark from collecting in corners, to hold it at bay with the bright discipline of an open page. People who bought things loved the oldness for the wrong reasons. They did not want the story strong inside the wood. They wanted the aesthetic of lived life without the labor of carrying the history.

Bits of family memory kept appearing like pennies in a soft sofa. June found them in the cushions when they were gone. A child's crayon drawing, the outline of a stick figure with big ears — perhaps June herself — had been left tucked in the seat. There was also an envelope wrapped tight and labeled "Do Not Open Until The End" in a hand that might have been her mother's or might have been June's own last-night handwriting. June had read the envelope the week her mother died and had then tucked it back into the hands of the house because the house had been a priest and the envelope's faith needed to be mediated by more than a person. Today, with the afternoon like a slow blade, she drew the envelope out and felt the paper break across the creases of years. Inside were the three small photographs that people used to keep when they wanted to believe themselves unbroken: a daughter with a crooked grin, a young man with a laugh in his eyes, the mother at some summer picnic with a hat like a ship. The photographs fit in June's palm the way a holy thing fits in a child's hand — small and immediate.

The young man with the laugh on his face in the photograph had been someone June had simply known as a guest of the house. He had sometimes come for a meal and sometimes lingered near the steps, and once, when June was young and had a fever that left her cheeks flushed, he had sat with a glass of water in his hand and told a story about a city with a river that sang. June had believed him for reasons she could not explain. He had been part promise and part absence. In one photograph his hand rested on the rail of a porch that could have been this very porch. In another he wore a hat at a jaunty angle. People in old photographs always wear their present as a costume that will outlast them.

There was a small, lacquered music box in the dresser. When June lifted it she found the machinery inside clean and patient. When she canted it and the tune began to work through the gears, the sound hollowed out the room and filled it with a childhood she had been taught to forget the minute someone taught her to walk. The tune folded into memory the way a hand folds a child's hair. June sat on the edge of the bed and let the music do its work until a neighbor knocked and the men outside shouted something about the van being a quarter full. The music box finish had a line in the wood like a scar; perhaps there had been a fall or a fight. The mother had once knocked over the music box while ironing and had laughed as if the laughter could glue the thing back together. She believed in laughter even in small torments.

There was a place in the back bedroom that June had always avoided because a window there looked out on the dead elm and because in that room a thing had happened once that had not needed a name; it only needed the weight of silence. She had been eight the day it happened. She had been tasked with dusting and had instead sat on the floor with her back to the wardrobe and watched the dust motes move like small planets. She had heard the sound first and then the rest of it. That room had been like a locked book and then it had been undone. But memory does not always behave like a script; sometimes it rewrites itself. June crossed the hall and opened the closet door. Inside there was a stack of yellowed newspapers and a hat box with tissue like dry leaves. On top lay a small sweater with the name "June" stitched in crude purple letters. It smelled faintly of mothball and of something sweeter, perhaps the perfume a girl imagines for her future.

She could have closed the closet and walked away. She could have allowed the professionals to do their work, to tag and tally, to turn a life into an afternoon of sales. But grief has pockets and holes and slips ways are often hiding in them. June sat down on the floor. The sun slanted through the blinds and turned the dust into possible roads. She thought of her mother's hands, how they had never been still even when she slept. The hands unrolled pie dough, washed pennies, smoothed hair, stitched seams. They were always moving and so, she supposed, must the world.

When the men took the rocking chair the whole day seemed to breathe out. The rocking chair had been almost a person in the house. It had a place by the window where the mother sat in the afternoons and watched the street like a woman who had learned to read faces. The rocking chair had the memory of elbows pressed into its arms, of prayers muttered into a cloth handkerchief, of the steady bob that came when someone tried to think and think again. June found that when the rocking chair left the room it was as if the house had mislaid its pulse. The men carried it carefully, and one of them, a man with a scar across his knuckle, asked "You okay, Miss?" as if the house could be in one piece without the bone and the skin and the registry of what had been.

"Yes," June said because "No" would have required a conversation with the sky, and the sky was not going to stop for her to explain. She stood too and let the men pass, the rocking chair between them like an honored corpse. The men had a humor to them, a thing people build around grief like a fence. "Big one," they said, and "Solid." They did not say "Ghost," which is what living houses sometimes become when the people have gone and the furniture follows. The men loaded the chair into the van and the van took it away. The van had a smell of new rubber and oil and promising things, the way strangers' futures smell.

June moved through the rooms doing small, official things. She wrote down a number when someone negotiated. She stamped a receipt with the greasy stamp from the little box her mother used to keep postcards in. The stamp left a circle like a sun on the paper and June found that she liked making the circles because circles felt like completed acts. She would have liked to close the house the way one closes a book, even to fold it into the shelf and never open it again. But the house did not want to be closed and neither did the town. People began to gather on the sidewalk across the street like a congregation, watching the procession of things. Their faces were open to the spectacle as if the removal of furniture was a public ritual and it was.

A boy from down the block took, practically for free, a stack of old romance novels bound with a rubber band. They were mostly picnics and clean endings. The boy smiled like the future had already opened a door. He asked if the novels were good and June found she could not tell him the truths of plucky heroines and brooding men because the truth of their pages had been collected by the mother and it lay now in the used corners where the thumb had worn thin. "Yes," she said. "They're good." She meant yes for the boy and yes for herself and a yes that was larger than both.

Lunch came and went without ceremony. June drank coffee and a neighbor brought a sandwich wrapped in waxed paper. The town's women had rhythms that were known to each other: who could cut the ham in thin slices, who liked mustard, who took the crusts. They sat on the porch and watched the scene. Old men came and leaned against the mailbox and told each other the sizes of trees. June had used to listen to them when she was twenty, hoping that some small part of their conversation would give shape to her own life. Now she watched their mouths move like people reciting a litany for their houses.

In the afternoon light things looked washed and smaller. The house had been hollowed mostly of its public bones. Someone had bought the reading lamp, someone the headboard. The dining room chairs were piled in a neat stack like a little city. Only a few things remained: a mirror still hanging on the hallway wall, a sewing machine that looked like a found relic, a little trunk with brass corners that had been at the foot of the mother's bed for as long as June could remember. The trunk was stubborn. It would not fit through the door. They tried rolling it. They tried lifting it on its edge. It did not move. The men looked briefly at each other as if two trained animals had met and decided that a break would do them good. June looked at the trunk and a memory surprised her like a child pulling a blanket away.

The trunk had not been opening during the years of the mother's living because the mother had closed it with a kind of ritual. She would put things in and zip them down and then pat the lid as if it were a living animal and say, "Keep," and the trunk would obey. Once, as a girl, June had tried to peek into the trunk and been caught by her mother's stare. "You don't need to know yet," the mother had said, and June had sat down and learned a patience she did not have. The mother had secrets the way certain trees keep their rings. June had thought she might, someday, coax secrets out of the wood with the right kind of question. Now the trunk lay like a thing that had finally been pressed into the grammar of time.

They rolled the trunk to the door and then discovered it could not, in fact, pass through the narrow threshold. It fit the entrance of the life it had come to rest in. The men tried again. June watched the sweat run down their temples. One of them wiped his brow and muttered, "There's always one." There is always one. That is the way small town triage works: one piece will insist on staying. June stepped forward. Her hands had a memory of the lid. She knelt and unlatched the brass lock. The shop it closed in had once been warm; it smelled like old perfume now. She drew the lid up and the trunk breathed out a smell like a field after rain.

Inside were scattered things: a stack of dresses, a small cedar-smelling box of buttons, a child's matte photograph with someone else who could have been a mother and was not, and on top of the pile a letter tied with a faded red ribbon. June took the letter with a tenderness she had not known she possessed. The ribbon snapped under her fingers like a brittle reed. The handwriting inside was her mother's, a looping hand that had never learned to be small. The letter was not the long epistolary revelation June had expected. It was a list of things to do and to remember: "Check the gutters," "Send a card to Mrs. Kline," "Remember to plant the marigolds by the mailbox." There was also, at the bottom, a line that read, "June — if I don't do it, you do." Five words that were not a command and not a mercy but something in-between.

June read the line and felt the room tilt a degree. Her mother was asking for a kind of succession. Not money. Not even a house really. She was asking for the ritual of keeping. The word "keep" had been embroidered on so many corners of the mother's life: keep the keys, keep the faith, keep the roast from burning. To keep was to continue. June had left home long ago for reasons that remain economical to a small heart. She had wandered into a life that did not require the domestic liturgies of this house. And yet here, in a sudden, she felt the inheritance like an animal at her feet: loyal, expectant, needing a belly to be rubbed.

Outside, a van's engine idled like a throat waiting to speak. The men had stowed the piano, the buffet, the rocking chair. A high school kid, lanky and eager, asked if he could help in exchange for odd jobs. "We could use an extra pair of hands," one of the movers said, and the kid's face broke into a grin. June watched him and thought of the years she had wanted to have a child to teach certain lessons — how to fold, how to finish something, how to greet the world like a neighbor. She had not had those years. Her life had been, instead, the steady accrual of small economies: odd jobs, late shifts, a landlord with an ear for complaints. Here now, in the hollow of her mother's house, she was being asked to account for a different sort of ledger.

She took the letter and folded it back into her palm. She picked up a small mirror that had been nailed to the wall over the hall table and stared at herself. The woman in the mirror was a familiar stranger. Her hair had the fine silver of crowed years at the temples. Her eyes still held the quickness of a girl who had once decided to run from a place, but they were clouded with something like a calculation. Her face had lengthened in the middle the way bread stretches when you leave it to rise too long. She thumbed the picture frame and decided, in a small, decisive way, that she would do the list. Not as penance but as promise. She would check the gutters and plant the marigolds and send the cards. She would, if necessary, keep things others had not known how to keep.

The washer and dryer had been bought by a couple who lived near the river and who wanted, the woman said, to keep the sound of spinning laundry as if it were a lullaby. The mattresses were rolled up and sold, and the mother of two boys bought the set of pots because she could not get the house to her new apartment otherwise. Everyone got a piece. The men lifted, carried, stacked. June tallied in her head like an accountant with a heart.

A neighbor nudged a fold of conversation into her ear. "There's something about losing the furniture," he said, "like losing the furniture of the soul." He was not pious. He was practical. He said things to make them slight, to hold the edge of sorrow so it would not rend. June nodded because certain words are bridges made of breath. She thought of the soul as a set of chairs, folded and stored and sometimes lost.

By the time the sun leaned into the street like a tired worker, the house had taken on the look of a patient that had been wheeled into the corridor and stitched up. Windows were bare. The parlor felt like the inside of an emptied chest. The house listened. The neighbors had retreated to their porches and drives, carrying the sermon of the day with them, their judgments or their consolations. The men had gone to their truck with the last set of furniture and the young boy with the novels had the stack safe under one arm.

June walked through the rooms like a person performing a last rite. She folded a cushion and set it on the sill like a small altar. She took up the mirror and read herself again, as if she wanted the face to be something more than a witness. She went to the kitchen and opened the cupboard where the mother had kept the special tea only brought out when the company came that was worth it. The tin was empty save for a sheen of old flavor. June tipped it and let the dust swirl into her palm. The dust tasted of a place and a time and of small mercies.

At the back door she found the mother's apron hanging by the peg, its pockets empty. She took it down and put it on because the fabric wanted to feel the shape of another chest. It fit awkwardly; it smelled the way a thing remembers the body that made it. June reached into the pockets and found, folded and warm, a small handkerchief with the initials "M." The initials were stitched with a careful, nervous hand. June imagined the mother standing at the sink, face bowed into the light, tucking the handkerchief into her pocket and telling herself small admonitions. She had been earnest in this way, with a stern and particular tenderness. It had preserved people to an extent. The neighbor's children, who were often given a place at the table, had been fed by this intention.

She left the apron on and stepped out onto the porch. The late light flung itself long across the street and made shadows of the remaining lawn furniture. A man across the way had a beer in a paper bag and was not ashamed of the smell that muffled his shirt. Someone called, "Good luck," and meant the word. It reverberated in the air like a blessing. June sat on what remained of the porch step — a carved indent where many butts had sat and many knees had been rested. She felt the pulse of the road as if it were a cord tied to her ankle. The van had gone. The house had been stripped of the physical pieces that will enshrine memory, and yet memory itself would not be packed into a truck.

An old woman — a friend of her mother's — came slowly up the walk and sat next to her. She had hands like dried fruit. Her eyes were very bright. "She taught us," the woman said. "Mae taught us to measure our life in slices. Give me the good slices, give me the small ones." She lifted a hand in a way that asked not for pity but for company. June put her head on the woman's shoulder like a bell in a mouth. The woman hummed a low hymn, not exactly a song but a habit. June thought that humans are built of such small habits and unremarked mercies.

When she stood to go through the house one last time, all the rooms looked like photographs in frames. The mirror in the hall reflected the hollow skin of the place. She opened the drawer of the old oak desk and found a penny and two tickets from the movies — cheap pleasures and the proof of a life lived in attendance. She slid them into her pocket and they warmed there like two sunlit pebbles. In the bedroom the closet remained, its air still heavy with the scent of cedar and the perfume of another decade. The trunk that wouldn't move was still where she had opened it. The dresses inside were small and pale. June ran a finger over the sleeve of a dress that would have fit a woman when she was younger and thinking less about debts and more about possibility.

At the doorway she halted. The house did not look like a house that could hold a ghost. It looked instead like a place in which many ghosts had been taught to bake bread and make beds. It was ordinariness made holy. How could you lay claim to ordinariness? Where does one register that? June let the question sit like a stone in a stream and made no shallow answer to it. She felt the weight of the list her mother had left her and began, in a slow and steady way, to make a different sort of accounting.

The key in the lock was cold. She withdrew it and placed it in her palm like a small coin of authority. Her mother had always kept that key in a jar with other keys that opened things unknown. She kept it there as a test of faith, perhaps. June fastened the lock and clicked it twice. The lock made a small, dry noise like the closing of a book. It sounded final and ceremonial at once.

She did not give the key to anyone outside the law had told her to. The law had a paper of signatures, and June signed. The signatures were like stitches in a wound. There was the buyer, a young couple with a laugh that contained both hunger and hope. They promised to fix the roof and to plant the marigolds. June watched them take photos and say they could see their future there, and she hated and loved them for being so simple about it. Simplicity has its own kind of bravery.

As dusk moved in and the town lights began to prick the street like a string of modest stars, June sat on the porch and folded herself in the way one does when the day has required more than you had. She thought about what it means to manage a sale, to make decisions for a life that had been lived largely in the small sacrifice of ordinary acts. She thought about the trunk and the list and the fact that the keeping she had been asked to practice was not the heroic keeping of monuments but the mundane guardianship of a mailbox and a marigold. She felt this small task weigh down on her in a way that was not disloyal. It was an inheritance that held both obligation and blessing.

The neighbors began to drift home. The man with the paper-bag beer waved from his porch. The children had drawn chalk on the sidewalk, a map of squares and monsters. A dog that loved every neighbor equally wandered up and sat by her feet and rested its head on her shoe. June stroked its ear. The dog licked her thumb and she tasted salt and the day's dust. In the far-off evening a train moaned its lonely song and the sound made the town remember its edges.

June put the letter with the list into her wallet. She carried the small jar of marbles and the envelope with the photographs and that felt like a quiet rebellion. The marigold seeds would need to be found and planted at the right time. Checking gutters meant she would climb a ladder and stand in the vertigo of being high and small. There were others on the list that would require nails and calls and the tightness that comes with running errands for a life you love and do not fully own.

When the house was thoroughly quiet now it seemed possible that it had been empty forever. June walked the rooms one more time. She stopped at the window and looked out at the street. She listened to the house and the house listened back. In the floorboards under her feet a sound like a small heartbeat lived; perhaps it was the echo of times that would not be accounted for. She pulled the curtain aside and let the night fold in, and in that darkness the house looked softened and even kind. June thought of her mother's hands and of how they had always found the right thing to do in any given moment, even when the right thing was merely to make the tea and offer a chair. She felt, then, an odd gratitude that the mother had taught her to see the work, and to be willing, now, to do it.

The porch light burned like an eye. June took the key from its place in her palm and placed it on the table as if to make an offering. She did not turn it in the lock again. The house belonged now partly to other hands and partly to her memory and partly to the simple business of the town. It would stand, perhaps patched and painted, with a younger laugh in its rooms, with a new rhythm of dinners and bills and the occasional heartbreak. People would bring their own objects and their own ways of laying down life. June would check the gutters and plant the marigolds. She had learned in the afternoon that keeping was not the terror she had imagined. It was a labor of continuity.

She stood then and walked down the porch steps with the jar of marbles cradled like a relic. The last light of the sun made the porch boards look like piano keys. A neighbor closed a screen door, and a radio somewhere played something old and good. June walked the sidewalk once more and paused at the mailbox where the mother had once told the mailman the thing about the package. She left the marbles there in the crook of the mailbox as if the box might need a consolation. They rattled like small prayers when she set them down.

When she walked away, the house looked up at her with all the casual sorrow of a familiar animal. It did not call her back. It did not need to. June had taken with her the recipes of keeping: small lists, a particular way of folding things, the commitment to check gutters. She had also taken small things that would be private storerooms — the photographs and the music box and the stubborn ticket stubs. The rest would travel to other houses where people would learn their own habits of keeping.

At the corner she turned back once and saw the porch light flame in the dusk and the outline of the woman who had been her mother seated in the dark, perhaps waiting for the kettle to sing, perhaps listening to a radio. June did not cry and she did not laugh. She felt the morning of the next day and of many small tomorrows like a hand touching a pulse. The town moved along the street as if nothing of magnitude had happened, and that was the town's way: life folds its losses and its keepings into the ordinary.

June walked home slow and easy, the house's address heavy as a line in her mouth. She was fifty and some change, and carried, secret and small, the things that would let her be a keeper in a world that wants so badly to be careless. She had managed the sale, had watched the strangers carry away the furniture and the shape of days, and had, in the tightness of her pocket, the list and the photograph and the marbles. She had, she thought, something like agency, which is to say the ability to hang a small flag in the face of the inevitable and say, "This matters." The day had taught her that the work of a life is sometimes only the keeping of small edges. She walked on, the town breathing around her like a familiar, and the night folded around the house across the street until the porches glowed like the ribs of something resting.
