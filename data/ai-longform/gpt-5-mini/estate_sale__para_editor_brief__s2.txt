They carried the couch out feet-first as if it were a sleeping person, two strangers in work shirts and one teenager in a hoodie navigating the hallway like they were trained in transferring fragile things. The sofa, a roomy thing upholstered in faded sunflower fabric, had defined one entire summer of Evelyn's life: knees tucked into its crook while rain tapped the porch roof, her mother's voice pouring from the kitchen into endings of sentences and pie recipes. Now the men grunted under its weight and angled it through the front door; it scraped the jamb once with a complaining squeal of wood, and then the three bodies and the summer of her childhood were gone from the living room, stepping down into the dappled light on the front walk and onto the flattened grass of the yard where the pickup waited.

Evelyn watched like a woman seeing the rest of herself carried into the truck. She had a clipboard in her hand, the estate sale agent's cheery, laminated list with item numbers and chalked prices clipped to it—a bureaucratic liturgy of liquidation—and she kept her face in the professional posture of the manager. Her right thumb found the crease where she'd signed the probate papers three days ago. When she had written her mother's name on that line—a name she had not said out loud for some weeks—it had seemed smaller than she remembered, less like the person who had scolded and hummed and crocheted through three generations.

"Watch the glass," one of the men said to the other, and she realized her voice was still in the house, everywhere, a soundscape of what had once been a life: the kettle's high note, her mother's small laugh, the slow click of the mantel clock. Here, now, the strangers didn't know those shapes. They only saw angles and handles.

People came at an estate sale the way gulls come to shore: with appetites specific to things. A woman in work boots and a layered thrift-store dress moved through the kitchen, fingering the blue enamel pans that hung on the pegboard. She had her hair scraped back under a bandanna and an expression of concentration that belonged to cartographers and jewelers. "You know," she said to Evelyn, holding up a pan like a treasure, "my old lady had one just like this. She made potato soup that would fix a weekend."

Evelyn said, "That's the set from when Mom taught at the school. She said she'd never buy anything new after she found those." The woman nodded, and for a moment they exchanged an intimacy based not on memory but on the mutual recognition of value. Then the woman handed over a fifty and wrote her number on the receipt pad; she wanted the set for $25. Evelyn counted the cash and felt the abstractedness of it. Money—crisp, impersonal—grew like a fog in the corners of the house, laying over the wooden floors and the pattern of the wallpaper.

The house had been a map of small rituals. A milk crate of records sat by the fireplace—Frank Sinatra, Patsy Cline, Simon & Garfunkel—all of them had been scraped into shapes by the recording needles of her youth. That crate had been set outside in the front yard minutes earlier with a price tag swinging from a brittle shoelace, because the estate agent had decided music sells well to younger buyers seeking vintage. A boy with a skateboard browsed through the titles and made a small, satisfied sound when he found something he liked. "How much?" he asked. Evelyn quoted the price and then watched him press his fingers to the album cover as if feeling texture gave him ownership.

The day was hot in the way that Ohio heat sometimes is—heavy and earnest, refusing to evaporate—and the house held that weight in its plaster. The man who had bought the oak dresser stepped into the doorway with his phone tilted to catch the exact light, angling the carved lion heads into a photograph. He told Evelyn he'd take it to his wife's studio and make it the base of a sink. "She does restoration," he said. "Turns old furniture into new things. Pays attention." Evelyn watched him measure with his eyes, thinking of the hairbrushes and the folded slips of paper that had lived in that dresser's top drawer for as long as she could remember. She imagined someone cutting a hole for a basin and installing metal where wood stood.

Her mother had always been practical in an old-fashioned way. Even when she saved birthday cards in a cedar chest, they were arranged with an efficiency that suggested no sentimentality could be allowed to spill. Today those cards were in an acrylic box on the kitchen table with a price tag, beside a porcelain rooster the size of a fist. People made linear progress through loss here: from cupboard to table to hand to bag.

Evelyn had arrived at the house before mid-morning, early enough that the dust motes were thick in the beams of light and the realtor had not yet opened the double doors. She had sat for a long time at the kitchen table with a cup of black coffee, the kind that her mother had always taken with a pinch of sugar, watching the house wake up. There had been a list of choices to make then—what to keep, what to let go, what would actually leave the property. Her sister, Laurel, had suggested they donate the grandmother clock and the good china to the church rummage. Laurel loved grand gestures, loved the idea that an object could be saved by being given away. Evelyn, who had lived in apartments and moved often for work, could see an aesthetic in every piece: the way the spindle bed caught or threw light, the bruise of the wall under the family photographs where some picture had hung for decades.

She had imagined this afternoon as if it were a ritual to perform correctly, all the ways practiced grief had suggested: lay out the things you will sell in tidy stacks, number them, price them like coaxing a child through chores. But you cannot price the things that have been used to locate a life, and the people who came to buy did not come to buy that either. They came for the usable, the decorative, the story to flip into their Instagram feed.

"I grew up on the other side of town," said Mrs. Hargrove from across the foyer, wrist leaned against the banister. She was a neighbor from the council of the church and had brought a brown paper lunch sack with a thermos of iced tea to keep vigil. Her hair was a pale cloud, her cardigan had elbow patches, and she moved through rooms like a scholar through a library, touching things with the hush taught by long acquaintance. "Evelyn, sweetheart, you need a break? I can man the front lawn if you want."

"I'll be fine," Evelyn said. The word felt solid and true to her own mouth. "I actually need to watch the tall things go. The movers like to knock over shorter things."

Mrs. Hargrove smiled with the kind of small menace that is friendly but precise. "I saw you talking to the boy with the skateboards. Don't let him go off with Granddad's watch."

"That's Dad's watch," Evelyn corrected, and then she was aware of the watch's absence, a small numb gap in the jewelry tray where her father's watch used to be. That watch had kept time over decades of porch-sitting summers; it had been stiff and ornate, a brass weathered with the years like an island of ritual on the dresser.

There were halting conversations that functioned as interstitial tissue, connecting immediate exchange to a stranger's story. A couple in their thirties asked about the upright piano in the parlor. "Is it playable?" the woman asked, brooming a strand of hair behind her ear. She had a child in arms; the man beside her had the distracted patience of a person who imagined family life in harmonies. Evelyn considered the piano the way one considers a particular part of a graveyard: it had been where her mother had sat and learned hymns and the few easy ragtime numbers she'd taught Evelyn before inertia and arthritis slowed the hands. She had said, yes, it was playable, and then found herself telling them that when Evelyn had first fallen in love she had learned to play "Moon River" on that very instrument. The woman smiled in a way that suggested the memory was an inherited garment that fit, and she bought it for a price less than Evelyn hoped, because of the risk of moving something as heavy as a century-old piano.

The movers were efficient, almost clinical in their care. They padded corners with moving blankets, looped straps under legs, worked with a choreography that erased years of sitting into a compact, rational process of relocation. They used planks as levers to slide a heavy oak table down the hall, the wood throwing up a thin loneliness at each scrape. These men—Tommy, who whistled off-key; Nathan, who kept asking for the right angle—were not mean. They were just necessary. Evelyn watched them like someone watching ocean waves: steady, unstoppable, little by little altering the coastline.

When a man took the brass lamp from the bedside table and gave it a tentative swing to feel its weight, Evelyn found herself saying, automatically, "That's the lamp I read palms by once when I was sixteen." The man, who was younger than that time, laughed and said he inherited his mother's suspicion of anything that read futures. Evelyn felt herself give up the story in the same way she gave up the lamp. The man put the lamp in his truck and drove away without knowing that the table under it had held the perishable kindnesses of tea and cake and scolding pennies for the light switch.

Pausing between buyers, Evelyn walked into her mother's bedroom. The wallpaper there had roses trapped in a lattice pattern; it peeled slightly at the corner by where her mother's dresser had always stood. Her mother had slept on that side of the bed, always with the same sleeping cap on her head for the past ten years. Evelyn ran her finger along the banister of the bed and returned to the dresser, where a life had been arranged in trays and little bowls. There was an earring missing—one of a pair she had given her mother for a Christmas, many years back. She had never managed to match them up again after years of misplacing them in the folds of laundry and the edges of purses. For a moment she thought she might find it when the movers opened drawers, something slipped between boards and revealed itself like a small forgiveness. But the drawers were empty, the jewelry boxes opened and picked through by a woman in a green vest who had more patience for the sparkle of things than for the memories they held. The green-vested woman said, "I collect costume jewelry. I can make a living hemming little things into big statements," and Evelyn imagined the earrings glittering on a stage of thrift-store fashion.

At noon the funeral home vans had left with the last of the printed flowers. The house, briefly, smelled of lilies and motor oil, of exhaust and violets. People drifted from the kitchen to the driveway with their purchases like migratory birds with their nests. Evelyn checked items off the list but kept making mental annotations of what she would not sell. The cedar chest under the stairs, she had told the lawyer, was hers to take; it would go in a quieter place, a smaller house where she would lay out the things she kept for reasons that were not necessarily rational—photographs, a wedding veil with yellowing lace, some of the handwritten recipes in her mother's fine, slanted hand.

She watched as a man with steady hands carried the wooden highchair her mother had used to feed Evelyn when she was small. It had a tray ring across its front and small scratches from a hundred clumsy meals, and a sticker on the back for a pediatric dentist with a number starting with an old area code. The boy who carried it had tape on his fingers and smelled faintly of diesel. He smiled at Evelyn in the way that people smile when they take on a job that has dignity. "You want this?" he asked, a little awed at the person who was its original owner, because in this town ownership is a currency of its own.

"No," Evelyn said. "I have pictures of me in it. I think it's time it had a new set of pictures."

A little later a girl in a sundress, hair in a messy bun, came to buy the highchair's tray for a planter. "I'm using it for succulents," she said earnestly. "Old wood, new life." There was a sentiment to this repurposing that pleased Evelyn because it was also a way of translation: what had been child-eating was becoming plant-housing; the cycle of nurturing did not end, it merely changed forms.

The front yard became a staging area for the day's commerce. People clustered there with armfuls of things—an enamel lamp, a stack of vintage postcards, a child's train set missing an engine. Conversations branched out into the town's gossip: who had a shop on Main Street, who had reopened the bakery, which church had a good pie sale next Sunday. They asked after Evelyn as though she were a literary figure in their town's ongoing story. "How's life up in Cleveland?" a woman from the PTA asked. "You must be back for the house."

Evelyn told them that she lived in the city, that she had two grown children who had their own lives and jobs and had called less often than she'd liked. It was the truth, but there was a softness to how she admitted it, as if naming the distance made it solid and manageable. People nodded and murmured their condolences and then asked if she wanted coffee or if she needed help hauling the china. It surprised her how quickly the weight of a relationship could be redistributed. Yesterday, the house had been the center of one life. Today, it was a resource for the town, a moment in the circulation of things.

Around three, a young couple arrived in a pickup with a bassinet in the back. The woman was small with a ring of freckles; the man had a beard and a bruised elbow from working on cars. They wanted the sewing machine, the one with the black cast-iron body and the treadle that had once coughed and clacked like a happy animal. "I can fix that," the man said, his voice low and tender, as if speaking of a long-term patient. The bassinet's blanket had been crocheted by someone whose fingers had been more deliberate than thin nerves allowed. Evelyn watched as the man lifted the machine from its place, the treadle's polished wood reflecting the afternoon light. She had sat on that machine's bench in other decades, learning to stitch hems in awkward lines. She remembered her mother leaning over the bench, saying, "You must keep your stitches even, like a steady heart." The young couple promised to set the sewing machine in their studio, to practice clothing repairs and baby blankets and to sneak it into living-room craft nights.

Evelyn sold more than she had imagined she would. There was no ceremony to it beyond the human negotiation and the slow tally on the clipboard. A neighbor asked for the brass lamp with the green glass shade because "it reminds me of the library in '57," and she took it home and wrapped it in newspaper. A teenage girl bought the tray of costume rings and laughed as she tried them on, stacking them like coins. An older man in a work jacket bought the dining chairs and told Evelyn he'd make new seats for them out of leather someday. "My wife says she wants a little of the old look in the kitchen," he said. "She likes the grain of that wood."

The chairs were hard to watch go. They had been the stage for many family meals—turkey nights, school report nights, awkward teenagers coming home with bad news. The dining room table was a map of scuffs and stains, a constellation of where glasses had been set. One of the chairs still held the dent of a child's bottom sunk into it, a soft history of hasty tucks and hurried kisses. As the chairs were carried past the doorway, Evelyn stepped in front of them like someone queening a funeral procession, and the movers, with quick respect, adjusted their course, moving like a current around her.

At one point, someone found an old tin, battered and dulled, at the back of the pantry. When they opened it, a smell escaped like a secret: lavender and something else—a faint whiff of pipe tobacco. Inside were letters tied with twine, the knotted ribbon gone brittle with years. Evelyn's hands, overseen by a polite self, reached for one and saw her mother's handwriting: slanted, practiced, with a proper signature. The letter was to a man named Charlie, someone Evelyn did not know. Her mother had written of the market and of an apple tree that had blighted. There had been a small sketch in the margin of a house with a crooked chimney. Evelyn read it as if opening an old map; the lines led her through a life before her, before the practicalities of the mother she had known.

"You can have those," Mrs. Hargrove said from below, where she had been keeping watch. "If you want them, keep them."

Evelyn held the letters in her hands and felt the past gather like rain. She had the legal right to whatever the house contained, but the age of objects carried with it not only rights but duties. Duties to memory, to the way one tells a life story. She slipped the letters into her bag. It felt like smuggling a small kindness out in her pocket.

By mid-afternoon the traffic slowed. The front lawn had dwindled of its human tide. The truck had already made two trips down the lane, piled high with things that would travel to new rooms and new neighborhoods. People who had bought small things now sat on the porch steps counting their bills and comparing future uses for their purchases. Someone joked that the town should host an annual "reclamation day" where everything old returned to usefulness by way of someone's hands. Laughter scattered.

When the piano player left with a patient smile, the house sank a little into itself as if it had been released from a strain. Evelyn walked through the rooms, checking that nothing was left for the movers' list and asking herself why she felt so bereft when the objects that were gone were replaced by more practical freedom. It could be that grief lives in things and in the acts that circulate them out of the living room and into new living rooms; it could be that the presence of those objects had been a daily lie she had told herself to believe her life was anchored. Or perhaps it was simpler: the house, stripped of furniture, allowed a kind of acoustic she did not like. Without the couch and the rug and the piano, the house's voice had become too high, too clean.

In the den, she stopped before the mantel. There was the mantle clock that had not worked in decades, its hands stopped at 2:17. Her father had wound it each Sunday until he forgot how the days fit, and then he had forgotten how to wind about everything else. The little brass figure at its side was tamped with dust. On the mantel stood photographs with names that belonged to the town as if the town were a family portrait stitched from many frames: weddings, graduations, a high school team holding up a trophy. Evelyn remembered her mother's dabbed-lipstick smile at a 1957 prom, her father's crooked grin as he held a beer at his fiftieth. The photographs were the territorial markers of a life; the edges yellowed like the pages of a well-thumbed book.

She placed the tin of letters next to the clock, patting the nook where they'd sit. She realized that she had been cataloging more than things all afternoon. With each item sold, she had been disposing of a version of herself. And with each purchase—every woman who walked away with Mom's casserole dish, every man who bought the desk where her father had balanced checkbooks—someone else had become the heir of a moment.

The last big thing to go was the cane-backed rocker that had sat on the porch through scorch and rain, the rocker that had been made by some unseen hand in the early twentieth century and had been smoothed by generations. Her mother had rocked there after baths, and sometimes had said her name aloud into the evening, as if calling herself in. The boys lifted the rocker and carried it toward the truck with a soft ritual. They paused at the gate and, as if obeying a chorus, tipped it toward Evelyn and then, all at once, placed it in the bed of the pickup.

Evelyn crossed the yard to watch. Up close she saw the rounded arms where the varnish had been rubbed away by the repetition of hands. There were dark spots on the arm's inners where sunlight had kissed the wood. The man in the truck turned to her. "We try to take good care," he said. "People keep telling us they get it back better than it was."

She imagined the rocker on a porch somewhere on the edge of town, a new woman settling into its hollow like water taking a shape. It made her imagine her mother’s days as not finished but continued, stitched into the quiet rituals of a stranger's life. Evelyn found the idea less like theft and more like translation.

When the last load left, twilight edged in. The movers had cleaned what they could and swept, though they had missed a splintered piece of molding near the stair. Evelyn found herself touching it with a considerate finger, willing it to mend. The real estate agent, who had arrived late in a gray blazer, clipped the final sign-off and discussed the listing with the detached efficiency of someone who had the luxury of moving on. "It's a charming property," he said. "Two bedrooms, original molding. Could be three with an addition on the back. We're thinking we market to young families, people looking for character."

"Character," Evelyn repeated under her breath. She had always imagined it meant something like a personality for houses. It occurred to her that the real estate agent and the men moving furniture spoke a kind of practical language for what was otherwise an economy of feeling.

The house, in the end, felt more like a waiting room. The couches were gone; the chest of drawers had been taken; the plates stacked away. Only a few things remained: a framed photograph of a wedding on the wall of the guest room, stubbornly hung by a nail that had not been removed; a hanging lamp with a cracked shade; the potted fern on the porch that had survived her mother's neglect. Evelyn went to sit on the stoop where her mother had sat for decades, hands folded in her lap. The wood was hot from the day's sun and smelled faintly of lemon oil. She set the tin of letters on the step beside her as if they might overheat.

On the lawn, the truck idled while the movers checked straps. A neighbor had set out folding chairs and was offering to make the leftover coffee into pots of chicken soup, a community gesture that felt patchwork and kind. Evelyn watched the truck pull away; each mile ate a little of the town's density, and the front walk felt stripped of its private history. People who had bought whittled furniture and mismatched plates waved as they drove past, jostled with the happy problem of where to put such things in their own yards.

When the last truck turned the corner and left a narrowing of sound, Evelyn stood and walked through the house again. The rooms were echoes. She touched the wall where a picture had been and felt the coolness of the plaster. An ache pulled at her in a shape she could not name; it was not grief for the person, not exactly—her mother had been dead for weeks and Evelyn had done the practical, legal, and necessary things that follow a death—but a more complex reaction to the removal of the scaffolding of a life.

In the kitchen, some man had left a coffee mug with "World's Best Aunt" printed on the side. It was a small, ridiculous claim; she turned it over in her hands. The handle had been repaired once with tape that had yellowed into an archival statement. Her mother had not been anyone's "best" in public stories, but she had been necessary. Evelyn felt a concession then—an allowance for what had been present that would remain in ways not measurable by sale or inventory.

She went into the bedroom and opened the cedar chest she had intended to keep. It smelled of cedar and dust and long winter afternoons. Inside were the ribbons of a hundred small victories: a yellowed certificate of merit, a school playbill where her name appeared in pencil, a dress pinned in tissue paper that her mother had saved and never worn again. She took out the wedding veil, its lace a cobweb of flowers, and held it to the light. The veil had become translucent and almost tender, the threads the color of bones. Evelyn saw not the wedding—her marriage had been practical and without spectacle—but the moment between who she was and who she'd become: a woman who left home and then returned with other lives under her arms.

She carried the veil out to the porch and sat a while as the light tilted. On the far edge of the town, the steeple clock chimed in fours the way it always had, solid and indifferent. A breeze moved through the lattice of the porch, finding the loose edges of lace and teasing them. Evelyn draped the veil over her head for a moment, as one applies an old garment more for the posture than for the warmth. She felt a small and ridiculous lightness as if some part of her had been permitted to be younger for a breath.

Someone had left a small ceramic cup on the mantel—a chipped thing with a sunflower painted in the middle. Evelyn knew the cup well; she had once hidden under the kitchen table with it, drinking warm tea on a night she had been sent to bed early for something she could not fully remember. The cup was not on any list. It had somehow not been priced, not been moved to a different shelf. The person who had cleaned the house had left it there because they did not see its value, because real value can be measured in market terms and not in the secret ways a hand remembers the rim. Evelyn took the cup and cradled it as one would cradle a small, active animal. Its glaze had a hairline crack through which light shone like an old scar.

She could have boxed it and carried it back to Cleveland. She could have placed it carefully on her shelf beside the other small things she had kept from her past: a ticket stub from a college play, a key to a locker, a child's doodle in an old textbook. She thought about how she would explain it to her children: a small, chipped cup, a piece of a woman they'd never know. And she thought about closing the door on this house and not coming back, about naming the moments that have always been there and letting the rest go.

Her hand tightened on the cup. The afternoon was closing; there were calls to make, paperwork to sign, receipts to shepherd. She could place the cup in the cedar chest and later take it into her own life as a talisman against forgetting. The house creaked with a habitual soundtrack: the floorboard by the door complaining, the attic door settling into the evening. Outside, a car passed slow, headlights brightening the drive.

Evelyn placed the cup on the steps beside the letters and the sundew of a dried-out fern. It looked at home there, more so than it would in a display case. She sat on the porch when it grew too dark to do anything but be. The town proper washed itself in the softer light of lamps. At the corner the diner still blinked neon even though it was late; a couple ate under a weathered booth, their laughter low and private. A church bell sounded from across town, and someone down the block swept a walkway, the bristles making a drag-lullaby sound.

She thought of the men carrying the furniture away—how they were not violating the house so much as translating it. There was a kind of continuity in it, she decided. Woods become driftwood, tables become new displays, a baby's highchair becomes a planter holder. She had spent the afternoon watching strangers reshape her mother's world and, in that watching, had learned that family was not only what we keep but what we allow to be reshaped.

The realtor called to say he would put the listing up tomorrow. The final paper would be signed and the agent promised to send photographs with the house's interior staged for the market: neutral pillows, a vase of lilies, a set of lanterns on the porch—i.e., the house in the image people like to buy. Evelyn imagined the photographed rooms as ghosts of themselves, touched up and softened. She did not want to pretend the house had been lived in for a time when her mother had been alive. But she did not need the house to be a museum either.

The caretaker of the town, Mrs. Hargrove, came to sit on the step beside her, patting the stretch of wood as if it were an old friend. In the distance, headlights slid around the curve by the brick bridge. The air was cooler now, the late-summer humidity settling into the soil and the curtains. "You did right," Mrs. Hargrove said. "You know, your mother would have liked you to make sure things went where they would be loved."

"That's the trouble," Evelyn said. "How do you know where things will be loved?"

Mrs. Hargrove's reply was a small concession to the world. "You don't. You hope."

They sat with the hope like a thing that could be warmed by hands. The porch light above their heads blinked and then held. Evelyn felt the cup warm slightly as she set her palm over it. The letters beside it were quiet in their bundle, waiting to be read slowly in the safe hours of the evening. The town's voice softened further into night: the distant siren of an ambulance, a dog barking once and then not again, the steady tick of the mantle clock in the house that no longer kept time.

When the dealer from the antique shop came by to pick up the last photograph—the framed wedding picture that had held three generations in its small perfection—Evelyn let him take it. He handled it with the care of someone who has to imagine narratives via the objects he sells. He said, "I'll make sure it looks like a thing of its time. People like that." He carried it down the steps and slid it into his van, and Evelyn watched the car go, the tail light drawing a small, red comma in the dark on the road.

She thought, for the first real time since morning, about returning to Cleveland. The city—its din and its traffic and the precise address where she kept a vase of plastic flowers—was waiting with her life there, both an anchor and a place where memory could be less invasive. She would take the letters and read them slowly on her couch in her apartment, and the cup would wait in a table drawer for mornings when she wanted to hold something small and speak its name. At the corner, a taxi down-street had stopped to take a man home; he waved at Mrs. Hargrove, who waved back and then rose to go.

Evelyn rose too. She stood at the edge of the porch and looked at the house as the dusk suggested a new kind of portrait. It seemed both less and more than it had been. The rooms were empty but not dead; they were quieter but not abandoned to silence. The house had been translated into something lighter, a form with less commitment, and Evelyn felt the peculiar relief of a burden set down.

She closed the door behind her softly and felt the click of the lock as if it were a punctuation. The key turned with a small authority that belonged to the living. She walked down the path, the cup wrapped safe in her bag, the letters heavy against her hip, and the house watched her go like a neighbor who had been given a last moment of gossip.

On the first step of the sidewalk she paused and took a deep breath. The air smelled faintly of cut grass and the metallic promise of rain. In the distance, the Perkins Road light blinked green and then red. A moth beat itself against the porch light once and then fell behind the lattice. Evelyn lifted the small cup out of her bag and set it briefly in her hands, feeling the hairline crack as a roadmap. When she closed her fingers around it, the gesture was modest and complete. She held the cup to her chest for a moment like a small prayer and then placed it back into the bag.

A last truck passed by, its radio playing country songs that washed their way across the lane, indifferent, forgiving. Evelyn walked toward her car on the street, each step filling with the assurance of things left undone but not lost. People who had come to take the furniture now had it, and the house would stand waiting in its quiet, waiting for someone to imagine its future. Evelyn walked away with a handful of small, chosen things, and as she turned the bend she imagined the letters opening in her hands like windows. She would read them slowly, like someone learning a town all over again, line by line, asking the living for permission to hold what used to be a life and to make it a small harbor for herself.
