They fell upon him with howls of outrage. They beat him down upon the stone and the sound of their fists was a dry thunder in his ears and there was the bitter bite of blood in his mouth and the mucous of his nose and the taste of the dust of the square and above them the black rim of the sun hung like an obscene coin and would not move, and everywhere the hands of men and the flailing of their arms and the fists that found his face again and again until his cheek was a great purpled map and his teeth were loosened and they dragged him by the hair and the rags torn from his back and set him prone so that the cold earth could answer back to the heat that would not be, and in that darkness and without the light he thought he could hear the prophet's voice once more making the old bright promises as one might repeat the our father when the house is on fire, as if words could lay a skin over catastrophe, and there were those among them who wept and those who cursed and the thunder of feet was like animals grown sore and a lamp in the wind.
He woke with the taste of iron in his mouth and the ache of the blows in his ribs as if they had been struck, and he sat up in the bed with his hands over his face and the woman's fingers were at his wrist drawing him back, hush, hush she said, her voice a small bell in the dark, and the nameless weight pressed in the soundless place of her belly as if it were a second small heart complaining, and he cursed beneath his breath but quieter now, as one who has walked out of a fever into a cold room.
What time is it, he said.
She told him quietly and there was beyond her words the sound of the river under the night, a long thin hiss like a thing being unmade, and a dog barked once far off and then ceased, and the sky beyond the torn shutters had the particular red of weather to come.
You been hollerin' again, she said.
I dreamed, he said. The same one.
She had heard it before and had learned to make light of it, to tuck it away in the small stores of her patience like a stone, but there was a hardening to her voice now and he read in it what he would never otherwise have had to name. Why, she said. Why these ones?
He could not answer her. He had no ready word to put to it. He lay back on his hand and the pillow took the shape of his jaw and the bed smelled of their bodies and of the wood smoke from the room below and of rot and fermented grain, and in that breathing place the dream continued to move as if a film behind his eyes.
And it had been the sun, he said at last, as if that explained anything. It did not.
She sat up and for a long moment watched him. Her eyes were the color of riverglass and in them was the calculation of the woman who carries a child between the ribs of a dying country. You going out today? she asked.
He had been thinking of it in the last days, of moving, of leaving town before the workmen came with the list of what was owed and the men who took payment in more than coin. He had meant to go to the river that runs like advice through the lowlands, to try his hand at the angling or at trade, to find news, to see if the bearded men had passed that way. Maybe they had, maybe not. He had the sense of being a man on whose back the rooms of the house had closed up like a fist. He would go out, he said, but he knew it was a small thing to tell her and a smaller thing to do.
She reached for her skirt to pull it about her knees and in the movement there was the live fear of what they would be leaving behind if they left at all: a door with a lock grown brittle, a little shelf with a bowl cracked, the small jar of bones of saints and saints' teeth that someone had kept through the winter, as if charm could hold back the histories of the town.
Outside the light was a hard thing searing at the edge of the world and it came not as a slow dawn but as a smearing, and by the time they had dressed and pulled the door to there was already a strange electric smell in the air that ran in the hair like a backhand. They walked in the lane between the rows of houses half of which had been gutted for wood, the doors off their hinges leaning like open mouths, and men in rags sat on the thresholds counting with their thumbs the coins of the morning or doing nothing at all, their heads bowed and their faces raw with the wind.
The square lay ahead and there the plastered pillar where the prophet had stood was a blackened stump and the cobbles were glassy with something like the gloss of riverslick. There were bits of what had been chairs and the torn shreds of banners that had once been painted with the prophet's signs, and nowhere was there the bright turret of his presence. The small crowd of the morning was gathered about the pillared shaft and in the center of them was a man who did odd work with his hands repairing a boot and who looked up as they came with a slow blanching of his face as if their passage had been foretold.
He recognized the man at once. He had been among those who had waded to the platform on the night the prophet had made his promises, had been there when the first rim of sun went thin and the man beside him had taken his words as gospel until the dark came down and the promise did not keep. The prophet had not returned to speak; he had gone away in the dark, or he had remained and been burned by the mob, no one could say, but his place in their memory was the empty place of a wound.
You been here long, the man with the boot said.
Just turned, the dreamer said. He tried to smile and the shape of his mouth was like a rag, and the man nodded and did not attempt more words, as if all language left them when the sun went out and could not be used for the making of small comfort.
We goin' farther west? the dreamer asked. Heard there was talk of the sea.
The man spat into the dust and shook his head. The sea was a tale told by those who had not been to it and it had the bright edges of rumor. People had been going west for years, pushing into the emptier lands where the law was less and the promises free, and some had returned with goods and some had returned with neither goods nor hope. The town took what it could and bartered the rest with luck.
A child ran by them and overturned a pail and the water went silver along the stones. At the far edge of the square a cart stood broken with its wheel sunk in the mud and in it were a man and a woman who had the look of those who had lain down for the last time and been left by the road.
We got ta be smart, the woman's eyes said without words to him as he passed, and he could only think of how hollow and how exact were such small counselings.
They were not yet out of the square when a voice broke the thin air, a voice that held a quality like the metallic ring of a struck bell, and people turned and the dreamer knew it was the bearded one he had seen earlier in his life on the roads, a man whose beard had the look of a winter bramble and who carried himself as if his body had been seasoned against long bad weather. The bearded one had been making a circuit of the outlying tents and he had that manner of voice that could call a dog from a hedge.
You there, he said, and the dreamer felt the small pang of being called as if by a bell. You seen three goin' on the river road? he asked.
The bearded one nodded. Maybe, he said. Maybe. He narrowed his eyes at them in a manner as if weighing whether they were a burden or an opportunity. He had with him two others, younger men who watched the town with the wary expression of predators. They sat on their haunches and lit cigarettes from a joint of tobacco and the smoke made them look like grey spirits.
We got food, the bearded one said to the dreamer. We got blankets. We got work for those who can swing an arm. He could have been offering a blessing, or a bargain, the dreamer could not tell which, and as often the words came burned by the heat in his mouth.
The woman took a step forward and her hand lay on his sleeve as if to warn him back. She did not see the way the bearded one smiled; his teeth were a little too white and the smile was a thing worked at, like a trade, and in it was something that did not reach his eyes. He had the smell of smoke and meat and something else in his coat lining that the dreamer felt as a small alarm. People who carried traded goods often had certain smells; people who made their living by taking had others.
We goin' on, the bearded one said. We go west. There is work on the line. They needs men who can take a barrel and not cry. He winked at the dreamer and the other two laughed softly, not in a pleasant way. The march of their laughter was like pebbles skittering over a tin roof.
Later, as they were leaving, the bearded one called after them. You two, he said, and gestured past them to a narrow alley where the town kept its refuse and its secrets. The sound of him was casual but the dreamer felt the sinew in the air around them tighten. You hear, he said. They got a place down by the river. If you need work, look us up. He gave the name and the dreamer repeated it stupidly and the bearded one put his hand on the dreamer's shoulder as if to befriend him and the pad of his thumb was rough and warm and smelled of old wine.
They took their pack and began walking west along the road that led out of the town and at their shoulder the woman rested a palm on the curve of her belly as if for the small life within, and the dreamer felt the strange newness of her touching and the old tiredness that lies coiled in the lean of a man's shoulders. For a long time they went without speaking, and the road uncoiled under their steps, dust and the tatters of human living, the bones of hedgerows and the silver of dead fishes near the gutter.
They made camp that night beside an arm of the river where the reed grew thick and the sky had the color of a cooked liver, and they slept under a roof of tarpaulin with the dog at their feet, and the river ran soft and untroubled like a man who had forgotten how to be fearful. There were, they had found, others along the bank, tents and fires, men who traded in the small consolations of a wandering life, who mended nets and smoked for warmth, and the couple set their meagre bowls beside theirs and drank from the river and ate stale bread and the meat of a rabbit they had trapped in the day.
At their fire that night the dreamer told the woman of the prophet and of the square and of the man who had the boot, and he told her with the air of one rehearsing something inevitable, and she watched him with that look that holds counsel even when the words are spare.
There were things they had to do, he said. We can't be here when the men come through. The bearded ones. She looked at the fire with that small, hard patience. You think they'll take us? she said. She did not say who they would take or what they would take, and the dreamer knew she meant everything.
He tried to remember the shape of their hands when he had seen them in the square, the bearded one with his thin white smile and the two who laughed like pebbles. He tried to set in his mind the ways of men who did not mind to take what was not rightfully theirs. There was the thought that he might go to them, that he might offer work in exchange for credit, or labour, or the chance to carry a bag for them, anything so long as they would leave the woman and the child to be.
In the morning the river glittered and the reed made a sound like a hundred sleeping men. They packed and the dog wagged a small uncertain tail and the woman watched the dreamer with an expression that carried the history of resolutions and their costs. I don't want you to go, she said, and he could hear the plea in the small phrasing.
I got to know, he said. I got to know where they're camped down river. He lied to her a little and meant it in the sense of someone trying to tell a truth not yet arrived. There's work, he said. Maybe for both of us.
She let that go for the moment and let him go into the morning with his backpack and his beltknife and the feeling of something like dread filling his chest like a fist. The road ran thin and the town shrank behind them into a geometry of roofs and the smoke of hearths; they passed other bands of travelers who cast their eyes away as if sight itself were a currency you must never spend carelessly.
At the ford they came upon the three men from the river sitting on a log and dividing their ration of bread. The bearded one had no beard in the sense of softness; it was hard clipped and bristling and he carried a rifle that had been sawed down and wrapped in oilcloth. He was chewing something that made his jaw work audibly and his eyes, when he looked up, were the sort of eyes that measured risk and reward with the same small interest.
You looking for work? he asked as the dreamer came near and there was that dry bell in his voice again.
The dreamer was a man new to the sort of life that needed to bargain for its continuance. He found himself saying yes, as if it were a prayer. The bearded one laughed and passed him a piece of stale bread then offered him a smoke and they sat on the bank and talked in the language of those who have eyes for only the present moment and little forward thought. He said that they had seen the prophet's road and that there were places to be cleared and that such work paid in goods and sometimes in coin. He said other things that were vague and promising and the two younger men nodded as if in agreement with a fate that required as little of them as was bearable.
You got a woman? the bearded one asked at last.
I do, the dreamer said, and he wondered suddenly whether the bearded one had known all along, whether the hand on his shoulder in the square had been the place of a claim.
We can take her, the bearded one said, and it was as easy a thing for him to say as to breathe. Taking, after all, is a trade most men are taught early by the world, and in this time the world did not have the patience of old etiquette. The two younger men laughed and the laugh was like bone.
The dreamer felt something inside him go cold and he put a hand to the small of his back as if to find a place to brace. He will not take her, he said then. He had the lit cigarette between his lips and the smoke drew long in the morning air and made a thin halo about his head. You don't want to do that, he said to the bearded one.
The bearded one smiled and reached out with a sudden slow movement and took the cigarette from the dreamer's mouth and tapped it out on the log as if to say no more. You got a child comin'? he asked, and the question was a spear in the quiet.
She will suffer if we stay, the dreamer said. There's talk of the line. There's work. We'll go.
The bearded one shifted and the two younger leaned forward like wolves about meat. They told him about the line in the low voice they reserve for those whose ears are clay for the world. The line, he said, is a place where men move goods from one narrow place to another and in doing so become the law of that strip. They load, they unload, they take what they must, they carry the rest to the market. Workers there are cheap and men are plentiful and those who take in the dark have fewer rival claims, he said. You come with us. He spread his hand as if to make the offer complete and the river moved on without an answer.
The dreamer watched his woman across the bank where she sat with the dog at her feet and the light leaning like a hand on her face, and he saw the smallness of the world between them and the bearded men. He heard the echo of his dream in the beating of his own heart and he had the sudden, fierce urge to snatch up his pack and run and to never let them find him or her. He thought of the child's small hands, of the smell of newborn milk, of the way the woman's hair fell about her ears, and in that small geometry of tenderness his resistance hardened into a thing like resolve.
I go with you, he said to the bearded one.
So be it, the bearded one said, as if that had been the plan all along. They spent the morning in the half light trading and talking as if preferment were inevitable; the bearded one proffered a bowl of stew in the name of goodwill and the younger men shared their cigarettes, and all the while the river licked at the bank like a sly animal.
The work, when it came, was a slow, steady unmaking. They mapped the roads, they learned the hours, they were where the lines of men passed and the river was the seam between the badlands and the places that tried to remember themselves. They carried sacks and loads between points on the line and returned with coins and sometimes with spoils that had been left by careless hands. The bearded one was patient in the matter of dividing the harvest and the dreamer found himself doing what was necessary and thinking of the woman and of the child in his mind like a candle kept nursed.
It was not all bad; there were days when the coins in his pocket had real weight and nights when the fire ate cake that was meant and the bearded one would hum as he worked, and at times the younger men spoke of the days ahead as if they were a promise. But the measure of any road of men is its appetite and the bearded one's appetite was a constant thing. What he took perhaps it was not that he took as much as that he would take first and most often. In time the dreamer learned to put his coin in hidden holes and to sleep with an eye open and to move his small savings like a squirrel with a secret stash.
One day he came upon the prophet's square again and this time there were new faces at the old posts. The town had been a place of slow ruination but now it had the look of a city that had been scraped of its old skin and had something raw and shiny beneath. They had rebuilt the pulpit with planks and there was a man on it now, a smaller man with a thin mouth and a voice that pitched like a thread, and he spoke of the sun and of the new hours. The crowd about him was feverish and in their hands were the tokens of small struggling lives: a pot, a child's shoe, a scrap of linen. They listened with that hush of the convinced and the weary.
The dreamer stood at the edge of the crowd and he felt the old dread close upon him. The man's voice was wood and he said He will return and He will take back what was lost and there was the sickening contrivance of hope and need that runs in the blood of such times. The dreamer tried to slip past but a hand on his arm held him, a small scrim of leather and callus. He turned and saw the middle-aged woman at the cart who had been dying by the road the first day he had left town, and in her eyes was the small accusation which the poor and the broken make upon those who would move on.
You're with them, she said, and it was a flat thing, an accusation not of law but of the heart. He felt the bearded ones' presence behind him like a shadow and he would have answered her and said I am only trying to keep them safe, I am doing what I can, but the words did not come out save as a small sound of wind and the woman at the cart turned away like a puppet with its string snipped.
The little prophet stopped speaking for a moment and the hush grew like the skinned throat of a bell. He looked up and the crowd surged forward as if at a signal, and the dreamer felt a sharp ache of the same mistrust that had been his dream. Men in the crowd made little gestures and the bearded one moved like a man whose right to be was not to be questioned. In a corner of the square a group of men setting up a stand looked up and saw their wares arrayed by the road as if in a harvest and did little at first, and then with a suddenness that had the feeling of predation they moved out and fell upon those whose wares were easy to take. There was a brawl and a scuffle and then the sudden collapse of a life in the making.
The dreamer tried to hold them back, tried to make overtures of reason, and reason is a poor weapon when men move like beasts. The bearded one said quietly, Take what you need. The younger men smiled at the ease of their manners. Nearby a woman who reminded the dreamer of his own wife had her sack split open and all the things within it lay on the ground like a little surety of life, and men with hands like harvesters collapsed them into sacks. She threw herself upon them and screamed and the dreamer tried to pull her back, but hands came to his chest and gripped like grasping roots and the world narrowed to the hard beat of throbbing blood and the taste of iron.
In the wake of the taking there was the rush of those who had been robbed into the night air and their cries followed them like a wind of small, exact griefs. The little prophet with his thread-thin voice yelled of order and law as if calling a child, and the town authorities - what few there were - looked on without the courage of their duty and the bearded ones opened their hands to trade and to take. It was all the world could do.
That night the dreamer walked the roads out of town and put his head down into his hands and wept for the smallness of his courage and for the hardness that had invaded him like rust. He found himself thinking of the prophet in his square and of the last thin wire of sun and how it buckled and dropped and did not come back, and he could not separate the man in the square from the man who had sold him the promise of better things. Promises have heavy weights and men who trade in them are often the worst dealers in the market.
He returned at dawn to the river where the bearded one had his camp and the two younger men slept with their faces to the sky and the dog whined at his boots. The woman was awake by the fire and her face was drawn into a fine map of worry. You should've come sooner, she said. You should've let me go on my own. There was sorrow like the present thing between them and he saw in it the shape of the choices that make a life grow thin.
I did what I could, he said.
Somewhere along that road there was a man who had been a prophet and another who would be a thief and there were always the blurred passages where one could look at another's face and not be certain of what lay behind the skin. The dreamer had thought in his sleep that he might stand in the square and call for mercy, or for explanation, or for a reckoning, but he had woken to find the world had no use at all for such longings.
In the days that followed the bearded one grew bolder and the trio's reach widened like a stain. They took from the smallfolk who travelled the lines and from the ones in the towns who had little defense. At times the dreamer found himself complicit in the taking and his stomach would knot as if to deny the food therein. He thought often of his child and of the bent face of the woman in the square and of the little prophet and all the images seemed to run together until his dreams were of a sun that lay full in the palm of a child and would not rise again.
At last the woman could not bear it any longer and one morning under the flaying light she rose and gathered what they could carry and said, I can't have him in this world. She watched him as if to weigh him and her eyes were the color of riverglass gone to a dark mood. There was an argument and a brief violent thing between them, more words than blows but still gray and awful, and in the end the dreamer surrendered to what he could and could not be. He kissed the curve of the woman's belly then and said I will find another way, and in the small motion the woman let him go for the last time.
The bearded one's men left in a long sheet of cloud of dust one evening with the excuse of work on the line and the dreamer watched them go with the feeling of one who has watched a cart take the only thing they'd been given. He stood by the fence and a little boy of eight walked by with a mouthful of stolen apples and looked at him and shrugged, the bright indifferent arrogance of the youth who had not yet been taught to fear consequence and who would in time be taught by the state of things.
Days became weeks as he walked and the landscape opened like a wound and closed again. He drifted from one town to another and always the sun had the same distant edge as if the world had been put behind a pane of glass and observed as a curiosity. The people he encountered had faces that had been pared to the bone of daily survival; some of them greeted him and some passed like shadows and some looked at him with hunger like seabirds.
He found on the river a place that men called a ferry, a crude flat raft that was moved by pulling a rope that ran along a series of towers. The man who ran the ferry was old and had at his side the little prophet's daughter who had the thin mouth of her father and the same determined lack of play. The ferry moved by muscle and by the patience of men who had not yet come to telling one another the truth of the world. The dreamer banded with them for a while and in exchange he gambled and even smoked the small leaves they kept for barter. In the twilight they told stories of the sea and the godlike things beyond and he let the comfort of such fictions hold him like a blanket.
But the bearded one has a long arm and a man who has learned to take does not cough his appetite away. One evening they came to the ferry when the light was a dull burn and the river looked like a slab of iron and there was the staccato sound of men moving in the reeds before he could see them. The bearded one had learned the ferry's timetable and he had with him the two younger men and a pack of other things and men who had the same look as his two. They came with a kind of officiousness and it was not long before the air was full of the scent of old terror.
You know your place, the bearded one said to the ferry man, who was old enough to have known many things and who had the slow tiredness of the unresisting. He offered payment and threats in the same shorthand and the man at the raft watched as if the world had some obligation still to the small dignity of barter. The dreamer tried to speak and his voice was the sound of a small bell being struck in a cellar. Please, he said. But the bearded one laughed and the laugh was a thing like a vane blown long in a storm and it had no warmth. They tied the ferry with a rope and took a handful of men and their packs and with it took supplies and once again the argument in his chest that had been his attempt at courage fell away like a dead thing from a ledge.
There are times, the dreamer thought, when a man must cut his losses and count what is left and when the counting becomes an enumeration of small cruelties you have been forced to incur, such a man learns to pare himself down to essentials. He felt at last the fierce necessity of escape, not from the bearded ones - for they were always near - but from the compounding of himself into something less. He took his pack in the night and left the ferry as it lay slack with men who slept and the dog who guarded the hold and the little prophet's daughter who had a fine voice used for singing laments at weddings and funerals. He did not tell the woman with the belly that he was leaving them; he would not burden her with the knowledge that he could not keep them safe. He moved like a thief in the dark, and the night swallowed him up as if to obey a command.
He went west and west and the road gave him up to its various moods. He slept in the crevice of a ruined wall, in a culvert with the sound of water like a sleeping child, under a bridge where the rats had marked their territory with a thousand little maps. He scavenged and sold and sometimes he worked on the line in the company of men who would take what they were given without gesture, and he lived in the thin patience of the man who waits for the next turn.
Time passed. Seasons unspooled. The dream came less often, or perhaps it came but he did not remember it, or perhaps there was within him a numbness that could no longer be pricked by the arrows of memory, and he came to live with a small belief that the world's center was a place that had been picked clean and that everything was an echo of its former fullness.
Then one night while the moon was young and the stars a clean and raw embroidery he came upon a camp by the river where men and women had gathered in a hurried congregation and there in the center of the firelight a group of people knelt with faces upcast and there was a man on stilts with a painted face who shouted into the dark about the return of the sun and the promptings of new men. The air about the man had that effusion of excitement that belongs always to those who trade in hope and to those who will live from noise. The dreamer watched and felt a strange and familiar cold move like a snake along the back of his neck.
He stood for a long time in the shadow and then someone at the camp recognized him and called his name and he felt a little pinprick of surprise at being thus known. They made room for him by the fire and gave him of the stew and the tobacco and a kind of acceptance that was wary and small and he accepted for the sake of warmth. Those who were there had come from various places and had all the alliances and the vacancies of people who had learned to sleep with one eye open.
The next day, in the bright hour when the river runs like an explanation, came from the path a figure who was unmistakable even at a distance: the bearded one, and with him the two younger men and a band of others who had in the smallness of their ranks the petty arrogance of those who have learned to rule in the shadow of ruins. They walked with the slow confidence of men who thought themselves important and they had that smell of smoke and meat and the polished brass of the coin on a man's fingers that marks his class. The dreamer felt his stomach tighten with something he had not felt for a long time, that old human fear that shrinks the flesh and makes the hands small as if they had been carved.
The bearded one saw him and the smile on his face was the same with which he had offered camaraderie and theft. He came toward the fire and the people at the camp rose with a sudden alertness like a flock. He greeted the men with his hand like a lord and asked where the dreamer had been living these days and the dreamer felt in that instant a rage and a pity like a sick thing. There was the old man from the ferry who had recognized him before and he watched the two with the slow judgment of those who have been cheated and found nothing to remedy it.
We got a proposition, the bearded one said, meaning several things at once, and he looked out over them with the calculating stare of a miser. He said he had taken the road down to the old town and found men who would pay handsomely for labour to move goods across the marsh and he offered to them a share if they would join his little band for a stretch, or longer if the work proved fair. There was a small shiver of interest in the circle and the vision of coin makes sticky hands among the poor.
The dreamer listened and his mouth tasted of dust and iron. He remembered back to the woman with the belly and to the child whose breath he had not heard since he left them. He thought of what he had done and how little it had bought him and the fruitlessness of bargains made in the dark. He felt, in the inner place of himself, a seed of rebellion and he stood up.
What if you were to leave us be? he said and his voice was the thin riverbone of a man suddenly daring to name a thought aloud. You goin' to take from folks who got no other place to go.
The bearded one laughed with that small sound like a knife. We takes what we can, he said. The world gives and the world takes. He made the motion of his hand as if to encompass the whole disc of the sky and the river and the pale houses that leaned against the water. The dreamer saw in that movement the cruelty of a man who rationalized.
You act like a thief and you call it law, the dreamer said, and in his chest the old heat of the prophet's square rose and with it the memory of the dark that had never returned. Around them others murmured and took sides and the night air felt sharp like a blade.
The bearded one stepped forward and his voice was sudden and filled with the authority of time. You think you own your life? he asked. He said the sentence as if it were an unstringing of a rope. The dreamer felt the world in that instant on the edge of a very small volcano. There was shouting and the two younger men moved in and pried the dreamer's arms back and the bearded one laughed and raised his hand and the sound of the clap echoed off the river and the night.
You gonna tell us where the woman is, the bearded one said, and the crowd about them hushed like an animal at scent. The dreamer thought then of the woman's face as she had left him, of that small courage and the way she had borne the child inside her like a second world, and for a long moment he said nothing because there was nothing in the mouth that could do the work he needed to do. He had thought in his head that he would never let them take her, never allow that theft, but the future is a fine glass you peep through and fancy you can hold. He had not thought of how to trade his fear in the present.
I don't know, he said at last, and the words made him feel like a traitor and a coward at once. The bearded one saw the weakness and used it as one uses a wedge. Maybe you should tell us where she is and maybe we'll let you keep your neck, he said.
The young men laughed and the crowd laughed and for a moment the dreamer thought of the prophet and of the last thin line of sun and of the way the world could break a man into such small pieces, and then he did something that surprised even himself: he drew forward like a man who had decided to stand in a place where he could be seen and he struck the bearded one across the jaw with the flat of his hand. The blow was not clean; it was a clumsy thing given with desperation. The bearded one's head rolled and for an instant the night had the taste of copper on it. Then the two younger men lunged and the dreamer felt a wild animal's hand at his hair and then his ribs and everything closed in like the mouth of a trap.
There was a scuffle, a falling, the sound of men cursing, and then guns were out and the light of them made rectangles on the dark. A shot rang out and it was a small thing that changed the measure of the night: a bird whirred up from the reeds and the sound startled the dog which barked and in the commotion the bearded one staggered and the younger men went down like puppets, and the dreamer crawled backward clutching at his side and the taste of iron again filled his mouth. The bearded one's eyes opened wide and there was pain and surprise and the light of his blood on his lip. You fool, he said and the words came like the last swell of a tide.
When the shots had ceased and the first men had fled and the others lay quiet and the ferry men had come with their small tools to bind wounds or to find the dead the dreamer looked about him to see the cost and the measure. There were two men who would not be moving any more and the bearded one lay with his head a little to one side like a broken fruit. The night had the smell of wet fur and the sharp metal of fear and the river moved as it always had, indifferent and terrible.
People gathered and the little prophet's daughter sang a small dirge and the old ferryman cursed softly and the dreamer felt the weight of what had occurred pile upon his shoulders like wet cloth. He had meant to protect and he had instead placed a hand in the maw of a larger thing. He had thought, in some selfish corner, that by fighting he would soon be able to go to the woman with his head unbowed. He had not counted the numbers and the wrath of men nor the long reach of the puppets who followed a smell of advantage as an animal follows a hot trail.
He watched the bearded one's men carried off what their leader had claimed, and the crowd admonished those who had struck them as if justice were something one enforced with a mutual shrug and not by reasoned law. In the morning the dreamer left the camp with a small sack and what little coin he had left and went to the road that led west, taking care with each step to not look back.
Months later the world handed him a scrap of news like a stone through a window. He heard in a market town that the bearded one had not died on the river as he had thought but had been carried off in a cart and had been seen in a town three days' march to the west, resplendent as ever and with one eye open to the cries of the market. He heard that the bearded one's men had been hunted down and put on trial and had been made to return what they had taken or to pay for it, but such things are often the concessions of a place that hopes to make a memory of itself. He heard his wife's name whispered in a tavern and the name curled like smoke and then was gone.
At the end of his long walking the dreamer came to a place by the sea where men had made a life of breaking and remaking the world on a small scale. He took work as a longshoreman and in long seasons hauled in nets and in short ones stacked crates of salted fish and dried beans. The winds off the ocean were like a new theology; they stripped away the old designs and left men naked to their own small decencies. Here he could rest for a time in the smell of fish and salt and the cadence of the sea.
He kept in his jacket a small sliver of carved wood; it had been the bearded one's, he thought, or the ferry man's, or perhaps an idolatrous thing from the prophet's first pulpit, and he turned it in his hand when the nights were long and the sea was loud. The wood warmed in his palm and in the warming he made of it a small anchor to the memory.
Sometimes, when the wind took a certain angle and the sirens in the port had gone to their low notes and the crates had been stacked like small altars and the men who ate at the table shared their bread without mention of the past, he would dream a dream of the sun again, of the prophet standing in the square with his arms uplifted and of the long thin wire of sun buckling and the black coming down like a shout, and he would wake with the taste of ash at the back of his throat. It would be, he sometimes thought, as if the world would have him address that which had failed and ask of it an accounting.
One evening as the sun rode low and the smell of fried fish was like a blessing in the lanes, a woman came to the docks with a child in her arms and he saw her and in the tilt of her jaw and the set of her shoulders something familiar startled him so that he almost dropped the crate he was carrying. She had been changed by her ordeals; there were lines of sleep in her face and a little silver at her temple and the child in her arms had a small look of a man who has nowhere to put his fear. Her eyes swept the pier and then they rested on him and in them there was a bewilderment that would have been mistaken for recognition by any other man.
He set the crate down and walked to her with the slow, sure steps of a man who has given himself away to the sea. You, he said then, and the word was small and foolish and full of many things.
She looked at him with the old hard patience and then gave a short laugh that had no mirth in it. I thought you were someone else, she said, and the child on her hip gave a soft sound like a gull. What happened to you? she asked.
He told her little things: that he had worked on the line and that he had defended a ferry and that the bearded one had been more dangerous than a man of mere conscience. He did not tell her of the long rotting in his soul or of the nights when the dream returned to gnaw at the edges of him. When at last she spoke she said, I had you in mind and in the same breath you had to let yourself go for me to be able to hold the child and to move. She did not ask him to come back and he did not ask to be forgiven. They were two people who had made the old bargain and had decided by degrees to break it; there was in that a mutual kindness and a darker thing too, a knowledge that the world had no place for tenderness when the wolves were about.
In the days that followed they spoke in small increments and the child—no more than a thing of fingers and sour milk—grew like a stupid plant, fed and turned by the small labours of his mother. He watched them from the side of the dock and learned his place again in the picture of what little family he could yet make and the sea, generous in its own indifference, gave them mornings of fish and men who would pay their wages.
At last, on a day when the light was thin and the gulls had a new urgency in their cries, the bearded one came down the pier with his new gang about him and the scent of old bad weather clung to his coat. He had lost the look of his pristine cunning and had acquired instead the kin of a man who had made many little compromises. When he saw the dreamer he laughed in a way that had the residue of old rancor. He had a new calculation in his face and he spoke of roads and of deals and of the sort of arrangement in which neither party truly wins. He had a rifle across his shoulder and a ring of men about him and he spoke of his plans with the calm of a man whose appetite had been sharpened by hunger.
The dreamer stood before him on the dock with the muscles of his jaw tight as if to keep the bitterness from uncoiling. He had learned to carry a new smallness inside him: a courage couched in fear and a refusal to run this time. There was room along the quay and the sea spread out beyond in a black skin and the gulls wheeled, and he thought of the prophet and of the sun that had failed to return and of all the ways in which promise had been made and broken.
What do you want now? he asked the bearded one.
The bearded one smiled and it was a thing like an old wound; he wanted the harbour-master's toll and the right to run a stall in the market and to extend his reach a little further. He wanted the little human things that solidify a man in place when the world is loose. He offered to pay him a fair wage if he would come and work under his hand. He would be a foreman, he said, to keep the dock orderly and to settle disputes as they arose. The dreamer could have accepted and slipped into a way of life that would have been a kind of quiet death and one that would keep the woman and the child safe by the ledger of small coin. He thought of the nights that had eroded him and the little girl who sang lullabies of old men and the old ferryman who had a kind of saintliness in his face and he saw the world as a thin ledger and the columns all bent in different ways.
He looked at the bearded one then as if to measure what a man can become in the time forced upon him and he found that within himself there was still the capacity to resist being absorbed by the greed of others. No, he said. No I won't work for you.
The bearded one was surprised by the firmness and for a moment his mouth had the look of a man who has been halted unexpectedly. He could not countenance being told no as if the refusal were an insolence. He said then, in a voice tempered now, that the world was a place of deals and the dreamer had refused to accept a deal that might have made his nights easier and his bellies lined, and the bearded one left with words that he stacked like stones, not threats for the most part but warnings that said plainly what a man was owed for the kindness of others.
The bearded one left and the tide came in and the gulls were noisy and the sea took rocks and bones and a thousand things that humans thought permanent and wore them in a slow change as if to teach a lesson. The dreamer stood a long while on the pier and watched the foam and the currents and the things they carried off. The woman came to him and took his hand and the child held on to the hem of her dress. They did not speak and the silence between them was a hundred small treaties.
At night in the room above the stalls, when the rain came and the sound of it was like a slow falling of the world's patience, the dreamer lay awake and the dream returned but with a new peculiar fold to it. The man in the square with the arms upraised now had no words because all the words had been traded and bartered and lost in the long passing of men. The sun hung as it had before but there was a thinness to the dark upon it as if the world had thinned and pressed. The dreamer thought that perhaps dreams do not only repeat to punish but repeat in hopes we might learn from the echoes, and he thought too that maybe it is not the sun that is the problem but the men who make of its failure the reason for their taking. He had seen it once too often.
In the mornings he went down to the quay and worked the net and the fish, and in the evenings he lit a small lamp and mended sails and ached with a new clarity of what it meant to choose. The bearded one was a man of the port as all those men are who do not know how to rest and the talk of him never entirely left the market. The little prophet died at last of a fever and the people held a small funeral for him with more sorrow than praise and the dreamer came to it and stood at the edge of the graves and remembered the square and the day the sun buckled and blacked and the crowd had fallen on him in the dark.
There are men who prefer to live by the taking of others and men who prefer not to and sometimes the world gives them both their due. The dreamer did not know whether he had atoned for his small complicities and he had not been given a sign that such things could be washed away, but each morning when he woke and heard the gulls and the sea he felt, like salt on his skin, the consequence of the choices he had made and the faces of those he'd left behind and the whisper of the woman's hand on his shoulder. The child would grown to be a man whom he would teach what he could, and maybe that would be enough.
And in the change of seasons and in the color of the light off the water, the dream returned less violent and more a visitation, like a bell you hear in the distance and bend to listen to. He would wake and sometimes not be sure where the night had taken him and in that unmooring there was both terror and a strange consolation that the world, even in its most unseemly hour, still left room for the stubborn small mercies of men.
