By noon they had started to carry my childhood out the front door. Not all at once, no. It happened like a slow, polite robbery: one thing at a time, eyes polite, gloves on, mouths dry. Two men in a beige truck—one with a lopsided cap and one with a tattoo that looked like someone’s idea of a map—took the sofa, folded it like a tired animal, eased it down the porch and into the world where people who did not know its weight would buy it with the same smallness they bought anything secondhand. A woman with a clipboard moved among us like a small, efficient wind. “What’s your name?” she asked. “Do you want to keep anything?” She asked as if the questions were a recipe and the answers would be measured and poured.

My hands were empty. They had been empty for a long time. They fit into their sleeves as if they belonged to someone else. I was the one supposed to answer questions. I was supposed to make decisions about what stayed and what went, to sign the forms and nod at prices like you nod at thunder—because things needed agreeing to, even violent things; laws had to be obeyed; paper got signed. I told the woman my name. I told her which things were to remain until my sister came, which things could go now. She wrote in a neat black script, like someone drawing the edges off a sound.

They took the chair with the floral fabric first. The one that smelled of lemon oil and a hundred winters’ worth of knitting. The one my mother had always sat in when the weather turned right and the radio sang low in the evening. When they lifted it, the springs sighed, and I saw—because seeing works that way when memory is in a room—you never just see the outside of things—you see the hands that have held them, the knees that have given them shape. The man who carried it grunted and set it in the back of the truck like a thing that had been promised payment. The fabric made a sound like a little prayer released into the open air.

People kept coming through the yard: a woman with pink nail polish who fished through the back of a moving van like a squirrel; a boy in a hoodie who did not know how to fold a blanket and made the blanket look petulant; a man who said he loved antiques but was rough with wood. They called each other by names I hadn’t heard in years—“Jules,” “Mike,” “Rosie”—as if brothers of a trade I did not understand. They did not know how to keep their hands to themselves. Their fingers learned the faces of the furniture quickly, finding the scars, the nicked corners, the spots where milk had dried, the groove on the table where someone—most likely me—had traced a map of somewhere warm. The groove was a river and had been for a long time, and each of them ran fingertips across it like they were making a wish.

I stood on the porch steps and watched the doorway cough up the rooms one by one. Inside, the house had been patient. It had kept our lives like a kind of fruit in a jar, a preserve that would outlast seasons. My mother liked things that lasted. That was her religion—picking something up, smoothing it until it looked like a promise. She said in soft circular ways that what you put your hands on becomes part of you. That chairs learn the shape of hips; that beds remember the contour of a hipbone; that spoons hold human mouths with a tenderness not given to knives. She said this and meant it until the day the bite of whatever it was that had made her small and large took her from her own house and left us with an estate that smelled like last night’s coffee.

People kept asking me, “Did she want this?” as if my mother had walked down a list of her things and given consent. She did not, I said. She did not because she was not here to say yes or no. But she had said things in her time—small dicta that were law in the house and looser in the world. Don’t sit with wet hair. Don’t leave bread open on the counter. Never let a man bring his troubles inside and set them at your table. Don’t let anything in that will come back. She had said those things so often that my breath learned them too. They fell out of me now like loose change.

My sister came late. Memory told me she would. Memory likes to be right. Memory says the late ones come with reasons, and our reasons are always long and tangled like a necklace of things: a flat tire, a child’s knee, a phone that would not ring because it was smart and proud and imagined itself better than us. When she did arrive, she was smaller inside the joints than I remembered, or perhaps I had swelled. She hugged me like a person returning with contraband. “They’re taking everything,” she said, the words leaving her like small animals that she hoped would find a hole in the earth and hide. Her voice had the same stitchless quality as my mother’s when she was angry—quiet, and therefore more exacting. She had brought a thermos and two plastic cups. We sat on the back steps like two women on a porch of a long day and drank coffee that tasted of the road.

We argued a little at first because that is what kin does when the house has been emptied of its owner. We argued about what was worth taking, what was not, about whether we should have moved her into our lives earlier, about whether the house should have held her until the end. The words slid over one another like small children in a narrow doorway. I said, “Do you remember the red lamp?” as if a lamp’s color could be something shared and sacred. She nodded. My sister’s nostrils flared in the precise way they had when we were both children and wanted to get up to something that might cause trouble. “Ma wouldn’t let me touch it,” she said. “She said it belonged to ‘before.’” She put the word between her teeth like a stone.

‘Before’ in our family was a place that nobody went without a jacket. It was a place the mother spoke of with a mouth full of salt. Before was the town—somewhere else—where the world had taught her things she did not want taught us. Before had eyes. Before remembered names she had rather forget. She kept a lamp from before. It smelled faintly of kerosene in the way old stories smell of the first time someone cried in the night. I thought about the lamp now as if thinking about it could summon the woman it came from. The woman who had born us and not been satisfied with the measure of the world. The woman who had love inside her like an animal and made it sit and look at her.

They took that lamp later, because the woman with the clipboard noted it on the list and someone wanted old things that came from before. They wrapped its lampshade in brown paper and the paper made a soft sound like leaves. I watched the man carry it out, and I fancied I saw the light under the paper. Of course there was no light; light does not sit in paper anymore. The man put it on a dolly like he was loading a coffin and pushed it into the truck with the others. He raised his hand to his mouth, coughed, and I thought he was saying a blessing. Perhaps he was. Blessings are cheap at estate sales.

There was a ledger somewhere—it had belonged to my father, a ledger with ledger hands, heavy, the kind of book that could anchor a ship. My father had died before I had reason to explain him fully. He had been a man who kept accounts not only of money but of hurts; he liked to count them as if counting would put them in order. His ledger was the house’s map of missteps. My sister found it on a high shelf, wrapped in a dish towel like a sleeping animal. She opened it and leafed through columns of numbers and in the margins, in his cramped, hunted shorthand, little notes about names and times. Once she read a line out loud, and it was like discovering a tooth that had been hidden—a neat, ugly secret. “He wrote who came for bread,” she said. “He wrote who left with it.”

We should have brought the ledger back with us, but we did not. Because an estate sale is run on a timetable and on a business sense that does not include ghosts and because the woman with the clipboard said, “Those ledgers are worth money to collectors.” She said it like a verdict. “We can sell them as a lot.” People who deal in lots do not make the distinction between a lot and a life. My sister hesitated and then nodded and handed it over with a sort of shaking politeness. She put the ledger into a box labeled BOOKS and the box went out with a man named Jules who said he liked “old things” and who smelled like melted sugar. I watched him carry it away like a nurse with a chart.

The house held on in other ways. The wallpaper peeled at the corners with all the dignity of something that had known too much. The kitchen faucet had a small place where it leaked, and the water beneath it had made a stain like a letter. The porch swing, when they took it, swung once as if surprised to be free and then settled like a tired soul. The piano—oh, the piano. The piano had a mouth. People who grew up with music in a house know that a piano is not simply wood and string. It is a throat that has swallowed the family’s harmonies and coughs them back up in scraps. My mother’s fingers had not been hers; they had been the piano’s fingers. Later, when I listened to the men lift it—because moving a piano is like moving a body: it takes extra hands—the music that had been settling inside seemed to come with them for a little while and then stopped abruptly at the edge of the yard, as if the instrument could sense that it was no longer breathing the correct air.

There were things I thought I would take. There were things I assumed I would not. There were things I had combed my mind for since the funeral and the hospital rooms and the small paper bag that had held her pills. I had a list, not of objects but of moments. I wanted the moments. But moments, like mice, are not easy to capture. You can watch the place where they were and stand there all afternoon and find yourself holding a pale, exacting absence.

A boy—maybe nineteen—took the icebox. He hefted it as if it might have been his mother’s, or else because he liked the heft of things. He whistled while he worked and he whistled a tune that had no home for the strangled words of our house. When he opened the icebox to put in his tools, a smell rose up like a memory of meat and time. For a second, it was as if somebody had reopened a drawer and the house inhaled. I wanted to tell him about the jams and the preserves. I wanted to tell him where the labels had been forty-five years ago and who’s syrup had leaked on which shelf. I wanted to put my hand on his wrist and stop him. But his hands were quick. He shrugged, closed the door, and the icebox looked smaller, as if the house had been cropped.

People asked, “Did she leave a will?” as if a will could be a map. She left us a list of things she liked and what she wanted us to do, which in truth is all a kind of will. She wanted the piano for the community center. She wanted the quilts sewn up in the closet to be given to anyone who’d keep them warm. She wanted us to have the brooch, her mother’s brooch that she wore for funerals and for nothing else. She wanted us to remember to say grace and to forgive. All of it—that want—had to be translated into dollars. The audition of estate sale is always money first, sentiment in the back rows. The woman with the clipboard told me, “If you want something, say so. Tell us now. Otherwise, it’ll be gone.” She asked me the question like a gun.

There was a mirror over the mantle that I loved because it reflected things without lying. It did not flatter. It told you your face properly. In it, I sometimes saw my mother’s stern mouth and knew that if she had lived longer she would have made me sit and measure my own life with it. The mirror had a crack down one side where a kid—maybe me—had thrown a ball and hit it hard in some clear summer that had no business being happy. The crack caught the light and broke the room into three small rooms. When they carried it out, they paused, and someone said, “You could sell that for wall art.” It was crass in the way speech can be when it sharpens into commerce. I wanted to say, “It tells truth,” but the words seemed too much like pleading.

There was a name that kept arriving in my mind like a visiting aunt. Her name was Mrs. Hargrove. She had been a neighbor, a woman who had the habit of lending sugar and not returning it until it had ripened into friendship. She had, in our youth, left a mark on our lives by saying things at the wrong times with the right voice. I remembered her coming in one winter and sitting in my mother’s chair and knitting something so fast it looked like a blur. She said to my mother, “You keep house like a woman in a photograph; cold and made up.” My mother had looked up and said, “Better that than the other way.” Mrs. Hargrove had nodded, and for a while there was silence that smelled like tea. I thought of her now because in the business of taking and leaving and letting, Mrs. Hargrove had always been a kind of mirror—someone who would tell you where you were and not sugar the news.

It would have been easier, perhaps, if strangers had been gentler. But they had to be efficient, the way people who eat with hungry mouths are efficient. They took the set of china, the good set that had been used once for my wedding and once more, when my sister cried at the table and the plates got wet with gravy and we pretended the tears were for the meal. They packed the china into hard boxes and placed a sticker upon it: FRAGILE. The word looked comical, like someone scolding a thing without knowing its history. A man with a handkerchief tied around his neck (as if the job could be swaddled) loaded the boxes into his van. He smiled at me like a boy who found treasure. “Vintage china sells,” he said. “People want pieces with story.” He meant decorations that would sit quietly and look interesting when guests arrived. He had nothing to say about the story inside the glaze.

Between the taking of things and the listing on a clipboard, time did something odd. It turned itself inside out and showed its flayed side. Moments from childhood started walking about the room in small groups, sitting where the table had been or standing like ghosts at the foot of the bed. My brothers who had died young—one from a fever, one from a mistake—came up for breath and stood where their small jackets hung. The air filled with small sounds: a radio that would never again be tuned, a dog’s chain scraping the porch in the middle of a summer night, the lid of the milk can hitting steel. I could have pressed my hand to the wood and called them back. You can call, but sometimes calls answer someone else.

Neighbors arrived too. They came slowly, like rain, some to see, some to gossip, some to take chances on what they thought belonged to them by old right. Mrs. Calloway, who had watched my mother bend her back in the garden for decades and who had kept a ledger of her own—one in which she recorded who borrowed what and who repaid with what’s-for-dinner—stood by the mailbox and said, “You look good.” It was a small mercy. It was also a wedge. People speak with the smallness that divides houses; compliments, like sugar, are currency here.

Money changed hands. A table that had been my father’s—the table where he used to file and then forget things; the table that sometimes he would slap his palm upon and call the dog useless—sold for a hundred and eighty dollars. A quilt that my mother had stitched in long nights for people she loved sold for forty. The men who carried things out were paid with cash in envelopes that smelled like the desk where the money had lived. They were pleased with the measure of it. I tried to be pleased with the measure, too, as if the math could be reconciled to a life lived. But arithmetic is only polite until it asks for a heart.

Once there was a pause and no one moved and the day seemed to fold in like a hand closing on a small bird. It must have been the heat; it must have been that people felt history as a pressure. The furniture in the yard looked like a small town after some calamity—chairs forming a council, the piano looking like a chest of drawers, the bedpost like a lonely flag. In that pause, a boy who had been with the woman with the clipboard came up to me with a small wooden box. He held it like a man who had found a relic in a field. “This fell out,” he said. “Under the floorboards.” He had a certain humility about the way he spoke, as if he had disturbed a prayer.

The box was not more than the size of a soap bar. It was plain, the wood watermarked and soft under my fingers. There were initials on top—M.A.—and a nail head had been hammered in crooked. I set my thumb against the lid and felt as if I had always known the nail there. We stood in the yard in the thin conversation of people who are not kin, who meet by courtesy and leave by obligation. “We can put it with your things,” the woman with the clipboard said, eyes calculating whether sentiment might be a value. “We don’t want to lose things under the floor.” She spoke as if floors are lives—flat, level, with no secrets. But floors do hide things. Floors are porous in the right ways. I thought about how my mother had hidden pieces of her life—photographs folded tight, letters written on a night of some startling discovery—under the floor in places she believed only roaches knew. She liked roaches. She trusted them to keep her unspoken matters.

I opened the box like a person setting aprons aside. Inside was a small bundle of hair tied with a ribbon that had once been red and was now the color of dried roses. There was a single thimble and a photograph so small the faces in it looked like seeds. The ribbon smelled faintly of lavender and sweat. It smelled like a waiting room. My mouth made a sound that had no language.

I remembered tearing hair as a child, absentmindedly, twisting it around my finger and thinking of ships. My mother kept hair. She said it was a thing people kept because it is evidence of having been held. She kept teeth, too, in a drawer that clinked. This was another thing she had held and hidden and then left. She had put the bundle under the floor as if it were a seed she did not yet want to see sprout.

“Where did this come from?” my sister asked, vowels cut like pieces of bread.

“Our ma,” I said. “She kept it. She put things away.” I thought of the ribbon and what it had tied in life. Perhaps it was hair from a child who had no years. Perhaps it was from some moment that had kept my mother awake. The photograph made my stomach ache. There were three people in it. A man whose jaw looked like a plow, a woman with a businesslike smile, and a child with an uncertain chin. It might have been my parents; it might have been strangers. Faces in small pictures look like propositions. You can make them into what you need.

“Put it with the rest,” someone said, but I did not hear the voice. I was listening to the sound the wood made when the lid opened and closed. I put the box into my pocket, where it sat hot and small as a coal.

Later, while they took the sofa and the piano and the red lamp and the ledger and the china, I felt the weight of the box like an offering. It was too small for much, but it fit perfectly inside the palm of my shame. Shame is a small thing; it likes pockets and quiet. I thought—because memory does these things—that my mother had perhaps meant for me to find it when I had grown tired of her hiding. Or perhaps she meant for it never to be found. People sometimes leave things merely so that their absence smokes with meaning.

There was, while the afternoon decanted furniture into trucks and vans, an old friend who came by. His name was Henry and when he appeared the air changed as if someone had turned on an old radio and the static steadied into a song. Henry and my mother had been friends the way two trees are friends when their roots share something underground. He did not have much to say about the yard, except that it looked “lighter,” which made me think he was wrong and right both. He told a story about a summer when my mother had let him sleep on the couch and he had eaten beans that exploded with such noise that the house thought someone had hollered. He laughed. He did not laugh because the house had emptied. He laughed because to laugh was a kind of keeping.

“Don’t throw out the kitchen clock,” he said as if reciting law. “It’s got the heartbeat.” He winked and his eye folded like someone smoking a small pleasure. My mother loved timepieces. She liked clocks because they told you to do something: to get up, to be polite, to stop crying. Her clocks were stern and obliging. Later they would load the clocks into the truck, though not the ones she had kept like small soldiers. Men who buy clocks often mean to put them in rooms where people can feel compelled.

We said small things to each other, Henry and I. Some of them were apologies in thin cloth. He asked me why I had not called earlier. I had been busy, I said. What is the right measure of busy? There are lists everywhere. My mother’s lists used to be the size of small towns. She made lists for birthdays, for funerals, for chores, for prayers. “Did she leave you anything?” Henry asked. He peered at the yard as if the house had become less. “She left me a recipe,” I said. “She left me a name on the inside of her coat.” I did not say aloud that she had left me a house full of small doors I could not open. “She left me this,” I said, and I put my hand in my pocket and felt the box. Henry nodded like a man who understood secret weights.

By four o’clock most of the large things were gone. The yard looked like a set of bones. Chairs sat neatly in the truck, pillows were stacked like promises, the piano was under a tarp with its corners tied. The men had a kind of celebratory look, the way people have when a job is nearly done and they can imagine the beer later. They wiped their hands, rubbed dust from the corners of their mouths, adjusted their gloves. Sometimes their sweetness was sharp, like the taste of a coin.

There were still small things inside. Drawer contents, a stack of magazines with old women's hairstyles, a used recipe card with grease stains that made the words soft. The last of the visitors, the diggers for the small treasure left in drawers, found them and made small festivals of them on the kitchen table. A woman who had come with a camera made a selection of photographs and told me which of my mother’s friends had aged “well.” I wanted to tell her that aging was not only a matter of appearance but of the story in your hands. But she was not my audience. She wanted images for a column.

The house, in its way, spoke a kind of indictment on all of us. It had soaked the love my mother poured into it until there was a residue. The pocket of the floor where the box had hidden felt like a secret lung. I stood by that spot and put my hand on the bare wood where someone had pried the floor up. Dust rose like apology. The men were careful in the end. A man named Mike—who carried things with the gentleness of a thief—handed me the small bundle of kitchen towels folded like squares of noon. “You want these?” he asked. I took them. They smelled like bleach and a mother who had scrubbed.

The trucks left in lines. The road that had once worried my mother with its speed became in my mind a needle that had been drawn through our lives. Cars passed, unknowing. The house now had a kind of new loneliness; things that had filled its rooms were out making their way to other rooms, other air.

On the porch, I sat down in the chair that had not been taken because it was broken in a way that an antique dealer might call “character.” The cushion had a place where my brother had once bored a hole and stuffed a pebble. I put my hand there like a child before sleep. My sister sat beside me and we let the pause settle. The sun moved like a slow truth across the top of the house and the shadow of the porch slatted its hands over our knees. “We didn’t get everything,” she said. “No,” I said. “We never do.” Our words lined up like two rows of beans.

“Did she want the house sold?” she asked. She was asking me to say what I already knew, which was that she had. She had wanted the house sold because she had grown tired of the rooms and of herself. She had said it, in the hospital, between breaths. She said she wanted someone else to sit in the rooms and not know the work of them. She said she wanted me and my sister to be freed from the nursing of a life that had the capacity to sink you. She said, “Don’t let the house keep you.” I had promised, as you promise to a person whose words are soft as bread; I had promised without measuring the cost.

Promises are curious things. They feel like debts and like light both. In my pocket, the wooden box had shifted and warmed. It made me feel less naked in the sweep of absence. I reached for it and set it on my knee. “You keeping that?” my sister asked, and there was no accusation, only the smallness of negotiation. I nodded. What else could I do? The box had a weight that felt like a thing of my mother’s making. I had always been given small things. I had to take this now.

Someone came to get the porch swing. They lifted it gently as if it were a child. For a while the swing held its breath, its chains ringing like a bell. My mother had taught us to be careful with motion. She believed things that moved too much were apt to leave. Perhaps that is why she loved rocking, because rocking is motion that curls back. We had rocked on that porch in seasons where the air pressed like a hand. We had held babies there whose skin smelled of newness and of sleep. We had said goodbyes under that swing that mattered in ways goodbyes rarely do.

The final thing to go was the breadbox. It sat small and white on the counter, a porcelain white that had been knocked about and then polished. My fingers trailed the lid as if it were a hat I had once worn. It went out into the yard in a handsomely quiet way. A woman bought it and held it to her chest for a second as if to check whether it would make a good keeping place for her own bread. She smiled at me, at a stranger, as if to say, “We will keep it well.” I wondered if that meant that we had done the right thing, that we had given the objects into hands that would give them new care. Or did it mean we had simply enabled the next cycle? Houses give and they take in the same breath.

Night began to be a thin thing at the edges. Shadows lengthened and the yard's new emptiness looked dressed in clothing that belonged to memory. The woman with the clipboard closed the ledger and folded the paper. She looked exhausted as if she had worked the day to earn a lie. She said softly, “We should get you a receipt,” and then looked at my face and saw there was nothing to itemize. “You can come by tomorrow and take anything else that was missed.” She meant well enough. Tomorrow felt like a country I did not have a postcard for.

My sister and I walked through the rooms once more, touching the places where things had been. We spoke in whispers, as if speaking loudly might haul back what had gone. I went into the little closet my mother had kept locked. She had a habit of locking things that did not need locks, like her jewelry, as if to remind the world that some things could be denied. The key was in the ledger, but the ledger had gone. I stood there for a long while and finally forced my hand into the small pocket of memory and found the habit of my fingers. The lock gave with a small sigh. Inside were aprons and a dress she had saved for “company.” I took the dress and held it up to the light. It had a mark at the hem where some child had dragged a nimble foot across it. The dress smelled like lemon and old dances.

“Do you want me to take the dress?” my sister asked, startled by the sudden portal. She had a look in her face like someone who had been given a map to a buried place. We stood and looked at the dress and then at each other. “No,” I said finally. “I’m taking it.” I did not know why. It was not much. But the dress had been sewn by hands that had loved and feared; someone had labored on its seams and loved enough to save the thing. Love, I had learned, gathers the small things until they are a kind of armor.

We came back out onto the porch and sat. The house was a shape now. Its rooms were empty, yes, but still there was a container for our talk. We sat and did not talk for a long time. The street pulsed with ordinary things—dogs barking, a truck passing, a kid shouting somewhere that life still had reasons to shout. We did not go to the truck where the furniture was piled because going to see it would be like watching people take your hands out. We were not ready for that spectacle.

The box burned in my pocket like a coal. I pulled it out at last, sat it on my knee, and opened it. The hair inside smelled of the ordinary things my mother had smelled of—soup and the kitchen heat and the small, heavy prayers people keep on their shelves. The photograph looked up at me like a window. I thought of closing it up and tucking it away and never opening the house of it again. But memory will not be stilled. It insists on being lived.

I took the ribbon and with the small care I have left, untied it. The hair spread like a small brown map across my palm. I held it and remembered summers of my mother’s hands in my hair, her fingers moving like a prayer, her eyes giving instruction and then, sometimes, a softening. I remembered her saying, when we were children in a kitchen with the radio playing weird songs, “You have to keep what makes you strong.” I suppose that line meant many things to her, and to us, but right then it meant the small bundle of hair, the weight of it against my skin, like a reminder that one’s self is stitched from pieces that never entirely belong to you.

“You holding up?” my sister asked quietly. She had water in the shape of exhaustion at her eyes. She had been small while the men took our things—smaller than the house, which was impossible. People shrink when their world gets rearranged. She reached and took the box from me and kissed the top of it like a child kissing someone’s forehead. She put it back in my hands. It was a private currency. We had no more to trade.

We closed the door together. It resisted politely, as if the house wanted to be kept. We set the key in the lock and turned it like an oath. The porch lamp came on in the way that a neighbor’s porch light might, small and obliging. The house looked like a thing that had been given a reprieve or maybe a small death. I did not know which. We did not pretend. We knew houses are not simply rooms; they are files and files are full of things that never leave.

As we walked away, my sister took my arm as if to steady someone who had forgotten how to lift a foot. We left the yard with some of the last sounds of the crowd still muffling into distance. The truck engines rumbled like contentment. Someone laughed over a joke that did not belong to us. They would take the bed and put it in a storage unit or in some house where a child would soon fall asleep. They would put a plate on the table and fill it with evening food. It would be no one’s fault and everyone’s mistake that the objects found new owners. This is the commerce of endings: things go on, people do not.

On the walk to the car my sister stopped and said, “Do you remember the fence?” Her voice was small with a memory of geometry. There had been a fence, once, that ran like a spine along the yard. It had fallen years ago in an enormous storm that took a roof and a prayer. The fence’s place in the yard had been the place where children learned to measure one another. We had run our palms along its posts and called each other names. There was no fence now. We had learned to live by other delineations.

“You think she’d have wanted us to keep it?” she asked.

I thought about all the things my mother had wanted and which had been wrested from the house on paper and in the hands of men with trucks. I thought about the little box and about the hair that smelled like hands. I thought about safety and the ways love can be too much or too little. The house had been her way to make a life safe, but safety is not a thing that sits on a shelf; it is something you build and you carry and sometimes you have to leave. “She would have wanted us to be free,” I said, and because truth is sometimes a generous liar, I meant it.

We got into the car and drove away with the box on the seat between us, and it did not stain my sister’s coat. The town slid by like a film. The streetlights came on with the politeness of an audience. I looked back and saw the house at the end of the street, its porch light like a small eye, and I felt something close to what my mother must have felt when she looked at us, her offspring, and saw the rooms where we had lived being emptied. It was not pity. It was an adamantine love, the kind that tells you what to do and will not be argued with. I sat and felt my hand close around the order of things and thought—because we do think things like this in the back rooms of our minds—that perhaps love is an inventory. Perhaps it must be counted.

We did not say goodbye out loud. We had said enough goodbyes for generations in a small house, and the ones that mattered were kept in the pocket of my coat. I reached in and touched the wooden box. It was warm and it seemed to purr, or perhaps that was the purr of the car, or perhaps it was only my own blood thinking to itself of home. The town dissolved into the dark of the highway and the houses folded into one another like cards.

I do not know what else to tell you. An estate sale is a way of moving air. People will have the sofa; someone will have the ledger; a child will chew on a chair leg and not notice what was handed down to him. My sister put her hand over mine on the small box. “Keep it,” she said, and I had a mind to promise—once again—to keep the house in the way my mother would approve, but I do not know how to keep a house that is not there. I know only how to keep small things.

We turned the box in the car and in the small light it made the grain look like a map. I thought of all the roads in that map, of old storms and younger laughter. I thought of my mother’s hands on my hair, of her saying, “You have to keep what makes you strong.” She was not wrong; she was sometimes wrong in ways that keep you alive. I put the box into the glove compartment, where the car’s manual and some old receipts lived, and closed the door. The world of the house receded like someone pulling a curtain.

Outside, the road was long and the town diminished. There are places you can go back to in your head like a garden in the winter, but the doors be locked and the roses dead. We drive on. The box in the glove is small and heavy and keeps its own company. For now it is enough. For now, that will have to be enough.
