They were up before the sun had broken the rim of the mountains and the orchard lay in that blue light like some thing waiting to be woken, the low rows of trees standing like prisoners at the bar, their leaves silvered and the fruit hung sparse and dull as if the season had lost its mind. A dry wind came down the arroyo and it moved the dry leaves with a sound like the rubbing of rope. Caleb moved along the trench with a shovel in his hands and the morning on his face, older than it had any right to be, the skin of his forearms freckled and the knuckles armored in a way that comes of long use. Micah was at the pump and the engine stuttered as if it remembered water and wanted nothing to do with them. They had ridden the valley with their father in other years when the water had come easy and the orchards answered like an army, leaves outstretched to the rain, fruit heavy on branch and limb. Those years were a memory that had the weight of law in it. But memory weighs in the keeping as easily as in the letting go.

The break was near the old plum row where the soil had slid in the winter and the trench had been warped and the PVC had cracked like bone. They had dug out a span of the line and set aside the stones and the rootmats and their hands were black with silt. A coil of the new pipe lay like a pale serpent on the edge of the ditch. The water itself was an argument in the ground, a voice that could be coaxed or shut away. Caleb had the coupler in his mouth between the teeth of his lip while he aligned two ends of pipe and Micah held the torch to make the fitting take. The flame hissed in the thin wind and a smell of hot glue rose like a temple incense.

It wont hold, Micah said. It aint the old stuff.

We aint putting bandages on a corpse, Caleb answered. We aint selling to someone that makes Band-Aids from the place. He pulled the pipe home in a solid move and the joint took with a snap.

You keep talkin about selling like theres only one way out, Micah said.

Caleb wiped sweat from his brow with the back of his hand. You been hearin them men with their papers and their smiles.

They had been hearing them for a year and a half. Men in clean shirts had come from the county office and from the development company and from the bank and they had a way of looking at trees that was commerce and not kin. They had names that fit in the mouths of lawyers and they had offers that sounded like consolation. We can also do partial, they had said. We can divide it. You can keep this. We'll pay for this fence. The figures were given and then given again and then given with paper where the zeroes lay like bones. Each offer was a small guillotine. Each visit unloosed a new enumeration of debts. The orchard had been mortgaged once for a new sprayer, once for surgery in the city, once for a season's crop failure when frost came in April and kissed the blossoms dead. The ledger had wormed and the bank had its cold figure and the county its levies. Each son of the land had taken the payments with the same weary faith as if the farm were itself a parish.

You aint listenin, Micah said. You think I dont know what them papers mean? I been down there at the office. I signed the papers when Pop needed the money for his last thing. You signed away the engine to the pump when it was hot, he said, spitting into the dust. That was you.

Caleb straightened and looked down the row at the old man thatched in the shade of the cider house where he sat smoking a handrolled and watching the boys. The old man had his knuckles twisted and a look in his eyes like someone remembering how to say an old name. He had been the binder of things. But age had a way of loosening those cords. The mortgage papers had been signed with the left hand when the right could not lift a cheque. Men come with words that are legal and final and the old man had said, Do it.

We keep it, Caleb said. I aint goin to have the place sold off in pieces. Its all or nothing. He set the torch down and the metal spat.

You aint got the money for all, Micah said. You ever see the ledger. The bank done tallied every foot. There aint no such thing as all now.

He knelt in the trench and with that sure hands that had put down irrigation line his father had taught him, he worked the joint and rubbed the softened collar until it seated and he shoved it home with both palms. Then he reached for the clamp and the clamp sang in his hands. They had an argument of hands that did the work while their voices found the shape of the grievance. Micah set two stones as a temporary run to hold the pipe till the glue cured; Caleb set his shoulder against it and the joint took and in the air between them a small and hard thing settled called the day.

You ever think about leavin? Micah said after a time. Going someplace where its rainin or where the ground is different. I been talkin to Joey over at the feed store. He says theres jobs in the city. Roofin mostly. They pay good. You could come with me. He looked at Caleb and there was a fearful hope in his face like someone holding out a lighter in a dark room.

Caleb didnt answer for a while. The orchard rose around them in rows that went straight as an arrow to the horizon. The trees cast long thin shadows that moved with the sun and their trunks were like the vertebrae of something that had weathered a thousand winters. You aint seen the way the fruit might be, Caleb said. Not this year maybe. Not next. But you recognize a place when you been in it your whole life. That was his argument and in it all the years of mowing and pruning and feeding and the small economies of a life added up to a theology.

You recall what Pop used to say when he was good? Micah said. When he was here to argue with. He had a voice that would make a joke sharp as a plowshare. He is gone now and you talk as if he aint. Maybe he aint talkin to nobody no more. I dont know. But he used to say a man leaves a thing to his sons. That aint how the world goes. The world sells men what they need to keep goin.

They had been boys at this work and the orchard had been the measuring stick of their time. For a while they had both been gone and had come back, as men do, and here they were in the old country and the trees seemed small surprises in the distance. The water hissed again in the pipe when the pump caught, like a first rumour of life. They waited for the sound of the flow and the trickle as if it were a bell.

You remember the night Pop pulled you up out of the ditch when you was seven and you had been foolin with the sprayer? Micah said. The sprayer had held you under in a way and he came runnin and he pulled your head up and you was coughin and his hands was like a map. He looked for an answer on Caleb's face.

Caleb gave a short nod. I remember. He smells the memory as if the smell of it were a thing. You remember when he cut his foot on the old tiller and he wouldnt go to the doctor and he had infection and the fever took him for a night and then he woke and he was him again? They both laughed and the sound was thin.

I owe him for that, Caleb said. I owe him all of that.

Micah leaned on his hammer. You aint the only one, he said. You owe him for a lot. So do I. But owing a man things aint the same as owing the bank. The bank aint no one you can sit and have a beer with and tell about the rain.

They worked on in the heat and the sun grew and the day put on its hard face. The orchard smelt of crushed leaf and the faint sour of early fruit. Bees went about their business with a grave industry. A hawk passed over the ridge and restated the law.

 There had been talk of selling since the drought had come three summers ago and the wells had dropped and the crops had shrunk like things afraid. The maps of the county had changed. More men from the city had come out to walk the parcels and they had a way of looking at the treeline that was always a calculation. We could put houses down there, one of them had said once, and watch the way the brothers had gone still at the thought. The county men spoke of infrastructure, a new road, of lines of houses marching up the watercourse. They said they would double taxes and bring in a lot of people who would pay. They said they had church along with it all. They called it progress. The brothers called it the taking.

Its the kids, Micah said. You got kids in mind or you think about moving on and leavin them? He said it like a man setting a hook because the sign of the thing that each man measures his world by is a child.

Caleb stopped and looked along the rows and to the far edge where the dry creek bed ran. The orchard had been planted by their grandfather who had walked the furrows with a stick and measured each space and set each tree like a promise. The promise had lasted a long time and it had cost them much. There were graves at the edge of the property, small stones half swallowed in the grass, where the bones of relation lay warm. How do you sell where your fathers lie? was a question that could not be counted.

You think about my girls? Micah asked.

I do, Caleb said. I aint the kind that forgets. The way he said it had an absolute in it. But he had also been on the other side, where you can see a town and think about a roof that dont leak and a school that wont close in frost. Micah's girls were small and their laughter was a thing they latched to like a prayer. Micah had taken jobs that took him away for months and he had come back with a steady hand.

If we split it, he said, we could keep this far and sell the other. We could keep the best. We could keep the plum row. They argued about lines on paper like a game played with teeth.

Caleb shook his head. You dont split a house with a man and call it a home. They had argued this before; words are a plow that turns over the same field every year. You keep one and you take one away and the land that remains is an amputated thing. Go ahead and split it and someday youre gonna wake up and its all gone.

Micah took his hat off and wiped his brow. The sweat ran into his eyes and he rubbed at them with the side of his hand and the gesture was one of a man who had not slept well these past months. You got no kids of your own, he said. You can say what you want. That cut like wind.

Caleb looked at his hands. He had no children. He had been, in the long way of the farm, the keeper of things and the one who deferred. There had been women once in the story and they had left like a season. He had taken other work and then come back. The orchard filled a shape in him. He thought in rows and grafts and the measure of irrigation. He thought of the smell of sap and the press of the fruit against a palm. The orchard was itself his child. The idea of selling it was like sending his bones to auction.

The day ran and they ate sandwiches in the shade of the apple bin and drank from a can and the talk turned as it always did to the weather and to the wells. The maples at the lane were starting to yellow and the sky began to dry. They had a small radio that had been placed in the cider house and it murmured like an old man talking about the market and a drought warning. It named counties as if it were reciting litany. The men with papers had offered a figure and the figure was big enough for a man to consider sleeping inside.

I been to church, Micah said after a while. Not of the fanatic type. They have a man there who talks decent and he says that sometimes the Lord gives you a way out and sometimes he wants you to hold. I dont know. I dont know what he wants me to do. He is a Christian. The orchard had been baptized once in their father's time by a watering and they had been given a harvest. The theology of the farm was a thing made of seasons.

Sometimes I think the Lord aint in the numbers, he said. Maybe the Lord is in the last push when the pump starts, when the water finds the root that has been thirsting. Could be he aint in the triple zero on the bank paper.

Caleb said nothing for a long time and then he laughed, a small and bitter laugh. The religion of a man is often the thing he makes when the rest of the world has been stripped. You think He sits with a ledger, he said. He sat down on the stone next to Micah and their legs dangled into the trench. A low plane passed over the ridge and the sound reminded them that other things were going on in the world. He put his hands in the dirt and let it run between his fingers.

You ever look at that ground and see the bones of things? Micah asked. Meaning, you look at the land and you see the ghost of the way it was or you seea the way it could be if we do it right? He had a kind of hunger in him that was both fear and a hopeful thing like a green shoot.

Caleb looked at the orchard where the sun was now high enough to set the leaves aglow. Once the trees had been denser and the fruit had been fattier and the irrigation had been new and the men hired had come in a convoy and the harvest had been the sort to make a man proud. But the years take their toll. The creek had been dammed upstream and a neighbor had put a siphon and the county had bottled rain like a thing of commerce. To think otherwise was to be a child.

You always get emotional about the rows, Micah said.

Not always. Sometimes I'm just tired. But the rows is a record and you cant just make a ledger and tear that out. That is what it is.

They worked through noon and the pipe held and the pump hummed as a small reluctant thing; the water came like a finger of mercy and seeped into the soil and the trees lifted a little like men getting their feet back under them. They watched as a little of the old life returned and the leaves flexed. There was a small quiet that came with that. For a handful of minutes the argument seemed far away, a thing you could hold as if it were a small coin. The orchard drank and the smell of wet earth rose and they both stood with their eyes half closed against the sun.

You do right by this place and it will do right by you, Caleb said. It sounded like a proverb and it fit into the day with a surety that made the place seem older than them both.

Micah spit. You aint never gone and done as much as a man who leaves. You dont know how to let go. He kept his voice thin with weariness and something like accusation.

Caleb looked at him. Letting go aint like that. Its not a simple act. When you sell, youre dealin in a thing that won't come back. You put a sign out and the men come with their trucks and their wives come with their shopping list and then you wake up one day and you cant remember the smell of Plums. He swallowed and his mouth worked. Think about your girls. You want them to be around a place where they know what a peach looks like on the tree? He said it soft.

Micah's face changed and the argument swelled into an animal that neither had body for at the moment. You think I dont want 'em to know? he said. You think I wouldnt like them to be in a place where nothing falls apart? But it aint me. Its the rent and the bills and the school and the doctor when somebody gets sick. You think you can keep the whole thing together on faith and old grafts? You aint got the income coming. You know that.

I know that, Caleb said. But you dont sell the bones of the house. Not if youre a man.

That had always been the rhetoric, the one who stayed had the rhetoric for staying and the one who left had cause for leaving. They ate dinner there where the sun found shade in the trunk of the storage shed and a radio said a weather gust and a man on the highway had died in a pileup. The news fit with their fatigue. The orchard had not much room for news beyond its own seasons.

You seen the realtor's car? Micah asked.

Caleb had. A white sedan with a dealer's tag had been parked a fortnight ago beneath the walnut and a man had come down the lane with a smile like it was the end of a ledger. The man had been in his forties or older and he had offered an appraisal that looked like a severance package.

Don't let him fool you with his drawing. He aint drawin a home. Hes drawing a colony. You wont like your neighbors, Caleb said, and he was thinking of that endless string of houses and the boy that'd ride his bike down the future streets with a new kind of daring. But Micah had looked at that drawing and at the money and there had been a light in his eye like someone who sees a door.

They did some other things that day. They pruned a broken limb that had a disease and cut away the fungus and painted the frames with a tar that smelled bitter. Caleb showed Micah how to graft anew a branch that had been saved with a scape of the knife. The art of the graft is a work of patience and violence in the same movement. You have to hold the tree and your intention with equal hands. You have to know when to bind and when to let the wound close. The graft took and they wrapped it with the strips and the tree did not complain.

You ever thinkin of marryin again? Micah asked later, his voice small.

Caleb looked at him. It was not a question that belonged to the day but it fit into the geometry of debts. No, he said. I aint think about it. Not like that. I aint what you call fit for the company of a woman no more. He smiled and there was a trace of the man that had been young in him. Maybe he would have been like any man who hadn't been raised in the orchard and his life would have taken another shape. But he had made his vows with the land and the land had answered.

You aint the only one, Micah said. He had his own private guesses. He had been turned down once and then accepted and then made father and then had to be gone. He had learned the art of half measures.

As the last light of the afternoon locked the land in gold, a truck came down the lane and the men from the county rolled in with a camera and a man in a suit. They were polite and precise and their shoes were cleaner than the dust that had been on the brothers' hands all day. They had come by appointment and sat on folding chairs and they offered a new figure. Caleb listened with his arms crossed and watched the leaves move. He had the look of a man who keeps the answer in his pocket.

What you figure then, the man in the suit asked. How you thinkin to proceed?

Caleb looked at him like one might consider a storm. We done what we could today, he said. We patched a line. We done a small miracle maybe. But this orchard is older than the math in your book. You cant just carve it and tell a story about growth.

The man smiled and unfolded his brochure. We can work with you, he said. Partial sale. Development. We can guarantee you steady income. Think about that word guarantee, he said with a pastoral ease. It hung like mist. Micah shifted. They had been having it out for months and now the thing spoke.

You show me the guarantees in a bottle, Caleb said. You show me where the man goes to pay the guy that will mow the grass and teach the kids not to climb the fence. You show me where the bones lay.

It was as if the men in the city did not know then that you could not parcel out history. They were precise and their numbers were neat and they had models where grass was green and children ran like in the page of an advertisement. They had points about soon and about market. Their voice was that of the accountant and the voice of the farm is often different.

We could keep some, Micah said to Caleb later as the men got in their truck and the sun slid below the ridge. He has numbers. He says there's good money. You can keep the plum row. You keep the plum row and sell the rest. You could make a life on that.

Caleb looked down at the turned soil and the place where the graft had been set. The day had been generous in the sense that the water had come. The pump hummed like a low animal in the night. They had fired the torch and the pipe held and the orchard answered with a hush. You put a fence here and a road there and pretty soon the people will come and the orchard will be a view with a price tag. He breathed the scent of the plum and the glue and had the sensation of something passing between his ribs and his mouth that made him feel the moment's gravity.

You still got Pop's hat, Micah said.

Caleb took his hand to his pocket and there was the hat he had kept, a battered cap with a stain and the old name on the label. He had sat in it sometimes and his father had talked to him in that hat. He had been a man who put his hand on the shoulder with the flat of his palm and the world had answered. The hat was heavy in the dark with the weight of speech. He held it like someone about to hand over a relic. You know when I was a boy he used to say, he said, half to the hat and half to the night, This place will always answer.

That may be true, Micah said. But sometimes the place dont answer enough. You might tell me a thing is forever and the bank will come in and say it has a price.

They sat by the shed with a bottle and drank and watched the dusk close. A flock of swallows came up from the arroyo and the trees fed them and the sound of their flight crossed the orchard like a chord. The world seemed to remember that night was its own authority.

I could go to town, Micah said. I could take the girls and try a life where the water dont decide everything. But I dont want to leave you with the bill. He said these things true and false. He had for a long time the idea that if he left the orchard he would be freeing himself and yet the idea of leaving had a price that was more than dollars.

Caleb turned the hat in his hands and then set it on his knee. You wont leave me with the bill, he said. I aint the sort that would let the place go to ruin. If you go we keep enough to keep the pump hummin. We make a plan. He spoke the words like a man threading through a field of dry weeds.

Micah scratched at his eyebrow. You got this notion like a bible. But you ever think you need to live? You aint immortal. What if you go first? Who's to keep it then? He looked at the dark outline of the trees. You got to think 'bout somebody else.

Caleb looked at his brother's profile against the last light. We been keepin this long as we can, he said, and that was all the argument he could give.

They slept in the shed that night on a pallet already used and re-used and the stars came down with a hard clarity. The orchard breathed and the insects started a small chorus. In the dark they each had a private map of debts and fruit and wrong turns. Micah dreamed of a town where his girls would have shoes that did not leak and where the doctor would be close and his wages regular. Caleb dreamed of grafts that took and a harvest that would surprise the ledger.

In the morning things seemed the same and things had changed. The water had kept the line and the fruit that had hung listless the day before had a new slight plumpness. They ate and then they went to town for parts and the hour was an economy. They stopped at the hardware and bought a roll of tape and some more couplings. The cashier was a woman with a braid and the town looked like a place that could be a world. They drove a truck that had known better days and the two of them were in the cab and they spoke of simple things like what to feed the chickens and who had mowed the lane. The town was a place where you can buy the things you need to keep the world going and yet it is also where men with papers come from.

You talked to the bank, Micah said. You call 'em on your way here? He had left woodsmoke on his collar.

I talk to 'em yesterday, Caleb said. They aint got no more patience. The man on the phone said the estate was due. We can arrange forbearance he said and Caleb imagined a man in a blinking office and a pen and a calendar. He hung up on hope.

When they returned to the orchard the afternoon was heavy and the light slanted. There were birds and a coyote had been heard on the ridgeline the previous night. They both moved about the trees and fixed up the spillways and set a new valve in the line and when the water came it did so like some small miracle twice given. At the end of the day they sat on the back of the truck and ate cold beans and biscuits and watched the orchard like men watching an animal. The water flowed and the hum of the pump breathed like a sleeping lung. The leaves glinted and the fruit took on a new look.

We might make it this way for a year or two, Micah said. We could put off the sale. We could tighten up the books. We could do some work on them fences and plant a small vineyard and maybe you could graft those old trees onto a hardier stock. He had ideas that were honest in their hope and he spoke them like a man assembling a raft.

Caleb listened and in his face the day's work had lined a weary peace. He allowed himself to imagine the orchard surviving another year and then another. He imagined a harvest enough to pay the tax and keep the pump. He saw himself older and the trees thicker and the girls playing in the shade. Hope is a small and treacherous thing and yet at times it is the only freight a man can take.

We do that and then what? he asked. We do that and then what comes? That was the question that sits at the root of most decisions. You could hold the place until you can't hold it and then they come with their papers and they plate the property for houses. Or you can sell and have a way to breathe. He had that compassion for the man who wanted to leave.

Micah looked at the orchard and then at his brother. I want my girls to have a school and a chance, he said. Dont you ever feel…he trailed off. He wanted to say more but the night took the space.

Caleb put a hand on his brother's shoulder the way their father used to do, with the same flat palm. You make the call you think's right for them, he said. Whatever that is. If you think you gotta go then go. If you think we keep the part and hold, then we do that. I aint gonna force you to stay. He had no right. He had never had the right. He only had the land and the memory and the grafted branches.

Micah looked into the dark and then at the pump's soft glow. He swallowed and for a long time there was no answer but the sound of the water. This place will take care of you, he said at last. I reckon it will. He had that saddling of jaw that said he was making a decision that was best known to himself. I cant be here the rest of my life. If I leave I want to make sure the girls are set. If I stay I aint a good provider as you know. He looked small in the night.

Caleb thought he heard the old man speak then, some ghost of counsel. You heard him? You thought he spoke into your ear? He smiled.

We had him once, Micah said. We gotta do him proud I guess. That as near as they would get to a conclusion. They sat and watched the orchard drink and the little life of the place eased them both.

In the morning Micah folded up a small stack of papers and put them in his pocket and came to the gate. I aint signin no paper imminent, he said. I cant do that to myself. But I aint goin to throw the idea in the river either. Consider it like that. He looked toward the town and the highway.

Caleb stayed after he left and walked the rows and touched the bark with the tips of his fingers like a man not trusting the memory. He thought about all the men who had come to the place before and about the ditch that had once been filled by a boy's shoe. He thought of what the orchard gave him and of what it took. Perhaps the place had always required the slow sacrifice of comfort. Perhaps the world that waits in the city took from a man what the orchard gives in return.

He took a spade and dug under a mulberry near the fence where the soil was looser and put in a stake that he had carved with a small notch. He bored his initials in the stake and then buried the stake and he left the rest to the season. The act felt ceremonious and small and to him it had the weight of prayer.

When evening came and the water sang again in the line and the orchard breathed and the pump purred on its night note, Micah drove in and the girls were with him and they trailed their small feet through the lane and laughed and the sound was like a bell in the dark. He walked to where Caleb was mending a torn piece of netting and he offered in his hand an envelope, small and hemmed. I got the appraisal, he said. It's good. He looked down and had a look of sorrow and relief in equal measure. You come to the table, he said. Maybe we sit and make a plan.

Caleb took the paper and unfolded it in the low light and there were numbers on it and a picture and a projection for revenue. He read it and folded it with his thumb and then he looked out on the orchard and the girls came and pulled at their father's shirt. They wanted to show them the plum that had almost been ripe. Micah lifted them like a king lifting small banners. The orchard listened.

We make a plan, Caleb said. We keep what we can that doesnt kill us and we hold the rest as long as we can. We do the work and we learn a way to feed it with small things. We get the loan fixed and the bank gets to know our names. Perhaps we start a cooperative. We contact others with orchards. Sometimes men together make more than they can alone. He was arranging bricks in his mind for a new house. He was doing what men do who have to build a bridge out of hope and truth.

Micah nodded and his face had the fatigue of the day and the relief of the thought that someone else might share the weight. They sat on the tailgate and the children played under the trees and the water slowed to a night trickle and it sounded like a prayer.

In the end there was no great decision made that would be written down in the book of the county, no signatures that would chop the land in two. There was instead a plan and a pact between two brothers who had been given one thing and who had to decide what to do with it. They agreed to a meeting with the bank and they agreed to look into a cooperative and to speak to men with similar rows. They agreed to place a sign not to sell but to advertise a shared investment opportunity, if you can call that not selling. The sign was small and hand painted and it said something like We Will Consider Partners. It was a thing half in defiance and half a solicitation. The men in suits may laugh at small signs but sometimes small signs plant the root of something larger.

That night they drank coffee on the back of the truck and the stars were a dry paper above them and the orchard breathed and the old pump hummed on. The life of the land was itself a kind of will and the brothers felt for a moment like small vessels in a great current. The future was uncertain. They had no guarantees and the bank had still its appetite. But they also had water and grafts and a knowledge of when to bind and when to let close. They had a memory of a man's hands around a boy's head in river water and the hat with the name in the sweatband. They had the girls' laughter and the sound of the plums when the wind came.

We keep it for now, Micah said. And if the world comes for us we'll do what we do. He wrung his hands and then rested them on his knees. They made no other promises that could be counted on. The days are a ledger and men pay with what they have.

Caleb looked up at the orchard and the line of trees Black silhouettes cut against the sky of the east he was thinking of the long work but he found he was not as afraid as he had once expected to be. For a long time a man can be afraid of losing everything until he is left with the only thing that was always his. He smiled at the thought and the smile had in it a small and ferocious peace.

They climbed down from the truck and closed the gate and the latch clucked and the place settled. The water kept the trees company in the night and the lines of the orchard were as a prayer pattern in the air. The brothers went in different directions to sleep and to think and as each lay down he heard the pump go on and off like a watchman's footfall.

In the dawn they would go back to the grafting and the plumbing and the numbers and the men with the papers that would come again. But for that night the orchard held its breath and so did they, and on the following day they would bargain and plan and measure and perhaps have to choose, but it would be by the sweat and the plan and not by some quick and elegant sale. In that way they were reconciled to the long'skeined work of life, which often consists not in the grand decision but in the daily keeping.

The orchard slept and in the sleeping it kept their fathers, the hands and the hat and the faint stubborn resolve that had always been a thing among men who tend trees. In time the trees would answer and the ledger would be read again and new men would come to the lane and make offers. But for now there was the sound of water in the pipe and the simple warmth of the night and the quiet accord of two brothers who had found a way to keep a thing together for a little while longer.
