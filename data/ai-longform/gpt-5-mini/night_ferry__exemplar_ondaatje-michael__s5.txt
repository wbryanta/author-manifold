He bought the crossing ticket with hands that did not want the heat of money, as if the paper itself were too bright. The harbour had the winter gloom of a mouth half open. Lights along the quay blinked like somebody trying to read a map inside a shallow pocket. He had been awake for most of the day — the waiting, the train that had coughed him into the town, the station with its burned-out bench, the coffee that was hot enough to be an accusation. The ferry announced itself not with a line of words but with the long animal groan of its engines and the careful rattle of chains. There were men with rubber boots and damp jackets wandering on the wharf. One of them, a wiry fellow with sea-salted hair and the tenderness of someone who keeps a file of other people's tragedies, told him about a current that could throw a small boat like a toy into the sweep of the strait. He listened and answered in half-sentences. There was no urgency in his voice. He had come because a hospital in a northern town had called that morning and said they had a body; they wanted a brother to identify it.

He had rehearsed what he might say on the way up the road. The rehearsals were all thin as breath. He had not seen Simon — Simon, whose name now lived as if it belonged to someone else — for twelve years. The last photograph of them together was on a mantel in their mother's house, swept eventually into a box during a move: two boys, shoulder to shoulder, one with his arm scratched by the rope of a dock. He had been the responsible one then, the one who stayed. Simon had gone. He had a life that wore its own weather: ships, sudden letters written in block capitals, the occasional postcard stamped from places that were not always islands. He had been a man of sudden gestures and of equally sudden absences, like a tire that had lost air. They argued on the phone a handful of times: money borrowed, accusations thrown like pebbles and then silence. There had been a fight once, in a bar full of stale laughter; the argument had been about their father, who had died years earlier sending boxes of things no one wanted to open. After that the calls stopped, thin as thread.

On the ferry he sat on a bench in the covered deck where the light was a kind that fell from many bulbs, each unhappy in its small circle. People sat around him like quiet villages. A woman with a red scarf kept folding a newspaper into the same narrow strip as if she had a ritual to guide her hands; a pair of fishermen argued about lines and the weather with a slow, polite violence. The ferry smelled of diesel and coffee and a trace of fish that had a way of making him feel both at sea and at home. He watched the water like a mind that keeps returning to the same memory. Each wave was a sentence in a conversation he had not yet had. The strait lay ahead — narrow, black, with lights of the opposite shore starting like a thin line of stitches.

He thought of the call. The hospital nurse had sounded efficient, the kind that wants to be kind but knows the world has a structure. There had been a name, a reference number, an address that read like a postal history of small tragedies. There was no other family, she told him. Simon's name and an age. “We need a relative to come, sir,” she had said. “We hold the body only for a short time.” He had not hung up but had sat there in the kitchen listening to the refrigerator hum. He had told himself he would go; he told himself then that it might be someone else, someone found on the rocks, someone who carried his brother's jacket. But he was a man of places. There are distances you carry inside you, heavier than any ferry fare. The northern strait was one such distance. He remembered Simon saying once, in a laugh that had no malice, that water could keep secret what land forgot.

The journey was slow, and sleep came in short, thieving visits. At some point he had closed his eyes and found himself in a childhood river, his feet sticky with summer mud, Simon holding the end of a rope while both of them shouted at a canoe that would not hold steady. There was a rasp in his chest when he woke — the ferry must have been passing a lightship and the hull had a manner of coughing — and a small kid a few seats over started crying until the mother pressed a pacifier into his mouth and the noise turned into a small, obedient river.

At midnight the wind rose up like a voice. There was an announcement from the bridge about conditions and a suggestion that people take care on the exposed decks, which sounded to him like a lighthouse speaking and insisting on its small usefulness. A family of three — a man holding a thermos, a woman in a wool hat — left for the upper deck. They were a little like actors who had been given a brief and excellent part: they laughed, they looked out at the sea, they pointed to a far light like men who own the horizon for a minute. He remained where he was, next to a poster showing the ferry's schedule with stylized waves and a smiling seal, a poster meant to assure people that the strait had predictable moods, though its predictability was only a fiction. He took his jacket closer and the strap of his bag left a bright mark across his palm like a secret.

When the ferry rolled he imagined a memory in which Simon had once slept in a place where the hull creaked like an old tongue. They had been boys then, climbing on a freighter that had been temporary as a ship of paper. Their father had paid for that thrill in his own way: with a look that said this must end before you break it. He could still see the river at dusk, reeds scratching a soft rhythm, the two of them holding a fish that would not die on their hands and yet looked small and luminous like a secret. It was a memory he unrolled like a map to find himself. The present had the sameness of a place not wanting to be loved.

The ferry docked at a town with lights that seemed to be gathered in a bowl. A man in a uniform with a high collar and kind eyes stood on the ramp and checked names with a pen that had someone else's handwriting. The cold there was sharper, a blade pulled from a drawer. He stood and dragged the bag over the lip of the ferry and walked, the town smelling of wet stone and someone frying something heavy in a bar two streets away. The coroner's office was not where he expected it; it was a municipal building with flaking paint, a plaque that had once been proud and had now sunk its letters into the skin of the wall. He had been given an address in the short message. He walked with someone else's directions in his head, like a phrase you say until it becomes mantra.

Inside the coroner's building the fluorescent light had a way of making the skin look like old paper. A woman at the desk with hair clipped in a way that said she had been through the world and had learned to fix it with the smallest tools, looked up from her screen. She was not surprised, perhaps because she saw this arrival sometimes: a man who had been called and then had journeyed across water and the weather and now had to do a job of recognition. “You're Elliot Harrow?” she asked. His name fell out of his mouth like a coin. He had told no one else he was going; he had not wanted to set off the echoes in others. The receptionist nodded and gave him a plastic form to fill. It had a column for the relationship. He hesitated over the word “brother.” The pen made a soft sound against the paper. His hand wrote brother, but the letters slipped into each other as if uncertain of the claim. When the woman had to ask for identification he took his wallet and handed a driver's license that was the sum of his closest small truths.

The stairwell smelled of cleaning solution and oil. There was a poster about grief and support groups. He wanted to read the soft, expectant words but felt as if he had been made of a material that couldn't be read. He sat on a bench and watched the hospital staff — men and women who understood how to sit with a body that had misplaced its story — move like patient birds around a nest. A nurse came with a clipboard, her eyes quick, not unkind. She led him down a corridor that had the cry of a pipe somewhere and into a room where the light was kept low, as if to be polite to the dead. The doorway clicked behind them.

Bodies have a way, Elliot had found, of changing the meaning of a room. He had seen small things before: the hand of an old neighbor, skin like dry paper; the white-shrouded figure of a child in a tragedy he had read about. Today there was a gurney at the centre of a room that smelled faintly of disinfectant and the dead, which smelled like prayer. The body was under a sheet and the nurse asked if he wished to see it. She moved in a way that suggested she was making available a piece of proper conduct. He had not known that identification could be so much like a ritual: nodding, yes, then the small, undoing moment of sheets falling away like a curtain. For a moment his heart contracted in a shape of old water.

He didn't recognize that body by posture at first, because the body was not a photograph but a thing that had been changed by its last hours. The face had been washed and pale. There was a braid of hair like a thin rope, and a line of bruises that mapped some secret. The mouth had the softness of a thing that had been speaking to itself. He looked for the small asymmetries of Simon: a nick in the ear from a childhood mishap, a gap in the teeth from a fight, a scar on the knuckle where a rope once cut deep. The nose was the same as in the old photos, slightly curved like someone who had always liked to look at the horizon from the side. The jawline, when he reconciled the distance of clay and memory, fit the way his memory had preserved Simon: as if he had been a shape that would tilt if emotion made the world heavy.

But there were differences, too. Time is a thief and a tailor: it takes and it reshapes. The body on the gurney had the lined hand of a man who had worked with his fingers, perhaps with nets and knots and oil. There was a blue bullet-shaped scar on the forearm that surprised him — something in him lit at the mark like a candle finding air. He could not remember when Simon would have gotten that. The chest rose with a rhythm that was no rhythm at all; he looked for breath and found only the pause of the world. He ran his own hands through his pockets like someone searching for a myth.

The coroner — a man who had the hands of someone who did stitches and also of someone who wrote with a pen for bridges — came in with a file. He had a mound of papers like a small island. He asked Elliot for details of his brother's last known whereabouts. Elliot stuttered what he could remember: a last hop from a ferry months ago to a remote place, a few lines in a letter about a woman who wore blue, a job that had gone sour. He noticed the coroner's mouth when he listened, the slight pull in a face that had seen more than it let on. The coroner closed the file because the files are like doors; you close them not to hide but to keep something from floating away. “Sometimes,” the man said, looking down, “bodies are found wearing another man's name, or carrying secrets that don't belong to us.” He looked at Elliot as if they were two men at the edge of a very long table. “Sometimes identity is the thing that keeps family alive. Other times it's the name the jacket wears.”

Elliot thought of names like soft stones. He had once been sure of what name meant — it meant lineage, the sound that opened a gate and let you into a house. He had been the one who stayed, who kept records straight, who paid bills and folded letters into drawers. There had been a sort of currency to his life that depended on the small ritual of contact. Simon had been the other kind: a man of urgent departures. There were things he could have said that might have stopped the distance: come along, call more, come home to mend the floorboards. Yet those were the words of another man, a man who liked solidity. He found himself wondering what he might owe Simon. There is a debt that is not accounted in money and which keeps a person awake at night.

The coroner left Elliot alone with the body for a while. The room remained still, the light a little too patient. He remembered the last letter he had received from Simon, the handwriting that had taken on the angled loyalty of someone on a ship. The letter said, I am fine. There was another sentence crossed out like a sun that had been rubbed from the sky. He could not remember what the crossed-out line had said precisely, only that it had seemed important when he saw it. Perhaps that omission was the shape of the whole relationship: a blanking out.

He reached, as if to anchor himself, for recognition by the small things that do not move with time. He took Simon's hand, if he could call it that, and felt the skin beneath the sheet — not yet cool, but already no longer the skin of laughter. He found a ring on the finger, a thin band with a dullness that suggested a life of water and wire. He thought of the childhood games they had played where the rule for bravery was to hold a fish longer than the other boy and then let it go. Memory does strange mathematics: it equates a left hand with a river. He pressed the ring with his thumb and felt, for a second, as if the world were able to offer him a fact.

He could ask to see the body more closely, to look for a tattoo that he knew Simon had — an anchor made with a child's hand on the inside of his wrist — but the coroner had said the name of the town where the body was found and the photograph from the police report offered an older jacket, a battered parka that had a patch sewn in a corner. The jacket on the body was of that kind, the thread coarse from wind. He wondered how often jackets travel without their owners, how often a piece of clothing holds a name that does not belong to the fabric anymore. He ran his finger along the seam of a coat and felt a rough line of filling, the memory of a woolen smell that might have been the smell of Simon when he was small and still young enough to fit into his mother's gaze.

There were also marks that did not belong to the past he had kept. A blunt force on the temple left a violet shadow. The mouth had dried thin; the hair had been clipped in a neat, indifferent way by someone who had learned to clean bodies with a profes­sional hand. He thought of ship-stern stories told at the bar tables: a man leaning over the rail and saying he can hear the voices of past ports, or the story of a man who had started to erase his name from everywhere. He wondered if Simon had wanted, somewhere in the slow nights, to become less itself.

When the coroner returned he had a photograph in his hand: a simple thing, printed on plain paper, the kind of photograph police hand out that is not meant to be said to be very accurate. The photograph showed the face of a man who could have been his brother; the angle was straight and the eyes closed. Elliot stared at the picture until the light began to dull and a small, private part of his mind — the part that kept inventory of missed chances — catalogued the missing things. The nurse had said there was jewellery, a note, a transit card in the pocket — little objects that may name a person more than a face can when the face has been changed by the last night. The coroner offered the list as if it were a menu of decisions. He must sign a paper if he chose to claim the body. Then there were options about burial, cremation, the choice to send the body back to the town he came from, or to leave it here in the hospital's care. Choices that seemed straightforward had the weight of doors.

Elliot's hand trembled just enough for the coroner to notice. He signed the sheet. The signature was not a bright claim; it was more like a small concession. He looked at the body again. The face, he thought, had a stubbornness — a slant of eyebrow that was the same as in the photograph on the mantel. Yet his mind, unwilling to be pinned to a single fact, began to rummage through other memories. Simon as a child with a scraped knee and a grin that had always been too quick; Simon at the pier two winters ago, unexpected, with the smell of tar; Simon once at a wedding, whispering about the ultimate futility of the open sea. There was a photograph of Simon with a cigarette stubbed out in his palm, a smallness that made him human. He tried to piece these images together like maps whose edges do not fit.

Outside the coroner's office the town smelled of frying and of someone else’s laundry. He walked without direction for a while and found himself at the water's edge where fishermen mended nets. The harbour had small boats tied like dogs to a fence. The men there had the kind of hands that can feel tides on the skin. One of them, an old man with a face folded by sun and rain, leaned against a post and asked about the name. He had an interest that was free of pity. “Harrow?” the man said. “You anyone I know?” It was a town question, not one meant to be sharp. He answered that he was, and the old man nodded as if he had known that grief tends to show itself on the face as if a new weather had taken over. The old man told a story of a man found on the rocks a month ago, of a cold tide that moves with its own logic. He meant nothing by the story except to name the world as it is.

Grief does not obey a single script. When it comes near it can be a flood or a slow leak. He found a café open late, its lights warm as if trying to hold a watch with a candle. He ordered coffee and sat by a window that looked out onto the harbour's empty boats. He watched glares of sodium light make the water look like black sugar. The coffee bitter and accurate in his mouth, he thought of his mother who used to make food with careful, hands-on gestures, as if baking could rewrite a life. He had been the son who stayed and the brother who did not fight enough. There are regrets that are inert and yet persistent, like barnacles you notice only when you are in a hurry.

Sleep slipped over him in the café with the heaviness of an old curtain. He had a dream where the strait rose up like a stage and Simon stood at the prow of a small boat waving with both hands, as if addressing an audience of fish. He woke with the taste of salt and the ache of no answer. He paid and left, and the night had gone on to perfect silence.

They had prepared the room at the hospital with a discretion that made him feel respected. When they moved the body to the simple, small chapel-like space the air seemed to change its scale. There were only a few others: a woman who said she had been there when the body was found, a policeman who had the tired, neutral face of someone who has been a witness of things too often. He listened to the woman's account. She spoke of finding a man on a sandbar at low tide, of thinking at first that he might be sleeping, of calling someone who called the authorities. The details were things that must be told plainly because they are of service. Her voice had the slow certainties of someone who had made a choice: she had knelt and covered the man with a jacket, a small mercy in cold night. The jacket had been green with a small tear. When she spoke of the jacket he thought of a jacket he had once bought Simon in the town, its green like a bargain; he remembered Simon returning it for a better fit as if he were particular about wind. Small things, he felt, were arranged in the universe like chess pieces waiting for a player who might not arrive.

He walked around the body slowly and kept visiting his old memories. He had been thinking, all those years of absence, that there were measures by which a life could be judged: marriages, children, steady employment. Simon had not chosen that course. He had rounded the edges of obligations and occasionally come home as if by accident. The pockets of his life had contained other people's names and the impression of nights open to possibility. There is a sediment to memory. You scrape it away and find fine strata of behavior.

At one point, the woman who had found the body sat close and took off her gloves. She told him that the man had a number on his wrist, a faint thread of ink that might have been a mark of some place he had been; the police had tried to trace it. She had been the kind of person who stayed at the edge of things until the current had her. “I thought of you,” she said, handing him a small scrap of paper with a bus route noted. She did not know his brother from any other; she had felt for him what one feels for a body in public space. Her hands were small and pale, the tips stained with fish. For a moment they were two strangers and the world had made of them a small committee to decide on the dead man's name.

He thought of identification as a kind of language: you say the name and the world is expected to fold into recognition. Here, he found himself trapped by the question of the name not only as a label but as a bond. What does a brother owe, if love was sometimes a closed door of years? The question was not only about the past. It was about what you must do to live with a face in your memory that might be another man's face.

When they asked him to sign the final forms he did so with a hand that had steadied. There was a formality to the act. He read words like “consent for release” and “confirmation of relationship” and signed as if the signature could seal something into being. The coroner advised him gently about the options: to take the body to be shipped to his hometown for burial; to leave it with the hospital under their auspices; or to have a private cremation which they could organize. There were prices attached like small black numbers on scales. He considered them with a detached interest, like one might inspect the ground for shells after a storm. The choice felt like making a map of obligations.

He had never been a man to cry easily. Suddenly, when they put the body in a cold compartment for a brief hour's privacy, something in him shifted. He went to a window and watched the light as it moved along the water like a hand wiping a slate. It seems to him now that there are moments when you are ready to make a decision not because you have evidence but because you want to be a kind of caretaker of a story. There is a small dignity in such unprovable choices. He had sat with years of silence like a kind of frozen meal; opening them again would be messy and unpredictable.

When he returned to the room he stood for a time at the foot of the gurney and allowed the face to make itself into the man he remembered. He touched the ring again and then the scar on the forearm. The anchor tattoo on the wrist — small and simple — was there, ink faded like an old promise. It looked like the handwriting of a boy who had wanted to stake himself to something honest. He had not expected to find it. It sealed the story like a signature. The coroner's eyes were on him, watching for the small sign that a man had recognized or not recognized. He looked at the tattoo and at the hairline, at the curve of ear and the way the brow folded. The room had a hush like someone who is listening for an answer to a prayer.

He said the name. “This is Simon Harrow.”

It came out as if from another room of his chest. The coroner noted it down. The woman who had found him nodded and looked relieved, as if the name had tied a loose end in a story she had watched unfold. He felt a recoil and at once a kind of small relief — not the ending he had expected, but a reduction in degrees of ambiguity. There is a mercy in naming. It gives a body the contour of a family. He answered the questions about next of kin with the procedural calm that comes from someone who has been through small domestic tragedies. He told them where to send correspondence and what to do about the few items the body had left. It was, he thought, like performing an old, practiced move, a movement of his life as if resuming a habit.

When the hospital prepared the body for release an envelope was given to him with a small lock of hair and a few photographs removed from pockets. He kept them as a man keeps a small, living directory. In one photograph Simon was younger and sunburned and holding a fish as if it were a sacrament. In another he was sitting on a step, cigarette between fingers, looking like someone who had learned to look away from trouble. These things behave like talismans: they protect the heart from being entirely unprotected.

The ship that took him back crossed the strait in the grey confusion of morning. The ferry this time was less populated; people who travel at dawn have the habit of moving with fewer words. There was the difference between a man who travels because he must and a man who travels because desire pulls him. He had been both kinds in his life. He stood by the rail as the land slid and reformed itself. The strait looked like a seam in cloth, the water running with a patience that would not be hurried. He thought of Simon in the line of bodies that drift beyond names. A wind caught him at the back of his neck and it felt like the breath of a door opening.

On the train home he had to carry a box containing a nametag and a ring and an envelope. He sat opposite a young woman reading a book about some island that no one had ever visited. The train hummed and his hands had that strange, hollowed feeling that such journeys leave behind, as if you have recently been carrying something you are not meant to keep forever. He thought of telling his sister — he had one, distant as some island — that the body was to be claimed. He tried to summon the empty rooms of their childhood house and the sound of his father's chair when he rose in the night. Father had always liked to keep the newspaper folded in a certain way. There are tiny rituals which form the longer gestures of home.

At home he walked the rooms like a man who had come through a doorway with mud on his shoes. The house seemed to accept him, as if it had been expecting him to return with news. He put the ring on a small tray and placed the photograph beside it. He put the lock of hair in a drawer under socks. They were small things, honest and private. He was not sure whether he had done the right thing but the rightness of any action is often discovered later, in smaller ways. He felt a light, as if the world had no more than been slightly rearranged.

A week later, there was a call from the coroner. There had been a mix-up, brief as a blink. A woman somewhere else had said the jacket had belonged to her husband. DNA tests were slow but the possibility was now set like a question in his mind. He had been told that early indicators could be unreliable, that errors happen when the world is made to prove what grief already knows. The hospital offered apologies that were mechanical and tender at once. He found himself pool-side in the kitchen, staring at the sink's curve like a man who was measuring how much water would be enough to fill a life.

There were two ways to fold a life: into a map that is very tidy or into a map whose edges are smudged. He had already chosen, in a small act of mercy, to claim the body. Was that falsehood? He thought of identity like a thing you can hold up and look at: names are hands, and who can say for certain where the hand belongs? He tried to imagine telling Simon — if Simon were to arrive tonight at the front door, smelling of tar — what he had done. He would say that he had wanted to do right by a boy who had been his brother whether or not the world kept to its strictures of fact. There are small acts of generosity that have no legal basis. They are paid out of an account of tenderness that cannot be audited.

Days later a letter arrived confirming that the DNA had been inconclusive, that further tests might decide. The language was careful. The ring they had found matched nothing on the registry, which was another kind of silence. He found himself glad that he had already driven back up to the north on a bright, blunted morning to be there for a burial he had not yet arranged. He had walked the harbour then, tracing the lines of nets and the familiar registry board that held the names of vessels. He had thought of Simon as a ship. You look for a way to keep a thing from vanishing by naming it as if in a ledger.

Months went, as months do, with the ordinary insistence of weather. He continued to go to work and the world resumed its more mundane gravity. The hollow in his days was not entirely filled but there were moments when it felt reasonable, as if time had hemmed something for him. Sometimes at night he would take the photograph from the drawer and look at the face that had leant into the camera as if to make itself kinder. He no longer knew whether he had been right, in the fact-based sense, to claim the body. He began to think of his claim as a small ritual of belonging; when the world will not give you a clean line, you draw one anyway.

There is a part of the mind that prefers myth because it rarely asks for evidence. He gave the dead man a burial in a small cemetery on the edge of town, where the ground fell away to where gulls circled and the sea hymned like a choir. He paid for a stone without an epitaph, reasoning that words can harden into a trap. Instead he scattered a handful of shells on the fresh earth. He stood there and resisted, in a small way, the desire to speak a full truth. What is truth when the only witnesses are gulls? He made a few choices: a small mound of earth, a loose ceremony, a handful of friends and strangers who sat in the fold of a hill as if that place was a secret kept by the world.

At the grave he spoke, not of facts, but of boyhood where two boys had held a fish and let it go in laughter. He spoke of how the river used to hide things and sometimes return them. He said, for himself as much as for the man they had buried, that if Simon were here he would have made of his absence a place to come back to, if only for a while. He did not know how close that was to the truth.

When the last spadeful of earth settled he felt that something in him had shifted, not because the dead had been proven to be his brother, but because he had chosen with a moral imagination that cared more for attachment than for certainty. The act of naming became a kind of verb that kept Simon from being entirely anonymous, which is a public cruelty. There is an ethics, he thought, to hold open the possibility that someone you love might be found by you, even if the world is uncertain. He had been given a chance to give form to an absence.

Back in his house he kept the photograph in a place of small honor. He did not put the stone's inscription on his mantel, nor did he take anything more official away from the coroner's office. Some things are better preserved as gestures, as acts of the heart that no one can exact. He had been a man who counted in ledgers. He had become, for a while, a man who counted in small acts: a ring wrapped in tissue and placed in a drawer, a scrap of hair that he often fumbled to find when his fingers were empty of other things.

Then one afternoon a man arrived at his door. He carried himself with the look of harbour men: a patient, salted face and hands that had not lost the way to lift nets. He had a piece of paper that bore signatures and a tone in his voice that suggested accountability. He was not a policeman but he had worked with the authorities. He had tracked a detail and it was he who now produced one last item: a cigarette box with initials that matched a set of letters on an old receipt. The records he had combed suggested that the man he had buried might indeed have been Simon, but the evidence remained soft and partial. He came because he had the manner of men who tidy up the edges.

They sat together at the table and the man laid out what he had found: a line, a photograph, a bus route, a ring that matched a certain description from a long-ago list. He had been to the harbour and asked the old men about a jacket and they had told him about the green patch. He had talked to the woman who had found the body and she had said that if she had known, she would have kept other details. It was as if they had been arranging the floor again and noticing where things had fallen. The man did not speak in triumph; he spoke as a man who had been asked to remove a pebble from someone's shoe.

Elliot listened with a kind of gratitude and not much surprise. He had been living in a kind of gray that had room for multiple conclusions. When the man spoke of evidence that suggested Simon, he found himself not relieved but satisfied as if a cut had been sewn with an invisible stitch. The stitch was not neat, yet the world asks for nothing neater than an answer of any kind. The man left with a quiet nod and the knowledge, perhaps, that the world will always leave its margins.

At night, he dreamed again of the strait. This time, though, the sea had the quality of something you could cross without being swept away. In the dream Simon stepped ashore and said little.

He had gone to identify a body and had found instead a place where absence might be made into a responsibility one could hold. The northern strait had not yielded a simple answer. It had made him step into a small ritual of naming. He had both claimed and been claimed by the act. The world remained uncertain. The coroner's letter with its hedged conclusions arrived in winter, a quiet paper that said the tests would not close with the certainty he had wanted. He put the letter in the drawer beside the photograph and the lock of hair. He felt, at last, a strange combination of grief and peace. He had given an identity to a man who had been found on the rocks, and in doing so had made room in his life for a person he had loved and left. Whether the man had been his brother did not change the fact that he had chosen the shape of a memorial.

He washed his hands at the sink, the ordinary rite of small living. The water ran and made its small, patient music. In the cupboard lay a small box with the few things he had taken from the coroner — the ring, the scrap of hair, the photograph. He closed the drawer and with that small motion the house seemed to gather its own pieces. Above the harbour the gulls carried their chorus into the afternoon light. The strait still kept its secrets. So did human lives. He had crossed it, and in the crossing had made of a vague half-life, a small grace.
