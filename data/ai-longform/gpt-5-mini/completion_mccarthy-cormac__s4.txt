He woke with her hands on his shoulders and the taste of iron in his mouth and the whole of the dream still clinging to him like the damp that will not slough off a man's skin after rising at dawn from a long sleep beside the river; she pulled him up, saying ease, easy now, there, steady now, while the lamp guttered and went low and the night outside the shutter had the thickness of a thing you can lay your hand on, and for a moment he could not disentangle the remembered crowd from the one in the room with them and he saw with a clarity that set his teeth on edge the ragged faces turned and the white of the stumps like pocked moons and the prophet's hand uplifted and the sun pinched out like a coin between two fingers until all that remained was a ring of air and that ring burned into him as if by a brand. He breathed ragged and took her by the wrist and she was warm and the calf of her belly moved faintly beneath the linen as if whatever had nested there slept and turned in its sleep and he put his face to the hollow where the sleep had left that small cavity of heat and for a moment he thought he would vomit up the worming dread, but instead of that he lay back onto the pallet and stared at the smokeblack ceiling and listened to the clockwork cough of some unseen vermin in the walls and to the pulse in his own ears and to the sound of the river moving on beyond the bank.

Hush, she said. Not a word of it now. You be still. You been hollering again. He said nothing at once, only turned his head and looked out through the slit of the shutter into the blankness and imagined the square full and how their speech had turned against him and the hands that had torn and the bite of nails and teeth and the mean little cry of the crowd when one of their own had been found wanting, how faith itself like a dog that has once loused will snap at the hand that feeds it, how men who have nothing to lose will quickly lose what they had in the first place and then that too, and he felt as if his entrails were loose with dread for the child and for the woman and for the small ragged life he could see bounding and darting like a morbid animal sometimes in his dreams, tugging at him with its wet small cry. He kept his face turned away from her, toward the night, and finally he said: It was only a dream. It was only a dream. She did not unstop her hands. She said: You say that. You been saying that a long time. Say it ten thousand times. Say it to the end of the world. He rolled onto his side to face her in the dim and for a long while they looked at one another like strangers who had somehow been bound together in some common misfortune and were now taking account, and each of them kept a word in reserve that she or he did not yet utter. He thought of the prophet in the square and how his voice had sounded when he spoke of the sun, deep and measured and with that spare assurance of one who trades in absolutes, and he thought of the infirmity of men and the rotten patience of crowds.

What were you hollering? she said at last. What was it this time? He wanted to tell her the whole of the vision from the first—how the beggars had waited, how the rim of day had thinned like a paper coin and all the faces had been upturned and their mouths all open for the miracle that did not come—but to say such things out loud gave them weight. He said instead: I don't know. I don't know why I holler. It comes on me. She frowned. You heard the thing about the prophet then, she said. Word went through the quarter quick. They said the sun swallowed itself. Not that it was swallowed but that it failed to come back. The people was mad. They went for the prophet. The woman said this with a kind of flat acceptance as if these things were the weather, as if the talk of miracles and the subsequent vengeance of the crowd were as predictable as a winter rose dying in February. Her fingers felt like knots on his shoulders. He put his hand to her hand and felt the line of bone and begrimed knuckle, the sign of a life lived in the scraping of odds and ends, and in the faintness at her wrist he read a worn tenacity the world could not wholly undo.

We should go, he said. The words were small and they seemed to make more sorrow than was their due. Go where? she asked, not looking at him but at some place past his shoulder where there might be an answer if a man could see past his wants. West, he said. The woman made a little sound like an animal and drew in her breath. West again, she said. You always say west. Where's west this time? He rose and dressed in the dark, his fingers moving by memory over the linen, buttoning and pulling on the worn coat, feeling the ridged map of his own life in the patches, in the burn that had taken a sliver from the elbow. He took her hand again and kissed the knuckle and then took down the small pack that lay by the foot of the pallet and reached for the beltknife they kept in a leather sheath. He turned and saw in the lamplight the small metal object lying on the stool and he took it up and tucked it into his fist and felt the comfort of cold iron and the thought of it was like a hand at his back.

She did not rise. She said: You'll bring something back this time. He laughed without mirth. He said: If I come back at all.

He does not belong to the square, he thought. Then he told her something he had not told before. There was a man they had known once, a thing of a man with a beard like a plaguing bush and a voice like a boat keel groaned over a weir, a man who had come down from the north with two others and with a small boat loaded with strange wares and with a book that had no letters in it but that he read from all the same; that man had told of places beyond the black water where food could be had and where bodies still warmed. The bearded one had been a presence in the periphery of his life since the long march to the river months ago, a presence wrapped in smoke and whispered counsel, the kind of man who might have been friend or thief on any given day. The bearded one had said little about the sun. He had said more about going and the need to move, the need to be always moving and never to be taken inside by any hands at all.

You talk like him, the woman said. Are you one of them now? He said nothing. At the edge of their hearing someone coughed in the lane and the sound brought a flare of remembered alarm up from his chest. He moved to the shutter and looked out. From the near end of the lane there came the slow beat of hoofs, the sound of a wagon perhaps, or of some wheeled contrivance that had learned a new way of life with wooden spokes and iron rims. A lamp bobbed and moved. The world was as dark as a bowl and the small light looked like a soul. He listened for the shape of voices and could make out none.

We can't let them take the child if it comes out wrong, she said suddenly, and the wish in her voice was raw as a newly shed hide. He held her then, pressing his face into the hair at her nape and whispering as if the whisper itself might change the fabric of the world, Should be no taking, he said. No taking and no showings. They'd make a theatre of it. He thought of the square and the prophet and of how men will take a miracle by the wrist and twist it to their own ends. When the child came it would be his burden to hold as long as he could.

They left at the first gray, quiet as mice. The town lay under an ashen sky, its roofs limned like bones and the short streets filled with the clank of a hundred hands stilled. Doors stood open like mouths that had no breath. From a distant chimney a thin thread of smoke still twined and they followed it, thinking some poor soul kept a pot on. But the smoke ended in a hut that had been stripped bare, the small hearth gone and the pots gone and the ashes cold. A child's toy lay twisted on the floorboards like an accusation. They did not linger. The woman walked with a gait that left him a little in her wake; the weight at her belly did not appear publicly and she moved with the odd sidelong vigilance of a woman who carries something neither yet mortal nor yet other than promise.

They met no one until the road brought them to the river and there the mud was thick and the reeds leaned like a church of shadows and the gulls cried as if in admonition. The river was broader than he'd remembered and its current had a certain slyness as if it had been given a new hunger, and the band across it had camped like so many small beasts with their backs to the water and their faces to the east. They had a fire and the bearded one sat by it with his coat thrown back and his hand on a battered tin pan and the two others, as ragged and badged by scars as ever, squatted and ate. As he came nearer he saw the bearded one's face in the firelight and the fire had picked out the hollows and the dry lines in it as if carving them and the beard hung in one uncombed mass. None of them lifted their heads when he and the woman came up: they had the ingrained caution of men who have learned to be untroubled by new arrivals because most arrivals were either traders or thieves or forlorn souls who would be gone by nightfall.

He stopped at the margin of the ring of light and unpacked without ceremony, making his movements without comment so that the two others would not be put in mind to question. The bearded one looked up when the wind of his motion stirred the smoke and for a second he only looked at him as if brushing at the memory of a face that had been seen before and set aside. Then he reached out and took the woman's hand, not with tenderness but with a kind of practical interest, the hand of a man who will measure things by their uses.

We be leaving, he said. He could not think whether the bearded one had heard about the prophet or the sun or whether he would think them fearers. He had seen fear and he had seen its opposite: the man who sees everything destroyed and thereupon plucks up a courage that seems like thievery because it was born of the certainty that there is nothing left to lose. The bearded one eyed the small knot at the woman's waist, and then he nodded toward the west where the low line of distant hills lay like a broken blade and said, Same as always. The river's just a crossing. That the only thing that matters most times is crossing.

They ate and the air was full of the raw talk of the road; the bearded one never wasted much speech, not for speech's sake, and when he did speak his words were like measured blows. He said that they had heard of towns collapsing and of everything that had been certain once falling like rotten bread, but he said too that the west had been spoken of as if an invocation, as if a faith itself could be walked toward and thus make itself a reality. He moved the pan aside and the brown line of his jaw showed like an old hinge. Near him the other two men chewed and spat and did little to hide their hunger. The woman leaned in then and said: You got food? Her voice rolled small and hard like a bean struck with a millstone. The bearded one grinned, a thing like a weather-beaten ham, and said: Food enough. Food enough for a little while. He reached into a sack and brought out a lump of hard cheese and a strip of some preserved meat and a loaf of crusted bread. The woman, who had been walking like a ghost between hunger and fear, took these things with the practised grace of consumption, dividing and wrapping and setting aside as if for some circle of small ceremonies; it was as if even in the face of more dire mischance she kept to an interior order of things that gave her the edges of the day.

The bearded one asked no questions that his eyes did not already know the answer to; he had that trade-worn prescience that men earn who have been long engaged in the barter of human trust. You been in the squares, he said to him. Seen the prophet. The words were spare and plain and did not fall heavy as accusation but as a simple articulation of a fact that might be of use. The man felt his throat close and he saw in the bearded one's glance that he was not merely being noted but had been filed. He could feel the old stick of memory striking where old debts lay. He said: I dreamt it. It was only a dream. The bearded one laughed, a short rasp of a thing without mirth, and spat a line of tobacco into the dirt. Dreams are more like compass points sometimes, he said. Not to be taken as gospel but to be noticed. Sometimes they give you a way to stand where the wind won't take you.

The boat lay at the water's edge, its hull dark and patched with scrap and its oars ready. The bearded one grew contemplative and spoke little of the voyage. He had once been a driver of men through hard country and the years had not dulled the quality of his eye that looks out upon what may yet be taken. He said they would go at dusk when the river takes a turn that would hide their crossing from folk on the opposite bank and the air cools and the water will be easier to read for eddies. He said that the one who knows the sound of a place can leave it without being seen. There was a small pause and then he added, as if to placate a world of fears, No talk of miracles. No talk of prophets. We keep our heads low and our hands tight on the oars.

They traveled at dusk. The woman leaned against the prow and kept hold of her belly with both hands as if the child were a newborn she feared might tumble from some unseen ledge. On the shore the wattle and stub of a township lay like used matches and the lamps shuddered and went out like prayers undone. The boat moved with a soft economy of tears; the river took them and returned nothing. When they landed and made for the far bank the flood plain lay like a spilled platter and the reeds cut the air with the cold sound of their passing. The bearded one had his own ways of measuring the night and the water; he found a shallow and set the oars with the patience of a man who has learned to expect betrayal from the world and yet to meet it with a temperate hand.

At times the woman would cry out in her sleep and he would lay his hand against her forehead and say: Easy. Easy. He would tell himself stories to settle his own heart: that the world had not wholly lost its mind; that somewhere men kept the trades and the slow turns of the seasons; that perhaps westward there would be a valley unstruck by the malaise and a small orchard tree that would bear a fruit no one had known had yet to exist. The river carried him past all manner of ruined things: fences grown over with the white snow of lichen, the rusted skeletons of automobiles like the bones of sea monsters, the outline of a child's bicycle half embedded in silt. The sun hung like a marble and gave no heat. It did not feel now like any weather he had been born under and its absence tugged at some deep chronology in his bones.

By the time the moon had set and the stars had thinned to the brittle line of a thing gone threadbare, they had come to a place where the earth rose and the grass ran thin. A small camp of other travelers lay there, men who had the look of a council about them, sitting with their backs to the wind as if listening to some inner voice. The bearded one was not much disposed to converse with strangers, but there was sometimes reason to trade and among those reasons is that in such times a man will take any scrap of news and hold it like a talisman. A man with a face like a split gourd sat and spoke to them in low tones, his hand on a carved staff.

We stopped, then, and the men around the fire made room, not necessarily with welcome but with a primitive tolerance, and a small exchange was held: tobacco for a strip of leather, a can of preserved beans traded for the route taken by a wagon that had gone north. The bearded one listened and was careful not to let his face betray whatever caches of bread he might hold. The splitgourd man spoke of a place seen from the top of a hill, a place with a ring of framed houses and a water well and a man who kept himself apart and who might be looking for a certain kind of hand-worker. The bearded one nodded. He listened to that and to another voice talking of roving bands that took what they pleased; he listened and the moon rose and laid its cold hand on the faces of men.

In the hush between two stories the woman got up and walked beneath the dark to sit at the verge of the grass and hold her stomach and look up at the sky where the void was heavy. He followed her at once and stood at her shoulder and for a time neither spoke. Then she said small: Do you reckon the sun'll come back? He thought of the prophet and the rim of light that had been undone and he would not say yes or no. He said: I don't know. She laughed a bit, short and without humor, and she said: You always say that. He smiled then in that quick, small way a man does when he has no words left to give, and he put his lips to the hair at her nape and felt the crease where the sweat had dried.

There were days when the river gave them food or when a town would be kind and trade would be easy, and days when there would be nothing but the old gnaw of hunger and the hollow of fear and the everpresent dread of being unmade by men who might find reason to blame and to burn. It was a life that could be spoken of easily enough by those who had not tasted it: inexorable, raw and constant. But there were other things in that life that the world had not foreseen: the small soft ministrations of a woman's hand, the way a blade at the belt lent a man a steadier gait, the way a conversation over a dying fire might make two strangers bound and certain to one another in time. He found that he was learning to make do with less and that the less he took the more certain he felt that there existed a kind of sufficiency for what was most necessary.

One night, as they slept by the embers still warm from the cooking, a sound like the scraping of iron came from beyond the reeds and men sat up and listened. Dogs somewhere gave a short bay and there was a murmur of voices. The bearded one struck a flint and put fresh coals to the pan, and the man saw that his hand trembled at the smallest of duties. The sound increased and it became a voice reciting names and then a chant and men began to stir and prepare in the way of men whose lives are no longer their own. The chant had sometimes the cadence of prayer and at other times the cadence of auctioneer and he could not tell which it would become. He could see, as once in a dream, the thin rim of the sun in a place where it did not belong: perhaps wrapped in smoke, perhaps behind a thing like a lid. His mind drifted unbidden to the square and to the prophet and to how a man's pronouncements might be like dominoes set down and then pushed and how they would fall and crush what lay in their path.

The chant drew nearer and presently a new set of figures rose from the reeds—men in patched coats, faces lined and notched, with a small standard raised like a ragged flag and behind them a wagon like a moving house laden with objects. They carried with them a kind of incense, a smell of herbs and something bitter, and they walked in procession with the solemnity of a funeral. The bearded one rose and went to meet them. There was talk and a trade of news and the men who had come in procession were not unfriendly but they wished to enter the circle of the known and to be let in, and in these times a man will trade for companionship as he might trade for anything else. The procession had with it a man who held himself apart and who watched, eyes small and pale, and at last he spoke to the bearded one.

We saw the smoke on that hill beyond the ford, he said. There was a stack of bodies near the crossroads. Men had been burnt in a pile. The bearded one listened and his face did not change but his eyes acquired a hammered look as if struck and then set. The man with the pale eyes said: They spoke of the prophet. They said that he was the cause of the dark. He said: But there be worse things than a false prophet. There be men who reckon that any man can be the cause. They will find a face.

The woman drew her breath in and her knuckles went white on her belly. He felt at that moment as if the whole of his life were a string of small fraying knots and that at any moment the rope might part. He thought of the faces in the dream as the shadow of them fell upon his minds like a galvanic thing. He said nothing and the bearded one nodded and said: Let them speak. Let them make their noises. In these times men will say what they must say to keep from staring at the ground. He sat down again and dealt out his tobacco and made ready to do what must be done to sleep.

Days passed and there was more talk of the uprisings and of the people gathering to demand answers and of the small brutalities common to human hunger. The bearded one kept their path away from big towns and toward old back roads that carried bruised trees and the small remains of fields. They passed farms and saw the earth fallow and the hedges unpruned and the broken stands of corn like a bedframe where the mattress had been removed. Once they came upon a farmhouse with a pole on its yard and a line of things hung upon it like a warning—clothes and hats and small garments with a child's shoe at the end—the sign of a visitation by men with no mercy. They did not stop. They kept moving west as if by movement they might outrun the memory of such things. He sometimes thought that through movement one might also outrun guilt, but such illusions are only for the young.

And still the sun kept a pall, a wrongness, a thickness that lay like ash upon their days. The light was pallid and treacherous, casting shadows in strange directions and making familiar things seem alien. They talked of the old ways, of crops and seasons and markets, and their voices would trail off because the maps of a former life had been consigned to the bin; they could recall the names of towns only as children recall names of lost playmates—faint and dolorous. The bearded one would sometimes sit and look at the horizon and, when he turned his face, there would be in it a small humility as if he had seen too many turns of fortune and had settled into something like a patience born of exhaustion rather than hope.

One night the woman went into labor. It happened with that suddenness that will mark a man's life if he proceeds to be a witness of such a thing: the small scream, the clinging hand, the curse, the cold fist of terror. The camp stirred like a hive crushed and the bearded one took positions and the two other men did as if they had been trained in some old and forgotten craft. He says little now of what he did then because words are poor carvers of such scenes. He took her by the hand and steadied her and did what a man can only do when instructed by instinct and the old animal wisdom of the body—he tore strips of linen for binding, boiled water, coaxed the fire into right heat, kept the pan to hand. In the throes she called to him and the baby knotted and clawed and pushed at the knife edge of life. He felt himself as if suspended above a sea watching a storm, and yet his hands were as certain as a flint.

The child came with the dark and the small cry of birth and the world seemed briefly to make a bargain with itself, to say: Let this be spared. For a moment he held the babe and it was nothing more than a small animal in his arms and the smell of it was alien and yet the sight of it pulled at something older than thought. The woman cried and then she laughed and then she cried again and her laughter had within it that bright and bitter thing that will sometimes break out in the darkest of hours like a strange weed. The bearded one drank some of the broth and spat and said we got to move in the morning. The men nodded as if they had agreed to an old law.

When the day came they packed and the woman carried the child with a kind of stern gentleness and he thought: here is a life I must keep from being made a parable. The bearded one had taken on an air of distance now as if the baby had made him reflect on his own worn edges and he had become pensive and sudden and forebearing as a man who thinks on his mortality. For his part the man who had once dreamed of prophets and an eclipsed sun kept his face close and his hand ready for any small thing that might demand he lay down his life for the child or for the woman. The days that followed took on the texture of a long prayer—hours and hours of walking under an indifferent sky, the child's small sounds like a bell in the middle distance, the presence of the river and its sometimes flattering invitations to rest beneath its curve.

They passed a town where a pyre still smoked and a face nailed to a post hung and swayed like an old doll. The sight of that small tangle made them step quick and the woman held the child tight and swallowed. The bearded one muttered that men would nail any face they chose and the other one said: They did it for a miracle that wasn't. The memory of the prophet pressed on the man like a bruise. He remembered the line of faces turned up in that first dream and how hunger can make a miracle be anything shaped to the need of the day.

As they moved westward the country changed in small things—hills that had been rounded became knife-shelved, the grass grew shorter and more brittle, the water thinned and the sky grew a kind of steely discipline. They met other travelers and bartered and shared and kept each other's counsel, and some nights they would all sit round a common fire and talk of things that had been and things that might be. On one such night a wandering preacher came in with his Bible there in his hand and he had the manner of one who had once believed a certainty and had been forced by life to barter pieces of it for the small economy of survival; he read aloud and his words were like flares, bright and then gone, and the men sat and listened as if to strangers recounting the old nation. The preacher spoke of judgment and of a day when the sun might be restored and the bearded one listened and then he spoke in his rough way of men who will find in every grief and every fear their own reason to lash out.

You be careful now, the preacher said to them when they were to leave. He had eyes that were like riverstones, dull and glinting at odd moments. He had come from a place where men had taken counsel and those men had put him out for his talk because they had found his peace wanting. The governor of that town had hanged his man for preaching that the dark was sent to purify. The preacher said he had seen the prophet and he had seen the faces bent in hope and he had been moved and he had also been frightened. The man who once had dreamed of a sunless square felt those words as a small flint against his heart.

We will take the road by the ridge, the bearded one said. He had that way of settling things with a voice that drew its strength from commonsense rather than sanctimony, and the plan was agreed to. They rose at first light and took their route, and the child sucked at the breast weakly and then slept and then cried again. The woman grew thin and pale but she had a resolve to her that made her look like a woman older than her years. She would sometimes shake and her hands would tremble as if with fever but she would not complain. He kept watch in the night more than once with the knife at his hand and his eyes wide open, feeling at times as if the dark might be the only honest thing left in the world because it required no explanations.

Weeks went and the stories drawn about the prophet grew wilder and took forms of their own. He heard that the prophet had been hanged and then burned and then that he had been recanted and now kept to a cave and perhaps the sun was indeed a thing men could bargain for, perhaps with the right man, perhaps with the right coin. He tried to avoid such talk but it always crept round the edges and sometimes the poor took it like a medicine. He bore in his memory the look of the crowd with the stumps, with the leprous sores, and sometimes at night it would rise up and take him and he would awake and shout and the woman would put her hand to his mouth and say hush, hush, hush. He could not tell whether his own heart had been responsible for any small thing that had passed. He knew only that he had said nothing to save himself when the prophet spoke, had been a listener among the masses and therefore had in some vague way been consent to the making of kings and the undoing of men.

There came a day of such great cold that the river smoked and a little crust of ice set along its edges and the world felt like some old mechanism winding down. The child was sick now and his skin had the yellowed look of fever. The woman would not speak of how she feared that the child's breath might fail; she sat all night with her hand on the small chest and felt the tiny movements and cursed the empty sky for the scant air that went to them. He had little to offer but the strength of his hands and his willingness to carry the small heat of the child's life for as long as he could.

One night a rider came from the east, a man with a cloak and the look of one who was sent, having ridden a long way to find them. He found the camp and made direct for their fire with the swift surety of a creature that has a purpose. The bearded one met him and his glance was quick and exacting and the rider's hand went to the satchel at his side and pulled out a scrap of paper and a coin and some little trinkets and placed them before the bearded one as if these could be the argument that would buy a man's trust. The rider's eyes were like pinpoints and his mouth was a line. He spoke first not to the bearded one but to the man who had once dreamed the sun's eclipse.

We was told, he said, that you had seen. He said it small and plainly. We have word up river that a man seen in the square was moved against the prophet and that things were said by him. He asked whether these rumors were true and the man's hand went cold on the blade at his wrist. The rider said that it was the law of the new councils that men who were thought to have been provocateurs might be called upon to answer and that some had found it better to reconcile than to let rumor or resentment grow to a fever. He sat for a moment and he said: We don't want trouble in our valley. We don't make a show of justice. We don't much use the old ways. But we must have peace. You allow us your name and where you belong and you shall be safe. If not, then we will have to make an example, which is some people's way of settling debts.

The man felt himself fall away from the world and become small as a stone. He thought of the square and the prophet and the mob and how the crowd had turned and how the sun had not returned and how the names of men had become weapons. He thought of the child and the woman's small shoulder and he thought of the river and how it had carried them this far. He could hear the rider's breath like the whisper of a blade. He could not give up his name because in this world a name is a thing a man may not part with if he would not be held to an account in the courts of the living; he thought too of the past and the extra sin of having been present where a thing had been said and not having lifted his voice to oppose it.

Better, said the bearded one, scratch his name from the page if you will. He spoke with that air of one who will not see his hand tied. The rider's face was an occluded mask of weather. And who are you to judge? he said. The bearded one met his glance and there set an end to his patience. The rider smiled in that thin way of men who have something to barter and nothing left to lose and he said: We only ask where you will make your home. He rummaged in his satchel and brought out a small thing of metal and put it on the coals and warmed it and then held it for them to see. Burn marks will make a man remember, he said. A small mark will have the world know you with a memory.

For a time no one spoke and the embers of the fire lay and the smoke drifted and the child stirred and the woman clutched the blanket around the small privation of breath. The man thought of running and he thought of the narrowness of that option, he thought of the river and the oars and the other bank and the smallness of any port of call. The rider uncoiled himself and there was about him the smell of the road and the iron and the economy of men who make their living at the edges of law. He said: Name. He said it as if it were a demand from an old god.

A man's name is a kind of armor and a way of being anchored and he thought now of how he had once been given names by his mother and by the world and how those names had been pried off by events and by the hunger of men. He thought of the dream with its mob and its howls and he felt that the world would make an issue of that dream whether he would or no. He closed his eyes and in the deep quiet of that closing he understood that some things of the past are not the property of the man who lived them but of the world that remembers them. He said his name. The sound of it in the ring of the fire was like a bell struck beneath water.

They wrote it down and the rider left as quietly as he had come. The bearded one gave a short half-derisive grunt and they all lay down again and did their best to sleep in that brittle light. The man who had spoken his name felt it as if it had become a stone in his chest. He watched the woman's small mouth and the child's chest rise and fall and in the dark he promised himself that whatever came he would hold fast. He had the sense, which was not without courage, that this might be the last chance to steer their ship away from the shoals of what men will do when they think themselves injured. He knew that the name might yet be called at some council and he knew too that the world was a meaner thing than any dream and that the only honest gestures left were those of care and refusal to yield.

The road that followed had about it a certain tightness of movement, as if all things were being pressed between hands. They moved when the light suited and stopped when it did not. They made small markets with towns and paid with coins they had picked up and with small services, and at times the bearded one left them and returned with information gathered from people who had been traveling different routes. The man thought often of the square and the men who had turned and how his own silence had in some way become a precipitant. He thought of the prophet's voice and of the rim of the sun that had buckled and of how men will make reasons where none exist because men must have agency even in the face of a cosmos that acts for itself and without malice.

They came at last to a place where the land dipped and there was a ring of old timber houses with a well in the center and a fence bent like a hand that had been twisted away. The people looked at them with a kind of new courtesy; they had heard of the tide of those who moved with small caravans and the burn's memory of names. The bearded one accepted a work that required skill, while the other two ramshackle men set to making repairs to a roof and mending a cart. The woman and the child were given a corner in a house that still had a stove and the outline of comfort and it seemed like a reprieve from a long rehearsal of fear.

Days passed and the child's fever broke and he began to thrum with a small robust note of life, a sound that set the household to a small glee. The man and the bearded one would sit together at dusk and argue about the right ways to make a living in such times, and they'd sting their barbs at one another in a way that was almost affection. The bearded one had an odd way of telling stories that combined the old mythic and the carnal; he spoke of a place farther west where the land held good and the people were kinder because they had less to fear. The man listened with that main of listening that is a way of making a plan without ardor, and sometimes he would fall into a particular caution at night as if the world itself might come to call for the debt the river and the square and the prophet had amassed.

Time had dulled the edges of his fear into a slow ache. He had said his name and it had become a mark among many marks in the little town. He had made himself useful and learned new things, and he had sometimes thought that perhaps the worst had passed. Yet in the sleep-hearty hours there would be the return of his old dream and the scent of the ring of faces, and sometimes he would rouse and find himself speaking aloud and the woman would listen and grope at the edges of the memory and bring him a cup and say soft not all of it, not all of it.

One dusk, as he repaired a cartwheel, a child from the lane came running up with a breathless note to a small assembly by the well. The boy's eyes were as wide as the mouth of a trap and he cried out: They say they've seen him. The word set a spike of temperature through the air. They looked at one another and the man felt the old thing awaken in him like a coiled snake. They took him to the bearded one who had the look of a man who would not be touched by such rumor easily. The bearded one took his pipe from his mouth and his eyes narrowed and he spat, and he said: Who saw him? The child answered: Old Maren by the stocks. He said there was a man with a voice like the prophet's and he was talking to a crowd and he said the sun had been moved and men cried and the man in the story stepped forward and said he was cured and Maren saw him and he is walking now on the road to the west.

The name cut the air. The man smelled suddenly of burning. He rose as if alive with an animal impulse and looked at the bearded one but then he saw the woman's hand at his sleeve and she shook her head and she said with a small force that made his knees go light: You said you would not, remember? He stared at her and the child in his lap and felt his mouth line like a knife. He knew now more than ever that the world would make a theatre of any face. He put down the wheel and took up his hammer and calmed the beating of blood in his ears as one would stanch a wound, and the bearded one walked away and the line of his shoulders was like a hinge that carried the freight of their days.

They waited and watched and the days stretched like a skin. The fever of rumor would pass and new talk would come, and men would speak of the prophet and of the man who had once cried for a cure and then of the sun that had not come back and the world would hum with its small consignments of truth and gossip. He would sometimes think that perhaps a man could live and feed his child and make a place of his own in the rubble of a once-honored world and that perhaps time might seat a kind of peace like a pocket of wind sheltered from the storm.

But peace is an easy thing to conjure and harder to keep in a world of men who measure their losses in the scale of those about them. He had given his name once and it had not yet been called further. He had taken a new name of silence and had lived by it. Yet the old dream would come and the memory of the prophet and the mob and the rimless dark would lie on his heart like a tar. He had dreams now less of the sun and more of his child growing and one day having to answer for the shape of the world it was given, and in those dreams he saw the child older and asking him how it had been and he could not find a way to answer without the small fail of the throat.

The seasons moved like a slow procession and the westward roads took them again past towns and through small passes where wolves might have once been but were now replaced by men just as hungry for law as for meat. In time there came the chatter of distant arms and the rumor of men moving in groups with standards and with compacts. The bearded one would say: Men will always find some god to fight under. He laughed then, short, and he would look to the horizon and sometimes he said little more.

There was a night in late autumn when they stopped in a hollow beneath the sweep of old pines and made camp again by the embers. The child slept under a blanket and the woman sat by the fire and watched the man with a small fierce pride that took him utterly by surprise. The bearded one had been quiet all evening and now he turned and said to him as if from some unresolved place in his memory: You were in that square the night the prophet spoke. He did not ask it as a suspicion but as a fact that would not be denied. The man looked at him and did not answer at once. Finally he said: I was. The bearded one took the smoke in his hand and said: Men will make what they will of what they see. The man watched the smoke braid the night and thought of the prophet with arms uplifted as if to take hold of some unoffered sky. He thought of the faces in that crowd and how their hunger had shaped them into supplicants or into judges.

You remember when you said you had been cured? the bearded one said. He had the odd craft of knowing what to say in the simplest terms that would sometimes carry a man's whole life to a freezing point. He looked at the man now and the eyes were not cruel. He said: It is not always the prophet who bears the blame. Men look for what they will. He put out his hand and the man took it and they sat for a time in the heat of the fire and the cold of the night and the world rolled on outside them like a hard-tossed sea. The man could not say whether the bearded one's compassion was real or merely the reflex of a leader who must keep his followers from falling into despair; either way the touch steadied him.

In the end their west became less of a destination and more of an economy of days—a clause in a contract they signed with fate. They learned the trades of being strangers, the modest crafts by which a man might hold a place and earn the bread that will feed a small household. The child grew and grew in ways that made the man think that perhaps the world had yet some tender mercy. Once the child laughed and he felt as if the laughter had the authority to alter the sky. He began to let go of certain things, not all, but certain things that had been like small stones in his pack. He still dreamed sometimes of prophets and of the choking dark and of the crowd's howls, and always he woke with the small hand of sleep gripping his throat like a talon.

Years, if one can call them such, passed. The river that had once been their entire map of movement had receded a little from their lives and in its place had come the small architecture of a settlement where they made their house from the bones of a long-dismantled barn and the woman kept the child in a modest patch of cultivated ground where she coaxed a few greens and roots. The bearded one had gone off on his own after some dispute over a trade and had left them a small jar of tobacco and a note that said in words that were more prayer than threat: Keep moving only if you must. The man kept his word and the name he had spoken to the rider became a thing in a register that he sometimes thought of and sometimes did not. He could tell himself at times that the world had made peace with him but such consolations were thin and fragile and often required a personal accounting.

At times men who had seen him in old seasons would come through the town and ask of his name and of his present doings and he would lie or he would tell an inadequate truth and the men would take his answer and file it away as if it were a small tool to be used in a later emergency. Once or twice he thought that his silence had saved him and once or twice he thought that it had damned him, and he could never tell which was the more just. The child grew and knew what not to say and the woman grew older in a way that made him continually ashamed at the canker of what had been done to her life. He kept his hands busy and the knife at his belt and his eyes wide. He listened for the sounds of the old world returning in the form of rumor and the clamor of councils and the new gods that men make when they can bear no more of the present grief; yet they came and went like weather.

In the evening of one particular year when the leaves had fallen and the watercourses had the clean outline of winter, a strange thing happened. The village woke to a sound on the horizon that was unlike those they had learned to know, a low humming that moved like a beast with slow breath. Men came out and looked and in the sky a silver thing moved and it was like a coin upon a string and it shed a light like a new dawn. People fell to their knees in the roads and some began to weep and some to shout and there were those who cried that the sky had been fixed. He stood and looked at that disc and felt the old sick pull of fear in his throat but also, oddly, a lifting like a hand untangling an old knot and he saw the woman's face and the child's small hand and the bearded one appeared over the ridge and tossed him a nod as if to say the world is full of surprises.

The disc tipped and the light that came from it was like the good of another life and the faces in the square that had once been a dream now looked up in a particularness he had not yet seen. The prophecy and the eclipse were no longer a thing of the past and men took their cues as men do, with bated breath and then with the immense speed at which the world will once again begin to make a story out of a blind act. He found himself at the periphery of that moment and he thought of the prophet and how men's faith can both bind and rend. He had not prayed in years and yet the impulse to fold his hands found him like a reflex and he did so and he felt the child's small weight in his arms and he thought: we have kept going, all of us have done this much, and perhaps it was enough. He understood that the sun was not a thing to be claimed by men and that whether it returned by some mechanism of devices or by chance, their lives would voyage on in the narrow economies of care and of watchfulness. The dawn that followed was not, finally, the end of his fears nor the place where all debts were reconciled; it was only another day and the world went on making its noises and its small wars and its small mercies. He took the child in his arms and walked out into the first shallow light and felt the warmth touch them—not the old sun perhaps but something that might be enough.
