The pier smelled of old diesel and iron and the sour, sweet tang of fish, and that was how he always first knew the town had woken. The cannery's long belly clanged and sighed with its own rhythms, a metal heart beating in slow, obstinate time through the cold months. Silas had taken to standing on the broken timber of the north jetty when the light went a pewter gray, when the gulls clustered and the foghorn rolled its slow apology across the water. He liked the way the harbor answered him there: a flat, indifferent surface that kept its distance and then, obligingly, told him everything he wanted to know if he knew how to listen.

Nora came the afternoon he decided to teach her. She was ten then — no, the first week of her eleventh winter — hair tucked back into a knitted cap that pinched her ears, cheeks flushed red from the walk down from their house on the hill. In the town's winter, children grew like driftwood: small pieces of something larger, sanded and darkened, waiting to be caught at the tide. She tugged her mittens off, and the cold made her eyes bright and raw. Silas saw the way she watched the water, like a bird watching tides: nearly motionless, intent.

"Don't stand there with your thumbs in the warmth," he told her, the words finding their place as easily as the old pilot's cap that had settled, for habit, on the back of his head. He smelled of sea salt and winter smoke. She folded her hands and stepped closer to the edge.

"The cannery's using the whistle again," Nora said. She spoke as if making a report. She kept her sentences like nets, neat and efficient.

"Aye," Silas said. "They've got one more round this season. Last one with the old batch. They'll keep the lights on for 'em while they still can." He didn't need to explain; the town had been saying "last year" for as long as he could remember. Men left one winter and came back; families closed up their houses and sold to strangers who thought the water romantic until the first February hard freeze. The cannery, though, was not something romance could purchase. It was a business and a ritual, and now the ritual was fraying.

"Will they shut for good?" Nora asked. She asked it without heat, like someone asking how far away the ferry was. She had the sort of voice that could hold the sound of the harbor, low and porous. There were things in the town that ignored certainty: the tides, the gulls. Everything else was up for talk.

Silas looked at the water the way he often had, as if asking a question of a sleeping thing. The surface lay flat, a sheet of pewter scored by the wharf pilings and the lazy wake of an oysterman's skiff. Little currents braided through the pilings, leaving clean bands of lighter color where the bottom's sand had been dragged thin. He could see knots of weed anchored to a rock, the ghost of a log, the ripple of a fish surfacing to the west where the water narrowed. For a man who had guided freighters into harbor by their sound as much as by their charts, all of this was a text.

"You read the water wrong if you start with fear," he told her. "You start with attention." He pointed with a gloved hand to the place where the mooring buoys leaned a little to the north. "See how the buoys list? Northeast wind's in the top layer. But look below — there's a south-running eddy tugging at the pilings. There's a mixing that's making that sheen. If you don't notice the layering, you'll be fooled by the whistle. The cannery's whistle is just that: sound. It means things, but it doesn't tell you the whole way through."

Nora leaned down and shaded her eyes with one hand, copying a gesture he'd seen boatmen use when they scanned horizons. She blinked against the flat light and the salt ghosted her lashes. "What tells you the whole way through, then?"

He smiled, a small, private lift of his mouth that curved the weatherlines in his face. "Everything tells you. The gulls. The smell of the water. The feel in your feet when you're on a boat, the way the keel answers. You learn to put them together."

They began that winter as if with an arranged task: once a week, on whatever afternoon Noraschool let out early or the cannery's schedule left them time between the men hauling in the hauls, they went down to the water. There were other mornings and evenings in which he watched the town's slow unravel, but those afternoons belonged to them. He taught her the language first: fluke, swell, lee, back eddy, standing wave. He taught her the way to read a tide chart not as numbers but as a story of water's hunger and slaking. He taught her to find a seam in the water where a current met a bay's breath and to see in that seam the path a boat would take to avoid the hushed, treacherous mud that lay only an arm's length beneath.

Nora learned with the exactness of a girl who had watched people weather the town's losses. She asked questions that were more like tests than curiosity. "Why does the water turn that green there?" she would ask, and he would tell her about eelgrass beds, about the light at certain angles, about the way a run of herring turns a ferry of water into a clamoring throat. She learned to read the birds. When the gulls wheeled in tight, desperate circles over a patch of water, it meant baitfish at the surface, and that meant nets would be taut and the cannery's trucks humming. When the cormorants dived deep and came up dry too often, it meant the sea had pulled its fish into corners out of reach, and the smoke from the cannery would taste of overwork and disappointment.

He taught her to listen not only to the patterns of day but of memory. The harbor kept records under its skin: the memory of freighter wakes on green afternoons, the single great storm that once rolled the little pilot schooner on her side and made a young boy swim out and catch at the keel, the years of plenty when the cannery bell clanged at dawn and the town's breath sped in a quick, warm way. Silas taught her to place those records beside the present, to read them like ghost charts over living water. "You listen for what the water remembers," he'd say. "And also for what it forgets."

He had time now to fold the stories with his hands. Retirement had given him the luxury of teaching them and the pain of redundancy. He wore his knowledge like a coat that no one would rent. Captain's hat, heavy boots with the soles worn into arcs by decades of walking slips and gangways, a logbook in which his handwriting favored the slow, deliberate loops of a man used to the calmness of long watches. He had been a pilot most of his adult life, slotted into the harbor's smallest seams, moving great hulls through channels as if he were sliding a finger along someone else's map. The job had given him a love of small certainties — the little notch of a reef, the angle of a lighthouse in a fog, the way a channel moved after a spring melt. It had also given him a body that no longer moved as quickly as his mind, a hearing that picked up the surface sounds but missed the thin timbre beneath, and a family that sometimes looked at him with a politely measured pity. He had not married again after the wife had gone. The town's people sometimes called him "Old Silas" with affection that was thick as butter and as brittle too.

Nora was different from the town's other children. She made a habit of bringing home small things she found on the beach: a bottle with a cork, a ring of an old pulley, a chipped piece of porcelain glazed with salt and time. Her mother, Ellen, who worked afternoons at the cannery on the filling lines, let her go, because there were so few anchors left for a child in a town that wanted to be elsewhere. "Get your boots on," Ellen would say. "Don't fall through the ice, don't talk to the men about money, and come home when the streetlights shine." It was a short list, but enough.

Ellen respected the harbor in the way people respect the last good work of strength. She had once been younger than Nora, and she remembered hands pulled up the side of a boat, the warm lean of a man's shoulder who loved someone else. The man, Nora's father, had drowned the year Nora was five. There were ways a town took grief and set it down as if it were a fixture, like a garden bench that never left. Some things were chronic in memory, and Silas had been the one to teach the child how the line between leaving and coming home could be read across a water's face.

Winter scoured the shores. The first real storm came in December with a wind that smelled like iron and turned the surface into a dropping black lace. Boats lay tied like sleeping dogs, and the cannery's men stayed inside, crowding the floor where the machines clicked and spat. Silas and Nora went out before the storm hit. They kept their day small and flat: boots, scarves, the old skiff that the harbor trust kept tied beneath the purser's shed. Silas brought a kerosene lantern because younger skippers liked electronics but he trusted a flame for each small hesitation.

"Don't be afraid," he told her when the first fat flakes began to fall, and he meant it for the water and for her. "Fear makes you forget the names of things."

"Will the fish feel the storm?" she wanted to know.

"Fish feel everything," he said. "They're more honest about it than men. We're given to thinking we can tell the weather and then make bargains with it. Fish don't bargain. They go to where the shelter is. They move. You watch the movement and you learn their routes."

They moved slowly down the shore, skirting the fringe of a harbor that was suddenly wild with a wind that drove the flakes sideways. The cannery hulk reared like an angry beast. Smoke came from its flues in a gray ribbon that stitched itself into the sky. Workers pressed against the loading bay talk of a last big run. There were whispers that provisions from a new contract might be salvaged yet, that some line of fish buyers in a city an hour's ferry away still wanted a small, artisanal line. But mostly there were layoffs, and there were men who pronounced themselves red-eyed and ready to move to where work was. For a town that had expected the cannery to last the way churches last, the closure was a slow, shocking unbuttoning.

Silas watched the men and thought of his own son — Nora's father — how he'd gone out in a fishing boat one night and come back an empty bench. There was a place where memory and sea interlaced; the knowledge of how to keep that place safe was a small, stubborn faith Silas held like a stone. He wanted to pass it on to his granddaughter not because the town needed another pilot but because a person needs a way to see the world that isn't merely transactional. Someone always needed to be able to tell the difference between a surface that sang and one that hid.

He taught Nora to notice the hidden seams of the days. They would sit on the breakwater and he would ask her to listen. At first, all she heard were gulls and the cannery's machine chatter. After a while she began to pick out the finer things: the soft thud of a distant trawler's engine, the whisper of a current against a moored hull, the thin metallic note that came when a lobster pot shifted and sang. He made her close her eyes and imagine a boat moving through a channel. Then he'd ask what the boat would feel: a hollowing under the keel, the pressure of two currents rubbing like palms, the quick heavy breathing of a net full of fish. She would answer with a child's certainty, and he would draw those images as a teacher does, so the child could step into them later when the sea demanded a decision and not a guess.

In January, they went out farther. The harbor seemed smaller some days and the world beyond vaster. They took the skiff into the channel and Silas positioned the lantern at the bow. He loved the way light wrote itself across water, a brief, holy thing. The pilings of the old pier cast their lines like the ribbing of a creature that had swum before this town learned its name. He showed her how a channel's mouth changed with every big tide, and how the edges held secret stories. "You don't have to remember every rock," he said. "You have to remember the way the sea changes around them. Rocks are inert; the water is the telling thing."

The town had grown used to his authority on water. Men and women who were younger now would nod when he walked past as if greeting an old lighthouse. He had a kind of recognition that contained no impatience. But before the cannery's announcement and the talk of closure he had been needed for reasons beyond merely guiding ships. He had been the sort of man parents called when a child's boat wouldn't find its way home or when a winter ice shifted and left a net hanging like a trap. In those times, he felt alive in a way that the quiet days on the jetty could not bring. He had a map in his head and a memory that kept the map from rotting.

One raw, glassy morning in late January, when the harbor's surface lay like a sheet of pewter beaten thin as the light slid under it, they watched the cannery's last large shipment roll out. The trucks were lined like a slow parade. Men carried empty crates back to make more room; women, who had been boned out years of fish and hardened into rhythmic movement, sang an odd little cadence that the machines imitated. The townspeople came down to watch, the way people go to look at a deerstalling on the side of a highway after an accident: to confirm that things were real. The cannery's foreman, a broad-shouldered man named Reyes whose hands had minute white scabs from too many years of salted cold, tipped his hat at Silas when he saw him. "Old friend," Reyes said in a voice like a shop bell, "come watch the last."

They stood on the pier, the town behind them a scattering of smoke and doors. The cannery's whistle blew, a single long note that bent the air. It cut through the gray and made the gulls lift in an angry flurry. And then, after the last truck left, the foreman pulled the stair gate and walked inside. For a moment, as if in courtesy, the machines and belts made a kind of sound like someone sighing. People clapped in the way that one claps at the end of a performance: to mark that something, in fact, had happened. There were tears among the younger mothers and a look of something like calculation among the men with callused hands.

Nora watched it all with a face like a cliff. She was not as loud as some children; she did not need to be. She made notes in the margins of a notebook her grandfather gave her, lines of a water take: "Whistle north; wind turns." She drew arrows beside the gulls and the pulsing rigs of the cannery. He watched her draw the town's end as carefully as if it were a navigation note.

"You ever think about leaving?" he asked as they walked home. The wind bit at their noses. The conversation was a thing that moves slowly on the shore, like a tide that fills the space between cliffs, inevitable and slow.

"Sometimes," she said. "Mama says maybe. She's talking to a cousin in Everett. Says there might be work at a warehouse over there. There's extra schooling, more libraries." Her voice softened and went to the old small thing it had been after the walk up. "But where would I learn the water if I go? How'd I know the eddies? How would I know which buoys mean trouble?"

"You take the water in your head," he said. "You carry it like you carry a warm stone. You can leave and you can still listen. And the town changes. Maybe you'll read water different there. Different currents, different stakes. You'll be better for it."

She looked at him hard, as if weighing the truth of his promise. You could not accuse the child of sentimentality. She read the economy of possibilities and then came back to the harbor. "You think there's going to be no more canneries anywhere?" she asked.

"No," he said. "There will be other canneries. The sea is big. But this one is ours." The words were small and heavy. He wanted to tell her how to mark the town's grief without letting it hollow her. He wanted to make a map for her future: measure the things you can control, know the things you cannot. He had been a pilot by training and an old man by accident; he had been broken and then mended in the slow stitch of community. He had the stubbornness of someone who had been given a particular window into a place and found it good enough to make arrangements with.

The winter deepened. In February the temperatures dropped further, and the sea steamed where it met the cold air, the vapor unspooling slow as the town's memories. They took more frequent trips into the channel. Nora grew comfortable with the skiff's rocking as with a lullaby. Silas taught her to read the small signs that saved lives: a gull's sudden quiet, the way a wavetrain from a distant ship could be read like an extra voice in a crowded room, the thud of a crust of ice breaking against a hull that meant the edge of a harbor shoal. Once, when they were coming back with hands numb and the lantern guttering, she called out and pointed where the water looked wrong: a strange, shallow shimmer where the otherwise pewter face folded like a quiver.

"What's that?" she asked.

He peered and felt his memory calve. "Sandbar," he said. "Shifts with each heavy tide. See how the wave tightens there? The current's making a lip. If a boat comes in and takes that wrong, she'll scrape and take water. We make note. We pass it on to the harbor trust. They mark it with a temporary buoy."

They marked the sandbar on their map after dinner, Silas with his old careful hand and Nora with a new, deliberate stroke. She asked questions about the old pilots who'd drawn routes before him, about the first cannery that had ever stood on that cove, about the old woman who used to walk the beach with a lantern and a basket whose contents no one knew. He answered in stories. Each narrative was a lesson disguised as anecdote. "You learn by story," he'd say. "People think it's myth, but it's memory wrapped in something useful. It keeps us less foolish when the sea takes a whim."

As the weeks slid, there were other things to notice. The town's population thinned, and the faces at the diner grew familiar in new, spare ways. The men who once had easy incomes took to stacking wood or fixing elders' roofs for a price. The children whose fathers had boarded other boats came back home early and tried to learn other trades. Sometimes there were fights by the fish house over the last of a line; sometimes there were practical jokes. The cannery's closing had a way of making small things magnified; the town's edges showed like a map where the ink had bled.

Silas kept his boat ready. The harbor men trusted him; they knew his hands and his way and, in the final months, called on him for advice about lines and moorings and the angle at which an old trawler should come in to keep from scraping her keel. There came one night when the town called on him for the kind of help the town had called for in older stories: a small sub-contract trawler had been caught in a sudden offshore squall, and its captain, a young man with a new beard, radioed in panicked, wrong-headed coordinates. The harbor pilot he should have called was in a town meeting; the harbor master rabid with bureaucracy was on the phone; Silas's mind, however, was a place of charts. He went down to the pier and launched the skiff, rust cracking in the cold air.

Nora insisted on coming. "I can hold the lantern," she said. She was now an efficient, grave person in scarves and gloves, the smallness of her hands replaced by the strength of endurance. He almost told her to stay, but then thought of the ways learning should be direct and thick. "Get the hood," he said instead. "Hold the lantern steady. And remember what we said about the gulls. Don't let your imagination make you an actor where a light and a pulse will do."

The night had a sound like a closing door. The harbor lay in parts black and parts loud where waves beat smallly on quay faces. They headed out by cutting low through choppy water and into the widening gray. He trusted his eyes and the lantern and the memory of the channel's bones. Nora watched the sea with a kind of reverence. At one point Silas looked at her and realized the strange justice of teaching: some things you could only pass in a gale.

They found the trawler, its hull raking, the skipper pale and muttering into his radio. The man had cut his engine in panic and had drifted into a sting of shoal. Silas talked him into floating off and into the channel where the current would help him. He placed the skiff in the line between the trawler's stern and the harbor entrance and used the lantern to give the faint, steady signal he had employed for years. The men on deck followed light because light was uncomplicated. In the end, they led the trawler inside with a rope and a measure of calm. When they tied her off beside a dock, the young captain shook and then looked at Silas with a kind of desperate gratitude. "How did you...?" he asked.

"Been here a while," Silas said. "We read the water."

Nora kept her lantern level, watching the men haul lines. Afterwards, walking home in the first hint of dawn, she said, "You could have done worse. You could have stayed home."

"I didn't," he said. "I came. And you watched."

"It smelled like fear," she answered. "But not the kind you said not to start with. It seemed real. Like a live thing."

He nodded. "You remember lots of small things tonight. A light is steady. A rope snubbed round a bitt doesn't look like it does in the books. The sea will ask the kind of favor we all must pay sometimes. We must be ready."

There were other nights of smaller rescue: helping a stranded dog out of cold water; advising the cannery on where to place a temporary net line so the packers had something to can before the end. In each case, he noticed Nora's hands, how they learned to tie a knot with a quickness that made old men nod. She learned not only the language of water but the grammar of small readiness.

The cannery's last day arrived with a brittle cold. There was a temporary energy in the air that had the smell of goodbyes and of a small, rare celebration. The town held a short ceremony on the pier. People made speeches about the work done for so long, a generous catalogue of names, a toast. The foreman spoke about the cannery's long service and the families that had turned its name into a kind of vocation. He said they had to accept a new economy. He thanked the town for its loyalty. For a moment the harbor held its breath and listened, like a person who had been touched by sympathy and then let go.

Nora stood near him, her glove tucked into a pocket. She had ink on the edge of her fingers from the map they'd drawn earlier. She was not small in the way she had been in December. There was now a steadiness in the line of her shoulders. Silas felt a kind of quiet pride that would not have been showy. Pride for the kind of inheritance people do not talk about: a way of addressing the world. He had not thought he would see his knowledge again placed like an offering, but he was glad it might be useful.

After the speeches, as the last machine slowed and the workers put on their coats, the cannery's light in one window remained a stubborn bright like a small lighthouse left on in apology. Men walked to their cars and climbed in, and there were hurried embraces. A woman named Margo, who had been there as long as the building had had corners, came up and squeezed his arm. "You taught my boy to trust the channel enough to come home," she said, and the sentence was heavy with things unspoken and then a small relief of saying.

After the ceremony, they went back to their jetty. The last shipment had left hours before. The cannery held its breath, and then the bell stopped. People drank and sang, but the sound had a hollow quality to it, like someone playing a piano with one hand and leaving the other motionless. Silas and Nora watched the water. The harbor lay dark and patient.

"Do you know why," she asked suddenly, looking at him, "we read the water the way you do? Is it just to keep boats safe?"

He took a long time answering. The surface had quieted into a mirror the color of pewter and soft iron. "It keeps us honest," he said at last. "It gives noise for the things that don't have it. Pilots steer between danger and need. We make allowances. But there's also the thing that comes after work is done — how you keep the story of a place so it doesn't get taken. Reading water is a way to keep a place from being just property. It's a way to hold the town in a language."

She thought about that and then, thoughtfully, asked, "Do you think someone can read land the same way?"

"I suppose so," he said. "But land is greedy. It takes stories and keeps them. Sea is leaky; it gives them back sometimes, and sometimes it asks you to listen twice."

She smiled in that lopsided way she had when the joke was private. "I like listening twice."

They stood in a silence that was not quite empty. The town hummed behind them. Somewhere a dog barked, a child put a hand to a window, a man hammered a pallet into a crate.

Nora did not say whether she would leave. She had a future that had not yet chosen her. She had the city in some distant cousin's advertisement and the harbor in the blood in her wrists. She had books and a teacher. She had a restless hope. Silas, who had sometimes thought the sea would be the last thing to have him, had the odd grief of outliving usefulness in certain ways and of not outliving his skill. He was older in the bones but not in the mind that read a channel.

As the winter leaned toward March, there were smaller battles to be fought. The harbor trust argued with the city about mooring fees. A developer who visited the town thought about converting the cannery into a restaurant or artist lofts; he walked the site with boots on and looked at the bones of the building and saw potential instead of people's livelihoods. Silas watched him patrol the cannery's skeleton with a smile that did not reach his eyes. The developer's words sounded like a foreign language to those who knew that the cannery, in its weirdly precise way, provided a structure of meaning that neither loft nor restaurant could replace. Nora heard the words and asked the developer think-polite questions; he seemed uncomfortable being questioned.

On a late March morning, the harbor lay like a cut gem. There had been a thaw, and warm brown water threaded through harbor mouth. Gulls argued in the thermals. Silas decided they could go out one last time. The season had drawn a line. They might still find fish to pack for some buyers, but the cannery would be closing, the men would be out of scheduled work, and the town would need to make new bargains with the future. Silas wanted to give Nora one more thing: the knowledge that a person could read an incoming tide by the smell of the air, by the way the hair on your arm rose when a current moved. He wanted to show her a place he had used to anchor ships in fog.

They moved by the smaller channels, the small islands that looked like sleeping beasts. He told her about a day in his youth when a mail steamer had vanished in a spring fog and how he had found it by listening to its belched exhaust. He told her about the first time his own father had taken him out and how the old man had pointed at a current like a finger and said, "Learn to expect what it will do and you'll never be surprised." Nora laughed at the sweetness of that belief. She kept her eyes on the water like someone who had just learned a secret about a friend.

They cut close to the cannery's side and watched as a young woman — not old enough yet to have a history of canning — walked the dock carrying a thermos. She stopped and dropped to sit on the edge and looked at the harbor the way a person might take leave of a friend who lives across a continent. "Don't be late," Ellen told her when she saw her daughter and Silas on the jetty. Ellen had a way of being direct that was mollifying. She was wrapped in a scarf though the sun had come out, and she moved with the long, efficient motion of someone who had learned to do a job with a minimal amount of language.

"Ready?" Silas asked Nora. She nodded, her cap crooked, the notebook in her bag, a small pencil tucked behind her ear.

They rowed out and found the seam he wanted to show her: a place where two currents met and made a thin, half-hidden ledge in the water. "Feel the swell here," he said. "Hear that sigh? That's where the bottom drops. Boats tend to hang if their keels aren't known."

She leaned forward and listened with her whole body. She felt the current tug the oars in her hands differently than thirty yards away. "It feels like a pulse," she said.

"Aye," he agreed. "Like a heart that left the shore and came back lighter."

They floated there and the sun struck the water and for one brilliant moment the harbor flashed like cut steel. Ships beyond the harbor came and went, small things with big hulls dragging the edge of the world. The cannery's roof reflected a bright, tired light. People spoke and their words drifted across the surface like small, ephemeral things. It seemed to Silas that this was what teaching was: placing someone in a small theater where everything mattered and then letting them carry the scene away.

"You could leave this shore," he said softly. "You could take these hours into a new place. Or you could stay and be a person the town can rely on to read the water. Or you could make up some way to be yourself in both."

"I'll read it," she said at last. "I'll read it sometimes with you, sometimes with someone else. But I'll know how to look. If a boat asks, I'll be able to say something." The decision hung in the cold air like a lit candle.

"Good," he said. He slid his hand into the wooden oar and smiled. "You know, the thing about the harbor — and don't tell anyone I said this — is that it likes company. It's not cruel. It teaches people to be careful, but it isn't unkind. People think the sea is either angel or devil. It's neither. It keeps its own counsel, and it lets you be as clever as you are."

They came back to the jetty as the sun dropped soft and round. The cannery's roof looked, from this angle, almost like a cathedral that had been repurposed. The last trucks had come and gone. Men were talking on corners about where they'd go and what they'd do. Children were playing stones on the beach. Someone had written a poem on a scrap of cardboard and nailed it to a post near the diner. It read, in a child's careful scrawl: We live here. The sea is ours and not ours. People laughed and felt a little less lonely for the admission.

When they reached the dock, Nora leaned her head back and looked at the sky. "What do people mean," she asked, almost to herself, "when they say 'read the water' like it's a book?"

Silas folded his jacket and touched the edge of her cap. "Books are for stories we can hold," he replied. "Water is for stories that move. You'll find that some stories want you to carry them along instead of understanding them all at once. That's the hardest lesson. You can read the edge of things, Nora, but the sea will keep the middle to itself sometimes. And that's all right. Sometimes it's good that not everything is known."

She nodded as if content with that. The sky had the thin, pale blue of early spring. The cannery's footsteps had trod a path to an end, and the town would recalibrate itself into something else. People would leave, some would stay, someone would buy the building and try to make art of it. Time would do what it always had: erode, accrete, alter the coastlines of memory. The harbor would keep its bones and offer them in slow motions to anyone who learned how to listen.

There were final tasks. Silas had a small logbook, and at the last he pulled it out and handed it to Nora. The leather was worn soft by the way he had carried it. "Take it," he said. "You may never keep full logs of ships, but it's good to have a place to write the things that matter."

She took it as if it were the last thing she'd been waiting for. She opened it and there, in the back, were entries he'd made years ago — small, precise notations about rips and shoals and the way the harbor had shifted in certain tides. She ran her finger over the words. "You're giving me your maps," she said.

"I'm giving you something else too," he said. "The willingness to look for the small things that make a big difference."

She hugged him then, unexpectedly, in the place where a small child and an old man imagine they might compress the world into a size they could manage. He felt her small arms fold like netting around him and knew she had taken the vital thing he had to pass. He did not know what she would do with it — whether she would stay and become a woman who could read water the way men had done for a century here or whether she would walk away and carry the harbor inside her like a relic. That was not his business. His business had always been to teach the map of the present and the use of old knowledge. She had learned it. That was enough.

The cannery closed its doors and hammered the last tin, and the town moved into the next chapter. Men found other jobs, women took other lines, children grew into other places. The harbor remained, and Silas and Nora walked the jetty often in the months after the closing. They watched the sea change with the seasons and with new patterns of boats. They taught other children, or simply sat and read the water as if it were a book that never repeated its sentences quite the same way twice. People came and went. The cannery, left quiet, held stories like a sleeping whale: vast, storied, and only partially known.

In the end, the last winter ended as winters always end, with a slow mud-suck of thaw and the scent of the sea turning from iron to green. Nora, having learned how the water asked for the kind of attention that was mostly humility, walked into her future like someone who knew she had the means to answer a call. Silas watched her read currents the way a reader holds a line and sometimes closes the book to live with the image for a while.

On a quiet evening they stood together on the jetty as the town's lights winked on. The cannery's silhouette hung, permanent and impossible to change at once. The harbor spread before them, normal as a pulse. Nora closed the leather logbook and tucked it under her arm. On the water a widow wave slid across the surface, and Silas watched it go and then come back in a different shape. He felt, with a kind of gratitude that warmed the iced places of his chest, that he had been given the permission to pass on the words the harbor taught him.

"Remember," he told her, "you never read the water alone."

"No," she said, and her voice carried the lightness of someone who had learned something necessary. "You read it with all the other people who know how to look and with the ones who didn't. You read it with the town, even when the town is quiet, and with the sea itself."

They stood together until the light bent the horizon and the first thin stars opened above the water. Behind them the cannery settled into its new silence, and the town retreated into its nights. Out on the sea the water kept speaking in the small, patient languages that had always been there. Silas felt, as if for the first time and the last, that the sorts of endings a person suffers are not, in fact, endings at all. They are only transitions in what will tell you the next thing to learn. He had taught one more person how to read the water, and in that giving there was a feeling of being made useful again. The harbor took their knowledge and returned it, as the sea does, in different forms. They listened, and the water told them its next sentence.
