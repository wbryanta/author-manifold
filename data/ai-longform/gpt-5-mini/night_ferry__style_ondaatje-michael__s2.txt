The ferry left like a promise retracting. The town dissolved behind us in a smear of sodium lights, a single lighthouse that blinked out of habit, then a low string of houses that clung to the shore as if prayerful. I stood on the outside deck with my palms tucked into the pockets of an old coat that belonged to my father, the collar turned up against the sea. The wind tasted of iron and old smoke, of diesel and fish and the medicinal brightness you find in hospital corridors. There was fog ahead, soft as wool, and the ferry's bow cut through it like a hand into dark fabric, revealing nothing but more sea and the thin white of its own wake. Somewhere on the other side of the strait there was a town sleeping on its ribs. Somewhere on the other side there was the place where they had found a body.

"You shouldn't be out here alone," said a voice, a woman with a scarf that had belonged to some distance. She was leaning against the rail, her hair pinned like a map. I said nothing because saying nothing was an office I had practiced. Her cigarette made a small, private star between us. She watched the horizon with the earnestness of someone cataloguing losses.

The crossing is a small ritual. You buy a ticket. You study the list of destinations printed in tiny type on the boarding pass and watch them darken as the ship leaves the pier. There is always a small, incomprehensible mixture of people: a man with a crate of flowers, a young couple who keep arching toward each other and then stopping short, an undertaker perhaps, but in this crossing tonight there was a Mariner with a wet cap, mud on his boots like ink on a page, a woman in a plastic parka holding a baby with its face asleep against her chest, a couple of truck drivers whose conversation barked and then softened. A boy, no older than sixteen, spoke to his phone aloud as if performing for us. I listened to the ferry's engines chord across the hull, the way steel sighs with the sea. It sounds like a throat clearing, regretful, patient.

I had carried a photograph in the inside pocket of my coat. It was creased, the edges worn to the color of old bone. I had taken it out twice already and looked at the faces as if they could arrange themselves into facts. The photograph was of two boys on a summer lawn, knees muddy, taken from a height that implied the photographer leaned forward with the patience of a stranger. My brother's face in that picture had the concentrated joy of someone tasting his first orange. He had sunlight braided into his hair and a cigarette dangling from his fingers long before he needed to know how to hold one. I put the photograph back, pushed my hands deeper into my pockets as if to press the hard paper against my chest where a single memory might warm it.

The body had been pulled from this strait — so the police report said — wrapped in a shirt, with seaweed braided into the cuffs like an improvised bracelet. The town on the other shore had called them: a fisherman had seen a shape in the grey at dawn, an absence that boiled, a looseness in the waves. The fishing boats had always been repositories of rumor and history and small tragedies. They have nets that catch more than fish. The word "may" had the heavy, slow weight of a bell. The officers had said it had features that might be his, an earring, a pronounced knuckle, a small plate of metal fixed under a gum — the sort of detail that could be true of many people and of one particular absent person simultaneously.

We had not spoken for years and yet no estrangement is absolute. It is a landscape with routines and that faint, inexorable traffic of things that pass between people: an owed apology that has taken up residence in the small chest of drawers; a postcard with a stamp you kept when you were young; a small thread of resentment that acts like a seam in clothing, holding all the rest together. The last letter I had from him was a single sheet of paper folded three ways and slipped under my apartment door in a season when heat made the city smell like an oven. He wrote: I'm leaving. I am sorry. He had drawn a small character in the margin, a crude sailor with a hat like a crescent. There was no return address.

That night on the ferry the sea and my memory began to occupy the same space. Memory is an illegal passenger — it has no ticket — and yet it moves easily between the decks. One moment I am being a man who must do a practical thing: identify a body; sign forms; say a name in a room lit for clarity. The next, I am a boy again — we were always being boys — splashing at the river's edge under the supervision of a mother who watched us with a kind of constant calculation, like someone who counts birds at a window. My brother taught me how to light a match, how to balance a creature on the tip of a twig; he taught me to steal apples, and to keep our hands out of scarf drawers where our father kept the small tools of his trade. He had lit a cigarette behind our house one winter when we were small and I remember thinking the flame was an animal he had made. He had a way of looking as if he was not certain which direction time moved, so he tried both directions at once.

The ferry rocked and the lights in the dining room were small planets of chlorinated yellow. I went below to get coffee. The coffee was a thin brown thought and a man with a face like machine oil smiled a toothless smile when I asked for two sugars. I held the cup as if it were an offering and there was a small paper napkin that folded the photograph. Men in rough coats sat and watched the horizon through windows with water trails like eyelids. A child drew the bridge on a tablet and then erased it. When you travel to identify a body you become aware, in a new and awkward way, of the machinery of endings: the forms that must be filled, the gloves that gloves that will be used to slide a finger under a cloth, the clinical detachment necessary for the profession to turn grief into documentation.

The crossing was long enough for me to invent various versions of what would happen. In one the body would be not his at all and I would feel relief as though I had been let out of a room where the walls had slowly been closing. In another the body would be his and the hospital light would flatten him until he looked smaller and more like the child in the photograph. In yet another the police would not let me in. They would say I did not have the papers, not enough of a legal tie, not a name that matched their records and the body would be given someone else to bury, or none at all. There is a particular cruelty to a legal tie when the human tie has been clipped and allowed to drift.

The boat docked at a quay that smelled of tar and cigarette ash. The town on the other side lay low and dank, roofs folded, chimneys stilled. You could see where the lights pooled and where the fog lived, like a drapery that someone had pushed at the edges and there it stuck. I walked off the ferry with a coordination that seemed to belong to someone else. My coat was heavy with damp. There was a light in my pocket, a small torch the colour of a children's book. I wanted to fold the photograph into it and let the flashlight spill over the faces so they would be visible without doubt. The police station was a white building that had the dignity of an old shoe. A woman at a desk asked me to sit, and I sat, letting the pleats of my trousers keep their shape. Coffee in paper cups came and went like prayers.

The man who found the body was small and spoke with the quickness of someone unaccustomed to waiting for words. He said that when they had pulled the body into the boat, it had had a ring on one finger, a plane ticket torn in half in one pocket, and the small, stubborn bundle of seaweed. The ring had an inscription of names that bent and glowed with other people's history. A woman in a raincoat had stood by the rail and called to the men: I know him, I saw him some weeks ago. They had the names of people he had known: his landlord, a woman in a shop, a man who sold rockets for festivals. All of these were facts that could be verified and yet the thing that defined him in the room that night was absence.

They took me to the cold room in a van that smelled of disinfectant and old rubber. The morgue is a place of applauded ugliness. It is opened on the hinges of necessity. They asked me to wash my hands with a kind of humility, to scrub the sound right out. They made me sign. The paper trembled like a leaf as my pen moved in a small but decisive arc. "Do you know his name?" one of the officers asked. I said the name and it sounded strange, not mine but the one my father had used when anger was being taught. There is always that echo of a name, how it sits in the mouth and how it feels when you say it aloud, like a stone you are carrying toward water.

The body lay on a stainless-steel table under a light that had the intensity of someone examining a small jewel. The room had a refrigeration whisper and the smell of iodine cut with something like the metallic whiff of old coins. They asked me if I would like to remove the sheet. The sheet was a thin white thing, folded in the certainty of its own function. They warned me that the body had been in the water. The water has a way of rewriting language; it softens the edges of facts. The man placed his hand on my shoulder and I felt gratitude for a hand that belonged to someone not entirely unknown. The sheet was lifted like a stage curtain. The throat makes a small, urgent noise when you are frightened; mine made such a sound.

He did not look like the photograph. Water does not only carry you; it rearranges you into someone else's geography. His cheeks were pale and the jaw had folded inward as if shyness had come to rest there. But there was the earlobe I had known, a slight split in its lower cartilage where he had once tugged a cat free from a knot and the cat had made a small, permanent protest. There was the missing front tooth he had always complained of — it had been replaced with a small metallic plate, an old memory that had been turned into metal, and in the mouth this gave me a different kind of proof. The hands had long fingers that curled slightly, like someone who had once carved small wooden birds. The knuckles bore the marks of a life of quick work and quick fights — a whiteness where the skin had been abraded and not a scar that map-makers could read. Most of all, he had a small sunburst tattoo at the base of his right wrist, ink faded as if it had been underwater before. That tattoo I had given him the summer before he left, a childish attempt at permanence: I had drawn it in his room with a cheap pen and then he had turned it into something more deliberate, a friend who would not up and go. Seeing it now, on a wrist that no longer moved, was both proof and accusation.

But there was also doubt. The eyes were clouded, a film over the lenses of recognition. I touched his hand, the skin yielding like a soft fruit. The fingers were cold. I had to imagine warmth into them. My own memories crowded into the room like unwanted relatives. I saw once more the two of us on a small porch, our mother humming a tune I had forgotten, and a cigarette between his lips. I remembered his laugh when the city turned summer and all the light fell into puddles. I remembered the argument that had pushed us apart, a word thrown like a stone, and then nothing — silence like folded laundry. Even with the markers — that earlobe, the tattoo, the small metallic tooth — there was a strangeness. My own face, suddenly, in the reflective stainless-steel, looked back at me old and younger and unkind.

They asked me if I wanted to see more. I did not. There are things that a body with slow certainty contains that a living person does not see until it is unfolded. The policemen offered a courteous explanation: sometimes it is not possible to confirm beyond the attributes; sometimes identity is an agreement rather than an objective truth. They brought out a small plastic bag with a coin and a ticket and a scrap of paper on which someone's handwriting said I owe you flour. The coin had the image of a ship that I recognized from a child's puzzle, the ticket had a torn edge that had a coffee stain. When you have lived with someone you can believe that small items are like fingerprints, but small items can be planted. There was a photograph in the pocket of the body, a man without moustache, his eyes oddly light, perhaps a cousin, perhaps a stranger. I could have called the names on the scrap of paper and perhaps the chain would have closed, links made of telephone calls and addresses and evidence. But in the cold room with its mercifully indifferent light, all telephones felt like instruments that had the wrong tone.

I stood by the table until the attendants wanted to close the case, to wrap the body once more and to resume the small domestic things that make death manageable. The paper in which they would fold someone expands and then becomes a place for the shape to return to silence. I wanted to say yes to claiming him but also there was a part of me that wanted to say no. To claim is to begin again with obligations you have been purposely avoiding; to say no is to allow the world to choose a stranger for him. You cannot be the only heir to absence. Being the man who says yes is a becoming of someone else.

When I touched his wrist, the skin under my fingers felt like a map I could read only in the dark. I thought of our mother in her bed, of the way she kept jars of buttons like a private religion. That memory was as heavy and as soft as a jar on a shelf. She used to say to us, in her peculiar grammar that cut the once-known words into new shapes, "Do not go away as though nothing's missing." I had gone away. He had gone away. We had both gone away.

In the end I said the name that would sign him into records, into a place on a form that will make him a something, legally. The word left my mouth like a little coin dropped into a wishing well. The paper awaited my signature. Men with quiet hands wrapped the sheet back over him. They suggested that I retrieve the objects found in his pockets — the ticket, the coin, the photograph — which I did. The small photograph was of a woman with a cap. There was a note in a handwriting I did not recognize. I kept the coin in my pocket. The tattoo would be photographed. I asked if they would let me have the watch. He had always worn a watch his father gave him the day he graduated from a school that counted in a foreign tongue. The watch had stopped, I learned, at 2:13, a detail I could have done without. The men looking at me had some experience at this; they knew which questions to ask and which to let die under the sheet.

Outside, the town had a color you get when a bruise is asleep. A gull followed the van as if it had contract with the van to locate a thing that had been forgotten. The police offered a ride back to the ferry; their shift would be ending and they would be pleased to be done. I declined, walking the streets with neither a map nor a plan. You cannot carry a dead person as you carry a living: you do not pack them in anything created for trips. You can, however, carry them in objects. I put the coin in my mouth between my teeth for a second the way a child might tuck an angel into a secret. It was salt-worn. When I spat it out it made a small sound like something ending and beginning at once.

I thought of the last time I had seen him in person. It was in a bar on the other side of the city, a room that smelled of oil and frying onions. We had sat with our backs to the wall as if what was behind us might reach us. We had drunk and not spoken; our silence in that room was loud. He had left a single unfinished beer on the table. When I left and turned for the door, I saw him stand, and then go out the other way, choosing the city, choosing the back streets, choosing loss. Sometimes the choice of not choosing is a choice in itself. In rooms where conversation is as much about the small noises as the content, there were sentences that bent toward reconciliation like saplings toward light; we never let them fully open. This is not to blame him alone. I left, too.

There is a sequence of images that returned to me with the insistence of memory: the sound of his laugh when we sat on the steps of the old bakery and he ate two doughnuts at once; his attempt at modesty when a girl he liked smiled at him; the way he had learned to fold paper boats and name them after animals, how the boats always sank in the right order. There was also the fight over the window where he had once carved our initials with a pocketknife because he thought we should make the house remember us; our father had found the knife and disciplined him in a way that left both of us shaken. The reason for our estrangement was a mosaic of small things strung into an accusation. It was neither all one person's doing nor simple to name. It had the complicated geometry of family.

I told no one immediately. There is a superstition about death and speech: to announce a body might be to own it. The ferry's timetable hung in my pocket like a calendar turned toward the winter. The crossing back would be at dawn. I thought about how to arrange the body to a place called home, how to tell our mother. The thought of telling her made my chest tighten because her grief would have to become something I could see and this would be new. She lived in a house where the light filtered like gauze. She kept things neat the way a widow organizes light. She had not known where to put her time and now she would have to place it into some new container.

Back on the ferry for the return crossing the sea had taken on the small, secret colors of nighttime. The town receded like an absent parent. As the deck lights hummed, a man with a guitar played a tune that had been made for leaving. His song made small concessions to the fact we would all get where we were going in time. People clapped politely; the baby in the parka woke and cried and then slept. I sat on a bench with the coin in my pocket and the watch tickless like a heartbeat paused. The photograph was folded and returned to its place in my pocket where it had the quality of a kept thing.

I thought how bodies are catalogued and how even now, when I had been the man asked to verify, I also felt like a witness in someone else's diminutive theater. Sometimes with grief you are assigned a role: the stoic brother, the man of few tears, the person who will tell the neat stories; sometimes you are not assigned anything and you have to make your part by noticing small details. The ferry rocked. In a sudden gust a newspaper page tore and slapped an arm like a wind-bent bird. People shuffled and settled as if they were bookends returning to their place.

When we cleared the channel the horizon showed a thin red line where dawn was making its first small noise. The light took a clean edge of the world and then softened it; things that had been shadowed found new shapes. I thought of my brother's face but refracted through time as if seen through a glass that bends images: there was what he had been and what he might have been and what now he would never be. I considered the coin and how it had been stamped with a ship that faced forward and that perhaps, in the end, the sea had simply done what the sea does: it rearranges, it erases, it saves things in its own slow way.

On the deck a woman rolled a cigarette and handed me one. We did not exchange names. The cigarette fit my hand as if it had been waiting for me. I held it to my mouth, inhaled, and felt the small violence of the nicotine cut by the salt of the air. The smoke wrote small letters in the fog. I thought of the things you inherit by default: your father's hands, your grandmother's habit of ironed linen, a name that will feel like both identity and accusation. There are inheritances you ask for and those the sea decides.

When I finally told my mother — I drove back through the thin morning with the radio like a small living organism — she was standing at the door with a kettle already on, as if the day had not yet decided its first move. She asked me if I was cold and then said nothing at all when I told her. She sat down and folded her hands. There are kinds of silence that are a skill. She did not ask for the details. She listened and at the end she made a sound that was less a word than a small negotiation between her lungs and the rest of her body. She asked me if I had signed. I said I had and she nodded as if some ancient ledger had been updated.

We went to the funeral home when the world had warmed to a pale morning. There were flowers in the reception that looked too clean to be alive. A man in a suit that had seen better years offered his condolences with a voice that moved in slow syllables. The body would be brought from the cold room. I thought of the steel table and the light that did not flatter. I wondered about the line between being practical and being unthinkably cruel. The people who do this work have a faith in apportionment. They have a belief in the small usefulness of steps arranged like a ladder: paperwork, burial plot, casket. They explain to you with a kindness that is almost a ritual how things will proceed. The body, whether you call it yours or not, must be clothed and compressed into a space that has a lid. The dead become, in their own sort of funeral, more efficient.

Our mother touched the photograph in my pocket — it had cooled with the night — and unfolded it. She looked at the boys for a long time. Her lips moved around the name as if testing pronunciation. "He would have liked a boat," she said. She remembered the tiny paper boats we had made and how he had given his to a neighbor's daughter so she would not be alone in the street. She said that when she woke at night she often imagined they were both asleep at the river and that she would go out and find them with a lantern. I thought: this is perhaps the radical act of maternal hope, to imagine one has the map to the absent.

I had to be the person who did the legwork: the registrar, the man who would sign the deeds to place him under a stone. But when it came to the actual part where I had to touch him one more time, I found myself wanting to do something else: to gather his clothes and fold them as our mother used to fold linens, to leave a cigarette in the pocket like a small secret, to place the photograph under his tongue like an offering. I was a man arranging a few small objects in a life that would now be presented as a set: a casket, a date, a list of people to call. My mother and I stood by his body and I saw how she was not the woman who had taught us the names of things only to watch them go; she was someone attempting to gather a life into a small shape so that it would be easier to carry.

When the day came — it had the pale light of a metal coin pressed to the skin of the earth — people arrived with condolences and with reasons. A cousin came who had a face like slow weather, and a neighbor who had known him in the years before he left and who spoke of him with an intimacy that rubbed against my own. At the graveside I watched as the man with the coffin lowered the body into the ground with the gentleness of a craftsman laying down a fragile instrument. There was soil like wet clay, there was a sound from the crowd like a small fabric being torn. I placed the photograph on the casket and I thought for a moment that perhaps photographs were not only to keep the living remembered but to make the dead look like something definite.

After, when everyone had gone and the grave had been tamped so it seemed almost indifferent, my mother and I sat on the steps and drank tea. The tea was too hot and we sipped in small measures. I finally spoke. I said the small lettered confessions: how I had left the way one leaves a room in school, without the courage to gather shoes; how when he had gotten into trouble I had not been there; how sometimes it had felt like I had been a man in training to be absent. She listened. She did what she had always done when we were small: she said a practical thing, a piece of sewing for a wound. She told me that absence takes up space and that sometimes you must put your hand into that space and say his name until the shape of it is known. It was not advice, not exactly. It was the way a person teaches another to remember.

On the ferry going back there was an enormous sky and wind that made the rail sing. I stood with my hands braced as if I were attempting to prevent some larger thing from tumbling. I had with me the coin, the photograph, the watch. The coast moved past in the ordinary way of land: it kept its angles to itself. I did not know whether what I had done was right. I had been asked to identify and I had. I had done what a stranger might do when handed a thing that sits at the intersection of law and grief: I had agreed. The sea passes on what you give it; sometimes it gives something back. I wanted him to return in some other shape: a line on a map, a sound, the warmth of the seat he had once occupied, the stain of beer on a table. Instead the sea had given me a day in which I folded things and an afternoon in which the world had the quiet of new settlement.

The strait is a thin seam between things. It is not, in any honest way, a boundary so much as a place where two things rub against each other and become different. Standing at the rail, looking back, I felt the two of us as a faint geography: the years we had been together like a single field, then the moment of friction, the rift, and then the long slow weathering. Grief is an element that works slowly; the edges of it will round with time. The coin burned in my pocket as if it contained an identity I could spend.

A ferryman dressed in navy moved past and handed me a cigarette lighter with a small scratch made by frost. I accepted it. The lighter had belonged to no one I knew. I flicked it and a small flame lived like a private sun. I thought of the last time we had shared a flame; we had both been too young to think this would be the thing that held us. Perhaps in the end we were not meant to be the same thing that began together. There is comfort in finality, strange as that sounds, but there is also the residue of what you could have been.

When the town came into view I thought of all the small mercies we have: the particular angle light makes on the inside of a cup, the way a mother folds a child's clothing, the dumb brightness of a child who believes in everything. I had not expected that the identification would lay me down like a shirt, pressed and ordered. It did not. It rearranged my living. I felt lighter and heavier at once. The sea behind us closed up like a book. The ferry's engines thrummed a mechanical benediction.

On the dock I felt the photograph in my pocket and the coin. I let the coin fall into the water between my shoes and watched it sink; the water took it with a small, decisive sound. It is a superstitious thing to throw away a token, but perhaps I was paying someone. I placed the photograph back into my coat and buttoned the collar against the wind. There are no good endings, only arrangements. I did the practical things I had agreed to do and the others I left; I did not ask for absolution.

I have come home with a kind of weather in me. Our mother called at night and said she had dreamed of the two of us sitting in a boat that did not sink. I told her I would come by tomorrow with the watch, which I had placed on my mantel like a slow, mute clock. At home, when the apartment smelled of boiling water and a small luminous stain of the television, I placed the photograph on the table. It seemed to look back with a patience that has no requirement to explain why.

The sea is stitched into me now in a new way. My brother sits in a grave that smells of turned earth and aftersoil. He is a name on a piece of paper and a small tattoo that will be photographed and recorded. He is a series of memories that will be unlocked with the key of a coin or the motif of a laugh or the way a cigarette smoulders in a winter room. Our silence was a shape we both learned to inhabit; I am learning now how to speak into it. I will not pretend the wound is closed. We are all, to some degree, passengers on the slow ferry of human decisions, moving between ports with the things we've chosen to carry and the things the sea decides to return. I do not know whether the man I saw under the sheet was wholly him or an arrangement of marks that looked like him. I know only that I said his name aloud in a cold room and that it cupped the sound of an earlier life.

That night, I smoked on the landing outside my mother's house and watched the street under the light's halo. My breath made small ghosts. The world felt slightly rearranged but also stubbornly the same. The ferry, the strait, the small white hospital sheets: they were all, in their way, languages for loss. My brother's absence has the dimensions of a room; sometimes I stand in the middle of it and move my hands to see how the furniture shifts. At times I think I can walk through into some other room and find him there, reading a paper or carving a small bird. At other times I know it is more likely I will find only the echo of a laugh, a coin, a photograph. That, for now, will have to be enough.
