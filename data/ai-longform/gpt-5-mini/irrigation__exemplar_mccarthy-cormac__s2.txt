The orchard lay long and low beneath a June sky that had no mercy. Rows upon rows of trees like a regiment in retreat, their leaves fevered, their trunks furred with the pale scab of old fire, their fruit buds swollen and then hollowed by the heat. Between the rows the ground was a map of fissures. Where there had once been a green hush there was a kind of rattle, as if the place itself had been stripped of language and left to cough out stones.

They came in by the lane that used to be gravel and had become more dust than anything else. The truck made the sound of something that remembered the road, a long slow complaint, and left a cloud that settled back over the land like breath. The elder brother sat with his hand on the wheel and watched the trees go by with a hollow patience. The younger leaned his elbow on the sill and squinted at a distant line of mountains that showed like a knife's edge. Neither said anything for a while. The sun was a coin. Their shadows ran in behind them.

They had come to mend a thing as old as whatever had been mended here for generations — a buried pipe that fed these trees their stolen life. It was a line of metal and rubber and joints that had been laid when their father had still thought to outwit the country's lean years. It had been laid with his hands and his curses when the ground was forgiving. Time had made of it more a story than a utility. This June the story was failing.

You take the valve, said the elder. Or you can hand me the wrench. He kept his voice small, like a man drawing a circle around his patience.

I'm not your hand, the younger answered. His jaw worked under the sun. He had the look of a man who had been given an impossible ledger and was trying to learn the sums by feeling the numbers. He swung his feet out of the truck and stood in the dust, boots scuffing. Give me the map. Tell me what you want.

I know what I want, said the elder. We need to get it to the west row by dusk. The pump ain't holding and if we don't get the line patched they'll be dry tonight. He said it without heat. His mouth was a straight furrow. His arms were lean with work. He was older than the younger by enough years that he carried them as weight rather than as story.

You want to stitch up the past, the younger said. Or you want to keep a ghost alive for the price of your own living. He spat a little dust. They had the same face in a way that betrayed the common hard work, but in the younger it sat sharper, as if the person had been whittled to a point.

Don't come at me with that, said the elder. We ain't arguing now. We argue at night at the kitchen table and waste the words. Today we fix the pipe. He walked with the slow surety of a man who had learned a landscape’s way of telling when to hurry and when to let the day pass. He had a canvas cap; the younger had a hat brim already turned pale at the crease.

They went to the pump house. It was a small building of boards the color of a dried bone, the door hanging on one hinge. Inside the smell of oil and old rope and something like the long ago of the house. Tools lay out on the bench in a stern geometry. A battered manual lay open where their father had read it in the nights after the lights had gone out. The elder bent and worked the pump's casing with the practiced hand that comes from repeating the same small surgery until it becomes second sight.

The younger knelt and unrolled the coil of pipe they'd brought and measured. He had the tape and the knife and his fingers were quick in a way that forgot the husk of the day. Every now and then he glanced up at the elder, as though seeking some undisputed law to guide him.

You ever think about selling, he asked then, as if the idle question might be a tool like any other.

Used to, the elder said. You know used to is a dangerous adjunct to think. He did not look up from the pump. Used to think about a lot of things. About what they'd do with the money. About the house in town they'd promised themselves. Used to think about leaving like men that had not had the weight of this place in their bellies. But used to don't fix broken metal.

I'm talking now, the younger said. Now's when we're getting old enough where now matters. He set the pipe down and pushed a sprig of grass aside with the toe of his boot. You ever think about what it would be like to have the money instead of the trees? To not be counting the days for the pump?

The elder's hands paused. He looked at the younger then and for a long measure of breaths read him like a field that had been walked over. You could sell it, he said. You could sell it and buy whatever town life promises, a place with men who don't look at the sky the way we do. He smiled without humor. You could sell it and the land'd still be here the same or not here at all. Money don't conjure rain. It buys shelter but not the size of the night.

You don't get it, the younger said. You think I ain't tired of the rows and the harvesters, of watching what my hands do while the months wash away. You think I want to water a dead orchard because it's tradition. He laughed once, brittle. Tradition don't feed you when there's no crop. Tradition don't pay for schooling if you're the kind of man that thinks there might be a different life.

The elder wiped his hand on his pants and the motion made more dust fall out of the day. The brush of his thumb across a bolthead made a small ringing sound like a bell in a distant church. You speak like a man with a map and no memory, he said. Selling the place ain't just a matter of price. It's a thing you live out with. We ain't only selling trees. You're selling the things that named us. Friends that gave us work, the creek that we swam in, the house that held our mother. He struck the wood of the bench with his palm and it sounded hollow in a way that made him think of their father's chest.

You make it holy, the younger said. You dress it in a language of ghosts. For you it's memory. For me it's sorrow. The orchard's failing, Abel. These trees ain't coming back. We throw our time into fixing a pipe and the crop is bled by the weather and the worms. There's a buyer down in the valley. He'll pay well. He'll put up a fence and build terraces and he knows machines that can coax money out of a landscape that only remembers drought. He'll give us a number and call it clean.

Names, the elder said. Don't call me that when you're trying to sell me a thing. He had his own anger now, slow like heat. He'd been the one who'd taken the oaths after their father. He'd been the one who'd promised the old man he'd not let the line run dry. But promises are made of the same air that makes dust. The younger watched and then looked away to the trees. He had a softness now in him — a patience for the why of things that was a little like mercy.

When their father died they read out the will like they were performing a ritual older than the law. The land was divided in part to the sons, a clause that assumed continuance and didn't account for a climate that had been taking counsel with itself and decided to forget them. They had the land and the machines and a debt that fitted on the shoulders of both like a collar. There were notices from the bank with the same sunny brevity as a funeral card.

You don't remember Dad at the ditch when the pipe first went in, said the younger. He remembers more than you give him credit for, come on. He remembers his hands turning the spade, the cursing of the dog when it dug out what had been buried. He remembers the way he made us stand and watch him measure and cut so that we would know how to do it ourselves. He taught us the bones of the place.

The elder's jaw worked. He remembered. He had the memory like an ache beneath the ribs. You had your reasons, Jonas, to be restless. I had my reasons to stay. The orchard offered a kind of claim on the world that would've been stupid to abandon. It offered daily truth. You either ate of it or you starved. He set the wrench down and reached for the pipe. The younger watched him but he did not move to take it.

Between them the land waited. They dug where the pipe had failed and the earth came away quick and hard. It smelled dry and animal and the old topsoil, what little remained, clung to their gloves. When they exposed the break it was worse than they'd feared: a joint pinched by corrosion, a seam ripped as if from the teeth of some metal foreigner. Water, when it had run, had eaten the soil around it and the roots that had been afraid of light were now open. The elder knelt and examined it. He had done this before. He knew where to lay the patch, what clamps might be coaxed to close a thing that once had been whole. He chose parts from their kit like a surgeon picking bone.

You know we could cash out and be rid of mornings like this, Jonas said when the elder fitted a sleeve over the seam and tightened the bolts in a slow cadence. He looked at the work as if it were a measure of their lives. What would you do with the money, he asked. Maybe I'd buy a place in town where a man don't have to measure out his food, he said. Maybe I'd go see the ocean. I never have seen the ocean.

He lowered his head and the sun drew a line across his cheek like a life slash. The elder looked up and for a moment the land seemed to consider their faces. He had his reasons. He had on him an obstinacy that took its root in the very bone of the place. My father left it to us, he said. Not in words only. He left his sweat and his shame and his joy. We keep it because some things deserve to be kept even when the world says let them go. It was like a liturgy the elder had learned and couldn't unlearn.

The younger laughed and it was a small broken sound. People kept things for reasons all the time, he said. Some of them were good and some of them were pride. You're not doing this for him, he said. You're doing this so you can sleep with your conscience. I could sell and buy a life that isn't stitched to a failing orchard. Why would you hold to dirt if it means hunger?

Hunger, the elder said. Hunger shapes a man's face, he said. It sits on him and names him. What you're calling hunger is a fear of what might be if we stay. Maybe that's nobler than what you're selling. The elder struck the bolts again with the palm of his hand and the metal sang.

You always make it sound like an altar, Jonas said. He had the knack for mockery that was gentler than cruelty. He set the last clamp and stepped back. Let it be said, he said, that I did my part for the orchard. He wiped his hands on his pants and watched the elder raise the little crank and set water through the line. For a moment the pipe hissed and then the pump took hold and the water came cool and blue against the dry air and the dust tasted like nothing.

They stood and watched the water run along the furrow. It made a small silver question of itself between the trees and the trees drank in the wet like a congregation. At the far end a leaf that had been shriveled uncurled and new sap rose like a small light. The younger breathed out as if having been surprised.

You think that proves anything? he asked.

It proves this one thing, the elder said. Right now the trees have water. Right now they might hold another month. Right now a thing that could die will for a while live. But it do not change the ledger. You can't argue a promise with a pump. We can hold on and maybe the rains come and maybe we sell at a better price in a year's time, or we can sell now and take the money and do other things. The way I see it, half measures and hope is expensive.

There was a silence. The orchard sang with insects. Shadows performed the slow rule of evening, lengthening. A hawk moved over the field like authority. The younger knelt and let his fingers touch the warm stone of the furrow edge. He had the small hum of a man who was measuring his own heart. You ever think about leaving this to the kids? he asked.

Leave it to nobody, the elder said. Who would we leave it to? Our cousin down in the county is in trouble with his wife. The neighbor's boy left years ago and took to the road. The younger looked up and there was a part of his face that showed a man in the grip of deeper loneliness. He had not married. He had no progeny to inherit burdens or bread. He had always thought that maybe he'd go to the city and learn a new language.

My daughter, the elder said then, not with any pride but the way a man mentions what remains. He said it like an offering. You forgot I had her. She lives in the next county. She works in that packing plant. She calls sometimes. He paused. She has a notion sometimes that the place might be like a memory enough for children. But children, he added, are not made of buildings and orchards. They are made of things you can't hand down in a deed.

The younger looked at him as if the earth had been holding a secret and had now let a small piece of it go. You never told me she was your daughter, he said.

You never asked, the elder said. He had the habit of closing the space between them with facts rather than speeches. It's not that he wanted to hide; it's that each had their own closet where their things were folded. You could speak of it and it would change nothing. The pump kept working. The water ran and the trees drank. Night would come whether they decided to sell or keep.

After they ate a cold supper of bread and cheese in the tailgate of the truck they walked the rows and looked at the fruit that dared itself into color. A few pears had the green tinge that meant they might swell to harvest, a few apples had the same thin hope. Mostly it was a place between hope and ration. They moved slow, speaking in the short sentences of men who have been bound to the same task since boyhood.

You ever think about the people who come here to work, the younger said. He had watched them in recent seasons, temporary hands who learned to pick and prune, who offered a foreign laughter that brightened the days. They always left at the end of harvest. The orchard had a registry of departures.

People come and they go, the elder said. The land keeps its own company. But there's something about this place that holds to a kind of stubbornness. It don't go gentle to whatever it's dying of.

The younger scoffed. You keep using words like stubbornness the way men use cloth on a wound. It don't make the wound better.

They sat on the bank where the irrigation spill ran out into the open and watched the water scatter in the dusk. There was a bug that walked a path along the wet edge and then leaped to the shine where the water pooled. The sound of the pump was a steady hand on a shoulder. Above, the first stars came sharp as errors. The younger canned a cigarette and lit it and the small light was a dark jewel.

So, he said. He had been circling his decision like a hawk circles a field. If we sell, would you come? He looked at him then. Would you take your share and move?

The elder's face was a map of places he had been and refused to go. He didn't answer at once. He thought of the house with its one crooked stair and the cupboard that remembered him as a boy. He thought of mornings when they had sung, two voices imperfectly matched, in a kitchen that smelled of coffee and boiled fruit. He thought of his father's hands on the plow when the sun had not yet been a scourge. He thought of his daughter, and how at the packing plant she watched bellies with the hard eyes of someone who had learned how to hold loss. He thought of the orchard as a thing that had taken them and given them back in equal measure.

I don't know, he said. Can't tell you that I can. There's a thing here that speaks to me. But I ain't so proud I won't go if there's something better. If it was only me I'd stay. But if you see a path and it makes a man out of you to go, I can't be the man to stand in the way of that. He reached and squeezed the younger's shoulder, and it was a small human contact that felt like treaty.

You say that now, Jonas said. He looked at the elder with the sudden tenderness of one who had always held a grievance like a cherished coin. But if I sell the place and you stay will you resent me for that? Will you look at the house every night and see me walking away with a sack of bills? He had the keen of a man who feared betrayal as the end of any love.

I ain't built for to resent my brother, the elder said. Resentment is heavier than the work. He would rather toil. But if you leave with the money and you go and you find something that makes a man of you then be my brother in the other country. He had been born to a law of upkeep. It would not suit him to take a man to task for the love of his own life.

They argued in words and the words were thin armor. They called the things by names: pride, hunger, duty, promise. They took their parts like actors at the end of a long rehearsal. The night folded them and the orchard breathed. Insects made a kind of litany. The pump hummed its small metallic benediction. A dog barked somewhere that could be nothing at all and everything at once.

We could do a trial, Jonas said at last. We could do a trial run. Put it on the market and see what comes. Be honest about it. Let someone give us a number and then decide. He had the sense of compromise, the willingness to hedge a bet with both hands. It would be like opening a door partway and seeing what comes through.

The elder considered the idea and the silence took its time. The trade of truth for uncertainty. He might be able to live with such. You sure you can live with a half step, he asked.

For a while, the younger said. Until I know what else is out there. He did not sound sure.

They had a neighbor once who had sold a swath of his land to a stranger who made a resort where once there had been a pasture. The man had taken his money and moved to a town and the children that had stayed behind learned to look at the resort and then stared away. The memory of that neighbor sat between the brothers like a picture of the future.

The elder nodded slowly. He had the look of a man making peace with a strategy that wasn't his first line. All right, he said. We put up a sign and we see what comes. But we do not list the place until you and I both agree on a price. We put a figure and we sit by it like men who have learned not to be bled.

Jonas grinned, a quick slash. He had the relief of a man who'd expected a retreat and found a parley. You mean it? he said.

I mean it, the elder said. But know this, Jonas. If we sell you got to accept some things are gone. They will not come back because you bought a house in the city where men walk past pictures and don't look at a horizon. You will have bought a life. But you will have also made a hole in this place that some other man will fill.

A hawk that had been circling beyond the line of trees struck down and rose with something small, and the brothers watched the dream of it. The air cooled and the first of the pear moths rose like pale moths of prayer. The pump kept its even mouth and the water went on speaking to the furrows.

They drove into town that night to put a note in with the broker who had once expressed an idle curiosity at the fence line. The broker's office was a rectangle of fluorescent and paper. He offered them his mannered smiles and a spool of figures. He spoke of trends like a man reciting a sermon he didn't believe in. He rubbed his chin as if the beard might hide the fact that he was counting men and not trees. He talked about possible subdivisions and a developer who liked the land's aspect and a price that would settle some notes.

On the way back in the dark the younger asked the elder if it felt like betrayal. He couldn't keep the sound out. It was the kind of question whose answer might shatter something. The elder turned the wheel and the road took them home. He did not answer at first. He had a small sadness now that wasn't anger but the more dangerous sense of resignation. He thought of his father teaching him to fell an apple tree and then graft another in its place. He had believed in the possibility of renewal, and now renewal itself seemed to be a conversation with absence.

Betrayal, he said. It depends on who's looking. If the land is dying and you shore it up for a while you might be saving nothing. If you sell it's like closing a book no one will open again. There are worse sins. There are worse mercies.

When they reached the orchard it was shortly after midnight and the sky hung full of stars like suspended promises. They walked the rows with a flashlight until the beams washed across fruit and leaf and the little glistening wet where the water took in. They both felt the old quiet come down upon them like a benediction. For a moment it seemed that money and market and plan and ledger were small things under the night.

I don't want to lose you, Jonas said then. Not the place only. I don't want to lose the kin of us, the tie of us. It was a small tremor in his voice that made the elder stop. He had thought of that too. Sale is a thing that can cost more than coin.

You won't, said the elder. He sounded older than he had earlier. We will be what we were by choice or by habit. The word came to him slow and full: family. He had long ago loved that word enough to spend it like coin. He had no appetite to throw it away.

They sat for a while on the tailgate where the metal couldn't warm any more and looked upon the orchard. The water in the furrow glinted like a line of thought. Somewhere a cricket argued with the night. The land went on. Men can choose, but choice lays upon the earth like a quiet. It does not absolve wrongs nor does it promise absolution. It is only and purely a crossing.

When they left in the morning it was with a small list of measures. They would call the broker. They would take some bids. They would set a price that meant neither of them would be made poor by haste. They would tend the orchard with what vigor they had and not let it go to shame while options were weighed. The elder had made a bargain with himself: he would not let habit become the only god of his days.

They worked that day until the sun made their backs like iron. They replaced valves and couplers, flared lines and coaxed the pump with belts pulled tight. They drank from a canteen that tasted of all the years they'd worn it and let the sky be the judge. They didn't come to conclusions any more than the field comes to them; they negotiated with small mercies. At noon they sat under the one shade tree and broke bread and said nothing for a time.

If we sell, Jonas said finally, will you come to the city with me? He had the bravado of someone who speaks of change like a dare.

If I come it'll be only for a look, the elder said. He smiled. The city makes men small in different ways. He liked his hands callused. He liked the dirt under his nails. But he didn't want to dictate the younger's life. If you go and you need me I will come. He put his hand over Jonas's and the touch was a warm thing in the hot noon.

A week later there was a letter and then another and a man with pleasant manners who walked over their land as if it were a business and not a sorrow. He offered them a check and a schedule and spoke on behalf of the future in a tone that made him sound like a prophet. They met at the kitchen table with paper and coffee and the elder read the offer and Jonas watched his face with a hunger that was part shame and part relief. The number was many times what they had in the bank and might pay all the debts and leave a margin.

You sure? the elder asked. He had the look of a man who weighs gold and thinks of the cost of its custody.

Jonas met his eye and there was no certainty in it, only that raw thing men mistake for resolve. I'm sure enough, he said. I can't promise I won't think of this place later. I can't promise I won't come back sometimes and sit under the pear tree and remember. But I need the chance. I'd rather be sorry for giving up than sorry for never taking it.

They set the price and they told the broker to set the wheels in motion. The days following were like a river where stones are shifted and the water goes on no matter the rock. There were visits from city men with clipboards and girls who measured sun patterns and a lawyer who smoked cigars in the guestroom. The elder found he could not sleep some nights and when he rose he would stand in the dark and rub his hands on his pants and feel the orchard breathe.

On the final night before the papers were signed the two brothers walked the orchard together. The moon was a pale coin and the trees stood like bowed heads. They walked their hands across bark and leaf as if blessing. They spoke little. At the gate Jonas stopped and turned. He had the look of a man who had been given a gift and had to decide how to receive it.

Will you come to the closing? he asked. He watched the elder's face.

I will come, the elder said. I'll be there to see the paper signed. And if at that moment I find a part of me that can't let go, then it will be because the orchard claimed me and not because you denied me of anything. He had the grace to say what he felt without bitterness.

They drove into town the next morning. The lawyer explained things that sounded like a liturgy of commerce and read at length of easements and covenants and the nature of possession. Their hands shook when they signed because there were words to be indemnified and promises to be severed. The younger forked through the cash and held a figure that would make a man carried by fantasy. He thought of what he could do and what he would buy: a narrow house with a window to the street, maybe a class at night school, maybe a trip to the sea. He felt the future like a tide.

When the papers were done the elder walked outside and he went to the orchard one last time with his cap in hand. He sat on the fence and listened to the wind make its small music among the rows. The new owner would take his place with plans and promises. The land will change and it will not change. Trees will be cut, terraces maybe put in, fences may rise. Memory will not be for sale. It will live in whatever men remember. He thought of his father and felt the old man's palm on his cheek in an imagined warmth.

Jonas came out and put his hand on the elder's shoulder. He had his eyes like a man who had given away part of himself and received his life back. I'll be back, he said. For visits. For the pear jam. For the way the light hits the eastern row in June. He spoke the way a penitent confesses and the elder nodded and the two stood there on the edge where what was and what will be met like two rivers.

They did not part then as enemies. They parted like two men who had made a covenant and accepted its cost. The younger left with a sack of bills and a headlight's cut of hope. The elder remained until the trucks came and the new men measured and the old pumps were hauled away. The last night he slept on the porch of the house and woke to the sound of distant engines. Morning found him in the orchard watering a single row as if to mark the place where his father had taught him. He took a length of hose and threaded a dribble to a single tree and watched the leaf tremble and in that trembling he heard all the talk and all the silence that had ever sat between brothers.

Later, after the land was changed, after the fences rose and the terraces made new shapes, there were pears that fell like small moons in another season and children who did not know the sound of their grandfather's boots. The elder came sometimes in the cool dawns to walk between the trunks and listen to whatever passage had been left. The younger would come too, from time to time, when his work allowed, and they would stand and watch the trees and the place they had been and would be again under a different sun.

Men choose and then undo things by living them. There was not a one final answer. The orchard taught them that the only just reckoning is the one you keep with the day: repair what is breakable, sell what is finished, and when you can do neither, sit in the shade and let the water run where it may.
