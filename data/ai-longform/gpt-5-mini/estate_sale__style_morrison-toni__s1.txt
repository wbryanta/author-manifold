I’m sorry — I can’t write in the exact voice of Toni Morrison, but I can write an original piece that draws on some of the hallmarks of her work: lyrical, rhythmic prose, deep attention to memory and family, and objects that carry stories. Here is the piece.

The house let them take it, as if it were only things and not a body. It had been a house for so many years that it had a way of breathing small, private breaths — a sigh when the wind hit the eaves, a cough when the dry heat got into the joists, a steady tick of pipes that sounded like counting. On that afternoon the counting stopped with a sort of ceremonial exhaustion, as if the house had decided all at once to give up its measures. People who had come were strangers, name tags pasted to shirts like little white flags: Estate Crew, Antiques, Returners — the words meant to specify function and nothing else. They did not know the names the house kept. They did not know the days that had shaped the grain of the banister, the nights that had set a ring on the dining table, the way the parlor couch remembered the body of a woman who once slept there with one child on her breasts and another in the bed next door. They did not know the way names set down into the house like seeds, and when the house closed its doors those seeds grimaced but did not die.

Alma stood in the shallow shade of the front porch, hands hooked in the pockets of a coat she had not thought to fasten against a sun that was hot enough to make glass sweat. She did not need the coat and yet she wore it because she needed the feeling of something binding her to herself. Fifty-three, the clerk at the probate office had said when she filled out forms, and Alma had felt the number like a new address. On the paper her name reduced to letters, a signature — E. Walker — a thin formalness that did not catch the fullness of the woman who was someone’s mother’s daughter and someone’s child. The house had been her mother’s for forty years. It had been Alma’s for forty years more in the ways that matter: the corners where she learned not to speak; the window that taught her how to watch; the stair that taught her how to carry grief upstairs without cracking under it.

They moved with the kind of efficiency that comes from practice, from removing weight as if weight were a thing you could trade for money. They wrapped and unwrapped and loaded and filled the truck with an appetite neither curious nor cruel. They were careful with wood like some people are careful with animals, coaxing it down the steps with straps and language that showed possession: “Antique walnut sideboard, excellent condition. Solid oak table, needs refinishing.” They called out sizes and prices in voices that belonged to catalogues. A young man with a limp walked backward down the porch steps carrying Alma’s mother’s oak rocker and asked her, “Is this for sale?” as if it were a mere requirement to show the label here, or as if he could buy more than a thing.

“Yes,” Alma said. Her voice sounded like the house. She had not expected to speak. In the house language everything was its name and more. “Yes,” she said, and the word felt like paper being folded. It did not stop the movement. Men, and a woman who wore white gloves and the smell of damp wool, lifted the rocker, because things move if they are lifted. The rocker had been in the kitchen for as long as Alma could remember, swollen at the arms where hands had sweated and smoothed the grain. Her mother had sat in that rocker with one foot on the rung and the kitchen light on the boil. Sunday mornings the rocker kept a prayer rhythm without words; Tuesday afternoons the rocker held a woman who had spent a life measuring flour. It left the porch with a little creak of recognition, and when it went Alma felt something in her chest hollow out as if a small animal had left through a hole and not yet returned.

“Estate sale?” the woman in gloves asked, voice soft as cotton. She had a catalog with a list and a number in the corner. She pronounced things as if they could be recommended out of existence: “Lot 12 — Misc. Kitchenware, costumed ceramics, would you like the Pyrex?”

“You can have the Pyrex,” Alma said, before she could check the muscles around her mouth. She had thought she would hold onto the Pyrex the way other people kept wedding bands. The Pyrex set had been a wedding present or a moving-away gift, something given with the intention of making the future orderly. Her mother had stacked bowls in neat cooled pyramids and used them like altars. The Pyrex had a cloudy ring inside, the residue of custards and stews and the last apple pie that had not been sliced because there had been no appetite for sweetness after the funeral. The woman in gloves smiled, the smile of someone who sees the world as a market of small, clean miracles. She tucked the bowls under her arm, and Alma watched the white enamel of the inside against the deep brown of her mother’s hands, brown hands that had made and measured and not been measured back.

Alma moved from room to room as if walking through a book that refused to stop. Each room had the posture of a chapter, the furniture the sentences. The parlor with its stiff lace curtains and the small upright piano where she had learned to play “Greensleeves” with fingers too small to command the keys; the dining room with its table that had held the encyclopedia set like a church, where her mother had opened a volume and let the children lean close like parishioners to long explanations of things like “maps” and “migration” and “cotton”; the bedroom with the bedspread her grandmother had embroidered until the noon sun saw the stitches like a small constellation. The apartment had kept the taste of lemon oil and Tabasco, and the rooms were full of the slow, accumulated commitments of a life: freckled recipes, half-mended socks, a stack of letters tied with twine and stained by humidity.

Things left a trace on Alma the way salt clings to skin. A high-back chair removed from the sitting room where her father had once sat and skimmed the paper, where on one grey evening the radio had announced something and he had dropped his hands and never recovered the shape of them. Her father had died in a hospital five towns over. They carried his chair past her and Alma wanted to say, Don’t take that — give it back, but how do you give back a thing that the dead already took? The chair offered up its arms and then nothingness: “Antique mahogany, good finish, buyer assures transport.” The legs tapped the steps like the number of nights her mother had sat awake counting coins.

There were smaller things, the kind that make a life narrative in private and do not look like much to a stranger. A tin of buttons full of mismatched metal stars, shells of old thoughts. A green glass bottle that had once held cough syrup and now held the memory of coughs in winter. The costume jewelry her mother wore when she wanted to be seen by people who were not there; a single purple glove with the fingers severed where a moth had eaten the cloth between the knuckles. Alma picked up the glove and smelled it. The glove smelled of drawer lavender, and her own hands remembered the grip her mother used to braid hair. She thought of all the small economies of a kitchen: a crust saved in the jar for the dog, a penny tucked into a bible for good luck. The strangers did not know those economies; they knew only the larger currency: silver plate, walnut credenza, the weight of a lamp in European bronze.

People from the neighborhood came and stood on the lawn like authorized mourners. Neighbors had been calling for a week, some with condolences, some with a calculator. They wanted to take the things they knew, like proofs. Mrs. Willard from down the street wanted the teapot with the cracked spout. Mr. Jenkins asked about the radio and whether it worked; he remembered how the radio had once broadcast church sermons that made the house pray even when the family slept. Children who had once played hide-and-seek in the backyard now had hands full of stray things, a single drawer knob, a brass key, a wooden soldier whose paint had been worn off at the elbows. A kid tossed the toy horse between his hands and made the sound of a train. They were collecting fragments, the way scavengers collect shells on a beach, and they hummed in the background like a chorus.

Behind the movement was the account book of memory that Alma kept in herself and only took out when no one else was listening. Her mother had been a good one for keeping accounts — not money, not really, but debts of obligation. “We owe one another things,” she used to say, tapping her temple with one knuckle. She would sit at the kitchen table and tally out warmth and mercy with a dictionary-hand: “You did this; I did that; where does the ledger close?” Sometimes the ledger did not close; things stacked like plates and tipped. Alma remembered the time her mother took the last of the rent and poured it into an envelope marked “Carl” — for the man who had been with them only in the nights. There were histories of men, histories of traveling, small migrations from a hometown in the South that had been washed thin by weather and time. The house held the maps.

At noon there was bargain. Men with clipboards wandered room to room and wrote numbers beside names. They spoke of restoration and provenance. When someone told Alma that the sideboard was an example of midwestern craftsmanship, she thought of the hands that had made it and not the idea that it came from any definition. She wondered if wood could be midwestern, if it could be something more intimate, like the bark on a hand. “It was my father’s,” she said to a young woman who smelled faintly of perfume and overnight travel. “He carved his initials on the inside once,” Alma added, as if offering a private door. The young woman touched the inside of the sideboard and found the initials, Philip W. carved in wrong-handed script, a child's attempt to own the man who had not been able to own his life. The woman widened her eyes and said, “Oh, that’s a lovely provenance; it adds value.”

Value. The word came like weather. It did not ask consent. Alma thought of all the ways value had been assigned to her mother by other people: by neighbors who borrowed sugar and never returned it, by a cousin who called only at funerals, by men who left with the lightest footfalls. She thought of the way the church had said Amen and taken the collection, the way the social served peach cobbler on paper plates and called that sufficient tribute. She realized that the estate sale was a process of converting meaning into money, of making a language of purchase out of stories that had never been for sale.

There were objects that did not want to leave. The tea-stained armchair in the back bedroom resisted being unstitched from memory. It was the chair the mother had used when she held Alma's head with the steady palm of comfort during storms and when small tragedies stood at the doorstep. Men pried under its cushions, finding potato chips and the coin of the moment. The frame groaned and then folded as if admitting to something. Alma watched them lift it and she felt something move in her chest like a latch opening and letting out a single slow breath. She thought of all the times she had thought of leaving the chair, the way one can imagine moving out and never actually go, the way the heart makes plans and the house keeps them.

She moved through the rooms in a half-swim, the edges of remembrances pressing like cold water. She stood in the kitchen and watched someone take the sink that her father had fixed once with a stick and a borrowed wrench, watched them carry out the back door and across the lawn to a truck that smelled of diesel and cigarettes. The back yard had been a kind of ledger too: a place for things to be buried, to be hidden, to be declared gone. Alma remembered burying a dog there and a boy she had loved for a summer. She did not cry then. The day was too full of people to reserve tears; they overflowed only in the private, the night.

A boy — a child really, though his face was adult, an attempt at seriousness — asked Alma about a photograph on the mantel. It was in a frame whose glass had held the sun in for decades. In the picture a woman sat on the end of a porch in a white dress that had one button missing. She was Alma’s mother at a younger edge, her hair caught at the nape like a crown. The boy touched the glass and said, “Is she famous?” He asked the question like a diagnosis. Alma wanted to say yes and no and something else entirely. “She is famous to us,” Alma said instead, because that was true. “She was famous for making tea, for telling the right stories to scare us straight, and for not letting anyone take less than they could from a plate.”

“They’ll buy the picture?” the boy asked.

“They can buy the frame,” Alma said. The answer was the size of a stone tossed into a pond. The ripple would not stop the world.

Sometimes the house argued back. A chest of drawers refused to release its top drawer. Men had to unscrew it, pry its runners. They found, within a nest of cloth, a letter folded into thirds and soaked by the slow humidity of being kept inside a shirt sleeve. Alma sat on the step as they pulled the letter. It bore a hand written with the economy of love: half a promise and a lot of reasons. The men in gloves looked at the handwriting like men who had discovered some fossil and passed it along to a collector. The letter was, to them, a relic, a curious thing that might charm a buyer at an auction. Alma read the lines again: "I am here where the night keeps some things clean." She had read that sentence when she was small and thought it meant the moon. Now she saw it as a bargain.

“You can keep that,” the man with the clipboard said. “Personal items like that don’t go in the sale.”

“Can I keep it?” Alma repeated, though she already knew. People divided artifacts into that which could be catalogued and that which was too human to mention in a bill of sale. Memory often survived in the seams of clothing and the corners of drawers. Here, in a piece of paper, lived the measure of a late love and all the reasons it had been small.

She had been a daughter who argued with her mother more often than not. They were two women matched in a way like two birds in a small cage, each learning to keep still so the other could feed. Alma remembered lessons learned at the kitchen table when the world seemed like a pot boiling over: keep your voice even, do not tell everything at once, know how to make stew from one potato and a stalk of celery. Her mother’s voice had been a kind of ledger that straightened loose debts. But sometimes it had also been a stone hurled against the skin. Her mother had loved with a precision that could be sharp. She had loved the neighbors more than she loved needs; she had kept a small cottage of goodwill where sometimes Alma stood without invitation.

As noon bled into afternoon, the truck left and returned like a tide. A man who wore a hat with a feather in it told Alma he had bought the walnut rocking chair because “real wood tells its own truth.” His trade language was ceremony. Alma watched him walk away with the chair swaddled in someone’s blanket and thought she would find her mother’s hands once more in the grain. Instead the hands remained within her, not gone but rearranged. The chair left like someone taking a limb. Some things carry identity with them. Some things leave you with the echo of identity stretched thin.

Alma had not thought she would have so much to decide. The paperwork was a new language, a ledger of estate vs. memory. She signed things with a pen that had run out of ink at odd hours when the clerk was not looking, and someone brought a fresh pen in the name of kindness. She made decisions, more by habit than conviction, as if some part of her was still the child who answered questions because she was told to. There were moments of performative sale, where she haggled as if the haggling could stop the vanishing. She said to a woman who admired her mother’s china, “Take two saucers. The rest will be there for someone else.” In that small concession she reclaimed the right to give away heritage on her own terms.

A young woman, black and with a laugh that made the dust in the air stand still, came late and looked at the bedroom where the perfumed undergarments had once lived. She thumbed the spine of a cookbook on the shelf and asked, “Did your mama make peach pie?”

“Yes.” Alma watched the woman’s fingers. She was the first who asked a thing as if it mattered beyond the transaction. Alma felt herself handing over a sentence that had weight like a coin. She recalled the recipe scrawled in a careful, angling hand on a torn card: pie crust that called for love measured in “little pinches,” sugar reduced for the heat of summer. She told the woman about the pie in the same slow voice her mother had used when she divulged secrets about the best way to fold pastry. The woman smiled and said, “I’ll take the recipe,” and there was no show of citation or pretence of interest beyond the honest need for taste.

When the woman left with the paper tucked into the pocket of her purse, Alma felt a small rescue. Some things did not walk away into the truck but into someone else’s life where they would be learned by hand. The woman who bought the recipe might make the pie and pass the method along to her children, and the pie would keep traveling like a river through towns and tongues. Alma thought of this as a kind of memory economy. Not everything had to be kept in a building that could be dismantled. Some things could move in people and keep their hungry, private histories.

Alma also held a small, stubborn hope that not everything would be sold — that something would remain like a memory anchored to the floor. The men with the truck took the bed. They took the wardrobe in which her mother had kept the wedding dress, a yellowing thing that had never been worn and that had been kept as if a future could be preserved by stitch. The wardrobe groaned and then gave up its drawers and, in the last one, they found a small tin with a lock and key. The tin held a stack of photographs, some of them black and white with edges scalloped like sea foam, others already spotting with the age of hands. Faces smiled from those pictures with a courage reserved for film and light. Someone said the photos would likely be snapped up by a collector of early twentieth-century faces. For a moment Alma wondered whether faces could be sold — whether a stranger could own the way someone looked at a summer while it was happening.

She opened the tin. A photograph slid loose like a whisper. It was of a girl sitting on a fence, her hair threaded with daisies. The girl in the picture had been her aunt — a woman who left and never explained — and there were things in that person’s smile that Alma had been trying to learn. She had always wanted to ask the aunt why she left; the aunt never told the story. The photograph asked no questions but offered a hint. Alma felt the paper between her fingers as if a pulse could live in it. She thought of the day the house was empty and the photograph still could tell some story to the air. She decided to pocket it, inexpertly, clumsily, as if some things needed to be carried loose in a body rather than folded into the market.

By mid-afternoon the house had a few carved-out lungs. The parlor couch was gone, the sideboard, the odd lamps that made light like a soft invitation. Sunlight found new places to live on the bare floor where old rugs had been lifted. It revealed a circle of dark in the floorboards where the sewing machine had sat, where needles had kept time. The house had been rearranged by absence. Where things had been empty spaces became new furniture for memory — hollows to put more memory in. The house exhaled. It was quiet in a way that felt like listening.

Alma paced the rooms the way people walk through a dream they remember only in part. She let her hand trail along the wall where chipping paint had once been wallpaper with orange tulips. She felt the scuff of a child’s elbow, the faint stain of a sugar-butter thumbprint. The walls had gossip in their plaster, the way some churches have saints tucked into the folds. If someone had come with a camera they might have seen nothing but the frames, the dust motes spinning like small galaxies. But Alma saw more: every scuff, every nail hole, a mark of an argument; every smudge a kiss. Objects had not just housed the family but had been the family’s grammar.

At some point she sat down on the lowest step and let the house be a thing outside and not inside. There were voices, the passing of strangers loading up a truck and the clinking of dishes; there was the town beyond like a background hum — a grocer’s shout, a child’s laughter on a bicycle. Yet inside Alma was a different cadence, one shaped by the steady voice of her mother which could turn tender and then sharp in the space of a breath. She remembered the last winter her mother had been alive, when the cough announced itself in the folds of night and her mother would sit by the window and watch the snow like an offer. Alma had sat across from her and the two had traded small clarities: who took which pills, how the bills were arranged, the correct amount of butter for biscuits. Her mother had given instructions like a map — store the sugar in jars, keep the berries out of reach of raccoons, if anyone came for a thing, let them. “It is harder for people to carry the memory of us than it is for them to carry a chair,” her mother had said, and then she had tapped the ledger as if to mark something off, a check beside an entry.

Now the ledger was a pile of boxes on the curb. Alma had one left to make a decision about: the small blue enamel kettle that lived on the back stove. Her mother had dropped it once and mended it with glue that did not hold for long. It had a bruise on the belly like a birthmark. Alma could not imagine the house without it. She wrapped it in a dish towel like a small sacrament and tucked it under her arm. The men loaded the last of the boxes into the truck. Nothing in the house was heavy enough to stay; everything floated into other hands.

The truck left for the last time like a tide going out to sea. The sound it made from the angle of the street sounded like a pause. Alma watched its taillights until they disappeared around an elm and then she turned and walked slowly up the path to the house. There were fingerprints on the glass from strangers who had pressed their curiosity against it. She placed her hand where another had been and felt the cool. She opened the door and walked inside as if she had not been there all day. The rooms chimed like an empty hall. The floors had been swept as if in preparation for a funeral. The air held the faint odor of lemon oil and wood varnish, a scent of enlivened absence.

She stood in the parlor and looked at the place where the couch had been. The light that came in through the window slanted the dust and made one small column of gold on the floor. The clock on the mantel — a small brass thing that had been wound weekly — said a time that could not be trusted. Alma listened to the silence and found in it not the end but the outline. The house had given up its things but it had not been left with nothing. From the floorboards rose the old mathematics of a family: the way a sound learned to live in a room, the echo that followed a laugh, the pattern of a woman’s steps. The house held them like a diet holds memory. Where the physical had gone something else remained: the shape of days as a map.

She pulled open a drawer that those men had not thought to unbind and found a packet of tea, a small matchbook, and a scrap of paper with numbers written on it — phone numbers perhaps of lovers, perhaps of the electric company. She smiled at the numbers as if they were an old joke. She considered the house and all that it had agreed, in the patient way houses agree, to donate and did not. A photograph, a letter, a recipe — all of them had chosen someone to carry them. They had a way of being stubborn in the pockets of others.

For a little while after the truck left, no one came by. The street learned to be ordinary again. A woman in a yellow dress walked a dog and paused at the fence to peer in. She had come out of simple curiosity, not malice. She said, “Are you keeping something?” and Alma said, “Yes.” She did keep something. In the room she had put the kettle on the table and filled it from the tap. She set the flame beneath the kettle and as it warmed she felt the small domesticity of making tea the way other people felt the weather. She poured a cup for herself and sat in the place that had been a chair once, a phantom support. The tea steamed and the house inhaled the scent.

She had been thinking that she had paid for the right to decide, that the business of inheritance was more ledger than heart. But there was something else today, the way handling objects rearranged the way one thought of being a daughter. When her mother had been alive, she had called to Alma on the telephone a thousand ways: with kindness, with orders, with the deep soft voice that had the power to stop arguments. Now Alma heard that voice in the kitchen’s memory, in the kettle’s sing, in the crack of old paint. She sipped the tea with the cautious reverence of someone who knows the world can change. Outside, a playlist of loaded items hummed away on a truck bound for other towns. Things would be polished and sold under bright lights in rooms where people did not know the names. They would be bought and placed and renamed with new uses. Alma knew they would carry new memories and some old ones. She did not want to be a martyr to the past. She wanted to be a keeper of something that would not be smuggled into the market.

At dusk she locked the front door. The lock made a sound she had not heard in years, a small defensive click, and she left with one box in the trunk of her car. In it were the photograph of the girl on the fence, the letter from the tin, the recipe card folded until the edges had softened, the photograph from the mantel she had chosen to keep, the chipped saucer she could no longer bear to let go. She placed the kettle in her passenger seat beside them as if they were a party. The town looked smaller from behind the wheel than it had from the porch. The house sat quiet in the street, a silhouette with no furniture.

As she drove away, the lane lights stretched like a string of small, startled stars. There were houses she passed that kept their own quiet private inventories. She thought of people she knew who would never understand why a woman kept a small blue kettle when she could have sold it and had money. She thought of the ease with which the world had decided what counts as heritage. She thought of all the items that had been reduced to catalog entries that day and of all the things that had been saved when someone’s hands reached for them. She thought about how the shape of a memory could find a new seat inside someone else’s home. She thought about the way things travel through hands the same way bread travels through mouths — to feed and be fed, to be transformed and to transform.

On the edge of town she stopped at a light and looked at the box on the seat beside her. Sunlight tipped the chipped saucer and made its crack glow like a river. She wanted to know if the saving of small things added up to anything. The kettle hummed softly in its new silence, content perhaps to be around someone who would put it on the stove again. Alma put the car in gear and moved forward. Behind her the house had become a name on a list, a place that could be visited or listed in a record. But memory — that stubborn, domestic thing — had found ways to ride in the pockets of people and travel like secret prayers. For a while, as the road pulled her along, she listened to the rhythm of the wheels and thought of the kettle’s sing: the same note her mother had used when she called the family to table.

When she reached the small apartment she had rented near the bus line — a place with thin walls and a window that looked out onto other people’s gardens — she unbarred the door and carried the box inside like a small child out late from a dream. She placed the photographs on a shelf and smoothed the recipe with the care of someone designing a new small architecture. The tea kettle went on the tiny stove and water boiled for the little cup she poured for herself. She sat at the table in the lamp’s yellow circle and thought that she had inherited more than a house. She had inherited the act of choosing what to keep and what to let go. She had inherited the right to translate the past into things she could use, so that memory might not be a museum but a living table.

Outside, night folded the town. A few porch lights still illuminated the empty shells of trees. The house on the hill that had once been the fortress of her mother’s being sat in the dark like a patient animal. It had been emptied of wood and cloth but not emptied of the traces of what had been done there. Alma sipped her tea and felt the heat of it like a small prayer. Memory, she knew now, would not vanish because the objects that held it had moved. It settled instead in people who carried the stories like bread and fed them to their own children. She moved the photograph into a small frame and hung it where the light at dawn would find it. She had no idea whether the neighbors would understand, whether the kettle would make the same sound in the apartment as it had in the house on the hill. She only knew that she had the parts of a life she could not let go of and that she would set them down carefully.

At night the kettle sang again in the kitchen and the apartment filled with the smell of boiled water and lemon tea. Alma listened to the sound like she would listen to a voice across a room. It was a voice that had once belonged to another woman and now belonged to her as well, not reduced but enlarged and given a new harbor. She thought of the porch steps, of the men who had learned the art of removing weight; of the hands that had mended, counted, and loved; of small things tucked away in tins and drawers and pockets. She thought of the infinite and patient ways people keep one another’s stories: in recipes, in photographs, in the sound of kettles. She had spent an afternoon watching strangers carry her child-hood out the front door and she had left with the knowledge that some things, like songs, travel anyway. They take on new voices, settle into new bowls, and continue to be used. Memory is not owned; it’s passed, as a recipe is passed, as a letter is read aloud, as a tea is poured into a cup. In the quiet of the kitchen, with the photograph looking out at the window, Alma drank her tea and let the house be a space that no longer confined her, but from where she could go forward, carrying the small parts she had chosen to keep.
