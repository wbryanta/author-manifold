They worked with greedy, mechanical mouths: the pipe couplings and the wrenches, the old aluminum scoops that had once been hand-me-downs from their father's shed. The sun was a hard coin in a June sky, a white coin that made the sweat in the crook of Henry's elbow taste like iron. He crouched with one knee in the trench and the other braced against the dry, mashed bank, and he could feel, in the small bones of his hands, the long ledger of this place—the hours counted on calluses, the lithic arithmetic of seasons that had once turned like clockwork and now staggered toward a reckoning.

"Pass me the coupling," Sam said. The coupling had been chewed, spliced with a length of rubber, wrapped in duct tape and prayer by their father when the first breaks had come ten years ago. It was ridiculous, a patchwork thing to hold a life.

Henry passed the box without looking up. "You find the shovel?"

Sam kicked at the lip of the trench and sent a scatter of dust into the air. He had a sunburn just beginning above his collar, an unaccustomed tan line where the cuff of his shirt met his wrist. His hair—once the same brown as Henry's, now peppered at the temples—was trimmed neat like a man who'd spent the last decade making presentations in conference rooms rather than fishing hooks out of irrigation lines. He had been in and out of the orchard his whole life, but this visit felt like someone else had handed him his own skin to wear for a day.

"There," Henry said. He slid the coupling into place with a practiced, slow economy of motion, like a man tying the same knot a hundred times. "We cut out the bad section, clamp this on, get a new T in and run it to the second row. If the valve at B's block holds, we should be able to get water to the center."

Sam's fingers grazed Henry's as he reached for the wrench. The touch was not conciliatory. It was the clanging of old metal.

"You still think that's going to fix it?" he asked.

Henry tightened the coupling until the rubber bulged, his forearms flexing under the sun. There was a doggedness in him, a refusal to let the orchard's trouble be summed up by a single break. "It'll hold for now."

"For now," Sam repeated, like someone learning the language of compulsion. He rested his forehead on his wrist, watching dust float in the shaft of light. Behind them, the orchard spread in rows that had once been black satin under a uniform sheen of irrigation. Now the trees leaned unevenly, like a congregation broken up by old arguments. Leaves that were usually a glossy green had edges browned and crisped by lack of water. Some trees wore their fruits in small, miserly clusters. Others bore nothing at all.

"You should have called a professional," Sam said. "There's a company—"

"If I wanted a company I'd sell the place to someone who plants parking lots," Henry snapped. His voice had the thinness of long deprivation. He wiped his brow with the back of his hand and rotated the wrench. The coupling shuddered, then steadied.

Sam watched him, and a memory rose as stubborn as the dust: a boy leaning against the orchard gate, twelve years old, watching their father with a headlamp bent over a pile of valves, his breath a white ghost. He had thought then that their father's hands would always know what to do, that they would be able to coax water out of the ground like a conjurer. When the man had died three years ago and the hands had gone still, something had shifted in the ground.

"There's an offer," Sam said finally.

The air stilled. A bee pounded against a leaf near Henry's ear and bobbed away. Water somewhere deeper in the line gurgled, muffled and teasing.

"How much?" Henry asked. He turned the wrench the tiniest increment as if protracting the moment might lengthen the orchard's life.

"Enough to pay the bank and my house and still have something left over." Sam's voice was clipped. The offer had lived in his pocket like a small, hot thing—a paper that could burn or cure. "They want the strip off the road. Two acres, maybe three. Buildable. They'll put up—" He hesitated. He had rehearsed this speech on the drive down the highway, in the supermarket parking lot, in the shower. "They'll put in a cul-de-sac. It's prime frontage."

Henry's jaw tightened. He had imagined the road as the artery of the farm, more than a line of asphalt; it was where the produce truck turned in the mornings, the place where travelers slowed to admire the deluge of blossoms in April and sometimes bought bushels from their father. The road had been a public face and a private wound.

"They can have the frontage," Henry said. The words came measured, like a man weighing coins. He had thought of this before, in the quieter, colder rooms of the house, in the months when calls from the bank had been daily and numbers like teeth gnashing in his sleep. But there was a difference between thinking and saying, between a plan in the head and a plan framed in breath. "But not the heart."

Sam's mouth pressed thin. If their father had been alive he would have cut the man off and taught them both how to fold the disagreement into a wrench-grip compromise. The father had kept his hands on the farm and on the family, and in his absence they had become two hands tugging, each in a different direction.

"You keep saying 'the heart,'" Sam said. He squinted into the orchard as if what he needed was a topographical map of affection. "You know the heart isn't a tidy thing you can fence off. Nobody's saying tear up the center line and pour a parking lot over your great-granddad's pear trees. I'm saying we sell enough to make the payments and get someone in who knows how to bring the line up to code. There's federal matching money if we do the retrofit the right way. We can put in drip. Less waste."

Drip—Sam used that word the way someone used a talisman. He had learned a new lexicon in the city: efficiency, modernization, retrofit. Henry had learned another: memory, continuity, the name of every tree in the orchard.

"Your mother planted those pear trees," Henry said. The two brothers both looked toward a cluster of gnarled trunks in the backline, the oldest of them bent as though listening to an old injury. "You can't buy that back with a check."

"You can keep the pears," Sam said. He offered the concession as if it were a thing that could be weighed and handed over. "You keep the back forty. We'll partition. The developer gets the front. They fix the culvert at the entrance, they put in a fence so the trucks don't roll over the seedlings. We keep what we love."

It was not a peace; it was a treaty that left both parties with their hands on fewer spoils. Henry tightened the coupling until his knuckles were white. The wrench clicked. Somewhere beyond the trees a line of traffic hummed, a low and indifferent presence.

"You always had an offer," Henry said. "When you left, there were offers every six months. You said no then too—said you'd come back, that you would help. You said you were saving things."

Sam's face lost the neatness of his city-made features for a moment. "I did save things. Paid for Mom's funeral. Put food on my family's table. Got a mortgage. It's not like I'm some rich developer. I'm trying to keep us afloat."

"You never had to sleep with the bank telling you what to do," Henry said. "You don't know what it is to listen to a man with your name say—"

"Don't," Sam snapped. The word was quick and young as a slap. "Don't make it into some kind of—some kind of virtue test. I left to have a life. I left because there were days I couldn't breathe under the weight of Dad's drinking and the bills. Do you think I wanted him to get sick? Do you think I wanted to miss his last year? I sent money. I tried to call. You don't get to—"

He stopped. The two men had always had the same tongue but used it differently; Henry forwork and repair, Sam for negotiation and retreat. The orchard took these small domestic tragedies and rearranged them into something huge and slow. Between their voices, the trees rustled.

"Listen to me," Henry said. His voice had gone lower, less brittle. He let the wrench rest on his knee. "I'm not trying to indict you. I thought—" He swallowed. "I thought you could tell me when something big like this came along. I thought we had a plan."

"We had a plan," Sam said. He fumbled in his back pocket and drew out a paper, a folded thing with a developer's logo and a price. "They put it in writing this time. It's real. You can read it."

Henry took the paper with both hands. It smelled faintly of machine ink and air conditioning. He smoothed the fold and read the numbers. They wanted the first strip—where the old sign once leaned, where the county bus turned—and they offered more than enough to cover two seasons of back payments and the legal fees. They said they'd insure, they'd plant a buffer, they'd make "an aesthetic statement" whatever that meant.

"The fire marshal will look favorably on cul-de-sacs," Sam said, as if a bureaucrat's nod might sway a memory.

Henry folded the paper shut again. He could not remember the last time county had offered him anything but notices. This was new. He had not known a form could feel like a lever.

You could fix the immediate break here and now, he thought. You could make a valve hold and get water to the backline for the summer. You could also sign a paper with an ink that dries and then becomes irreversible, a line of small domesticated violence.

He put the coupling in and turned the wrench another notch. The line beneath them gave a little sigh and then a sound like a throat clearing as it accepted the fit. He lifted the end of the pipe and watched a thin stream bead on it; the black rubber swallowed, then let go.

"You should eat," Sam said abruptly. Sweat had crusted in dust on Henry's lip. Sam's voice had a gentleness to it then, an offering. He had brought sandwiches wrapped in waxed paper, two of them, not enough to be bribe, but enough to be a language of civility.

Henry nodded. He let his shoulders drop an inch. "You got any cold tea?"

Sam produced a thermos from the toolbox and unscrewed the lid. Inside, the tea had gone lukewarm; it tasted of lemon and of miles. He handed Henry the sandwich and sat back on his heels, his boots leaving impressions in the dry earth.

They ate in silence, and the orchard seemed for a moment to rest its breath with them. There had been a time when such a silence had been easier—when the absence of talk was simply the grunt of work. Now it was a swell of thought: who would keep the apples, who would take the rest.

When Henry finished, he rolled the paper between his fingers like a kernel of a seed. "If we sell the front," he said, "they put a road in, they cut the first row of peaches. That's thirty trees. Maybe more. The next line will have to stand alone, without the windbreak, and then we'll get more die-off. You know that. The microclimate shifts if you pull that barrier up. You can't sell ten and keep the rest. It doesn't work like that."

Sam nodded. He had read about microclimates, about soil moisture and the effects of shade. He had built models in his head, plug-ins to show values; but there was a difference between a soil report and the feel of bark under your palm. "We can put in a berm," he said. "We can plant windbreaks. We can—"

"You can put in a berm," Henry said. "Or they'll plant this pretty little hedge and call it a design feature. Those trees won't be our trees. They'll be somebody's landscaping. They'll have shorter lives, more careful pruning, no fruit for us. The whole place changes."

Sam had imagined a neighbor—someone with a lawnmower and a barbecue, not a picker cart and a late-night buyer. He had pictured money and his son making a better future. He also felt a sharpness in his chest whenever he thought about how the old pear trees bent like a supplication in the back forty. He went between images like someone between two homes, unwilling to close the door on either.

At noon heat sat on them like a hand. Their shirts clung damp to their ribs. Henry stood and stretched his back in a way that made Sam think of their father's gait—sturdy, slightly heavy. "Let's finish this," Henry said. "We can figure out offers later. For now, get me the T-joint."

They worked methodically. The coupling that Henry had so carefully tightened held enough to bear the pressure. Henry dug until the roots of an old almond tree spit dirt into his palm; Sam loosened the clamped coupler and slid the new plastic in with the careful liturgy of two men who had done this before. Once the T was in, they rerouted, turned on an old brass valve that had been half-buried and full of rust, and the line licked at its own edges like a living thing.

Water came back to the orchard with a sound like permission, sputtering at first, and then a steady, breathless flow that saturated the topsoil. The trees seemed to exhale as the water hit them; leaves, under the scorching air, threaded themselves through the narrow bands of wet and shimmered. Dust gathered along the path and became mud, black and grateful. A robin lit on a branch over them and began to preen, as if nothing in the world could change.

"Look at that," Sam said, and there was a softness in his voice that surprised him. His cheeks were streaked with dust and sweat and he felt, suddenly, the old compulsion to touch a limb and feel the bark's answer. He ran his hand along a trunk and felt the living heat.

"It will hold for the summer," Henry said. "Might give us fruit this season if we can keep at it."

Sam sat back and watched the water creep along the drip. In the middle distance, the old packing shed leaned and sagged like a tired animal. There were weeds in the gravel drives and an old sign that said MURRAY ORCHARDS in flaking paint. He could already see the developer's rendering in his mind's eye, clean lines and a glossy cul-de-sac where dirt and fruit clung now. It was impossible to imagine both things occupying the same space.

"You think Dad would have sold?" Sam asked.

Henry laughed, a short, rough sound. "Dad sold something every winter. Not the land, but the idea of it. He'd sell fruit, sell labor, sell water rights when he had to. He'd beg a neighbor for fertilizer. He'd tell you the soil spoke and you'd call him sentimental. He'd cuss a bank. He'd lie awake at night making up spreadsheets. He believed in the land because he thought it was the last honest thing he had."

Sam listened to the list, the litany of contradiction. Their father's hands had been both practical and soft: able to graft and able to forgive. It was not reconciliation that made men into heirs; it was the constant running out of breath against the thing.

"I remember," Sam said, "the year the hail took half the peaches. He drove around at dawn with a shotgun yelling at the clouds. When I asked him why, he said 'so the trees know I'm still here.'" He fell silent, watching the irrigation line spit and whisper. "Maybe that's what you want to do. Tell the trees you're here."

Henry watched him for a long while. "Maybe that's what you want too," he said. "To tell us both you're still here."

Sam's eyes stung. He nodded without meaning to. The offer slipped in his back pocket like a folding knife.

Later, when the ridge of afternoon dragged itself across the orchard and the air acquired that wash of heat that made voices sound thin, they sat on the edge of the packing shed and argued like two men who had been rehearsing their lives' scripts from birth.

"You left," Henry said finally, not an accusation but the settling of fact into a well-worn socket. "You left us."

"I left," Sam agreed, because you couldn't pretend otherwise. He reached into his pocket, drew out the paper again. "If we sell this frontage, we pay off the bank, we put money into a trust for the orchard, enough to pay for maintenance for—what—five if we're careful. We could get a manager if you don't want to keep doing the daily. You could have whatever's left and someone to help."

Henry blew dust off his knee. The word trust landed between them like a foreign coin. "A trust," he said. "For an orchard. That's not what the orchard needs."

"No, but it makes sure the bank doesn't come and take it," Sam said. He folded the paper in half, then again, then tucked it into his shirt pocket. "And you don't have to run yourself ragged and scrape pennies on fertilizer that he refused to buy but we needed. There are people who know how to run a modern farm. They'll take the tech and the grants and make the water go farther."

"And you'll keep your city life?"

Sam swallowed. He had been carrying the double life of men who play two parts: a father, a commuter, a son with obligations. He had thought about pocketing the money and walking away into a life of new carpets and new salads. He had always thought himself practical. "I keep mine," he said. "I'm not trying to be the last one standing here. I'm trying not to be the one who loses his house because we couldn't get the numbers right."

Henry stared at the line of trees into the late light. They were two men folded out of the same cloth who had been pressed by different hands. Sam had become paper and Henry had remained cloth.

"You never asked me how I felt," Henry said. His voice had a roughness to it that was not anger but a kind of mourning. "You asked the bank. You asked the developer. You asked the city. You asked God, probably. I was always—"

"Sleeping?" Sam tried to joke and did not succeed. He dropped his face into his hands for a moment, then looked up. "We can arrange it so you can have—" He gestured vaguely with one hand. "Payments, management. You don't have to give up the rest."

Henry's laugh was small and lived in the ribs. "And what about the children who pick the fruit? The men who've worked these rows for thirty years? You think a developer will keep them?" He shook his head. "They'll be gone. They'll be replaced by HOA rules."

Sam's hand pressed on his wrist like an apology. "We're not talking about wiping it all clean. Two acres on the road. It hurts, I know. But it will save the rest."

They argued until the sun bruised the sky mauve and then purple, until the orchard's hum became night insects and the work settled down into the quiet of the day's done. Stars came out in a pale scatter. Henry and Sam sat with their backs to the shed and their knees almost touching. They chewed on the ends of their thoughts like men chewing on caramel.

"Do you remember when Dad got the first irrigation line in?" Sam asked. His voice was soft as a ledger. He could picture the younger man, eager in his fedora, eyes bright at the sight of a system that would make the trees believe in water again.

Henry nodded. "He took us down to the creek at night. Showed us the pipe he had laid before dawn." He closed his eyes. "He said if you can move the water where it needs to go, you never have to beg rain again."

Sam laughed, a small sad thing. "He told me once that water is memory for the land. Keeps it remembering how to be green."

"Who remembers him?" Henry whispered. "We can't sell our way into remembering."

Sam felt like a child then, small and fragile. He reached across and put his palm on Henry's forearm. "We both remember him," he said.

"We both remember different parts," Henry said. "You remember the bright things. I remember the nights he lay on the couch and the bottles on the table. I remember the week he didn't get out of bed because the bank called three times and told him what they'd do if he didn't come up with payment. You've got a life where those calls don't have to ring. I'm the one they call now."

Sam's jaw flexed. He had been the one to bring that life into being and it had cost him the right to be untroubled. He had been studied by maps and mortgage rates, by newsletters about greenhouse efficiencies. He had thought himself measured.

"You can't keep everything," he said. "I know you can't. Nobody can."

Henry breathed in. The night smelled of crushed leaves and dust and the old sweet rot of fruit. "Maybe we can keep enough," he said. "Maybe we don't have to give them the whole place. I won't sell the back forty. But I won't be foolish either. If the front two acres pay the note and give us a buffer, we do it."

Sam looked at him, surprised by the compromise that was half concession and half armor. "You'll let them put in a cul-de-sac?" he asked.

"They can put in a cul-de-sac," Henry said. "But you write into the contract an easement for access to the irrigation ditch. You put in a clause that if they want to cut more of the orchard later, they have to go through us. You make it as unhandy as possible for them to be careless."

Sam let out a breath that was half laugh. "You know how to trap a developer."

Henry shrugged. "I lived with him."

They sat in the dark, the sudden silence thick with the weight of choice. It was not an ending. It was a calibrated beginning. They had not solved the problem of how to hold grief and money and legacy in the same hand. But there was a table of things now, a set of items that could be discussed and parceled: front acreage, cul-de-sac, easement, trust, management. They had a way to go forward that kept some of the tree-lines breathing.

Finally, Sam reached into his pocket and felt the paper with the numbers. "I'll call them tomorrow," he said. "I'll get an attorney who knows agricultural easements. I'll make sure the language—"

"You ever think you know an attorney who knows us?" Henry asked, dryly.

Sam grinned, the first unforced expression that night. "No. But they say the right things sometimes."

They stood and hefted the toolbox together, the movement a small, familiar choreography. The ache in their backs would be the same tomorrow; their minds would be full of bills and banks and the intangible count of what was right. But there was a way now to be two brothers in one place: one who kept the daily, the other who negotiated the edges. The orchard, for tonight at least, lay wet and breathing under the stars.

As they walked up toward the house, they passed the old pear trees in the back. Henry reached out and touched a low limb, feeling the ridged bark. It gave him a kind of permission, or maybe it gave him a knot of sorrow. Behind them, the irrigation line hummed quietly under the soil like an animal settling down to sleep.

Sam paused and picked up a fallen fruit—half-eaten, the flesh browned and soft. He brought it to his nose and inhaled the smell: fermented sugar, old summer, the slow rot and the sweet. It was all of it: the orchard's history pressed into a single living kernel. He felt his throat ache with a mixture of longing and reason.

"Promise me one thing?" Henry said.

Sam turned. "Anything."

"That if I get sick," Henry said, "you'll come back and help make the decision. Not sign any papers without coming back. You hear me?"

Sam thought for a moment, the image of hospital lights and the sound of machines, of years of night calls that had once been his father. "I promise," he said.

They clasped hands, two bony grips that had once wrestled in the back forty and now negotiated land. The promise felt like a hinge put in place.

Later, after the house had been lit and they had eaten, after the day had been measured out, they went to the shed and found the old ledger.

Their father had kept a book in a bottom drawer of the workbench—a notebook with a sewn spine and pages yellowed at the edges. Sam remembered it as a catalog of small obsessions: receipts, graft notes, lists of men who came by in winter to prune, phone numbers that stopped working. Henry opened it and found in the back, folded and grubby, a photograph: their father with a younger woman, arms around her waist, both laughing while a tractor blurred behind them. On the reverse, in their father's hand, it said: "For whatever it's worth—keep it green."

Sam looked at the photograph and laughed then, an absurd, raw sound that made Henry smile despite himself. "He left us instructions," he said.

"Not instructions," Henry corrected. "A wish."

Sam rolled the photograph between his fingers and put it back into the ledger. He could already see the meeting in his mind: the developers with their glossy renderings and polite smiles, the city planner with his nods toward "progress." He could see himself in a suit and tie, and he didn't hate that man. He also saw Henry with dirt under his nails, the orchard in bloom, a boy delivering bushels to the farmer's market. He wanted both things and knew, completely and finally, that desire did not make for a simple life.

"In the morning," Henry said, "we'll draw a line on the map. There'll be a few trees that go. We'll make plans for the others. We'll talk to Roger at the co-op. We'll do the paperwork."

Sam stood and stretched. He could feel the muscles unspooling after the day's labor. "We'll make it as humane as possible," he said. "And we'll make sure the easement stays."

They left the ledger on the bench and went out into the yard. The night was dense with the smell of the orchard's green: fruit and leaves and the dampness of irrigation. Fireflies danced between the rows, small, electric punctuation. Sam watched them for a long moment and then, without quite knowing why, he reached out and cupped one in his hand like a tiny life.

"It didn't have to end up like this," he said.

Henry shrugged and looked up at the sky, which was thick with stars unbothered by municipal plans. "It doesn't end. It changes. That's all."

Sam nodded. They stood there for a while, two brothers trying to remember exactly what they'd promised the world and what they'd promised each other. When they walked back to the house, the sound of the water under the ground was a soft, methodic reassurance, as if the earth itself had blessed the compromise.

The decision was not the end; it was the first in a string of small, necessary losses and salvations. Sam would call the developer. He would come and go on business trips and sign papers and perhaps, if the language was careful enough, bind their hands so tight that the orchard's memory could not be extinguished by a careless bulldozer. Henry would keep the back forty and tend it like someone tending a fevered child, watching leaves for signs, saving his pennies for fertilizer that would bring life back to the edges.

When at last they climbed the creaking porch steps and pushed open the screen door, the house smelled like the years: oil, old bread, lemon from the counter. There was a photograph on the mantel of their father leaning on a wheelbarrow, sun in his hair and a grin like a weathered coin. Sam touched the frame as if to steady it.

"Tomorrow, then," he said.

Henry nodded. He took the water bottle from the sink and held it for a beat, thinking of the pipe, the hum under the soil, the way water could remember its course if you coaxed it. "Tomorrow," he said. "We make a map."

They turned off the light and went to the rooms that were theirs by custom. There was the sound of the old house breathing in the last of the day. Outside, the irrigation line ran the length of the orchard, a faint metallic murmur under the earth. For the first time since winter, there was water in the lines where there had been a gap. It was not salvation, only survival. It would have to be enough.

Sam lay awake some time after Henry had fallen into a sleep sound and even. He thought about the rows they would have to sell, the cul-de-sac that would make the front of the orchard sterile as a postcard. He thought about the men who picked the fruit and what the sale would do to their lives. He thought about the little son he had at home who knew not the weight of orchards but the glow of video screens, who deserved a future that wasn't measured in orchards at all. He thought about a photograph in a ledger and a note that said keep it green and he felt the strange, hot alignment of desire and duty.

When he finally slept, it was with the knowledge that water moved beneath the soil and that somewhere in the map of his life he had made a line that held. He had not been wholly compromised. He had not become wholly affluent. He had come and, in coming, had made a binding with his brother that was messy and human and, in the end, a kind of mercy.

In the morning they walked together through the rows, charted the ground, and began to draw. They marked the trees that the road would take and those that would remain. They talked to a man at the co-op and to Roger down the way and to a lawyer Sam had found through a friend-of-a-friend who had, as it turned out, done quite a lot of land partitions in exactly the same county. They argued terms that sounded like foreign tongues—easement, covenant, buffer strip—and they wrote them down with a kind of reverence as if the right phrase could make the difference between remembering and forgetting.

At noon, when the surveyor came and walked a line with a flagging ribbon and a clip board, Henry stood under the peach trees and watched the men with the bright vests. One of the surveyor's flags snagged on a low limb and fluttered like a small, mournful banner. Henry reached up and untangled it and pressed it between his palms as if to put it somewhere that wouldn't blow away.

"Do you want to keep picking this line?" Sam asked.

Henry let the question sink. There were days when he would work until sunset and dream in the rows, feeling the trees thrum in his chest like a second pulse. He loved the work and he was tired. "I'll keep," he said. "But not alone."

Sam laughed. "You're not asking that. You're asking more."

"Yeah." Henry looked at the flagged ribbon in his hands and then at his brother. "You're not the only one who gets scared of losing things."

Sam felt a sudden, hot relief. He had been braced for a fight but instead found himself stepping into something that had room for his fear. "Then we'll keep it together," he said.

They walked the lines and agreed on a map that was half arithmetic and half nostalgia. They signed one small, preliminary page of concession—a letter of intent that would allow the developer to pursue planning. They made phone calls and poured coffee that went cold in the sun. They listened to the surveyor's measurements and the lawyer's caveats. They wrote "easement" on the ledger in block letters and under it, in a different hand, "no additional cuts without unanimous consent." They made lists.

At the end of the day, when they had covered half the paperwork and both of them finally admitted exhaustion, they stood at the gate of the orchard and watched as the sun lowered itself behind the hills like a copper coin sliding into a drawer.

"You okay?" Sam asked.

Henry breathed in, his chest rising with the smell of crushed leaves and water. "Yeah," he said. "I think so."

Sam let the orchard sound settle around them, the faint hiss of drip tape and the chat of crickets. He had not fixed everything, and he had not wanted to; he had only wanted to make a way to live without forgetting where they'd come from. It was a small vote for memory and a small vote for reason. It would have to be enough.

They stood there, two brothers under the heavy June sky where the heat still held a kind of sweet and the future was divided into stripes of irrigated green and the pale, unplanted road. The water ran, the trees drank, and between the rows they found, at last, a line that they could both walk.
