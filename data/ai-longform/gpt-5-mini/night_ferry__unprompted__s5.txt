The ramp vibrated under Tom's feet and then the hull steadied, a muffled, animal motion that had nothing to do with him and swallowed him up as promptly as faith. The ferry smelled the way the strait smelled: diesel and salt and the faint sweet rot of seaweed. He stood where the car deck emptied into the passenger concourse and watched people move in their small private trajectories—an old woman clinging to a handbag more precious than its contents, a man hoisting a battered guitar case, a teenager who believed she alone knew the shape of the night. Somewhere above, out where the dark rubbed itself against the sky, the engines made a slow, indifferent conversation, and every so often a bell under the bridge-house rang like something someone had left behind.

He had not wanted to come, not at first. He had made all the easy excuses, the leftovers of an adult life—work, money, words that translated duty into necessity—until the envelope arrived from the police: an official ink, a typed sentence that held its own small gravity. An unidentified male, found on the rocks below Bran's Point, white foam in his hair, the tide half-keeping him. There were measurements, the size of his boots, the colour of the jacket, a note of rigor and the tentative kind of language that belongs to institutions: may be known to you. It did not say was. It did not close the distance between bodies and names.

Tom folded the letter until it softened and let the edges crease in new places. His fingers recognized the familiarity of the paper: the station's font, the stamp. He had not seen Eli for five years. Five years is not the kind of division that had a compass; it is an incalculable geography. There had been silence, a line in which events accumulated and became reasons, and then the sound of someone else's life on the other end of the line that used to be his brother's. He had told himself, often and loyally, that some things settle into their own shapes and that people who leave do so because they must. But when the envelope came, something—less a feeling than a weather—pressed him toward the ferry and the strait and the island with its closed little houses, like a bruise behind the ribs he could not ignore.

The ferry's interior smelled like an empty school after the class has left: the citrus of cleaners, the stale reassurance of carpet. Tom found a window seat and sat with his bag at his feet, the weight of it both portable and as if it were some member of his own body. Outside, where the world was less polite, the horizon stood leveled and undisturbed. The water had gathered its sullen black and was only dimly stirred by the ferry's passing, a slow furrowing. He watched for the lighthouse at Bran's Point, though he had never known to trust a light entirely; one could be meant for you and still pass without notice.

At the café, a woman with a child did a clumsy dance of sandwiches and toys. She had a laugh like a small bell and kept patting a hair that had escaped its tie. A man with a fishing rod stood near the rail and chewed on the inside of his cheek. They were ordinary people engaged in the ordinary tasks of moving across a body of water. To be one of them would have been a simpler narrative: travel, arrival, return. But Tom had a name in his pocket that did not belong to him and a face, which perhaps had belonged to a brother, waiting to be confirmed by light behind glass.

He tried to remember the last concrete thing Eli had said to him. It was an argument, of course; words that wedge into a memory and then harden like silt. They had fought about their father and money and a truck on a wet lane and who had the right to rage. Eli had flung a sentence and left town, the sort of dramatic exit that people confuse with meaning. The town, in which they both grew up, had the kind of memory that does not partition into years but into events you could point at on a map. The chip shop where they ate on Friday nights, the pier where summer boys swore they would jump when they were brave enough, the narrow street where their mother had once stood and cried when the rent was due. Tom's memories were a mixture—festival lights and small, bruised domestic details. Eli's memory might have been only the spaces between.

He thought of the last photograph he had—if you could call it so—of the two of them together. It was taken in the summer they decided to leave the town and not look back. Eli had his arm around Tom, an arm that Tom later realized you could have hung a coat from, broad and confirming. They smiled the way brothers smile when the camera is a ritual, when the world outside wants them to be a certain shape. In the photo, a stray dog trod around their boots. Tom had written beneath it, in a hand that made the words sound casual so they would be less painful to read later: Come home when you like. Eli had not.

The night slid by in a long, practical darkness. The ferry's motion thrummed through Tom's bones like a sleeping thing. People drifted off to sleep in seats, their faces relaxed into small deaths, and someone snored quietly, a trusted sound. He tried to sleep but emotions are poor sailors; they move around below decks and make the whole boat tremble for want of something steadier. He replayed scenes like a child sitting with toy trains and remaking the landscape to his satisfaction. There were scenes where he begged Eli to stay, where they reconciled in a public square and the town applauded; other scenes where Eli walked out in the rain and never looked back. None of them bore the weight of the truth because the truth, when it appears, rarely does so in any shape we had planned.

At some point, perhaps the middle of the night when the ship seemed to be alone with its engines, an old boy—someone who had been alive before Eli left—sat opposite him. He had eyes like dull glass and a thick, river-worn hand. He looked at Tom for a long time before speaking.

"You it?" he asked, the question falling into the space between propeller beats.

"For who?"

"For Eli." The old man's name for the missing brother was as if it were an inventory item. "You kin?"

The question was the kind that unlatched something. Tom said, "Brother," and then felt small and private, embarrassed by the accuracy of the admission. The man nodded once, as if this settled something for him.

"There's talk," he said. "Seen him round this side now and again. He didn't stay. Men like him don't stay. Salt takes em."

Salt. The word was a small, biblical force. "He had a name for things," the man went on, "a lot of names. Used to say the sea was an honest thing even when men weren't. Said it gave back what it wanted. If it's him, it'll be... well. If it's him you know best."

"You don't," Tom said to the ceiling, not to the man. The ceiling smelled of late-night coffee and had its own nervous wallpaper.

When they arrived, the light had a wrong, flat yellow as if someone had set the whole town under a lamp. The island's houses clustered around the harbor like a village in a music-box, their windows thrown open to a thin dawn. Men in orange coats moved with a purpose that belongs only to work and not to grief. The police station was smaller than Tom had expected, a building that had once been a shop, the sort of municipal place that holds its own kind of grief in forms and forms only. They made him sit; they offered him tea. Their sentences were soft and mechanical. There was a folder and a pad of forms and the same neutral language that tried to lubricate the protocol of death.

"You can see him if you want," said a woman—young, then older when she smiled—who introduced herself as from the coroner's office. Her voice belonged to people who spoke to many and who had to allow their own pulses to hint at nothing. "There's no hurry."

There was nothing in Tom's body that behaved like hurry. He did not know whether to hurry to see or not see. It sounded stupid but there is a kind of terror lodged in the muscles around the jaw that belonged to the particular moment when you are about to encounter a truth you might not be ready to buy. You stand before a thing that might or might not be your brother. You have a name and a face and an entire history that is partly memory and partly fable. Then there is a body like any other body, which may have passed through hunger and damage and the sea, and you must ask: which of these is the throat I will accept as the voice of the man I used to know?

The building smelled like disinfectant and lemon peel. He walked down a corridor that had the calm silence of a place where people learned to speak only when necessary. The room where the body lay was smaller than he had thought it would be; there was a cold table and a window that let in a pale, indifferent light. The man under the sheet looked like a man sleeping if you were generous with the word. He had hair—too much hair for Tom to be sure it was Eli's, hair that had been matted and darkened by water—and a beard that had started to go grey, which was also a small, terrible surprise. There were bruises like old maps staining the skin. A mouth that had once told things now lay slack and private.

Tom's first impulse was to look for the kinds of signs people grant named authority: ears, the cartilages of the nose, a chipped tooth. He moved closer, and the room's fluorescent light made the skin look artisanal and fragile. For a while he looked and learned to be patient with not-knowing. He had loved the brother in whom he saw himself, sometimes in embarrassing reflections—how they used to stand at a window together and mock the shore. But love and memory do not always equip one with the facts.

Then Tom saw the scar—a little half-moon nick near the wrist, tucked against skin like a parentheses left open. It was the kind of thing that becomes a private map. He had gotten such a scar, he thought, years ago, when he and Eli had been seven and found a shard of glass in a tin of old nails; they had been daring one another, elbows white with the strain, and Eli had taken the worst of it with a kind of dramatic grin. Tom had a small scar like that too, on his own hand, and in their childhood the brothers had sometimes matched each other's scars the way sailors matched knots. But this came as a commonality and as a betrayal. Scars, like fingerprints, are intimate little legacies, but not unique enough to hold a whole identity.

He expected, oddly, to feel something like recognition as if identity were a face waiting to be unearthed. Instead he felt a dull sliding of the world; that old childhood certainty of sameness receded. The face on the slab had a nose more Roman than he remembered, a squint in the left eye as if years of wind had taught it to close by habit. The beard had broadened the chin, given it a sternness Eli had not had. It could have been him. It could have been anyone.

An officer stood by and watched him with watchful politeness, as though guarding the very fragile act of grief. "There are papers to sign," she said. "If you don't... you don't have to. We can proceed with records."

"Records?" Tom echoed, the word carrying an authority that could bury the soul of a family.

"Dental," the coroner said. "Fingerprints. If you're sure... but if you can't say for certain we will have to use the formal channels. It might buy you time."

Time was an offer he had not expected to bargain with. To hold the name of Eli open was also to keep the possibility of being the man who could say No, it is not him—an answer that would be as clean as white paper. He could refuse and let the bureaucracy perform its slow, neutral work. It would be a way to avoid the work of belonging to the dead and the peculiar responsibility of the living who knew a name.

What he could not do was remember the last words Eli had said to him because there had been so many things left unsaid since then. If he named the body, would he be signing an erasure of the years in which Eli had not been his? Would he be accepting a stranger in order to make his own past neat? If he did not name him, he imagined letting the town make another anonymous heap of sorrow and watching the last of his brother's possessions—if there were any—disperse into the hands of strangers.

Tom put his hand near the body, and the cold was not the animal, sharp cold he expected but an evenness like the absence of heat at the bottom of a thawing pond. He did not want to touch, but then he touched, fingertips like trained instruments measuring a horizon. There was a small ring on the fourth finger, plain and worn, a ring that had belonged to their mother. He remembered her hand in an afternoon light, knitting and counting the days with the quiet economy that marked the poor and the patient. She had lost many things, sold the watch, the coat, the radio. But she had worn that ring for twenty years, and when Eli left he had taken small things — a knife, a lighter, a metal box of cigarettes—but not that ring. Or had he? Memory makes the past legible in whichever hand is handy.

The ring fit a kind of narrative. Eli, who had not been given much by way of surety, had possibly taken the ring and kept it because it was a small mooring. But then again, people robbed one another of meaning with a certain cleverness. Maybe he had sold it years ago to feed himself on a night he would not tell anyone about. The ring was a totem; it could belong to anyone who had stolen old jewelry or been given such a thing by a mother who couldn't keep it any longer.

The coroner asked whether Tom wanted to view the face. Tom had been doing that quiet theatre of self-inquiry where people wait to see whether they will see what they most want to see or what they most fear. He said yes, because saying no would have been a way to avoid owning a life. The sheet slipped away and the face, which had been anonymous under linen, became more particular. Up close, small features matter like fossils: a crooked tooth, a tiny freckle near the jaw, a hairline fracture in the cheek that the light showcased. The left ear bore a small fold that matched a childhood memory of Eli with his ears pushed back by a winter hat; there was a lopsidedness to his smile that used to make Tom laugh. He looked at the mouth and the mouth was not a liar. Through the window of the eye-sockets there was, even supine and passive, a demand to place this life in a story.

There are gestures between brothers that do not register to strangers: the way they lean toward one another in kitchens, the way they loop arms when there is a drop of blood to hide. There are also gestures only a brother knows because he created them. Tom looked and found the hollow the size of a sand dollar in the deceased's foot where months ago, in a life that could have happened, Eli had stuck something small and left to heal. This was the kind of thing men forget about themselves and only brothers can point to: the brittle resilience of a past wound. He had a sudden, ridiculous surge of recognition, like being handed the right key and finding a house that you had spent years sketching and not inhabiting. It is an odd, almost mathematical sensation: a set of facts aligning into a plausible resolution.

And yet as he looked, there was apprehension. If he claimed Eli and later the dental records disagreed, what then? Would he be accused of lying? Or would the town murmur that it was unsurprising he would lay claim to someone who had been part of his childhood, an act of redemption or of vanity? Memory is a treacherous currency.

He thought of his mother. He saw her, in some clean mental photograph, weary and small, sitting by a sink rinsing something and humming a tune that had made him think of Sundays. Eli had been restless in her presence. He had promised her then, in his clumsy reckless way, that he would look after himself. They had all promised each other a hundred things with the absurd sense of permanence that belongs to the young. Those promises had been cheap even then, but grief has a hunger to be fulfilled, to at least have the pretense of continuity. To tell the truth felt like asking his mother to take a seat at a table he had not cleared.

"Do you know what you want to do?" the coroner asked, softly. She always asked questions that resembled adult manners. She offered him a form to sign and left him alone with his decisions.

Tom could have walked away, left the name to be decided by telephone calls and later by strangers who ring bells and read lists of names. He could have called his sister—there was one—who lived two towns away and who might have had a different set of facts, a different memory. But Tom had that fatigue that precedes action; he wanted to anchor at least one thing. He had not been around for Eli. He had been a distant brother who had decided not to be willing to be a witness. Maybe claiming the body was less about truth than about reparation: a small, desperate attempt to be in some way responsible for a thing he had let diffract into ruin.

He signed. The pen scraped and his hand trembled with a curiousness that was not precisely grief; it felt more like a tiredness being willed into ceremony. He wrote his name and his address and noted his relation. The coroner, with the practical elegance of someone who has seen debacle and quiet reconciliation, nodded as if this solved a complicated knot. There were practical details about funeral directors and the law. She mentioned the need to notify next of kin properly, to wait for test results. Tom listened and gave them the words the world needed, as if any one of those words could stitch a seam across the torn fabric of his life.

Outside the station the wind had shifted toward morning with a sharpness like a blade. He walked the quay as if fiction might spontaneously form on the surface of the water. Men mended nets, and gulls argued about the edge of things. A figure moved toward him with ease; it was an old friend of his, a man who had watched Eli leave and had not been surprised the way a body understands departure.

"You chose," the man said. The words were not a question.

"I couldn't not," Tom said. He did not say how he could not have whether because of love or because of the calculus of shame. The friend expected no explanation because he understood the cost of not acting. There were many kinds of cowardice, and the sea had an expertise in collecting them. He had always admired Eli for many things, and if there is one small, human truth, it is that sometimes the only possible act of love left to a person is to name them.

The funeral director, a woman in practical boots and an expression that approximated sympathy, offered him a coffee and a list of coffins. He declined the coffins as if refusing everything were an easy way to create moral distance. He asked about payment and about how much control he would have over the funeral. He did not have the money the director expected; he thought about that and felt the old primitive shame of someone who had never learned how to manage the small, cruel currencies of adulthood.

In the hours that followed the island turned into a petal slowly unfolding. People came and went, some with old memories that superimposed themselves like maps. Some were neighbors, some were people who had once known Eli at the edge of a bar. There was a woman who claimed to be a long-time lover. There was a man who argued, very loud for the small room, that Eli had been seen in the winter, drunk and singing. Tom listened as if to a radio playing different stations. The voices did not cohere into a single, authoritative biography. They layered.

On the ferry back, he held in his hand a small leather wallet he had taken from the deceased's pocket: a wallet that was scant on cash and heavy with the residue of a life in receipts and cigarette stubs. There was a photograph of Eli younger, with a dog that did not exist now, and on the back, in a handwriting that might have been his: FOR WHEN YOU NEED IT. Tom considered this and let it sit between the pages of his palm like a small verdict. Had Eli left it for him? Had he imagined his brother might one day return and want a relic? Or had someone else kept it and it ended in his pockets by accident? The sea, which does not care about small logic, had returned a thing that might be evidence or might be a trick.

He thought about identity as a pragmatic system of claims. Identity, he realized with the obstinacy of someone discovering a dull truth, is often less about who you are than who will hold your things when you are gone. A person exists in the world by being named and held in other people's hands. By signing his name, Tom was, he supposed, choosing to take on the duty of witness. He would arrange a small ceremony; he would tell a few things, maybe lie a little to make the story softer and more continuous. He would take the ring of their mother's and place it in a box.

It was not elegy, not wholly, but an attempt to stitch. He felt grief as a kind of cold concentration; it did not arrive as waves of hysteria but as a practical apprenticeship. He needed to tell someone simple things about a man he had not seen for half a decade. He wanted, in his imperfect fashion, to make of this death a belonging. He saw the arithmetic of things in his head: who would come to the funeral, who would pay, how would the body be transported back to the town that had not invited him back when he left it. The world has order enough that these things can be decided. Funerals have forms and people to arrange them and ways to make them seem like events of proper gravity.

When he told the funeral director he wanted Eli to be buried in the plot their mother had, the woman considered the papers and then shrugged as if she had known they would fall where he wanted them to fall. "He's come home," she said, and the phrase was as much instruction as consolation. To bring someone home is to make a tidy kind of finishing. But Tom did not know if he had returned the right person; he only knew he had decided the right thing for the time being. He understood, with the clarity of someone who had finally decided to move, that identity is not only vector but verb—that to claim a name is to enact a life backward into coherence.

There were things he refused to do. He refused to make a speech at the funeral, because speeches felt like theatre and he did not trust theater to be the instrument of truth. He refused to cast blame in public for the estrangement because blame turns people into monsters and monsters are easier to bury than to understand. He could not pretend he had all the memories needed to fill the silence.

In the small stone church the pews filled with a slow procession: fishermen who shook their heads and slapped shoulders, neighbors with their faces scarred by weather and worry, a woman in a red scarf who claimed to have given Eli a cigarette in a doorway once, and Tom's own friend who sat close and allowed him something like company. The coffin was plain and smelled faintly of varnish. The minister said words about rest and sailing and kept them just vague enough to be universal. The world, when forced into ritual, makes provision for both the known and the unknown.

When the lid closed and the grave received the small weight of the box, Tom felt a curious relief, as if he had completed a small, required transaction. He also felt the familiar, sharper absence of a man who had been a part of his life in ways he could not reconstruct with a neat analog. The lie of narratives is not always malicious; sometimes it is an act of mercy. By naming the body he had accepted a story in place of a hundred ragged fragments. He did not know whether the man beneath the ground was the same character who had sworn to come home or a stranger who had carried his brother's story in a pocket. He decided, for the sake of the people who cared and for reasons as small and as large as guilt, to maintain the narrative.

At the edge of the grave he slipped his hand into the pocket of Eli's jacket and found a small, folded scrap of paper. The handwriting on it, scrawled and a little drunk, read: Call me. Maybe. It was the most ordinary of things: a request, a promise, a hole. He had not been the one to answer. He folded it back and let it remain a private relic.

On the ferry back to the mainland the light was skittering: the sun fell like a coin between waves, then was swallowed. Tom went to the rail and leaned against the cold metal. He had no illusions of being absolved. He had no certainty that, in the long journey of this world, he had done a thing that would make their mother peace. But he had done something else: he had named a life and placed it in the continuity. If identity is, among other things, the willingness of one person to take another's absence onto his own shoulders, then he had undertaken that burden. Nothing in the calculus of the sea is fair, and mercy is often the last, smallest thing you offer when the world has depleted you of other pretenses.

The town receded like a page torn from a child’s notebook. The strait lay smooth with its own quiet indifference. People would tell the story in different ways and make of it whatever salve they preferred. Some would call him a hero of a kind, others would say it was an inevitable action to keep a town tidy. He would accept neither version; he had made a choice that was equal measures of duty and shame.

At night, in the small room above the snug where he had slept badly, he found he could not sleep but instead lay with the window open and a half of moon visible between the jagged roofs. The small, thin memory of Eli at the kitchen table came and went like a raft of gulls. He imagined Eli younger and full of that sharp energy; he imagined him older and less sure but steadier. In either portrait the man had been a person who loved and who was loved, in a messy way that people are loved. Naming him had been less a declaration of fact than an act of partial faith: a way to say yes when the question had seemed reluctant.

Over the next days the phone would ring, and officials would call, and the town would arrange the last things. There would be receipts, and sometimes small, imperfect mercies: a neighbour who brought soup, a woman who insisted on tending the flowers at the grave. Life folded back into its usual compartments. Yet the ferry and the strait remained in him as a geography—with its dark halfway places and rooms of white light—where he had chosen to perform the lonely labor of deciding what a boy was called when he was gone.

When he finally boarded the ferry to return to his own small life, he carried with him nothing but two small things from the pocket: a photograph and that scrap of paper. He did not have to invoke any evidence beyond the private talismans held in the bones of memory. Whether the man who lay under the island's soil had been his brother in the strict form of blood and name mattered less now than the fact that he had been placed where people could remember him. There is a dignity to being remembered, even if it is only the memory of a man who might one day be proved otherwise. And in this case, Tom felt, his decision had given the town a story and himself a small reprieve from what might have been the perpetual undoing of words and faces.

As the ferry cut its slow wake through the strait, Tom watched the water close and reform as if erasing a pencil line. He did not know whether truth had been kept or whether the world had arranged a story that would hold for a while, but he felt the small rightness of having performed a final act of brotherhood. The engines hummed, the night stretched on, and the ferry moved forward through an unliteral darkness. The sea had returned something to its own terms, an object of sorrow that could be owned and named; Tom had accepted it. He did not imagine that anything about him had changed in the large and moral ways people imagine after an event. He would go back to work; he would still forget birthdays; he would still misplace the small things that signify love. But at the edge of the ordinary, he had put his hand on a cold arm and said this is mine. For a man who had learned how to be absent, this was a new and strange thing: to decide to belong.
