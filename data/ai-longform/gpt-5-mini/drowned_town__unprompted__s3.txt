The reservoir had retreated to bone. The shoreline, once a soft, reflective circumference that took the country with it in one wide mouth, had withered inward until it left ribs of road and the pale skulls of houses. Where the water still pooled in the hollows, it was the color of old coins; where it had gone, it left skin—stretched, cracked, dusted with salt and algae. A wind that smelled faintly of iron and dry kelp moved across the empty millpond and lifted the light into horizontal bars.

She parked the county truck, its governor sticker a pale stripe against the dust, and sat for a long minute with her hands on the wheel. Her surveyor's kit lay in the back: total station in its foam-shell case, folding tripod, the orange range pole, GPS antenna like a polished insect, field notebook with a strip of masking tape for dates. She could have checked the network from the road and plotted everything from the truck, let the satellite do the work. But when she was a child—when the town still had a roofline—she had learned the town the way small people learn the world, by pressing palms to rough surfaces and measuring things with their feet. The old, stubborn part of her wanted to walk the bones.

She had been born in this place, before the bureaucrats arrived with models and maps and the legislation signed in a smoke-choked hall. She had been small enough that her father could carry her under his arm when he walked the gravel into the grocery's doorway and when he took the rowboat out to the deep-roped buoy. The flood that drowned the town came like a slow eraser, a set of instructions delivered by engineers with slide rules and promises. Her family left. People left things behind—their choice more often than not, because dragging century-old things down into a bathtub would only give the bathtub new shapes. She remembered boxes on the upstairs landing; she remembered, too, the thing her mother wrapped in oilcloth and then in a towel: the blue enamel bowl with a small chip on the rim. That bowl had been on the counter in a house that now lay under five or six fathoms of water. She had last seen the shallow concrete threshold of that house above glass years after the dam closed, when the reservoir fell in a hard winter and the old chimneys loomed like islands in a cracked sea.

She climbed out, the sole of her boot sinking a little into the dust. A heron lifted from a cattail and then froze in a sentry pose across a pool. In the air there was the faint cry of gulls and an insect that rasped like an old zipper. Around the end of the road the dam reared, an uneasy curved thing of concrete and rusted rebar, its spillway a dry pale throat making a shadow. Below, the former town lay like a map whose ink had been scrubbed away.

She took the GPS unit and turned it on; the screen blinked while a stubborn little satellite locked on, a blue dot that felt like an eye. Profession required that she name things and fix them. The county kept a legacy of paper maps in the courthouse basement—folded tracings like the entrails of older cartographers—along with the digital shapefiles that were matchstick-and-ghost. The commissioners had called her because the drought had opened corridors. There would be questions about submerged rights, about who owned the quay of an old mill, about whether the gravel road that had once been Main still crossed parcels of county land or had slipped under the state's purview when the dam's easement had been accepted. "We need a line," the supervisor had said over the phone. "We need coordinates. You know the protocol."

Protocol had a way of flattening things she did not want flattened. She fixed the tripod at the first point nearest the dust road and extended its legs, feeling the cheap aluminum settle into the ground. The total station complained that it wanted to cool down; she hummed to it in a practical voice and waited for the hiss to stop. Memory would hem around the tidy little numbers she would deliver to the county server as a geodatabase named with the fiscal quarter and the notation "exposed features." Someone in the planning office in the county capital would import those coordinates and, with the cursor-clicked love of functionaries, overlay them on the municipal map and re-zone and stamp. Her role was being the hand of clarity.

She walked the first street she found, older than the road, a north-south thoroughfare whose bricks showed through the sand and moss like teeth. You could still see the gutters, half-filled with silt, and the curbstones, because curbstones were stubborn and would cling. There were houses at angles to the street suggesting a layout everyone had built to the land's peculiarities rather than to any tidy grid. Foundations stuck up like the pale lids of half-buried coffins: square, rectangular, with the hollows of hearths visible as shallow depressions. At one spot a stoop leaned as if refusing to come out of its water-wet dream. The walls of a church—the top courses only—showed their masonry like a jawbone.

She stopped at the first foundation, set the range pole down, and clapped the survey prism into place. The total station blinked its eye; the instrument registered the vertical angle and the distance, the numbers stuttering into her device as if these dead things had little lives again. She wrote the point in the field book: Point 001; Foundation—residential?; bearing to benchmark 124. She drew a little square and shaded it, a child's map within her own adult marginalia. The ink was a lifeline in a way she liked. Ink spoke of decision; it refused to get lost in the server.

The square could have been any house, a house with an unremarkable life. She paced the edges anyway, measuring her footfall in meters and in memory. The concrete threshold was still there and beneath her palm it was almost the temperature of the riverstone she had held in her mother's hand. She remembered the bowl in oilcloth. She remembered the way her mother used to stand in that threshold and call because her father was always late from the mill. The house had a three-step stoop and a little step like a pause that had always felt as if it existed for someone small to sit on.

She had been small. She had once timed her life to doorsteps and thresholds, to the safe run between porch posts. When you are a child you divide the world into small rooms and climbing places and the quiet places where you can hide. The town had been a place of small rooms. In the summers before the dam its alleys collected leaves and lilac and the smell of the bakery and on the bright mornings the boys in town would run with a football and you could hear the scrape against clapboard in that specific way children make a sound when they are alive and paying attention.

Her practiced hands measured the thresholds as if she could convert the weight of that living into an order of numbers that would not alter with time. But memory is not a coordinate system. When she stood where the hearth had been, the present and the remembered overlapped like multiple transparencies—her father stooping, the high, small laugh of a neighbor, the way the dog had waited with an impatient tail for scraps. The dog was gone. The laughter became something like the wind in this altered place.

She moved on, taking points. Half the town was a scatter of foundations; the rest were rotted piers and a gravel pattern so precise it could have only once been called Main Street. The bakery's oven registers, rimmed in bronze, lay in a dish of silt, their inner seams crumbled. Down by what had been the riverbank, there was the rusted hull of a pickup truck, the body whittled by water, the seats softened to the shape of people who had long since left. On the dashboard sat the curved skull of a porcelain doll, glassless eyes staring skyward, a child's hair braided in a muddlehead of brown algae. She took a photograph and then resisted the impulse to touch.

At a cross-street she found a faded signpost. It slanted with the lean of age, its wooden face stripped of paint. A careful tilt of her head made out the ghost of letters—one might have been an M. She wrote "possible Main?" in her notebook and added a question mark, the drawing of her doubt. Later she would upload the coordinates; someone else would clean the signpost in Photoshop and trace the words with the cunning of someone who had never seen the town when it had a name.

There were newer markers too. Someone had driven stakes, yellow flagging on them, with hand-written notes that said things like "property boundary?" and "cave-in?" The stake nearest the church foundation had a photograph attached with a magnet: a family in Sunday clothes, the children squinting, the mother's hat like a mushroom cloud of lace. The photograph had taken to mildew along its edges but the faces were still human. She left the stake as it was, adding a number to the list. Her job felt like a courtesy to the dead: to give them coordinates in case anyone ever wanted to excavate or claim or complain.

She was half a mile in and the sun had climbed to a white coin in the sky. The mud was soft in places, a fine-grained silt that held your foot like a bed. Once she nearly stepped into a cellar hole; she had to swing her leg over the rim of the open well and feel the echo under her foot. A gull wheeled low and threw its shadow across the page of her notebook. Often, when the reservoir had been higher, those same holes had been hidden and she had imagined the town as a set of lines on a map—clean, geometrical, subject to planning. Now the plan had been revealed in its physical particulars: the walled basements, the silted staircases, the lost garden beds—square frames of cultivation, starved of sun and now coming up with salt-resistant weeds. In one of those beds a small sprig of corn stood like an earnest soldier, utterly uncomprehending.

Her father's cylinders—unspoken things—came back to her as she walked. She thought of the way he had taken measurements with a pocket level, the way his thumb had smudged the same page of a ledger as if the ink had been an anchor. He had taught her to call things by what they were, not by the stories attached to them. "A wall is a wall," he would say, "not a place to pin the past." Her profession's marrow had come from him. When she had first taken the county's exam the supervisor had said, half in awe, "You come from the old town." She had nodded. It was not something she generally said with pride; it was something she carried like a scar.

It was where she came from, a place of modest, steady lives that the county had decided should be relocated because the river could make more money as a laminated sheen of electricity than as a salad of houses. People had grieved at the courthouse meeting that year and again; some had cursed and some had been philosophical. Her mother had gone quiet and buried herself in preserving things—canning and jars and labels. For a while, when the town was still theirs, her mother kept the blue bowl on the shelves like a talisman. When they left, her mother wrapped it, set it aside and told her daughter to take whatever she could carry. What does a child carry? She had taken a watch and a coin and one of the little school reader's books with a dog on the cover. The blue bowl had been too heavy.

The further she went the more the town seemed to rearrange itself to match the narrative she fed it. She found the schoolhouse near where she thought it would be, its granite threshold mottled with lichens. The lower part of its bell tower jutted from the sand like a cut tooth. Inside, the tile floors were long gone; the classrooms were empty but for chalk dust embedded in the corners of the exposed joists. She ran her fingers along the lip of the foundation; in a child's memory the lip had been lower, good step, and she could imagine the particular slant of the old teacher who had kept order with a handbig ruler. She had been a woman in what her adult sense called the first wave of the town's children—those who were taught to read by men returned from the war, whose lives had a particular economy like the rationed sugar they used for jam.

There were fragments of domestic life scattered about. A rusted spoon lay half embedded in silt. A porcelain doorknob, the opalglas like an eye, peered upward. She found a piece of a child's sneaker, the rubber sole scarred in a pattern of a cartoon dog. In one cellar she kicked up a tin of nails that clattered in the shallow echo and then, among the debris, a tattered ledger. The ledger's spine had been eaten by damp and time but inside the ink still lived. She opened it and read a list of names and payments—an accounting not of fate but of flour and coal, of dates and small domestic privations. She made a point: find the ledger; preserve it; photograph. She closed it carefully, as if the paper would wake if mishandled.

There was a sense that the town had been converted into a grammar of absence. Where once there had been "corner store" and "blacksmith," now there were brackets; she could write their names and they would be true, but the truth would be something you had to construct from the grammar. Her work made it official. She recorded the location of the postbox—its lid crested with the county's faded stamp; she took the coordinates of the cemetery's stone slumps. The cemetery was at the highest ground and so had stayed mostly dry even at the reservoir's fullest. The stones leaned and some had sunk. She read the inscription of a small child's marker and felt a pressure at her throat that had little to do with work. She noted the graves relative to the courthouse benchmark and traced the lines with her pen like someone drawing constellations.

Toward noon she heard a voice behind her. "You with the county?" It was a man's voice, older than the voices in the photographs—a voice that owned the kind of roughness accumulated by years of sun and work. He had a truck farther back on the old road, an older model, and a cap that had been sun-bleached into modesty. He came up with a thermos and a paper cup.

"I'm with county survey," she said. She had said it before to people who wanted the information for a particular use—insurance, development, curiosity. His eyes went to the tripod, then to her, then to the town that lay open like a bird's belly. "I knew there was a surveyor back there," he said. He called her by her father's name, the way people do who grew up with intimate reference points. "You got the bad memory gene or the good one, honey?"

People age into directness. He sat on the curb of a long-ago curb and poured the coffee like a man administering succor. He told her he had been a salvage worker, once, before the dam closed, or after, or both. "We used to pull up the boats and see what rattlebaggery floated," he said. They called it "rattlebaggery" affectionately: toilets, pictures, a piano in one case. He had found a saxophone and kept it because he liked the look of it. He had been to the town before, when the reservoir had been low in an earlier drought. "You know," he said, "you come for the bones and the bones give you teeth. It bites in the end."

She told him she was mapping. She told him she had been born there. He whistled, impressed only in the way things are impressed by a history they had always known belonged to them too. "You ever take anything?" he asked, blunt and private. People who come back often come back with things they can't explain; they stand on the exposed stairs like vultures of sentiment. He watched her face for a moment and, seeing her hands unclenched around the field book, he said, "You know, I've seen grown men cry picking up a doorknob. The water will take what it wants. We just walk and pick what we can."

She told him about the ledger she had found. He offered a cigarette and then seemed to change his mind. He had a metal detector in his truck; he had used it once to find a wedding band on a bank of the old riverbed. "You can claim little things," he said. "But you can't claim the whole place." The words were not legal; they were human. She thought about the bowl in oilcloth and about the little sins and omissions of people who had packed and left and had not taken certain things for reasons that didn't need to be judged.

They walked together then for a while, picking up points in a pattern that had a tacit choreography: she took the station and the man kept an eye on the horizon and on the gulls. He told stories: of the mill whistle, of a prohibition-era smuggler who used the river like a backdoor. He told small paradoxes of the town, like the time the mayor's wife would not leave her sunroom because she said the town would miss her if she did; the town, she said in a thinning voice, seemed like someone who would not forgive. The man laughed and said, "You get to love a town like that and it will eat you in the end. Or it will leave you."

At the north end of town, near where the old feed store had been, she recognized a slab of concrete that was not a house at all but the foundation of the café where she had cut her milk teeth on coffee and black bread. Her father had argued with a man there about a load of hay. She could recall the exact intonation of her mother's anger when she had gone to town and come back upset. That memory rearranged the whole afternoon like a sheet pulled across small furniture. She needed the coordinates but she also needed the small ritual of crossing the threshold now and measuring the lip of that foundation with a hand that had been taught to measure by watching men who were never believed to possess tenderness.

She found, at the café's foundation, a nailed-down board where someone had once affixed a chalkboard with the day's specials. The chalkboard had gone, but the nails remained, dark and corroded. She tugged at the corner with her fingernail. The nail came out and she held it like a skived memory. She imagined a black coffee poured into a chipped mug, the sound of chairs dragging, talk about the harvest and the judge and the dog in Mrs. Lyle's barn. A small part of her wanted to pry and find the whole kitchen, to find the blue bowl. Another part of her understood that there were things you could not maximize by recovery, that the town had been in the water's hands for so long that what you found would be like bones: white, porous, and useless if you tried to stand a living on them again.

She walked to the place she was most afraid to find, the footprint of the house where she had been born. On the maps she had been given in her youth, it sat like a dot of assertion in the town's grid, but to find it now she had to imagine the routes of the old streets, the coaxing of decades of memory. When she finally stood at the edge of the foundation, the place felt both right and wrong. The concrete was smoother than she remembered and spattered with a moss pattern. In the corner there sat a brick, slightly askew. She recognized it by its size, because small children learn bricks by size and weight.

She sat on the foundation lip and took off her glove. The sun was hot enough that the air wavered and the gulls' cries looked like punctuation marks on the horizon. She had made a line on the county server that would render this foundation as an entity in a system that cared for angles and legal descriptions. She had the exact coordinates that, if someone wanted to claim an easement or to mark a historic site, would be enough. Yet she felt suddenly that numbers were inadequate as a means of saying the thing she felt: that this place had been a cradle for secrets and small hopes.

She opened her field book and wrote notations—bearing, distance, angle—and then, on a separate page she never used for anything but private capitalization, she wrote in looser script the things she remembered: the smell of her mother's hair when she leaned over the stove; the way the dog used to park itself under the table; the sound of the rain on the kitchen window like someone in the next room speaking softly. The field book took this as reliably as it took numbers—ink was ink, after all. When she lifted her pen she saw, entangled among the moss, a glint of metal. She pried it free with her thumb.

It was a child's bracelet, the thin copper wire twisted into a loop and soldered where taste had been decided. It was scaled for a small wrist, and on it someone had scratched tiny initials: M.E. The bracelet's metal had been tanned by water, but M.E. remained clear. Her chest tightened. She had not seen the letters for years—her mother's initials. Her breath came sharp and a little funny.

She had not expected to find any such intimate thing. She had expected instead objects cast up by time: door hinges and signs, the micro-architecture of the town. This felt like trespass in the way thinking about a mother can be. She held the bracelet like a small consequence. It could have been any of a half dozen sources. Small towns recycle names. M.E. might have belonged to another woman entirely, someone whose life had trembled parallel to her mother's. But to her it spoke with the clarity of a bell.

She could not, in strict professional ethics, disturb artifacts of potential historical interest. There were procedures for inventory and chain of custody. For a second her hands hovered. She thought of calling the county historical society. She thought of the man on the curb, puffing from his thermos. She thought of her mother, who had wrapped the blue bowl in a towel and set it aside, not wanting to leave but also not wanting to spend the last hours in carrying. She imagined her mother on the morning they left, fastening a scarf in the rearview mirror and saying, with the determination of people who make such decisions, "We will be all right." She had believed then that being all right meant moving house, but grief had a way of filling in the rooms where the bowl had been and making new furniture of it. You don't always solve grief by hauling things across a county line.

She closed her fingers around the bracelet and held it for a long time. There was a way in which she could obey the law and preserve the object in the county's chain of evidence, but there was another law older than the county's ordinances, the law of her own inheritance. She slipped the bracelet into the inside of her jacket, down into the pocket over her heart. It rested there like a rumor.

The sun slanted and made the water pools glitter like stolen coins. The man with the cap stood up and spread his arms and said, "God's been stingy this year. Or maybe he's cleaning house." He had half a smile that acknowledged the impossible joke of people fixing intention to such a weather.

They walked a while longer, the man and the surveyor, and took more points as they went, making the town's skeleton for the county's careful hands. They marked a drainage culvert that had not been in use since before she was born, sketching its aperture at three dimensions and measuring its slope because the commissioners would one day want to know whether the road that crossed it had been a county road or private right-of-way. The man picked up bits of ceramic and sometimes held them up like curators, and sometimes he threw them back where they had been, because some things were more beautiful when left where they'd been.

As the day slid toward a thin gold that made the land look like paper money, she made the last measurement at the far end of town where the old lane met the new riverbed. The coordinates registered correctly on her unit. She recorded the point and then the total station hummed as if it were pleased with its day's work. She packed the tripod and the station into the foam case with the care of someone who loved wooden instruments. The field book had a tidy line item for each foundation, each curb, each cemetery stone. Later, in the office, she would take the notebook and type the entries and digitize them and attach a little metadata where she would record, in an unemotional voice, that the site had been "inhabited, 1892–19—." She would keep a separate sheet in a locked drawer where she would write the stupid things she had found: the child's shoe, the ledger, the bracelet. No one in the county would ever look at that sheet.

On the way back to the truck she walked without sound, like someone moving through a house after a funeral, picking up the bare cues that told her what belonged where. The gulls argued for territory over some newly exposed comb of driftwood. The man walked with a slow dignity, his cap in one hand, looking at the town as if it had become an old friend lying in a hospital bed. He said, "You going to keep on coming back? Or you going to hand it off to someone else with a clipboard?"

"I'll map it when they ask," she said. "They'll ask again." She did not say that she had been there on nights years before because the water had gone down and she had wanted to see the chimneys. She did not say that she had been there as a child holding a boy's hand in the summer of some indefinite year, filling a pocket with marbles. There are ordinary things you do not narrate because they do not change another's world.

They walked to the county truck together and she told him the name of the road and the numbers of the points she had placed. He wrote them down on a cigarette packet with a stub of pencil because that is what people who live with improvisation do. He handed the pack back, and she thought then of taking the bracelet and handing it to him as well, leaving it back in the world where salvage men dealt in twinned loyalties of memory and profit. Instead she tucked the pencil in her pocket and started the truck.

"Take care," he said, and when she looked back he had set the photograph from the signpost onto one of the bricks as if it needed to be watched now that it was not bound by wood nails.

On her way out she drove past the edge of the dam where a construction crew had left an orange netting like a wound dressing. The workmen had left in the heat. She stopped and climbed down, walking the last few meters to where the land met the reservoir's rim and under the water the long lines of the town vanished into shadow. She took out the bracelet, feeling its roughness against her palm, and put it on the lip of the foundation where the bowl might have been kept. It was small and stupid as an act and ceremonial in the way rituals are: no registrar of the county would record it. She folded the field book closed and set it on her knee and then, without thinking, she traced the old map in its margins with a finger, following her father's lines and the ridges of her own childhood. The bracelet lay in the place of the bowl as if two small lives were touching.

When she climbed into the truck and drove off the county road, the reservoir broadened like a swollen thought. The town behind her lay open to the sky. She had given it coordinates and a set of reliable lines. Someone would write a lengthy report, and the place would be described with neutral nouns, as a "drowned settlement with exposed structural remnants." People would say "drowned" like one says "lost," and sometimes that would be true. But the thing she had learned, standing in the dust and chalking the graves in the sun, was that places do not die by map. They sediment. The county could place edges and easements and name who owned the land. Nobody could take away the fact that for years the town had hummed like a radio and then had been turned down and sealed under water. Whether because of drought or design, its outline would reappear in the world—sometimes visible, sometimes not—and her life would be a kind of indexing job between those two conditions.

At the rim of the road where the county signs announced weight limits and liability, she extracted her old photograph from the magnet on the stake she had seen earlier. The family in the photograph would make no claim to any of the newness she had mapped. She tucked the photograph into her jacket and wrote the final note in the field book: End of field day; all points logged. Returned to county server at 1700. She wrote it in her clean formal hand and then, beneath it, in the script she used for secret things she added, "If the water comes back, it will kiss the lip and go on." She closed the book.

She left the town behind, but not entirely. The bracelet rested against her sternum and sometimes, when she moved, the metal clicked softly as if the small initials were now moving with her pulse. She thought, for a moment, to keep walking until she had circled the reservoir and seen every exposed sidewalk and every cheat of garden, but the truck needed to be returned and the forms needed to be done and the data needed to be backed up. She had, in the coordinate system of the county, given the town a new life as data. She had in her private system given it a more human life as memory and small, private thefts.

The drought, if it was a one-time cruelty, would pass and the water would come back with the inevitable partiality of nature—sometimes silent, sometimes furious. The town would recline again beneath the blue. She imagined the blue bowl, wherever it might be now, turning and collecting its own new films of algae and sediment. She wrote the bowl's imagined arc in her head and then stopped, because the truth of the place was not only in the things that remained but also in the things they carried away: the habit of crossing a porch at dusk without looking back, the single way your mother wore her headscarf and the words she left unsaid.

She had measured corners, calculated bearings, and recorded the angles of the world. She had also found a bracelet that pressed against the old shape of her heart. She could have dropped it in the intake box at county hall; she could have put it in a small evidence envelope and typed its chain of custody into an orderly file. Instead she drove home with the thing against her and allowed herself the small indulgence of wearing it for a day. When the sun set, and she sat at her kitchen table in town—no longer the drowned town, the living town, the place where she now paid taxes and made prints for property deeds—she took it off and placed it on the shelf with the old photographs of her father measuring the world with a pocket level. She made a point to not show it, to keep that found thing a private permission.

Two days later she uploaded the shapefiles and the photographs and the ledger's scans to the county server. Her supervisor sent an automatic reply that contained the expected blend of praise and procedural next steps. She typed back: "Standby for submittal to Historical Society." She would wait and let the historians make their cases and the commissioners make their decisions. She sat back and looked at the line on her screen that now represented the town like a ghost ship on the county's mapping interface. It was beautiful in a dry software way and yet it had no capacity for the sound of a child's laugh or the heat of a bowl on a saucer. She thought of the bracelet and the way metal gives an echo to light.

The map was a thin thing in comparison to the town itself. She had, in a way, mapped not the sum of the town but only the places her mind could bring into focus. There would be corners she had missed, things nobody would ever see. But she had given the place coordinates and with the coordinates came the possibility of return. People would, in the future, come and stand on these stones and call the names etched in the cemetery and read the ledger and take small objects that spoke to them. That was their right. Her role had been to bridge the world of water and the world of law, of river and ledger, and to do it with both the exactitude of her instruments and the soft smuggling of the bracelet.

She turned off the screen and sat for a while in the dim, listening to the house's refrigerator make its small mechanical speech. Outside the light pooled in neighbor's windows like the residue of other people's hearts. The town beneath the water would wait. Her father had always believed that maps were honest things. He would have been pleased that she had made the decision to put a place into a system that refused myth. He would also have been quietly amused to know she had worn a small theft like a talisman against the sternum. She touched the small ridge beneath her blouse where the bracelet had been and imagined, with no need for proof, that the blue bowl had lived inside its own way under the water and had sometimes, in quiet hours, received small fishes who had mistaken it for treasure.

The drought would end or it would not. The bone would either be covered again or it would remain exposed a little longer, and the county would update the maps and the next surveyor would print new paper and perhaps a child would find that the world could be measured. She had done what she was paid to do and something she had not been paid to do. She had made the town legible for whatever hands might someday pick it up, and she had stolen a small piece back for herself in the margin.

At night she dreamed of walking through the town and having the water rise in slow, polite waves that licked but did not claw, until a blue bowl floated at the surface and turned like a small sun. When she woke, the bracelet lay on the shelf and the field book closed at her elbow. The map on the county server glowed with its clean lines. There would be committees and legal titles and a woman at the Historical Society who would say, with a kind of professional hunger, "We must get a preservation grant."

She folded the small photograph she had kept from the signpost and slid it into the field book between the date and the neat column of coordinates. Then she made tea and sat at her kitchen window and watched the horizon for the cool flash of gulls over the reservoir. The town was a thing you could go to and measure, but it was also a thing that wanted, if you asked it gently, to be told its stories. She had told one small story that day, half in numbers and half in theft, and for the first time in many years felt the exact weight of both. The bones of the town would outlast the field book and the total station and maybe, if historians and weather were kind and if the county had a heart beneath its forms, someone would come and put a small plaque near the church foundation.

But she knew that no plaque could marshal the full idea of the place. The town existed in the rubbled mortar and the lost ledger and in the places your hands remembered how to touch. There are some things maps cannot hold: the particular ridicule of a dog's breath, the exact way a woman's laugh split in the doorway. You could, however, make sure the place remained a place that others could find again if they needed to. That, in the end, was what she had been asked to supply.

She turned the bracelet over in her fingers, the initials almost readable now under the badge of time, and thought of her mother wrapping her things and her own child-sized pockets. She set the bracelet down carefully on the page of her field book where she had written her private line and closed the book with the tilt of someone who knows how to pack away a day. The county server hummed like a distant machine. The reservoir breathed slowly under a sky that had no beginning and which only occasionally remembered it might one day return.
