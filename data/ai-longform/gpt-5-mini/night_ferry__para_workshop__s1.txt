The deck was a narrow place to be honest. The ferry's lights cut the dark into rectangles: square windows of sleeping cabins, the cafeteria's long strip of sodium glow, the telephone booth where a trucker leaned with his face hidden under the collar of an old coat. Wind took the sound of the engines and turned it into something softer, a low animal breathing, and the water took everything else. I stood with my palms on the steel rail and watched the shoreline thin to a suggestion, a smear of sodium lamps and an occasional house light that blinked like a hesitant promise. It might have been the first time in years I had let the sea move under me without looking for something to latch onto.

The call had been at lunch: an expressionless woman from the coroner's office had told me they'd pulled a body from the strait. She read me his name the way someone reads the name of a landlord you used to owe rent to — factually, then quickly, as if closing a file. She asked whether I could come identify. There were legal reasons, she said, ways the state needed a next of kin. There was the authority of procedure behind her but no pressure; it was a yes-or-no question that somehow carried with it the weight of a yes-or-no life.

I told her I would come.

In the time between the call and the ferry, I practiced a kind of thrift of feeling. You tell yourself you are doing the right thing in practical terms: the body needs a name, perhaps a family would want closure. But beneath that was a tangled script: guilt, a ledger of unresolved debts, the memory of a father's hands on a rusted steering wheel, the silence that settled over our house when one of us stopped answering. His face in old photographs: a thin kid with a crooked front tooth and a lip that never entirely stopped leaning toward a smile. A thousand small things that argued both for and against — for the possibility that this could be him, for the possibility that it could not.

I had told myself I would be calm. I brought a tube of photographs, the small stack tied with an elastic band — birthdays, a graduation I did not attend, a picture he had sent from a cheap motel in a town whose name had meant nothing to me — and one of those black-and-white school portraits with the cheesy backdrop, his hair combed down and his ears sticking out like two islands. I put them in my jacket pocket and felt the weight of them like a talisman.

On deck the wind smelled of iron and old fish, and every so often a wave would slap the hull with a dull smack that sent a vibration up through the soles of my shoes. The ferry rolled in a predictable, bureaucratic way, and the darkness beyond it was not empty so much as close — dense and unyielding, like felt. The stars were dim behind a smudge of cloud. Somewhere in the bowels of the ship, a generator hummed, untroubled by the human histories it carried.

There was a family in the corner, two children wrapped in neon blankets, whispering secrets that would crumble between them. A man in a navy cap smoked a cigarette and read a paperback. The crew moved like pale ghosts who had been taught to be practical about weather and sudden deaths. One of them — a woman with a long braid and hands that looked as if they had been softened by detergent rather than time — had asked, when I had bought my ticket, whether I was looking for anyone. I told her I was just going to identify a body and watched her blink as if the words caught her off-guard, like a foreign tongue.

"You're brave," she said.

Maybe I was brave, or maybe this was the only way I knew to be cowardly — to cross water instead of crossing a street into the morgue and signing something there and never finding out anything at all. The ferry had a timetable; grief had none. I let the engines steer me, and with the dark making a muffler of the world, I tried to remember what it had been like to be a child with him — the way he could invent dares like weather, the way his laughter could lift the whole room for no rational reason. I remembered the way he fell from a bicycle on a summer afternoon and lay on the pavement, sobbing not from pain but from humiliation, and I remember thinking, then, that humiliation and I had the same hair.

When I closed my eyes on the deck, I could see him standing on the rocks behind our old house, ankle-deep in the cold wash, daring the sea to take him. He would stand with his arms spread, not like a flier but like an offer; he expected the ocean to catch him the way it catches driftwood. I had watched him and known, with a child's certainty, that he would be all right because he always had a way of getting out of things. Then he grew older and made different choices. There are ways people drift apart that feel like accidents at first: a missed birthday, a name not returned. Then there are ways they drift apart that feel like punishment: a promise broken in a way that cannot be repaired by apologies. We had drifted in both kinds of ways until there was a hollow where a brother should have been.

The ferry's cafeteria was empty except for a man who introduced himself to the emptiness with a laugh that filled the linoleum floor. I ordered coffee and watched the steam gather and disperse above the cup, wondering at the little rituals that anchor us: sugar, cream, the way both the coffee and the ferry had the same smell of maintenance and compromise. The ticket agent had given me a folder at the harbor office — forms, a copy of the report, a photocopy of the last known address. The body had been found by fishermen, she said, tangled in rope and some blue netting. The coroner's brief description had been evasive: "adult male, approximately thirty to forty years old, clothing degraded, decomposition advanced; further identification required." Forensic facts that might as well have been a weather report.

I slept in a small cabin, the way one does on boats: lying flat, suspended, the ship breathing and my body learning its rhythm. Boats are honest in a way that cities are not; they reveal movement the way a truth reveals a scar. I dreamt of my brother as an animal I once had fed: thin-ribbed, hungry, and sure I would not fail him. I woke with my hand against the pocket where the photographs lay, and for a long minute my fingers traced their edges like a priest checking an altar.

On the dock at the other side there was fog, the kind that hides everything but insists on proximity. The harbor lights became soft moons at the edges of visibility. A coroner met me under the awning, an older man with a face that had been softened by decades of looking at bodies. He spoke in the muted cadence of someone who had found his way past shock into a resigned curiosity. He said, "We have the remains with us. We can take you to the office, get it done."

The mortuary was a block-stone building with fluorescent corridors and a coffee machine that dispensed something approximating life. It had the smell of disinfectant and lemon that is intended to deny the other smells it is designed to cover up. The woman behind the desk asked me questions about my relationship to the missing man — had I last seen him? When? Had there been trouble between us? The questions lodged like pebbles in my mouth. The truth is not a neat coin you can pay with; it's more often a string of small evasions. I told her the last time I had seen him was a phone call three years ago, that we had grown apart gradually and then suddenly, a seam that had finally given way. It was enough.

They led me to a small room that smelled of lemon wax and something else I could not name. A shrouded form lay on a slab, the sheet drawn up over a face as though a tender hand had tried to guard against what was left. The coroner moved with a gentleness at odds with his job; he wore gloves and handled the sheet as if a paper score had been entrusted to him. "Are you sure you want to do this?" he asked.

"I'm sure," I said, but the word felt used, as if from an era when certainty was not adaptive but decorative. The sheet came off in a slow reveal like the lifting of a curtain. For a moment I thought I was seeing not him but a stranger's wrong answer to the question of what he had once looked like. The face beneath was pocked and swollen in places where the sea had not been kind, the hair thin and matted with whatever the water had fed it. Even the light in an antiseptic room is a kind of theft — it shows what you have no right to see and then asks you to turn it into memory.

I had the photographs. I took them out, fingers fumbling, and held one up beside the face. The coroner watched my hands. There is a bureaucratic etiquette to identifying a body: you look at the face, you compare notes, you provide a signature that will allow a coffin to be ordered. On a table beside the slab were things that had clung to the body when it was found: a flattened cigarette case, a ring with no stone, a frayed wallet that had been mostly eaten by water. The wallet had an ID card with a name, but water had smudged it into illegibility. The cigarette case was a brand he had smoked as a boy: small, cheap, the sort that is more a tool than an indulgence. I remembered how he had called it "fuel" once. None of these things were proof.

I looked until my vision blurred. The face had features that might have been his: the slope of the nose, the curve of the upper lip. There was a faint scar along the jawline that I half-remembered — a childhood memory, maybe, of a fight with a bike chain or a door. It could have been anything. The coroner gave me a minute and left the room. Alone with the body and my photographs, I felt like a thief who has broken into his own past.

I kept thinking about the way we argued the last time I saw him — or rather, the last time he tried to see me. He had called me one evening from a payphone near a truck stop and said he wanted to borrow money. My instinct, then as now, was to protect myself. We had shared no bank accounts, no real estate, only a history that did not translate into help. I had refused. His voice on the other end of the line had been small and raw. He had said, "I thought you might be there," and I had heard something in it like a brim of hope. I had not been there. The conversation had ended with us each being exactly as we were meant to be: separate. For months after that I told myself a refusal had been a practical thing. It might have been the reason we never mended the seam. Or it might not. Regret is a muscle that will grow around any truth you give it.

Back in the room the light was bright and the light was cruel. The face did not match the precise geometry of my memory, and memory is a bad measuring instrument — it enlarges the beloved or burns it to ash. The coroner reappeared, palms up in the way of someone who had the habit of offering not conclusions but processes. "You can take your time," he said. "You can go through the items. If it helps, we can do dental records or DNA, but that takes time."

We stood on the stupid hinge between truth and expedience. The practical man inside me — the one who paid taxes on time and kept his car battery charged and avoided complication — wanted the certainty of dental records. The other man — a man who carried photographs tied with an elastic and felt a hunger he could not rationalize — wanted to be decisive and quick. If I said yes, the body would be labeled with a name, arrangements could start, a coffin could be made, an obituary could be printed with a date and a list of survivors. If I said no, the corpse might rest unknown, identified by a number and a coroner's file. I thought about my father and whether an anonymous son was worse than no son at all.

I thought of my brother's laugh, the way it had the power to make my chest loosen. I thought of the ring he had once worn, a cheap thing with a name scratched inside. I thought of the cigarette case and of the ID that had been smudged but not entirely erased. The body had a burn across one knuckle, thin and pale, as if from a cigarette or a red-hot iron. I had seen the same burn on his hand in a photograph where he had been holding a mug, that same place on that knuckle catching the light like an argument. It could have been coincidence. It could have been enough.

I slid the photograph closer and placed it next to his mouth. The dental alignment was different now; a missing tooth at the front in one photograph matched a gap here if you allowed for swelling. Our faces change, softening and hardening like cliffs under weather, and time and sea had been the worst sort of weather. I had no expertise in the geometry of faces. I had an elastic-tied stack that hummed with the rightness of youth. In the end my decision was less a judgement of identity than an act of naming; I decided that naming would be my small defiance against the indifferent machinery of loss.

"Yes," I said, and it was not a single sound but a condensation of too many things. The coroner wrote my name in a ledger with a green pen, the nib hissing. He did not look surprised. He had seen acts like this before, small reconciliations slapped together at the last minute by people who carry their lives in pockets and hallways.

Later, under different lights and with different forms, I had to give more information. I signed my name with a trembling hand and answered questions about last known addresses and possible allergies. The formalities were slow and necessary like knots. The mortuary offered to contact the agency that dealt with unclaimed bodies, or the public funeral fund, but I told them I would arrange it myself. There was no one else to ask, really. There were distant cousins, friends-of-friends, a name or two on a social media page that had not been updated in years, but none who would show up and hold the kind of claim a brother could. In absence of proof I offered to be present.

The coroner said, flatly, that identifying the body as my brother would start a legal chain: he would be released to me or to a service I hired. There would be forms, and the state would close the file in its ledger. The language around death is efficient and cold, meant to sterilize the element of human guesswork. The mortuary director, a woman with a chain of keys at her waist, gave me a brochure about burial options and another about refrigeration and transport. It was as if you could pick a ritual from a catalog the way you pick paint. You could schedule a wake between nine and noon on a Tuesday and choose whether the urn would be polished oak or simple pine. There was comfort in such choice. It was absurd in another.

Outside, the night had turned colder, and the harbor wind had the taste of old metal that you forget until the arteries of the city remind you. I walked along the quay until my feet ached and then doubled over at an angle where the sea looked like an endless blanket. I thought about the life that had belonged to the man on the slab. He had been able to find places where people did not notice him; he had limned his days between pit stops and cheap motels, between men who had been willing to lend and then forget. There is a shame in choosing that life and a different shame in judging it from a safe shore. I had judged him. I had refused him. I had been practical. I had not been brave.

Behind me the ferry's horn sounded, a long note that carried like an unsettling memory. I pictured the body crated and strapped and then lying inert in the back of a van with the heater running in case the driver was sentimental. I imagined digging a grave on a hillside, a place where the leeward side of the wind would keep the cold away. The ritual is often a way to argue with the fact of absence: you build a box and bury it with precise action, as if precision could fill an emptiness.

There was a grief that was not the shape of tears but the weight of a decision. Claiming the body was not a confession that this was definitively him but an act of possession against loss. In choosing, I made an agreement: I would stand and, if necessary, tell the lie that the body belonged to my brother. I would not speak it as a lie — I would speak it as a salvage operation, an attempt to give someone a name rather than a number. There is a difference between lying and telling the truth that you have decided to live in. It is a small comfort, but sometimes those are the only comforts available.

The next day we loaded the body onto a service van. There was no fanfare, only a sliding of metal doors and the soft buckling of straps. The mortuary director handed me a contract and an envelope with information about cremation and burial sites. I read until the print blurred; legalese is the hydra of our times, and I signed where they asked. The driver, a man with hands full of calluses, smiled in ways people do when they are good at their jobs and bad at sentiment: hospitable and uninsistent. He asked whether I wanted to come, and I told him I would. I had to tell myself, in the van's enclosed warmth, that to come was not to perform affection but to perform responsibility.

We drove back to the harbor where the ferry's schedule meant bodies and people and trucks met with the same indifferent choreography. The ferry's crew prepared a small room for the transfer, a corridor with a hose of bright in the overhead lights. The van doors opened, and I watched the black shape of a stretcher load up like something reanimated. Then the ferry became my vessel again, not of a crossing I had planned but of a transport I was now responsible for. The crew wrapped the stretcher in heavy cloth and secured it in a space usually allocated to the sick or to excess baggage. It was ridiculous and ordinary at once: a body cushioned beneath a seat next to a man carrying a tin of smoked salmon.

I sat in the passenger lounge while the ferry gathered speed and turned toward the wide part of the strait. The stretcher lay where the crew had put it: between a vending machine and a bank of unused seats, the body anonymous beneath layers of cloth and straps. It struck me then that our acts of closure are often clumsy and public — a contrition performed in a place where strangers are still required to move around you. The ferry ride back felt like a second baptism. The sea that had given up its body had no interest in who claimed it.

I thought of how many men cross seas and never see the men they once were. We imagine ourselves as fixed and then find that time and water are better judges. I felt a small, stubborn tenderness that had nothing to do with forgiveness: I had made a promise to give the motion of a funeral to a life that had had none. It felt like the most I could do. If the identification had been wrong, then I had carried a stranger home. That was a dangerous thing to do, but the alternative — letting the body rest unknown — felt like an act of cowardice toward memory.

On the deck, as the ferry cut through black water, the horizon was a thin, electric line. I held the hospital bracelet the mortuary had given me — a strip with a bar code that would become paperwork and then finality. It wasn't jewelry. It was an instrument of procedure and my signature attached to it like a promise. I read the name printed there, pronounced it aloud in a place where the sea refused to answer and where the only reply was wind: "Evan."

Saying his name was not a healing; it was a naming. Names are small, stubborn things: they can keep the dead in your mouth long after they have no breath left to answer. I felt both the muteness and the power of what I had done. The deck hummed. The ship's wake frothed in a long scar of white.

When the ferry docked, I walked through a narrow ramp and watched the crew move the stretcher with efficient gentleness. The city at the dock was itself sleepy, and the cold made everyone quick. I arranged a funeral with a small director whose face had the look of someone who knew the economy of sorrow and the means by which it is sometimes legally facilitated. The coffin would be plain wood. We spoke about music; I asked for something simple, not hymns but a song he had liked in another life, a record with words he had loved because it had argued with the quiet in ways he did not. He had been taken by the sea and we were going to give him land.

There were formalities that required me to be explicit about my relationship to him. I said the words "brother" and felt them land with both relief and a sense of violation, as if I had been given a title I had not earned and had accepted it in the posthumous convenience of a social construct. The funeral director asked about family photographs and about a biography to include in the program. I did not know whether to write the thing that had been or a sanitized version. In the end I took the elastic-bound stack out, and with fingers that found their certainty in private, I chose which images to include. I kept the crooked-toothed school portrait and the picture of him at twenty-three, hair longer than it should have been, smiling at a camera that had asked for more than we were willing to give.

The day of the funeral the sky was low and apologetic. A small number of people attended: a neighbor from down the road who had once fed him soup, a woman who said she had known him briefly in a tradesman's block and had liked his laugh, a man in his fifties who seemed to have been a friend-of-a-friend and whose eyes held the element of shared secrecy. I spoke at the grave because someone had to speak. I told the story that was true and manageable: that he had been my brother and that despite our estrangements I had felt an obligation to him in the only way he allowed me — with a name on a form and a coffin on a hill.

There were moments of absurdity: a wren that insisted on hopping along the fence, a mourner who cried in a way that sounded like a cough. The funeral director handled the lowering with the same care as everything else; the coffin went down and the soil was flung in small, messy shovelfuls. When the last of us had thrown in our handfuls, the hole was level and the ceremony was finished. People lingered as if the end of a public script required a few extra beats to feel plausible. I smoked a cigarette, an old vice that seemed to connect me to a man who had kept small sparks alive to steady himself.

That night I found myself back on the same ferry, the same route, but the return passage felt different, as if the sea itself had registered some small alteration and was planning a different weather. The ferry's engines were steady; the water took the light of the town and swallowed it. I stood on the deck and watched the swell under the hull like a pulse. I thought about the funerals that make things better and the ones that do not. I thought about how identification is less about certainty and more about the space we make for memory to take root. I had claimed a body on a ledger, but what I had claimed most of all was the right to say his name in public and to tell a story about who he had been. That was not an act of absolution. It was a concession and, perhaps, a reparation in the smallest coin I could pay.

The ferry cut through night, and the sea answered in a long, dark voice. I kept my hands on the railing because the movement of the ship was an honest thing, and in the honesty of motion I allowed myself a brief indulgence — a lullaby I had not sung in years, a memory of two boys crouched on the rocks at low tide, daring one another to go further until the cold bit both legs. I could see his profile in the memory, half-silhouetted, his hair sticking to the side of his face, a smear of light falling across it that made him not myth but a person who had once leaned toward every edge.

When we docked, I walked off the ferry carrying the quiet of what I had done. There was paperwork to finish and an urn to decide upon and a house to visit where his rooms would be cleared. Grief did not present itself in grand gestures but in a ledger of small responsibilities: a number to call, a form to forward, an old sweater found in the back of a closet and put into a plastic bag. In a way, that was where lives ended and began: in the domestic arithmetic of handling what remains.

A week after the funeral I sat at my father's table and told him what I had done. He listened the way old men listen — with an attention that is as much about past habit as it is about present interest. He did not ask whether the body had been his son, only whether I had given him a decent burial. I told him yes, and in the telling I felt a small relief like the easing of an old stone that had been trapping my foot. "You did what was right," he said, and he believed it. He had been a man of hard choices and harder silence. His approval was a thing I had once wanted like air; now it felt like a surprising gift.

There are people who imagine identification as a stamp of irrevocable knowledge, but it is more often a pact negotiated for the living. We mark the dead with names to ease our living, to let us know where to place our small rituals. I had chosen to name, not because I was certain but because I refused to leave that man, whatever he had been, nameless. The sea had been indifferent; the state had been efficient; my life had gone on between receipts and small consolations. But naming him had altered my day's ledger. It had been, in the end, a way of admitting that we are tied by more than blood: by the old, inconvenient fact of shared history, by the scrap of being that makes two boys on a shore into "brothers."

When I stood again on the ferry months later — this time with a heavier coat and an absence that had settled into my shoulders like a familiar garment — I looked at the place where the town's lights would appear and then fade. The sea kept its secrets. It had given up what it had taken and would take more in time. I felt, oddly, less empty than I had expected. Identifying a body had been less about deciding who a man was and more about deciding what you owed him. I had owed him the right to a name, and in giving it, I had given myself the right to stop calculating his absence as a debt and start, instead, to let it be a particular kind of silence: one that knows the score and does not have to tally it again.

The ferry hummed beneath me. The horizon was a thin blade of light. I let the wind ruffle my hair and thought of small things: how the cigarette case had fit in his palm, the way he had laughed at my poor attempts at fishing, how once he had stood in the doorway and told me he was going out and would be back in time for tea. I closed my fingers around a photograph in my pocket and held it like proof and like prayer. The sea moved on, and the ferry took me home.
