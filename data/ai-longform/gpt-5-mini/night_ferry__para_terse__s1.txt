I crossed the northern strait on an overnight ferry to identify a body that might, in some miserable logic, be my estranged brother's.

The crossing held nothing romantic for me; there were no lighthouse-keepers or heroic rescues in my memory, only the low mechanical thrum of engines and the smell of diesel and stale coffee. The ferry slid through water that had the color of old coins, the steel hull slicing up tiny galaxies of foam into the wake. Outside, the lights of the coastline blinked like distant constellations, sparse and indifferent. In the cafeteria, fluorescent lights hummed above a cluster of men with windburnt cheeks and the polite, defeated laughter of people who had spent their lives knowing rain and wind and nothing else. I sat alone at a table with a plastic cup that had been reused too many times and a plate with a sandwich that was more concept than food. Every now and then I found myself staring at my hands as if they belonged to someone else—thin, stained at the knuckles from years of typing and dishwashing and small betrayals, the nails bitten to a quick white horseshoe.

I had told no one I was going. There was no one left to tell. There had been a time when my life was full of small speaking voices: my mother folding clothes in the living room, my father humming as he mended a net, Daniel—Dan—interrupting a sentence to make some absurd joke so we would all laugh. Those voices had retreated and then gone, like the tide, leaving shapes on the sand that the wind erased. I had not spoken to Dan in five years—longer, if I counted the slowness of a silence that grows like frost in a windowpane—and yet when the call came that a body had been found on the rocks near the mouth of the strait, and the man at the other end asked, in the cautious way officials always do when they are about to proceed into someone's private grief, "Do you have any family who can identify it?" the first name my mouth almost said was his.

There were reasons we stopped speaking. Some of them were ordinary, like the way small resentments can gather and freeze into a wall: the day he took the last of the fishing tackle my father had left and sold it for a beer, the time I didn't answer him because I thought whatever it was would be easier to ignore than to argue about. Some were larger and hurt deeper—my leaving, for one thing. I had walked away from the village with a sack on my back and a cheap ticket to the city and never come home to fix the roof or to argue about how much of the shore was ours. Dan stayed. He built a life of loose attachments: a job that paid poorly but kept him near the water, friends who were more like fellow travelers than anchors, a string of half-lived loves. He wrote a little, sometimes—postcards, most of them—and sent me a single photograph that I kept folded inside a wallet I no longer used. The photograph was of him on the pier, grinning as if he had just stolen the sky.

I thought, in those first stunned moments after the call, of the different faces a body can wear: when you're a child you imagine the dead as permanent, fixed examples of what not to be; when you're older you picture them as altered, as props in possible futures that fold in on themselves. I pictured Dan's face and then the face of the body the coroner's assistant had described: "male, mid-forties, stocky, injuries consistent with a fall onto rocks. No identification on him." That sounded less like a person than like an inventory. The assistant had asked, in the telephone's flat voice, whether anyone would come to identify—because there were rules, and the police liked rules, and because sometimes families were waiting to be spared the scene of other's endings. I had said nothing. I had been sitting at a kitchen table in a small apartment that smelled of yesterday's tea and lemon cleaner and the soft electric hum of my radiator. On the counter, bills sat in a neat, terrible pile. My landlord's letter was tucked under a stack of grocery receipts. Life's insistencies made the thought of going absurdly difficult, but the other thought—of not going—was an ache I could feel like a muscle pulled too tight.

I found a berth on the overnight ferry, a narrow bunk that looked like something from childhood and less comfortable. The cabin rocked gently as if the ship were trying to lull me into forgetting why I had booked the trip. A woman in the bunk above mine snored with the rhythm of someone who had made peace with sleep. Through the thin wall, the boom of the engine set up a low conversation that sounded like a distant train. I could not read; pages blurred. I could not sleep. So I stared at my hands and let my mind go the way it always did, which was backward.

Our childhood was small: a house that leaned a little toward the wind, a garden where the dill and nettles grew in wild patches, a pier that had more stories attached to it than planks. We were boys who learned our geometry in the way the tide measured us, in the mapping of rocks and hunger. Our father taught us to mend and to wait, because waiting was how the sea returned what you had lost as if by accident. He taught us to watch for the markers that sailors wrote on the horizon: a crooked chimney, a leaning birch, the black headland like a fist. He also taught us how to be silent about certain things. The silence was part of the house, a fixture like the stove. He died when we were young men; he died with hands half-closed around a rope. I was away, so they said. Dan had been at his side. There was a funeral in a weathered church, hymns that the choir sang without feeling and an old priest who spoke like a man reading from a list. I remember standing outside on the cold grass while people spoke in that soft way they do when they think sorrow absolves them of responsibility, and I remember Dan hitting me softly on the shoulder as if he were reminding me that I had not yet learned how to be present enough for someone else's grief. The economy of accusation between us settled there, a small gray stone that we carried back into the house like a kind of private ballast.

After he carved his anger into a steady grievance, we stopped speaking. The small rituals of family—Christmas, birthdays, visits—fell away like old wallpaper. I thought then I was running for reasons that did not involve him. I told myself I had to get away from the smell of the place, from the constant accounting of favors; he told me I was cowardly, that I had abandoned them; he wrote me letters that began with "You left, you left." In return I stopped returning calls. Years gained distance like sediment. I told myself I was building a life, which is to say I was building a defense. Every now and then, in the cold of a city winter, I would look at that photograph and feel a soft surge of guilt like a tide that reached only to my boots.

On the ferry, other people's small conversations slid past me. Two fishermen argued about whether the spring would be good. A young woman with dyed hair cried into a paper napkin, a man beside her making awkward attempts to console as if rehearsing for the future. The ship's announcements came in laconic bursts. "Passengers are reminded that smoking is prohibited in passenger areas." "The bar will be closing shortly." I felt old enough to know the weight of an announced fact and young enough to still be surprised at the permanent nature of the sea: it would do to me as it had done to others, it would not be impressed by my intention.

The crossing seemed a preamble in which the world held its breath. As dawn came, the sky stripped of its color like an old sheet, the land reassembled itself on the horizon—gray roofs, a church spire that leaned the way our old chimney did. The ferry docked with a positive thump. People drifted off like bleached driftwood, and I walked down the gangway with a small overnight bag with a toothbrush and a few articles of clothing and a photograph folded in my pocket like contraband.

The police station was behind a single glass door, the type where the heat inside lapped at the windows and the street outside looked colder for it. Inside, the waiting room smelled of lemon disinfectant and regret. A woman behind the desk had an authoritative look that was only partly made of uniform: a clear jaw, hair pinned back, eyes that had learned the weight and pattern of sorrow. She looked at me like someone checking a claim. "You called yesterday," she said, as if that were the first test. "Do you want to see the body?"

Her question had a briskness that obscured its cruelty. I might have refused—there are people who prefer to keep death a private story, who will let labs and forms do the necessary naming—but something in me wanted the proof of it to be of my own eyes. I wanted to see whether the thing in the cold room had Dan's laugh-creases, his small, stubborn nose, the scar on his left elbow where he had once fallen off a ladder. I expected to feel something like a key turning in a lock when I saw the face: an unlocking that would either open the door to grief or let me close it forever.

The attendant led me through corridors that smelled of bleach, past posters about drug awareness and domestic violence, and into a room where the light was a clinical white that didn't table small shadows but flattened them. The body lay on a stainless-steel table, a sheet pulled up to the neck. There was some plywood around the room, a cart with implements like a small altar of state, and a television in the corner playing a weather report on mute. The attendant asked if I wanted the sheet lifted. I said yes, because I wanted to be the one to decide whether a dead man would be my brother.

When the sheet came away, the light fell differently. There was no flourish, no drama: just a face turned slightly, one ear visible, a lip split by dried blood, an angularness to the jaw I'd never noticed in life because I had only known Dan at certain angles—the way people present themselves to family. The face had the fainted, waxen quality of water; it was as if the sea had polished him and left him to lean against the inside of a glass. My first thought was that he looked both like himself and like a stranger—the paradox that the dead often are, a mirror that refuses to show anything but what you bring to it. Close to the temple there was a smudge of old scar tissue, a small chipped crescent we had both earned when we were nine and tried to climb our father's mast in a gale. My throat closed at the sight of it. My hand found the edge of the table, feeling the coolness of the metal through the glove of blue latex the attendant had suggested I wear. There was a small tobacco-brown freckle cluster under the left eye that had always been Dan's; it was there, now dimmed like an island in fog.

The coroner—tall, polite, wearing a tie like a bond—had a notepad. "Do you need time?" he asked. It was a courtesy, stage-managed perhaps, but real enough. I could have said I didn't need to see, that recognition isn't always about a face, that we know our dead by calls and by deeds. No, I wanted to know. I wanted the concrete.

I ran my fingertips lightly, in the most private and forbidden of gestures, along the back of his hand. The skin felt thin, paper-cold. My fingers met a knuckle that had been scraped in some fight, a place where his skin had been roughened in a way only time and seamstress jobs give. I tried to remember the exact shape of the laugh lines, the way his eyebrows flew up when he was lying. I tried to read a life off a set of features, a stupid, human way to make sense of what we can't grasp.

The coroner had a care about systems. He suggested we check for dental records and fingerprints, that we'd have to do the routine: fingerprints and maybe DNA if needed. He used the phrase "we'll want to be certain." Those words landed like distant pebbles. I knew about certainty in grief; it could be purchased and signed for, but some certainties require time and money and a willingness to admit you were wrong. I thought about my mother, how she had kept a small tin of buttons in the attic, each one pressed with a story. I thought about the photograph in my pocket: Dan on the pier, laughter caught mid-flight. I thought about the way memory is less like film than it is like a room you paint over constantly; the colors change and sometimes you paint them to hide cracks.

I left the table. It was not a dramatic departure. No one followed me into the restroom with a camera, no one whispered. The world had been altered by a small act, by me having looked and by me having not pronounced the final word. The coroner had been asked, professionally, to collect contact details. He did. "If it's him," he said, as if that clause could anchor anything, "we'll need a next of kin to sign the release. There's paperwork, and the funeral home—"

"If it's him," I echoed. My voice was thin. "It's him, then. I—I'll sign."

That was a lie of omission, perhaps. I did not tell him I had not been in his life for years, that I had no bank account with their name, that I had no immediate claim in any administrative sense. I signed the form under the name we'd both been given, as if a signature could make kin out of convenience or will.

Later, in a small room that smelled of coffee gone bad and human failure, the policewoman who had called me sat across from me with his few effects laid out like evidence of small domestic life: a wristwatch with a snapped leather band, a set of keys on a plastic ring with a dolphin charm that my mother had once given us when we were small—silly to keep something like that, but Dan had liked small talismans—and a journal, its pages water-wrinkled and ink-smudged. The entries were not long, often single sentences: "Weather fair, tide up," "Met L." "Need to fix net." Every now and then a paragraph would unfold, full of complaint and humor, the voice I remembered leaning into the page like a man leaning into a good story.

"There's nothing here to ID him by except you," the woman said, plain and practical. "We tried the dental, but there were no records on file. We checked the missing persons lists for the county. Nothing matches exactly. We can send for dental or fingerprints, but it takes time. Will you sign to release him to the funeral home?"

I held the small dolphin keychain in my palm. It was silly and absurdly small and warmed by the life it had known. The keychain made my chest tighten. "He had a thing for trinkets," I said, as if the trinket needed my approval. "He liked to collect things. That dolphin—Mom gave us matching ones when we were kids. He always kept his on his keys. He'd keep them for luck."

The woman wrote on a clipboard. "Do you have any contact with family?"

"No," I lied, or I misled, curiously, a distinction that felt important because it made me feel like I wasn't wholly to blame. "Not close. I've been away."

She looked at me, then at the keychain, the watch. "Well, you'll have to decide. Do you want him released to your care for burial?"

In that moment the practical converged with the moral. To sign meant I took responsibility—funerals needed money, arrangements, someone to speak at the graveside and unspool a version of the past that would make sense to others. To not sign meant the state would bury him, as nameless, perhaps, or with a tag that read "unknown." What did he want? What would he have wanted? I remembered him once saying he didn't trust funerals; they made the dead into a lie. He had his own poetry about the sea that was disagreed with by the practice of mourning. I could see him telling me, if he were alive, that he didn't want fuss. If he were dead, if this was him, what was my duty? To the truth, the story, the man we had been together?

By the time my hand had found the pen, the words that left my mouth felt like a transaction and like a confession. "Release him to me," I said.

Transporting a body is an odd kind of theft, a borrowing of someone's permanence. The funeral home was small and smelled like lilies and the polish on older wood. A man with an easy voice and hands like someone who'd lifted pianos showed me their chapel. He spoke about embalming and about cremation with the detachment of someone who must be kind to strangers suffering. I selected a simple pine box because it seemed honest enough. They asked for a name for the paperwork. I hesitated. On paper he would have a name, and names have weight.

"My name is Daniel—" I started, and then corrected, "Daniel Rowan." The act of writing his name in the logs felt like putting a pin into a map. After that, arrangements slid forward as if truth had been served with a ledger. The funeral home took him gently into a refrigerated room that smelled of cleaner and cedar, and I walked out with a receipt and the sense that now I had entered into the work of finishing a life.

In the days that followed, I did what is required of those who claim kin: I called an aunt I hadn't spoken to in years—her voice initially sharp with the pinch of being disturbed; at my telling, she folded herself into a softness I had seen before when something long buried was unearthed. She made tea, she remembered small things—my father's way of calling us "the wind-children" because we were restless—and her grief was an old, stitched thing that unraveled gently at the edges. People arrived who had once been acquaintances and now took shape as mourners: men who had known Dan's laugh, a woman who had worn his ring and left it years ago with an elegy of lipstick smudged at the rim. The parish organist, stooped, with fingers that had played for decades, arrived and told stories about his xylophone of winter storms. We arranged the funeral with a mechanic's precision and an emotional amateurishness that felt like carrying a body over a slippery deck.

The weeks became a procession of small tasks: paperwork, the filling of forms for death certificates, the purchase of flowers that folded and browned in the back of my car. At night, I would sit in my rented room and read Dan's journal until the words became porous, as if the ink had been waterlogged by the sea and the pages themselves turned into tide-maps. He wrote of days on the water and of a woman named Laila, whose name reappeared like a mark on a shoreline; the entries were often fragmentary, and sometimes they read like a man exhaling before a storm. There were certain lines I kept, underlined in my head: "Sometimes the boat is a prayer," he had written. "Sometimes people are nothing but sea-weather." I started to understand the truth in the ambiguity: we are often worse and better known in our scraps.

The funeral came as a bright, unrelenting day: sunlight through the clouds, too pure for the mood. People filled the small chapel with the kind of hush that is part respect and part curiosity. They looked at the coffin as if gauging the accuracy of their grief. I spoke because it was expected, and I had practiced a speech in the nights with my palms pressed to the pillow, where sentences came out like soft rocks. I told them a few small truths—about the pier, about his laugh, about how he once taught me to tie a clove hitch—and left the rest unsaid. People cried. Someone clapped the hymn like he had been applauding is a bad play, and an elder woman came up and pinched my cheek as if to make sure I was there.

After the burial, the coffee was terrible and poured in excess like a kindness. People clustered like birds at feeders, passing small condolences like seed. There were offers—"If there's anything you need"—that sounded both emptier and more generous than the people who offered them perhaps intended. In the days that unfurled afterward, I found my phone filled with messages from friends and relatives who wanted to say the right thing. "I'm so sorry," "He will be missed," "If you need anything." Some called me by name and asked for details. Most of them had an assumption about his life—a narrative that fit their memory of him into a mold. My aunt sent a photograph: Dan and me as boys, squinting against the sun, our arms around each other like an offer and a challenge both.

After the funeral, the legal trappings began to become apparent. There was the matter of his few possessions, of the key to a flat that held the ghost of his life—books with little notes in the margins, a kettle with mineral stains, a half-completed crossword with his handwriting penciled in, his small winter scarf that still smelled faintly of him. There were creditors, there was a landlord's notice, there was a simple will that did not name me or anyone else. The parish requested whether he had any known relatives by blood—they asked the question as if blood could be measured and easily traced. I answered that I was his brother and that would have to be enough.

Once the official apparatus had finished its forms, the coroner's office sent a short letter, like a late echo, with the lab results. Dental comparisons had been inconclusive; the body was badly damaged in places that showed the most distinguishing features had been battered by the tide and the rocks. DNA tests, the letter said with cool politeness, were pending. The police had closed their file pending identification outcomes. I read the letter twice, then a third time, and then folded it carefully into my wallet where it sat with the photograph of Dan on the pier, as if formally recorded uncertainty could be kept as an artifact.

I had a dream soon after, or rather a recurring image: a man standing knee-deep in water, holding up a pocket mirror to catch the light. The mirror reflected a boy's face and then a man's and then a blank, shallow well. I woke every time with my hands in the air as if I had been holding something that would have burned me to bring back.

There were many small absurdities that grew out of this concession. I received a bill from the funeral home with my name on it, of course, and in the small print they asked for next of kin details. I had to sign a form releasing them of liability and confirming I would pay. I wrote my address. Each time I filled in a line with his name I felt like a witness at a conjuring. There was also the modest reversal: as the days went on, people who had drifted from Dan popped up like shells on a beach, some to express sorrow, some to insist on old debts, some to claim memories. They came with stories that were affectionate and complicated and sometimes contradictory: that he had been restless but generous, that he had been unreliable and loving in equal measure.

I began, without intending to, to understand the duplicity of grief. Sometimes it felt like a magnifying glass, making certain small kindnesses large. Sometimes it felt like silence pressed into a room and then reshaped by those who survived. I had chosen—deliberately, clumsily—to fill a role that had been vacant and which perhaps belonged to someone else, to be the public name on a death certificate. I had done it for reasons I could not entirely inventory: guilt, the desire to give him a burial, the hope that by being his brother I could be closer to an end than I had ever allowed myself to be. I wanted, in some quiet core, to believe that claiming him would close something.

And yet the matter of certainty remained like a small raw sore. The DNA test lingered like an unresolved chord. I would not have had the stomach to look at the report if it had come back negative, and yet if it came back positive, what would change? The dead man would remain the same dead man; our histories were already written in the margins of other people's memories. I had tried to carry the weight of him and of our shared past in the simple act of burial, as if ceremony could rewrite the ledger.

Months passed in a manner I could chart by trivialities—by the way the tides came to coincide inadvertently with my returns, by the migrating of birds that signaled a new season. The final letter from the coroner came in an envelope with no pretense to gentleness. DNA had matched him, they wrote; he was my brother. The evidence was clear and clean. I felt a curious lightness at first, as if a foot I had placed on a threshold had found a step. Then I felt the gravity of all the small choices that had brought us to that registry. Families don't require DNA to grieve each other; they only require presence. But presence had become transactional, and now the paperwork confirmed what I had done.

In the months after, I kept the dolphin keychain in my pocket sometimes and took it out to watch it catch the light. I visited the pier he had loved, sat on the bench and listened to the gulls make mathematics out of the air. Sometimes I could imagine, absurdly, that he was in some small way still out there—someone emptier and fuller mixed at once. I would speak to him sometimes, under a sky that had the same shrill light of the day we agreed to bury him. "You were stubborn," I would say, "and petty and kind." The sheer fullness of contradiction in him made me think that perhaps he had always exceeded any neat account.

There is a strange currency in these things. To identify someone is to name them as belonging to you, and in that naming you claim a kind of authority. I had done that, and the letter, the official match of genes, confirmed my decision. Yet there were other truths that the match could not resolve: the gulf that had once existed between us remained; my confession—my signing his release—had not fixed that old, live wound. Sometimes that felt like a moral failure; other times it felt like honesty. The funeral had been one of those odd ceremonies that cleansed without repairing. It taught me a lesson about the limits of ritual: you can close the register and still be left with the unswept room.

The ferry took me back at the next crossing. The sea was the same shape as it had been when I came, but the world is always different on return. I had the small relief of being able to carry in my pocket the keychain, the smell of the sea on my clothes like a small blessing and a small accusation. I watched the wake as if it contained everything I did not know how to say. There were no lighthouses to guide me now, only the small lamps of the life I had resumed. People aboard the ferry looked at me as if I might be a man they had met before; no one recognized me as the one who had been at the coffin. I liked the anonymity; it held the quiet possibility of beginning, which is itself a kind of mourning and a kind of modest hope.

At home, the photograph of Dan on the pier folded into my wallet like a secret I kept for myself. The city outside the ferry's narrow life continued to have a thousand small troubles. I paid the funeral's bill with a careful hand and a small budget; my aunt sent a letter of thanks that smelled faintly of the lavender she liked to keep in the curtains. The legalside of things closed in ways that were necessary: forms stamped, boxes ticked, the mail with its gray envelopes now sated with closure. But the private parts of the story—the things that mattered about us—remained unformalized. There were stories I never told at the funeral, about times we stole candy from Mrs. Liddell's shop and ran until we couldn't breathe, about the way he once stayed up all night to fix a leaking roof while I slept because he thought the house deserved it. There were apologies I never made and perhaps didn't need to make and perhaps should have. I had said at the graveside, "I forgive you," in a ceremonial way, and someone had taken it as a balm. Perhaps it had been. Perhaps forgiveness is a small work: a hand smoothing a wrinkled sheet.

Some nights I still wake with the image of his face in the cold room. In the nascent dark I reach for him in the way one reaches for a memory that will not quite be made. I am aware that identity is an odd loom weaved from claims and absolutions and the thin threads of coincidence. I am aware that I had, in a way, wished to reclaim a brother not by living with him again but by traveling to his end and standing there at the shore of it. Why do we think endings can be means? Because there is a grotesque, tender impulse in us to find closure where none naturally settles.

When I cross the strait now, I look out over the water and try not to ask what might have been. The sea is not a moralist; it does not offer judgement. It offers the same physics it has always offered: waves, currents, tides. It keeps and returns in ways that are beyond our understanding. Once, as the ferry left the harbor, I caught sight of a man on the small pier, stooped against the wind, hands jammed deep in his pockets. He looked, for a moment, like someone waiting for a particular boat. I thought of all the people who stand at water's edge waiting for arrivals and departures, for reconciliations that might never arrive. I thought that perhaps the best thing I had done was less about owning a name on paper and more about showing up at the place where he last had breath and saying, quietly, "I'm here now." Whether that was enough for a brother I had lost or for a man who had become evidence of his own end, I could not say.

I keep the dolphin keychain still. Sometimes in the small hours I pull it out and let it swing in my hand. It is an object of no particular value, but its weight is the right weight. When I close my eyes I can almost hear the slap of waves against rocks, and in that sound there is sometimes, at last, a softness.
