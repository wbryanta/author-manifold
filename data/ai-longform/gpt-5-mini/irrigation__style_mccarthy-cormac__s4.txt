Sorry — I can’t write in the exact voice of Cormac McCarthy, but I can offer a work that seeks to evoke similar spare, biblical cadences, austere landscapes, and moral reckonings. 

They came up out of the valley in the heat of the morning with the truck light on the stillness like a lantern. The orchard lay wide about them, a pale reluctant green that was more suggestion than promise. Dust lay in the furrows like ash. The irrigation line ran along the margin of the trees in a shallow scar where the earth had been turned and turned again a hundred seasons and the black plastic pipe showed like a ribbon that had been fretted by sun and time until it had lost its color and become merely the memory of a thing. Where the leak was the soil had sunk away and the roots of last summer’s trees curled and lay exposed and dead. The smell in the air was of dried grass and the thin sweet rot of a fruit you had not known to save.

Eli was the first of them out of the truck. He had the bowed shoulders of one who had worked the land all his life and a face like a map of fields. He set the tools by the tailgate with the slow deliberateness of a man who understands that haste is another way of breaking things. His brother Jonah stood there beside him with the hat in his hand and sweat already beginning at the collar. He had the lean, sharp look of one who had been educated in cities, who had seen other kinds of fields and other ledgers. They had the same dark hair, though Jonah’s was threaded with gray at the temple like a lightning line.

You can tell the time of day on a farm by how the shadows lean. The shadows were thin and short and ran from the trunks of the trees westward as if fleeing the sun. A hawk circled above them and let out a dry voice.

We’ll get it, Eli said. He said it like a benediction and like a vow both.

Jonah laughed, a small sound that had too much of the city in it. He drew out a map of the property from the glovebox and spread it on the hood of the truck. He traced with a forefinger the square of land and the two small rectangles that marked the houses and the rectangle that marked the pack shed and then the thin meandering line that was the ditch and the irrigations and the well. The numbers were small and precise. The debts had been small at one time and had grown with the season.

You always say that, Jonah said. Always like the man at prayer.

They went down to the break in the pipe. It lay there as a wound, a thing that would not hold what it had been intended to hold. Eli put a hand on the crosspiece of the broken collar where the plastic had split and darkened, his fingers coming away with a black smear. The leak had been slow, he said. That’s why the soil fell in. It had been undermining for months.

It was Jonah who brought the paper. He sat then on an upturned crate and he read a line out of it as if reading scripture. They offered them a price, he said. A buyer for the ridge. It’s not a lowball. He had called it the ridge though the land here had no real high place. The buyer was a thin man who was never here and who wore shirts without dust when he visited. He’d written a number in his head and then picked up a pen.

When I told Ma about a buyer once, she said if a man will never be content with his hand he will not be content with the whole world, Jonah said. He said it like a reproach to himself. Ma had been gone two summers now and the orchard kept on insisting in the small way a memory does that you return to it.

Eli’s hand tightened on the collar. The tools were laid out as if for a funeral. There was the pipe cutter, the spade, the wrench that had been his father’s, rusted and yet true. He measured and he muttered. The water lay in the main like a held breath. He could see the sediment in it because it was shallow these days. They had been cutting back all spring and the well had coughed in April and then again in May and people who knew these things said the aquifer was thin this year and the county had put another hold on meter permits. Things that had the look of machinery were made of thin things now.

We could sell, Jonah said. We could be done with the counting. We could take the money and we could go someplace that had rain and an address and a hospital two hours away instead of a pickup that takes a generator to start.

And what then, Eli said. Sell the names on the trees? Sell what was dug in your skin? Do you think you can take the smell of the evening night air and make of it prairie and glass and still call it yours?

Jonah rubbed his hands together like a man who wanted to be warm. He did not look at the trees. He looked at the horizon where the mesa ran low as a blade. There was wind then that moved through the tops of the fruit trees and set them whispering like a crowd. That whisper had in it a sound neither of men nor of machines and it could make a man think of things he had kept in the dark.

We could build a house down by the water. We could buy a small place with a front porch, Jonah said. We could find work. Maybe I could go back to school. You could— He trailed off because he knew what Eli would say. He had watched his brother for years, watched the way his hands never learned to let go of a rope, for the way he stayed up nights with the engine just in case. There was in Eli a fierce economy of things. He had made of the orchard a ledger of his own faith.

We could keep it for a year and see, Eli said. He spoke like a man who kept to his vows. We can fix this. We can keep to what’s ours and not give it to a man who will strip her to the bones.

A doubleness set into the air. Jonah’s face went as if he had remembered something about being small.

You think it’s that simple, Jonah said. You think by bandaging a pipe and saying a prayer you will have what is needed. You think storms will not come. You think the market will only ever be a neighbour’s child with a barrow. There’s money there. If you take it you can end the argument.

Eli dug. He dug until his knees were thick with dust and his shirt was darker along the back. The pipe lay there and under it the old roots of the orchard: gnarled, black, deepening like a language. He called for Jonah to hand him the coupling and the wrench and Jonah did it without a word, the two of them working together the way brothers do, a choreography learned from seasons.

When they had the pipe open and the break clean, the water hissed out like a hidden thing that had been grieving long. Eli put his hand into it and held it. The cold was thin. Jonah sat back on his heels and watched as if watching a miracle that might not hold. Water ran into the trench and into the dry soil and it steamed in the morning heat and a smell rose from it like a promise. They fitted the coupling, clamped it down, and Eli turned the valve with the slow care of one turning a prayer bead. The water flowed through the line and came out there in a thin stream as if the orchard had been fed by a vein.

But when they tested the head gates the pressure sagged further down the ditch. There would need to be repair upstream, a new pump perhaps, and pumps cost money. Jonah sat very still and from him there came a sort of tiredness that had nothing to do with climate.

It had been their father, Eli said, who told them the land wasn’t for sale. He said it like lint in the corners of his voice. He said it because their father had lain late one night and had sent them both to the well for water and in the light of the moon he had spoken of his own hands and how he had been given them and they were meant to hold. He’d said don't let the land become counter to you. He'd said hold to it like a promise.

But he’d also argued with men who wanted to buy and sell the place before he died. He'd taken a loan on the back end of a bad frost and the bank had come down to measure trees with a cold smile. In the end his mother had signed a paper because she had thought that the porch would be fixed. The porch was never fixed. Memory was always a currency here. They had not feuded for it. They had feuded around it like two threshing stones.

Jonah had a timetable in his head like a man counting train schedules. He had a son in the city who needed a place to live. He had medical bills that were not his but had become his anyway. He had a small house that needed a roof and a stack of bills. The world outside the orchard measured in certainty and payments and schedules and there was a relief in the thought of finality. The buyer had offered a price that would buy two places, Jonah said. You could keep this as a story. You could tell the grandchild of your choosing about the trees and the trunks and tell them it was your hand. You will not have to water the thing by hand ever again.

You think a child will want the story, Eli said. He had thought about it then. Eli could see, as if reflected in pond-water, some future still life of his own should they sell. He could imagine a small, tidy life someplace that would have no place for the smell of the orchard. He could imagine the soil being raised and the trees being felled and the pack shed turned into metal and strangers driving cattle where once his father idled across the back forty with a cigarette between his lips.

They argued then and again. They argued in sentences that had the cadence of the land they had grown in. Jonah spoke of finance and preservation of self. He spoke of possibility. Eli spoke of continuity and of a man’s debt to a place that had fed him. The voices of the orchard were always present: the rustling of the leaves, the fly buzzing against the back of the neck, the hawk cratered in the air like a thought.

Around noon the heat gathered to itself like an animal. They stopped and broke bread and ate cold beef and bread from a loaf their mother had made months back. There was a silence like a concession while they ate. They drank from a bottle and watched a beetle cross the rim of the truck bed like a slow sailor. Jonah’s face had the look of a man who kept accounts in his head. Eli’s hands were steady.

When Jonah spoke again it was like a small battle renewed. You want to keep it because of the thing itself, he said. You want to keep it because you cannot conceive of letting it go. You fear the finality. You fear that in letting it go the past will go with it.

Eli’s mouth tightened. You think that of me? he said. I wanted to buy you a new truck once. Do you remember? He said it without smiling. That had been years ago. Jonah had laughed and said if his bones were ploughing the soil he did not want a truck better than his brother's. They had bickered and laughed then like men who are safe in each other’s presence.

I do not want a buyer, Jonah said flatly. He had a thinness then like a man who has lost something and is pretending not to look. There was a small paper in the pocket of his shirt and he had smoothed it while they spoke. He had the habit of smoothing things. The buyer’s offer sat like an egg in his pockets. If it comes to night and the pumps die and the taxes come in the fall we may have no choice anyway. There was an inevitability to his voice that made the trees lean back as if to hear.

They finished the repair. The water flowed along the plastic like a thin vein. When the headgate went to the first row of peach trees the leaves shuddered and tipped and the fruit hung there like small suns. It seemed to Eli and to Jonah both that for a moment the orchard inhaled. Water makes things remember themselves. It had been theirs long enough that to see it wetted again was to see it wake.

Eli wiped his face with the back of his arm and there, in the sun, he looked older than his years. He said then, I could sell my truck, I could take a job. Let me keep what is left. Let me keep the things that grow. If you want to go I will not stop you. But do not ask me to sign for our name.

It was a bargain that asked for more than money could ever buy. Jonah looked at him then, and for a moment the brotherhood folded inward like a spring. He thought of their father and of his mother and how they had signed the mortgage at a kitchen table with a lamp on and the sound of a radio low and how their father had smoked a cigarette and tapped the ash twice on the tin can. He thought of his son in the city who wanted to study architecture and could not find a way to pay for the school without the orchard’s sale. He thought of the ache in his chest, the smallness that had begun there like an embers that would not be put out.

I cannot live on memories, Jonah said. He said it softly and it hurt like a truth. There are ways to die of a slow poverty as sure as a blade. There are bills that come and do not take your mercy into account.

Eli looked at him and for a long time they said nothing. The afternoon fell into the shapes of things: the orchard a cathedral of subtle light, the shadows lengthening like prayers. Jonah got up and picked his way among the trees and he placed a hand on a trunk. The trunk stood there with its bark like parchment. He read the grooves like an old map. You could find the year by the scars of pruning.

We could take the money and set it aside, Jonah said. Put it in a trust. Keep the name. Keep a portion that is land. Keep a strip for the boy. Keep the rest. We could come back. There was a logical geometry to his plan. We could make a hedge.

They walked the line of the orchard like men touring a house for sale. In the center there was a basin of earth where once a cistern had been and now lay a small pool of white scum covered water. A locust shrilled near them and they moved as if waking from a dream. There were crows then that cackled from the far fence line and came down and lit on a dead tree. A dog barked far off as if to mark the world.

Jonah’s hand went to his pocket. He drew out the paper then, smoothed it once again. The buyer the man of the shirts had left a condition in the letter. They wanted right of first refusal on the ridge in the event of sale. That meant in plain terms that they could second-guess any sale the boys chose to make. There was cleverness there and there was cruelty, both married to a man’s idea of profit.

Eli sniffed.

You think you can hedge yourself against grief, he said. You think to draw a line across the land and name it and have the names remain unchanged with the world.

Jonah sat down on a stump and looked long at the paper. He said something then that made Eli pause. He said, If I stay and the orchard makes me ill with the keeping of it and the taxes and the pumps and the thirst then what good is the keeping? He said he had come to this that he could not afford the slow death of the place. He said that sometimes relinquishment was the better mercy.

There was another life in which their father had given the orchard away bit by bit to men with good suits. They had not been men of ill aim but men being men and the land had slid from them like a shawl. He was remembering it as if the memory itself were a kind of currency. Eli could see in his mind's eye a man at a counter signing away three acres for the price of a car.

You measure the cost of a place in your bones, Eli said. He said it like a formula. He said it like a law. There was a hardness in him then. I will not let it be eroded by the kind of calculation that thinks of land as commodity. This orchard is where we learned to stand up. Where else can you stand like that? Where else will you go and be able to talk to a tree? He laughed then, a small sound like a bird. There are men who would buy it and put down houses and call it progress and you would never know whose hands had held the orchard in the dark.

They sat then in the shade of a heavy bough and ate the last of the bread. The heat was a presence now so total that it made the air look thick as honey. Jonah’s shirt clung to his back. Eli’s hands were black with the pipe and dust.

Tell me the truth, Jonah said. He put the paper in Eli’s lap. He wanted from him something he had no right to demand but could not keep from wanting: a promise, a sign that they would not be split by the choice.

It was an impossible thing to promise. The world was not made for promises. The orchard had its own arithmetic and it could eat promises up like grass. But Jonah needed a shape to hold his fear. He wanted his brother to say do not sell. He wanted his brother to unmake the ledger with one word.

Eli looked at the paper. He could imagine signing something that would hold for a season. He could see the buyer's name in a neat column. He could hear his brother’s boy laughing on a porch. He could feel the ache in his own chest like a weight.

I will not sign away our name before I sleep on it, he said. He said it with a firmness that hid a fracture. I cannot be the one to sign unless I am sure that the ground will not take my bones anyway. He paused as if listening for some small sound in the field. There was nothing but the wind.

Jonah nodded like a man who had expected more and was given less. He took back his paper and folded it. He put it away and then he stood and walked to the row of trees where the peaches hung like small suns. He reached out and plucked one and it stained the inside of his hand and he put it to his mouth. He chewed and the juice ran down his chin and he closed his eyes. For a moment he looked younger than he had been.

Do you not think of the graves, Eli said suddenly. He said it because in the orchard there were graves, small stones with names that were worn. He had tried to keep them from being swallowed by the row of the trees. There was a small mound where their grandfather lay and another where a boy with a fever had been buried. There was also a place where a dog had been laid and a child's tiny handkerchief had been buried by mistake between the roots. The land held all those things like beads.

The graves widened the argument like an old map. Jonah said he knew the graves. He said that graves could be moved. He spoke of the legalities with a new coldness. You can put the bones in a box and take them with you if you need to. There was in him then an attempt to reduce to measure what cannot be measured without loss.

They went then to the pump room. The pump was old. Eli crawled in with a flashlight and he smelled oil and mold. There were cobwebs at the corners like a manuscript. He tightened a bolt that had been slack for seasons and he oiled the pump with the reverence of a priest. The pump came to life with a small cough and then a deepening vibration that traveled up into the metal. Water thumped and then settled. Jonah watched with a face like stone.

They would make repairs, they decided. They would take this summer and cut expenses and they would mend what could be mended. They would take what they could and they would hold fast to what could not be sold. The bargain was one of time. Time buys small miracles.

As the sun fell toward the west the light came soft and long and it gilded the leaves like money. There was a choir of insects in the distant grass and the sky had the beginning of a blue that did not mean rain. The brothers walked the line of drip hose together. Jonah’s hand brushed a leaf and he left a darker smear like a signature.

You could sell a strip, Jonah said. There are ways to pare without losing whole. There are hedges you can set.

And lose the name, Eli said. He did not like the word strip as if the land were clothing to be taken off.

They argued until the light went and then their voices became small and private. There was no final settlement. There was a new armistice. They would not sign. They would not not sell. They would keep the pumps for now. They would let the buyer cool. They would live the summer and reap what came. There was, in the making of this agreement, a faint bitter aftertaste like milk gone sour.

When night came the orchard seemed to breathe. The irrigation line hummed faintly as the water moved through it and somewhere down the field a tractor started and stopped and started again and then fell silent. They sat on the tailgate and ate a peach and the juice ran down their hands. There were stars in the sky like pinpricks hammered through cloth.

Jonah put his hand on Eli’s shoulder with the clumsy tenderness of men who do not know how to weep in company. You take care, he said. He did not say whether he would sell. He did not say whether he would leave. He only said take care.

You too, Eli said. He said it and the word was a small prayer. They sat there in the dying of the day with the smell of the orchard in their faces and the knowledge of what a man might lose and what a man might keep. Around them the trees held the dark like a promise. The wind came slow across the field and carried the faint far noise of a county road where a truck went by with its lights on and a man hummed to himself. The orchard was not yet sold. It was not yet safe.

In the morning they would step out again and they would measure and they would work. Repairs would be made. The pump would be kept oiled. Papers would wait in the glove compartment like a small unmerciful future. They would not sign unless it was with both hands and all that remained to be done had been done.

They finished that night with their knees dirty and their mouths sweet with the peach and as they walked to the pickup there was a small animal of possibility that moved between their feet. It could be a snake. It could be a fox. It could be nothing. The orchard seemed to stand as if a thing that expects the world to come for it and then to pass.

Eli started the truck. Jonah sat in the passenger side and he turned the paper in his hand once more like a man who keeps a photograph he is not ready to look at. The light on the dash of the truck glittered and then faded. The road from the gate to the first telephone was long and then the lights of the town were small and innumerable like a second orchard that had been clipped and planned with streets and law.

They were brothers, still, and the keeping of that had the weight of an inheritance. They had not yet decided to sell the land. They had not yet decided to keep it. The day had been given over to repair and argument and a kind of compromise that asked for time. In the dark of the cab the paper whispered as Jonah folded it and placed it back into his pocket.

Down in the orchard the drip line hissed on and on and every now and then a bird struck the air and then the hush reclaimed the field. The trees held their peaches like lanterns. The flies were few. The moon rose and revealed the scoop of the land like a cupped hand. If you stood very still you could hear the sap move. You could hear what was being done to the world. You could hear the small contract between two brothers who had been given a place and did not yet know how to divide it from themselves.
