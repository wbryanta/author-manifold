Snow pressed itself against the corrugated roof as if it meant to wedge the whole world closed. The flakes found every crack where light had once leaked; they threaded themselves under the lip of the barn door and skeined along the concrete, making paste in the grooves. Inside, the shed smelled of hay and lanolin and that warm, metallic tang that lambing brings—blood and afterbirth and the sharp, clean smell of a newborn’s damp fleece. Maeve had learned to read smells the way other people read the weather: iron on a cool wind for a ewe about to push, sourness for a ewe with retained placental, the faint sweetness of colostrum if she stooped close and listened.

She was sitting on the overturned crate that had been her husband's seat for twenty years, the surface grimed with oil and the impression of his weight still there, a little depressed in the plastic. The headlamp on her forehead made a narrow halo; the power had gone sometime after midnight, a thin click and then nothing. Tom, sixteen, had been lying among the bales, forehead pressed to the straw, and woken as if from a page of the past when Maeve reached down and shook him. The shed had been alive with ticking thermostats and quiet humming—lights that keep the lambing pens from freezing and boxes that open and sigh—but now there was only the wind and the occasional staccato of the ewes.

Ewes kept coming. That was the thing that had not changed. The season marched on as if apology were unnecessary. Between the gusts that threw themselves at the shutters and the thin cone of light, they found bodies that needed hands.

"Two waiting at the second pen," Tom said, voice rough from sleep and cold. He kissed his own knuckles to warm them. He had his father's big hands, wide palms with the calluses at the base of the fingers where he'd gripped a flail or a fence wire, but his fingers were too long still for the sockets of the gloves. He kept tugging them on and off, adjusting, covering his mouth. He was tall enough now that when he stood under the hay loft he could brush his hair against the beams and not stoop.

Maeve nodded. "I'll take the black-face," she said. The names had been reduced to coat and weight and temperament over years of births. The black-faced ewe had always liked the corner by the hay; she liked closeness, liked to brace herself against something and push. They had both set the old habits into muscle—how to hold a ewe's shoulders, where to place a palm at the rump so that the extra weight could be borne together.

When they entered the pen, the ewe was up to her elbows in straw and panic. Her eyes were a dull brass, slips of white at the rims. The lamb's feet were there, twin little hooves like the tips of spoons, and the head was turned, partly obscured, the cord still slick and tense. Maeve smiled at Tom, the ghost of the smile that used to be for no one but the barn floor where he'd stand, hair wind-fouled, watching his father cut away a membrane.

"Pull with her," she said. "Slow. Feel the shoulder. It won't come until you find it."

Tom braced his feet as his father had taught him, leaned in, and took hold. He remembered the grip—knees along the ewe's sides, elbows tucked—then the sudden weight, the slick resistance. He grunted and the lamb's head came free, a small wet thing with closed eyes and flaring nostrils. Maeve clapped her hands under it without being asked, rubbed its chest with a towel, brought its nose to the ewe's side. The ewe flinched, then nudged, bewildered but attentive, and the lamb took a confused, desperate lurch at the teat.

They worked like that all night, by muscle memory, by the mute code of a life built on repetition. A ewe after a ewe; short, purposeful conversations: "Dry him off, Tom—no, not like that—pat along the ribs—hold him up—tie the cord—dip the stump." They had small liturgies: the iodine at the navel, the string cut and knotted, the prayer of heat beneath their palms. When the generator refused to turn over because the battery had been compromised by the cold, Maeve had sworn and then sat on the crate and taken a deep breath. Her husband had loved the generator. She could feel him as if through a static in the air, an ache that focused her. He had been the sort of man who knew the difference between a good, tired grunt and a worrying one.

At three in the morning the wind dove its knives against the sliding door, and they held a small army of lambs pressed against the straw, bodies hot and damp in the cool shed. Tom had put on his father's waxed jacket without meaning to, the collar raising to take the spiky salt of the wind. "We should light the old lamps," he said, voice small.

Maeve had already thought of it. The oil stove lived in the tack room, its glass blacked with old breath. They found the tins of paraffin, the long fat wick, the globe like a small moon. When the first lantern lit, the halo was soft and forgiving, a pool of warmth that made the flakes outside look like a faraway net. The shed smelled of paraffin then and of straw warmed by slow flame, and for a moment the world was less as if it had been opened and emptied.

They did not sleep properly again. Each pen delivered its punctuated demands: a ewe with a slings; a pair of twins that seemed too indifferent to the world; a single lamb with a blue string of fluid that muffled its breathing and required a quick mouth-rope and fingers clearing wet from its throat. They came with the regularity of tide. Maeve kept a pen near the back for the ewes that were stubborn or old; in her head she could hear the list her husband had kept on a scrap of paper in the kitchen drawer: names, birth weights, quirks, who’d bit a shepherd in spring. He'd scribed the list with a stub of pencil, and she found herself turning it over on the crate between births now, as if the graphite might still offer counsel.

There was a stall with an ewe that had been silent for hours, lying splayed as though the wind had wrapped her in a wet blanket. She had bled; a bruise like a dark moon on her flank showed where a hand might have been needed that they did not yet have the time to spare. Maeve had an old trick—massage the sheaths, press the pelvis—much of it borrowed from moments of help between them and the town vet in summers when there was time to think. Tonight they had no vet. Emergency was a lonelier thing now.

"She's cold," Tom said. "Her ears are gone."

Maeve felt around. There was a lamb half-born, shoulder gone wrong. She could sense the position, the impossibility of too much sheep in too little space. "Get the lambing pail," she said. The lambing pail was a basin that sat by the door, nicked and stained with the history of births. Tom pulled it out on instinct and dressed his hands in a long bulbous glove like a diver's limb. He looked like someone trying on a piece of someone else's skin, but his face was steady.

They did the work that needed doing. Maeve had always thought of herself as practical, but the edge of practical had softened since his death into something that felt more like hunger. There is a learned cruelty to survival—call it decision—and it makes a woman into a wolf. She told herself that the lamb would have wanted to be saved. She thought, too, of schools and committees and the small kindnesses his death had revealed. People had left casseroles, then coffees, then nothing. The hedge at the lane still bore the tape left by the ambulance the day the sirens had come. She hadn't taken it down. It fluttered like a flag, an accusation or a reliquary; she could not choose which.

Hours thickened into a motionless, suspended thing until rain at the skylight turned to sleet and the barn's tattoos grew short and urgent. At one point Tom dropped into the straw and breathed out, "He would have been here by now," as if the world were wrong in keeping time without him. His voice made the straw look like a small, feral city.

Maeve hugged her knees. "He is," she said, and she thought of him, the specifics of his hands and that small laugh that came at nothing. But she did not mean a ghost. She meant the muscle memory he had left in Tom and in the shed. A man accumulates habits on a farm the way lambs acquire dew on their noses; they transfer, quiet and useful.

At dawn—if dawn could be counted in a weather like that, a white bruise across the panes—they had saved forty-three lambs. There were marks for mortality as well: some lambs would not breathe; a ewe had been placidly waiting, the slow resignation of a body that would not be the same again. The ewes kept coming. By late morning the storm had not relented; if anything the world had closed harder. They worked in pairs, and sometimes in tandem without speech: one would hold, the other would pull, and once a lamb was out, they would lay it on the warmed tins under the lamp and coax the living into movement.

Near midday the pen with the old ewe that had bitten the shepherd three springs ago—black nose and a small notch missing in the ear from a barbed wire scrape—demanded the sort of attention that makes the skin burn. She had twins, cleanly separated like two small boats, both breathing, but the ewe's udder had a sudden, angry heat. She arched and pressed the lambs into the warm straw and then set her head down as if to keep the impulse from breaking and walked, absurdly defeated, into a corner.

"Milk fever," Maeve said. The name was a pathway through a thick place. She had seen it before in another ewe on harvest-time nights, when men's hands were fewer, and she had been the one to bring the sugar solution and the shots. They had no electricity to heat a bottle or to run the pump. She had a syringe and sugar packets in the feed shed. She pushed a chair under the ewe's jaw and opened her palms to Tom.

"Hold her," Maeve told him. He wrapped his arms around the ewe's front legs in the awkward hug of a teenager holding down a much larger thing. He smelled like the barn and the paraffin lamp and a faint trace of his mother's skin on his collar. "Talk to her," Maeve told him as she worked, because the sound of a human voice calms beasts as it does children.

The syringe was a blunt thing in Maeve's fingers. She drew sugar, boiled with a small stove, then cooled enough to temper the sting. The ewe's eyes fogged and cleared, then she tried to stand and fell again. Each time she tried, Tom's hands were there to guide her. Maeve sang then—not to the ewe, but because singing meant there was rhythm to the work, and rhythm steadied a hand and a breath. Her husband had once sung as he mended a fence—low, indecipherable lines—and she'd realized after he was gone that the tune had been an instruction as much as a comfort. She taught Tom a piece of the song that fit a slow two-step: hold, pulse, push. He hummed it back badly at first and then better, mouth tight, and the ewe let him.

They got her on her feet. When the fever broke, she tucked her nose into one lamb uncertainly, then the other, and Maeve felt a small current of something like triumph move through her ribs. Tom leaned against the pen and coughed, thin from the damp, and laughed, which was all slitted and brittle. He had been the boy who left his boots at the back door for his father to kick off; that boy had been replaced overnight by a man who would take calls from a vet in the middle of a snowstorm and who could size up a ewe's gait and correct it.

"He's the one who'd have known without the song," Tom said, a brief, soft apology to the absence. He had never had the time to be fully angry at the world for the time taken from him; instead, grief had congealed into competence.

Maeve thought of his hands and of her husband's old list and of the way men say things between themselves that women have to iron out afterward. You do not always need the words, she thought, and perhaps that kept them going more than any list. There was a way her husband used to take the lamb against his chest that seemed like an offering, like a priest's canticle. Tom had learned it naturally; perhaps all sons do.

Midway through the afternoon, a sound came from the lane that had the shape of human urgency: tires grinding, questions shouted. Two figures in high-visibility jackets stood at the barn door, their faces red from the gale. Maeve's chest tightened; the first call-outs always arrive like a promise. The neighbor, Aileen from up the hill, shoved the door and came in with her partner, who carried an old flashlight that threw a lance of light across the pens.

"We've got three over at the Cremery field," Aileen called. "Broken through the fence where the drift came. They're calling the shepherd—God help him—but there's a ewe down and a couple of lambs frozen."

Maeve set the lamp down on its crate and wiped her hands on her trousers. "Take the left rope," she said, motioning toward a stall with dry blankets and jugs of warmed water, which they kept for emergencies. They hadn't planned for visitors tonight—no one had planned for the kind of stubborn April the year had delivered—but the neighborly code of their valley had always been to give what warmth you could.

Tom pulled on his father's old welly boots and a jacket that still smelled of burnt toast. He looked like he'd been fastened into a life he hadn't chosen and somehow approved. Maeve handed him a flask of something steaming—soup they'd warmed in the kettle over the paraffin. He swallowed it in a single, rude mouthful.

Outside the snow had become a slap of white driven into the chest; they walked shoulder-leaning into it. The walk to the Cremery field was a thin line between hedges blurred by frost. The lambing instincts work like a map of the body—the ewe down there was a body like any other, vulnerable, and they worked in a small choreography to move her without injuring her further. When they got the ewe on her feet, there were frost-crusted lambs that had refused to live on their mother's warmth alone. Maeve wrapped them in straw and rubbed them as though a muscle could be made to believe in itself again.

On the way back to the barn, support came in a ragged line. Farmer's tractors with headlights like comets, boots tracking, blokes from all up and down the lane, and then children who came to watch with the comprehension that next year would make them into something else. The community had arrived, a small constellation that always appeared in the face of weather and loss. When people bring you biscuits they mean to fill a hole with crumbs; when people bring you blankets they mean to stitch you back into a world that is still functioning. It is a barter of kindnesses.

When they returned, the shed had filled like a hive. Maeve put her hand to the flank of a ewe who had come in like a tired thing, who had the look of someone who had done her best and ran out of fuel. There was a lamb, a little grey thing with eyes like a bead, who pushed into the crook of the ewe's leg and found what it was looking for. Maeve's fingers trembled and Tom's did, too, when he wrapped the lamb's stump with iodine-covered twine. They worked in a close constellation of bodies. The barn was a universe in which each breath counted, where breath could be held or given.

At one point the wind took a long, deliberate pause as if to allow them to hear something beneath the din. Maeve thought she heard a human voice in the howling, but it was just the echo of memory. She thought of the last night he had been with them, with that laugh and those hands and the small surety of how things should be done. The grief came and went like weather; sometimes it was hail that beat hard and fast, and sometimes it was the long, cold rain that seeped into the ribs. On the worst nights—she had told the vet one time—they had felt like they were waiting for a chest to stop, and when it stopped, the machines would not begin again.

Tom, for his part, had stopped asking questions. He wasn't trying to replicate his father exactly; he wouldn't be able to. Instead he had folded the memory into performance. In the store-room later, with a lamb tucked under his arm, he pulled a thread of straw and made a small crown out of it and set it on its head. "He's coronated," he said, eyes wide. Maeve laughed then, briefly, the sound like something a sheep might make, surprised and delighted: a bell in the snow.

There were edges to the night that the lamp could not illuminate. When the winds settled to a softer moan, there came the moment everyone on a farm dreads and expects: the ewe that will not take her lamb because she is unfamiliar with it, or because instinct has parted from experience. There was a black-headed ewe near the far stall who had turned her back to her own lamb as if to treat it as a stranger. Maeve put her hand on the ewe's neck and felt the jitter in the muscle, the rawness. She knew that somewhere in a corner of herself the last season without him had made her too quick to fear abandoning things. "Hold the lamb," she told Tom.

He held it and made faces at it, small ridiculous faces that made it twitch. "There," he told it, as if a child might be named by instruction. The ewe looked, then sniffed, then nuzzled the lamb with a slow curiosity. "This is what he would have done," Tom said, not to Maeve but to the lamb, then to the entire shed.

Maeve smiled at that and thought of how the dead live on not only in stories but in the practicalities of life: the kettles he had polished, the marks he had made in places that would not be replaced. There were small comforts—his gloves in the back room had finally been put on Tom's hands that morning, as if the leather understood that the shape of the hand had changed.

They worked through the afternoon until the storm's white hand relaxed just enough to allow daylight to be a presence. But the power was still out. They used the lamps until the wax was low. They stacked blankets. People left in turns, boots wet, cheeks red, leaving Maeve and Tom to the last pens. The shed held an army of new lives that sniffed at the air with little, fine curiosity.

At one point Maeve went to the back wall and took down a board that had a small nail driven into it. Her husband had written on the wall once, years ago, scrawling in pencil the time of births, the weights, the small notes—"Twin boys, strong"—and she had kept his hand in the grain of the wood by not painting the shed interior. She would not have realized how much she missed the mark until now when she traced the letters with her thumb and felt a map. There is a certain sacrament in such acts, a way of keeping time.

Tom watched her without speaking. The barn felt full now in a way that would make the household more than it had been—two of them and their bright, lawless herd—both less and more. The loss sat at the small table at their kitchen like a cutlery set; sometimes you used the knife and sometimes you reached for the wrong spoon, and sometimes you refused to set it at all because it reminded you of everything. But the sheep do not care about table manners. They care about milk and shelter and the warmth of a body in the dark.

As the afternoon burned into a kind of mercury-lit early evening, they were called to the last pen. A ewe had been quiet all day but now she pushed with a force that set her in a sweat. Tom knelt, sleeves rolled, and put his ear to her flank to listen for the flutter of a small heart. He looked like his father when he concentrated; the angle of his jaw, the set of his mouth. Maeve breathed in, the small flare of the nostrils—she had been in this position so many times that the motions had become an anatomy lesson. The lamb was large, the shoulders a problem, the lamb's head set wrong.

"Go slow," she told Tom.

He pulled and he felt something give, and then nothing. The lamb came into the shed like a small creature cut from wet cloth and there was a red rag of umbilical cord and a mouth that would not take breath. Tom set the little thing on his calf and slapped the lamb the way his father had taught him, a rhythm like a metronome, a code of urgency plainly taught by years of repetition. The lamb convulsed and then settled, lungs opening like a curtained window, air pouring in with a sound like a settling door. It lived. They both breathed as if they'd been granted a reprieve.

Maeve thought in that moment of the inevitability of the seasons—how winter will not mind whether you are ready; how a man can be taken and the land will go on being a field. And she thought of the smallness of the miracle: a breath, a swallow, the soft trilling of a newborn. A world could be compressed into a single intake of air.

That night, after the last lamp had burned down to its wick and the stars had been smudged by the remains of the storm, they sat on the crate, shoulders close enough to be a puzzle that kept two shapes together. Tom was tired in a way that made him look younger and older at once; grief had lined him with a seriousness that had not been there two years before. They had been touched by the hands of neighbors, by canned soup and hot tea, by the displaced kindness of those who bring what little they have.

"What's next?" Tom asked.

Maeve considered the question. There was a ledger to keep of feed, of lambs tagged and earmarked, of the extra costs the storm would bring. There would be fences to mend, hay to order, a vet bill to meet. There would be the funeral matters that had loomed while the ewes were in labor and that would now require their attention in a proper, mechanical sequence. These were things to be sustained by a woman who had learned to hold an army of small emergencies.

"We go home," she said. "We get some sleep. And in the morning we feed. We check the fences. We make a list."

Tom nodded, and then he surprised her by saying, "Come tomorrow, we'll have to sort the big ones. And the crates: you always put them by the north wall." He sounded like a man reading a map he had drawn himself.

They were quiet for a moment, listening to the settled breath of the lambs and the soft rumble of a tractor somewhere on the lane as neighbors did their rounds. Near the door, a lamb that had been rejected the week before pushed its head until it found the warmth of a hand. Maeve put her palm on its head and felt the rapid thud, the tiny, precise life there. She felt the smallness of her hands in contrast to the enormity of the weather, the loss wider than a handspan, but also the way a hand could close and keep something.

"I miss him," Tom said, as if naming was a way to set the measure.

"I know," Maeve said. She did not deny it; she could not. There was a particular silence around the name of his father that sat like a folded shirt on a bed—present, folded, waiting to be used.

They were not solitary in the way townspeople thought of loneliness. Their business was the outward tending of animals that fed them, and that keeping meant there was always a reason to shoulder up and go outside. The landscape required the same kinds of care you give a wound: you keep it clean, you keep vigil, you dress it, and you bring it light. On the farm, there is no point in waiting for the perfect weather for grief. You must dress it in waterproofs and go about the day.

Outside, the wind was quieting; the flakes that came later were like confetti and the sky had taken on the color of old tin. Tomorrow they would count lambs, weigh them, ensure that each had a mother or a foster and that no one would be left out in the cold. There would be hard decisions—what to supplement and what to let nature decide—and other things that required a ledger and a steady hand. Tonight the ledger was a remote thing.

Maeve reached for Tom's hand and found it large and reliable. She thought of how her husband had once taken her hand in public as if to say, "this is mine," and she thought of how she could not take what was his but could keep what he had built. They were not full houses but they were inhabited. The lambs stirred against the warmth of the bales and then slept. The storm had become a story they could tell in later seasons: the year the power went out in late April and they still kept the lambing. They would tell that story in small rooms at pubs, by kitchen tables warmed by peat, over the steaming of a kettle steamed in a pot on a stove.

When eventually they got back to the house, the world outside had been smoothed to whiteness. The lane bore prints of tire treads and human feet like lines on a palm. In the kitchen, the clock on the stove read wrong and the radio—on battery this time—flickered. A cup of coffee was too bitter and just the thing they needed. They drank of it and allowed exhaustion to make a brief, consented shelter.

Tom went to bed and slept like a child who had been given permission to stop pretending to be strong. Maeve sat at the kitchen table and made the list for morning. She ticked off a column of things in her head: check milkers, tag the new lambs, put out the feed, call the neighbor about the wire. She added, at the end, a line that was not necessary but that she had put there in the way someone might set a vase on a mantel to measure a space: "Remember to take down the tape from the hedge." It was a small, ceremonial act she would do because some rhythms needed to be kept visible.

Outside, morning crept in. The snow began to slouch off the roofs in languid ropes. A lamb behind one of the pens had its first taste of cold and leapt in astonishment. Life, it seemed, could be raw and bright even when the sky had been ripped open and the lights had failed. They were not healed. The loss was a rawness that would come with weather and seasons. But they were alive to meet it with hands that remembered how to tie and pull and soothe.

Maeve hung the last lantern on its nail. The shed smelled clean and mineral. She thought of how things are always made fresh by touch—how a hand on a cold forehead will alter a shape. She tied a ribbon on a fence post to mark the place where the drift had been worst. It was small and pointless in the face of a blizzard, and that made it the more necessary.

In the barn, under the low late-April light, Tom caught his reflection in the window and smiled at the way his eyes matched his father's. Maeve watched him and felt the shape of the future as if it had been pointed out to her on a map. She did not know what all the coordinates meant. She knew only the next step and the next: feed, check, mend, bury, console. And between them the lambs bleated into the white, demanding and soft in a language older than grief.

Outside, snow clung to the hedgerow like cotton wool. Inside, two people who had been made almost alone by loss kept the world turning because the world required their care. The power had been cut and the storm had shredded the visible, but there were other kinds of power in the room: the slow acquiescence to toil, the small mechanical approvals of hands, the memory of a man's voice that taught without speaking. The ewes kept coming, and they kept answering. The shed was loud with new life and the work it demanded, and in that noise there was something like honor: that when the world drops around you, you still pull the living out and warm them until their lungs learn to hold a cold, bright air and you suppose that perhaps, between the two of you, this might be enough to stitch the rest of the world back together.
