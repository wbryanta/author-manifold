When she turned off the freeway the hotel rose up as if it had always been there, a pale, rectilinear absence on the curve of a river that, ten years before, had seemed to belong to someone else. It had been rebuilt, the brochures said, and rebuilt in the present tense; the new owner's name—clean, muscular consonants—appeared on the awning, on the Google ad that had come up when she had tried to find the registry, on a brass plaque by the doorman which was as new as the doorman's glove. The construction fences were gone. The palms were the same palms, which seemed to be the useful test of continuity in that part of town—palms survived hurricanes, survived storms of civic taste; palms could be planted to look immediately venerable. The façade had been gutted and made to look older. There were stone planters, slate walkways, a fountain that was not quite the fountain she remembered because its water was too clear, the jets too even, a family of small bronze birds in mid-trajectory that looked manufactured for the sole purpose of being admired from the reception line.

She had been a chambermaid at the old hotel. That is how she thought of herself in sentences she would never speak aloud. It was an identity that had the cleanliness of a uniform and the endurance of a scar. She had been twenty-two the winter of the fire. She had been eighteen the summer she first learned to light the iron without singeing the cotton of her apron. Twenty-two was a good age to learn how to make a bed quickly, to learn which rooms would be given the best tips for a polite smile and which would leave no tip at all, to learn not to say what she knew about a guest when a guest leaned in to tell her his small triumphs. She had been a chambermaid that winter when the smoke started in a third-floor linen closet and, with the industry of a short story, filled the stairwell and the corridors and, finally, the rooms. She had been in room 317, folding a duvet, when a spray of sparks leaped from the vents of the hall. The man who had been in 319 had a bell pepper haircut and a family of comics he kept stacked on his nightstand; he had called down to the desk three times before anyone answered. Later the paper would say the smoke detector had been tied to the light in 321 and the switch had been snapped off, as if some rule of private comfort took precedence over municipal code. It was never explained why the sprinklers had not gone off until it was too late. The questions were asked and archived and the answers the city gave were the precise, bureaucratic sentences that promised something like the truth while consistently avoiding the one thing anyone wanted to hear: that there had been a neglect so ordinary it could have been the subject of a short story.

She did not go into the hotel at first. She sat in the car and watched the bridesmaids rehearsing on the terrace, their bodies in the long, uniform sweep of a dress she had seen often enough—twenty-seven years later?—to predict the angle of their hips when they laughed. She had been given a silver invitation in the mail with the couple's names printed in a serif she recognized from invitations she had once dusted in their cardboard sleeves. The bride was a woman she knew only as some daughter or other, not by name, a connection made through acquaintances: she had cleaned the guest rooms of the bride's grandfather and had once, as duty required, made up a cot for the girl's mother who arrived late and in a mood. It was, she thought, the most natural of social progressions that an old employee of the hotel's service staff might reappear as an invited guest to a family celebration. There was no necessary irony in it. People moved on. Hotels were public in the particular sense that they were private in parts; the public remembered the ballroom and the coffee shop, not the underthings. If anyone cared to recall, the story could be told in the newspapers, in the lawsuits and the small indictments that followed: there would be settlements and apologies, presumably never printed at full length.

She had worn a dress she had bought two days earlier. It was a dress that matched her hair and made the backs of her hands look smaller. At the register she had told the clerk it was for a wedding and the clerk had said, That will look lovely in photographs, and she had wondered whether the clerk thought this was an encouragement. She had worried about the length of the hem, whether it would be too long or too short when she sat. She had ironed the dress in little, obsessive firings of the iron, as if the heat could make one history fold into another, as if an applied temperature could alter the past. The dress had cost a fraction of what a bridesmaid's dress would cost and an enormous sum compared to the wages she had earned as a chambermaid, wages that did not appear on any invitation list. She considered that in any good story there would always be a scene where the protagonist looks back and realizes the cost of her return is small and exacted in a currency nobody else understands, like the price of a memory paid in the thinning of a palm frond.

She went in through the side door because the valet had been instructed to hold the main entry for limousines and families with overflowing arms. The side door had a familiar heaviness to it. It clicked the same way the service doors had clicked. There was a small, unfussy plaque on an interior wall with names listed in a neat, legal script. It was a memorial, corporate and tasteful, in colors that matched the carpeting. Her name was not on it. The names were those of donors, developers, people who had made the hotel’s resurrection possible. There was no mention of "incidents" or "previous staff," only a sentence about "an honorable commitment to safety and community." They had made safety into a phrase and kept it to themselves. The plaque's glow was diffused. The housekeeping entrance smelled faintly of lemon cleaner and, for a moment, the smell of lemon was strong enough to displace the itch of smoke in the back of her throat. Smell worked like that. It did not always tell the truth. It displaced it.

In the lobby the chandeliers were modern in a way that suggested a kind of restrained opulence: brass arms with light bulbs the size and temperature of small moons; a carpet so thick that shoes went down into it and a soft rustle accompanied every step. There was a reception desk, and behind it a woman in a suit so perfectly tailored that the shoulder seams looked painted on. The receptionist had hair in an engineered bob and a name tag that read "EVAN." She asked the usual questions: name, room confirmation, and then, with a professional curiosity, whether the guest had any special requirements. When the receptionist said "Welcome back," the words were casual, the kind of phrase you said to someone who’d checked out and checked in again and whose return you did not construe as anything other than what it was: an administrative event.

She said nothing about her past. There was a man from the couple’s family waiting near the receiving line. She had dusted this man's shoes once—a habit that made her say his name in her head now because it made him ordinary again: Mr. Latham, with an imperceptible bend to his left knee and hands that stayed tactile and deciding when they were not needed. He took her hand as if he knew her from somewhere and did not. That hand was cool. He said, "Evelyn? Is that you?" and she was struck by the way her name sounded to him—like the name on a guest list that one realized only when called aloud. Her name sounded to him untroubled, unembroidered. She had been prepared for a different register, the embarrassed blur of recognition that often attaches itself to past employees when they reappear in zones of ceremony. Instead he said, "You look well," which is another permissible sentence at a wedding. It made her think of the photographer in the old hotel who would, at the hour before a reception, pronounce that people "photographed well," meaning they had the proper bone structure for glossy prints.

She let herself be taken into the receiving line as if it were a kind of procession that required the minimum of her narrative. The bride hugged her father, then hugged the groom, then hugged the assembly of cousins who lined up in a way that made the building's architecture seem part of the choreography. People said, "Congratulations," and "So happy for you," and there was the customary comedy of squeezing cheeks and kissing on both temples as if this restored an order of things. The couple's faces were the practiced faces of people who had given time to rehearsing their smiles; they held them like currencies. The groom was an investment banker, probably, the sort that learned how to speak in sentences that did not yet reveal their bankruptcy. He kept his jacket unbuttoned. The bride's dress had been chosen in consultation with a mother who had opinions and a planner who had a clipboard and two references. The menu was vegan with three courses that sounded less like food than like promises. That was one part of weddings she had never understood: the guarantee of future happiness as a matter of taste.

She found herself placed at a table with people who were neither intimate nor strangers: a woman who had written a book—her name was on the spine and in conversation—and a man who had been a junior senator before he moved into a consulting firm and had a problem with ex-wives that made him inordinately attentive to napkins. There was a small card at the place that read, "Evelyn Hayes," in a hand that balanced flourish with legibility. She had chosen to keep the name she was born with because it was easier than explaining the few marriages she had endured and the single sibling who was not particularly communicative except for annual cards. She glanced at the people at the table and tried to understand what role she had been given in the schematic of the evening. Was she a curiosity? Was she meant to be patronized? Would anyone require that she retell a survivally narrative in a tone they could applaud? She had not written herself as a survivor for their applause. She had, in the years since the hotel fire, developed a habit of avoiding the felicities of her own life story: they tended to remove her agency by making the past into an anecdote.

There were toasts, obviously. Toasts are the social structure by which groups formalize affection and settle accounts. The groom's father rose and said that days like this were not about money but about tradition, which is always a phrase that illuminates the speaker and not the subject. He spoke of the hotel as a place that had, in the family's memory, always offered a certain kind of sanctuary. He mentioned the renovation in a sentence that slid gracefully past the history of the fire; he said, "We are especially grateful to be here tonight," and that was an indirect way of saying nothing. People clinked glasses. The band began to play a set of songs that suggested mid-century gentility; the saxophone was called on for communal nostalgia. The speeches had the particular structure of good public arguments: acknowledgment of debt, tribute to the couple, a small, permissible joke. They always ended with the same line, which in that crowded room sounded like an incantation meant to keep something at bay: "To love, and to health, and to the future."

There was, eventually, motion toward the dance floor. She felt the meal and the wine as a physical weight behind her eyes and did not dance. She had, for reasons she could no longer say, chosen to be stationary. There are small permissions that a person gives herself and that seem, in the moment, to be deliberate declarations of character. She sat and observed the room and the function of the room, which in many ways was the same as the function of the job she had once held: to attend to the needs of others while keeping a careful inventory of the objects that might belabor the day. She watched the servers who glided by and observed them with a mixture of kinship and distance. They moved through the room in a choreography that was strict and, like all skilled choreography, invisible. Their uniforms were not the aprons she had once worn but fitted suits in a color that made them part of the decor. They did not speak. They cleared plates, refilled water glasses, took empty champagne flutes. She watched a young woman clear a saucer and noticed, with a kind of clarity she had learned from long shifts, the way the girl pinched the napkin against her thumb to pull it forward. That small motion told her everything it needed to tell about training and efficiency and the possibility of being photographed at the wrong moment.

At a certain point a man came to the table and said, "Would you mind if I asked you a question?" He had the polite earnestness of a person who believed questions would be answered in full. He was carrying a clipboard and a small rectangle of paper with a schedule printed on it. He was the wedding planner, and he asked whether the guest at that table might give a brief recollection to be recorded for the couple in case the family wanted a "memory archive." The word "archive" in that context meant a constructed history. She thought, as many people will, that to be recorded was to be made into an exhibit. There would be an edited version. There would be a choice of who asked whom in the edit. She said, "No, thank you," because she did not wish to be a curated element in someone else's narrative of restoration. She said it politely, and the planner's face tightened as if at an absence of resources. He said, "Of course," and moved on with the exactitude of someone whose job is to convert personal relapses into a smooth event.

Later, standing in the corridor outside the ballroom for a cigarette she had promised herself one because certain circumstances demanded a small, illicit pleasure, she found herself in the old service hallway, which had been made into a passage for staff to go unseen. It smelled of citrus and new varnish and of something else she could not immediately name. There was a framed photograph on the wall, black and white, of the hotel's grand opening in the nineteen-fifties—men in suits, women in hats, a ribbon cut with scissors. She remembered the other photograph, the one that used to hang in the back corridor of the old hotel, the one that had been taken the winter after the renovation there had been a gala and an asphalt convention and the newspaper had run a two-column spread. That picture had a man in it who looked like he had been singing and then, several lines down in the caption, it mentioned the hotel's new safety features. She remembered the photograph of the fire in the paper: a curling page with the headline and the picture of flames, the captioned faces of evacuees. She had kept the memory of that photograph as if it were an object in a drawer. It was private. She had not given it away to the state's investigating committee, which had been more interested in testimonies that made neat cause-and-effect arcs. The committee had asked, "At what point did you become aware?" She had answered in the way one answers a crossword, supplying the words that led to an end. Real danger does not fit the pace of committee questions.

A woman had emerged from the staff door—one of the hotel's new employees—who had not seen the photograph. She wore the hotel's gray uniform and a hairnet the color of an anonymous appetite. She asked, "Excuse me, ma'am, are you a guest?" and there was no dialect of the question that would allow her to say she had been a worker. She understood, with an economy of comprehension that was typical to her generation of labor, that there are places in which one used to be invisible and now is considered unexpectedly interesting. There are optics in which the worker becomes, in a ceremonial context, a quaint element of authenticity. The girl who had asked if she was a guest had not grown up with the hotel's smells; she had a clean memory of training manuals and fire drills that meant something very different from the actual burning of curtains.

The cigarette was neither illicit nor particularly satisfying. It tasted like the margined edges of a day. She had smoked during night shifts to survive the monotony and afterward had quit because of a husband who disliked it and later had started again because grief and anniversaries of small private things sometimes require gasoline. In the stairwell that led from the ballroom to the service floor there was a sign that read, in friendly capital letters, "NO SMOKING." Nobody bothered the sign. Near the sign there was a photograph of the hotel's foundation laying the first time—the one that seemed to promise a lineage and also to be a thing people could point to when they groped for continuity.

She thought of each room she had cleaned. Room numbers had been shifted in the new iteration of the hotel; where there had once been 317 there was now, perhaps, a 421. The habit of renaming things is one of modernity's great consolations. You could change a number and it would not demand an explanation. You could change the pattern on the wallpaper and remodel a grief out of public view. Yet there were some details that had resisted, threads that memory held taut. The same dresser with the brass handles in a certain configuration; the same angle of light through a window that suggested a small neighbor had not yet redecorated the sky. Those were the details that could not be purchased with marble or repainted by committees.

At the reception line she had been offered candied almonds in a silver dish and, without thinking, had told the woman pressing the dish that the almonds made her think of the hotel's previous candy dish which had been a different silver yet had the same kind of dent on its side. The woman had laughed as if this remark was the required anecdote for a wedding. She had a talent for turning every recollection into a confetti flurry. That laughter made her feel as if she had been a good employee once; people loved it when you made small things ring like orchestrated bells.

When the band started a slow number, the lights softened, and couples rose. The bride and the groom had their first dance. People turned inward to the small circle of light that the couple occupied. It was an observance out of time that she had always watched from the edges during her shifts—the practice diners of lovers in the lobby that would, when they believed no one was looking, kiss with the privacy of people who can pay for it. Watching the couple, she realized that she would never again be able to see fire without filtering it through that memory: the way the smoke had moved like a thing with purpose; the way the light in the corridor bent and made the wallpaper pattern as if it were a body turning. She found herself thinking of a man who had been a bellman—Andre—who had tried to keep everyone calm in the night of the evacuation. He had been an older man then with a jaw like a question and the ability to place strangers into the right taxi. He had been one of those employees who became a kind of moral support for guests who mislaid their continuity in a laundry list of personal crises. After the fire Andre had left town. He had said, in the last sentence she heard him speak, "If they would build it right, we'd be safe." He said it with the precise faith in civic engineering that many men of his generation possessed. The belief that rebuilding could be proof.

Then there was the moment when someone at the table decided to mention the fire because everyone knows you can talk about disaster at weddings as long as you couch it in the right affect. The woman who had written a book leaned forward and said that the hotel had been "tragic," and that phrasing was brittle and postured as if to show compassion without the complication of taking blame. She said, "I remember reading about it. The ladies were so brave." Her voice suggested she had been taking a lesson in public sorrow. People nodded politely. Their plates had not been fully cleared. The man at the end of our table asked if there had been a definite cause and then used the word "negligence" as if it were a mathematical variable. Nobody looked directly at the word because on such occasions the word, like a sudden thunder of rain in a wedding tent, would make everyone feel the water. The bride’s mother insisted that the company had "taken responsibility" which was a sentence that could be read in more than one tense.

It seemed impossible to her that any one sentence could contain the whole past. She thought of the letters that had been sent to former staff. There had been, after the initial investigations, a kind of small compensation program. Some got checks and rode away from the city and never bought property or retrained; some got vocational training that put them into other service careers; some, like her, went to community college and studied something that made sense, something that made the nights quiet and the mornings bolder. She had used the money to pay off a car and then used the car to move. She had kept no photograph of the fire and had been careful not to appear in the pages of the local paper. There is a way of living under the radar that does not require testimonial.

Near the end of the night they announced the cake. The cake was a monument in tiers, precisely the sort of object that, in its scale and frosting, repairs social anxiety. The cake had the hotel's name piped on the base in the same hand as the place cards. It was an act of branding that coincided, in the same breath, with love. People took photographs. They held up champagne glasses and one man made the joke about why the cake had to be tiered because "marriages, like cakes, need structure," which drew a laugh so sharp it made her think of the sharpness of glass. She thought of the charred linen she had once handled—the resin smell that had clung to towels like a second skin—and the nonchalant way the caterer plied the guests with hors d'oeuvres as if the past and the present could exist on the same serving tray.

At almost midnight they moved the party outside to the terrace. Heat cased the night air; and the city, in which the hotel had always been a vertical, breathed around them. There was a moment, as they stepped onto the terrace and the band played a cover of a familiar song, when the moon was visible between the palms and she distinctly felt the old house as if it were behind her ribs. It is not uncommon, she thought, for buildings to retain one’s internal temperature. Some rooms, like the ones she had used to clean, held memory the way bones hold heat after a bout of fever. She looked out at the river and the taxi lights and—oddly—at the place where the old hotel’s chimney had once been, a small remnant on the skyline that had been used for a long time as a landmark. It was, in her memory, like the sight of a man’s hand when he reaches for something and does not find it.

She had an urge, irrational and without the permission of the wedding, to try the service door again. It was, she understood, the common thing to do when one wishes to test an old scar. In the courtyard was a plaque with a short commemoration to "those affected by the previous incident," an official phrase that pointed out the delicacy of an institution wishing not to be defined by failure. The plaque had names on it—not of employees but of donors who had made the memorial garden possible. She put her hand on the plaque because she preferred the tactile to the verbal. The metal was at first cool and then took on the warmth of her skin. It felt, in an odd way, like a passport.

The bride found her at the plaque and said, "Evelyn! I'm so glad you came." She had that practiced calm women of her class use to make sure pain did not sound like pain in public. She had also, Evelyn believed, never been in the service corridor. She had not smelled lemon cleaner in the way a person who had been an employee smelled it. She thought of asking the bride if she had checked the smoke detectors when she had had her house rewired last summer, but she did not ask; it was not the wedding's content. Instead she said, with a civility she meant to be light, that the new garden looked lovely. The bride thanked her and added, "You must tell me the funniest thing that ever happened to you while you were working here." People like to be entertained at weddings by the past as if it were a caricature.

She thought of the night the man in room 505 had insisted the television was broken and refused, in a manner that was not rude but was tediously insistent, to let the desk send someone at three a.m. She thought of how she had been sent, at that hour, to fix the television and had found, instead, that the wall outlet was loose and that an electrical spark had been the prelude to the conflagration that would later be discussed with a language of causes and human errors. The memory was like a stitch. It was small in proportion to the disaster, but it was the thing an investigator might want. She considered telling the bride the minute details. She saw, perfectly, the bride’s eyes would go soft, and she would say in return something like, "Oh my God, I can't imagine," and then the conversation would return to the cake or the dress. She thought of the many ways one can domesticate catastrophe to make it tolerable. She had no desire to domesticate what she had lived through.

Instead she told a smaller story: about the man who had left his housecoat forgotten on the bathroom hook because he had rushed out to his brother's wake. It was a small human thing; the bride laughed and clapped, and the bride's father, who had been standing nearby, said, "There you go—there's the humanity we need," which is the phrase people use when they wish to balance the scales of class by discovering the universal in the specific. That is what weddings are for: to create universals on which to hang adoration.

When the night wound down and the band played a final song of that consoling kind that allows you to move without meaning to the doorways of your life, she walked back through the lobby. The chandeliers were half-lit, the carpets heavy with footsteps. A child was asleep in a chair with her little legs tucked under a blanket. She watched the child with a kind of tenderness that was independent of any event; it had the single, clear focus of someone who had once put a child’s forgotten stuffed animal into a lost-and-found sack and expected it never to be reclaimed. The little folded hands suggested, to her, that the thing that most needed protection in the world was not the reputation of an institution but the simple, vulnerable geometry of sleep.

Outside, the valet's light had gone down to a small, solitary lantern. She stopped for a moment and looked back at the façade, at the hotel in its anonymous dignity, and felt the small, bitter recognition that buildings do not remember people the way people remember buildings. The hotel would advertise its safety record and its fine cuisine and the new night's slogans would be recycled for the next wedding. People who were married at the hotel would grow old and perhaps recall the night the band played and the cake was cut; but the hotel's voice would be different: it would be the voice of an enterprise, not a witness. It is a particular kind of grief to understand the difference.

She took a taxi and when the driver asked where to, she gave him her address. He nodded, and in the way of taxi drivers everywhere asked if she was from out of town. She said she lived in the city and then, because some questions require truth, added, "I used to work in a hotel a long time ago." The driver asked, with the practiced curiosity reserved for small confessions, whether it had been that one. She replied, in the plain manner she had used throughout the evening, that it had been. He made a brief sympathetic noise and then asked if she had suffered serious injuries. She thought of the bandage on her ankle that had been placed there by the emergency room that night years ago, then peeled a thin tissue of memory off and said, "No, nothing like that." She did not mention the nights of insomnia that followed, the way a body refuses to accept quiet. She did not mention the recurring image of a door that will not open because a person has not the strength to pull it wide enough. It remained, among other things, an image she kept in the hold of her own life.

At home she unhooked the dress and folded it with care, placing it in the closet between two dresses she had never worn. She left the invitation in a drawer and, after a long bath, lay on the bed and looked at the ceiling and found herself again in the corridor of a hotel she had once cleaned where light had bent in a way that suggested one more story in the building could have been built. She remembered a child in the lobby that night who had clutched its mother’s hand and had said, "Is it going to be scary?" and the mother had answered, "No, baby, we're fine." There are phrases you are given that are meant to guarantee safety. But no guarantee is ever absolute. There are always, in life, small discontinuities that are the birthplace of larger stories.

Years later she would pass the rebuilt hotel in an evening run and glance at the windows with a casual attention that was no longer inspection but a kind of acquaintance. The city changes the surface of the things it wants to forget and keeps quietly the things it cannot rewrite. There was no large moral to be extracted from a wedding invitation. There was only, perhaps, the sense that the past and the present are, in crowded rooms, two people who stand near one another and who rarely speak except in the margins. She made a private resolution—one among many—that she would not go back to work at a hotel again. That was a different kind of right to change: the decision to keep one's life bound to a different set of rules.

In the months after the wedding she found herself telling a friend that she had attended an elegant affair at a newly opened hotel, that the band had been swell and the cake had been the size of an altar. The friend said, "And did you—" and waved the question as if she knew the rest. She said, "No, I didn't speak to the press." She had the odd sensation that she had been to a funeral that had been dressed as a party. That is one way people attend to catastrophe: they celebrate when they can and do not always realize that to celebrate is to insist on continuity. Continuity is both a comfort and a narrowing.

When small news items jumped, some time later, about a faulty wire in a banquet hall in a city several counties away that led to an evacuation there was, as always, a fitful interest. People read the headlines and thought of a thing that could happen to other people and meant nothing to them. She read the same item with a different eye. She thought of the tiny elements—the loosened outlet, the covered detector, the check that was cashed and spent—elements that combine without malice to become an event. She thought of Andre, who had, in some unremarked way, been correct: to build it right would have made a difference. But everything is built in parts. A city is an architecture of intentions and omissions.

The wedding was over. The hotel stayed. It would continue to market itself, to collect small fortune's worth of ceremonies, to host funerals and galas and conventions for people whose names would be on the plaque. It would do so, she reflected, because the world depends on institutions that are willing to reinvent themselves. She would go to other weddings, to other dinners, to other rooms where she would be a guest and not an attendant. She would not be, in any official sense, changed by the hotel's turn toward dignity. But she had a private ledger. The ledger consisted not of legal documents but of exact sensations that would recur for years: the dry heat of the stairwell, the taste of lemon cleaner, the sound of a bellman's hand on a stranger's shoulder. They were, in the end, small things with a certain weight.

On the anniversary of the fire there was a public memorial arranged by a town council that made a charm of the hotel's name. People who had been guests that night and who were now visible in the city's newsfeeds were invited to speak. She did not go. She had declined the invitation on the grounds of distance, and the committee accepted the phrase. Some people who speak at such memorials do so to create a record. Others speak to feel the warmth of an audience. She had the quiet satisfaction of knowing that she could, if called, recount the sequence of small decisions that led to disaster, but she would never again be asked for the details in the moment she was present at a wedding when the cutlery was too bright and the cake too calculated. She had, in the end, learned the value of keeping certain stories folded in the way one folds cloth, creasing them so that they would take up less visible space but remain present when needed.

The hotel would be, of course, a place she had loved and left. That is the reciprocal arrangement most places make with their employees: they give you skills and you give them memories. She had given the hotel the methodical care of someone who makes beds without complaint and in return had received a set of sensations and an education in comportment. The wedding had been a polite testament to that reciprocal life. She had, for a time, been its witness and then its guest. She had been allowed, for an evening, to cross the invisible barrier that separates the pleasured class from the working one. She had not been a curiosity. She had not been a scandal. She had been present, which in its own way is the most telling kind of survival.

When she told the story to herself later, she sometimes began with the simple declarative sentence: I went back. That was often all one needed. It placed the action in a grammar children understand. The rest—what she remembered of light, of the weight of linen, of the smell of lemon—was additive. It was the life of most people that the past remains a series of additions. She kept her hands to herself and closed the closet door gently, as if she were tucking in an old pet. The door clicked. The sound was like every service door she had ever closed. It was the same and not the same. That, perhaps, is the meaning of return: a small audible proof that some things do not change. Others insist on altering the interior. She lay down and did not sleep at once. She thought, for no particular reason, of the child's hand in the lobby and of Andre's jaw and of the plaque warm under her palm. The city hummed outside the window, a continuous, indifferent chorus. She was, in the end, alive in it and against it. The distinction, she knew, required nothing more than the slow, steady attention she had learned on long nights when the rooms were empty and the people had gone.
