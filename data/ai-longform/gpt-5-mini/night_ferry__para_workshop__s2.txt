I stood on the stern rail until the metal went numb through the thin gloves and watched the wake tear itself into the dark. The ferry cut the strait like she had done every winter of my life and probably long before mine, diesel and ballast and the patient, indifferent force of a ship whose job was to move people and freight between two points and not to care for the lives in its hold. Lights along the far shore were low and spaced like an old man's teeth. The moon was thin and weak; clouds smeared it out, and when the wind pushed them away for a while the surface of the water flashed like a sheet of broken glass. I kept thinking of things that had no business being thought of at that hour. Old gloves. The way my brother used to knot his scarf. A photograph in a drawer, face cut off.

My ticket in my breast pocket felt clumsy and heavy, cumbersome as an obligation. The envelope the hospital had sent with the coroner's name and a time to appear hadn't had my name on it; it had his. But his name was nothing but a rumor to them until someone said otherwise, and the someone the coroner had been able to reach and who had agreed to come was me. "If you can," the man on the phone had said as if pleading with a stranger; "we need next of kin."

It had been raining when the call came, a hard, steady rain that made the kitchen window look like it was sweating. I had been pouring coffee and listing to a late-night radio presenter read the weather. There are small separations built into life — the way weather reads something like a domestic guardian to ordinary days — and when the woman from the hospital said, "There's been a body recovered on the rocks near Kelvin Head," I felt a kind of theft. The rain kept beating the house but the sound retreated into another room. The woman asked my name and I told her, and she said the name she had and the two names were not the same but that was because he'd used his middle for years. I could hear the telephones and some rustle of papers and then she said, "Would you be able to come down to identify him?" as if asking whether I could walk across a hallway, not whether I could put on shoes and step off the map of my life.

I had seen my brother — we used to call him Owen, sometimes O, sometimes just "you" when we were boys — three times in fifteen years. The last time he had stood at the edge of our mother's driveway and not come in. He'd looked like he was still off on a trip, like his pockets were full of other people's lives. We argued about money and a truck that never started and said words that settled like sediment between us. He left in a cab with his duffel, and I watched the taillights until they crossed the bridge and were swallowed by the dark. I had told myself in the years since that leaving him would be easier for both of us, or that he had wanted to leave. The distance had been my construction, and perhaps his too. Maybe we had both believed in that distance like a wall we were not meant to cross.

Now a body had been found. It's a strange corridor of events to travel: the lightless stretch between not knowing and knowing. You move through it by small, practical steps. I called my sister and she told me to go. "If it's Owen," she said, "someone should know." If it wasn't, then someone should be told. The point was not justice and not even necessarily grief; the point was completion. We have rituals for the dead because we know bodies are not the same as names until breath stops; the dead require us to place them where they belong on family trees, to pin the flags on the map. I kept thinking of how many things a body might demand that weren't on the list of things alive people wanted to give.

The lounge was thin with people who had the same temperature in their eyes: tiredness and the uneasy electricity of an in-between. Truckers in reflective vests snored with mouths open. A woman with a child's backpack slept with one shoe missing. A young couple kept to themselves, the man tracing a finger along the woman's forearm like he was reading a ship's lane. The ferry smelled of damp wool, stale wood, and coffee that had been reheated one too many times. I sat and held my hands over my cup until the heat softened them and it felt like I had done something to steady myself.

On the deck someone yelled about a gull and was laughed into silence. The ferry made a soft, metal groan, the machinery shifting as if waking from sleep. When the cigarette-smoke-carried man sat down opposite me I said nothing at first. He had a face you might have thought you recognized from a thousand harbour towns: wind-leathered, the corner of an old smile like a broken hinge. He asked me if I was from here.

"No," I said. "From the mainland."

"Been out much?" he asked, as if the smallness of my lifeable travel was something to be inquired after.

"Not in the last while."

"Aye," he said. "There's things you know and things you don't. People come and go. The sea keeps its ledger, but only it understands."

He said it because that's what men who work on ferries say to people who have trouble keeping the horizon in their heads. He didn't ask why I was going. He didn't have to. We traded a few sentences about the weather and about whether the crossing would be rough. The boat rolled in a steady, honest way, and the cold made the whiskey in my flask taste like a small, sacred thing I could sneak.

I thought of my brother walking the shoreline in the winter once, barefoot, just afterward when we were children and he had dared us to feel the water. He stood very still until his toes went numb, then laughed as if he'd been made anew. There were pictures of him looking out over water in any box with his face left in the frame. He had always been restless in ways the family didn't quite understand. We called it "the sea in him." Some people are kept by soil and others by salt; Owen was salt-faced even when he'd been sitting in the middle of the living room watching television. He seemed, once, to move as if he expected the tide to turn to him at any moment.

We docked in a dawn that looked like an apology: thin, gray, apologetic light that drifted up the walls and under the hull of the ferry. The quay smelled of diesel and something metallic, like coins or old keys. Half the town was awake in a way that made the world feel smaller — a man letting his dog out, a window with lights on and the shadow of an arm reaching for curtains. I walked toward the car park and the hospital like you would walk toward a small, necessary errand, but the muscles in my jaw tightened. There are simple oddities about grief that don't make sense until you feel them: how you gather small things to buffer yourself against a decision, like rubbing the edge of your thumb with your teeth.

The coroner was a compact woman with hair cropped against her head. She shook my hand the way people shake hands when they have practiced the gesture. She didn't smile much. We went through a room with forms and a kettle, the waiting area where someone had propped a newspaper over a child's colouring page. The hospital smelled of menthol and paper and the faint adhesive tang of labels. The man at reception made a soft sound like a small animal and said, "You must be Thomas?" He had a paper badge. I said nothing.

"Yes," the coroner said. "We understand there are complications. The body's been in the water. The face is not... not clear."

This was the part where the body would be reduced to features: the list of what can and cannot be used. If a person is to be attached properly to a name after death there have to be things with which to stitch them on: scars, tattoos, dental records, DNA. There was a photograph on a clipboard, an image forty-eight hours old, sunken cheeks, hair plastered to a head the color of pond silt, a jacket with a small tear at the cuff. "You said you have a brother," she said. "The family listed you. We can show you; but I must warn you—"

I nodded. She led me through a corridor where the tiles were the wrong kind of white, and into a room that smelled like a library's back room and like cleansing. The slab was colder than I guessed the skin could be, a thing that had been lying out in the wind for too long. The body was covered except for the hands and a bit of chest. The coroner asked if I wanted to see him and I thought of all the possible ways to refuse: let the bureaucracy of names and forms do its work, let police and statistics and a landfill of unknowns swallow another life. I had come to this place through a train of guilt transparent as glass. I had come to say something and to be able to say later, to my sister and to myself, that I'd done what was needed.

They brought the sheet down.

He was a thing I recognized by geometry more than by face. The line of his jaw, the long, knife-cutor cheekbones I'd seen in photographs were there, softened by the water as if the world had been working at smoothing away what was sharp. The hair was a darker, stranger color with seawater in it; a curl that had never obeyed a comb. For a moment, before the coroner's voice filled the room, I looked at him and saw not only my brother but every other man who had gone missing from memory: the boy who couldn't sit in Sunday church, the man who once took a wrong bet, the one who took a long way home.

The hands were clean enough to show small pale crescents where his nails had been. There was a faded tattoo on his forearm: a swallow in flight, wings thin and a little blurred with age. How many men had a swallow tattoo, I thought, and then, of course, mine did. He'd had a swallow; we used to call swallows "good-luck birds" as kids because our father grew up on stories, and when Owen came home on one summer afternoon with ink on his wrist my mother shrugged and said, "Another expedition." He used to say he was built for leaving. The swallow, crooked as it was, leaned to the left.

There was also a scar, pale and puckered, on the left calf where he'd cut himself on a boat when we were sixteen. I'd been with him when it happened. He stood on the gunwale and swore at me for holding a lantern so close. The wind took the words from his mouth. He had slid and the blade had nicked his leg. The scar had been nearly vertical and he'd refused to see a doctor. He told stories about how it would be a mark he kept like a medal. The scar was there now, white on a skin that in life had been freckled. I traced it with my mind like a thing that has been mapped and then faded at the edges.

"Is that the tattoo you were told about?" the coroner asked.

"It looks like it," I said. My voice sounded like paper. I want to say that in that moment recognition bloomed like a light, sudden and bright, but it did not. It was more like the slow, productive click of a key sliding into a lock. I looked at him more. There was a silver filling at the lower molar if you pushed his lip aside; I remembered the dentist in our town who told him he'd be better off without sugar, and Owen had laughed and said, "Sugar is the whole point." There was a small, regular scar on the knuckle of his left hand where he'd once cut himself peeling an orange in a way that made me laugh in the kitchen. These were small evidences, not court orders.

"And the missing tooth?" the coroner asked softly. She knew the rules: you needed to be sure. The rule is not to allow sentiment to make up for evidence, but families have long used sentiment as the substance of law.

"He had a front tooth that was small," I said, and then I saw the smallness — how the front tooth was smaller than the one beside it in pictures — and how the dentist in the town had called it "cosmetically different" as if that fixed anything. In the awake days before that night I could reconstruct him as a mural made of small, unimportant things.

"Do you want to sign the form?" the coroner asked. The paper was a little smear of bureaucratic white that breathes the air of finality. I thought: if I sign this, then the towels will come and he will be placed in a cooler and a tag will go on and the family will be called and the room will be too small for such a thing. If I do not, then an unknown stays unknown and bodies languish without names and the water keeps its ledger. Who was I to say whether his ledger should remain secret?

I thought of the years of silence, of the thing I had done that made him leave: a letter that said what I thought was right, a cut-off, a refusal. I had wanted him to be better, to stop asking money he hadn't earned. He wanted other things. It is not uncommon for brothers to quarrel over the living things that look like petty grievances and build a canyon out of them.

"You'll want to be careful about this," the coroner said. "We can make a positive ID, but we should be sure."

I put my hand on the edge of the slab. It was cold enough to take a breath out of me. He looked like he belonged to the sea at the moment, but he had a watch on, a cheap thing with a band that had once been bright. It had stopped. I knew that this was not how people should be judged in the light of the living — a watch stopped is not a man's story — but the watch would be one more line in the list.

"My mother," I said, and the sound of it was wrong in my mouth as if the wrong vowel had been used. If I said nothing would they let him lie, unpinned? If I signed, they would contact the funeral directors and my mother would have to be told. Either way, the family would be summoned back to that same small room of decisions. My sister would look at me with those small, tired eyes and say, "Why didn't you tell us?" as if one of us could have made the whole of him different.

"Do it," I told myself. It wasn't just for my mother; it was for me. There are certain acts that belong solely to the living: to name, to carry, to gather. I signed the paper with a pen that left a thin indented line on the paper like a small boat track. The coroner folded the paper and said we would need a name for the record. I told her Owen's proper name — Owen Samuel Barnes — and the coroner wrote it down as if writing down a prayer.

Afterwards, the small, pragmatic things took their turn. A woman from the police came to get my details and to ask for the contact numbers for next of kin. She read a statement to me that had the sterile authority of a map: found on rocks, presumed to have drowned, no signs of foul play. She would ask things like where he was known to frequent, the places he had been apt to sleep, and the names of his friends. I gave her what I could without thinking because one doesn't need to think in these rooms; you simply produce what memory will provide. The woman took my mother's number and promised to call.

When I walked out into the morning I had an object in my mind like a small stone: a fact that would anchor the rest of my days. I had explained to the coroner that I'd seen enough to be sure, and that in some small way being sure had made things possible. But the being sure was not a clean thing. It was a patchwork acceptance. I could have been wrong. Bodies recover names both gently and wrongly. This is always true, even if we do it for charity.

The town was waking as if from a long nap. The baker had put loaves in the window and the bell for the grocer sang when a child pushed. I bought some tea from a vending machine to have something warm in my hands that wasn't obligation. Across the quay a group of men leaned on a railing and shouted small, private jokes at each other. I felt like a person who had been given permission to enter a room where there were two small chairs and a coffee table of cold porcelain, and I had to sit and say nothing for an hour while the world adjusted.

There was a noticeboard by the police station where someone had posted photographs with a small plea: "Have you seen this man?" and the corner of his mouth smiled in a way that the old photographs always did when the subject knew the camera was watching. I stood there and tried to recall when he'd last been cleanly at peace. I couldn't. How often do we remember the living in fragments? How much of our memory is simply a sequence of the way things ended?

My sister arrived with hair confined under a small wool hat and a face the color of someone who had slept too little. She held my mother's hand. We stood in the hospital car park and the three of us were a small, uneven geometry: the present, the past, the future folded into a single, unmovable thing.

"You came when I asked," she said. It was not so much gratitude as recognition of a contract.

"Someone had to," I said. "I don't know what else to say."

She put her hand on my arm the way people do to stabilize one another when language is failing them. "Come home," she said. "We can tell her."

We went back and the house was small and smelled of brewed tea and the cat had decided that the mornings were no longer solitary. My mother was sitting in a chair by the window with a blanket over her knees. She looked as if the years had been less kind than she had hoped. When we told her that we'd identified Owen she waited until the words were said and then did something quiet that made me think of all the small, automatic rituals of family. She folded her hands in her lap and said, "Thank you for doing that," as if that was the most ordinary thing in the world. She had never asked me to be brave in my entire life, and now she did it quietly and simply.

The funeral came with a weather forecast low on warmth. The undertaking agency had a black van that smelled of cleaning polish, and the minister's voice filtered through the chapel like a static line. People came who had known him and some who had known of him. There were friends who looked like they had been waiting for something to happen, who had perhaps always known the geography of his choices. We all spoke of him in the tenses that allowed him to be made respectable: "He loved the sea," "He kept to himself," "He always wanted to be free." No one said "I forgive you" because we reserve forgiveness for the past or for the living, and the dead require other work: naming, burial, the arrangement of small tokens on a coffin.

At the burial, I threw a handful of earth onto the coffin and felt its weight thud on my palms, the grainy gesture of finality. I thought of a time when Owen and I had been boys and we had dug a trench on the beach for reasons we could not give names to: to see if the ocean would notice, to mark the place where we had decided to lay something to rest. The trench filled at high tide and we watched it accept the water without complaint. The idea of the sea taking what it wanted and giving nothing back had been planted in me then.

Afterwards, people drifted like pieces of seaweed, meeting in smaller knots to exchange small condolences. My mother said things that had the blunt dignity of old women who have lived long enough to see tragedies repeat. My sister put her hand on the small of her back and we walked home together. It was the middle of the day and the wind had dropped and the sky had the sharpness of days that will remember weather.

The next evening I found myself back on the ferry like a man trying a door again. The crossing was quieter now, like a room that has been emptied. Rusted light from the quay retreated. The sea had a thin blue sheen like the bruised underside of a thing. For some nights following the funeral I couldn't sleep. I would wake at odd hours and find myself wanting to place something on his grave that was not a flower — something of value, a token. I walked the decks thinking of old jokes and of the one line of a song we had both known by heart. I had signed papers that had made him a legal thing, a named corpse in the ledger, but pieces of him remained in the unfinished: a sweater at a friend's house, a letter tucked in a library book, a cigarette lighter with initials rubbed by many nervous fingers. The act of naming him had not stitched the past the way one might have hoped.

The ferry's lounge was empty and I sat near a window and watched the black line of coastline pass. I thought of all the ways a person can become unknown: by moving, by hiding, by refusing the small social contracts. In my head I made a list of the moments that had gone into the unmaking of a brother: the slights, the money, the condescension. The list was a modest thing, not an enormous indictment. The one thing it had was time. Time does many things; chief among them, it erodes the edges of our certainties.

At the final rail, a young woman leaned against the bars and took a deep breath. She was pregnant in a way that made her smile like a secret. She remembered a boy she had once loved who drank the sea in himself and had written him a small note, folded and kept under the mattress. I imagined the small rooms we all keep secret in our chests. She tapped the rail, looked at the water, and said, "Do you ever think the sea remembers us?"

"I don't know if it remembers," I said. "I think it holds us. In its own way."

She nodded. We said nothing more. She had asked a question that didn't require an answer, only companionship. The night pulled at us with its own rhythm.

Weeks later I found myself at an empty desk in a quiet room where the papers for the settlement were being arranged: small amounts for little things, a cheque for a life that had slipped between the cracks. The world continued in its arithmetic. I took a single item from Owen's effects that had been returned to the family: a key on a ring attached to a small wooden carving of a ship. It was the sort of thing lovers buy for each other when pockets are empty and hopes are abundant. The key had no label. I pressed it in my hand the way a coin is pressed between the fingers and then slid it into my own pocket as if to keep it warm.

If there is a last thing to be done in a case like this it is not only the arranging of stones but the permission to carry memory differently. The living get to write endings, and often those endings are not tidy. In the quiet hours I'd go down to the harbour, sit on a bench with my hands in my pockets and watch the boats come in. Fishermen's lives are written in nets and knots and not in paragraphs, and among them Owen had looked like one who knew how to tie a hawser hoping the tide would pull him. I have come to understand that identifying a body is like making a promise — not a promise that the past can be undone but one that the living will do what is left to do. For me that meant calling my mother every night for a while, driving to the grave to put odd seasonal things there, keeping an eye out for swallowed items that might be his. It was a kind of maintenance, the kind you do for someone you love but cannot rescue.

In time the coroner's formal letter arrived, confirming what we already knew, and in my quiet I flattened it with my thumb until the ink wore thin. There are all kinds of finalities that need official ink; in those places we like to believe truth is tidy. But when I folded the paper and slid it into a drawer I felt something else bloom: a kind of acceptance that is not contentment. It is the acceptance that people end in ways that do not answer the questions we have for them.

Months later, on a day when the wind was shy and the sea wore a sheet of pale glass, I took the ship's key out of my pocket and looked at the wooden ship. The carving was small and worn smooth by the hand that had held it, perhaps his, perhaps someone else's. I set it on the hollow of my palm and thought of the nights we'd dared each other on the rocks, of the way he had taught me not to be afraid of water's cold bite. The thing I carried was not proof; it was a talisman, the last piece of a geography that was now a memory. I could have let it go into a drawer and let it be another relic, but instead I walked down to the quay. The tide wore slow and the sea spread out like a bed at the end of a long day. I stood there until the wood warmed in the sun and then I let the key go.

It slipped from my fingers with a small, decisive sound and sank, first showing a bright flash of metal and then swallowed. For a moment I watched its little arc and felt something uncoil in me, something like the letting go of a rope you had tied too tight. The key hit the water and disappeared and the sea kept on as if it had gained nothing and yet everything.

No trumpet blew. No remarkable revelation had been bestowed upon me. The act was small and private. I walked away lighter and not light; I had not made the past right, and I had not undid what had been done. But in the space left by the key's leaving the water took its place and the ledger was amended by a minor hand. It was enough, perhaps, that a hand had acted with intention.

The ferry pulled away and I watched the shore recede. There are edges that cannot be crossed twice, and there are shores that will call you back in ways that you cannot foresee. I had gone across the strait to identify a body because the world needed anchors where it could, and because somewhere in the quiet of those decisions a man can find the room to name himself and his failures and carry them. I had come back with pockets emptier but memory fuller, with the shape of a brother's life stitched into the cloth of my days. The strait lay behind; ahead, the mainland waiting with ordinary obligations and the thin, ongoing grace of mornings. The ferry hummed on, and the water smoothed itself into a ribbon of light.
