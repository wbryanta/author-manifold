At the ramp the wind cut through his jacket like an old accusation, and he thought of Rasmus, of the way Rasmus had once stood on the quay with his hair sticking up and his pockets full of shells, insisting you could hear the weather coming if you put your ear to a hand as if it were a shell. The ferry made a low, lumbering sound, the kind of sound that belonged to animals and machine mixed into one, and he walked aboard as if the sound had something to tell him—some small verdict on what he was about to do.

They told him over the phone that a body had been found, that it was likely Rasmus, that it would be at the small port morgue by morning. Likely, they said. Most likely, they said, but then, at the end, maybe; possibly. The words hung like the thin fog over the strait: visible because of how they softened edges, because of how they made the world slightly more distant. He had packed a single bag because packing seemed like a ritual that might purchase belonging. Inside was a sweater his mother had knitted, a toothbrush, and a passport he had not used in years. He had also brought the small leather notebook where, over the last few months, he'd written down Rasmus's names: tramp, vagabond, brother, fugitive, poet—words scrawled and erased and scrawled again in a hand that grew clumsy when grief and anger had the night-shift.

He found a seat by a window and watched the harbour lights unpeel themselves from the shore, small, deliberate, like stitches. The other passengers were ghosts in the reflected glass, their faces smeared by condensation. Some wore work boots and smelled faintly of diesel; a young woman with a baby had a scarf wound too tightly and talked on a phone with a voice that tried to be untroubled. A man with a newspaper neurally read the headlines, as if reading them aloud might prove their existence. The ferry's annunciation hummed, a synthetic voice polite and indifferent, telling them to enjoy the crossing, reminding them of the cafeteria. He did not move to the cafeteria. He bought two coffees and left one untouched, as if propping it near the window might be an act of waiting.

When the police had called—small precinct, man with a careful voice—he had stood in his kitchen with his hands in dishwater and let the water go cold. The woman at the other end of the line talked about the body being found at first light near the rocks, about injuries that suggested a fall, about the difficulty of distinguishing a face after time in the sea. She read the options: If you would like to, you can come to identify; if you prefer, we can proceed with temporary measures until identification can be carried out by dental records; you can appoint someone else; you can do nothing. The list of options read like the catalog of a failing language, like choices that unraveled into each other. He told her he would come. He told her nothing else, which was true: he had not known what else to say.

He'd last seen Rasmus four years earlier at a house that smelled of paint and lavender air freshener and a woman who had married a man named Tor and then was quietly happier than when she had been with Rasmus. They had argued—sharp, quick exchanges ricocheting around a living room that didn't belong to either of them—and Rasmus had left with his coat and folded anger. He'd promised to call. He hadn't. Later, months later, there had been postcards with vague landscapes and slanted handwriting, promises to visit, complaints about trains, a letter that spoke of a job that didn't last. Then silence. Names disappear in silence. The longer the silence, the less culpable you can feel. The world makes room for forgetting.

He remembered the last time his mother had asked, weeks before she died, if he had heard from Rasmus. He had said no and watched something move in her mouth, a small convulsion that was all at once disappointment and an old, worn hope. She died before Rasmus could be found to stand beside the coffin. At the funeral his aunt had patted his hand in a way that suggested a map of duties. "He's out there somewhere," she had said, as if distance were merely a geography. After the funeral he had put Rasmus's picture in a drawer and closed the drawer like a promise.

A woman in a navy coat took the seat beside him. She asked if the seat was taken. He said no. She said she had family on the island and that bad weather could make the crossing an old memory, that there were ferry crossings where time seemed to fold upon itself. Her name was Ingrid. He did not say his. She looked at him as people do when they expect a sign of kinship—an old photograph, a locket—and he felt at that moment like a man who had given away his last useful thing: the right to be read at a glance. He had no immediate grief that showed on skin; grief had become, in his life, more like a joint ache, a complaint of the weather.

The sea was not the black that pictures show. It flexed grey with a rib of moon when they left the deeper channels. There were lights on the far shore that blinked like a memory throttle. He thought about Rasmus's life as if it were a biography written in the margin of another man's book: snippets of song lyrics, a photograph of an alley, the smell of cigarettes on a rented jacket. He thought of all the practical things people say you should think about when someone dies—funeral, inheritance, paperwork—and felt their uselessness: papers and manuals for living belong to the living. He had imagined two possible ends to the matter: either he would stand in a morgue and know in the sudden, physical way that only the dead make possible—recognition by scar or shape—or he would stand and find only absence and be left, finally, with complete silence. Both outcomes terrified and bored him in equal measure. He wanted closure like someone wants a cold compress for an old bruise.

The ferry began to roll with a small, steady moodiness. He could tell by the way the coffee in his cup rode the swell; the ship's pitch told him how lethargic and generous the sea was that night. He had once, as a child, watched Rasmus reel with laughter on a small rock spit during a storm and been furious at his levity. Rasmus had said you couldn't be alive all the time, that you had to test your skin against weather to feel it. "You're always counting," he had told him, and there had been both an invitation and an edge. Counting implies an inventory of loss, and he had thought that in later years his inventory would make sense. Instead it was a list of unpaid debts and notes-to-self and the odd, lonely encomium of things undone.

At some point the sea opened into a particular brightness—Dawn wasn't a single moment on that trip but a patient smoothing of difference—and the lighthouse on the opposite headland blinked as if in Morse code. Passengers who had been asleep began to stir. The synthetic voice announced their arrival in the small town, naming the port with a corporate cheer. He stood and took his bag and walked to the ramp with the other passengers. The air near the quay had the smell of fish and old rope. A police car waited, blue light uncaring in the dim.

The morgue was tidy and unadorned, like a small schoolteachers' office that had been converted into something far colder. A man in plain clothes who called himself Henrik met him at the door and talked with the spare voice of an official trying to be human. He led him through fluorescent corridors, past a vending machine with a slant of a ghost, past a waiting room that smelled faintly of lemon. There was a form to be filled, a box to check if he wanted the body released or held. He sat at a plastic table, the paper under his hand creasing. The man's questions were small and precise, like someone sewing in public: name, birthdate, relationship. The name looked wrong on the line. When the man asked if he wanted to be present when the body was uncovered, it felt like an invitation and a trap.

They asked if he wanted coffee. He did. The waiting room television showed a slow loop of a children's program with bright graphics. It was obscene: cartoon birds spoke in cheer. For the first time, the scene looked like a caricature of himself: a man waiting for a revelation he wasn't sure he wanted. He thought of how his mother had once told him that Rasmus always liked to leave doors slightly open, a way of telling the world he could return. Maybe Rasmus had gone because he couldn't stay; perhaps he'd gone because the world was heavy and he couldn't carry it. People go, he thought, in degrees, faltering at first and then gone so long you forget the step. 

The corridor smelled of antiseptic but also of tea, like the remnants of some longer domestic life. A nurse who had the soft, all-knowing hands of those who work with grief came and guided him down a small hallway, into a room with a single table and a curtain. The sheet over the body was a white line on white, a kind of punctuation. She asked if he knew the person or if he expected to recognize anything. He wanted to say yes and no at once.

When she pulled back the sheet, the face under it did not look like any of the faces he carried inside his head. It was a thing made from loss, every feature dulled by water and time. The eyes were closed, swollen, and the mouth was set in a way that suggested the last thing the man had seen had been a hard, then softer, then softer light. There were bruises on the cheek, the jaw was broken at an angle like a failed hinge. The scalp had been shaved in one place, a pale patch that might have been an old scar or simply the work of time in the sea. The skin was mottled, a watercolor where the pigment had run. He thought of the old sea maps with their cartographer's care—coastline and depth and a note here: shoal. The face showed depth and cliff and wreck.

He walked closer and saw, at the temple, a long, pale line, a healed scar, shallow and ragged. It was Rasmus's scar. It had been received when they were children—an apple-tree fall onto a root, Rasmus crying the way he had always cried—face red, mouth open at the astonishment of how easily the world could hurt you. The scar ran like a script across the skin, a small map of a particular weather. He felt the blood move in his neck. It was a ludicrous thing, recognizing someone by an old, healed injury, like identifying a town by the pattern of its chimneys.

But then he thought—what if he was wrong? What if Rasmus had once had the scar and some other man had, too? People have similar bruises and the sea levels universality. There was a mole on the lower lip that he imagined to be Rasmus's, but the mole might have washed away under the sea like anything. He had no photographs of Rasmus from the last decade that were recent enough to compare. He wanted a certainty he could not buy.

"Do you need time?" the nurse asked gently.

He found himself listing things: a set of small tattoos they had traced on the backs of their hands at fifteen—an anchor and a bird—things they had drawn and laughed at. He wished he could say, There is the very bar from which he stumbled home, the shape of his ear, the scar. The list came out in his mouth like an inventory of debts owed to the dead. The nurse nodded without judgment and left him there with the body as if that were only right.

He touched the man's hand. The skin was cold and strange, the bones a loose architecture beneath. For a moment he expected the hand to belong in his palm like it had when they were boys, when hands were small and asking and the world was a list of dares. He remembered Rasmus in a doorway at nineteen, a thin crown of hair and the audacity of someone who expected the world to be pliant. He remembered him at twenty-seven sitting at a bench with a dog-eared notebook and talking to the empty air as if the air were a person. He had loved Rasmus then and resented him for the same incandescence. Love and resentment had a way of orbiting each other in the same small hemisphere of his chest.

The face had a smell—the mix of disinfectant and something older and saltier. If you have loved someone, you know the smell of them like a route. When he pressed his face near the chest as if to hear the breath that was gone, some animal part of him expected recognition. The body was Rasmus and not Rasmus, like a photograph of a place you had once been: if you enter it you feel you are home, but standing in the doorway you could be in someone else's house.

He did not cry, which surprised him. Grief, he had imagined, would be a waterfall that drowned his lungs and left him sputtering on the shore. Instead it was a small, precise ribbon of cold that ran between his shoulder blades. In that cold there was room for a thought that made him recoil with a tiny, professional shame: he could sign. He could sign the release forms and bring the body back, bury it in the soil his mother had lain in. He could bring an end to the list of obligations. Or he could refuse. Refusing would be an act of principle, as if pronouncing the name of the absent made it more true. Accepting, he was aware in a softer voice, would be an act of mercy, or of selfishness, or both.

He thought of a time when they were twelve and Rasmus had taken the skin-tight watch his father had given him and thrown it into the river for the pleasure of seeing something valuable disappear. "It was stupid," Rasmus had said, as if offering apology and mockery together. The watch had been recovered by boys who'd meant nothing by it; the face cracked, the hands frozen, but the story had become a desert to pass around. He had been furious then, because he was the older, the keeper of possessions. To Rasmus, possessions were a kind of weight that collected scolding and dullness. He had wanted to teach Rasmus to be careful of things. Rasmus had wanted to cut things loose.

The grief was the accumulation of these cuttings, like a shore strewn with flotsam. Accepting the body as Rasmus felt like attaching a label to a piece of driftwood and deciding it was the trunk of the old birch tree that had been uprooted in their childhood storm. He could do that and be relieved. He could do that and be a liar to himself, preserving a lie in order to sleep in a house that had fewer questions. It was dreadful to think that funeral rituals sometimes exist to make the living exist less fearfully.

When he signed the form there was a small point of contact—pen against plastic—and it felt like a hinge. The nurse asked if the family wanted to view the body at home, if they wanted an open casket. The phrase "open casket" had a theatricality that he also found obscene. He declined. He had imagined, as a child, funerals would be certain things—dark wood, a man singing a voice so deep it vibrated the air—and each time life had offered him a new version, smaller, less theatrical. The nurse wrote in a tidy hand and passed a photocopy. He would be given the body for burial. He signed his name and the letters seemed graceless.

He took one last look as if the look were a sacrament. Up close the face had a small, almost imperceptible smile on one side, the way people sometimes hold their lips as if keeping a secret. He tried to read that smile and decided against anything too fanciful. He could not know whether Rasmus had thought of home, of a woman, of a cigarette, of a song. He could not know what flickered in someone's chest in the last seconds before surrender. He had never been the one who could make peace with Rasmus's choices. He had been a man of rules; Rasmus was someone who seemed to assume there were loopholes in the universe.

Back at the small desk the paperwork smelled of toner. He signed another line about belongings found and placed in a sealed bag: a lighter, a battered photograph with corners frayed, a ring that might be brass, two coins, and a key. A name scratched on a tag was faded. He opened the bag and took out the photograph. A younger man was leaning on a rail, grinning. The grin belonged to a line of teeth he recognized. The man holding the photograph across the table had rough hands with short nails, and he looked at the photograph the way people look at things they mustn't touch.

"You sure?" he asked. His voice was the weather: mild and somehow unhelpful.

He was not sure in the way the world asks for surety. He was, however, certain in a way that has to do with closure. He told the man his brother's name and that this was him. The man nodded and made another note. Once you pronounce a name in an official room, with forms and signatures, the world rearranges to accommodate it.

On the ferry returning, the sea was neither bright nor rude; it had a kind of slow, unbothered indifference. People spoke in low voices about trains and breakfast and bad hotel beds. He had, beneath his jacket, the small packet that had been the man's belongings. He kept running his thumb along the rim of the brass ring as if fingers could memorize the contour of other lives. The photograph sat in his notebook like a ghost. He thought about what he had done and whether it would make him a better son. In some small and vulgar way he felt the release the paperwork promised. He felt as if he had stopped a leak. The leak was not sealed—there were still questions like nails under the floor—but at least he had put his finger in the hole.

In the evening before he left, Rasmus had been a rumor in the kitchen, a line in a letter about a place with too much sun and not enough rain. In the morning he would be a body, then a set of boxes, then a grave, then a story told at intervals. What mattered in the near term was the tactile: the deed of burial, the small things that need doing. He had always liked being useful. Usefulness is a kind of cruelty: it asks you to do the work of the living so they don't have to feel the rest.

On deck the wind had sharpened into a knife and there were seagulls that seemed to fly without a plan. He stood and looked across the wake where the boat had been. Somewhere below another night had given up its breath and fish had turned and men had lost themselves in deep blue. The strait could keep its mysteries and its things; the sea took objects like it took people and polished them, or hid them, or forgot them altogether. He thought about Rasmus as someone who had favored motion over anchor. He had always envied his brother that ability to be a leaf on the current, even as that envy made him dread the suddenness with which life could throw a leaf against a stone.

A small child ran by and knocked his little toe against his boot, looked up at him with an authority only children have, and then was tugged away by a parent who smiled apologetically. The child had on a bright hat with a pompom and laughed as if the world were still a place made to be laughed at. He felt, odd and tender, like some old appliance that had been turned on again. There was a sense, faint and ridiculous, that by naming Rasmus dead he'd put his brother in a closed category, and this made space in his life to be otherwise. He had always thought of closure as a traitorous thing, but perhaps it was a kind of modest mercy.

At the headland port the lighthouse blinked again. The town had not changed much. There were the same rows of houses with paint flaking at the eaves, the same fish shop with its sign that had been replaced three times and was never quite straight. He had imagined an audience of old neighbors to witness his confession—a litany of what he had done and not done—but the town offered him only the small indifference of people busy enough with their own griefs. He carried the photograph in his pocket like contraband and the key felt like weight and promise.

The funeral was a small, functional thing a week later. It was held in the little church that smelled of beeswax and cheap florists' foam. The coffin was dark and modest. He had asked that the photograph be placed inside. In the pews were people with faces from childhood and strangers who had known Rasmus in ways he had not. A woman who had been Rasmus's friend in a town he'd never visited gave a small speech about how Rasmus had loved small things with the affection of someone who knows he cannot keep them. "He noticed," she said simply, and it was the most beautiful accusation. She said nothing of his failures. A man from a shelter where Rasmus had stayed talked about Rasmus's laugh and how it brightened a room full of people who thought they'd run out of that kind of light. The pallbearers were men who had once sat at tables with Rasmus and who now carried him as if his absence had become a new skill.

When the earth closed over the coffin it was not dramatic. The shovel sounded as if it were committing a gentle, necessary punctuation. The priest said words that had never seemed entirely to speak to him before but which felt right in the associations they made: rest, peace, travel well. He put a little packet of dried seaweed into the ground because Rasmus had liked to joke about eating the ocean as if one could devour a coastline. It struck him as an oddly useful joke, one that could be part charm and part ritual.

Afterward they went to a small hall where coffee tasted like grief and cake and people swapped stories. They talked about Rasmus as one speaks of an old song. They said the usual things and the uncommon ones, those that managed to singe the edges of memory: that Rasmus had once stood in a rainstorm to watch a truck pass and we'd all laughed. Once, he had stolen a loaf of bread and given it to a cat. People told stories as if memory were a theater and they had been cast. He listened and at some point realized the stories were more about the telling than the person. That was the way humans hold the world together after pieces break; they breed narrative from loss because narrative makes things predictable and therefore less cruel.

In the quiet that came later, after the last of the guests had left and the house was a space again, he stood with Rasmus's photograph and the key. He put the photo in a drawer because the photographs in drawers keep their weather. He kept the key in his pocket. It was small and ordinary. It did not feel like theft so much as adoption. He could claim it without theft because no one else had stepped in to take it. Objects claim you sometimes more than people—in the dark they are companions because they do not ask.

He had, by signing, made one of those ordinary promises that water the dead. He had, in asking for Rasmus's name to be pronounced by officials, turned rumor into record. He felt guilty for the relief this produced. Perhaps grief could be commodified in this way: a paper signed in a sterile office buying you a kind of quiet town. He wondered, with a private ferocity, whether Rasmus would have wanted to be discovered, recognized, buried in a plot in a town that remembered him as someone who could not stay. He wanted to imagine Rasmus somewhere with wind and room, but perhaps Rasmus had chosen to be here where everyone could know the story and recite the parts that made him interesting.

At night, he found himself at the small window of the house looking out at the sea. The strait glowed faintly where the moon had left it, a washed silver. He could see the ferry's blurred wake slowing into a line. There were names you learn out of civil obligation and names you keep in the heart because they are soft like bread. He thought he could not speak Rasmus's name without a reflexive tenderness. Saying it aloud in that small kitchen made the air look slightly different.

He began, clumsily, to forgive himself for old judgments. Not because Rasmus could be excused—people do strange things that leave others to collect the shards—but because a man cannot stay angry forever without becoming a monument to himself: solid, turned inward, a warning sign. Forgiveness might be a way of stepping out of the cold. Or perhaps it was a practical generosity, the kind that allows you to sleep.

Weeks later he cleared the drawer and found among the old letters a photograph of them as boys, knees dirty, looking daring and stupid in equal measure at the camera. He sat and studied the photograph until the light bathed the two of them in a kind of sepia mercy. He touched the edge of the paper like the edge of a map and imagined Rasmus on a voyage that was equal parts error and freedom. He did not know whether burying a body made a story true or merely tidy. He only knew that his life had resumed a measure of its prior momentum: mornings that required him to get up, a job that asked for small attentions, a mother buried and a brother named and put to rest, for now, within the earth.

The sea remained a stranger and a companion. Sometimes weeks would go by and he'd dream of standing on a quay and feeling a hand slip into his. He would open his eyes and the bed was empty and the house was the house of a man who had been where he always would be: in the small world of dishes and rent and mail. In those moments a tenderness would flood him like a tide. He would pick up the key and turn it in his pocket, feeling its smooth relief against callus. It would be absurd to think that the key could unlock what had been lost. Yet sometimes a small object stands in for the whole of a life, like a pebble stands in for the cliff from which it fell. You carry it because the weight is known.

He did not travel back to the stretch of coast where they had found the body. He did not need to. The sea had already kept its own counsel. In time, the town went back to its rhythms: the fish shop sold its fish, the ferry continued its crossings, people continued to build lives on both sides of the strait as if distance and death were not altogether the same. He kept the photograph in his wallet for months, a small, private scandal. In moments of quiet he would take it out and look at the two boys together and imagine them younger and foolish and alive.

Sometimes, in particularly earnest weather, he would set his ear to a shell and try to listen for the sound of Rasmus. The sound was, he realized, mostly what you made of it: waves, wind, the sighing of the house, the rustle of paper. If you held the shell insufficiently close, the image would blur, and you would imagine a thousand strangers had hearts like Rasmus's. If you held it taut and put your body into the listening, you could find the particular—not because the world gives it, but because you make it.

He had come back across that strait at an unheroic hour and signed a name that a man had not appended to a life for many years. In doing so he had placed himself, however reluctantly, as a keeper of a small story. Burial had been a courtesy one man paid to another, a civic action in miniature. He had made it because his mother had wanted it, because the world expects certain things, because you cannot always wait for absolute certainty before doing what's human. He had not forgiven everything. He had not even forgiven himself in total. There remained blunt places in his character where winter sat like an ungenerous guest. But the small mercies—tea prepared in the morning, a neighbor's brief joke—arrived with the steady, if unheralded, patience of the tide.

At night he would sometimes step outside and watch the ferry lights disappear in long, patient lines. The strait was not only a map of loss. It was a geography of return, of crossings completed and of ones still to come. In the end, perhaps, identity is less a fact than a course we choose to set. He had set his course in that office with fluorescent lights and a pen that clicked. Whether Rasmus would have approved was a question that had no polite answer. On some late evening when the sea was a slack skin of glass, he could imagine Rasmus smiling at the mischief of it all, as if death were the last private harbor in which to play tricks. He pictured him, somewhere on that dark, broad road men call disappearing, doing whatever it was he had always done best: choosing movement over staying, weather over roof. And the thought was enough to make him both angry and relieved and, in a small, unsteady way, wholly loving.
