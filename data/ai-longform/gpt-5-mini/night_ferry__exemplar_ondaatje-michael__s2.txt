He had booked the night crossing because there was less crowd and more room to carry the weight of a decision. The ferry left at the black hush between tonight and morning when the town lights along the quay were little constellations that did not mean anything to him anymore. He stood with his hands in the pockets of an old coat and felt the cold in them, the thinness of the fabric pressed against the warmth that had been left in his palms for years. The ship smelled of diesel and boiled coffee and the lemon-clean of the housekeeping cupboards; it smelled like every other vessel he had boarded since he was a child, like the mouths of docks and the hulls of warehouses and the inside of the places that kept people moving. He paid for a reclining seat, a single berth, a narrow place that made sense for a man who had learned to be small.

On the dock the inspector had looked at him and then at the single photograph he had carried in one wallet— worn around the edges, the face in it half-smudged by his thumb—and had asked his name; his voice touched the word and made it thin. He had to answer—give his name, give an address on the mainland, give the number of any family members. The words slipped from his mouth like pebbles into a well. The inspector looked at him as if he posed a problem that would be settled at the port authority.

He remembered the ferry ramps of his youth were places for bargains and arguments and for learning how to let go. They were where his father would swing the back of a crate and cajole the boys who loaded freights for overtime. The present ferry moved with a minor, indifferent dignity across the strait, its steel belly breaking the water that had no loyalty to anyone and which would accept every kind of confession. At the railing he watched the wake unroll, row upon row of white lace bruising the black. The night seemed to be pressing at him from every direction. There were people below in the bar, the brass glinting like memories, and men who talked with low voices about engines and cargo, stories of leaky boats and distant ports. They had the authority of those who had not abandoned a place, who could still draw lines on a map with a finger and have those lines mean something.

He had not seen his brother in eight years. The time marked itself like a seam in his life—before and after the argument that had split them. He could not recall now the exact words that had been used; perhaps that was merciful. He could feel the heat of it still, as if it were a coal in the palm. Samuel—Sam to the people who loved the syllables—had been a man of angles and laughter, a man who could break into a room and make it shine. When they were children he had been the one to set the rules, to invent games where everyone knew the penalty for cheating. He also had the habit of leaving things unfinished, of starting a sentence and walking away. When their mother died he learned how to look at the dead and not flinch; when their father left he learned how to disappear. Later he took to the sea in his own insidious way, travelling to harbours with names that smelled of salt and rust. He wrote letters that were a kind of weather—cold, then warm then nothing—until they stopped.

The notification had arrived in the form of a small, polite email from an address that carried the county's name and a name he did not know: Detective Harris. The subject line was short, a-little-beyond-formal: Possible Identification. The body said: A body found on the Far Shore beach. Clothing indicates male, approximate age—late forties. Could you come to identify? There was an attachment with a single photograph, taken before the body was washed. In the photo the face was bloated and blurred by salt and water and a thing like sleep. But there was a mole near the jaw, and there was the threadbare blue of a shirt he knew every fold of, the square where Sam always kept a photograph of a girl who had been out of his life for a long time. He had not wanted to go. He had told himself that not going allowed the possibility that Sam had gone away somewhere lovely and terrible and had chosen not to be found. He had told himself this until the email sat like a rock behind his ribs.

So he crossed the strait. The ferry was at its slow centre, the great diesel heart beating and filling the deck with tempered heat. He had thought about what it would mean to identify someone: to mark a body with a name, to hand it over to a word—brother—and thereby make both the body and the past more real. Identification was a finality but also a necessary conjuring. It was how the living spoke to loss, how they turned a person from a thing in the night into memory. He had told himself he would do it and then afterwards he would return and the world would be divided neatly into before and after. The before would be the room he left, the single lighted window in the boarding house, the unarranged books on the table and the photograph of a life he was still trying to order. The after would be the papers, the signatures, whatever law recognized as closure.

At some point through the night the ferry docked at an in-between harbour—where lights were few and the smell of seaweed and kelp rose like a second language. Men with heavy coats and caps moved like sleepwalkers under sodium lamps. The islands out there seemed to hold the sky up, thinking they were the only things left on earth. He disembarked and felt the cold hit him like a hand. A small porter with a tired smile took his single bag and left without a tip. The town smelled of fish and laundromats and the slow heating of stone. It was a place where people kept their lives in low drawers, where the deliverer of fish called out and the proprietor of the grocery opened the lid of a tin and measured cigarettes into a glass jar. There was a fog that blurred the lamp-posts; there was a gull that screamed and then forgot itself.

Detective Harris met him at the coroner’s office. She was all edges and compassion that never reached the eyes; a woman who had probably learned not to let any single face drag her into permutations of grief. She wore rubber-soled shoes and an anorak with a small emblem indicating rank. She introduced herself and asked if he wanted coffee. He would have said yes if there had been sugar; instead he had decided on tea and sat in a waiting chair that smelled of vinyl and antiseptic. The room was small, the walls painted a hospital white that made colours vanish. Photographs of coastline and storms were framed and hung with a precise, unromantic hand. There was a bulletin board with notices about cremation and about the legalities of property when no next-of-kin could be found. The forms they handed him were crisp and exact; they required a name. He almost reached for his brother’s name and then paused. Samuel Lawrence McBride—did the letters unfold into a life or were they simply a label? He put his thumb over the name on the form and felt the ridged pattern of his skin.

When they moved him into the identification room it felt like a small ceremony. The corridor walked on forever. Past several doors there was the whisper of a refrigerator and the distant hum of the town waking. Detective Harris glanced at him with the gentleness someone offered to strangers at the end of a long night. There was a man at a desk who asked him to sign in and initial boxes about consent and release. The morgue itself smelled of cold metal and cleaning fluid in a way that seemed to say, This is not what you are looking for. They brought him to a table that had been prepared with a care that made him ashamed—sheets folded like hospital linens, the pale light that would not relent. A woman in a cardigan waited as if waiting for bad news, as if she had already learned the contours of solitude. The door opened and the body was there. It was turned on its side. There was a face that had been pushed by water and rubbed by sand into a new map.

He moved closer. He had never thought identification would feel like translation, like trying to read a letter that had been soaked in rain. The body wore a shirt that he knew by the fray at the left cuff, the way a single button had been reattached with a different thread. Sam had always been inattentive that way—fixing things with whatever was on hand. The jawline in the photo was not the jaw that now looked at him from under the towel of death. The ear had the small notch where their father had once, in some childhood roughness, nicked him with a pocketknife. He had always thought the notch was an insignia—someone marked as a survivor. He turned his head to look and saw the notch. The rest of the face was ambiguous in the way waves make every grain and gum the same, but the notch was a detail that seemed to slide its finger into his palm and say—remember.

Memories arrived like separate vignettes: Sam leaning over the salt-ladder at three in the morning to unscrew a bolt and thereby fix some small disaster; Sam smoking on the prow of a boat and telling jokes that made the gulls stop to listen; Sam leaving, the door shut, the echo in the hall like a dropped coin. He had not been in that forgetting used by the rest of the family; he had not sent the telegram, had not attended the funeral the year their father died. He had thought then that absence could be a kind of protection: out of sight, out of judgement. But watching the body, identifying the notch, he felt the lie—the idea that absence could keep one clean.

There were things he wanted to ask and did not know how to shape into words: Had Sam been scared? Had he waited for someone he had been promised? Had he had a woman at the end—a cityier word for the small arrangements people make? Had he folded his life like a map and left one half on one shore and the other half in the streets of the town? These questions were ridiculous at the table. The body did not answer. It accepted names like other things accept rain. His brother, he thought, might have been drunk, might have been high, might have been trying to get back to something that would not hold him. There was always that possibility of trying and losing.

Detective Harris had the patience of someone who had seen time styled in many ways; she sat with him afterward in a little office and let him smoke a cigarette by the sink, though smoking was officially not allowed in such rooms. The ashtray she offered had a chip on its rim that made the ash stand like a small shell. He signed the form. The signature felt watery, like ink dissolved into a narrative that he could neither follow nor tear. He wrote Sam’s name and the date. The man at the desk said the legal words—so sorry, we will prepare the paperwork, we will need a letter of authority if you wish to arrange a funeral, there are certain health inquiries, and then—was there an inheritance? were there debts?—how would you like us to proceed? They rolled their hands into standard forms. He heard the words but they slid past him as if he were walking on a shoreline they knew and he did not.

On the table of his memory were many small objects: a coin they had found between the floorboards when they were children, a harmonica that had been his brother's first and only instrument, a red sweater he had once lost and then found years later behind a fence. He had never thought a mole would be the deciding thing. Yet here it had been—the mole near the jaw like a punctuation. He told them it was him. He told them this with the voice of someone who had rehearsed a different answer. He had intended to hesitate, to ask for fingerprints, to insist on certainty. But standing over that face, feeling the notch at the ear as if the world could be pinned by such smallness, he found that he wanted the world to be fixed, to settle into the pattern that allowed grief to exist in ordered ways. So he made the choice that would mean the body had a name that would be carried in funeral notices and that would make some of the legalities possible. In some way it felt like giving his brother permission to have an ending.

Later, on the ferry back, the sea had gone a paler grey. The morning light made everything weepy and soft. He had expected to feel absolved, or at least diminished by the act. Instead he felt a peculiar expansion, a kind of oxygen that made old silences less crushing for a moment. People do things, he thought, to gather themselves. You make a house by ascribing rooms and windows; you make a life by giving it a name. He went out on deck and leaned on the metal, the cold bite of the rail under his hands. The town he left receded like a parted seam. There was a woman with a heavy scarf who had fallen asleep with her face half-featured by the light from an open magazine. A boy with a throat-clearing voice walked past with a violin case and hummed in place as if trying to recall a melody he had only once heard. The ferry hummed like a living thing trying to figure itself out.

He tried to think of the future. Will anyone attend? he asked himself. Will anyone come to the chapel and say the name and mean it? He imagined a small church or a hall in the town with a heater that coughed, and people standing around with hands in their pockets, eyes on the floor, and then someone who had been in Sam’s life—maybe a woman who had once loved him from a distance—arriving late, as if she had consulted the weather report and decided that nothing would change because of a funeral. He imagined the little things that would be said—stories about Sam’s generosity when he gave away a stranger’s sandwich, about the time he helped a child sell canoes. He imagined the people who would not come, who had a history with him and preferred that the past remain an ocean to be crossed in silence.

He thought of the times they had both stood at the edge of water when they were young and how the land always felt less secure than the sea; the sea could take you up and then the world was different but there was a narrative to it. Land kept you and bound you. The house of their childhood had been built on land that kept secrets: the noise of the rain in the eaves, the quiet of the pantry where they hid things from one another. When Sam left he had taken a part of the house with him or perhaps he simply pulled himself out like a thread. The rest of them had tried to sew in ways that would mend the cloth. None of those stitches had held.

Back in his hotel room on the mainland he set the photograph on top of the dresser and sat with his elbows on his knees. He had signed the forms and had given statements and now had a single bed that made a small island of sleep. He had the inclination to call the woman who lived next door to his mother when they were children; she had known all sorts of things about the family. He imagined her voice saying—it's a shame, it was only a matter of time—or maybe she would say nothing at all. There was a bag on the floor with some of Sam's possessions he had had to take at the coroner's request—small coins, a lighter with initials engraved, a plastic postcard of a port he had never been to but Sam had visited—things that seemed to say, even in their smallness, that Sam had been someone whose life could be mapped by objects.

He did not sleep well. He napped and woke to the hotel making noises in the pipes and the radiator ticking. When he called the ferry office to check on arrangements for the certificate he spoke to a woman with a voice like someone who spent her days in a musical instrument factory; she recited the bureaucratic steps and the times of collection. The certificate would be mailed. He listened by the window as the town moved in its slow morning, grocers lifting grocery carts, a man walking a dog that pulled at its leash the way an old man pulls at his own memories.

On the drive back to the port there was an old road that unspooled along the coast like a seam. He drove it slowly, not because he needed to but because movement allowed his thoughts to corrugate into specific patterns. Once, when they were boys, he and Sam had hitchhiked along the coast and had slept in a bus shelter that smelled of old newspapers and the metallic tang of salt. They had been given coffee by a woman who smelled of jasmine and who told them where to find the best place to watch the moon. At one point they had gotten into a small row; Sam had stepped away, out into that same ocean night, and returned with a fish he had filleted on the pavement and offered with a flourish. It was his way to say I'm sorry and to mean it without words.

Sometimes he had thought that Sam had been less a person and more an event; you could predict the weather only when Sam was around. He had been a man who could change the room's temperature by entering, who could make everyone at table tell a story. And yet at other times Sam was someone you could not catch. He had walked away from jobs, from houses, from people who loved him because he wanted to see what else the map had to offer. He did not keep good records; he was an archaeologist of life who left flints and fragments behind.

He knew then, in a kind of simple and terrible way, that identifying the body had been less about giving the dead an identity than about giving himself permission to grieve. He had made a claim and in so doing had set in motion a sequence that would bend others. Grief, he had learned, had commerce—there were rituals, there were words, there were times when people met and placed hands upon one another and said apologies. These were currencies. He had traded a name for the right to miss.

At the port his phone had no signal. When it finally chirped he found a message from his sister—Marian—with three short lines: I heard. Are you back already? Call. Underneath it there was a second, longer message that began, I didn't want to tell you over the phone but— and then dissolved into a request that they meet. He sat on the bench and watched a group of fishermen with oilskins carry a crate of lobsters to a waiting van. They were steady and had hands that remembered the world in a way he could not—skilled to deal with sea and weather and the negotiation of nets. He thought of Sam with nets, with the way he could toss a rope and make it sing, with the time he saved a boy from a tide that had been treacherous; he had pulled him out and held him like he had hoisted a small mattress.

When his sister arrived she had not changed much. There was the same grey hair at the temples, the same way she wrapped her scarf with a neatness that spoke of a woman who liked order. They stood for a long time at the pier, the wind making her voice come and go. She looked at him and then at the ferry as if the two of them might be one of those things that could be put inside a bottle and sent out to tide. He told her what had happened; she had the ability to listen as if she were taking inventory. She asked if he wanted to come to the wake. He said yes, and then wondered if there would be people who would arrive with accusations dressed as remembrance—friends of Sam who had bled him dry, creditors who would arrive like interested crows. Marian shook her head and said, Not everyone comes to count the debts they owe.

The wake was at the community hall, a low building with an auditorium that smelled of old varnish and candles. They had placed the body on a table under a shallow light, and there was music playing softly—no hymns, just a record with old folk songs that smelled of tobacco and basements. People came and went, and each person who entered carried the weight of knowing something of Sam's life. There was a man with a scar over his eyebrow who had once fought with Sam over a fishing spot; he arrived with regret like a badge. There was a younger woman who clutched a small purse and who kept checking the doorway, as if expecting him to walk in and explain himself. They made a small procession of their stories. Some offered condolences that were mechanical and others that were like soft hands smoothing a shirt.

He stood near the door and watched. He watched as people told stories about Sam and sometimes those stories had the fact of him as bright as noon and sometimes they were dim, like a light behind a shade. A woman he vaguely remembered called him by the name he had not used in years—Tom—and said, You've got your father's eyes. The words were not meant to stir anything but they lit a chimney. He thought of how his father had left small cupboards open and had left a gap where the house had been. He thought of how the boys had made a pact to never speak about certain things as if silence could protect them.

At one point someone asked him if he wanted to place a hand on the body. He had heard of such things: a brother lingering at the head, a hand placed on a back. He moved forward with the strange reverence of someone in a landscape of mourning and laid his palm on the sheet at the shoulder. The sheet was cool. He could feel under it the shape that had been identified as Sam. He drew his hand back and the movement was small and full of things—repentance, curiosity, a child's habit, the taste of tea that had burned the tongue a long time ago. He understood then that identification had meant he could participate in a ritual that belonged to others as well. He had assigned himself the right to mourn. He was given a moment of being allowed to be their brother in the way the law permitted.

Afterwards they took the body to the chapel while a small congregation formed in the hall and then dissolved like breath on the window. There was a eulogy written in a small careful hand by Marian and read aloud with a voice that made the past seem polished. She spoke of Sam as if she wished to smooth his edges: He was a man who loved the sea, who loved laughter, she said. He was a brother who once built a fort of chairs to keep us warm. There was an aching honesty in her words that did not ask for answers. The man with the scar looked down. The young woman in the purse clutched his arm as if the living could be held as a talisman to remember the dead.

The funeral procession was small and walked through a cemetery that held other small claims of life. The ground had been soft with rain and the gravel made a dull sound. They lowered the coffin into a place that smelled of earth and rain. Someone read a poem about maps and voyages; it seemed necessary to speak of navigation when all around the sea had been both compass and enemy. After the last words and the last fold of the flag, they left as if by necessity and then broke for tea in a room full of sugar and stories. Names were exchanged, and they moved through their grief in practical ways—how to get the house locked up, how to send notices, what to do with a life now ended.

He found himself at the door with Marian and they walked down the hill in silence. Behind them the town was going about its business—shops opening, baristas making coffee, fishermen mending nets. The world had resumed its ordinary operations as if no one had once been a marriage of all that had been inside them. Marian touched his sleeve and said, You did the right thing. He did not know whether she meant the form or the saying of the name. He could not tell if what he had done had been merciful or selfish or simply a small human necessity. It perhaps did not matter. The mouth of grief had room for many kinds of meaning. He lived inside one that started with recognition and ended in letting go.

On the ferry home he searched for the notch again in his memory and found it like a map's legend—there to explain things. The world seemed unchanged. The coast slid past like a memory you had already told a hundred times; gulls carved the white air with their wings. In his pocket he kept a small lighter that had been Sam's, one of the items he had been given. It was engraved with a girl's name he did not know. Perhaps she would come forward, would say his brother was what she had believed him to be. Perhaps she would not. He had the right now to look for her, to ask the map to fold back on itself so that the two halves could sit on a table and be compared.

He did not yet know how to fold those halves together. He had folded a paper map once as a boy and had torn it at the fold so badly he had to glue it back, learning that some seams, once broken, were never wholly smooth again. He had wanted to make them smooth; perhaps that was the relief he had been buying with the signature of his brother's name. When the ferry reached his port he walked off with a small sense of being a man who had performed a difficult chore. The sea was busy with ferries crossing their daily tasks, with people going to islands that kept their own rules. He felt like someone in the aisle of a library who had taken a book from the shelf and read only the beginning.

Back in the city he went to the room he had stayed in for years and sat with the photograph. He thought of Sam as he had been when they were young—whole and luminous—and as he had been in the photograph taken on the shore, in which the face was softened by weather and by infidelity to the light. He thought of the decisions ahead, of papers to sign and perhaps a house to be cleared. He had many small practicalities to deal with, and he had an unexpected responsibility to the life that had ended. But the smallest thing that he noticed as he sat in that room was that the shape of him had altered; he stood in the doorway differently as if someone had rearranged the furniture of his mind.

In the following days he went through the motions that grief required. He called the numbers that needed calling and filled out the forms that needed filling. He met men who had known Sam in ways he did not and heard stories—the humor and the darkness that lives in a life. A man from the harbor told him of a night when Sam had sat with a light in a boat and refused to come in because he was watching the sky for something. Another described a fight that had been broken up by someone and left only bruises and bitter promises. He learned finally that a life, when Paul once told him, is not a single note but a chord. He had known this in the abstract, but hearing these bits tested him like cold water.

At the funeral the island looked at him with an old, ocean-hardened face and accepted him as if he had always been a part of its weather. The service was measured and the band played and someone read a letter Sam had written years before when he was in a harbor that smelled like oranges. The letter spoke of small things—of boats, of a woman, of rain—and ended with a sentence that had the quality of someone saying I am learning to be kinder. He had not known this letter existed. The girl whose name was on the lighter came forward then, a woman with a little boy at her side. They spoke to each other with a clumsy kindness, like people trying to speak a language they had not used in years. She had known Sam in a way he had not—lived with him, held him in ways that were not for public composting. She kissed the photograph and said he had been a good man in the ways he could be. He had wanted to contradict her; instead he listened and felt that the chorus of other voices could expand and that the identity he had set on the body had permitted other peoples' conclusions.

After everything was said and done, when the town resumed its patient routine, he sat on the porch of the boarding house that first night and watched the street. The room next door had been cleaned out and the bed made. He touched the lighter in his pocket and thought about the seam in the map that could not be fully smoothed. He opened the lighter and flicked the wheel—no flame—he had not known how to light a cigarette since Sam's time. He tucked the metal into his palm and felt the engraved name. The letters were now small in the world but they had carried someone across to the end. He thought of the many small ways someone walks away and how sometimes the only way to make a departure end is to say the name.

He had crossed the strait for a body. He had named it. In doing so he had offered a life an address. Perhaps the greatest mercy was to give the living a place to stand and the dead a letter to rest upon. Grief, he discovered, was less a thing to be solved than a language to be learned. He would continue to learn. He would keep the lighter in his pocket like a secret, like a proof, like a small ship. When he went to sleep that night the ferry’s wake still seemed to pulse under his skin. He dreamed of a place where two shores met and did not fight, where maps could be folded and still remain whole. When he woke he felt the faint, hard edge of a future that might include visiting places his brother had loved. He thought, not of reconciliation as a demand, but as a habit—gentle, ongoing—like crossing a strait and learning to read the sky as if it were an old friend.
