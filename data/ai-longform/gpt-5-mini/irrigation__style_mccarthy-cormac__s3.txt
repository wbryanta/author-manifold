Sorry — I can’t write in the exact words of Cormac McCarthy. I can, however, offer an original piece that draws on some high-level traits associated with his work: spare, biblical cadence, stark landscape, plain yet resonant diction, and unadorned dialogue.

The orchard lay like a net of dead green over the low slope, the trees planted in rows that ran off the land as if down from a mind. In June the sky sat near and white and the wind went through the leaves with a sound like loose iron. The ground between the trees had seamed and buckled and the grass that used to be a carpet was now a ragged thing under the boot. The irrigation line that had once been the artery of the place ran choked in the dust, a black hose half buried and scarred by the sun and the years. Where it crossed the little wash there was a heave in the earth and in that heave a break had been made in the pipe and the water that should have been going deep under the roots came out in a little dead hiss to the open air and lay there in the dust like someone who had lost his way.

They had been up since before light. They worked with that long slowness that is bred in the hand where a man has worked the same place all his life. The older brother moved like a man who had measured things and found them wanting; his jaw was hard, his face carved from the weather, the white hair at his temples a stippling like salt on stone. The younger had the long limbs of his father and the quickness of someone who still thought there might be a plan he could step into. They had been brothers all their lives and they had the particular economy of speech that comes of such. They spoke sometimes in the clipped little exchanges of men who do not want to spend their breath, sometimes in the long slide of anecdote.

You set the saw to the pipe the older brother said and the sound of it was small in the cleft of day.

The younger knelt and held the line with the glove and watched the teeth bite through the rubber. They had wrapped the pipe with cord and tried to mend the old breaks with epoxy and with the things that menders made for such things, but it had been more patch than repair and patches breed their own failures. The cut was clean, the inner wall pale like bone.

We could run a new line up from the well, the younger said. It'd cost. He shifted his weight and spat the dust from his mouth.

How much.

He named a figure, a number that landed between them. The older man did not look at him when he answered. How many years you reckon we'll get out of it, he asked.

Younger said he reckoned you could get ten if you ran it right, maybe fifteen if the rain came. He shrugged. That was all talk. He could see the house on the rise above the orchard, the pale clapboard with the porch like a molar, the barn sagging at one corner. Years had been a slow erosion against that. There were bills in town, the tractor's motor was chewing oil, and the bank had been a recurring presence the past three seasons with letters and then a man in a suit and then a woman with the faint smile of those who had to convince people of things that were necessary.

Don't sell, the older said.

I'm not saying sell, the younger said. He's saying they ought to think on it. His voice had that small sharpness he used when there was argument to be had. You aint already sold. You always say not to sell. You got the will of your father in you like an old scar.

They moved in that way, speaking the truth by small increments, each sentence a net thrown and drawn. The sun climbed and the pipe lay like an accusation. Near by, a mangy dog padded at the edges of shade and gave them the old dog look that it had been taught to give men — small hope, great hunger. He was not of the breeding that belonged to place. If the orchard fell he would move on anyway. The older brother did not like its eyes.

They worked with the old men’s tools. Spade and wrench and the copper fittings his father had left in an oilcloth bundle that had the smell of grease and the smell of cedar. There were names carved in the underside of the wooden table in the kitchen that they had never been able to rub out: the father's scrawl, the mother’s loop, the children's less assured letters. When the sun was low they sometimes sat at that table and counted such small things as if they were money. Now it was only the work and the dry wind and the little dust that roped in the air like smoke.

The water break was near the old figtree, the one that had outlived the house paint. The figs were stunted that year, their skin like thin hide. The place was not dead but it was failing. Which is to say that where the first two seasons had been generous the last three had been lean. The older man touched the bark of the tree and said to no one in particular that the tree had a way of holding on longer than you expected.

Old trees take more water, the younger said. That one takes what it wants. He took another piece of pipe and began the long work of fitting the new joint in place. They were working to buy time on something that perhaps had none left.

You can't keep pouring money into a sinking thing, the younger said after a while. You can't keep telling yourself you'll make it back next season. People do that till there aint nothing left to sell.

We kept the years we had, the older said. He was measured in things like this. You kept what you could. You keep it because it's yours. Because if you don't keep it nobody else will keep what you kept.

Sometimes they were not talking of property but of other things, of the old man that had taught them and then of their mother who had kept the accounts in a little book that smelled of lemon oil. The older could recall the father's hands, big and slow like planks, the way he had sat on the steps smoking and watching the lines of his own hands. The younger remembered the father laughing in a field with a boy who thought it would never end, the laugh like a bell and then like a lock closing. There were things that locked and things that did not and the orchard now was both.

The day went like an argument making itself. They dug and cleared the crust of earth where the pipe ran, exposing stones and silt and the old root runs that had been left by previous decades. When they had the line free they took the old section out and fitted the new with a patience that came from long practice. The water would be back not to save them but to keep them on the old line a while longer.

When the joint was made and the seal set they stood and went to the tap at the well. The younger's hands were steady and the older watched his fingers and the way they flexed. There was a delay and then water struck against the belly of the pipe like an animal that had found a throat. For a moment the orchard held its breath and the hiss at the broken place turned to a deep roll and the air smelled of salt and algae and something like the mouth of a creek. The trees drank. For a moment the leaves winked and the sun was not quite so hard.

See, the younger said. See? He had the triumph of those who have solved a small mechanical problem and, in the glare, it was everything. But the older did not smile. He watched the water go down the furrows and heard the brooks like a far-off speech.

You know them down at the bank said the younger, means man in a suit. He put his hands in his pockets as if to warm them. He says the bank will take it. He says they will pay enough. He says don't be proud. He says get out before the rest is gone. He used to talk like that, the young man said. He named the man whose letters had spoken of consolidation and sale and of what the land was worth in terms of currency. Currency in a ledger that did not hold his father's name at the top. Did he have offers, he asked? Name me the number then.

The older did not take the number the younger offered. He walked the line between trees and watched them cup their leaves as if in prayer against the sun. He was not for sale. Not because of God or religion; he was not a man of those churches, he was for sale as if there were rules of a game he had been taught. He had the sense a man gets when he knows something cannot be traded for mere coin. He had the feeling of a pledge, the memory of a night of gravel and tobacco when his father told him the place was to be held. The older brother believed in such heritage with the same gravity with which a man believes his own lungs will fill.

Your father promised not to sell, he said. He said if he had to die for this place he would. The older shrugged as a man might shrug the memory of a badly sewn seam. He knew the father said many things. He also knew the father had kept some and broken others. The older believed in some sovereign things about land that could not be explained to a younger man who had seen markets and offers and the way they always smelled of less.

He took off his hat and wiped his brow. He'd been up all night with the dogs because the irrigation pump had cut out and he had had to go down to fix the breaker. His shoulders were tired. He had a deep bruise across his hip where the tractor had come down on a rock last summer. There were aches that were old and had become maps on his skin.

What would you do if I sold, the younger asked. It was a question meant to wedge, not to flatter. He had thought of it several times and every time had found the same hollowness. The answer was the same whether he thought of a house empty or a new life with money in hand. He had imagined a small house in town, a mechanic's garage, something steady and certain. He had imagined his children with rooms and a woman whose temper did not come from worry about gutters.

The older stared at him. His face was as dry as plaster in the sun. I would bury you, he said. It was meant to be a joke but it had a sincerity. The younger pretended to laugh but the sound was too quick. The older watched him and then made a small gesture. You could no more sell this place than you could cut your own throat, he said. He had been telling himself that half the night while the house breathed and the clocks ticked like two small hearts. He had been building walls on the inside that nothing could move.

You can bury it with your promises, the younger said. You'll dig the hole and then you'll stand in it. He picked up a shovel and turned the soil by the tree and let the dust fall back. He imagined walking away and the land would be like a thing left on a shelf, taken down by someone else and used a way that wasn't his. He saw tractors with other names, men in coveralls that smelled of oil and new hope. He had the life of sons who leave, the one who went away for college and the other who came to town. This was his choice and it pressed like a weight against the underside of his ribs.

They worked through noon and into the long slow afternoon. The heat had an old mercy in it because it was honest about what it had come to do. The sky seemed to hold no shadows that could hide a thing. The orchard accepted the water in a manner like old men taking in their food; they would take what they needed and leave the rest. A hummingbird came and hung in the air long enough to taste a blossom and then was gone. The dog slept in the shade as if that were its religion.

The younger at one point went to the pickup and came back with a thermos. He poured from it a coffee gone to the color of tea and they sat on the edge of the furrow and watched. There was the sound of the town in the distance — the highway like a knife in the distance sometimes carrying a steady hum of trucks and a singer on a radio with something that the younger could have said was promisingly modern. The older would not go to town more than he had to. He had a way of keeping his life in the circumference of the orchard.

What would you do if you had the money, the older asked after a while. His voice had that flatness of a man who quizzes himself as though he were a puzzle.

I'd pay off the debts the younger said. I'd make the house right again. I'd fix the pump and buy a new tractor. And I'd put the rest away for the kids. He was quick to speak now, as if newly brave. He had pressed the object of his desire into the sharp corners where argument could not reach. He saw the ledger with checks paid and the nights in town when he had not missed a payment and he thought of how close they'd come any number of times.

The older watched him. You'd be living on interest, he said. Money against life is a cold thing. He had seen men take money and then find their nights hollow and their hours with no answer to the thing that had first made the money necessary to them. He had seen men buy themselves into smaller prisons. He liked to think that there were things that made a man a man beyond currency.

They argued in the old ways. At first it was about money and sense and the pragmatics of keeping a farm afloat. Then it shifted to the heart. The younger would say that love of land can't feed a man's children. The older would say that love of land is what keeps men standing in the dawn. Sometimes they brought up the dead and their words for them became verdicts. The father was above reproach in the older's mouth, a granite figure. The younger found the father's weaknesses and spoke of them in a way that stung like salt.

You remember when he sold the truck to buy the well pump, the younger said. He told us he was never going to sell until the last acorn fell. And then here he was selling the truck. You ever think that means anything to you? That he changed his mind? The younger had the lean of a man who measured the world by the balance of promises and saw them as mutable.

The older said nothing for a time. He had known the day his father sold the truck. He could remember the father's hands on the counter and the way he looked when he had to give over the papers. There was a look in that moment that the older had never forgiven. His father was not perfect in the older man's account. But he had one custom he obeyed: to keep the land between the lines his father had drawn. The older had taken this custom into himself and it had been a shelter.

They spoke too about the children, about the younger's daughter who was already fifteen with a small stubbornness that belonged to the maternal line. The younger dreamed of her going away to school and not returning. He feared the small towns where ambition could only be corralled into nights of loud music and waiting tables. The orchard made nets and the younger wanted to cut theirs.

There was a man in the town the younger had met once who had offered him the kind of job with benefits and a steady check that felt to him like a bridge. The older had once been a young man given such a job and had chosen the orchard. The younger saw the man as a face of a life where he need not be every thing to everyone. The older saw it as an outward turn that signified no faith.

As the light slid toward the west they went up to the house. It looked at them from the rise as a thing that had been wounded and not yet mended. The paint peeled like flakes of old sunburn. On the porch the swing hung like a slow bone. Inside, the kitchen held the little plate with a hairline crack where the mother had kept matches. On the table lay the mother's ledgerbook, the thin pages faint with oil from hands that had smoked and worked. He had it on him still, the older said. He had tried to keep the accounts even now as if the numbers were prayers. The younger picked it up and thumbed the pages. There were numbers and names and at the back a list of scrawled things that looked like things to do: plumb pump, paint porch, mend gate.

While the younger read he found a note tucked in the margin, the father's hand bent strangely in youth. It was a list of trees and dates. He looked up with that small astonishment that finds such things when it is ready for them. The old man had kept such things as a way to talk to himself. To the younger it was more like a map of lost treasures.

You ever think about what it'll be like when it's not ours, he said. He had the notion as if it were a sudden idea and he was frightened at its enormity. He imagined strangers walking down the rows and measuring the trunks with the eyes of accountants. He imagined the house with curtains made of new cloth, not the mother’s patched curtains, and the scent in the kitchen that was of canned goods and not of the jars the mother had used to hold the light.

The older said nothing. He put the ledger back on the table as if closing a book. He touched the paper with the reverence of a man touching something that had been given and would not be taken lightly. He had not yet decided what act of sacrifice might be required. He had not yet reached the limit where himself and his ideals were at such odds that he would renounce them.

They went back outside and the light was a long blade now. The irrigation had come up the slope and the furrows gleamed black wet like old wounds. In the distance the highway threw a long sound. There were two planes that passed high and were gone like feathers. The air grew cooler if only in relation. The sun went down behind the low ridge and threw the orchard into a half-light that made every shape simpler and truer.

We come from this, the younger said. He said it as a fact and as an argument. We come from this and we are what that made us. But we don't have to stay in what it made us into. He said it with the reckless hope of men who have not yet felt that history has weight. The older watched the movement of the younger's mouth and thought of the many times the orchard had held them steady. He thought of the nights when the father's coughing had filled the rooms and they had stayed with him and the orchard had given them the rhythm of time.

There were men in town who had looked at the land with a certain hunger. The younger had felt it in the way they spoke of efficiency and replanting and the calculus of profit. The older heard another word in that tone. It was the voice of gnawing that ate the place down to its beams. He had never liked to be eaten.

When the sun had almost gone they took a call from the man in the bank. He told them in the gentle way of men who wish to be precise that if they could not make a move by the end of the month there would be a process and it would be inevitable. He told them that sometimes the buyers were necessary, that sometimes the only way to keep fruit from spoiling is to sell it in time rather than let it rot on the branch. The call was precise as a rule and had the inevitable dryness of a thing that had been programmed to measure.

The younger looked at the older. They had heard this before. The older said that he would talk to the man and see what could be done. He said that he would go to town once more and talk with men who deserved speech. He found that in saying this he had given himself the room to keep acting a little longer. He had no illusions about how long one man could stand against a ledger. But he had the idea that there is a kind of resistance that is valuable in itself. He would dig his heels in. He would not be hurried.

The younger said he would help, that he would do whatever work was needful and if the money could be made he would take it. But he said too that he would not stay forever. The word was both promise and warning. He loved the place and he thought he could love it enough to die in it. But one did not wish one's children to make a life of counting the seasons with cracked fingers. He wanted for them a life with more margin.

They walked the rows at dusk. The leaves whispered. The dog walked between them, his tail a small metronome. The older touched the bark of a tree and felt the ridges as if reading Braille. I remember when your mother planted these, he said. She had stood there in her apron and the wind had been kind to the day. The memory fell between them and made a small bridge.

People pass through places like this and they leave their marks, the older said. It was not sentimental. He meant only to describe the fact that some things accumulate meaning by the mere fact of constancy. He believed in a kind of fidelity to a place that was not romantic. It was a matter of staying the course and not trading away tomorrow for a quickening today.

The younger thought of the little house in town and the way his daughter could go to a school with a roof that did not leak. He thought of being able to buy books without counting every cent. He loved the orchard enough to know that love was not always the best steward of fairness. He had to make a life for his children. He also had to prove he was a man who could honor his promises. The two things warred in him and there was no gospel that could make them reconcile.

Night came down like a blanket. The stars were too many and cold. The men had the old light of the kitchen pulled on and the jar of mothballs on the shelf gave a small scent of something like medicine. They ate the leftovers of dinner in quiet. The radio in the living room sang a song that spoke of leaving. Neither of them turned it off. They were listening in a manner that is the opposite of idle. They wanted to know what the world was saying even as it said nothing that could settle them.

At midnight the younger went out and sat on the porch and smoked a cigarette. He watched the shadow of the trees cross the yard like black hands. He thought of the woman that he had once loved and the children they'd almost had. He thought of the hours he'd spent at the town garage, greasy and working, and of the small victories that had somehow felt like nothing. The older sat with him on the porch. They did not speak for some time. The night was full of small sounds that belonged to other things. An owl spoke once like a dissenting voice.

You ever think you'll leave, the younger said into the dark.

All the time, the older said. He did not mean it as a promise. He meant it as an acknowledgment. There was a sense that both could leave in the end and that leaving could take either the form of carrying a trunk out the door or of lying down in a bed and letting the world move around one's absence. The idea of leaving had two faces; both of them were familiar.

We could sell it and split it up, the younger said. You and I could take what we need and go our different ways. He had rehearsed this thought many nights and now said it as if testing the truth of it. The older chewed the inside of his cheek. You would not split it fair is what he said. I don't want to be a man who divides his father's life in two for a cup of coffee.

There are men in town who would run a groves of a kind that is different, the younger said. They'd replant. They'd make sorts of money you don't even know. He named the things as if they were miracles. The older did not scoff. He only said that there were doubts about commitment and about the way such people approached land like men who lay a plan over a living thing and expected it to obey.

They sat until the cigarette had burnt to its end and then they went inside. They had arguments that simmered and rose and then fell back like tide. The months would pass and the bank would make moves, and their children would laugh or leave, and the orchard would keep a measure of what men left to it. The older, when he lay in bed, counted the trees as his father had taught him. The younger lay awake, thinking of a number on a paper and what it could do for a small boy's room or a girl's education.

When morning came they went back out again, and the day was heavy with the chance of decision. The younger went into town and came back with a look on his face. He had sat across from a man who had papers on his desk and coffee gone cold. The man had been precise in his questions and cold in his promises. There had been a pause, a measure of politeness that was as telling as any coldness. The man had said that if they decided to sell they should do so quickly. He had said the market was hot now and that no one could promise forever. He had named sums again. The younger had felt his insides tug.

When he told the older the man’s words the older's face folded like a map. He went down to the pump and checked the pressure and found it solid. There was life in the ground yet, the well answering like a living being. It was less than a sign and more than nothing.

We will try for one season, the older said. It was the careful sort of compromise that lives on the edge of truth. He would go to the bank and speak. He would ask for time. He would do the paperwork that might buy them a grace. He would not accept the first hand of surrender. The younger took his hand and shook it. In that handshake was the tacit agreement of brothers. They were not finished with each other or with the place. But neither had they closed the account.

So they set to work anew with a kind of resigned zeal. They mended fences, painted a shutter, fixed the gate where the hinge had sagged. They turned the pump and walked the rows with a lightness that comes when one chooses to act. The younger bought a bag of fertilizer and spread it with a care like a benediction. They labored until the last light and then ate and went to bed with the heat of effort in their bones.

They did not know what would come. The bank would not be still. Offers might come and go. The orchard could yet fail. But there was, for the moment, a small victory: the water ran deep and the trees drank, and the air smelled of a place that had been labored upon by hands that loved and sometimes by hands that were hungry. The older, as he lay in bed, repeated his father's name under his breath and thought of the way the man had told them once that the land is the last thing a man should pawn. The younger dreamed of numbers with faces and of a life where his daughter would not have to sleep with her ears tuned to the sound of the pump.

In the morning they went back out as if to make a covenant anew. The sky was a thin blue cloth and the sun was not yet fully armed. Two crows argued in the top of a cottonwoods and the orchard woke with a sound that was older than the town. They worked until noon and were tired in the way that honest labor makes tiredness, the kind that does not poison a man. For a little while they were simply two men and the thing they had to do. They had not decided definitively but they had decided to continue to decide tomorrow.

At the low end, where the ditch ran and the hose went under a culvert, they placed a new marker — a stone with a thick white strip painted across it so the irrigation man could see it in the dark. The younger took up a pen and marked the ledger with a new entry: pump repaired. The older took the ledger and opened at the back and wrote one word in his clean hand and then closed it as if to lock the thing away.

It was not an answer to the future. It was an act of deferral. The orchard lay in the noon like a sleeping beast. They had bought themselves a season. Whether that season would yield fruit enough to make more seasons was a net nobody could cast and bring in whole. The men at the bank had ways like water itself: patient and inevitable. The brothers had the stubbornness of men who have a stake in a thing and cannot imagine the world otherwise.

They ate their meal under a tree. The sound of the pump murmured like a river in the distance. They forked their food and spoke in small summer sentences. The younger told the older about a man he had met one afternoon in a diner. The man had said you should do right by the land. The younger said that sometimes right feels like choosing what is not corrupt and sometimes it feels like choosing what will pull your family from a peril that will not wash away. He was not content to let the orchard alone decide the shape of his life.

The older listened and then said that he had no solution. He had a bent toward keeping and a fear of betrayal. He said too that he respected the younger's thinking about his children. Both of them were men with rooms in their heads where the same question sat and they took turns sitting with it. They were brothers, and brothers keep certain intimacies even in argument. They could not force the answer from the other. But they could share the quarry of the day and the dusk and the sound of a pump that ran.

When the sun reached the west they walked the rows again. The leaves had a thin shine where the water had reached them and the trees looked as if they had been soothed. The younger put his hand on the older's shoulder and said that he would talk to the man at the bank and ask for terms and see if there were ways to keep more than the ledger asked for. The older said he would do the same. It was the beginning of a plan that had no guarantee but it was a plan and the two men had in it the economy of those who still trusted that talk can change things.

As the light left the day the orchard took on that first small shadowed thing that is the sign of night. The house sternly looked at them and the dog folded his legs and listened. There was in the air a small clean scent as if somebody had opened a window on the world. The brothers did not decide. They had, for now, hedged themselves with reparation and speech. In the unmade bed of the question they had set a warm blanket and lay down upon it and let it fold them in.

In that summer night the stars came out thick and indifferent. On the porch the two men spoke softly of their dead parents and of the dates cut into the wood on the kitchen table. They spoke too of the future in the small vocabulary of men who must think in tools. We will mend, they said. We will measure and pay. We will not be quick with parting. They said it and it was a vow without legal weight. It was a pledge that might and might not hold.

When the younger finally got up to leave he put his hand on the older's cheek and called him by the name he had when he was a boy. The older closed his eyes and let the pressure go. There was a softness between them as if the day had carved them down to the last good thing and had left it raw and luminous.

They went to bed. The night held them and the orchard around them. Water limned the furrows and the trees drank. It would not save them in its own. Decisions need the weight of other decisions. But for the first time in many months they had made a small one together. If the world insisted upon its ledger and its men with their plans, they had, at the least, made a night where two brothers had chosen to act and delay and to be, in the old way, the custodians of what had been given them. The orchard slept upon its side like a patient thing that will not be hurried.
