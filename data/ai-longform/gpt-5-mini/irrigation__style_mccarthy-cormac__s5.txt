They worked with the sun high and hard and a wind that came soft and hot through the canopies and carried the smell of apricot and old wood and the dry underfruition of the soil. The rows lay like beds of a resting beast with ribs showing and the trees stood there in their summer leaf as if waiting for some hand that would not come. The irrigation line ran like a vein along the furrow between two rows, a black hose laid when the trees were young and headstrong, and now ragged at the joints with tape and zip ties and a hundred small betrayals. The pump in the shed coughed and went quiet while the brothers bent and worked by muscle and knowledge.

They had been boys when their father had taught them how to splice pipe. The father had had the look of a man who meant to be believed and who believed that the land would answer if you gave it the right thing at the right hour; he taught them to hold the pipe steady when you cut because it would slip like a snake, to sand the inner lip until it bled a fine powder and to fit the coupling like a second skin and then to drive the plastic fittings home so the water would not find a way out. He was dead now and the orchard had the soft stoop of a thing that had been well used. The brothers put their knowledge to work like men who must keep their word to somebody who is no longer there.

Heavier things complain when you wield them. The older brother, James, had the measure of a man who had been much in the sun and the rain and was not easily surprised by the docility of wood or the way metal would take a set after being long heated. He wore a shirt that had been white once and a cap that had the name of a town they had not seen in years. The younger, Henry, had the same face but a little less like flint and a little more like the orchard itself, a hand worn thin and a detail in the way he moved that had the look of a man not yet willing to be finished.

When the sun was at its height the heated air shimmered and the sound of the orchard was a susurration, the humming of bees and the single unending note of the irrigation pump like a far-off animal. Their talk went like the work — slow and exact at first, a sifting of needs and measurements and what the part would cost if it was needed and where to find it. They spoke with hands and with the sound of two men who know the old words of tool and trade.

We can splice it, James said, and the words had the prosaic closure of a man who measures problems by what he can fix with his hands. He knelt and put his face close to the break and eyed the coupling as if it were a wound.

You splice that and it will leak again, Henry said. There are places you don't want to keep doing the same thing.

They did not use quotation marks. They had long since learned to speak like men who had served at the same table and had eaten the same losses. Henry pushed back a curl of damp hair with the back of his wrist and pulled the blue tarp over the tools. His hands were stained with grease and the cut where he had nicked himself last spring had left a pale furrow.

We've got to decide, James said. Speech comes sometimes like a small stone dropped into water and makes its circle. He had the ledger in his head and the numbers and the calls he'd taken that week from people who were not men but solicitations. The orchard had carried mortgages like an animal carried flies. Taxes come like tide and creditors come like men passing in the sun and the farm rusts where no one looks.

Decide about what, Henry said.

Sell it, James said. Not tonight. Not in anger. I'm saying we put it out and see what comes of it. He finished with a sentence like a nail driven home. He could imagine a number and sometimes he let the number be a thing that opened like a window. Sell it, buy something that won't eat you with another mouth. Move on. He might have meant life without the constant tally.

Henry's hands paused. For a time he merely stacked away the broken coupling pieces in their box as if it were a ritual. The orchard is the last thing that remembers us as the boys we were, he said. His voice did not pity himself. There was no pedantry in it. He only stated the fact as you would between men about the price of wood or the weather.

That is not an argument, James said. He set his palm on the lip of the hose and felt the heat. This place costs more every year. The trees are going. You can see it in the bark. Look at the roots. One good summer and you get a harvest. One bad one and it's a year's wage down the drain. He put the ledger between his words like a flat stone.

He lit a cigarette with a hand that had been trained by seasons to be steady. The smoke went up and the orchard's sound took it in. Henry had been in town for the last eight years. He had come back for the season because their mother had been sick and then she had been dead and the house had been left to stand and to hold the things she had touched. In the mornings Henry would stand in the kitchen and look at the pans and the chairs like one expects a thing to tell the truth of itself. He loved the smell the orchards made when the heat pushed out the cool and every leaf seemed to give up a small breath.

It's failing, James went on, as if to make sure they were speaking the same language. I put out the numbers. The agent who came last month gave me his cards and his good will and he had in his hand that knowledge of what people do to land. He had a way of speaking about bulldozers like a poem. He told me the neighborhood's changing. They want houses. They can rake the soil and find the water and they're swimming in wealth they didn't make. We could sell. We could be done. We could have a place where the rain isn't a thing to reckon with and the word mortgage is only a word you read in the paper.

Henry watched the line and the trees. He had the look of a man who has decided something before he speaks it aloud. You got the ledger, he said, you got the numbers. But you don't got the light the way he had it lived with him. You don't got the house in the morning.

James smiled and it was as dry as the peeled bark of a dead branch. The house is a thing you could move through once or twice and be done with, he said. Women die in houses and men die in sheds and trees fall. I thought we'd be beyond clinging to our things the way small boys cling to rocks. He pushed a stone with his boot. Things are burdens. They cost to keep and they cost to be inside of.

You don't mean that, Henry said. He wiped his forearm across his mouth and left a smudge of soil like a map. They had been boys who had crested the orchard with nets and poles and had known the satisfaction of a crate that rang true with fruit. Sometimes he could still hear the hollow ring of fruit against wood when the sun was low and the world seemed all the size of their hands. It was not only memory. It was a manner of being in the earth, a contract with the ground.

James's mouth set. You think I'm asking to sell because I want to be rid of the sound of boards creaking at night, he said. It would be easier to go. It would be easier to move to some apartment where a man can buy a fresh orange and call it as good as a summer. But I don't think that. I think we can do the math. We can put the money right and we can take care of mother. We can get out before the thing eats us.

Henry looked away down a row where the trees made a dark green wall and a sparer light fell like a promise. Their father had said once, in the shed with the radio on low, that the orchard was more than fruit. He'd said it was a province of the boy who had been him and of the father he had been and the father his sons might be. He had not said sell because he had understood that the orchard was where men tested themselves.

When men argued about the value of ground they often meant more than soil. They meant the continuity of a story. They meant the traces of a life that were not yet a sum. Henry folded the parts of the old pipe together so the sun could strike like a blade and he pressed the coupling into place because the job in hand was to make water find its way to the roots and he believed rightly that the easiest way to get what you wanted was to do the work the world would pay you for.

If we sell you get something right away, he said. He was not sure if he wanted the thing because he wanted it or because he did not want the other thing. If we don't sell what are we waiting for? Another ten years? The last two harvests haven't been good. The trees are old and their cores are soft. A buyer will take what he can and he will know how to beat the last dollars out of us. He looked at his brother as if expecting a reason that was not numbers.

You want to know what I am waiting for, James said. When our father planted that first row he planted it with the measure of a man who had been broken and who needed to bind his children to something. It was not a bargain for the old man to die in. It was a promise. Maybe I'm a fool to buy into that kind of thing. But you can't tell me it doesn't matter. He stopped and his eyes slid to the house where the paint was peeling and the porch rose and fell like an old man's breath.

Henry felt that the word promise had a weight that could not be easily set down. The orchard had been their father's speech after the war, his way of speaking when he could not speak of other things. He had said that land would answer if you were patient and that patience is a sort of hunger. Henry had listened as a boy and learned the cadence of labor. To him the orchard was the place where one could lay out a life and expect one day to be able to see the hand you had left.

He reached for the coupling again and the plastic scraped and the dust came away like old skin. They worked together in the midday heat and their argument rolled like thunder in the distance and up close. The conversation circled and returned like the water they were coaxing. Men will tell you that the land will save you. Men will tell you that the land will take all from you. Truth is often a well you draw from until the bucket breaks.

In the shade of the shed they paused to look at the numbers again. James opened a notebook and the pages were rubbed and stained. He had scrawled figures and the pencil looked like a flight of small dark birds across the white. Henry read under his breath and there was a small jitter of something that could not be read as a number. He pointed at a line and said, That's next year's taxes. It will go up again. They have them on the school board and they keep voting themselves raises.

Then why are you asking, James said. Men are cunning to themselves. He shifted his weight and the dust clung to the creases at his elbow. He could hear himself like a man who was reading a script he had learned without remembering the author. I can do it, he said. I can sell and you can keep the house and we can see what that money does. Or we can keep and watch as the orchard takes us to the poorhouse.

There was the shrilling of a hawk and somewhere a dog barked and the small sounds of a day. Work creates its own peace and also its own arguments. They bent again. Their hands knew their way as a conjuror's hands know the coin. The coupling closed and they wrapped tape to make sure and then the pump was started and water began in the long soft whisper that meant life or the semblance of it. For a moment the water jumped and danced on the surface of the furrow and then slid under the trees and the leaves took a little with the wet.

It was not a great deal of water. It was a serviceable amount, a thing that would make the orchard breathe through this week. Henry watched the lines absorb and the dirt darken like a change of mood. For all the numbers in James's notebook he also watched the runoff and knew how quickly a season could turn.

You don't want me to sell it because you're afraid I'll throw away the past, he said.

I'm not accusing you of throwing anything away, James said. He lighted another cigarette and the smoke lay in the heat like a thin cloud. People sell out of fear as much as need. People also sell because the ground itself is changing and it will not rest inside the old definition. Sometimes there are places where you cannot make a future because the present is a furnace.

The argument kept its own pace, a metronome set to the day. There are people who will buy a place like this and make it pretty for a while and then let it go the same way. There are people who will bring in trees from a nursery that are irrigated and watered and their leaves will shine and their fruit will be perfectly like things in catalogues. But the land will keep your footprint. It will not forget the things you put in it and that can be a comfort and a chain. That's what James meant and he meant it both ways.

Henry thought of the peaches when they were ripe and heavy and came off the branch with the sound of a small coin. He tasted them in the memory as if the mouth could conjure sweetness. He had a habit of smelling the inside of his wrist where the sweat gathered at the bend and he could bring up the smell of fruit and dust and his father's aftershave just like a conjurer. He wanted to ask for time. He wanted to say Let's let the year come up and see what it gives. Maybe it will be good. He wanted to believe that some seasons change without the man's account being settled.

You know how that goes, James said. You know too. He had done the calculus up in his head until numbers looked like carved wood. You wait another year you might lose the chance to sell at a fair price. Or you'll waste the money it takes to keep the thing alive and you'll end in a state where the sale is under full and they take the place with a neutral shrug. He said the words with the dryness of a man who had watched auctions on a tiny television and felt the weight of those voices.

Henry had a dream once that you could walk into a place and set it down and have it be the thing you wanted. He had the image now of the property with houses around it and new people living in it and maybe a strip mall down the road. He hated the idea of the orchard becoming a backdrop for somebody else's pictures. He hated the idea of the house being empty and someone else sitting in his mother's chairs. You can do a thing and be done with it, he said, and his voice was small and terrible. You can sell and be finished. But you can also sell and never be done with the fact of selling. I can't handle the idea of the other men walking their dogs through where I picked my first peach.

There is truth to that, James said. There is truth to the way your life can be measured in the passageways of a place and the way you can feel like you gave it something by living where you did. Men who moved away told me that land is only land. Men who stayed said it was everything. Sometimes I think both are true. The ledger only holds so much truth. There are things you cannot account and after a while you know the balance and you accept it.

I could buy you out, Henry said. The words were a small trap. James looked up. They both knew the numbers. It would be a gamble in the dark and Henry had not been untouched by city salaries and he had a small account that he had kept and enlarged with dribs. He also had a debt he could not mention like a scar. If he bought James out he would be the man who kept the orchard alone and that would be the thing he would have to live with. Would you take such a thing, James asked, and his voice had the wearied kindness of a man who is offered pain to spare another.

Henry shook his head. I don't have the cash. I have a little but not that. And besides I don't want to tie myself to this thing and watch it take everything from me. He did not say that he had also been thinking of the city and girls with hair the color of lamplight and wine made warm and places where the rain did not mean work.

They leaned on shovels and the sun spun its slow wheel above them. An airplane went high and the sound was like a ruler drawn across the day. There are certain arguments between brothers that are not meant to be resolved. They are the way two men keep their ballast. When they began, they engaged with the small facts, the pipe and the coupling and the way the pump would overheat if it ran too long. Later the conversation drifted to the house and the mother and the funeral and the small practicalities such as whether the curtains would be sold and whether the old photographs would be framed and what to do with the truck that sat unused in the lane. Each of those things carried an emotion that made it larger than its size. A curtain is a piece of cloth until you know it covered a bed where someone read a book and paused.

You could go to town, Henry said. Go to a place where you don't have to worry about frost every spring. He was not so much tempted by the town as by the idea of being a fresh person. There is a weight to being one of the men of an orchard. You are judged by your harvest and your vigor and some things that cannot be improved by better gutters.

James nodded. I know. He began to talk about a contractor who had come through last spring and who had a kind of gleam in his eye that came from numbers. They will cut you a price and they will tell you they will plant you the newest trees and cover your losses. He said the words like a warning. They know what they are doing. They know the measure of a man who has lost his patience.

At one point the older brother stooped and put his hand on an old trunk and felt the tree's ringed bark like the ridges of an old hand. He had the thought that trees were not only living things but like repositories of the things that passed through them. To fell one was like changing the chronology of a life. Henry watched him and felt the great smallness of wanting to keep what cannot be kept.

The afternoon slept on into itself and they took a break in the shade where the grass grew low and the sound of a neighbor's tractor came faint across the fields. The men ate dry meat and bread and the coffee tasted like something someone had brought in a thermos. Each man ate like someone who needed to keep the body alive for the argument that had become the day. They were not so different as youth sometimes pretends; the difference between them was a tipping scale of needs and fantasies. James needed certainty and a clean break. Henry needed continuity and time.

If we hold on we can make a plan, Henry said, and the plan was the old one of grafting and new rootstock and taking in a younger man's work. There are things like grants and new irrigation and people who will help you convert. He said the word convert like offering a religious solution as if conversion might save an orchard in the same way it might save a soul.

You can write letters and make a plan, James said. You can apply for money and hope the bureaucracy is kind. But bureaucracy has a face and it is not kind to places that are dying. They will ask you for paperwork and they will want inspections and the answer will be slow. The land will need the money now. There is no waiting for money like manna from heaven. You have to earn it.

They sat for a while and the argument folded into silence where both men heard the beat of insects and the far-away rattle of tires. Once Henry had dreamt of leaving the orchard and writing and of poetry and of a life without the count of crates. He had the shame of the dream because it was not a noble one and because he had let admiration for other ways of being soften his own resolve. The orchard had a cross-section of his younger wants. He was a composite of his father's sweat and the city's bright promises. He had less need to be righteous than he had need to belong somewhere that did not feel like loss.

When the sun began to slide toward the west the work in the furrows took on a different urgency. The valves had to be set to keep the run of water in the right channels. The older brother climbed the ladder to the pump and tightened fittings that had been made threadbare by an erosion of consent. After a while the water ran clean and the trees drank and the clearing between the rows displayed the darkened skin of the soil like an old map. There was a moment between their work and their weariness where the day seemed neither made by them nor by anything else.

Then James said, You could keep it on a contract. You know the men in town. You could sign some papers and let them take care of the big ends. Henry heard the offer and the offer was like a net. The contract had been the way of men in need. You give up the right to fuss and to worry and you get the reprieve. The reprieve has a price.

I don't want someone else to make the decisions, Henry said. He had knew the look of a farm watched by men who kept accounts and whose chief virtue was the size of their comfort. He wanted a say. He wanted to feel the power to give the bark a name. He wanted the right to choose the season of cutting even if cutting would break his heart.

James looked at him and there was pity in the look but it was not the pity of superiority. It was pity like a man gives another who has decided to take a lesser pathway. We may not know what to do, he said. We may not know how to keep the orchard alive. But we will have to make a decision soon. There is no way to let things be without consequence. He had the air of a man who had read the newspapers and felt the future moving like a machine he did not fit in.

They finished their work as the sun dipped closer to the line of oaks that marked the boundary of their father's field. The last of the light made each leaf into a coin of fire. A wind came in and the orchard sighed. They walked the length of the furrow and watched to see if the water took. The soil drank like a face at a long-lost well.

We could leave it, Henry said finally. Not sell. Leave it to stand empty and let the next man who comes and wants it take it if he wants, but don't sell it to men who will make it into something else. He had the logic of a man who would prefer to entomb the place than to have it turned into something unfamiliar. It was a stubbornness rooted in fear.

You can't leave it and expect the taxes to stop, James said. People will take it by law if they get tired of your absence. It doesn't keep. These places are not like heirlooms you can lock in a chest. They weigh on the living the way old debts do.

For a long time they walked in silence and the sky bled toward the western border. When the light went thin and the orchard's colors went to brown and gold, both men sat on the back of the old truck and let the day's work lie in their muscles like an ache. The pump in the shed ticked softly and the soft breath of the trees was a hymn.

What if, Henry said at last, we make a rule for ourselves. We put it on the market, but we put a condition on it. Something that keeps the heart of it for a time. He had not thought it all out but the thought itself contained salt. We ask that the buyer keep a portion, or that they allow us to live here. There are ways people write things into deeds.

James rubbed his chin. He had the wear of a man who had seen a hundred contracts and knew their loopholes. You can demand a covenant, he said. You can ask for open space in the deed. But men who buy do not take covenants as a favor. They will cut them out with lawyers and they will sleep content. And you have to be ready to go to court if they break that covenant. It's a fight.

So, said Henry, let it be a fight. Let it be some form of the old bargain. He looked at the trees and their faces were thinning into axial light. He thought of their mother at the sink with her hands cupped and the way she'd hum to herself when peeling fruit.

He is not the one to decide with a ledger, James said after a while. He smoked his cigarette down to the nub and then dropped it into the dust without bother to stamp it. He had been thinking of the house and of what it would mean to stand by it and count the losses and feel no more blame. He also loved his brother and he knew that you could not make men give up the dreams of their youth without making them into other things.

Let's put it this way, he said. We give it up the least we can. We list it and take offers but we make our terms. We have counsel. We don't sign anything without sitting down with Lazar. He paused because the name had a feel about it like a good boot. Lazar would be the man to read the papers and tell them what was a fair covenant. He has a way with the law.

Henry's shoulders loosened. He had not wanted to say this but once James said it the decision felt like wood set in place. We get Lazar and we write the covenant so that a portion is held for a certain time, he said. We demand a clause that the house remains available to us to be lived in for two years and that there be an easement that keeps the orchard a minimum plot. We won't sell the whole thing in the snap of a man. They can negotiate. We can watch.

James nodded slowly. That's reasonable. The word reasonable in the late day had the weight of a ceasefire. It meant they had not given up but they had not held on to an impossible thing either. It meant they had drawn a line and were standing on both sides of it. There would be lawyers and contracts and small men and long tables. There would be a day when they signed and a day when they didn't. There would be money and the absence of money. Life, they had both discovered, was measured in the same way as irrigation — by tending small things until they make larger ones.

They sat until the light went to the color of old bone and the pump's ticking was a lullaby. In the house, a kettle on the stove warned the evening was coming and somewhere beyond their fields the far road carried the light of cars home. Their mother would have called them in and fed them and made them laugh with some story about the chickens. She was not there to make the small reparations. The world had a way of making small repairs itself and then relinquishing them for the next man to decide.

We shall put the things on the table, James said, as if sealing the day's work. We will ask for the covenant. We will not do anything until the lawyer says. We will not sign in a hurry. He looked at Henry and there was something like truce in the look and also an old hunger.

Henry stood up and stretched. He felt the day's work in his shoulders and the old furies had dwindled into that peace that comes after labor. He thought of the peaches and the smell of their mother's hands. He felt the future like a horizon and in it a possible sale and a possible harvest and a possible house that neither had to keep alone. Maybe, he thought, and the thought was a light, maybe the land will allow them to be two things at once: a place to grieve and a place to trade. He did not have the arrogance to believe that the land would yield to compromise. He knew the orchard to be its own judge.

They turned the pump off and the sound like a held breath disappeared into a silence that was full of other sounds. Fireflies began out in the low field and something about their little bright wings made the world small and intimate. For a while neither spoke. They stacked their tools and cleaned the fittings and laid the tarp over the repaired section as if the tarp itself were a hand.

On the way up to the house they walked between the trees and each placed a hand upon the bark as if to feel the continuity. It was a ritual of men who knew that touch had an argument of its own. The bark was rough and warm and underneath them there was the slow giving of the earth. They imagined the buyer's eyes and their own pockets and the ledger where numbers would be placed like saints.

I can't tell you it won't hurt, James said, and he said it as if to acknowledge that he had the same wound as all men who make weighty choices. Henry nodded. He knew the hurt would come and go and perhaps the same man who sold would laugh some years later in a new place. He also knew a sale could be a winter that never thawed.

They passed the house and the old porchboards sighed. Inside the rooms smelled of their mother like things left in jars. A plate remained on the table with a smear of jam like a memory stitched into cloth. They left it. They had not the heart to move it. They did not need to decide now what to do with the flat things of domestic life. It would be one of the measures of whatever covenant they would make.

That night they ate without hurry and washed their hands and one of them read the ledger aloud by the light of a single bulb. The numbers looked blander in the evening and an inkblot of worry there could be seen until you could not anymore. When they went to bed the roof creaked and the house remembered them. In the dark, Henry rolled to his side and thought of their father who would have liked such a bargain, and he thought that both of them were men who loved places and would do anything short of cruelty to keep them.

Morning will bring a lawyer and a neighbor and the day will open its wounds or its gifts. They had reached a small agreement and its meaning would be tested like any new tool in the field. For now the pipe was mended and the water ran. For now the men had found a way to keep their counsel and both had the sense of having acted with some care for the one thing they had and some care for the other one. There are days when decisions are certain and the orchard will sleep in the knowledge. There are days when a man has to let the ledger and the heart share the table.

They slept and the orchard folded itself around the house. Dawn came and with it the sure small voice of birds. On the porch they drank from tin cups and the light cut the fog into pieces. The world was uncommitted. The men who had argued felt more like compadres than enemies. It is a small consolation to make terms when one knows life will bring more. Still they had made a plan that was neither the end nor the unaltered past. It had in it the likeness of both compromise and fidelity, the two weights a man can put in his pockets when he has to go out into the world.
