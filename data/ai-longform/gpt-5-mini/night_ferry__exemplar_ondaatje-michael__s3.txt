I can’t write in Michael Ondaatje’s exact voice, but I will write an original piece that captures key qualities of his work—lyricism, fragmentary memory, sensory detail, and layered time.

The ferry moved through a throat of water that I knew by the way the hull sighed underfoot, a low animal sound you felt rather than heard, a muscle working through sleep. It was the night crossing, when the lights of the mainland loosened themselves and the islands became a scatter of small lamps and then nothing at all, and the passengers folded into the boat like paper birds finding their balance. I stood where the deck met the rail and the cold kept my jaw raw. From this edge the sea looked quick and private, as if it had stories it was embarrassed to tell in daylight. The air tasted of salt and diesel, and the dark had the consistency of wool.

They had called me in the morning, slow and official: the coroner, or someone speaking with the coroner’s softened voice. “There’s a body,” she said, “a man washed ashore. We have a name that might fit. Come on tonight’s ferry. We need someone to identify him.” I had a photograph of him that I kept folded in my wallet like a town map that had become accustomed to being opened and closed; I smoothed it with my thumb, like a person doing a minor ritual to keep an object warm. The photograph was from a summer that had belonged to no one else—him on a cliff, hair a little longer, grinning with the kind of angle I had learned to mistrust because a grin is the place a face keeps its itineraries. The edges of the photograph had gone soft. On my way to the ferry I had a sense of everything compressing toward that photograph, as though the hours and the toll-booths and the cheap coffee at the roadside had been building themselves into one small paper truth.

I had not seen him in ten years. Some estrangements aren’t made of dramatic slams but of slow withdrawings—doors left slightly ajar and then unlatched, rooms no longer addressed. We had been boys who understood rocks and tides in a way that felt like religion: a taught knowledge of where to stand when the sea seemed eager. He called himself Little at home and then swapped it for names like Tom or Tony depending on who he slept with that month. Names were things he could lay down and pick up again like clothes. I had gone away; not fully, not in the legal sense, but I had folded myself into a life that didn’t require him—work that rewarded steadiness and rooms that accepted my punctuality. He stayed, or at least he kept returning, to that map of small towns and bars and the long, gray coastlines. It is easy to imagine that absence feels like a physical place. It does not. It is more like the habit of not looking where someone might step, the practiced turning away.

On the ferry the passengers were like props from parts of my life I hadn’t visited in a while: fishermen in game hats, two women who carried matching duffel bags and spoke in short sentences, a man asleep in his clothes with his head cocked back and the ferry’s motion holding him like a lullaby. Someone had left a paperback open on a bench; a bookmark—receipt, a theater stub—stuck out the top. Children’s voices filtered from the interior, nothing to do with my geography. I watched the water and thought of hands. My brother had hands that could find a small thing in the dark, pick it up, and make it an instrument by the way he held it. I remembered one salmon season when he had taken me out before dawn, his breath a thin cloud between us, and he had cupped his hands under the hatch of the old boat and said, with a crafty kind of happiness, “This is lighter than air.” I had thought then that he believed he could fit the world into his palms.

The ferry’s engines kept a metronome. Beneath my boots the deck seemed a board drawn tight across a wide bucket of water; occasionally, the boat rose and the metal railing sang like a small bell. At one point I stepped inside for warmth and took a slow cup of coffee from a vending machine. The taste was bland, but it steadied me. I sat near a window and watched the wake—the white trail that always looked too innocent for what it carried away. In the reflected glass my face was paler than I had seen it. Age marked me in little ways: the crinkle at the corner of my eye that used to laugh cleanly, a pause in my voice where a sentence used to tumble out. The photograph in my pocket felt like a currency I’d been hoarding.

When the ferry docked the town was mostly asleep. The port lights made the water a smear of smeared white. I remembered leaving this place once, with everything jammed into the back of a truck, father standing by the gate with two hands in his pockets and an expression on his face like someone who had been told to hold still for a photograph he did not want to take. He had watched us go in a way that made the road feel like a body between. There had been a promise in his silence that we would not have need of one another; he had always been generous with that kind of amputation.

The coroner met me in a narrow office where the calendar hung ten years behind and a radio murmured weather. She wore a cardigan and had the professional gentleness of someone working to convert shock into tasks. “Do you know his habits?” she asked. Her questions were a string of commas, ready to be filled. I said what I could: the names, the missing teeth he once had fixed and then broken again, the habit of rolling his cigarettes to the end with a nail before lighting, the old tattoo on his left forearm he had shown me once in a doorway, a small ship, so small and clumsy it could have been drawn by a child.

She wrote these things down and then she said, carefully, “We’ll bring him in.” There was another room, a place with clinical blue light and a table that looked like a stage where dead things were placed to be watched. The coroner told me to sit and wait and then left with a soft click that might have been the rustle of keeping things orderly.

Rooms that house the dead are constructed with a different architecture; their geometry is angled toward the containment of surprise. The morgue had an air that was at once cold and dry, a temperature designed to keep letting go from getting messy. I sat on a vinyl chair with a back that did not conform to thought and watched my hands. The photograph in my pocket was a living thing now, a small animal thrashing quietly. There was a fan somewhere and the distant rhythm of someone answering a phone. A blue surgical light hummed above a table where a sheet was folded down like a blank. Somewhere in a file a number waited like a small weather event.

When they rolled the stretcher in I felt again the ferry’s rhythm, the same sound moved into the room, only now it was the stride of steel wheels instead of engine chug. The coroner’s assistant—young, with a practiced solemnity—stood by the head and said, “If you’d like to view him, take your time.” He folded back the sheet like a stagehand showing a prop. The first parts of a person you get are not the face but the edges, a suggestion of shoulder, the white of a wrist. The room smelled faintly of chemicals and the kind of soap offices choose that smells like nothing in particular so as not to offend anyone’s memories.

I told myself I wanted to be brave. In the old house we had a ventilator and a smell that returned every winter to the pantry, a smell of things kept for seasons; I could locate bravery in the way we had closed the door on damp apologetically. But here, in the light, a body looked like an object that had been folded and pressed—someone had balanced him now for scrutiny. He might have been younger by years than the man I had last seen; the fact of being dead compacts the face in ways memory doesn’t account for. When the sheet was fully drawn away the first thing that struck me was how his hair lay, how the sea had smoothed it into a pattern that could have been a small map. His mouth was slightly open, and in that gap there was the faint suggestion of a tongue, but no sentence. In the pause between his eyelids I watched about five different versions of my brother live and die in the space of a breath.

My eye found the forearm. There it was: an old tattoo, faded into a gray sketch, a ship with three masts, clumsy in execution like a boy had started a complicated thing and tired halfway through. I reached out before I could stop myself and touched the skin with a finger that trembled. It was cool and had the papery feel of someone who had been wrapped for a long time. The skin gave, slightly, beneath my touch. That tattoo had been a kind of oath he made to himself at seventeen in the bar where the light always sat low and men ordered coffee at five in the morning because they liked the way it stretched time. I hummed a line of memory—his voice, then, thinner and more theatrical. “If I have a mark, I leave you a map,” he would say.

The coroner watched me with careful distance. She could not tell me what I already knew, and I did not yet know if what I knew would be sufficient. There were no obvious scars on the face, no things that were unknown to me like a missing ear or a ear-ring I had gifted. The dental records were unhelpful either to soothe or to confirm—he had a dentist then I did not know. He had always, in his twenties, been good at not being reachable; the notion that he would have kept records and appointments felt foreign. It is sometimes simpler to think that the dead take all their complications with them, and to leave the living to stitch what they can.

I moved closer and allowed my fingers to brush against the wrist where a watch used to sit. He had an old watch, a thin leather strap, a face that had fogged and then been buffed, that he sometimes wound open with a coin in a dark room. That watch was not there. I looked at the hands. The left hand had the stub of a finger where, in one childhood accident, he had nearly lost the tip on a door jam. The scar was modern now, knobby with a healed ridge. I had thought I was certain about that; for a moment I was sure. Then the city started to trade in doubt again with every breath.

Grief is a kind of arithmetic. You count back years and try to reconcile them with what remains. I thought of the day he left us for good, when the house was full of the kind of silence that happens when everyone is pretending nothing needs saying. My father had stood in the doorway and made a small noise somewhere between a cough and a laugh, and my brother had taken his boots off and set them beside the stove like someone placing a small and certain object into a niche reserved for habit. Then he had walked out with a guitar and an eight-dollar pack of cigarettes and a face that tried too hard to seem free. He would send a postcard now and again, a sentence in an almost unrecognizable hand; once a postcard from a town that had an impossible name and a photo of a bar with a neon palm tree. The postcards smelled of travel and cheap ink. I would hang these like talismans and then fold them into drawers when I began to be busy.

“You can look at him more if you need,” the coroner said. Her voice came with a clinical patience as if she were showing a private collection to someone who had an intense but private interest. There were no tears from me then—not from the depths I had expected; instead there was a kind of practical thing building in my chest: the task of deciding whether this face was my brother’s. I studied the ears, those small topographic features that rarely change. I studied the hands again, the knuckles, the way the carves of veins made a map as familiar as the hand of a clock I had used to set a timezone in my head. The knuckle above the index finger had once been split and scarred from a fight over a girl named Mara, who later married a man who sold boats. The knuckle was present. The mouth, too, had a small split scar at the corner, a souvenir from some bar room diplomacy.

Recognition is an odd currency; sometimes you have it, sometimes you buy it. I had a photograph in my pocket and a handful of memories that arranged themselves like witnesses. I had withheld judgment in my head for most of the day, like someone taking the stance of a person waiting to be consulted. Now, with the ship tattoo and the knuckle and the small scar—the small reconciling things—my chest unclenched. They formed a story line I could write on the inside of my palm and carry like a comfort down the street. I said his name aloud then, soft and tested, and the voice in the room acknowledged it with the kind of silence that accepts and changes the facts.

There was a moment after that where the coroner asked me whether I wished to view more, to confirm details further. I said yes because the action of saying yes created the illusion of control. She left to get forms that smelled like bureaucracy, the odor of a building that believes paperwork can make the world tidy. While she was gone I watched the body and noticed that the hair at the temple had grown thinner; in life I had not liked the way he had trimmed it, how he left little islands of white at the edges that insisted on a salt-spray aesthetic. I could have sworn, at one point, that the mouth made the hint of a smile. Is that projection of the living? Sometimes grief gives you little permissions to see what you want.

The paper had to be signed. They asked me for a birthdate. I supplied dates like a man reading from a prayer book. I gave them the old address, the rough description of the room he had lived in where the curtains never closed properly and the floorboards made a sound like a low complaint. The assistant took my handwriting and said, “We’ll need a photo ID.” I handed over my driver’s license like handing over a small, fainting thing. They took copies of it and set them with quiet efficiency into a folder. The paperwork made the moment legal and also made it ecclesiastical; the signature meant the death could move from being a possible to a fact.

The coroner said gently, “Because you’re estranged, do you want us to give you some time alone?” It was a question that could have been offered to anyone at any time, as if estrangement were simply another form of grief you could measure in hours. I wanted to say that estrangement was not a measurable container of sorrow; it is like a river with hidden bends, sometimes shallow, sometimes deep, sometimes running over the stones that make the bed noisy. I asked instead if it was possible to see his face once more, to confirm this with the architecture of my eyes. She understood what I meant and nodded.

When she left me alone, the room felt like a bell you had the right to ring. I stood and pulled a chair close to the table. My hand hovered like a conspirator. I placed my palm on his chest, over the place where a heart had been, as if the heat of my skin could set a memory or a proof into place. The skin was calm beneath, the fabric of a shirt faint under the sheet. For a long time I just breathed with the regularity of someone who has rehearsed an action to perform on cue. My fingers found the slight ridge of a node he had once talked about in a way that made me smile; he believed in the particularities of anatomy the way a poet believes in feet. The coroner’s earlier diction returned to me: “small, perhaps, but enough.” Enough for what, I asked myself. Enough for a burial, enough for a name to be called, enough to shift the ledger.

When I left the morgue the world was a shape that had been pared down to its essentials. The ferry had that nocturnal foreverness about it and the town was up ahead like a stage that had been waiting. I walked back to the dock with the photograph in my hand now, unfolded. The photograph looked like a portal into a summer that had been precise because of heat. I felt a lightness I didn’t trust. Perhaps confirmation had given me the right to a kind of grief that accepts. Perhaps it had given me the right to start a sentence I had been avoiding. Or perhaps it had simply allowed a practical mind to tick off a box. One does not always get the right story; one gets a story that is serviceable.

The funeral home offered arrangements, a stack of brochures like a small language. He had no money for a cremation he had once joked about—“Make me a bonfire and tell them to think about my songs”—so the practical routes had to be discussed. I sat with the director who had a face smoothed by countless condolences and who treated grief like an inventory to be itemized. He proffered options like someone might insist on colors for a new suit. There was a comfort plan my brother might have liked, a minimal thing with a pine box and a shroud and a handful of people to sing in an echo that would not outlast the day. The director’s voice went in and out of my hearing the way a tide draws and releases.

I called my father from the parking lot outside the funeral home because there was a rule I had not verbalized until I spoke it aloud: some doors should be left unlocked for those who were absent to come back through. My father answered like someone who had been waiting with his hand on the latch for years. “It’s him,” I said, and the way those words met him on the other end was a small thing that carried everything. He made a small sound that was not a full word. I told him where I was and he said he would come, but not quickly. I could hear the wind through the receiver and the quiet of a house that had been furnished with patience.

The days that followed had a pattern like a ship’s log. I organized a small funeral, more because the dead should have gestures toward the rituals of a life than out of a firm belief in rites. People came who had known him by different names; some brought stories like gifts that were both accusation and praise. There was one woman who said they had loved each other badly in a way that made both of them better for being bad. A man from a longshore crew told us about an evening when my brother had given his shoes away to a man who couldn't afford them; he did things like that, the kind you leave the past with and which the past keeps like unpaid debts. We listened and called the stories to be evidence that he had been.

The burial was small. It was raining in the way the northland rains; not like a confession but like an old friend who would not leave. My father stood at the edge of the grave with hands pocketed, his silhouette an answer to a question the rest of us were not comfortable asking. I stood by the edge and felt the weight of what I had decided to do—beyond signatures and the small acts of legality—because moments after the coroner’s assistant had taken my name and written it on a file I had felt the subtle tilt of obligation shift over me like a scarf. I had liked, and still liked, in small and private ways, to believe that I had the power to close chapters. It is a kindness to the living to offer the dead a shape.

At the graveside the priest did not ask too many plot questions. He had a voice that could be low enough to be a conversation between two old men and thus appropriate for a man like my brother. The wooden box was lowered and the rain became something that polished things. I could not decide whether the ceremony opened me towards an emotion that felt pure, or whether it played upon shreds of guilt like a musician tuning an instrument he could not quite master. I thought then of the ferry, of the way the night crossing had suspended me, of the sound underfoot, of how the sea remembers things you have tried to bury in drawers and may lay them out again like illicit laundry. My brother had been a man who loved in small, terrible ways and seemed always surprised at what he had taken that he did not mean to. The sea had what felt like a grievance with him.

After the burial we went to the small hall for coffee and talk. People arranged themselves like shorebirds—some staying rigid and watchful, others tripping among the crumbs of conversation. I sat in a corner next to a radiator and watched faces that knew him by either loyalty or loss. My father arrived late, rain-slimed, hair plastered down. He stood for a moment by the door, looked at the people, breathed and then sat without speaking. Later he took my hand as if he wanted to measure what remained of a life by how strong the fingers had become. He did not speak of the past or of the reasons that had driven us apart; he simply said, “You did the right thing,” as if rightness could be determined by a single act and not the long ledger of families.

There was, over the weeks, the slow work of eyes on the will, the closing of the small accounts, the calling of the people who might care to know. I found faces in my mind that I had not seen in years. Some of them came forward to tell me small things I had not known—how my brother had once carved a name into the back of a bench so he could remember where he had sat when he decided to leave town, how he had kept a collection of ticket stubs in a jar, each one a small shrine. One neighbor brought over a box of his things—small books with pages underlined, pockets with coins, a lighter I had given him once that had an inscription I half-remembered. It was the little things that constructed a man into an heirloom.

At night sometimes I woke and went to the window that looked to where the ferry crossed. The sea still moved with the old animal sound. The town was mapped now like a memory someone had glossed: the dock, the slop of the tides, the local diners where he had eaten his breakfasts raw and loud. There were places you could go where the past had been scraped down to the bare bones and seemed to glow in that vein, and there were places you could not go without risking a memory that would return like a stubborn tide.

In the end I could not say whether the certainty of his identity had softened a guilt or sharpened a loss. It produced a kind of reconciliation that was practical and quiet: telling stories about him felt like putting him in different rooms of the house, giving him furniture and clothes that suited. I could still, in private, see him at seventeen arguing with a radio like it had insulted his mother, his face lit by the idea of future trouble. I would miss him as men miss small songs, as people misplace their keys and then find them in a place that felt both obvious and miraculous.

Perhaps the thing that stood out the most in the months after was how the sea kept reclaiming its share. The town by the strait had many names for weather; its people could watch the horizon and tell you the mood of a month by the slow swell. I had always thought of myself as a man who had left to find steadiness; in returning, and by finally participating in that final act for my brother, I had moved into a range of belonging I had not expected. Sometimes belonging is not about geography, or even kinship, but about being present at the thing that declares a life finished and beginning to face the ledger. It felt like a debt paid, though what counts as payment is always disputable.

The ferry continued to run its nights. Some evenings I would go to the dock and stand, not to cross but to stand, like a person at a grave. The hull sighed, the light washed something pale against the water, and there were men I recognized waiting with nets, their hands and faces the color of fish scales. At night the small town slipped into dreams and the boats bobbed like questions.

There are things I should confess, though they have no neat syntactical place to go. There are elements of our story—of poverty, arguments, a girl he loved who left and did not return, and a stack of uncashed paycheques—that cannot be made tidy. Our lives had been arranged like a patchwork quilt, one sewn with both care and neglect. I had made my way away from that quilt and then stitched myself into a different piece of fabric. Standing at the edge of my brother’s grave, I had learned something I had avoided: that absence sometimes breeds a regret that is not calamitous but the kind that gathers in small pockets like moisture under roof tiles. The sea will not erase those pockets; sometimes it reveals them like hollow places where driftwood might collect.

On the last evening before I went back across the strait, I walked the little road by the water until the lamp posts were few and the sky beyond the islands was a bruised, slow sunset. I took the photograph from my wallet and folded it, then unfolded it, the way I had the night of the ferry. It was now more than memory; it had become an anchor. The tide moved with the same secretive patience. I could imagine him, on another shore, leaning on the rail with the same ease he had always managed to bring to his shoulders. I could imagine that the sea, which keeps everything it takes and rearranges it into new geographies, had him somewhere in its slow memory banks, making islands out of things he had once called his.

And then the ferry came, a large, steady thing that belonged to no one and everyone. I got on board without the urgency I had felt before. The crossing was the same, the engine a familiar heartbeat; I listened to it like someone who puts an ear to a clock. I watched the mainland grow like a wound closing, the docks taking back their lights. I felt, in my pocket, the photograph and the lighter and the tiny, leftover guilt of having been late to say the things that might have prevented his leaving. It is easy to imagine that things could have been otherwise, that the map of our lives could have been folded in a gentler design. It is harder to sit with the fact that we are what we made ourselves into, and the sea takes what it will.

At the rail I stood and let the wind play at my collar. The ferry yawned and then took its place among the water. There were other passengers whose losses I did not know. The cold made my face sharp and honest. I thought of the small ritual I would perform at home: taking the photograph and placing it on the top of an old shelf where its light could catch. Maybe I would place a coin on it, or a pebble, as if leaving small offerings helps anchor the story. It is ridiculous, this belief in tokens, but tokens are how stories keep themselves when the rooms are closing down.

I am not a man given to proclamations. I did not make any grand resolves about being more present to my father or handing out absolutions to my past. But sometimes there are acts that return to you of their own accord: the making of calls to old friends, the sending of postcards that smell faintly of travel, the small measure of forgiving. That night in the ferry’s small light I let go of a list I had been carrying like an old grocery list, folded and stained: the things I would have insisted on doing for him had he stayed. The page was not blank, only lighter, as if a shift had occurred.

We are our most honest at certain thresholds. The ferry’s rail is one of them: you can feel the boat hold you and the water accept you as it moves, not asking for more than you can give. The sea is a patient witness. I left, and it carried our story into its belly. It was not a neat ending, nor a tidy absolution. But where there had been a sharpness of absence there was now a small room, arranged with the things a man might want when leaving—a photograph, a broken watch, a lighter with an inscription—objects that form a human inventory and which we keep to tell ourselves who we were and who we might yet become.
