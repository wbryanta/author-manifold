The ferry moved like a memory being turned over in the dark — a slow palm sliding across the map of a body it could no longer hold. The diesel breathed in a long sigh and let it out the way an old man clears his throat: uneven, finally admitting the night. I sat with my hands cupped around a paper cup of coffee that had gone lukewarm, listening to the engine and to the small creaks in the metal under my feet, as if each protest might tell me the name of the man I had come to identify. The announcement of my own name would have been less strange than the thought that someone I had not seen in years, someone who had cut the thread of us with one small knife of a decision, lay somewhere a few miles ahead, inert and blue as an onion peeled too far.

They called at dusk. A constable who spoke in the plain bluntness of people who handle other people's ends over their lunch. There had been a body, they said. A fisherman had found it tangled in the ropes near the old light. They did not ask if I would come; they told me which ferry left at ten. He gave the coordinates, the boat's name, the clumsy sequence of numbers that would become a route in my head: the ferry, the strait, the morgue. I told him I would come. I told him I would come because my mother had made me come. She had the stubborn belief that blood keeps its voice even when it is quieted. The voice said each thing in that room with the flatness people keep for their dead: come, identify, sign.

My brother's name is a place in my mouth I have not visited. It is curled like a stamp at the bank's corner of some envelope I have never opened. When we were children, he would write the name of our street in the margins of his schoolbooks and then cross it out, as if names could be experimented on and revised. He could vanish from a room the way a moth disappears into a lamp's heat. He left in pieces. A suitcase, a train ticket, a suitcase with a hole patched with duct tape, a cigarette butt in a book. And a single thing I remember then as the pivot: the argument about the key. For a week it became the shape of us — whose key it was to the coal cellar, whose piece of cloth allowed entry to other rooms. We argued with the neat irrational hatred of brothers who think possession proves love. When he left he took a key as if to hold our house inside his pocket, as if to keep something we had shared from becoming the property of the quiet town where our mother still lived.

Across the deck the ferry held others who had lit their own small lamps against the dark. A woman in a woolen hat read from a paperback; the pages smelled faintly of books that had been kept in someone’s drawer too long. Two men in fluorescent jackets, fishermen, leaned on the rail and pointed at a light some hundred yards away that one of them called "the sleeping eye." A child ran along a corridor and then was gone, leaving only the sound of rubber soles on the metal like the tapping of rain. The wind had the thinness of money in winter; it pushed at us as if we were trying to hold up a sheet between ourselves and a blankness we could not afford to see. I thought of my brother's hands, always quick-moving, making small economies of touch — the way he could steady a jar lid, the way he could find the corner of a page to start a cigarette. I thought now of how those hands would lie, unbothered by ownership, while his pulse slowed to the engine's rhythm.

He had been a habit of my life: accidental and recurrent. He would arrive at odd times with news that made something in my living room unsteadied — money jammed into envelopes with handwriting that tried too hard to be neat, a photograph of himself with a new beard as if to suggest a life that had not included us. The last time we lived under the same roof, he took out a map and traced a line with his finger from our town to the north. He spoke like a man joining dots on a page, intent on forming a constellation out of errands that looked like decisions. When I said he should stay he shrugged as if the word "stay" belonged only to other people's sentences. "I will be back," he said and left in the way men leave their shirts on a chair and later forget them.

I had made peace with forgetting because time has a way of building a wall around what it will not carry forward. But death does not respect walls. It arrives like a letter with a wrong return address. They told me the body was clothed. That helped. Clothing offers biography: the margin of a cuff, a collar's soft fold. The constable had said it was a man of the right age, the right height. They had found a ring. For a moment I let myself imagine jewelry that would be one of ours, inherited perhaps from an uncle no one ever spoke of, or a signet with a name. But the constable used the word "may" as casually as one breathes in and out. May or may not. He said this quietly, as if the worst confirmations were not made of facts but of possibility, the way a house insists on a voice that is no longer there.

I have spent time in a life of measured risk. My work involves accounts and balances, the slow discipline of ledgers. I know the precision of numbers, the way they can be laid out and checked, checked again, and still fail to reflect the small thefts memory performs. The ferry steward kept coming by and asking if I wanted more coffee. I shook my head and told him no and he nodded as if to a ritual he performed on every crossing, as if caring for anonymous, sleep-smitten people while engines sang was a meaning he could live by. It was the kind of ordinary kindness that seemed to gather the ship into a fold of civility: neat, small, enough to steady me.

My mother had wanted the names to be said aloud. "If I cannot go," she said, "then you must." Her voice is made of gravel and honey: the two textures women who have lived for years on the edge of bodies tend to be. She called me in the afternoon and mentioned a story I had not heard before about my brother and a tin soldier they had lost behind the oven. The story was so fine with its precise domestic detail that I was surprised it had not already been folded into my childhood. She spoke at length until the call became a kind of liturgy for return. "You are almost his age," she said, and in that remark there was a small arithmetic: our ages like counters on a board. "You two share a number like you shared a room."

I thought of the morgue as if it were a second skin of the town. I pictured its lights like an anonymous truth. I have spent hours in state rooms before, signing papers, settling an estate, buying time. There are forms that fold lives into their boxes: name, date, place. There are also hands, tremblings, small explanations that come out sideways. In the days that followed the call I found myself making lists: what might prove it his, what would prove it not. A scar behind the shoulder blade where he had fallen off a bike; a missing front tooth; the smell of the cologne he had worn that smelled of oranges and the gutter of cheap whiskey. Memory supplied each detail and then took it away. It behaved like a court witness whose recollection strings itself into the narrative your life most wants.

On the ferry I tried to rehearse my face. I tried to make sure my expression would not betray what I felt in my bones: a mixture of curiosity and something that approached a primitive civility, the obligation to say his name and admit the loss. It struck me that identity is often recognized by the repetition of small habits — how one folds a sleeve, the way one clicks their tongue. My brother had habits like handshakes with the world: he chewed the inside of his lower lip when he was unsure, he kept his cigarettes in an old Altoids tin whose lid peeled at the corners like a man peeling himself away from things.

I thought of a photograph I once saw of him sitting on a dock with a fisherman’s cap, the sea behind him like a promise, the cap at a jaunty angle as if to say he had no time for gravity. He had a way of standing that suggested he had never learned to settle. He seemed to hold his life on the edges of his palms as if everything might slide and be lost at sea. That is what a brother is — someone you knew at the edge of yourself and who has now fallen beyond it.

The crossing took several hours. Night thickened around us and then thinned, the lighter strains of fatigue in passengers' faces. Someone had left a guitar in a corner and a soft picking began as the boat hummed along the water's back. The melody was something I could not anchor, it kept sliding like a memory of a melody. I tried to recall the last time I had seen my brother's face as a whole: at a bus terminal, the harsh light of a streetlamp, his jaw unshaven, his coat too small for him. We had exchanged words about money and then a silence had established its rights. I had watched him go and felt a lightness at the cuffs; I had felt almost as if a window had opened and there was a way I could breathe more clearly. That sense of relief lives in me like a smudge I cannot rub out.

When we neared the northern shore the sea became a low band of iron. The lighthouse was a slow camera shutter that blinked and then drew its circle away. The ferry's wake left a yawning scar and the bow cut into the water like a letter. I could see the town at the edge of the world: lights neat as someone folding shirts in the dark, buildings with weary faces, a line of boats tied like breathless dogs. The constable met me at the quay, mid-age, flanked by a small dog that watched me as if it were cataloguing the reason grief came dressed in a human shape. He had a manner that suggested he had been through this motion before and that there was a standard script. He kept his voice in a half-suppressing tone. "They brought him in about dawn," he said, as if the hour were important. Perhaps it was. Dawn changes colors. It remaps the world.

The town had the kind of shape towns take after the sea has made its slow law: an architecture of repairs. Boards replaced, a door rehinged with a line of nails that were slightly too long; names of boats peeled off outboard engines. People spoke to each other with an economy that never reaches the extremes of cities. The morgue building was part of the hospital: square, concrete, lit with fluorescent tubes. Inside, the air was cold in that particular, clinical way that smells like onions and old linen. The attendant, a woman with a voice of practiced softness, led me through a corridor whose tiles shone in the light. She kept her hands folded in front of her as if to show she had nothing to hold back.

They asked me to sit in a small room. The walls were the same indistinguishable off-white the way hospitals seem to prefer to neutralize any narrative. I waited with a patience that carried the particular numbness of people who anticipate the end of something long gone. The constable left me with a cup of instant coffee warm as an answer. He told me again where the body had been found. "A fisherman saw it," he said. "He went down to the rocks at low tide to check his traps. It was caught in the seaweed, he said. It looked like a harvested thing, cast up." The language had the literalness of those used to counting birds. He showed me photographs. The images were taken on a small phone, a blue-smeared mass among the ropes, the sea folded like cloth. These pictures were abstracted; they admitted nothing. What a sea can do, I thought, is rearrange details so that a face becomes a surface.

When the door opened into the cold room I felt a kind of vertigo. The air had a punctuation: antiseptic, cold, the smell of metal. The man lay on a slab as if he had been requested to sleep in a place that had no bedtime. There was a blanket pulled over him to the chest and his face showed the slow painting of a man at the end of a long argument. The skin had the translucent look of late paper. The hair at his temples had been shaved in a crude way, or perhaps the sea had done it. I moved on light feet toward that quiet architecture. There was a sheet over the lower half of his body with a fold that mimicked a hand. The coroner, a plump man with a face like a softened coin, waited with a pen and a small file of notes. He said the body had no identification. The clothing was a denim jacket, scuffed boots, a scarf twisted with the taste of tobacco. "No wallet," he said, and his voice was the sound of a man hitting the same note twice.

I walked around the slab like someone circling a small island trying to find a path to the middle. I looked for things my memory could claim as truth — the scar on the left knee from the time he tried to climb the church spire, the small crescent-shaped mark under the chin, the way his left ear bent slightly because he once got into a fight with a gate. I found nothing that would be the key. His face was both generic and unbearably specific. It had the particular fold of age someone acquires not by years but by how often the mouth has been used to keep silence. I reached without thinking and touched the back of his hand. It was cool in the way of rooms that keep their details like secrets. His fingers were long and tapered in an economical way. The nails had ridges like the rings of a tree.

My brother had a small burn on his thumb, the souvenir of a piece of youth that could not be explained. It had been picked at in our apartment's kitchen, the two of us standing over a pot with water that had boiled away, our mother calling us to eat. I ran my thumb lightly over the skin of the corpse's and found a ridge like a river I half-remembered. For a moment the room filled with the implausible logic of a child who insists the world is the way it remembers. The coroner asked if I wanted to see more. He was professional in his restraint. "We can do dental," he suggested, "but it will take time." He had a stack of paper forms that suggested an order to things. There would be a legal naming, a box ticked and signed with ink.

What we mean by identification is often an economy of small certainties shoved up to fill a whole. Is a shirt enough? A tattoo? A name on a score of the pocket? I remembered my brother asking me once if I could declare that two people might be the same if they shared enough parts. "What if a man borrows my voice?" he had said. We laughed then, careless, thinking words could be traded like coins. But a body asks to be claimed in ways that a life never did. It asks for a dwindled vocabulary. My mind reached for a ledger and I wanted to make an entry that would make sense: Name: Thomas Albright. Relationship: Brother. Evidence: Burn on left thumb. However, the burn could be a trick. The cold makes hallucinations of kinship the way fog makes bridges appear longer. The coroner's pen tapped on the paper. "You can take your time," he said.

There was a photograph on the slab near the man's head — a small Polaroid someone had slipped there — folded in half, the crease making a valley that separated two skies. It showed a dock with an old rowing boat, two boys in an improvised cape, their hair full of the recklessness of having been warm once. The photograph had been cupped in an envelope that smelled faintly of salt and lavender. I recognized the angle of one boy's face. It was my brother at nine, and he had smeared jam across his chin as if to decorate the moment. I had never seen this picture before. It hit the chest like a fist. Who had given them the photograph? The coroner said he did not know. "Sometimes fishermen find these things in pockets," he said. "Sometimes people carry talismans." The small image was a talisman, a token flung forward like a scattered prayer.

I thought of my mother again, the way she would insist on small reliquaries: a dish of sugar, a comb she never used, a scrap of ribbon. "You must do this," she had said. "You must go and look." For her, seeing a face is a ritual of naming and thereby of acknowledgment. For me it was an act of making an account. I could sign the forms and I could be the final arithmetic: this equals that. I looked at the man on the slab and began to reconstruct our list of contact points. The hand with the burn. The ring that had been found on the shore: it had no inscriptions, but there was a dent like a small moon. He had once worn a ring made from the metal of an old watch. It had been hammered into something that resembled a commitment if a commitment were something you could twist from scraps. I looked to the constable who had found nothing else. He shrugged in that local way. "Not everyone carries the same things," he said.

I thought of the many ways a person slips out of the view of those who love them. He had had a lover once for three weeks, a woman whose hair turned rapidly from sea to salt. They had argued publicly and then stopped calling each other's names. People had a way of being present and then invisible. My brother had become invisible as though by design. People sank into the north as if the sea had an appetite for human details. There is a myth in our town that the water takes only what it needs. We had all learned to keep our small things close.

When I leaned closer to his face something in me wanted to memorize the slope of his nose, the particular blue of the veins under the eye. There was a loneliness to looking that the room tried to codify. There is a mechanical cruelty to a morgue when you are there as kin: it organizes grief as if it were a task. "Sign when you're ready," the coroner said. I turned the photograph in my fingers and traced the fold where two skies met. When I was small we used to fold time in such ways. A paper could hold a summer if you folded it right. To identify someone is to fold them into a story you will continue to tell.

I thought of what it might mean to refuse identification. That would be a different kind of claim: to say no, this is not mine. To refuse would be to keep the distance I had cultivated. I considered leaving without signing the name. It would be a coolness, a last punctuation to the life we had. But two things held me. The first was my mother's voice, alloyed with fear. The second was the photograph. There are images that act as a clasp. They lock two lives together with the small certainty of a shared visual memory. The photograph had the shape of kinship. It was a proof as thin as paper, but proofs can be thin and still hold under pressure.

I signed. My hand trembled very slightly. The stroke of the pen across the dotted line sounded like something simple admitting itself into a greater pattern. The name I wrote felt in my mouth like a stone I had not tasted. The coroner showed me where to sign and his finger left a faint oil mark on the paper. With the signature the world contracted by a small margin, as if some portion that had been held loose had been gathered up. They would take the body for further formalities. They would file the report, send a notification to the registrar, ring the mother if she wished. The constable asked me if I wanted to leave a note for the fisherman who found him. I said yes and wrote a few lines that were mostly thanks and a small attempt at explanation: He was my brother. Thank you.

Outside, the town had the cold neatness of a world wrapped for preservation. I walked to the quay again because the water called me with an old and intimate knowledge. The sky had the dubious gray of compromise. A woman was mending nets with her own hands, and a gull hovered like a question mark. The sea had returned to its normal business of erasing. I watched the tide pull at the ropes of boats. The strait was the color of used chrome.

It is strange to feel both the dullness of an ending and a curiosity about the thing that produced it. I had done what was necessary, I told myself, but in that sentence was an absence — an absence that had the weight of a heavily made bed in a room you no longer inhabit. In the days that followed I found myself revisiting the images of his face as if I might coax from them a detail that would change everything. There were moments when I felt a kinship with someone else in the town who had lost a person and had been given the task of thinking that the act of naming was a kind of love.

Sometimes at night I would find the photograph in my wallet. I would lift it out and look at the two boys with jam on their chins and imagine that the image asked of me something that words could not catch. There was a specificity to the photograph that mocked the ambiguity of the body. Perhaps nothing is so cruel as the fact that some part of someone can exist once more in paper and ink and be more alive than the person you have just seen. Photographs are particular kinds of resurrection. They obey different laws than flesh does.

I spoke to my mother over long, careful calls. She offered a recipe for a soup and an unusual recollection of their father, who had a way of tying his scarf into a knot like a tiny crown. She asked whether I had inquired after the fishermen, whether I had asked the men at the pub who might have seen him. Her curiosity was the pointed, domestic kind that insists on a story in its many contradictory versions. She wanted me to call the woman who had been with him once for three weeks. "She might know more," she said, like someone filing details in a notebook. She tends to believe the world is describable. I try not to reveal too much. To tell is to be shaped by what you assert.

There was a small parcel later, delivered in an envelope the color of cheap parchment. Inside was a cigarette case. It was dented and had initials engraved crudely: T.A. The metal had the smell of lacquered tobacco. There was a small card tucked inside the case, black as if from a funeral. No name. I did not know if it had been found in a pocket or slipped into an envelope by some hand that had read my name in the paper. The case fit into my palm with that peculiar intimacy objects have when they have been bound to someone whose absence is now a field of questions. I carried the case around like a talisman too.

There are moments when you claim something not because you know it is yours, but because the act itself creates a continuity you can inhabit. I had signed a name and in doing so had given the man on the slab the claim of our blood. I told myself this was only lawful. I told myself this was what was required. Yet even as I did so, the question lived on: had I identified him because he was my brother, or because I needed him to be? The mouth keeps its own calculus.

In the months after, the town adopted a new set of rituals to account for the man the sea had returned. They held a small memorial by the quay, a table with scones and tea, a woman made a wreath out of seaweed and white daisies. The fishermen spoke less of the find and more of the weather that had made it possible for the ropes to cough up a man. My mother sent a photograph of a small flame in a window. She wrote a note that said, "We must make a habit of remembering." She sent a small dish for offerings. I kept a place for him at my table for a week, setting down a plate that was not used and then scraping the untouched food into the bin. The act felt like the articulation of a thought that had been hanging by a thread.

Sometimes I dream of the strait. In the dream a man treads water and holds up a paper with my brother's name written on it. I do not know whether to read the paper or to pull him up. The dream is always at the edge of something and when I wake I find my hands empty. I have thought often about the nature of claim. There is a language for those who have been given the task of naming. We speak in clear verbs and make lists. Sometimes I wonder if the coroner and the constable saw in me something different — not a brother so much as someone whose signatures would serve the town's need for order.

There remains the small quotidian residue: the smell of the cigarette case when I open my coat pocket, the way my thumb fits the little burn on his knuckle in memory. There is no drama in it. It is as simple as a key in a pocket that no longer fits any door. My mother asks me if I will attend the burial. What does it mean to be at a burial where the grave may or may not be the one you imagined? We lay people into the ground using names as if names could keep a body in place. I think about the grave on that gray north shore and the way the sea will always be near enough to make the soil giddy.

On the crossing back I sat staring at the line where sea met dark and thought of the long accounting I have kept in my head. I had identified a body as my brother's with the thin evidence of a photograph and a burn and a feeling of responsibility. It was an act at once legal and sentimental. The law gives you a place to stand; sentiment fills the pockets of your coat with trinkets that might be his. I had done what was asked. I had named him and the town had nodded. The engine's slow complaint stitched the night into a seam.

When the ferry reached the slipway and the town's lights divided like coins, I folded the photograph and put it in my breast pocket against my heart. The photograph warmed in the faint heat. My hand rested there as if to press the image into me. I thought of my mother, sitting at home, her hands quiet on her knees, and of the small domestic acts that feel like preserving. I thought of why we insist on naming things. Perhaps because a name makes less of a ruin of a life. Perhaps because a name keeps a moment from being simply a thing that the sea takes and forgets.

At the last, as I stepped off the ferry and the land had that hard unexpected solidity of a life resumed, I did not know whether I had reclaimed a brother or had only folded a claim into the ledger so the town could rest. The difference, if there is one, matters to mothers and to ledgers and to the sea. For me, the smallest truth is that if a man is caught by ropes and given a photograph, if someone signs his name with a hand that once fed him soup, then for a moment a place is made where before there was only absence. The sea keeps its habits. We keep ours. I walked up the quay and into the town where a light burned in a window and, like any man who had been away, I pretended the room would not have changed. The photograph in my pocket was folded a little softer at the crease. I smoothed it with my thumb and the image of two jam-faced boys looked back as if to ask if this, at last, was enough.
