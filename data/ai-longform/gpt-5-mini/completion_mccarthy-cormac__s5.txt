They tore at him with fingers that had been hardened on other people's roofs and at other people's hunger, ripping his sleeve and catching at the cheap cord of his neckcloth as if it were the tether of a god; they spat into his face and into the hollows of his eyes and in the pressing of their breath he seemed to feel the globe of the sun itself—if the sun had a smell—gone down into the foul mouth of the world they carried with them, and they beat him with hands and with slaps that were hollow as ladles and with the stockinged heels of shoes and with stones and with curses that had been taught to them by the seasons of want and were older than their mothers, and he thought as he saw their faces, all those faces like the faces of his own kin when they had first learned that the grain bins were empty, of the way they would look at the backdoor and count with their eyes the slow days and nights, one on top of another until they were in a column long. He tried to cry out and the cry got caught in his throat like a fish on the clipped bone of a hook and would not pass, and then hands were about his hair and twisting and it hurt him horribly, and then they dragged him and held him out like a thing at which they might some way ease the hunger in their mouths by watching the bright red of his palms, and all about him were the prophets' words blowing like white flags and he could hear the prophet's voice, loud as a bell, saying he had called down the dark and that now the cure would come, and the voice turned and pierced him with its explanation, and the word in the prophet's mouth that meant blessing had become a rope.

He remembered, then, as memory can in such places, the look of the prophet's eyes upon him when the rim of the sun went and the world closed, as if the prophet had never seen him; and in that look was a new thing, something that looked like pity but was not pity, and perhaps there was in that absence the worst of lies, a new and quieter treachery in the place where a man's face ought to be. They bound his wrists and set him upright and as they did so his feet smote the dust and he felt, with a sensation of amusement full of dread, that the dust was not the dust of any season he had known but the dust of some older, unreckoned time, the dust of the Bible books that had been left to dry in an attic, and they propped him before the prophet and the prophet spread his hands and the crowd quieted and held the shapes of their faces like shrouds against the dark; and the prophet looked upon him with a face that had once been handsomer, before the sun had left, as though some surgeon had been at the bone of him and hollowed it, and he spoke, and what he said was this: Look upon this man, he said. He who would make of himself a mirror for what we have asked for, let him now be made to answer. He did not look at him with such eyes as a judge might have, or as a man might who forgives, but with the eyes of one who counts coins, and in those eyes the dreamer felt the measure of all his wrongs. They hustled him forward and there was a plank set across two barrels, and a noose not yet tightened, for even in a dream the faces of men will sometimes be humane enough to leave the device only half finished, like a joke withheld at the last.

It was then, at the moment when the noose waited hunched and patient like a beast that will not be angry so long as it is not disturbed, that a hand came down upon the dreamer's shoulder and shook him awake, and he broke with shriek and with the taste of iron at the back of his mouth and the sudden, dear astonishment of living, to find himself in his wife's arms, and her voice, thin with fear but measured as a small rope against the dark, said Hush, she said. Quit hollerin. He had to make himself breathe three times before the air could find the places in his chest that had been crumpled like paper in the pressure of the dream. He lay there with his eyes open to the black, and the bed—if one could call it a bed, that narrow heap of quilts and patched blankets that smelled of river mud and of tobacco smoke—was full about him and his wife and the half-seen suggestion of another weight, the blessed bulk of her belly, and the room, if room it was, was a lean black mouth with a candle stub somewhere across the room that died and lived by fits.

What of it? he said at last, half laughing and half crying, because speech for him always came like rain, the first drops breaking and then a downpour, and telling her the dream would have been like telling rain its own way, and she had the old, fierce, practical ways that would not suffer airy words without ballast. Could you see the prophet? she asked. He tried to smile at that question and failed. Only his teeth showed in a small bar, and she reached and put a hand over his mouth and then away, as if touching him could steal his voice. The room smelled of the stew they had had two nights ago, and of the smoke from the candle stub, and of the cloth of her dress; and there was about her that hard light of women who have been up in the night keeping watch, the same light that watches a child in the fever hours.

I couldn't see, he said. I could feel him, though. He swore softly, the oath thin as thread. He told her, in that small and blunt way that speech will take upon itself in the dark: He promised. He said the sun would come back. He said we'd all be made well. I stepped forward, he said. I told him to take me. Me, I said. Tell me, I begged him. You will be cured, he said, maybe. Maybe. And then the dark did not lift. It went all black. I thought they'd kill me. I thought they'd tear me in pieces.

Her hand stroked his head and in the dark he could feel the hair on her fingers, the callous at the thumb where the scrubbing boards and the knitting needles had made their trades, like a moneyed place. You're in a damned way to be dreaming prophets, she said. Here we are with the water down at the town another day and some fool standing up with a handkerchief on his nose and a voice about the great cleansings, and you dream of being the martyr. She laughed—something like a weed pulled up and wrung out—and it made him laugh too, because laughter is a fine thing to keep your teeth in place at such times as this.

You don't think it means anything, he said. He wanted to be reassured by the simple fact of her unbelief; he wanted some human door to close on this ghost. But there are doors she would not close for him and windows she would not bar. Maybe it does, she said. You better not go down to the square to see, she said. I heard they were talking of making an example of somebody tonight. They had been making examples for so many nights that the phrase had become a thing with teeth. Who? he asked. She shrugged in the dark and her belly knocked his arm; she made a small, fierce sound from her chest. Lots of them, she said. Sometimes it isn't one thing that dooms a man. Sometimes it's the look on his face. I heard they'd picked up that boy with no ears who used to beg at the church and brought him out by the market and they put him with a rope and the prophet told them to let him hang until morning and nobody knows if he slept or died but they left him there as a sign. They need signs, she said. They eat them at night like men eat bread.

He had the sensation, then, of someone else's life opening like a map under his hand; the dream sat upon him with the weight of prophecy, and prophecy in that place was not idle but a marketable thing. He thought of their house—two rooms if that—somewhere near the river where men hauled their nets and where the traders had sometimes come in for a night, and he thought of the baby that was nearly no baby but a hope, and he thought of the want in the town that made fables and made saints overnight, and of the propensity of the poor to find gods who answered in coin. He tried to picture the prophet's face again and found it not a face but a surface: a white-barked tree with a crooked limb in the middle and no leaves—the prophet had no roots, he thought; he was a man that floated like driftwood.

The mattress creaked under their movement as she rose and clapped out the stub of candle and they were in the dark together, and in the dark their hands were not strangers, and they clutched at one another with the small savage satisfactions that hunger will give. I'll go down at first light, he said. No, she said hard, and there was a small crack in her voice like a twig broken under boot. You'll go down if you must, but not past the larder. Not tonight. There's talk they'd meet at the market—they have to have someone to hang when the word of the prophet stops working. The fact was a blunt iron bar in them: men will find an altar out of any loss. Remember that time, she said, you remember when your brother got sick and you ran to the Quack at the edge of the county and he bled him with a forked twig and told you it was the only way? You spent the last of the harvest then. You're not going to that again, are you?

He lay quiet and felt the old tiredness push against his ribs, the part of a man's body that will not refuse its release when the small, terrible responsibilities of the day have been shouldered into the corner. He could hear the river outside, a steady hush that in other times had meant nothing but water and had been a comfort, and in this late weather it meant routes and travel and the endless passing of people who had no other road to go. He could hear the town's life like a jar of small metal things being lifted and tipped somewhere beyond his wall: the rattle of a shop's shutter, the far-off bark of a dog, voices like wind in the eaves. He felt absurdly grateful to be in a house that might not be a house in the morning; gratitude was a small thing that a man can afford as if it were a coin.

And then there was the other sound, like someone moving on the stair outside. For a moment both of them listened and held their breath; then the stair gave its little complaint and the door cracked and the light of the mother-river rose through the crack like something that had been left burning and their neighbor slipped in and sat on their mattress edge without speaking. The neighbor was the bearded one from the other nights by the camp; his beard was a map of grey and mud and the skin beneath it was set like a dried thing, but his eyes were quick and raw, full of the light of watching. He brought with him the smell of smoke and fish, and something metallic, like the far-off smell of the market.

He saw us, the bearded one said without preamble. Who? she said. The prophet, he said. He went to the square to see if it was a true thing the people spoke. He looked like a man who had not slept for two nights, and his words were blunt and quick as a plow. He is making a list, he said. Of? of men, he said. The prophet is keeping a paper and names. He looked at the dreamer with something like pity. You better move, he said. The air in the room slid thin like a skin being stretched. You better be ready.

We will not run, the woman said. That's not what we do. We keep and we stay. She might have said it with the reason of a woman that loved the land, but there was another small thing in her face that told him she would run if the price was death and that she would run with the child at her breast. They spoke little by little in the dark, like two skeins untying. The bearded man had been at the river and had eyes full of its knowledge: there was talk of boats being loaded and leaving; there was talk of the governor's men being sent to other towns; there was talk, too, that a patrol was coming tonight to see whose doors were barred and whose windows were dark, to make an example of the stubborn.

The dreamer tried to make sense of this and found it like trying to pick up a fish with gloves on; he could not grasp it. He had been no speaker of crowds; his past was that of a man who mended other men's nets and did not go to the meetings until his own hunger drove him. Yet the dream had left about him a stench of accusation, and the bearded one's words had landed as if they were pebbles upon a glass roof. He felt that he was being read by faces that had yet to see him for himself.

You should leave, the bearded one said at last, and he spoke as if saying it were a work. There are things moving. There will be other things. There is a boat I can get you on at dawn, he said. He sounded again like a man who counted how many men could be carried on a raft by the river and how much food would last a week. The dreamer thought of leaving—of the wind on his face, the river pulling westward like the mouth of a thought—but he thought also of the woman and the child and of the wooden cradle he had made with his own hands and which he did not believe he had the right to abandon.

And then there was a knock at the door, three soft, authoritative knocks, and all three of them stiffened. The knock was an event; it was an instrument that cut the moment in two. For a while none of them moved and then the bearded one rose and went to the door and the dreamer felt his pulse in his throat like a trapped bird. The door opened and a man stood there in the doorway with a lantern that burned with a steady, dull light, like the eye of a fish; his coat was the coat of a man with authority—stiff under the arm—and he brought with him the smell of other people's houses and of the commerce of law.

Even in this weather authority had a suit, and the man folded himself to make it presentable. Morning, he said. Is the master of the house within? He had the voice of a man who had been made small by his job but who would not confess it. The bearded one returned and answered in a voice that was nothing at all like his when he spoke in the street. He's sick, he said. He's sleeping. The boy can be told he is not up yet. The man outside considered for a moment, his lantern making a pool of light at his boots, and then he laughed softly and made the joke that men make to one another when they patrol others' houses like sheep-footed pigs looking for the quiet ones. Tell him to come to the square in the morning, he said. The prophet wants those who were there last night to register. We must have all present for counting. The man said it as if it were a bookkeeping.

They closed the door and the man outside moved on. He could hear the soles of his boots on the stair, which sounded in the hush like the striking of a bell. The bearded one came back and sat down again and for the first time since he had arrived he was other than brassy. If they ask, he said, tell them you were in bed. If they say more, tell them you were up before the dawn and you had a cough. Keep to the small things. Lie small. He was a man who had learned from his own life that a small lie could be kept whole and could feed a man for a year.

The dreamer sat and was quiet, for he felt as if he had been seen by a far-off eye and as if the seeing were not yet over. He had the knowledge, stubborn as vine, that if they asked him direct questions he would have to begin with the truth, and the truth in him was a thing that liked to come out like a thick wood smoke in the night and fill the open places. He thought of the prophet again and the ease with which men will make up a god when their sewing needles break, and he thought of the truth they would expect from him: that he had been in the square and had lifted his hand and had cried Me when the skin around the sun had been drawn away. He could hear the crowd; he could see again the empty rim, and he thought of how closely men stood to their own kills when they expected to be mended. He thought too of the simple, hard face of his woman, and in his mind a scale tipped one way and then the other.

He slept in the end and woke at first of birds, the swift thin chatter that warns of morning, but the birds here were not many and their song was the song of survivors. The bearded one went before them down to the bank where the rafts lay, and the town was a smear of chimneys and blank faces and in the square there were already people moving with reasons soft and hard, and the prophet's flag had been set like a small point of white upon the stone by the well. He could see, as they came, that the square was crowded; there were men with sticks and women with eyes that held no tears except those that they had been saving for worse weather; there were children with the vacant faces of the penniless. The prophet stood at the head of the well like a headstone, and beside him men in coats that cost more than a year's nett income for many, and their mouths moved and they made a hush like that of a man about to read the last lines of a will.

He kept his gaze down. People knew faces, and faces know betrayals. Perhaps too the prophet had a memory in him like a net, because the prophet's assistants were men who had been trained to pick out the crooked faces like fish from the river. They walked among the crowd with little slips of paper and the prophet's voice, a bell turned up in the night, called names, and there were those who went forward and those who lingered and were then taken by a hand. They led some to the board where the trial by the masses might be held and some to a rope that had been made taut at the edge of the square. The rope hung like a question and sometimes the prophet would shake his head as if in answer.

Who else was there? the dreamer asked himself as they pressed onward, and he saw among the faces men he knew—men that had borrowed of him in better seasons, men who had once danced with him at a wedding and then had not recognized him when the meat had gone—and he felt that they all had turned themselves away from him into the stranger shapes of hunger. He kept his wife's hand in his and she kept his; they walked, arthritic and brave, and the bearded one slipped like a ghost at their backs. They pressed among the sullen faces and at the edge of the square the prophet spoke and his voice carried like the bell of a church but without the comfort that church bells sometimes make.

It was when the prophet called a name that the dreamer felt the iron band close about his chest. The name he heard was one that had been given to him as a child, one that his mother had loved to call when the moon was bright and the soup at the pot simmering: Simon. For a moment the word had no shape to it—it was merely syllables—but then as the prophet intoned it the syllables fell at their feet and took form like seeds in mud. Simon of the river, whose wife has the child. He heard the title sort of folded in like a cheap suit to the name, and then the prophet said: Step forward, Simon. The voice met him like the point of a nail.

He stopped. His wife's hand sank into his like the shadow of a stone. There was laughter somewhere at the back of the crowd; the sound was small and mean. He stepped; his feet went without his consent. He had the impression, now, that life had become bit by bit a theatre in which he had been cast, and for all his old crafts he was simply a prop upon the stage.

When he came before the prophet the prophet looked at him as if it were an old cup and he breathed upon it the breath of a man who tasted a thing and found it bitter. You were seen, he said. You cried Me when the sun went. He did not call him a liar; the prophet did not trouble himself with such small niceties. He said, instead, You were wanting, Simon. We know what a man like you wants. Men like you are thieves of the public joy. You will pay in the place that gives us something for our mournings. He said these things in a voice that had been taught by the schools of intimidation.

The dreamer stood and felt his knees give a little. There was a man in the crowd who had known him as a boy and his mouth took the small, expensive shape of a grin. The bearded one at his shoulder leaned and whispered Do not look at them. Let them do their staging. He said staging like a scoff and his breath smelled of stew and ash. The dreamer tried to answer and the words stuck like the fishbone in the throat.

You have a chance, the prophet told him still, and his voice was clinical. Confess. Speak the lie that will make your death noble. Tell them you are a false seeker and that you would barter the lives of men for coin and the prophet will spare your family. The dreamer opened his mouth and the words came out as if some other man was the speaker. I did it to be seen, he said. I wanted to be lifted. I wanted to be mended, he said. Whether he believed that or whether it was the reflex of a man taught to explain himself to the law he could not tell afterward. The voice in the square took his confession and wrapped it around the noose as if it were meat to be dried.

There was a murmur of satisfaction. The prophet smiled, a small, economical thing like the clipping of a note. His assistants drew near with the paper and the pen and asked him to write his name on the list of those who had lied. He took the pen and his hand shook and when he wrote it his letters, poor as they were, seemed to him to be the spelling of his fate. Sign here, they said, and the noose below densified as if a bellows had been breathed upon it. He looked at his wife then and he saw in her face that particular despair of one who has been asked to trade a thing dear for another dear, and in that look the decision that made him small was made: he refused to sign.

I won't, he said. The word fell with a heavy sweetness like bread. He felt as if he had pushed his fist into the mouth of a dog and had found his hand bitten in return. You would have done better, said the prophet, to spare your family. There are men who will do that for the public good, he said, and he made an example of himself in his own way by looking at the crowd and letting them put upon him their accusation as if it were a crown. The crowd loved the act of accusation and they loved the idea of the scapegoat as one loves the certainty of a weathervane.

They took him then—he could feel the gripping of those cold hands about his arms—and carried him to the board and the rope and put the noose about his neck. The world thinned to a point. He felt the warmth of his wife's hand hit his arm like a final benediction and he thought of how the baby inside her stirred like a small beast in the dark. He remembered his father speaking of debts as if they were rooms in a house and he thought then that debts were indeed rooms with doors that would not open.

And the rope tightened. He had a last clear moment in which he saw the prophet turn his face away, and in that turning—in that mercy of his heart—he understood that the prophet had never been like his mothers who had sewn his shirts and seen him to bed; the prophet saw only what he had to see, and he had seen the man that the crowd required. Then it happened—in the way that such things happen in a dream and in the way that they happen in the public square: the noose did not quite take; it hung slack and the man on the board sagged a little and the crowd, whose appetite had been whetted, howled and pushed and even as they did so there came a pressing from the back of them, a sudden body of men who had their own axes to grind and their own lists of names, and there was a great scramble and some fell and others were trampled, and in the confusion the rope came away and the man fell—not hanged but injured—and the crowd, having tasted bloodless justice, turned rather upon one another, and in that turning the law lost its direction and in the blackness that followed the dreamer woke.

When he waked from the dream the bed smelt of the morning; the blackness had been burned away by the thin light the river had stripped into the room; the woman lay against him and the bearded one snored like a small beast at the foot of the bed. He felt a queer lightness and a heaviness at once, as if some small piece of him had been cut away. He lay thus and watched the dark shapes outside his window, where men were already beginning to move. The dream had lodged itself in him like a bone.

They were to leave, the bearded one said when he came to whisper. The man at the door last night had asked questions and the prophet had gathered a list. There would be trouble tonight. He had arranged for the raft, a little flat thing with a motor stolen from an old mill, and his voice had the metallic quality of a man who had bartered and bartered and had nothing left to give. The woman rose and put her dress on and they packed what little they had in a sack; he made his child's cradle a bundle and the bearded one took it up as if making an offering. The river was open and the fog that rose from it like the breath of beasts made the world a place of low things.

As they walked to the bank the town receded behind them and the river was a black thread and the raft upon it a dot, and in the dot were the shapes of other men with the same hard faces, men who had folded their homes like letters and had put them in their pockets. The river took them west like a hand that pushes. For a while the talk was of no more than practicalities: how much grain to carry, where to find water, and how to keep the fire at night without being seen. But conversation in small groups will always circle back to the same places—who ate what, who killed whom, who lied—and before long they were exchanging the news of the town as if the knowledge were a thing with market value.

At one bend of the river they saw the remains of a bridge that had been destroyed in the winter; men had made a great sport of that bridge in its day and had danced upon its span, and now it served as a playing place for crows. The raft drifted and the men fished their nets out and threw them into the dark water and pulled up small silver things that glinted like the teeth of children and then thawed in their hands. They ate without seasoning and told of improbable things: of wheatfields that had been found with stalks still standing, of a woman up-river who had amassed a store of preserves and who had been burned out because the neighbours had a generosity for destruction, and of a road to the sea that led to a town where the governor's men had fled and where there was talk of a captain who would count men for shipments.

The bearded one told the story of how he had once been a man of some consequence, in a manner of speaking—had known a place where coins had been counted—and that he had learned to survive by the slow art of theft that men call ingenuity. He would lie beside the fire and talk of his past as if it were a cloth held up to the light. He had a face that could be trusted if one did not look too closely; he was a man that had played the part of thief and of saviour with equal facility. The dreamer listened and sometimes laughed, but he had the deep-set sense that their raft was only a stage upon which other tragedies might be performed.

On the third day they came to a place where the water widened and there were more rafts and the river carried on its bosom entire families and carts and cattle and the whole slow exodus of the season. Men traded things like songs at an alehouse: a pot for a blanket, a length of rope for an old map, an old man's story for a child's coin. The river had the habit of putting people together and separating them just as two stones will strike together and then be washed apart. There was a point through there where a man might find work, someone said, if he had the tools; there were towns inland that still kept their machines running and would pay a man in food for his labour. The notion of trade—of going somewhere and being paid for one's labour—was a kind of religion to these folk, the dreamer saw; they said it as if they prayed.

He would wake at night with his hands clenched on the side of the raft and see the ghosts of his dreamers: the prophet, the crowd, the noose—and he would think that a great many people in this late season believed in the same things, and believed them for different reasons. He kept thinking also about the child in his wife's belly and how its presence made the world both more innocent and more terrible. A child is a shrapnel of hope; it will not be comforted easily in such times. He thought, with a sudden and singular clarity, that he had put his name into a book once, many years ago, when a minister had asked the young men of his parish to sign their names for the service, and that he had written his name in a hand that would have pleased no clerk. That old signature had held him like a chain across the years; it reminded him of how a man may be marked by an action and be required afterwards to answer for it.

At the same time the raft bore them west and the banks unrolled like a map of one thin complaint after another. There were fields of dead corn that rustled their bones in the afternoon wind and boys by the shore that could turn any passerby into an object of wanting with a look; there were chemical plants and their chimneys, the ruins of which the men on the raft could point to with a familiarity that would have made a stranger sick. People pointed and named: there goes where the women once wove; there goes where the hospital burned; there goes where the old school had been converted into a loft for horses. They made a small geography of loss.

One morning, three days after the town had become a smear behind them and the fog had been left for others to choke upon, they pulled to the bank at the place a man called Red Hollow. There was a small town there, not yet eaten up by the season, with a row of houses and a general store that still had a bell and a man behind it who kept his accounts with a neatness that made one of them laugh out loud. The bearded one slipped ashore and came back with a note of news: there was a rumor of a gathering at the old mill out beyond the ridge, men with rifles and with a flag, who were saying they would restore order. They said the governor's men had sent word that if one town would show stability then the rest might be made to follow. The dreamer listened and felt again the old sensation: prophecy will always seek a machine to make it work, and the machine of men with rifles is a cunning thing in the mouth of rumor.

They talked about staying awhile at Red Hollow; it might be a place to trade and to rest. The dreamer thought of how the prophet had at his back a power that used language like iron and made men believe that darkness was a moral failing. It occurred to him that in any town a prophet had as much chance of being right as a man with a lantern in a field of thistles. He thought also of the rope and of the way a crowd uses a scapegoat to absolve itself of the need to cure its own ills; the removal of a man is in some places an economical cure.

They landed and went in. Red Hollow smelled of bread. The men washed and mended what they could and the women took their places among the shelves and the babies were calmed. The dreamer watched his wife as she moved among the others and made small talk and bartered for a tin of dried milk and a handful of beans. She was proud when later she held up a small tin as if it were a jewel; there was in her a grace that had nothing to do with their fortunes.

That evening, as the light came down and the men sat by the little fires and traded their stories like cards, a rumour came in—one of those rumours that cannot be checked easily and which, once spoken, has the obstinate life of a rumor. They said there had been a killing on the other side of the river: a man had been accused of being a follower of the prophet and had been taken by the new crowd and hanged in the night, body left to the crows. The man who told it had the voice of a man who liked to hoard such things and who loved the fact that he could cause the fires at the edges of another's heart.

They sat in silence for a time and the fire's glow made the faces of the men like coins. The dreamer felt his back go cold. He kept thinking of the prophet and the rope and the way the public uses such things to teach itself some new virtue. After a while they drifted to other talk—of engines and of a job—a kindness, the give of bread. The bearded one nodded and said in his small way that it was a bad night for such news and that they would be fools to think themselves safe.

The dreamer realized in that hour that you cannot live by staying away from an argument you did not cause. For the first time he thought to the thing that sometimes men tell in taverns about how misfortunes travel and how the stories of one place colonize another. There will be men, he thought, who will not have their appetite stilled by the death of a stranger. They will want the flesh of one they know.

It was these thoughts that ached against his ribs as he lay awake that night and watched the sky above Red Hollow, punctured and cold. He felt the old irresistible pull toward the square and he thought of the prophet's face as a white-barked tree, hollow. He felt as if a net had been thrown and his name was among the list. He did not know why he was so afraid; he only knew that fear had the shape of a noose and that in that shape the world might fit. He slept and woke and the river continued to take them west, and with it took the small illusions of his town, and time, as if in ridicule, made the future no thicker than the paper a man hides his bread in.
