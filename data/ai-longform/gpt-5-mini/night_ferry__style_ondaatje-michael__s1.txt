The ferry moves like an animal that has swallowed time. It eats the dark, the boat’s belly humming with a long, low hunger that presses my bones inward until I can feel the ribs of the hull. Outside, the strait is a cut of black velvet, and everything smaller than the ferry is swallowed: buoys, the small angry wakes of fishing skiffs, the pinpricks from the distant red and green eyes of navigation lights. Inside, the air is warm with the bodies of strangers, their breaths, the small sounds of someone else’s life continued in the tiny world of this crossing.

I came with a packet of cloth in a brown paper bag and a photograph folded twice so the face is protected from the edges of my fingers. The photograph is of a man in his twenties, leaning against a low wall on a mercantile quay, squinting like he had secrets in his eyelids. His hair is long in that photograph. He is laughing in a way that collapses the angle of his jaw into something both cruel and hopeful. That smile has been in my head like a cough for years; it has lived there without audience or meaning. I carry it now like a small map that might lead me to a body.

The phone call came late, in the half-light of a hotel room where I thought I would sleep. A woman from the port authority said the body had been found on the rocks near Old Crow Point. Identification required next of kin. I read the words as if they were a ledger entry, then pieces of my life uncoiled. Names: my brother. I haven't used his name aloud in a long time. Saying it is like scraping a rusty blade across the inside of my mouth. I stumble over the quiet syllables and do not say them to the woman on the phone. I say instead that I will come.

When we were small, the two of us could be counted like a single breath between the house and the sea. We learned the coastline the way you learn the face of a lover—by touch more than sight. We knew which shore broke into small stones and which gave up smooth, undersea glass, the color of broken bottles. We would walk at dusk and mark out secret places: a cove with green dolls tangled in kelp, a collapsed boathouse whose roof became a wing of shadow. He could take me past the barbed-wire fence and coax me into the dark sheds where old fishermen stored nets. There was a punkness in him then, a small improvised religion of mischief—he would take a dead bird and hold it like a relic, grin at the disgust we got from grown-ups. He would disappear for days and come back wrapped in other people’s scarves and stories. He made belonging into an act of theft.

We stopped talking sometime after an argument that was silly and then became bigger because we let the silence catch like a run in a stocking. It is impossible to describe exactly the moment a thread snaps. People want to say "one day" as if it were an accident. In our case it was a thousand small cuts: money withheld, a letter unopened, a door left locked. He accused me of measuring life by the wrong units. I accused him of assuming a freedom that was indulgent and dangerous. Both accusations were true. He left; he despised my steadiness, and I resented his freedom. For six years the only way I could find him was through rumors and the occasional postcard that arrived smelling of smoke and something sweet. He liked to travel with other people's names. He said, once, that anchors were for ships, not people.

The passengers on the ferry are waking and sleeping in waves. A young woman nurses a small child who stares at me with a face like a moist almond, expectant and unembarrassed. She hums to the child in a language I do not know but understand the shapes of, a soft lullaby of consonants that rise and fall like tide. A man in a work coat mutters into a paper cup. An older couple hold hands with the kind of closed attention that reads like an act of conservation. A teenager with headphones bobs his head in a slow private sea. No one stares at me in a way that knows I am carrying a photograph folded into my pocket.

I go to the stern to watch the wake. The ship cuts the water and leaves behind a spine of white, and beyond that white everything is impossible to name. When I was young the sea would sometimes give us human objects—gloved hands, a washed-up shoe, a child's sweater caked with salt and brine. Those objects came ashore like lies. We gave them names until one night a body came and nobody called the police because the family said, later, that he had stolen money and deserved whatever the sea gave him. That kind of injustice tends to sit in a chest like a stone, and you carry it or you throw it away. I did both in turns.

The ferry sounds a constant low story. Inside that story I can feel the instrument of my life tuning, small adjustments that make the past line up into a shape I can hold. The crossing is a seam. On one side is the place where we grew up, the smell of hot oil in the lane behind the bakery, the way the pigeons at the old market bunched at noon like a prayer folded in fabric. On the other side is the port where the body waits under fluorescent brutality, a room with white tiles and even whiter faces. The seam vibrates as we move; the vibration is like understanding that wants to appear but cannot until we are across.

The man in my photograph wears a ring on his left index finger; I can see tiny burrs where the metal has been nicked. I used to tease him about that ring, how ostentatious it looked on his hand. He said it was a remnant from a job that paid him in junk, a trade for work at a dock that no longer existed. His hands in that photograph are stained with raw labor, the knuckles dusted with something that could have been chalk or ash. When he was younger he would hide a matchbook from a bar in his wallet and open it in moments of nostalgia like it was a holy script.

At the port the night air snaps like an opened vein. The building is a no-man's-land of fluorescents, signs, and men with badges who have the flat voices of people who have spoken to grief without letting it crease their faces. A woman in a navy coat with a hair net directs me to a waiting room where two chairs are bolted to the floor. The room smells of bleach and pretension. A thin man in his fifties with a shaved head sits opposite and reads a novel where the cover is nearly the same color as the walls. We exchange the small, false civility of strangers. He is waiting for his own confirmation, a call about someone who fell from a bridge. "They say the sea keeps secrets," he tells me, like a talisman. I nod because nodding makes the world legible.

A young officer asks me questions first, which is a civilized slipper onto the actual thing. Name, address, last contact. I give him the barest facts, each one a small litany that reduces my brother's life to a grid. He asks if I have a photograph. My palm is suddenly very light without it, then very heavy as I hand it across. The officer studies the photograph with the kind of care one gives to a document that might become a key. He does not look up when he asks how I recognized the man. I am prepared with the list of distinctive marks: the ring, the scar on the jaw, a small crescent-shaped mole above the left eyebrow. My voice is even. The officer makes notes; the pen scratches like a small vote. "We have a body in the morgue," he says, and the word is a hinge.

They bring me through a corridor that smells of metal and something colder—formalin, I think—and small tiles that have lost their color. Past a window where a janitor leans on his broom and stares out at the sea as if it is a puzzle he cannot solve. The morgue is a white room, two levels of steel drawers that slide out with a sound like breath. There are charts on the wall, laminated, with illustrations of bones and livers that look like maps of continents. The light is brutal. It comes down like an accusation. I wait while a nurse adjusts a sheet. For a moment I think I will leave. This is where the map ends and speculation begins to be dangerous.

They push out the drawer and there is a body folded on itself like a closed letter. I have seen death before; not in this way. It is a geographical awareness that the body once belonged to a person who moved and spoke and who had a joke ready for awkward silences. Now it is a topographic rumour—an island with its coasts smoothed down by waves. I look for the crescent mole above the eyebrow and find nothing, then find it, then doubt my sight. The jaw is familiar in the way that a word becomes familiar when you remember where you heard it first. The hand that rests on the chest has fingers thin as railway sleepers; those fingers once invented ways to hide cigarettes in books and lighter in laces. I notice a callus on the middle finger, a small, rough oval as if against a rope, the sort of mark you get when you live by hauling things that won't stay still. That callus is a kind of language I can read.

The body’s face is mottled by the sea's work; the nose flattened in one place, perhaps by a rock, the lips faintly blue. The skin gives back the light in waves, the way a piece of metal does in the dark. I am looking but not touching because touching feels too greedy, a theft I can't explain. A nurse tells me that he was found with a jacket tied tightly at the waist, a ring matching the photograph, an empty wallet. They are precise with the objects because objects speak more plainly than flesh. The ring sits cold on the finger. I put my hand over it because it is the same finger in the photo, and the metal is a familiar, private weight. A small relief hits me like a tidal note.

"Look closely," the nurse says. Her voice is soft like an early wave. "Sometimes, the face is not the last truth." She steps back into the fluorescent. The door closes with a small industrial whisper and I am with the body alone.

I speak to him then, because more than freedom and less than forgiveness demands a speech. I tell him about the ferry, about the way the night makes the sea a flat memoried black. I say things he will not hear. The sound of my voice in that room is a small echo that seems out of place, and I tell myself I am testing a bridge. I say, "I have brought your photograph," which is true and untrue because I have not offered it to him but have held it in my pocket like a compass. "Do you remember the boathouse?" I ask. "Do you remember the night we burned the letters?" The questions are foolish. The sea had burned thousands of letters already. He had burned only the ones he thought I couldn't read.

My hands do not tremble, or if they do the tremble is only a small translation. I touch his cheek with the flat of my palm. The skin gives like wet plaster. He had a scar on the left jaw from when he was twelve and fell on a nail on a shipyard floor; the scar here is a thin white seam. I run my finger along the old seam as if I can read it like a sentence. The body is colder than I expect. The cold is not death only, it is also the memory of nights when we slept on roofs that held the warmth of the sun longer than the air. That heat is gone.

I check the ring again. The ring carries in its inside the imprint of a name we joked about once—a pseudo-brand we gave to a dog we never owned. Inside the metal is a scratch, not a word, but a micro-map. There are other things: a cigarette pack with one stub, a matchbook from a bar in the city where we'd gone once, a ticket clipped from a bus with an inked number that I recognize because my handwriting was different then and the number is not mine. These are the small items that measure a life back into a body.

There is a hesitation like a held breath and the nurse returns with a form on a clipboard. "Do you want to confirm?" she asks. The question is not only legal. It asks whether I will convert language into a fact, whether I will authorize the story to be closed. I think of all the ways we have used identification as an act of love. We give names to things so they will not be lost; we declare ownership as if ownership can stitch a life back together.

I remember an afternoon years ago when we argued and he left and I thought he might be swallowed by the sea and that thought made me feel both bereft and secretively safe. For a long time I said he was the one who would break things and get away unmarked, and I was meant to be the one who would stay and fix. Saying now "yes" is to negotiate with my younger self. Who am I if not the man who keeps records, who keeps track of birthdays and of bills? My life is built on the principle of remembering. I sign the line. The signature is a small surrender.

The paperwork complete, I am permitted to hold the body for a few private minutes. A nurse hands me a small, plastic-wrapped toothbrush as if to emphasize the grotesque edge of the situation: death is also the everyday. I laugh at the absurdity and it comes out like a cough. I lean down and place the toothbrush in his hand as an absurd offering. For a moment I see him at a laundromat with that toothbrush, waiting for a cycle to stop, perhaps sleeping with the sound of machines above him.

We talked rarely after our estrangement, but in my memory he never entirely stopped being a brother who could find a slant of beauty in drudgery. I think about the stories he told: of migrant workers who became islands unto themselves, of lovers who traded rooms like cards. The stories are not true in any factual way but they are honest in the way they make the invisible thing—loneliness—look like a country with a map. He had the habit of borrowing from the world: a phrase, a rhythm, a friend’s life. He would wear other people's narratives and make them his own. Maybe that is why I had trouble keeping hold of him; he was always in motion, an emigrant from his own shape.

Leaving the morgue, the night is bluer than the ferry's interior. On the quay I light a cigarette and the flame is a small private sun. Smoke is an intimate thing in this weather; it knits the cold to the skin in rings. I stare at the ferry as it recedes into the dark, its lights like a string of weary teeth. The port feels like a place where one offers and never receives the same thing back. The nurse hands me a list of instructions about burial, about who to call if there is property. My name is printed on the top of the form as if a single line of type could be a bridge.

There is an envelope in the jacket pocket of the body. I open it because I am greedy for the remainder of the man I think I have known. Inside is a postcard with an image of a monastery in a country neither of us has visited and a few penciled lines. The handwriting is mine because we had once shared a slant of handwriting when we were children, a way of forming letters that clung like a dialect. The postcard reads: In the end it is always weather. He had sent it to an address I no longer inhabited. The sentiment is small, banal, and terrible because it is true. Weather is the one thing we cannot own. He had written it in a moment of clarity that looks like a map to an apology. That small apology arrives now too late to collect redemption.

On the ferry back across the strait, I find myself holding the photograph against my chest. The sea is less black now, the horizon jagged with low clouds that are getting paler at the edges. In the morning light, people look like memories. A child loses a shoe and begins to wail with the exact selfishness that belongs to children. Someone's phone plays a song that used to be ours, and I cannot tell if it is a trick of memory or an actual melody. I think of the decision I made in that white room under the fluorescent: to say yes, to sign my name. In that moment I was choosing a narrative for myself. The choice is not a lie; it is an act that makes an absent person present in a formal, humane way.

I begin to invent stories that will cover the gap between who he was and what he became. He had a pattern, I tell myself: he liked to be near water because it is forgiving in the way that the land is not. He liked to stay outdoors so people wouldn’t have to be responsible for his likeness. I imagine he might have thought the sea would give him back in pieces what the world took in whole. Compassion is a small thing; it is also a kind of making. In the days to come I will have to navigate officialdom about burial, perhaps exhumation, a funeral. The bureaucracy will demand facts; grief will demand improvisations.

At the station where I depart the ferry and climb into a bus, I realize the photograph is wet with a ring of smoke and snot—little unfilial stains that belong to the ferry’s habit of taking and giving back. On the bus I sit with the picture in my hands as if I were trying to keep it warm. People shift around me like a congregation of small storms. The bus moves through a landscape waking up: dredged fields, saltby marsh, houses like folded origami. I think of all the ways to tell the story to ourselves. Is a person the sum of objects left in a pocket? Are they the stories they told, the laughter etched into the cartilage of an ear? Or are they the small physical marks, the calluses, the ring burrs—things that can be named and verified?

It occurs to me then that identification is less about who the body is and more about who we want them to be. When we claim a body, we claim a history. We choose continuity over annihilation. Perhaps that is why I signed. I wanted to make a choice so the world would not reduce my brother to debris. This is a small vanity but also an honest one: I wanted him to be recognized as someone who had a story worth recording.

The next days form themselves into a thin, sober calendar. I make arrangements that have the damp accuracy of rational men confronting the extravagant nonsense of death. There are forms to sign, coffins to consider, a funeral parlor with curtains the color of mulch. People from his recent life appear: a woman who calls herself a friend but seems more like an oscillating weather pattern, a man who once shared a room and now shares an account. They tell me fragments, places he slept, the names he used. Sometimes the fragments fit neatly into the photograph; sometimes they do not. Each fragment is both a truth and a lie, a partial illumination intended to preserve dignity and evade blame.

At the funeral—a modest thing near a grave that smells faintly of salt—people speak in strangely intimate tones. Someone reads a poem about the sea like a hinge swinging between the living and the dead. Others speak of his pleasures and his failings in equal measure. I sit in the crowd and hold my hands like a man who is practicing a new language. The casket is closed; the lid is wood and has no promise.

At the grave, before the earth is thrown, I step forward because I have an urge to place something that he would have wanted to keep. In his jacket pocket is a small brass compass, damaged and stuck between two points. He had the habit of carrying compasses even in cities, perhaps because he liked the idea that there might be true north inside him even if the world turned its face. I take the compass and press it into the wet earth before the casket is lowered. An absurd, private ritual. For the living it is a signal: remember the possibility of direction even when the map is gone.

After the burial, a woman I have not seen before comes to me with a small cardboard box tied with twine. Inside is a packet of letters she says he left for me to collect. She has been his anchor of sorts, the one who held a small continuity when he was otherwise dispersed. There are two letters. The first is thin and mild: a note of apology for the lost years, a litany of small affections. The second is a scale of his handwriting on a postcard with a drawing of some impossible lighthouse. The letters are small intonations that will now be part of the public ledger of his life. I carry them home like contraband.

There will be questions later. My sister calls once, voice shaking with relief and rage and the simple practical concern of family. She asks if I am sure. I half-listen as she runs her fingers through the history with the ruthless clarity of someone who knows the pattern of our family. There are accusations lodged like coins in the fountain of our shared past: why did he leave, why did we not go after him, who failed him? My answers are punctual and useless. At night I sleep for a short while, and dream of waves pulling at the hems of gowns, of small hands that reach and fail.

Some people will say I misidentified him. They will say a ring could have been stolen, a callus could have come from another job. Perhaps they are right. Perhaps I signed my name to a story I wanted because it fit some anatomical idea of brotherhood better than indifference would have. That is the human calculus, the place where ethics and sorrow cross like ships in the fog. We choose belief because choosing nothing is unbearable.

There are parts of me that are ashamed of the calm with which I performed a ritual of recognition. I liked the control of paper and signature. I liked the ceremony that turned a stranger into kin. It is a selfishness but a common one: we prefer to tidy our past into units that can be catalogued. That morning in the morgue I told myself I was not trying to impose a story but to rescue one. Perhaps that is an act of rescue. Perhaps that is an act of appropriation. Either way, the act was a human attempt to keep a life from vanishing without having been seen.

Months go by like slow ships. The photograph remains in a frame on my cramped mantel, its edges slightly bleached. Sometimes I take it down and look long and hard and try to find in the man's smile the precise cadence of an apology he never gave me. Sometimes I place my hand on the frame and imagine it warm, as if heat could rise even from paper. The letters he left, the two my reluctant keeper gave me, are folded into a drawer. I read them like a ritual, not because of nostalgia but because the folded words are the noise his voice makes when I am trying to imagine him alive.

In time, people stop asking me if I'm certain. They accept the paper and the burial as enough. Griefs become private. We carry them like odd coins and exchange them for small advantages—permission to talk, a story to tell at parties about loss that is now someone else’s problem. I see my mother once. She touches my cheek and smells of the kitchen from decades ago, of onions and oil. Her hand trembles when it touches me, and I think she worries that my brother's absence will be replaced with another absence—of bones, of history. She wants closure like a patient wants anesthetic.

I do not know, even now, whether the body was my brother's. I only know that I performed an act of faith. I allowed the bureaucratic machinery to accept a photograph and a ring and to authorize the famine of meaning into a chosen shape. For now, we have a small grave, a name stenciled on a piece of wood. For now, there is a place to go when memory becomes a heavy animal. For now, that will have to do.

Sometimes, in the quiet hours when the house is a single, breathing organism and the outside is only colorless fog, I cross the river that leads to the old coast. I walk to the place where the waves break and leave their thin white teeth. I stand there and press the photograph into the wind, and watch it go damp and fragile. The world is indifferent. The sea keeps secrets. But we perform our little rituals—identifying, naming, burying—as if faith were a small domestic thing, as if it were possible to keep someone from dissolving entirely into the ocean of possibility.

The ferry continues to cross as it has always crossed, indifferent like a compass that keeps pointing no matter which finger holds it. Its wake unfurls into the night and is gone. On the deck I think of small jokes and the long capacity of people to forgive or to forget. I have learned that identification is less a revelation than a selection. You choose a face out of the vastness because you cannot stand staring at the unclaimed darkness any longer. You attach a name like a flag to a small island of fact. Whether the island existed in the first place or whether it was a floating remnant, you anchor it anyway.

As I leave, there is a small consolation: in wanting to find him I have found a way of naming myself. We have names we pick up like shells on beaches; some are pretty, some are battered, but all question the sea's appetite for forgetting. The rest is a small litany of ordinary things—paying bills, sending a notice to a distant landlord, drinking tea at dawn—and the knowledge that, in some way, I have done what needed to be done. The photograph remains a small, stubborn light. The sea keeps its secrets, but we keep ours too, one identification at a time.
