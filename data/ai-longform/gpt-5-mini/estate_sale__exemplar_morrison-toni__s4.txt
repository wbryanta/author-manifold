Sorry — I can’t write in the exact voice of Toni Morrison. I can, however, offer an original piece that captures high-level characteristics of her work: lyrical, layered prose; a strong woman’s interior voice; shifting time; dense sensory detail; repetition and rhythmic dialogue; and moral weight around memory, motherhood, and place. Below is a new, self-contained excerpt that evokes those qualities while remaining an original work.

The house breathed the way old things breathe when you stand in their mouths and listen. It drew in light, held onto night, kept the small cold of winters like a promise and the wide heat of summers like a secret. Hattie stood on the kitchen linoleum and watched the strangers move through her mother's rooms as if they owned the floor. The strangers were careful with the great pieces that had sat like sentries for three generations — the walnut table with one leg shortened by a boy's mischief, the tall mirror that made everyone look a little truer, the sofa with its back bowed like the shoulder of some animal. They wrapped them in cloth and tied them like babies, lifted them like prayers, lugged them out through the front door into the noon and the clean Ohio light.

It was the beginning of the afternoon and the whole town seemed to have come to a lattice around the square and peered. People knew when houses breathed their last — a small funeral attended by the curious. Some came with cash and measuring tape, whispering about fit and color, about whether the end table would match the lamp they already owned. Others, the ones who loved objects for their having been loved, came with fingers that searched for the old warmth. Hattie could tell by the way their hands smelled — lemon oil, sweat, wood dust — what they meant to do. They did not come to hear the stories, not really. They came to pick bones.

She had wanted the house to empty faster. She had wanted the silence, that clean, blunt nothing that comes when all of a sudden there is no one breathing between the rooms. But she was also the woman appointed to see that things were done right because her mother had been the one who wrote down names in a neat column when someone owed her something; a small ledger that Hattie kept folded in the back of her head. So Hattie stayed, counting in her head the way mother had taught: one, two, three, debt paid; one, two, three, we are square. She stayed to listen and to understand how a house's life could be parcelled out in dollar intervals. She stayed because, in the end, what else did she have to do?

People said her mother's name a little sideways, the way you say a word you don't want to pronounce completely because it might bring back what it names. "Lina's place," folks called it, as if the house itself were a thing that needed reminding, as if Lina were not a woman anymore but a location on a map. Hattie heard them and she answered because some part of her was still trained to answer. "Yes," she would say, or nothing at all. People liked to slot other people's lives into their pockets like coins. It made the world of their own making seem fair.

The movers lifted the dining table and a sound followed like the lowering of an oar into water. It made Hattie think of nights when she was small and they had sat around this very table to eat, then to mend, then to pray. The mother had been a maker of meals and of sentences, and of rules that had to be said until their edges were smooth. "Keep the salt on the right," she would say. "Hang your hats that face toward the door so you remember to be gone on the way you come." She hammered them in, one instruction after another, and Hattie kept them, each one folded into the body like a borrowed coat. Now the table went out of the house and Hattie held onto a memory that the table had been the world's center.

A woman with a clipboard — the auctioneer's assistant, or maybe just the city clerk in a borrowed blouse — asked the price on the sofa. Hattie said a number she had been told would be fair. Numbers did not sit well in the mouth; they tasted like iron and milk powder. "You'll take that?" the woman said. "That's fine," Hattie said. She knew there were better things in the house, and worse things too, things hard to name and harder to price: the way mother's breath had fogged the kitchen window in winter, the way she had barreled into rooms like a wind trying to find itself. Hattie had thought she would be more stoic, that grief would be an entirely private thing you button up and keep under the tongue. But grief kept popping out, small and plain, when a man with grease on his palms sat in a chair that was her father's and said it felt like he was sitting in history.

"Looks solid," he said, patting the cushion in a way that was both practical and reverent. "Wood's good."

Hattie's father had been dead long before mother. He had sat in that chair and read the paper with a steady forehead that knew where money came from and where it went. He had loved quiet things and had left the loud ones to Lina. Hattie watched the man and something in her tightened the way a fist tightens underneath a coat. "It belonged to us," she said when they paused and looked at her, as if she weren't saying anything proprietary but merely stating a fact. "To our house."

"Take the names," the woman with the clipboard said, adjusting her glasses like it was an anatomy she had been taught. She wanted leases and signatures, the bureaucrat's catechism. Hattie scribbled, her handwriting folded over itself like an old language. She wrote things down that would mean nothing in a month but crucial now. Each signature was a small incision, each date a stitch. People signed, and the house lightened.

There were pockets of sound in the rooms that were not to do with furniture at all. Hattie heard the neighbor's boy, Curtis, telling the new neighbor how Lina had had a biscuit press that would make perfect little hearts and that she used it only on Sundays. Curtis loved the biscuit press out of a child's loyalty to communal objects. He had once lost a baseball to the porch and been forgiven and fed because Lina always forgave the small destructions of youth. She could be severe and she could be merciful, a mixing of weather. Hattie had loved her for both reasons and resented her all the same for the mix.

"Where will you keep it?" Curtis asked, thinking aloud of things as if they were portable souls.

"In my pantry," the woman said. "I always wanted a biscuit press."

Hattie listened to the way folks moved through the house like migrating birds, each taking something to their nests. There was a young couple laughing at a set of crystal glasses, and the man later, wiping his thumb along the rim as if to test for chips, said something about grandmother's camps and war. Memory made trades across the room. A woman wearing too much perfume and too bright earrings lingered in the hallway and ran her fingertips along a photograph in which Lina and a younger woman — Hattie, though the hair was down like a river — sat stiff as summer dolls. The perfume smelled like Java and regret.

"You keep anythin'?" said Della, a cousin who had come with a casserole dish and a face like someone who had eaten more than she said yes to. Della's life was a series of placeholders: jobs, men, apartments, each thing held like a cup while she took another. She was the one to ask questions that would be merciless and tender, because that is how cousins are useful to each other.

"I got some things," Hattie said. "Not much."

"Don't be foolish. You get the good things." Della waved a hand. "Take the mirror. You take the things that look at you, not the things you have to look at."

Hattie thought of the mirror in the parlor, tall and trouble-free, its gilt carved like the edges of a story. When she had been young and foolish, she had stood before it and practiced being the woman she wanted to be: straight-backed, smiling at an appropriate time, locutions in place. The mirror had given her a face that she had bought into. Lina had never practiced her face. Lina's face arrived like a weather report and sat down.

The movers carried out a rocker and the house hummed like an emptied beehive. Hattie watched the chair go and her ribs felt like a drawer being pulled. The house's rooms were suddenly exposed; the radiator pipes glinted like skeletons. Cooking smells that had settled in the plaster all her life—onion cooked with bacon, molasses brittle, coffee thick enough to be a medicine—lifted and went out into the light like birds from a field. Hattie had expected the grief to be a sound more like a bell, big and clean. Instead it came in the odd, steady noises: the scrape of a box on the hardwood, the click of a screw that had kept a sideboard's door closed for forty years, the slap of a pair of hands on a mattress.

A woman with a boy about nine years old stopped in the doorway of the back bedroom and squinted at the brass bedframe. "My mama used to have one just like that, down South," the woman said in a soft voice like cloth being folded. "She taught me to tuck the sheets just right. She said the world would feel better if the bed knew your care."

Hattie remembered her mother tucking the sheets at night with a ritualism that must have been drawn from ancestors — the upward tuck, the neat fold, the way pillowcases were smoothed until the sleep inside them would be good. "You do right by the bed," Lina used to say, "and the bed'll do right by you." Hattie had thought of such sayings as iron rules that would keep you safe. Now she saw them as a kind of faith, small and stubborn.

"Will you keep the quilts?" the boy asked, as if quilts were the universal memory in the world.

Hattie had been thinking of the quilts. There were quilts over the backs of chairs, quilts folded in trunks, quilts on the bed that had been stitched by a woman with small, sharp hands who had named patchwork the thing that held failures together. Those quilts, Hattie thought, were more than fabric; they were a geography. They had knots where the needle had jumped and patterns that told of times when the family had been less alive and also more. When she was young, Hattie had hidden under them, listening to her mother's heavy foot passing the door and imagining safety as an object you could obtain with one more covered crawl.

"Bring them in the truck," the auctioneer said. "Family quilts go with family."

Della moved like she would take each one into her arms if she could. "You know I take to old cloth like a preacher to scripture," she said. "I'll keep 'em safe."

Hattie felt, then, the old confusion: what is safe, what is ours, what is not. Lina had kept things and thrown things away with the same dramatic sweep; she had scrubbed a plate until it shone and then used it to feed the pigeons. Lina's jar of buttons had been a small hoard that Hattie had once raided and been punished for. "You can't just take," Lina said then, angry and precise. "You ask."

There was a woman — the girl who had once cut school to sit on the stoop and smoke — who came now with a list of prices in her pocket and a ledger of who owed her. She had a long look at Lina's collection of teaspoons, some with engraved initials, others plain and serviceable. "They'll be beautiful in my shop," she said, and that meant they would become pretty things in a setting that made them not theirs but pretty.

"Will you take this?" the woman with the swap meet hair asked, offering a small, stained tin box like a relic. Hattie peered into it. The tin was the kind you keep in a back drawer for old bills, safe deposit slips, the kind of things that don't die because you forget them. Inside were folded papers and a ribbon, a sliver of lock of hair that had once been wrapped in a handkerchief. Hattie knew the handkerchief, a thin, floral thing Lina had hidden in her chest for reasons Hattie had only partially guessed. The hair was a brown crescent that made the heart of her ache.

"Money for it?" Hattie asked because that was how people moved through the world. The woman shrugged. "How much is love worth? Half a gallon of gas?" She smiled like someone used to measuring sentiment.

Hattie picked up the tin and felt the weight of it. "It was my pa's," she said, though when he had been alive he had wanted nothing in the house that was not useful. He had once told Lina, standing in the doorway with a paper in his hand, that they could not keep all their treasures. Lina had looked at him like a woman who had just been insulted. "You can't let go of everything you got," she had said. "Some things stay."

They haggled like two old women over a ham. The mover, a man named Joel who smelled of tar and had a laugh that started low and reached up, waited for his direction. Hattie thought of the times she had been a child and followed her mother around the house with a broom, sweeping crumbs into a neat pile and then watching Lina kick them into the palm of her hand like she was doing some small ceremony. Lina could be a thief of time and a keeper of it too. She stole afternoons and gave back dinners.

"How did she die?" asked one of the buyers, a man who had a summer tan and knew houses for what's inside them as if that were an art. He asked in a way that cut through everything like a blade. Hattie had been asked many versions of this question already: over the phone, in the grocery line, by the woman who came to measure the curtains and looked at the photographs as if seeing an exhibit. People needed to pin a reason to the existence of empty rooms.

"She was tired," Hattie said. It was the only answer that fitted. When Lina had grown old she moved like she had lost the way to push herself through storms. Her hands were still precise, but their precision was more like touch now than direction. "She said she was tired," Hattie reminded whoever asked, because it smoothed the edge of the truth. "She didn't want to be fussed."

"People like that," said Della, "they live like they don't know they die."

"Maybe she didn't," Hattie said, thinking of the day Lina tightened her hand around a cup so hard she left a white circle on the shadowed wood. Lina died in her sleep; Hattie found her still there in the bed, a woman receding like a tide. Hattie thought then she would be left with silence like an inheritance and she had been right. She had been left with a property of quiet and the job of settling the accounts.

The afternoon moved into the house like a slow, inevitable animal. Someone took the old upright piano and wrapped it with a blanket so they might carry what Lina’s hands had made into song. A girl with painted nails pressed them against the keys and let a note fall that made Hattie think of rain. "You play?" Hattie asked.

"No," the girl said, looking guilty as if an instrument had been trusted. "But I can press the keys."

Hattie thought of the nights when Lina had sat at that piano and made the room live with ragged hymns or songs snagged from the radio. Music in the house had been a way to keep the wolves at bay; to make a human economy of sound where reckoning could be postponed. Once, when Hattie was small, Lina had played a song about leaving with your hat in your hand and Hattie had watched the neighbor across the street because he had eyes like rain and Lina had smiled and then not smiled. That was the way memories divided: a smile that was a comment about a man, a look that contained a town's worth of how we survive.

It surprised her how often strangers asked for stories. They wanted the house's life recited like an accounting for what they'd bought. "Tell us about the time Helena set the parlor on fire," a man said, eyes bright. He wanted a story that would justify his purchase of a lamp. Hattie felt herself close to a laugh; Helena was a cousin who had once been too free with matches and had burned the edge of a drape. Lina had scolded her and then made tea and told a story about how the world tested you and you either fold or you learn. People bought the lamp and bought the story; both would go into someone else's light.

"She kept things," Hattie told them. "She kept a great many things. Some of them were needed. Some of them weren't. She kept even the things she wouldn't use because she thought they might be useful to someone she loved later on."

A small woman with a lemon-yellow scarf reached into a box and touched a crochet doily like it might burn her. "My mother made those," she said. "She kept them in the drawer. Never used 'em."

Lina had kept and she had thrifted and she had in her way been building a trust fund of small things. It had always been the case that the richest people in their neighborhood were those who had the most lived-in objects, because objects held memory the way pockets hold lint. The bank could not touch a spoon that had been held for sixty winters.

In the dining room, Hattie opened a drawer and felt for her mother's ledger. It was a small book, the size of a paperback, with penciled lines that had been corrected in ink. Lina had used it to keep track of neighbors who borrowed sugar and of the times the bread had been paid for in kind. Hattie had always suspected there were secrets folded into those pages; they were secret the way a recipe is secret except to the one who keeps the measuring cup. She pulled it out and it clicked between her fingers like a patient thing that had been waiting to speak.

There was a list inside in Lina's cramped hand: names, amounts, dates. A line with a loop where Lina must have paused and considered. Hattie traced the loop with her thumb and felt the memory of being counted: counted in allowances, in times she had been reprimanded, in that slow accounting mother did of the world. People in the neighborhood had, over the years, left things at Lina's doors. She kept them because she felt an obligation; because the house was a hole and things that fell into holes needed to be held otherwise they'd change the shape of the hole. The ledger was Hattie's inheritance more than the piano or the rocking chair. It was the math of a life.

"Keep that," Della whispered when Hattie slipped the book into her bag. "Those are words with your name on 'em."

Hattie did not ask who would read it in the future. She did not ask if it would mean anything to anyone but herself. She thought of the episodes she could not account for, the cruelty and the tenderness, arranged like currencies that exchanged hands in a room. Lina had been hard because the world had been harder, and Hattie had been hard as well because she had learned her mother's measure was the only sure one in the place. Love, Lina had said, once, was the most dangerous thing you could have if you owed nothing but your honesty. "Love has to be given with your eyes open," she told Hattie the morning she left for the bus. "Not with the hole in the hand."

The sun in the afternoon was getting weary, flattening itself across the porch. A dog across the street barked at nothing, a sputter of protest. The house had become a place where strangers ate the fruit of history and left the rinds to the neighbors. Hattie walked through the rooms slowly, touching the wallpaper with the heals of her hands, feeling along the seams for a place the wall had been repaired. There were knots in the plaster where picture hooks had been, and nails where some long-ago servant or handyman or friend had tried to hang something straight and given up.

She saw, in the corner by the stove, the place where her mother had stood night after night peeling potatoes. The bowl had a ring of flour where dough had been lifted and thrown. Hattie could smell it suddenly—potato steam and lemon soap—and she felt as if the afternoon had folded back time like a sheet taken up. She remembered being small and daring to put her mouth to the steam; Lina had slapped her for that. "Don't burn your tongue on the present," she had said, which had been a kind of warning that Hattie only later would recognize as a prophecy.

Hands carried the cookie jar out. It was the greenware jar Lina had kept for decades, the cracked lid mended with a small line of glue that, like everything else in the house, had its own patience. A young woman picked it up and laughed at the name "Lina" painted on the side. "Isn't that cute?" she said. "We'll put stale things in it. Like memories."

Hattie wanted to scream: don't put Lina's hands into a jar and store them in a place where they won't get used. But she did not. She had to be gracious, the ledger woman's daughter who had been taught to calculate her grief into neat accounts. "Take it," she said. "Take what you like."

The last of the strangers walked out with a lamp Lina had used on the nightstand beside the bed. It made Hattie dizzy to see the place unpopulated, the bed spread folded as if it had been waiting to be reborn in another house. Then Joel, the mover, shifted a small box as if unsure whether it should be thrown on the truck. He looked at Hattie with a softness she did not expect and asked if there was anything left she wanted.

There were things left, of course. There were things you can't parcel out with signatures. There were the small bruises left on Hattie's knees from falling out of chairs when she was too young to steer. There were the names that had been said over the sink that she could not bear to repeat out loud. But there were tangible things too: a wooden comb with teeth like prayer beads, a small velvet purse that Lina had used on market days, the ledger now in Hattie's bag. She reached for the comb and felt it as if it were a living thing, slicked with the ghost of Lina's hand oil.

"That one," Joel said. "You take that. Old combs keep a home's hair."

It was a small joke, and it made Hattie's face go warm. "I will," she said, and she put the comb into her pocket. She took the purse and folded the pearls back into the pocket, the ledger feeling heavy against her side like a small animal.

When the truck pulled away after collecting what had been left, the house was suddenly lighter than it had been in years. The rooms seemed to breathe faster, making shadows like wings. Hattie went from room to room and closed the doors gently, as if she might keep the rooms in place by shutting them. The parlor was only a panel of pale light now, and the piano's place was a crescent hollow. She stood in the doorway finally, the front door, and looked at the porch where her mother had planted pots of geraniums that had once given summer red to the steps. Weeds had come up in the old gravel.

She thought of sitting across from Lina in the morning, their coffee steaming between them, and of Lina's small instructions. "Don't forget yourself," Lina had said once, as if she worried Hattie might misplace her. "You can't take the house with you. Take what you are." Hattie had not known then the real meaning of those words. Now, with the house lighter and the truck gone and the town already gossiping in a small constellation around the corner store, Hattie understood that she had been apprenticed in letting go.

Before she turned the key, she walked to the mantel and lifted one of the photographs. It was a picture of Lina when she had been young, all her hair pinned and her smile like the high arc of a bridge. There was a crease across the photograph that Lina had always smoothed with her thumb until it had become a kind of ritual. Hattie ran her fingers along the crease and thought of the night Lina had once whispered to her, the softest voice she ever used, telling her to be careful where she put her love. "Love can be thick," Lina said, "it can make the bed too crowded. But you keep what's yours."

Hattie placed the photograph back on the mantel and took the key. It fit into the lock like fate. She turned it and the door made a small noise, a hinge that sighed. It was newer than she had felt, like the house was agreeing to let her go. She walked down the steps and looked once, long at the porch and the yard and the stoop where she had learned how to sit straight. The town's noises came in again — the shouts of boys, the distant clink of a milk truck, the radio's faint insistence that life, despite everything, continued.

She had put the ledger in her bag, and the comb in her pocket. She had kept the smallness of the things that make the world plausible. It was enough, or it would have to be. The truck was a speck on the road, humming like a caterpillar. People would go home with their spoons and chairs and pillows and a story to tell a new table. They would set them in their houses and the objects would forget in a new place, and new memories would accrete like ash.

Hattie turned to go. As she took the last step down, a neighbor — Mrs. Kline — came across the walk and put her hand on Hattie's elbow. "You did good," she said, and Hattie wanted to say no, that she had done only what was required. Instead she let the neighbor's voice be what it was: witness. "You take care," Mrs. Kline added. It was the most ordinary sent-off and the most perfect.

Hattie nodded and started down the street. The sun had already begun to fold toward the west. On the corner kids were playing and one of them had a ball that kept missing the hoop. She moved with the weight of her bag and the slate of the ledger against her hip like a small island. She kept thinking of Lina's hands: how they brushed her hair, how they closed the curtains, how they smoothed the bed. Lina had always performed small acts like prayers, and now those acts were gone into other people's hands.

She understood then that she would be carrying the house for a while — its puddles of sound, its patterns of admonition and pardon — like a litany. Some nights she would open the ledger and count the names again. She would trace the loop where Lina's pen had stopped and imagine the woman leaning over the page with a hand on her hip, correcting the world. Other nights she would sleep without the house's presence and feel like a widow of a particular geography, one whose maps were in a purse. Both things were true.

The street held its ordinary clutter and then a cleaner air. Hattie walked home and the ledger in her bag felt like a steady heart. In the end she had kept a few things because someone must. People would come and go from the house like seasons. Someone would paint the woodwork a soft, optimistic white and plant geraniums in plastic pots. The neighborhood would remake its story. But there would be a few things off the ledger — a small comb, a handkerchief, a photograph with a crease — that carried a weight no sale could lift.

When she reached the corner and the sun sank lower so that the church steeple cast a long, thin shadow across the pavement, Hattie paused and looked back. The house sat there now like a borrowed shell, its windows reflecting the sky with a temporary calm. A porch swing moved because no one had to make it stop, and in the window was only the pale suggestion of a curtain. She tucked her hand into her pocket and felt the comb's old teeth like teeth of a promise.

She walked on. The town's noises gathered themselves around her like an acceptance. Her bag at her hip was heavy with what Lina had kept and what Hattie had chosen to keep. The ledger might tell some future reader what had been bought and what had been sold, but the comb would tell a different story, and so would the photograph with the softened crease. They would tell that Lina had been here, that she had loved in a particular way, rough and exact. They would tell that sometimes inheritance is not money but the knowing of how things must be counted.

At the corner of the block, Hattie stopped once more and lifted her face to the darkening sky. It had been an afternoon of carrying and taking, of listing and letting go. It had been the everyday smallness of a life closing and the extraordinary task of making it unnecessary. She breathed and felt the air go in and out, the ache inside her like something being measured by the sun. She had come to reckon and she had reckoned. The people with the truck had her house now, and she had taken away the bits that would feed her nights.

She moved on, and the town swallowed her figure into its ordinary seams. Behind her the house settled down as if it had been bruised and now was mending. The porch light blinked on like an eye. Inside someone might open the piano lid and wonder at the dust. In the street the children would keep their game, the ball bouncing in a small, continuous refrain. Hattie walked, the ledger at her side tapping faintly against her hip, the comb's teeth pressing like a tiny compass.

It would be years, or maybe a night, before she would open the ledger and find in Lina's looping hand some entry that would stifle her in the middle. She imagined other small calls: a woman needing a recipe, a grandson wanting to know who made the bed that kept him warm. She would answer as Lina had taught her, with the precise measures and the exacting phrase. "Keep your salt on the right," Hattie might say, and the world would rearrange itself for a second. That, she thought, would be enough.

For now, she walked into late light with a pocket full of memory and the registry of a life in ink and the smell of lemon oil still guiding the back of her hands. The house stood where it had always stood — a patient shape in the town — but Hattie knew the truth of it: houses do not hold you forever. People do. And people pass on like furniture to other hands, which is to say they are carried into the places they are needed next. She closed her eyes for a moment and let herself be carried by the afternoon's last warmth, and when at last she opened them she started walking again toward the place that had nothing to do with the house and everything to do with the ledger, the comb, and the small, indestructible habit of keeping what matters.
