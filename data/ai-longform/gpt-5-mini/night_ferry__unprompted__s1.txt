The ferry smelled of diesel and old coffee, the two scents braided with the river-rough leather of the benches and a thin, clean salt that lived in the seams. I had come aboard with a plastic bag and a thin coat and a press of hand I had given the man at the ticket window because I did not have anything else to offer him, because there were no other words. The night outside the portholes was a flat black, and the little cabins on the nearer islands blinked like the secret houses of sleep; when we eased away from the pier the lights dropped behind us and the ferry's bow cut a steady white through the dark.

They had said "found in the strait" in the voice on the telephone that woke me at two a.m., the voice of a hospital worker who felt remote and therefore right. The current had curled him on the rocks, or had packed him in kelp, or had held him just long enough for the cold to do its work—she didn't say consciously what I needed to hear, only the facts: there was a body, there was no identification, and could I come, identify, sign? The word identify sat neutral and professional in her sentence; it was as if she were asking me to help her pick out an umbrella from a stand.

I told myself I had driven straight through the night because I owed him this. I told myself I had come because I'm the only family left who might know. When I left the house, the kind of place I could not by any honest means call home anymore, I took the things one brings for a short stay—a pair of old boots, a cardigan that smelled like my mother's mothballs, a book I always meant to finish. I did not pack anything for what comes after. I had not packed for confession or absolution or watching someone who used to laugh at the porch light become a patient of the morgue. I had packed a small, cheap lighter, which I carried by an impulse I had forgotten I possessed until later.

On the ferry there were other people; if there had been anyone I expected to meet it would have been exactly these kinds. Someone with a dog that sat with patient, gummed eyes like a friend who had been to war; a couple up front who kept their elbows touching almost without meaning to; an old man in a wool cap reading a paper whose headlines were about other people's thefts and storms. None of them seemed to notice me. When we pushed through the night the ferry rocked and the metal sighed, and the light from the windows cut ovals across the carpet like moons passed through cloud.

I had not seen Jonas in eight years. The number stacked up like a kind of wrongness I could no longer ignore: winters when the house smelled of his cigarettes; summers when we had put an oar between us like something that could be turned into a border; the mornings he went away and slammed a door, sometimes balking at the idea of returning. There were small things he had left behind—an orange knit hat he had abandoned at a fishing contest, the chip in the kitchen cupboard door that matched the look of his fists after too many arguments. Everybody says "We were close once," in the way of people who mean it without wanting to be precise about what "close" was. I could see his face as a child, newborn and puckered in a hospital bassinet, and then later in my mind the black hole of the last morning we had shared a roof.

We had been brothers in the sense of being two halves of a coin you could not quite flip: an allegiance and a rivalry folded into one. He was slight, wiry. He laughed at things a beat too late, then with everything, so that the laughter could sweep the room. He could fit two or three other people into his charm the way a man folds a raincoat. He also had a way of taking things as if they were left for him: a bicycle when he was thirteen he never paid for; the money he lent that he never repaid; a girl he ought to have left alone. Once—or rather twice—he had taken a wrong turn that looked, from where I stood, like a deliberate misunderstanding. We had shouted in the doorway until it felt as if the house would split; I've tried to count the hours since that I had not spoken his name. He had a laugh that came from his ribs and a scar along his left brow from the time we were boys and he fell off the swing and the branch hit him. He had, I remembered now and with a quickness that felt like a theft, a small crescent missing from the end of his right index finger, an accident with a tin of sardines at sixteen. It was improbably specific, and the mind returns to details like a magnet.

On board the ferry I watched a man in a paint-streaked jacket make a small circle in the condensation on the window. The sea beyond it was an unmoving black, catching nothing but the diffuse glow of the ferry's lights. The crew made a round one hour and then the next, offering the coffee and a small pastry wrapped in plastic. I took the coffee because there was something to do with its warmth, something human about holding it between my hands. My fingers smelled faintly of the lighter as I blew the coffee and tried to remember the sound of Jonas's voice.

I had considered, in the way one considers a list of acts of duty, how small this pilgrimage was. A ferry ride and a signature. There are whole rituals that grow around dying as if death were a postal system—there are boxes and forms and signatures like stamps—but the core is compressed into a single human task: recognition. To look at a face and say this is him. To put a name to a temperature. To sanction the passage formally. There was a coldness in that responsibility, but also a kind of unspeakable gravity. If the woman on the phone had been younger, I would have imagined her as someone who had an easy hand for papers and a practiced, efficient pity. But she had the rasp of somebody accustomed to the night, someone for whom closeness was work.

When the ferry slowed for the crossing I went out onto the deck. The wind struck my face like an accusation, bracing, and it made my thoughts smaller as if I had to tuck them down inside my collar. There were other passengers up on deck—two teenagers with headphones, a womanlighting a cigarette with a kind of deliberate care that told me the cigarette was not merely for nicotine but for holding—whose worlds did not brush mine. Even in the dark, the sea threw back a cold translucence, and the ferry's bow churned foam like a ragged white collar. An old lamppost stood on the prow of the nearest island, its light steady as some small church.

I began to think about the surname we shared and how, as an adult, I had usually referred to Jonas as simply "Jonas" in the way you address the absent by their first name so they hang closer. There were times I had done something like this for other people's ghosts—named them in a shopping list or at the sink. Naming is a small incantation.

The hospital was the pale building that looks like it's always been growing, assembling extensions like grafting; when I walked in I tried to adopt the posture of the reasonable, the willing. There was a man at the desk who looked up and said my name, which surprised me; it was as if the world already had a list. He led me through a couple of linoleum halls, past a television no one watched, into a cool, windowless room that smelled of disinfectant and something softer beneath it—rubber, the smell of plastic gloves. The man behind the desk, who had a low, clear voice, asked me to sit. He asked me if I had any identification for Jonas, and I thought of the things one carries in pockets that are not the small proofs but the private imports of a life—train passes, a brass lighter stamped by a bar where we used to gamble, an old key with a tag scratched with a name.

In the waiting room there was a pamphlet about death that looked unhelpful. The chairs were arranged at a weird angle, as if for an audience of strangers. A woman in a cardigan sat by a vending machine and pressed a coin into it, and when a pack of peanuts dropped she looked down at them like someone who had been given a small animal. The clerk—an efficient man with hands that were interesting enough to be distracting—held a folder and told me what would happen. There would be a doumentation form. There would be a photograph. He never used the phrase "viewing" or "open casket;" he used language that was precise in its meaning but kept itself distant, as though it wanted to be kind by ignoring romance.

The room in which they keep the unknown is cool as a church basement and bright with an unforgiving white. A steel trolley and a sheet and a refrigerator door that breathed out its cold. There were hooks in the wall and a clock that ran on a small, steady tick. They brought the sheet in and the man's face closed with my voice in the way a relay snaps. The sheet was folded back at the shoulders to show the jaw and the cheeks. The body, for all the surrender that is in every corpse, had a dignity even in its defeat. The lips were blue, but the face was composed, as if it had been posed for a photograph.

I looked for the small things first—signs you have carried down the groceries aisle or in the pockets of your coat. The man beside me—the clerk who had become a human geography of necessary phrases—said softly that sometimes people had jewelry or a limp that could be recognized in the bones or a scar that held. He spoke as if he were laying out options on a table.

I had scripted this moment in my head like a dumb rehearsal: I would look and instantly everything would fall into place. Instead, the mind is slippery. Faces are curving things. The cold does curious things to the skin; salt bleaches hair; water changes the lines around a mouth. There are grainy things that happen when our memories choose to protect themselves, and sometimes memory will bolster a detail that isn't there like a small lentil in a bowl. I peered at the face and tried to remember if the chin was more forked than this, if the cheek had been higher. The scar above the left eye—yes, there it was, thin and white, a little arc. The missing crescent on the finger—was it there? I asked to look at the hands, an awkward, intimate plea in the sterile room.

A nurse in blue gloves unfolded the sheet with the kind of slow ceremony that business requires; there was a ritual to everything here, as if moving too fast will tear the fabric of an agreement with something beyond us. The hands were pale, slightly water-stiff, with the fingers like small pillars. I saw the right index, and I felt a hot, immediate lurch of recognition like the closing of a trap. The tip was smooth where it had been bitten away or bruised, not a jagged thing but a neat crescent of skin that ended the finger as if someone had decided a story should end mid-sentence. I whispered, "Jonas," and the room waited.

They asked me a few practical questions—had he been in touch, had he called—and those were questions that were both bureaucratic and surgical. In the bluntness of lists they wanted to know when we had last spoken, an address, any surviving kin. I told them that we had not spoken in some years. I told them I didn't know where he had been staying exactly. The clerk pushed a pen across the paper and the point of it bobbed on the sheet like a small, patient insect.

There were various possibilities: they could take his fingerprints. They could do dental records. They could call some list of numbers. But the dead were in a hurry not to be alone any longer, even if the living still preferred their rituals. The man had no wallet. He had no phone. He had a ring of keys tied with a rubber band and a coal I've always believed in as a kind of human relic—a lighter with a name scratched on it. The nurse found a lighter in the pocket of the coat that had been left on him—small, brass, scratched with the name of a bar in the town where we had grown up. My old mouth tasted like metal.

I touched the brass with a gloved finger as if that would make it talk. The clerk asked if there was anyone else. There wasn't. Only me. For a second the thought of saying No, it's not him, passed like a small bird—because it couldn't be him?—but it was slighter than the air and it vanished. What the clerk needed was a signature. What I needed, perhaps, was a singular truth to lay down like a seam across a wound.

It's strange what becomes a proof in the end. We measure identity in objects as if the objects hold the soul: a ring, a scar, a lighter with a bar's name. We measure it also in habitual posture—the way someone's shoulders fall, the incline of their head—and the body, robbed of its small living habits, can still betray something. I leaned forward and looked for the slope of the brow he had, for the hollow just under the jaw where his beard had never quite taken in the way that true bristles form. I saw the scar, the finger, the lighter, and I felt the story of us open with one small tear.

"Are you sure?" the clerk said. His voice was careful as a spoon, and I saw behind him a slip of human kindness. He wanted certainty because the paperwork demanded it, because certainty is simple and knows how to do the rest. I found myself thinking of the old groove in our house where Jonas had scratched his initial with a pocketknife beside the doorframe. I saw it as clearly as the curl of cigarette smoke that used to hang over his shoulder. I had been counting down the years to the moment where we would be able to mark him down and place it: dead, yes; brother, yes; identity, yes. There are things one carries until the point that you no longer can.

He was, by every humane measure, my brother. He had the scar; he had the finger; he had the lighter. He had a jaw like the curve of the sea. I said his name again; it was thin yet solid in my throat—the sound we make when we're trying to test a bridge with our weight.

I signed the form. The pen left a dark bird of ink across the paper, my name printed with a hand that trembled but did not betray whether it trembled because of anger or because of relief or because of something else more complicated: the strange easing of a burden and the immediate exposure that follows.

Outside, the night had begun to wear. A light at the horizon winked and then held. The ferry in the small channel had left a braid of foam behind it, and there was the peculiar loneliness of the sea that had been party and grave and everything in one. The clerk told me what would happen next: the coroner would examine; there would be an inquest if need be; the body could be released to a funeral home. There were words like "arrangements" and "next of kin" and "estate." I listened as if the words were planks laid across a chasm. There was a small legalism in the way grief is administered.

They wrapped the body in a clean white sheet and wheeled it away on the trolley. It slid like an island from the room. For a moment I wanted to grab the sheet and hold on; I wanted to insist on something there that would hold the body to my life like a tether. But there are rites you cannot reverse. Somewhere in the back of my head a small animal of a memory gnawed at the corner of the evening—the last thing Jonas had said to me in any kind of decent conversation, which was a line I cannot now conjure whole without fear of falsifying it. I had thought, in those years of miscommunication, that if the world got touched by a soft enough veneer of time, he might come back in the way people sometimes come back from traveling with stories and a dogged desire for a new start. It had not happened. And now the sea had acquired the authority of a seal.

I left the hospital with the document folded in my coat pocket and the lighter in my palm. The night had thinned into a pale that was not yet dawn but too bright to be full dark. The ferry across the last stretch was almost empty; the sun would not be up for another hour and yet the sea was making a slow, uncertain suggestion of color. I smoked a cigarette—what habit there is to ritual in grief—and I thought about the relationship I had wanted to write into shape and could not.

The middle years between us had been a kind of negotiation, or perhaps a competition. Jonas had never been the good-money type; he gave as he received. He gave apologies in spasms and fury in good weather. Once, when we were young, he had taken the small savings I'd kept for a bicycle and gone instead to a friend's house for a night of gambling. He came home with less and apologetic and I was younger then and savagely moral. He took a train to the city the following week and never told me why; the story fractured into half-answers. There is always, with brothers, a currency of injury that sees no insurer.

People like to tell stories where there is a single cause for things—like the prodigal child or the villain. Our story was dirtier. We had loved as much as we had hurt. We had thrown rocks at each other like children. We could talk of our father's house as a geography of small violences. Jonas had been a toad in the back of the throat for me in that sense; he lived in the parts of myself I did not like, and sometimes on the wrong evenings I want to say I absolved him only to make peace with my own choices.

On the return ferry the light was changing. The islands were cut into the horizon like the backs of sleeping animals and the sea had something of a pewter sheen to it. The small lights of fishermen were dots that looked like promises; the sky was no more than about to offer itself. I thought of the lighter, its brass worn and coppered, the name of a bar where we had spent mornings like a shabby epigram. I held it in my hand and watched the ocean move.

People carry a number of false consolations when someone else dies; some of them are practical—"he was no longer suffering"—and some are moral. This one had been partly his own doing. He had walked away from commitments and sometimes from people; he had taken a dangerous pleasure in being free. I had walked away for other reasons, which included a careful arithmetic of shame. When someone disappears for a while you build an entire absent life to explain it and when they return it is often too late to say the things you mean.

I thought about the funeral and about who would stand at the back and who would make the speeches. I thought about the image of my mother—her knees like a map of nicotine, the way she would haunt the kitchen now and light a cigarette at odd hours—if she were alive. She would curl her fingers around the lighter the way people curl fingers around anything they can possess. This is how small objects become talismans: the lighter would be an emblem of all the grubby, stubborn affection Jonas had left behind.

There was, in the end, a kind of simplicity in claiming him. The clerk told me they had done what they could to check—dental and prints and a coroner's initial call—but that there would be confirmation later. My signature had placed me as next of kin, which is more a function of geography than spirit. It meant I would, in the days to come, sit with forms and prices and the small machinery of funerals. It meant I would learn new words: burial, cremation, wake, eulogy. It meant I would be in the room where decisions are made, and in making them I might keep the account of him he would have liked—or I might simply keep the account I had conjured of his life, which is always an act of recalibration.

There was one more thing, a particular of the way grief arranges itself. In the pocket of the coat they had given him, someone found a photograph folded into a cigarette packet. It was the kind of found thing that a coroner's assistant called "personal effects." It was a small square of faded color—two boys on a crumbling pier, hair like nests, the water behind them lifted and turned into a sheet of glass. One of the boys had an orange knit hat and a grin larger than his face. The other had his arm slung around him like a life preserver. It would not have been a provable thing. It would not have been proof for the authorities, but to me it was the most coherent evidence of all. There are times when we lean on a photograph like a steerage pole.

On the ferry back, the sky teased the idea of blue. I sat with the lighter in my hand and the photograph face-down in my pocket. I imagined Jonas as he might have been at the end—how thin the lines that separate us are; how many small bad habits you can put into a human life and still allow yourself the luxury of being loved. The signature on the paper had not made anything true but it had made an arrangement in the world: someone would bury him; someone would count the days toward the wake. In the coming days I would hear the stories from the town: where he had slept, who he had talked to, the friends who would show up and the silence of the old ones.

A woman sitting two seats down had a map folded on her lap and she was tracing a line with her finger as if she were making a plan for a life. I thought about the ferry as a machine that takes you back and forwards and up to some point where you will have to step off. The strait is a place of crossing, of small faiths and decisions. The water that had taken him had long ago learned to take without malice, folding men into swells like paper into the mouth of an envelope. It is easy at these times to imagine the sea as a thief, but it is also a kind of equalizer: it takes the laughter and the crime and returns them all in the same slow tide.

The town was coming awake by the time we docked. Lights cut their way into the streets and the air smelled of wet pavement and roasting fish. I walked up the pier with the lighter in my pocket. The harbor was a collection of small shapes vaguely arranged. A gull harangued the early morning like an old politician. On the quay a small boy held his mother's hand and pointed at me as if seeing something rare.

When I reached the street I took the photograph out and looked at it. The two boys were smaller than I remembered us being in any memory. But there was that hat, orange and small; there was the way the arm slung around; there was the way one of the boys' had an expression like he had swallowed a secret and was about to laugh. I put the photograph back into my pocket and then, with a kind of quietness that felt precisely like the thing the signature had promised, I took the lighter out and struck it.

For a moment the flame was all intent and nothing else. It burned with the polite ferocity of a single match. I watched it as if I were measuring the present against the past, and then I dropped it back into my pocket. The heat lingered in my palm for a half-second—a small, senseless thing I did for nothing but the relief of doing anything at all. It was small enough to be true in a way that huge things are not. I looked out over the strait and thought how many stories the water keeps that involve us as actors and bystanders both. Now and then a wave will fold a small scrap of paper against the hull and the ship will not even know it; to claim the thing is to say, briefly, that it once belonged to someone.

The funeral would come and I would have to speak or keep quiet, and in the small and woolly hours I would sort through images of Jonas and perhaps restore him to the kindness he sometimes never allowed himself. Or perhaps I would hold to the negative space, the way memory is mostly the underside of a torn map. The lighter would sit on my bedside table. The photograph would be in the small wooden drawer of the kitchen that smelled of lemon oil. The signatures would sit in a folder where bills and incense and the small legalities live.

We are, all of us, at certain points the only possible friends of people who die by drowning. We are the only ones left to decide whether the small, private proofs are enough. The clerk made his last small notes and looked at me with the kind of respect that is close to pity. His eyes said the world doesn't end if you get something wrong; the law will not hold you. The world, he implied, will continue, indifferent and thick and merciful in its own shallow way.

I thought, on the pier with my pockets warmed by the lighter, of Jonas laughing at me the way he used to, and of the way his voice would swell like a sail and fill a room. I thought of the last argument, older than some obvious points of grievance, and of how we both left words unsaid because the geography of the conversation had no good map. I thought about the lighter as a small coin of our complicity, and the photograph as a small but accurate truth.

I had no certainty beyond the small things. The scar, the finger, the evidence of the lighter, and the fold of a photograph. It was enough. It was not clean and perhaps not compassionate in the way the sheltering would have wanted; it was not perfect. But it was me, and when that is all there is, it becomes the proper instrument of what we call responsibility.

I put my hand in my pocket and felt the cold brass. The ferry's horn sighed once like a distant old animal. The water moved where it wanted. I walked toward the town and, as the light thickened and the streets began to flex themselves awake, I felt the strange hollow pressure in the mouth—like the press of a tongue against a missing tooth. There would be arrangements to make and people to call and a place set for a man who had both taken and given more than I would ever fully reckon.

When you say the name of a person who has died you make a small bridge across whatever has been lost. I said his name again, aloud, not because I believed that names pull people back but because it is small and human to do so. Jonas. It fit the face in the photograph like a hat. It matched the lighter and the finger and the quick, crooked laugh I could still hear when the world was empty of other applause.

We carry one another across many straits in life—the water is its own authority, indifferent and repeating. My signature had been a physical thing; in the end it was the thing I could offer, as if in a ledger agreement between me and the world. When I got home, days later, there would be calls I would answer badly and speeches I would not want to make, but I would make them. I would stand when the coffin was lowered and I would think of the sea and of the particular way he had taken up room and given me leave to hate him and love him and, finally, to keep him in the small wooden cubbyholes where we keep people alive.

On the morning the harbor filled with light, the ferry's wake behind me was a white bruise spreading and fading. I watched it until it blurred away into the open sea and I felt for the first time a small peacefulness settle itself like a cheap, honest bird on my shoulder. It was not reconciliation—too big a word for such small acts—but it was, at least, a bridge assembled of the few bricks that remained. I walked up the quay, lighter folded in my hand, photograph in the inner pocket, and felt that sometimes a man will name a thing in order to keep it from being only lost.
