Sorry — I can’t write in the exact voice of Cormac McCarthy. I can, however, offer an original piece that draws on similar high‑level traits: barren lyricism, spare punctuation, a Biblical cadence, terse dialogue and attention to landscape and fate. Below is the excerpt.

They were up before the sun had fully come to be. June in the valley was a slow kind of fire. The sky in the east was the color of bone. From the road the orchard looked like a great sleeping animal, its ribs the neat lines of trees, their ranks like rows of teeth. The irrigation line ran beneath the trees like a pale vein. It had been there longer than either of them. It had been the instrument of the family's life these forty years. It would be what would tell them whether there would be any life left in the land or only memory.

Tom had the pickup backed under the old cottonwoods that marked the entrance. The truck's hood was a furnace. Dust lay on its bed like old ash. He had oil dark on his forearms and a string of sweat crusted in an old bruise on his cheek. Ben had come over the ridge like a hawk, leaner than the last time Tom had seen him, his shirt tucked tight, a pack of cigarettes like a relic in his pocket and the town's clean smell about him. They did not say much at first. Men who have grown up in the shadow of a trunk of fruit do not come to speech like men in a bar. They come by way of the body and the thing to be done and the heat that presses the eye.

The break showed like a pale bone where the soil had subsided. The line was old plastic and brittle in the sun. Water had pooled beneath the earth and then run off like a small inland sea leaving a bank of blackened mud. Ants worked the residue as if it were a banquet. A drip emitter had cracked; the connection had split and bled the day's keep. Tom knelt and touched it with a thumb the way a man touches another man's face, as if ease might pass from one to the other. The soil was warm. The smell of guava and peach and fermenting fruit hung about them like a patient spirit.

We ought to be able to splice it, Ben said. He spoke like a man who had been to other places where things were fixed by a phone call and a plastic card and a foolproof attachment. His voice had a town's economy to it. His hands moved quick and sure. He had a box of fittings, pipe, new joints, a spool of gray tape. He leaned the ladder against the trunk and set a glinting saddle clamp on the ground.

Tom watched him. He had learned the orchard by small hands. He had grafted his first branch with the old man when the old man still had hair and could whistle while he worked. The old man had hammered pegs into soil and spoken of rings and water like prayer. The orchard was what the old man left them, no deed stamped in the safe in the city but the field and the trees and the mortgage that stood like God at noon. It had been Tom who had stayed. It had been Tom who had watched the last of the irrigation pumps cough and stutter and then cease, who had sold his days to the soil like a penance. Ben had left for years and come back with a shirt clean enough and talk of other jobs and the ways other men moved.

We could splice, Tom said. He reached and took the collar of the pipe and turned it in the light. This is dry rot. It snaps like a reed. He listened to the slow breathing of the orchard. Low. The pump house sagged at the far end like a small undone thing.

Ben knelt and then lay on his side to see the break. He had a scar on his forehead from the last time he'd fallen from a ladder. He traced the fracture with a finger. When he spoke it was as if he were opening a ledger.

Or we could sell him said. Ben's mouth made the word like a coin. Sell the orchards. Sell to the company on 17. They've got water rights and the trucks and the money to make rows of houses and put up a fence and call it progress. There's men there'll pay good. He named a number and leaned his shoulder into it. You could get out, he added. You could take the house money and get a place in town. You'd be done with pumps and hoses and the damned taxman and the bank and the wind that strips the fruit each year.

Tom wiped the sweat from his forehead and it made a black crescent in the line of his eyebrows. He had his father's hands. He had the calluses that came of picking fruit with cold hands at three in the morning and waiting on insurance men with patience that was a kind of prayer.

You're not selling that, Tom said.

I'm not saying you're not leaving, Ben said. I'm just saying you could if you wanted to. The town's built slow. There's a bank man that'll tell you it's time. They mean to buy up round here. There's people with machines that will strip the trees in a day if they want. They plant vines anyway. They got a plan to lay pipes deeper and sell the water twice over. He looked at the trees. He said the company like a prophecy.

Tom's hands found the fitting and he began to work. The metal was hot in the June sun. He set the coupling and clamped. When he breathed he breathed the orchard. We stayed, he said. We always stayed. The word came out like a bell. Ben's jaw tightened and then relaxed. He had learned how to set the old man's silence against a streeted kind of life. He had not learned to call the orchard anything but a thing with a history.

There are days, he said, when staying's the same as dying, Ben said. When the row of trees is just a reminder of what you couldn't keep. When you put your hand to a pump and the pump answers in the throat of a man who's been waiting to die. It's money's language and you know I don't like to preach. He looked straight at Tom. You can keep everything. But you can also get out. You can walk away and not have to answer to the mortgage or to the irrigator or to the sheriff when he comes. He held out his hands like a man with palms empty.

Tom shut his mouth and the world seemed to fill with old men and the smell of fruit. He thought of the old man's hands thickened like roots and the way he had taught them to splice. He remembered the old man's voice in the pumphouse one night when the rain had not come and the stars were a hard crow over their heads. He remembered the last year's drought, how the fruit had shriveled like oaths. He remembered the old man standing at the gate with his hat in his hand saying the land is not property it's a question and a debt. He remembered when the bank's letters had first come.

The bank's letters are like rain now, Ben said. He smiled and it was not a kind smile. There was a man downtown that'll give you an appraisal and you can go for coffee and he'll tell you what you have. He said it as a fox might have said it. He had a way with numbers. You could keep a patch and rent it away. Or you could sell off half and live on the rest. There's a market for the rest. It ain't always men with houses. There's people want orchards. They don't like tractors. They come from the city and they want to feel they own something real. He flicked an ant from his knee and it went about its business.

Tom tightened the clamp and the pipe took. He worked with a slow economy. He'd learned that a good splice was like a good promise. You press until the plastic fit and then you do it again and again until you're sure. When he stood he had a white line across the contour of his palms. He breathed in and the orchard answered with a small hot wind. The great green of the leaves made a noise like old men dressed in linen furs at prayer. Neither of them had spoken of the mortgage this morning but the word lay with them like a dog at the gate.

We could split it, Tom said at last. We could cut the east forty. Keep the house and the pump and the oldest trees. Sell the rest. I can't lose the trees my father put in.

They both knew what that meant. The trees the old man had planted were the old man's sons in the way of things. Those trees had a geometry to them that was beyond finance. They threw shade in the way a hand will. People who are raised under such trees learn their hours by the dropping of fruit.

Ben touched the trunk of one near him. The bark was scarred with a hatchet mark they'd made to mark the day the old man had died. You can't hold everything, Ben said. He folded his hands and then unfolded them like a man making a new thought. The taxman'll come for the mortgage on the rest. If you hold only the east forty it'll be a patch. It'll be the kind of life they can buy for a song. And the bank'll prefer a sale to a stew of people who owe. I mean think. You'd be done with summer nights of listening for pumps that won't start. You could have a house with a yard and a woman who wouldn't have to watch fruit rot. He did not speak of any woman he thought Tom might wed. He spoke in the abstract.

Tom rubbed his thumb across the scar and felt the ridges of the ring. The old man had not been one for modernity. He had liked to plant Clementines and apricots and he had argued with men in suits. He had believed in seasons and the green law of the root. He had said once in his shrub of a voice You plant what you like but you tend to the debt. Tom could see him in the dirt, sleeves rolled, eyes like the sky at evening. He could see his mouth form names of pests like a priest forming absolution.

If I sell the east forty it won't be the east forty no more, Tom said. The names will change. The rows will be numbered. There'll be surveyors with gaunt white faces. There'll be men with cable and trucks who will put up fences and call it progress. They will call it a subdivision or an orchard or a park. Names change things. Ben said that things change anyway. Tom thought about that. He had thought about it every night as cicadas sang and the moon set in the valley like a bruise. He had woken him out of the bed at two in the morning to go tend a pump or to snake a line around a broken emitter. He had learned the orchard in his flesh.

They worked through the day with that speech broken into small pieces. The sun moved west and the light grew linen and thin. The soil baked like bread. They cut away the bad piece and fitted in new plastic. They bound it with tape and then with sealant and then they buried it as if burying an old wrong. When they had finished Tom took the handle of a spade and filled the trench with dirt. The water turned on and at first nothing came and then a small sound like a hidden animal ran and the drip emitters took up the story and the leaves sighed with a sound like relief. Water is a believer. It believes in its own flowing. The trees drank and their leaves reddened and they hung their heads like men taking the cup.

Ben lit a cigarette and watched the steam of the repair. He had a look in his eye of a man who had seen a settlement and found it wanting. You see, Ben said. You see it works. You can keep the thing that matters.

Tom spat in the dust and smiled in a bare way. You could sell, he said. But even at that price you'd be buying what you cannot buy back. He said it slow, as if tasting the words. There are some things the bank cannot understand. You can't mortgage a song. He looked at Ben with a dry humor.

That means nothing to a man who's got bills, Ben said. He shrugged and his cigarette hung in his lip like a miniature mast. He looked at the orchard in a particular way. He had been stung by city living. He had loved the cheap and quick pleasures of other places—the theater, the little bars, the quiet dark of apartments. He had also learned the appetite of men with cash and how it would come like fog. They were men with an eye for packing an escheat; they'd turn this place into lines of houses and a pool and call it convenience.

Tom's mouth became thin. You're not the bank's mouthpiece, he said. He had the patience of a man who had waited out many things.

It's not about being the bank's mouthpiece, Ben said, not really. It's about surviving. It's about making a way so the rest of us can live. He pushed his cigarette into the dirt and crushed it with the heel of his boot. You can keep the trees until the trees are only old bones. Or you can do something now. The town's paying good. They want the land. It's a hard wind. You can stand and let it blow your house down or you can put it in the hands of a man who'll pay you and you won't have to listen to it anymore.

They fell to remembering, as men will when the day is long and the argument grows thin. They spoke of the old man, of the way he had stood with a glass of sweet wine and pressed his thumb into a peach to find its give. They remembered the last winter when the frost took half the crop and the old man had sat on the back stoop and not said a thing for a long time. He had been the sort of man who could not be bargained with, who loved the dirt as if it were something holy. He would tell them then that fruit was not merely sweetness but the result of long seasons and careful theft of the earth's patience.

Another time they spoke of the neighbor man who had sold and then found his money had given him new debts. He had bought a big truck and a new wife and a house with a trim lawn. He had phoned them once with the news of his daughter's first steps and then not called for months. The idea that money might buy memory as if memory were a commodity had the strangeness of a dream. Ben argued that the memory was not on full display but in the body's knowledge. You can carry the orchard in your hands, he said. You can bring it into the town. Tom listened and felt the pull of the town like an itch.

There is a man in the city who buys land, Ben said at another point. He buys it to put a name to it. He owns a piece of land and then he goes and plants flags. It's not only houses. He buys orchards because people think they will get a piece of the good life. He wants us to sign papers and we will sign them because we think of money as a cure for all that ails. But there's a cost. The cost is what you can't write down. He looked at Tom and his face had a softness that came from knowing the world can be cut down into neat sizes.

Tom sat on the tailgate and watched the trees. He had a beer sweating in his hand and the black label was a small thing like a promise. He reached up and snapped a leaf between finger and thumb. The underside of the leaf was a pale silver. It made a small sound. You can sell, Tom said. You can sell and you can go to a place where the gutters are clean and they sell an hour of peace in a tin. You'll be a man who has bought out. But the land is the land and it will not let itself be bought clean. You can buy it and then find you have bought a hunger instead.

Ben shook his head in a way that made his hair fall in his eyes. The town's full of hunger too, he said. It just has a different name. You can go to sleep in the land, Tom, and think that means a life. But it's often just waiting. There's a lot of waiting.

They drank until the sun had taken on the shape of old coin. They finished the splice and laid the dirt back. The water ran and the trees drank. It is a small miracle when water moves where it is meant to. The orchard answered in a thousand small gestures. The leaves folded and then unfurled. The small birds came and drank from the drips as if they had always known the time to come. The light fell through leaves and made them saints.

At some point the talk diminished and they walked the lines to see that the water had reached the furthest trees. There were trees that had not been made to survive the last few summers. Some trunks were hollow as a man's jaw and some limbs hung like old arms. Tom had pruned what he could and tied with cloth. He had taken from young trees and grafted into the old ones. A man with the old man's patience could make a kind of life.

I might sell, Tom said in the end. He did not make it sound like surrender. Maybe we split it. Maybe we cut off what the bank wants and keep what is ours. Ben stopped walking. He looked at him and an answer came like a wave breaking. He nodded. I can talk to them, he said. I can see what they will do. It's better you don't if you can help it. He said it with a new currency of care.

Neither of them liked the idea of the town people with their polite hands and their planters full of city roses. Neither of them liked the sharpness of the deals. But some things have to be given over to stop the slow attrition. Tom felt the truth of that like hunger. You don't keep every tree, he said. You only keep what you can feed.

Ben said he would go into town in the morning. He would meet with the man from 17 and see how the numbers went. He would take tea and talk in a square room with other men who like the smell of paper. He would come back with a story. Sometimes the story would be cold. Sometimes it would be a light. Men tell stories to put the world into pieces. Tom listened to that and felt nothing but the weight of his father's thumb in the earth.

They sat on the back of the pickup as the sun went toward the hills and the air grew a more forgiving thing. The cottonwoods chewed their own wind and the sound was like a low argument. Crickets began. There came the smell of cooling earth and the faint rot of overripe fruit. Somewhere in the east a truck went by and its noise faded like a small animal.

Ben took a bottle from his pocket. He offered it. Tom took it and drank. There's an old man in the diner on Main who says this country will be turned to paved things yet. We both laughed because it was both a joke and not one. We got no choice in the matter sometimes. He looked at Tom. We'll do this right, he said. He laying his hand on the truck's tailgate like a benediction.

Tom thought of the old man bent over a graft. He thought of how the old man had taught them to measure the depth of an incision with the care of a surgeon and to place the graft in the crook of life that will take. He had told them once the world isn't just to be endured but to be tended. Tom thought of that. He thought of the orchard as a project of love and of grief.

If I sell half and keep the east forty, he said, will you look after the east forty with me?

Ben looked long at him. He thought of his life beyond the ridge and the small bright things of the town. There was a girl he had loved in a sort of half life and a job with a man who taught him the language of deals. He had come back because he had a restlessness that the city could not satisfy and the land's smell had called him in shorter and shorter breaths. He touched Tom's hand and the callus was warm.

Yeah, he said. We'll do it like that. He sounded younger suddenly. He looked at the trees as if he saw them new. He reached toward one and rubbed the bark. The night fell like a curtain. The first star came like a pin. We can keep the oldest trees, he said. We can keep the house. We can keep the pump and put our name to it and say this is ours. He found a sentence of comfort and set it like an offering.

Tom let the sound wash over him. It was not a promise sealed. It was a truce. It was a plan like an old fence: repairable maybe, maybe not, but standing for a season. He had never thought of the orchard as only dollars. He had thought of it as a covenant. But covenant is sometimes a kind of pact with necessity. He took the bottle again and drank.

They slept in the old house that night with windows open. The mattress was the kind that remembered their shoulders. The air came in smelling of peaches and the small ferment of the last season. In the dark Tom listened to Ben's even breath and thought that perhaps the land would give them what they could still coax. He dreamed of the old man standing with his hands in the dirt and of a season where the trees would be heavy and men with suntanned faces would come to pick and call the fruit a good thing.

In the morning Ben left for the town. He had the look of a man who must bridge two things. He drove the road with a small knot in his chest and a list in his head. He would talk to men with paper and they would speak numbers. He would return with farrago and perhaps a means. The world of paper and money had its own weather and Ben was good at reading the clouds.

Tom stayed and walked the lines with a cane that was mostly for mood. He pruned a limb and set a tape. He mended a gate hinge that had rusted. He did the ordinary things like a man refusing to will the world into being in one stroke. Each gesture was small and it gathered. Old men like him learn to be patient with the slow gathering of small things into a life.

The afternoon came full and white. The pump thrummed and the drips kept time. A hawk circled like a coin in the sky. The land around the orchard was not the same as it had been. The hedgerows had thinned and the creek had a smaller voice. The neighbors had sold off parcels. A new road had been laid in a straight line like a rule. The town moved and the orchard stayed as if clapboard and stone and roots could argue against progress. It did not argue loud.

Ben returned at twilight. He had a number and an offer and a face that had been seasoned. It was what he had predicted and perhaps it was less than either of them had hoped. He sat in the kitchen and told him in terms of assets and deeds and a clause here and a holdback there. The words had a geometry. Tom listened like a man hearing the growl of a beast that will not be named.

There were pauses. There were words like inlets. They breathed into the spaces. Ben told him they'd take the west parcels first and then the rest in sequence. They'd keep the oldest row as a sentimental tract and pay for the rest in one sum that would make both of them think of ease. But deeds would require lawyers and the lawyers wanted a course that would give them what they wanted—what the bank wanted too. Tom looked at the arch of his wife's photograph on the mantle, sun faded and smiling. He thought of the old man's laugh when a graft took and how once the old man had danced at a fair and lost a tooth on a horse. The old man's life was a catalog. His hands had been olive colored in the dusk.

Finally Tom spoke. You be partner in this, he said. You be the one to look after the money and the papers. Ben's smile was a small boat setting sail. He would do it, he said. He would make sure the east forty stayed with them. He would get the papers drawn. He would see to it that the old trees were protected. And if the bank calls in some debt then they'll be calls to make. We'll answer them. We'll answer them with a pen.

The pen is a strange god, Tom said. He liked Ben's willingness. He had hoped. He also thought of the way paper will change a man and the way a ledger will make strangers of brothers. He had on his hands the salt of the orchard and he felt the weight of the years like an armor. He told Ben where the old man's notebooks were and where the deeds would be lodged. Ben put a smoke in his lip and nodded with a businessman's solemnity.

At night they went out between the trees. A half moon lay like a coin. The orchard was a dark cathedral. Tom's hands brushed the bark like a hand on a prayer book. Ben walked close. For a while they did not speak; the silence was an arm around them both. There was a hum in the leaves like a far congregation. The world had changed but the trees kept their old knowledge.

At last Ben said, I don't think selling is the end. It might be a way. He laughed because he supposed that was his best consolation. And there are things you cannot save and yet you do right by what you can. He had the face of a man who believed in a bargain. He had, after all, come to the place where men make choices.

Tom looked at the sky and he said, The land will remember who we were.

It was not a plea but a note like a stone. Ben took it in and folded it into his own chest. Both of them knew it wasn't for the land to judge them so much as for themselves.

They sat then on the back step and the farm lights warmed the porch where the old man's boots were placed as if waiting. The smell of the fruit was the smell of a long story. The land would go on. It might be pruned or parceled. It might be deprived of its own prophet's voice. It might be broken and sold in pieces. But for the moment it drank and the water sang through its veins. For the moment they had made a repair and the hose would not leak. For the moment, that would be enough.

In the morning Ben would go and make the deals or fail them and come back. They would sign papers or refuse. The future was a ledger and also a prayer. They would choose. Perhaps they would keep what they could and sell what they could not tend. Perhaps they would watch the town move close like a tide. Perhaps the orchard would be turned into something else and the old trees would be left as altars. There was no clean answer.

Tom rose with the sun and the orchard took his shadow up like a long companion. He walked the rows and their hiss was like breath. The day was June and it would be long and uncaring and yet generous in its way. The two brothers had found a way to live within the argument for now. The splice was made. The water flowed. The money had not yet changed hands. The orchard stood in that common hour like two men at prayer: not quite finished, not quite absolved, tending the earth because it is what they were given to do.

They would make their bargains under the same sky and the sky would be unbothered. The orchard would take what was given to it and do what it had always done. And in the slow breathing between the rows the names of their dead relatives would rise and settle again like birds. Whatever else would come, that small liturgy of tending and repair would be theirs for a while.
