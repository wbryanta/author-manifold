When I drove up to the Parkmont six years after the fire it did not occur to me at once that the building was the same building. There was a doorman in a cap and a brass button that caught the sun, and there was a bellman who moved with the practiced economy of a man whose job is to save other people's exits, and there was a line of polished cars that would not have looked out of place along the front of any hotel that is used to its own photograph being in the paper. The Parkmont now had a façade of cream stucco and a band of glass on the ground floor that reflected the sky in a way that made the whole block look more expensive than it had been. In the doorway a woman in what might have been called an evening coat smiled at me with an ease that assumed either recognition or pretense, and because the weather was perfect and because the car behind me had a license plate from a state I did not care to know, I told her my name and handed her the envelope that had been pressed upon me.

The envelope had been embossed. It had "MRS. ELEANOR CARVER" in a script that I recognized at once because it was the same script they used on the menus at the club where her sister had always sat at the back table near the window. It was the same script they used, when one came from the right address, on wedding invitations and on the cards announcing dining rooms and when one wanted to buy a place in the warm rooms of dinner conversation. The envelope was heavy and warm in my hand and the woman at the door said, "Congratulations," in a voice that had been taught alongside a certain kind of cutlery, and when I told her that I would like to keep my coat she did not seem surprised. Weddings, she said, had been authorized by the management; we would be seated in the east wing; the ceremony would begin at five; champagne would follow.

They had given me a table in the east wing on purpose. I do not say this as accusation. I say it because it is the sort of arrangement that is self-evident to people of a certain education and conscience. The east wing had been, before the fire, the smallest wing of the Parkmont, narrow corridors and low doors and rooms so small that maids learned to clean them with a single sweep of the broom. The east wing had been where the summer teachers stayed, where a woman with a book would sit on a suitcase and read while the elevator took her up to the floor where the other people who came to read while on holiday were staying. After the fire—after everything—the east wing had been chosen for its intimacy and because, as someone said to me that evening, "it seems more suited to family," as if family were a décor.

I had been a chambermaid in this hotel when it burned. I had, in the parlance of those years and of the papers, "worked her way through" in a job that required a uniform and an apron and a key that jingled like coins and a cart that had been given to me by a woman who had died before she could teach me how to fold a fitted sheet so it lay flat. I was, at the time, twenty-two, and there are things one is permitted to be at twenty-two—practical, heartless, brave in fits and starts. I had a way of making the bed that accommodated sheet and pillow and the invisible standard of the inspector who walked through the halls at noon on weekdays. I knew where the spare slippers were kept and how to remove the toothpaste flakes left in the sink by men who had traveled for business and who would not admit that hotels made them sentimental. I had lists in my head. I had a route that made sense of dust and silk and the way a room's temperature changed when the afternoon sun crossed the windows. I moved through the hotel the way an animal moves through a landscape it knows; the hotel's plan was in me.

The fire began on a Saturday in late October, and it began, according to the police report and the press and the woman at the register who used the word "terrible" in a tone of congenital decency, in a linen closet on the third floor. We were short-staffed that weekend because it was between seasons, and the house manager had gone home because his wife had had the baby, which he announced to us in the hallway with a kind of relief—the sort of relief that announces a withdrawal from obligation. I remember that I had turned the key to Room 317 and that a man had stepped out of the tub with his hair in a towel and had said to me, "Leave the towels, dear, I'll pick them up later" and I had because I had been trained to do as asked. I remember the smell before I saw the smoke—oil and char, an arrested sweetness that suggested cake left in the oven too long. I remember the first bell; I am not sure whether it was our bell or the hotel alarm, but I remember the sound of someone shouting "Fire!" in a corridor and the way the men who worked in maintenance suddenly had a ratio of muscle to motion that was startling.

There are certain things one thinks one will remember forever and certain things one knows one never will. I have, through the years, been asked more than once by people who wanted a story to tell in other people's houses to "recite the scene" as if memory were a dramatic reading, as if a neat roll call of the dead would make the rest of us feel better. I will not do that. I will say what I know: the fire moved fast in the way that flame does when it has the proper fuel; the door to the linen closet had been propped for ventilation; the oxygen rushed in and the dry cotton made astonishing heat. I was not the first in the corridor to notice. I began to shout—"Get out, get out"—and then there were other shouts. The elevators stopped; the sprinklers did not go off in half the floors; smoke filled the stairwell the way water fills a sink. I have had to explain afterward why I did certain things, and explanations are usually an apology in the form of detail. I left a room where a woman had been asleep with a paperback folded in her hand; I left a room where a man had been asleep across the bed with his shoes on; I left an iron in a shawl someone had been pressing; I went with the smoke.

I found Mrs. Carver at the head of a narrow corridor, with her back to the wall, trying to work the radio under her arm as if noise might ward off the noise of smoke. She did not look at me at first. She did not cry or laugh. She called me "dear" when I told her that we had to move. She was the one I carried out on my back because when she tried to walk she staggered as if she were on a boat and people in the hallway were shouting at us to hurry and the bellman had a cart and the cart's wheels made a rhythm that I have never been able to hear without thinking of that night. When we reached the street and the neighbors were looking out of their windows and the firemen had become large presences with hoses and axes and the news vans had arrived and the lights were high and flat and the photographers were taking pictures at an angle that made the building look wavy and glorious, Mrs. Carver took my hand and said, "You saved my life, child," and later, when the police asked me my name, the woman from the paper had it in her notebook and printed it in a column that ran with grainy pictures of the hunched-up building and the candles that people left on the sidewalk.

I am not given to heroism in the abstract. I am, in fact, given to an economy of action that is practical and not theatrical. I will tell you plainly that I did what seemed necessary and that it was not, at the time, a way to change my life. It was, instead, a way to get from one place to another under pressure. Afterward, people called me "heroine" as if the word created an identity card I could carry. Mrs. Carver, who came from a life of receptions and housekeeping that was not imagined as service, wrote to me. She wrote, among other things: "Do not think of yourself as less than you are." She gave me money; she insisted that I take it. She visited the hospital on the second day after the smoke cleared and told me, with a precision that the press later admired, that she remembered a child of sixteen who had once come with her to the Parkmont to stay with a friend and who had always been "so efficient." She told reporters at a breakfast among train schedules and coffees, "If it had not been for that child, I might not have had the strength to do anything."

In the months and years after, the Parkmont was referred to in more ways than one. There were, in the opinion columns and in the society pages, analyses of how the fire had "changed" the downtown, and there were fundraising dinners and a benefit committee made up of people who had come to dinner at the hotel before and who would, in another time, have been mentioned in the same breath as the chairmen of trusts and the women who served punch at gala openings. We received offers of settlement from the insurance company and there were firms who said they would represent me pro bono. The press wanted "the face" of the tragedy and then, for a while, wanted not the face but the scar, and there was a particular photograph published on the front page of the Sunday supplement showing me with a bandage on the inside of my wrist and looking, as the caption put it, "resolute."

There is a conventional arc in such narratives which I will avoid because, if I am candid, I do not find it true to what happened. One does not rise in a straight line out of calamity. The hospital was a ledger: visits from people who called themselves benefactors at first and then who did not call at all; a woman from the Tuesday club who brought a casserole and later sent cards; one good book that someone left on the bedside table with the inscription "For your courage." There was also the matter of work. The hotel did reopen, temporarily, under a different name and with a different manager who had been brought in to "stabilize" the business. They asked me to come back at first as an example—"We would like to show the public we've recovered," the press releases said—and then, because it is cheaper to rehire someone who knows the layout, they asked me to return to a shift. I did, for a time. The rooms had an electrical smell that never entirely left the air. We were careful about the irons and we were careful about the linen closets. People came and went and in the face of the recovery the papers ran features about "comeback cities" and how a good hotel was the index of civic confidence.

Then, in the later winter, I met someone at a dinner someone had arranged and he paid for a table at a small restaurant that had the ambition of being a bistro and which, for the price of dessert, offered the possibility of a life that was not entirely provisional. We married a year later, and we left the city to the suburbs. We bought curtains that followed no code of formality. I went back to school for a certificate in something that would not be used to define me years later. I ceased, in time, to be in the habit of humming the Parkmont's elevator tune and learned, instead, to understand the sound of a lawnmower as a regular thing. It is true that sometimes people who do not live in the city proper succumb to a nostalgia that is cruelty disguised as taste, and I was not immune. But in the end we made a life that had the hard surfaces and the small pleasures that are the usual promise of survival.

When the invitation to Mrs. Carver's daughter's wedding arrived, I was, for reasons that have less to do with intention than the ways in which debts are recorded in social life, asked to attend. I would not call it an invitation in the strict sense. It was, rather, a courtesy. Mrs. Carver insisted. She wrote that she would be delighted to have me "on her table" and we had, over the years, come to the custom of writing to one another about the weather and the fact of our children—Eleanor's daughter, Elizabeth, had been engaged since last spring; my husband's job had a new title—and these were the comfortable facts that sit on the table in polite conversation and serve as napkins for harder matters.

I told my husband, who has a liberal's disposition and who is not given to ceremony, that I would prefer not to go. He suggested that we go early to avoid the speeches. He suggested, in the end, that it would be "right" and that many social obligations are fulfilled for reasons that are not self-defining but useful. He wore the suit that we keep for funerals and official nights. We arrived in the late afternoon and the line of cars at the Parkmont made of the block a procession. There were gardeners straightening the flower beds with a tenderness that seemed disproportionate to the labor and there were placards directing people to registration and to the valet and a tent where the attendants were offering programs and the programs were printed on paper that smelled faintly of cedar.

At the entrance there had been, before the fire, a small fountain with a stone bird that had hairline cracks and where the pigeons liked to sit. The fountain, I was told by a porter who recognized me by sight, had been replaced with an urn. The urn was large and geometric and would have seemed to belong to a museum. It held roses and the roses had been cut so the stems looked like branches. There was a plaque at its base that read, in a font selected to avoid the sentimentality of script, "In Memory of the Victims of the Fire." The plaque did not name people. It named a loss in the passive voice, which is to say it was a form of consolation that used grammar as a bandage.

Inside, the carpets were the color of tea and the chandeliers had been replaced with fixtures that were designed to hide their own weight. The place smelled of a new cleaner applied by men who had been taught to make the sense of cleanliness read as luxury. I do not mean to be damning about such things; there is a place for beauty that is arranged by committees and there is a merit to an establishment that desires to make itself safe again. But the ballroom in which the wedding was to be held had been gutted. The molding I had once polished lay in a crate in a storage room; the bandstand had been raised and then lowered and raised again until the acoustics were right. The walls were painted a color that was trying very hard to be both warm and neutral; it failed in being neither, and this failure had the same effect as a dress that does not fit.

People came up to me in the lobby and said, "How are you?" in a tone that was supposed to be casual and that carried, instead, a measure. There are old injuries that a community keeps as a kind of asset—people speak of them as "shared experiences"—and the Parkmont fire was, for a long time, that asset. Some of the people who had been present at the time had moved away or had become reclusive; others were more interested in the finals of a league. Mrs. Carver, who aged in the particular way women of her class do—quickly on the face and slowly in the carriage—saw me as I was led and crossed the room to me with the ease of someone who has rehearsed a proper display of gratitude. She held me by the arm and said, "There you are. You look marvelous," and "marvelous" is the sort of word that can mean either admiration or abatement, and she meant both. She introduced me to Mrs. Gordon, who had been a neighbor of hers at the time of the fire and who told me she had heard "the most dreadful thing" as if that were news, and to a woman who had been a sponsor of the benefit ball and who had a mouth that accepted speech for a living.

At the table—Eleanor Carver's table—there was a place set for me with a name card that had a typeface which, in books about taste, would have been called "appropriately minimal." I sat between a man who had once been the Post's entertainment editor and who now volunteered on the board of the new arts center and a woman who taught interior design at the women's college and who spoke with the certainty of an instructor. The men at the table were mostly in the habit of speaking of property and of projects. They spoke of the city as if it were a garden plot ripe for the community's attention. The women spoke of fabrics and of the school children who have a "program."

It is remarkable how much of the truth of any given time in a community is revealed by the small talk at a dinner table. People reveal their priorities in the topics they choose to make ephemeral. Conversation that evening moved briskly from the recent mayoral race to the last play that had been offering at the small theater to the problems of the schools, and all of it had a patina of philanthropy that is often used to disguise the desire to be seen to be doing good. I will say also that the table was apparently in a place where the acoustics were favorable to compliments and the compliments came as if they were coins to be spent. "You look well," said someone, and there are ways of saying that which read as gratitude and there are ways of saying that which are an offer to trade grief for dinner.

It was not as if no one spoke of the fire. People did, at certain points. A woman two seats down from me, Mrs. Keating, who had not been there the night of it but whose brother had, began to tell an anecdote about "the little sister of a friend" who had "never quite recovered" and then laughed as if she had given the room permission to be amused at tragedy. People told the story as if it were a local legend, a story that belonged to the block and not to the people who had actually faced it. "Everything has changed since then," someone said. "You'd never know by looking at it now." "Isn't it just extraordinary?" echoed someone else, and the sentences passed among us like a tray of sweets.

During the reception there was a lull, which is to say the band stopped and a man took the microphone to say something about "the dangers of life" and "the strength of community," two words that, when conjoined, become a kind of formal prayer suitable for printing in the next day's supplement. The man who spoke had a face that was good at being earnest; he spoke of the Parkmont as if it were a friend who had been to the hospital and had come home wearing new clothes. He said "We are thankful for how far we've come" and the room clapped. At that moment my husband—who tends to show his emotion in small, unintended betrayals like adjusting his tie—looked at me and said, "You don't need to stand for this." He was right. I did not. I remained seated. I am not given to speeches and when I am given the choice between standing and sitting my body's preference is, generally, to remain where it is. But what I did afterward was not an act of defiance so much as an attention to detail.

A young woman—a guest, a bridesmaid—came over and asked if I would dance. She had the sort of face that belonged to a photograph in a magazine and the more precise trait of a woman who had practiced her expressions for the camera. She called me "Aunt" because the Carvers had been in the habit of extending kinship to important servants until kinship was made to read as formality. She said, "Wouldn't you like to dance?" and I said I would prefer not, and she said, "It's just a little waltz" and I said, "Yes" and we rose. The dance was not long; it lasted three measures and a falter. There were, in my mind, certain rules about dance that I had learned in other people's living rooms where shoes were removed and secrets were spoken in the dark. The waltz ended too soon and the girl smoothed her skirt and asked me where I had learned to dance. I said, "At home" and meant it to be literal.

People watched us in the way of people at occasions. They watch because they want an image to pin into the evening's memory. When I had once been a chambermaid, the only people who saw me dancing were the guests who left their doors open at breakfast and who believed they had a right to observe. Now I was on the right side of observation, which is to say people were seeing me in a way that conferred interest rather than indignity. There is an oddness to being re-placed in sight as an object of benign curiosity. It is, in fact, another form of re-formation—forgive the phrase—where one finds oneself accounted for and re-entered into the ledger of the respectable.

After the dessert—an asymmetrical confection of berries and cream, the sort of dish chosen for its photogenic qualities—the father of the bride made a toast. He spoke of Elizabeth as "our jewel" and of marriage as "the joining of two houses," a phrase that amused people who liked who liked the architecture of metaphor. He mentioned my name in apology and thanks. "To Mrs. Stanley," he said, and named me by my married name in the presence of my husband and our friends, and there was that small, ceremonial applause that is given to the person who has been included in the historical account in correct order. I raised my glass because I had been taught that gestures are forms of attendance. I drank.

Outside, when we left hours later and the night was high and the stars were clear, there was a smell in the air that made me think of smoke and of the old days when the city had a different temper. I am not superstitious, although there are sullen moments when I think that the world accumulates signs and that one should not ignore them. We walked past the urn and I read again the simple line that said "In Memory of the Victims." It was meant, as such things are meant, to settle something. The plaque had a faint saintliness to it, as if what a community needs after disaster is the sanctification of what it has lost.

When I look back on that evening now—months later, weeks at most—I find that what persists is not a single image but a set of small items that sit in my mind like a drawer of mementoes. There is the band breaking into a slow number and the way the saxophone sounded as if it wanted to be solemn but could not entirely. There is the toast that mentioned a "new Parkmont" and the way the words "new" and "old" kept colliding in my thoughts. There is the face of the woman from the Tuesday club who asked me, in a voice that intended generosity, if I had "ever thought of writing my story." There was also, oddly, the smell of the cream carpet when I leaned against the wall for a minute to take off my shoes. It has, as most things do, layers of meaning. The smell was, in the end, simply a smell.

People change the hotels where they have been young and significant and nothing in the end is the same; yet there are also patterns. The Parkmont was at once a physical place and a container for reputations. It was the sort of establishment that could house a wedding and, at the same moment, hold in its files the names of those who once polished the silver. It can be rebuilt with the best materials and the best intentions and still not return to a life it once had, because a life is not only beams and plaster but the daily movements of bodies who do not keep their own hours. When the fire took that hotel's previous life it also scattered a history like confetti and then, later, the city gathered the paper in the way one gathers a bouquet—that is, out of habit, out of the desire to make something wholesome.

I am conscious, when I tell this, of the temptation to moralize. I will resist it, or at least to make my conclusion small and private. The Parkmont, as I left it that night, was good in its way. People danced. Children tucked flowers in their sleeves. Mrs. Carver gave me her hand twice and, beneath it, the skin of her palm had not the warmth of heroism but the warmth of a person who had been touched by survival and had learned to smooth the experience like a tablecloth. My husband and I drove home and did not speak for some time. We had no need to. The engine gave the measured sound that is so unsentimental it reads as love.

Later, in bed, I noticed the small triangle of scar on my wrist. It has, in the light, the color of an old receipt—something to be kept in the wallet of the body. I pressed my thumb to it and felt the slight resistance of time. I thought of the linen closet and of the way a door can be propped open and of the way that a small human decision becomes, in another calculus, a fate. I have, over the years, watched the city renovate, to tidy, to reissue itself as if newness erases suffering. I have watched the people who once were anonymous become noted in the paper and then become anonymous again as habits change. None of this comforts me.

What comfort is left, if any, lies not in the buildings but in the fact that people continue to make marriages and to hope, briefly, that a wedding will be a time when everyone present will be not themselves but the better version of themselves. That evening the guests at the Parkmont were at their best within the allotted period. They did not mean to be cruel. They were simply social animals performing an exquisite animal ritual. It is, finally, the lot of the survivor to watch such performances and to note which parts are real and which parts are ornament. I take no pleasure in being a critic.

If anyone asks me now whether I would go again, I will say yes, though with the small reservation I have always carried: that once you have taken a measure of a place by the way fire can move through it, you will always notice the undertow of materials and the way they are chosen. I go because Mrs. Carver still puts her hand on mine and calls me a name that belongs to the past and the present at once. I go because weddings are invented to do just what the Parkmont has always attempted—create a new narrative out of a pile of years. And I go because, in the end, the rooms of the world are always issuing out invitations and we must decide, at each point, whether to take the envelope and to sit.
