The theodolite liked being where the world had narrowed into a figure you could point at and name. I set its tripod feet into silt the color of old tea and thought of all the things I had once believed the three-legged legs of a stool could do: hold a stove, steady a sewing machine, steady a child's weight while she swung her feet. The instrument's bubble sat level, and the world, for a moment, agreed to be certain. The county license plate on the truck read GRAHAM, and the emblem on my chest pocket — a stitched compass rose, the county seal — reminded me of the things I had been taught to trust: grids, baselines, meridians. Those are things that live without stories, or so I had always told myself. Today the stories were louder than any baseline I could establish.

They had said it would be low, this reservoir, because of the drought; they had said it would be interesting for the historical commission, that its exposure might offer new data on submerged structures, that salvage yards and salvage-hungry people might appear, that artists might come and that vandals too, and that I'd be in demand for legal descriptions before somebody else wrote a map and a right-of-way around it. They had said it because government people are correct in proportions: they heap reasoning on top of facts until the facts become tidy.

This was the lowest level in sixty years, the press release said, and someone's photograph of chimney stacks rising from glassy water had been printed in the paper last week, and neighbors called me to say the town I'd been born in had returned like a bruise being turned over.

Holloway was a name most people in the county pronounced with a crispness that made it sound tidy and dead. To me it was a place with dust still under its nails when it drowned, a place that had smelled of yeast in summers and of woodsmoke and coal in winters, a place with a schoolyard whose cracked asphalt had holes we used for marbles. I had seen its foundations poking from the water twice more since the dam was sealed — once as a child and once in a photograph my father kept tucked behind a ledger — but the first look had been the fiercest. I remembered tin sunlight on a plastic rowboat, the smell of varnish and fish, my father's hands, and a small hand gripping a battered wooden rail and then the sensation of being set down on cold concrete at the top of a stoop that wanted to spill into water.

Setting up was almost ritual. I ran my hand along the tripod leg, felt the grit, the tiny shells of whatever had once been a river's mouth. A heron watched from a mucky reed; it was like all the birds in the county had been deputized to keep an eye on the flood. The truck behind me sat with its door open and a notebook in the passenger seat where I had scrawled tide times and my own coordinates. I clipped my hat to a peg then realized I had been holding my breath for the last thirty seconds and let it go all at once. The air smelled of rot and something sweeter, as though the sun were warming a field of elderberries left too long on a vine.

What a town looked like when it was submerged depended on what you wanted it to be. To preservationists it was archaeology; to property assessors it was litigation; to folk songs it was a cautionary tale about progress. I was a county surveyor, which means I had to measure other people’s certainties and make them legible to courts and commissioners. But being a county surveyor also meant I could stand somewhere like Main Street and give a number to the shape of what was beneath my boots.

I took a bearing on a rusted manhole cover standing like a coin at the center of a circulation that no longer circulated, and the line it made on my screen — a simple vector from this point to that — kept reminding me that measurement is somewhat like naming: it doesn't change the thing but it gives it place in an argument. This is a corner; this is an eastward-facing foot of a foundation; this is a parcel line. My screen blinked like an eye.

I had last seen these foundations in the warm year when I was eight. My father had taken the boat out that day, and I had sat on an upturned crate with my knees tucked, and as the water lapped it had left strange halos of algae and pale paint where porch thresholds used to be. We climbed a stoop — I'm sure there were laws against it, sure there were all sorts of grown-up reasons not to — and I had pressed my palm into something cold and rough and left a handprint there beside his. He had leaned down and traced my fingers with one of his callused fingers and said, "We'll come back to this when you're older."

We hadn't come back. He died a year later, of something that made the hospital smell like lemon oil and cotton, which I had never liked the smell of since. My mother had moved us into a trailer on the ridge. The town dissolved in storytelling then. It was not interesting to politics anymore; it was a hole that became a lake for the county and a story to tell visitors.

So that was why I was here at seven in the morning, my yellow prism pole like a stake of some kind of small claim. The low water had made main possible again — not the busy throng of people, not the old hardware store, but the geometry of it: curbs, storm drains, a long line of rooftops' shadows in the silt. Each street held a memory like a coin under glass. I stepped with the caution of someone who had learned how fragile old things are; the silt gave way in places with the whisper of wet flour.

The survey is a business of method. I walked the line, recorded each corner, let the laser throw its neat thin line, and the device translated those lines into numbers I would carry back into a courtroom or an archive. Legal descriptions like "Commencing at the NW corner of Lot 4" have the air of absolutes, but standing on a street that had once held a bakery, a barbershop, a line of children holding hands, I felt the strangeness of absolutes. That day the numbers were my way of listening. Each coordinate was a question I could ask and the ground could answer after a fashion.

Walking made memory come in shivers. The church foundation was easy to find: a rectangle of foundation with a small mound where the altar had been, the stone ring of its steps showing pale mortar like teeth. I put my tripod on what had been the sidewalk and took a shot down the nave. The signal beeped obediently. A gull wheeled low, hungry. In my mind there was a Sunday in 1980, a dress with embroidered daisies, my grandmother squeezing my hand until I could feel the bones in it like gravel. The preacher's voice thundered through the rafters where water now reached to within a foot of the bell. The bell's clapper had been cut and saved for someone who said it would one day be returned to the town if the town ever came back. I wondered where that clapper had gone.

There are certain places the water leaves less forgiving than others. The school yard had a barefoot geometry I remembered: a square patch of asphalt, a faded painted hopscotch, a tree whose roots had lifted the pavement by the boys' locker room. Now there was a foundation kept unsettlingly honest by exposed rebar. I stood on a place where a boy named Tommy had once taught me to whistle by arranging stones in my mouth and blowing. Tommy had drowned in some other way years later; he died in a different town after we had been scattered. If I had to hold an argument between grief and duty, I would say duty generally won, but here, at Holloway School's concrete flank, the argument was useless. The teacher's desk was only memory; the desk's place was a coordinate.

I placed a peg into what had been the teacher's lot and remarked my GPS for the record. The instrument's noise took the edge from my voice. There were no children now, only the wind in the reeds chewing at the abandoned playground equipment. A swing hung from a frame like a tongue. In another life that swing had moved with the smallness of the things that make up joy. I tested it with my foot and it made a small unhappy creak. My name was not written on any of the concrete in the schoolyard in a way that was legible anymore, but my memory had a marker carved into a bench by the gymnasium: MJ underlined, a simple childish arrogance. I put my palm where I thought it might have been and found algae instead. It didn't fit quite right in my hand.

People came to the exposed town in small auto-routes of need. An elderly man rowed in earlier that morning and wore a hat like a boat, brim frayed. He stood where I was setting up and watched me work. He called himself Elias, which is the sort of name for a man who has always expected to have to travel to be remembered. He had been born in Holloway the same year the dam was planned, he said, and his family had been one of those that left with bitterness measured in sacks and small comforts.

"You making a map?" he asked. His voice was sandpaper and coffee. His eyes held a surprise, like someone seeing an old friend dressed in clothes they had never seen them in.

"I am," I said. "Graham County. Historical commission asked for a formal survey of what's left exposed. We're documenting the foundations and streets for our records."

He looked at me with the slow appraisal people give those born to a place. "Born here?"

"I was," I said. "My mother said they moved us when the trailers were still new."

His mouth made a small smile. He tilted his head toward a row of stones that used to be the main block. "I used to sell soda there, when I was a boy," he said. "Better soda than they make now." He said it like a blessing.

We fell into the kind of conversation that forms around places where memory is newly accessible. Elias had been a man who came every spring to remove a plank here, a bolt there. He had tried to salvage a porch step once and had found, beneath, a box of tin buttons which his daughter scraped clean and wore at her wedding. He told me where the bakery had once smelt of sugar and where the undertaker's had its discreet back door. His stories were the way people stitch themselves back together: with names and small forgotten facts.

"What are you hoping to find?" he asked finally, like a test.

"A survey," I said. It was what my mother would have said if she had been asked. It was true enough. But I added, "And to see."

"That's enough," he said. His hand brushed the side of a foundation where lichen made a map of its own. "If it's you, it's enough."

Sometimes people are kind in ways that irritate you because they are simpler than you fear. Elias rowed away in the late morning and left me to my grids. I walked down what had been Main Street in measured progress, and each foundation held a memory like a pebble in a sock: the barbershop had a razor that caught my finger once; the hardware store sold rope and sugar and the way my father drank coffee black; the post office had a man who stamped envelopes with a practiced elbow.

The house where my grandparents had lived, where my mother had shown me how to shell peas, where my father had absentmindedly carved the edge of a doorframe with his initials — that house was a little off from where the deed said it should be. Deeds have a habit of being honest about lines. This difference in physical placement allowed my mind to wander the strange territory of who owns memory. I found the foundation by aligning three bearing points: a crack in the sidewalk, a cistern cap, and a little low wall that may have been the base of a mailbox. There it was, squat and lichen-ridged, the rectangle of what had once been two rooms with a porch between them. The porch's stone step was still there, an uneven platform with a notch in it.

I crouched and put a hand on that notch. It was rough with the texture of compressed sand and the small grit of the stream that had run into a dam. My fingers fit into something like recognition. There had been a coin there once, I remembered now, the way a child's memory picks up glints. A coin my father had hidden in the joint when he was teaching me about treasure. He would say, "A map is nothing without a treasure turn," like something in a child's book. I began to scrape, for reasons equally childish and practical, with the edge of my pocketknife.

It came loose like a small argument yielding. Not a coin but a rounded thing, a marble of cloudy green, worn at one edge where it had rolled against some other grit for years. I had not known what I was expecting. I expected a photograph, maybe, or a letter, an item which could have owned more of a story than a marble. Instead this small globe sat in my palm and caught the light like a small island or a planet. My fingers remembered that a marble rolled in a certain adolescent way, like the roll of a story you tell someone to see if they'll catch the weight.

"Thought you'd lost that," my father had said once, but that voice was a memory now.

I wrapped the marble in a scrap of my shirt and tucked it in the inside pocket of my jacket. There are certain private things you carry back into yourself when you are alone and you do not tell anyone. A marble can be such an object — a piece of childhood sealed by silt. I still had the map to finish, and the instruments wanted attention as if they were jealous of my nostalgia.

The sun moved across the pond-black flat and made certain places luminous. The old hotel — no more than a foundation wearing its own disgrace — had a kitchen's ghost aura where broken tile hinted at a once- busy room. I recorded the corner, the hearth, the cistern line. The coordinates printed like final words. Each time I heard the little voice in my head say a number and record it, I imagined filing a name in a drawer that couldn't forget how to be a drawer.

They had moved the cemetery when they flooded the valley, the county pamphlet said. They had lifted stones in the night, said to have sometimes misread names, sometimes mislaid a latch. The truth is never neat. I had heard a story from a woman in the clerk's office about a row of small stones being discovered somewhere on the new Gentry Ridge — stones that had names with the wrong years, names rearranged like loose buttons. I had wondered what happens to things people bury when the ground rises to a man's tooth.

Now the exposed land had, in parts, kept its graves like knapped, poorly arranged teeth. A small lot with a low wall had a headstone that refused to give way to the silt. My instrument could not weigh grief. I walked that lot with a different kind of reverence, one with shoes that had seen too many county buildings. The headstone that stuck out had been turned to the wrong sun and was half buried, but scrubbed without ceremony by water and time. The name on the stone was one I recognized — not my family, but a neighbor's: the same name as the woman who'd taught me to bake biscuits at a fair. My mouth made the syllable, and it was like opening a letter you had known the existence of. The stone's inscription was weathered but there were still letters and dates. I traced them with my fingertips because my equipment did not deserve the intimacy that touch could give.

There is a distinct sound when silt collapses in on itself, a muffled sigh. I heard it as I walked to the town's far edge where a channel had once taken water out to the river. The river itself was like a folded-up map; its path now sat stuffed with reeds and lichens and the bones of summer. At the last place where the road bent into the water, the foundation of the steeple remained as a stubborn speech. It was a stub of stone and metal. I could see how the two things — geometry and ruin — brushed each other. I thought of the preacher's hymn and of my father once pointing to the horizon and making a comment about the way a place knits itself into the body of a person.

When you have been trained to make things exact, you learn how to stand with one instrument and write the things of the past into a file people can open and xerox. My job was not simply to make a map for historians or for developers who might someday argue that certain parcels were now accessible. It was something subtler: I was to map the boundary between what we call land and what the county calls asset, and to specify exactly where one became the other. The moral complexity of the work did not belong between my tripod legs. My father had been a man who liked the hiding and yet the telling of things; he had drawn maps in the margins of his pay stubs and then destroyed those stubs years later. He had been practical. I had learned to be practical.

A day like the one I had is a day of small discoveries. I found a child's shoe — one half of a pair, leather still attached to a sole that had once been white — leaning against a foundation wall as if someone had set it there to remind the present. I found a broken radio with its dials melted but somehow still handsome. I found the rusted handle of a bicycle, the kind made for boys with ambition and no sense of cost. Each artifact was a possibility and an accusation.

By noon the sun had grown sharp and the mosquitoes, which had been discreet in the morning, came out in little diligent armies. I stopped for lunch on the stoop of what had been the general store and drank the tepid coffee from a thermos, and the thermos warmed my palms the way a small kindness will. People had left their elastic bands in the drawers of memory; towns are like that. There's always more to unfasten. My county radio crackled with a base of interference; someone asked about tarp procurement and another man asked to be updated on whether salvage crews had started work. I felt as though we were cataloguing the bones of the earth itself and had to do it with a calm voice.

It was late afternoon when the thing I had not set out to find found me. I had been walking a line, my tripod remembering steps like a hinge, and I came upon a doorway or, what remained of one. It was my house's threshold, not merely the address but the place my mother had said goodbye to childhood: the iron slug of the doorstep with a groove worn by decades of feet. The city gave it coordinates; my fingers gave it meaning.

I stooped down and the marble in my pocket chimed against my ribs like a small bell. The edge of the threshold had a smallness of ordinary damage, the kind that only people who walked thought to notice. And there, caught in a crack like a last breath, was a small piece of paper, wholly impossible and wholly reasonable for its smallness: a postage stamp softened into a square along whose edges silt had pressed itself into the paper. I took it because the country of my childhood believed in small things; my father had taught me to take little things when no one saw, or at least to note their existence. I brushed it with the tip of a finger. The ink had mostly gone but there was a portrait face, the kind you see on stamps, and the serrated border remained, a fragment of other people's journeys.

I realized, as I cupped things in my hands, that grief and curiosity were not simple enemies. They could coexist like the instrument and the instrument's case. They could be placed near each other and be useful.

The sun leaned toward the cottonwoods and the shadows lengthened. I set up one last station on a knoll by the old post office where the town had once convened to get news. The theodolite beeped confirmation and a faint wind came through the reeds which moved like a congregation. It felt fitting to watch the last line print and seal the day's work like a closing of a notebook. I had the coordinates, the bearings, a set of photos and the messy list of artifacts I had inventoried: a marble, the shoe, bits of porcelain and a scrap of phonograph needle. The data would be typed up and entered into a system that liked tidy files. The historical commission would make decisions about preservation tags and restricted access. Salvagers would petition for a license. Artists would come with cameras and paint. Somebody would publish a short article and in it Holloway would be called both a relic and a lesson.

I took photographs for the record, as a good surveyor does, and then two more, because sometimes even professionals are eccentric. One of those photographs was of the little notch in the threshold where the marble had been hidden. I did not take the same satisfaction as a thief might; this was something different: an acknowledgement that the place had been there for me and I had been old enough to remember and small enough to still feel the taste of pennies in my mouth.

I have a habit, when I finish a piece of fieldwork, of clearing my instrument with the same pattern of gestures: legs up, bubble checked, lens cap on, scars counted. The ritual is a prayer for accuracy. I carried the total station out to the truck, set its case like a sleeping animal in the back, and placed the marble in the palm of my hand for a long moment before tucking it back into the inside pocket. The marble is not much in the ledger of things; it might be dismissed as a child's junk. But the marble is mine to remember. It will sit in a drawer at the office on my desk in a small glass jar with other found things: a screw, a beer cap, a lady's button with a rose pattern, and the stamp. There is a bureaucratic need for me to keep things properly filed; there is also the human need to keep things close.

As I drove the county truck up the narrow ridge road that overlooked the expanse of the exposed lakebed, I thought about how water takes and keeps. Some things never entirely drown — memory among them. You could lose a town to a dam as you can lose a mark on your body to time, but the contours remain for someone who knows how to look. We put lines on paper and sign them and call them final. But a map is always a statement subject to revision, a truth that will be challenged by weather and human need and drought. I put one foot on the gravel of the county road and felt the earth's small compliance.

At the top the overlook had a placard for the reservoir they had put up the year after the dam was completed. It spoke of progress and power and the thirst of cities. People read the sign and then walked away, as if the writing would keep them harmless. I looked at the water which now sat as a small ribbon east of the ruined main, and it reflected a strip of sky like a thin hard mouth. Boats passed in the distance, tiny as insects, the kind of leisure we grant ourselves when the earth is not furious.

I left Holloway with the knowledge that the map I'd made was only one map. There were maps you make for deeds and maps you make for history and maps you make for the heart. They are not the same and each claims to be truest. I would do my legal descriptions, write the numbers, enter the data into the county server, sign the forms. I would annotate with a few personal notes, because some part of me had been trained in that peculiar and human art that is both holding a pen and remembering to breathe. I would photocopy the plans and send them to the historical commission. I would send a notice to salvage crews telling them about rights and responsibilities. I would write "Holloway — field work complete" on the header and then, perhaps, I would go home and take the marble out of my pocket and turn it in my palm and think about the day I had stood on a threshold.

The drought would not last forever. Rain would come, perhaps, months or years from now. The water would slowly cover the streets again. It would fill the gutters, pull at the foundations, lap at the porch steps, hide the marble and the shoe and the radio and every small thing people had left. New children might row to the submerged chimneys and spy box-like reminders and think them like islands or excellent places to throw stones. That is the way of things. We measure the ephemeral because we must, but we do not pretend we own permanence.

I drove down the ridge with the county radio quiet in its socket and the marble like a tiny planet against my heart. Elias's boat was still visible near the far reed line, a small person making small arcs in a big thing, content perhaps in his ritual of salvage and memory. I thought of the work I had done, the lines I had drawn, the instruments I had trusted. I thought of my father's hands, and how they had been steady in small ways. The world is a thing you can sometimes make legible; even when it is drowned, there are seams you can pry open and hold up to the light.

At the county office I recorded the data with the meticulous patience the job demands, and then, after the forms were signed and the files uploaded, I went to the little cabinet where the things I considered unsanctioned were kept. In the drawer I placed the marble next to a photograph of the school we had once taken on a picnic day, a photograph whose faces had become less sharp with the years. I closed the drawer and the lock made a soft finality.

That night I dreamed of the town under water, but in the dream the water was clear and bright and I could see the roofs like the backs of sleeping animals. I walked through that town and my footsteps made ripples that moved with the small noise of pebbles knocking. I woke with the taste of river in my mouth and the certainty that the marble was there and would remain there in the little glass jar on my desk, a small geometry of memory.

Some days later, after the paperwork cooled and the county had what it needed in tracked files, a representative from the historical commission called to thank me. "We can't bring the town back," she said, as if I had expected such power. "But we can keep its shape."

"That's all any of us get," I replied. I could hear the small seriousness in her voice, the one that people use when touching the past. "Shapes are better than erasure."

"Good work," she said, and the phone clicked off like a small concession to what had been done that day.

I have since mapped other things: property lines by sleepy creeks, the new subdivision whose lots fit into a market like teeth into a jaw, the easements for windmills nobody asked for five years ago. Each map is an act of faith. But there are maps that once in a while pull you like a rope. Holloway's open streets, its mud-sour breath, the marble in my pocket, the foundation notch where I had pressed a child's hand into stone — these were ropes I could not ignore.

At my desk I roll out the final print and study the lines. The drawn lines do not capture the smell of fish, the texture of silt, the precise way the wind moves through reeds. They don't have space for the small kindnesses Elias offered or the stump of a steeple that counted for more than any ordinance. But they do show where a man can stand and say "this is where my house was," and then sign his name.

Some nights I think of the town under water like a bird under the surface. There is a solidity to it still. For now, in this drought year's strange grace, Holloway is not yet quite a ghost and it is not yet quite a village. It is an argument between water and land that I have made a little more legible.

I keep the marble where I can see it, and sometimes when the office is quiet I take it out and roll it between my fingers and remember the weight of standing with a tripod on silt that tried to swallow onto itself. A map is a kind of answer to the question of what we leave behind. I think the better truth is that we are all maps for each other: coordinates of sorrow, small ornaments of joy, acres of unmarked, tender refusal. I drove past the reservoir last week and watched the water smooth over the trace of my footsteps from the day I left, a thin gloss making the world look like a polished stone. The line of chimney stumps jutted from the flat like a pulse. For a while the town was legible. I had measured it and made notes and put the results into a public file.

Maps say, in numbers and arrows and tidy fonts, what is and where it is. They do not say why; they do not have the room. But when the county calls and asks you to measure what has been revealed, you do it because someone has to be the person who breathes an accurate line around the past for those who will talk about it later. And sometimes, in the act of measuring, you find a marble in a notch and you remember to be a little kinder to the things you cannot fully explain. The measurement goes into the file. The marble goes into my pocket. The water will come or it will not. Either way, the town was here long enough for me to put a number on its shape and to place my own, small thing within it.
