The ticket was a rectangle of skin against my palm, warm from my body, stamped with a number that might have been another man's name. The port smelled like old rope and hot diesel. My breath made a white coin in the air. I watched the ferry pull itself away from the concrete as if reluctant — a great soft animal heaving up from a rock. The town behind me had already folded into itself; the lights dimmed in the windows like eyes going to sleep. I had not prepared to be sentimental. I had not imagined that standing there would feel like the unfastening of a seam.

He had been gone too long for me to still call him a brother in the bright, familial way. "Jonas," I say his name now and feel it as though it were a clothing left on a peg outdoors, stiffened and smelling slightly of wind. We had kept in touch in scraps, in the way people retain scraps of newspaper from a fire: a postcard here, a murmured voicemail there. For the last five years the voice at the other end of things had become mostly static and Marian, the officer from the station, had said on the phone two days ago, we have a man. We need you to identify him. The phrase sat like an unexpected coin in my shoe; it scraped at every step.

On the deck the wind moved through people, deciding who it would trouble. A woman with a fur collar held a thermos close to her chest. A boy clung to the railing and shouted to a gull as if to prove to himself that he could make the world answer. The boy's father, a man in a navy parka, did not look up. In the cafe, a television played a program about a fisherman who had hauled in a whale. I watched the fisherman blow smoke from the end of a cigarette. He had the concentrated, habitual face of a man who measures his hands by the sea. I thought of Jonas' hands, always stained with something dark from his pockets, quick with collecting things that did not quite belong to him.

The ferry moved like a body that trusted nothing but its own momentum. The engines thudded with the slow pulse of a sleep. I had chosen to take the overnight service because it promised edges — the dark of that crossing would be a tissue that could be sealed around memory. We passed small islands, each one their own rumor of trees and rusty houses. Lights on some of them blinked like teeth when a hand is brought to a mouth.

People sleep on ferries. It is a peculiar social indolence, a group falling into rest in a place that is not home, a tribe of itinerants. I found a corner near the windows and watched the water put on itself a different skin: black and then a gel of luminous threads where other boats cut through it. I thought of all the hours I had let Jonas slip out of sight, the way afternoons with him had once seemed lodged like a foreign coin in my pocket, possible to spend but mostly kept.

I was trained, very practically, in the art of small departures. I had learned to pack in layers, to place things not to see them, to allow the world to carry on its various griefs with no interference from me. Jonas had been the opposite — a man who placed himself at the center of his own weather. He would stand in the middle of a market and, absentmindedly, change the direction of a conversation; he could stand on a bench and speak with such audible conviction that pigeons turned their heads to listen. We had once tried to map our future on a patch of sand with pebbles, naming each pebble a job, a house, a path. He had drawn a line straight out to sea. I had drawn a line toward the mountains.

There are small domestic things that act as passports: the scent of someone's shirt, the slope of their jaw, a nick in a tooth. You think you will be able to recognize a person later by those things, but time sharpens some, erases others. I had been holding images of Jonas in a jar of doubt for years; sometimes he was a young boy with gravel in his hair and an enormous laugh, sometimes an empty chair left at table, sometimes an elastic voice on a crackling line. The photograph I kept in my wallet — two boys on the pier, arms thrown around each other's shoulders — had become both proof and talisman. I looked at it now through low lamplight and wondered whether paper could be trusted to hold a thing that might have sifted away in water.

The ferry's cabin was a place where lives spilled small things at night. Shoes abandoned by bunks, a wool hat hung like a small moon, a stack of paperback novels left to accumulate the sleep of other people's hands. I watched the rooms open and close, like the small throats of cavities breathing. Once I fell asleep for an hour, and in the dream I wandered a childhood kitchen where Jonas sat at the table cutting a strip of leather with enormous concentration. He had promised he would leave the town and take the leather to a cart in the east where men would exchange it for stories. I woke with my mouth full of that leather, and it took me a long time to remember that promises sometimes existed only to teach people to interact with absence.

At dawn, the air was brittle and the islands had become stilled shapes. We pulled into the eastern port and the ferry eased against the quay on a sound like knitted fabric brushing a table. Passengers gathered in sullen queues, acclimating themselves to the idea of land. I walked to the police station with a small weight behind my breastbone, a tangible, private thing like a moth you keep in a jar. The station smelled of tea and disinfectant. Marian met me by the reception desk. She had a way of leaning against a surface that made her look both tired and edgeless, as if she were a small, honest instrument. She took me into an office that had a map on the wall with pins of various colors, like a child's story told with tack and string.

The man in charge of the district morgue was not a theater man. He used words as tools, not as curtains. He explained bureaucracies first: the form I would be asked to sign, the rights of the family. He had a pen ready that looked like a long twig. He did not look at me as if he expected anything but the performance of duty. He told me that the body had been found on a small cove to the north, tangled in kelp and wind, and that identification by physical means was awkward because of the water's work. He offered to bring the body for me to see. I felt like a man who had been given the task of choosing a face from a drawer of shoes. It is harder than you think; the same shoe appears different on different people.

The morgue is cool like a cellar where fruit is stored to delay the rot and prolong the lie that things will return to their initial state. The hall had a hum that was not entirely electrical. Light fell like a strict hand. The drawers were long and metallic. They made you aware of the smallness of your own body in relation to the body's disposability. He told me the name they had used — an unknown — and that no one had come forward from the cove. He suggested I look. He told me to take my time. There were eyes in the room that did not watch me but waited for me to begin.

They rolled him out on a slab, covered with a sheet like the way a blanket is folded neatly on a bench. For a long time I could not see past the simple geometry of folded fabric. Then the officer gently peeled back the corner. Water had done many things: it had softened the edges of the face, it had paled him into a kind of unseasoned bread. His hair was plastered and dark. A bruise, pale as badly mixed paint, showed on the temple. There was a wound at the back of the head that the officer called something clinical and removed himself. The mouth was slightly open. When they called my name — it was odd to hear your name spoken by strangers with kindness — I felt a scraping on the inside of my jaw. I thought of being a child and trying to force a coin to stay in one's mouth because it felt like a secret. The eyes, when they were opened later by a gently turned hand, were glassy with the light of something that had ceased. I had not known I would need to see them in order to be made to believe.

Recognition does not always come as a thunderbolt. It came as a slow composite. A scar under the arm where a lighter had once burned someone; a thin diagonal on the knuckle, the width of a thumb, that I remembered from when Jonas had caught his hand on a boat winch while we were boys; the way the hem of his coat frayed in an odd, confident manner, as if he had always liked a garment a little stained — he had never been much for domestic neatness. In his jacket pocket a small square of plastic with the outline of a coastal pub glued to it; it was the kind of thing people pick up and keep, like a rock. He had a lighter in one of the other pockets, a battered silver zippo. They opened it for me. Inside there was a tiny emblem scratched into the metal — two crossed oars. That emblem was not unique to him, but it had been his amusement, his private cartography; he kept it habitually — a keepsake from a summer when he had been apprenticed to a man who taught children to row.

He also had a small photograph in an inner jacket pocket. When the officer unfolded it I thought my breath had left the room. There we were: two boys, smaller than we were in any memory I had, arms folded around each other's shoulders on a wooden pier. The wind had seized my hair like a child's hand and Jonas had his face turned toward the camera, mouth open as if he were mid-laughter. I had been younger than in any memory; I looked delighted, a smallness of jaw. The photograph had been taken in a summer of a heat so intense that the light seems to have been poured like sugar. Someone, perhaps a stranger or a passerby, had taken it with a decisiveness that made us both small and upright. The edges of the photograph were worn. I had kept a copy for years in my wallet to prove to myself that we had not only existed but existed together in a single frame. It had not occurred to me that he might carry the same image. It felt then like discovering that a language you believed you had made up in solitude was, in fact, used by another.

I placed my hand over the photograph until it fogged in the warmth of my skin, like a window of glass you press your palm against in winter. The face of the dead man before me became, temporarily, a surface on which my memory could write. He had a small crescent of scar on his left hand, half-mooned like a coin that had been bitten; I had seen it when he was nine after he had fallen with a bicycle. I had once traced it with my fingertip in the dark while we sat beneath a bridge and shared cigarettes too small for our mouths. I could no longer be sure whether my hand had really touched that memory; the room had a florescent certainty about it that made me distrust the claim of my fingers.

The officers, properly unobtrusive, suggested that I take a moment. Their industry was of a different romance — the romance of things as things, not of the meanings people give them. I thought of all the things Jonas had collected: a ticket stub from a theatre we had never attended, a button with a face printed on it, a message in a bottle that had never contained a message. I thought that identity, too, is a collection of such things. If a man dies wearing a coin from a summer and with the same photograph in his pocket, the argument becomes less about the absolute and more about the coherence of a life. Perhaps that is what is asked of those left behind: to assemble the shards into a semblance that can be recognized by others and thus made acceptable.

My mouth was dry. I had rehearsed the word I would say and the way I would sign my initials at the bottom of a page. I had imagined that the word would be "yes" or "no," sharp and undebatable. It arrived in my throat as something like the wind, thin and indecisive. The form asked for dates, relationships, particulars. I could put in the date of his birth from memory, but the form called for a precision that would feel like treachery if wrong. Who were we to say with authority the shape of his days? The world prefers lists and ticks where grief demands rough edges.

"Can you identify?" the officer asked. He placed on the desk a second little photograph that had been taken from the wallet. It had my face, but also me as a version I had not kept to myself, a hair that had been brushed to one side with a childish pride. I recognized a small freckle near the jawline that I had often attempted to vanish with the oil of adolescent creams. He looked like me, or I looked like him. The room was a place of doubling.

There are ethical games one plays with the dead where each move is a moral test. If I claimed him unequivocally as Jonas, I would be placing in the hands of the state an identity that would have consequences: burial, the legal closing of a life. If I refused, the man would remain anonymous in the grid of the morgue, a subject of maps and pins. He might be embalmed, numbered, forgotten. The question was not merely who he was but what I owed him for being, long ago, both my brother and my rival, my confessor and my antagonist. The estrangement had been like a smudge, slowly worked into the cloth of life until it seemed inevitable. There had been moments I had wanted to scour it away, to return to some small domesticity; there had been others in which I had enjoyed the distance, the space that allowed me to control who I loved.

"Do you need a signature?" I asked. The officer nodded. The pen he put in my hand was heavier than it looked. I held it and felt the weight of the signatures that had been scratched on it by other men, as if the pen had a memory of its own. I thought of how many decisions we perform with instruments that do not know the consequences. I thought of the small coin of the pier, the photograph, the lighter. I tried to imagine them being catalogued, their edges filed neatly, set into a paper where my truth would be one of many.

I could have said no. I could have written upon that paper that I was uncertain. That would have been honest in one register. But another part of me, the quieter one that had waited through marital separations and the coldness of winter nights, felt something like a stubborn loyalty. To name him would be to provide him a door to go through, a small, formal manner of passing from one ledger to another. To name him would be to record that the life he had lived existed and that it had been mine in some tangled way. I thought also of the image on the pier and of the fact that two people who had been boys in the same sun might both choose at different times to carry the same proof. What else could I do except render that evidence legible?

When I signed, my hand trembled so badly that the letters shivered like leaves. The officer did not look surprised. He had seen too many hands quake from too many reasons. He took back the slip as if it were a small, delicate stone he needed to tuck into his pocket. He said the thing that bureaucrats say to make the world understand that it too can be contained: we will take care of everything.

Later, on the way back through the corridor, I asked if I could have the photograph. The officer hesitated, then handed it to me with the same manner one has when offering a damp towel to a man who has been in the sea. It was colder than I expected when it touched my palm, but it warmed quickly. The picture, once mine and then fallow in someone else's pocket, became suddenly like a map. I put it back inside my wallet. The wallet was a thing that had many pockets, and I thought of the way people keep themselves safe with trivialities. Outside the station, the sun was low. The sea had become a strip of hammered silver. The town looked as if it had been arranged for me to pass through, with no particular ceremony.

I walked back toward the quay. On the way I passed the fish market, where men lifted fillets like flags. One of them looked at me as he always had looked at the town's regulars — the look of a man cataloguing whom he might owe. I thought of funerals as theatrical things, but the real theatre had been inside the morgue. There, in the quiet, the performance had become true. I thought of Jonas' laugh and the way it could arrange a room. I thought of all the times we had been near each other and had not understood the exact schedule of our lives.

On the ferry I did not sleep. The open sea at night is an expanse that arranges the world into horizontal truths. I sat with my hands in my jacket pockets and the photograph sometimes sliding toward my fingers, the parchment skin of it softening in the warmth. At one point a man sat beside me and struck up a conversation about storms. He said, "You never feel them until you're on the other side." He lit a cigarette and smoked like a man punctuating his sentences with punctures in the air. I replied listlessly, like one who has practiced inarticulate answers. People on ferries assume the honesty of being between places and they offer secrets as though between stops. The man said, "I once found a life jacket on a raft. There were initials in the collar. I kept them for a while." His voice sank into the hull's breathing. I wondered if he had ever signed a paper that told the state a man's name.

At dawn we reached the western quay and the town unfolded itself again, houses like small boxes pressing toward the shore. The ferry disgorged its passengers like a ship that had been giving birth to many small solitudes. I stood still on the pier as if waiting for a friend to appear from the crowd. People passed me and the town assumed its usual motions: delivery vans, breakfast cafes, the smell of frying fish. A child with cheeks like a ripe apple ran past and glanced back, seeking permission to keep running. I had the photograph in my hand, damp with the condensation of long journeys. I opened it and looked, and for a moment I saw only light.

When you name a person as someone you loved you do more than sign a form. You cut a thread between what once was possible and what has now been recorded. I had spoken his name into an official vocabulary. I had told the world that the man I had seen in the morgue was Jonas and therefore his life, in all its wandering and unaccounted-for fragments, belonged to the family ledger. I did not know, and I still cannot be sure, whether every part of me had done it to be generous or to confess, or to lay a small proof across my own conscience like a bandage. The irony is that death sometimes requires the living to be more inventive with love than the living ever managed when the loved one was alive.

The boat back across the strait felt less like a departure and more like a relay of a relay. I had left a statement and returned with a photograph. The sea wore the same kind of indigo robe as when I came but now, when I watched the small lights on the islands, I thought of them as pins on a map, each one possibly the place where stories ended. The photograph sat in my wallet as if it had always intended to be there, slipping into the crease as a leaf fits into a book. Sometimes I thought I heard Jonas' voice murmuring around the edges of the ferry, as if the world itself kept giving him small lines of speech for me to notice.

That evening back in my rented room I spread the photograph on the small table and let it sit under the desk lamp. It was my own face in the background, younger, cheek sunburned, hair in the foolish cowlick that only children can know. In the foreground Jonas had his mouth open, caught between sound and the silence of the paper. The room smelled faintly of fish from the dinners downstairs and the mattress springs complained when I sat on the edge. I lit a cigarette — a private ritual that had lost its original exhilaration — and sat with the image as if it were a small sleeping animal.

Estrangement has many flavors. It is not always the salted bitterness of a fight. Sometimes it is a long succession of little practicalities — a missed birthday, a letter unanswered, a phone call that dies in a pocket — that accumulate until the two bodies that once knew how to fit together no longer do. We had been, I think, both embarrassed about the ways we were similar and afraid we might become too like one another. Jonas took his life outside the frame I had made for mine; he gathered scraps of existence and wore them as capes. I had taken comfort in the careful arrangements of bills and insurance and appointments. At the end of an afternoon the arrangements wore me thin. Identifying him was not an attempt to repair these things but a kind of pinning down; I wanted to see what parts of him were made of the same matter as I.

The next day I sent for a small box from the morgue containing the things they had listed as personal effects. They obliged with efficient kindness and a paper bag that smelled faintly of formaldehyde. Inside were the lighter with the crossed oars, the photograph, a small coin from a fair, a cheap watch that had stopped at a time that meant nothing. When I held the items they felt accretive, like a tiny archeology of one man's route through the world. I placed them in a drawer as if arranging a small shrine. I found that I wanted to touch the objects in a way that I had not allowed myself with the living Jonas. I polished the lighter and traced the scratched emblem until it looked almost new.

A week later I went down to the cove where they had found him. The tide hummed the way a throat hums a tune it will never finish. The beach smelled like kelp and forgotten boats. The rocks were arranged like teeth. There were two men gathered there with a dog; they acknowledged me with a nod that implies familiarity rather than friendship. Someone had laid a small arrangement of wildflowers on a jutting rock; the petals had been arranged with the casual earnestness of people who feel that flowers can say what words will not. I stood a while looking out to the channel where ferry wakes stitched the water. The sea was indifferent as ever. It gulped and exhaled. A small wave lifted and folded, a private thing.

I did not know how to hold my grief. Grieving for someone you have not loved in a tidy, continuous manner is a strange business; it is like turning toward a flame you have previously ignored. I felt many small embers lit in me: guilt for absences, relief for distances, a peculiar pride that in the end I had been the one to fold that life into an official shape. Perhaps that was the wrong pronoun. Perhaps the shape was not a finality at all but a name, and sometimes names can be a kind of shelter.

At night I dreamt of a pier that stretched out forever. Jonas stood at the end waving at something I could not see. When I reached him in the dream he held out an object that glowed faintly, like a stone the moon had heated. I took it and it was the photograph. I woke with the image of it heavy in my hands and the taste of salt on my tongue.

Months later someone would ask me whether I had felt redemption in the moment of signing the paper. I would say no, because redemption implies an economy I do not believe in. I felt instead the relief of a small task completed and the knowledge that the world had accepted a certain story. A life had been mapped into the ledger. But I also felt an opening. The photograph remained in my wallet, now not a token that proves we had been together but a relic I carried like a small board across which I could lay my fingers and feel the beginning of things.

There are days when the world arranges itself into two clear types of weather: the weather when someone is present and the weather when they are not. The present weather is luminous; the absent weather is an even light that makes everything look slightly pale. I now move through both kinds of weather. I have found small ways to keep him near: looking at a place at the same hour we once did, walking down a street we had both disliked, keeping the lighter in my pocket so that I might touch it with the same finger he had used to flick it. Sometimes I talk to him as though he were in the room, telling him about small, ordinary things — a neighbor's new dog, the rain's way of arriving late. Perhaps these are consolations for me; perhaps they would have irritated him. He might have laughed and said, "You always were slow," and I would have conceded it, because I had always been the slower of the two, turning things over until they revealed their insides.

On really quiet nights I hear the ferry's horn in my dreams, long and laced with a loneliness that is neither wholly painful nor entirely peaceful. In the sound there is the memory of motion: something big moving from one place to another, the whole of its metal body creaking against the sea. Sometimes I imagine that Jonas is on some other deck, smoking the same cigarette he had always liked, looking for me in crowds of people and missing on purpose because he thinks it will be fun. In the morning when I wake I will sometimes lift the lighter to the lamp and watch the flame breathe. The gesture feels like a ceremony that binds me to both the brother I had and the one I have become.

Once, months after, I went back to the pier in the photograph. The wood had been replaced in places; nails glinted with newness. People were fishing, their faces working into frowns like those of men considering possibilities. I walked out to where the boys had stood and felt the wind lift my hair just so. I imagined the photograph as a map that could be read only from certain angles. I took it from my wallet and looked at us again. The image had not changed. It was as honest and as indifferent as always. I folded it gently, and when the light hit it, the glint made the boys' eyes appear to move.

What does it mean to identify a man? Perhaps it means to tell the world you will take responsibility for the memory of him. It was not a heroic thing; there was nothing grand in the signature I left. It was an act performed by a man who had tired of waiting and decided to close a ledger. Perhaps the most tenacious thing is that after the papers are signed and the world has taken notice, the body that was removed remains, in small ways, with those who have touched it. A lighter pressed to a thumb becomes warm in a way that is intimate and private. A photograph becomes a portal. The sea keeps long shapes in its memory and sometimes releases them like a secret.

I do not know whether I will ever be able to say that I loved Jonas as he deserved. Love is not a coin to be measured by its metal but by how often you carry it into common day. I carry his photograph like a small currency. Sometimes I look at the lighter and think of the way his thumb had worn the metal, the way his laugh had left the air. Other times the memory is simply a place where I go to see what used to be true. I have learned to accept the mixture of relief and sorrow, like a tide that brings both seaweed and shells.

At times people ask me directly what I saw when I looked at the body. They expect narratives: a gesture, a look. I tell them the truth that there was no cinematic flash, only the accumulation of small signs — the lighter, the scar, the photograph — and the feeling that in the end we had shared a narrow geography: the pier, the town, the habit of walking into wind. The rest is conjecture. For me, the act of naming him on that paper was not a conclusion but a relocation. I put him into a formal envelope and mailed it to the clerk of the world. That is all I did. The rest is salt and wind and the long business of remembering and unremembering.

When the ferry crosses the strait at night, sometimes I stand on the deck and watch the islands slide by. The sky is generous in winter. The stars are thin and intensely certain. I think of the things that bind us to one another: shared jokes, fights that leave grooves in our speech, photographs that we both fold into the same pocket. I take the lighter from my pocket and flip it open at random, not to smoke — the habit is more a ritual now than need — but to see that flame appear and assert itself. It is a small visible thing in a world of intangible losses. The flame is, for an instant, as bright as any photograph. I hold it up and squint as if looking into the face of a man I once knew. Then I close the lighter and put it back, because there are some private recognitions better held close.

The boys in the photograph still stand, arm in arm, in a summer that will not return. I have made the formal decision that the man who lay in the morgue and the boy in the picture were the same person. It is a decision that lifts a small weight from the world and lays it into the township of paperwork and ritual. I do not know whether it is the right decision. I only know that it broke a kind of paralysis in me. For what it's worth, sometimes decisions that are not grand are still the ones that allow us to move, and moving is, in its own strange way, the only real way to continue.
