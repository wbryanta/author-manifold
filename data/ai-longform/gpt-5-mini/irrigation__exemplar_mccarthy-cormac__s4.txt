They were on the road before the light had hardened and the orchard lay there like a thing half remembered at the foot of the slope, the rows of trees a dark geometry against the dawn. The brothers moved together along the headland where the ditch ran, the older with the pack slung at his shoulder, the younger with the coil of hose over his arm and a wrench tucked into the back of his jeans. The air was cool but heavy with the promise of heat and the smell of the earth rose up from the furrows where the water had seeped overnight and made the soil near the surface soft and dark as if rubbed with oil. A constellation of small flies hovered over the moist ground; a hawk rode the current above them and let its shadow pass along the rows like a hand.

Eli was the older by three years, a long narrow man with a face cut from the same country as the land, the brows over his eyes heavy as lintels, the mouth set in a dry line. He had his father's hands. Micah moved with a litness that was half adolescent; a boy in a big man's body. He spoke in short sentences and relapsed sometimes into laughter that was more like a cough. They had both come down from the house at the head of the orchard where the two-story barn leaned like an old crutch and the barn cat watched them go from the hayloft with eyes like cold beads.

The ditch had been their father's work. It ran as a vein through the field, cut by his foreman hand in seasons past when water was a thing to be coaxed and not bargained for. Now the ditch was patched unevenly in places with fittings and clamps, and where the ditch had lately been lined with plastic the lining had been gnawed by sun and salt and now lay in ragged surrender. The orchard itself was failing. The trees stood with a gaunt dignity, leaves parched, a few of the trees sprouting a conspicuous putrescence at the crotches where rot had set in; fruit hung in sparse clusters that seemed to mock the memory of summers when the limbs had been bowed beneath their weight. Insects had grown fat on the sap. In one line the trunks were ringed with scars where a plow had once been careless; in another the soil had shrunken so that roots rose in ridges like old snakes.

We had a good run, Micah said. He stood for a long moment with his hands on his hips and watched the water move sluggishly toward the first tree. The line had been patched the week before with an improvised sleeve of tin and wire and hand me down clamps. The water came out in a thin rumpled ribbon along the weeds.

Eli did not look at him at first. He set the pack down and knelt and unrolled the coil. Don't say that, he said. Say nothing if you can't say better.

Micah scraped a toe along the dirt and then spat. If there weren't any money coming in we'd be starvin by fall, he said. You got to admit that. You seen the numbers? Taxes, the bank, them kids buyin twenty acres for what they paid for a pickup. They want us. That boy Harper's been by twice. He left a paper on the porch. He says he'd pay cash. He says he can put up a fence and cut the alders, make a show out of it, sell it to people who don't know what a passport is.

Eli reached into the pack and drew out a roll of electrical tape and a piece of rubber hose. He had the way of his hands then, quiet and late, with the small mechanical patience of a man who'd learned to practice mercy upon inexorable things. He looked at Micah in that wait that was his native measure. The orchard was a map, he said. We are on it.

Micah laughed and then the sound ran away from him and lodged somewhere in the roots of the nearest tree. A map, he said. We got no town in the middle of us. We got dust and a mailbox and a roadside cross where some drunk slipped off the blacktop. We got the house but it leaks and the stove ain't been right for two winters. We got a note in a bank envelope that says pay or we take. That map you keep talkin about don't buy a bag of feed.

Eli's face was like a stone under wind. You cannot sell the ground as if you sold a plow, he said. The ground is where our names were learned. It's where the boys learned to stand up. You think the years will vanish when you sign a paper? You think history will wake up and find itself unoccupied because a number has changed hands? You don't see what it is you owe.

I owe my life, Micah said. I ain't owe the land. I owe the life I got. I owe walks in cool evening and last year's peach jam and the summers I could leave and go. Ain't that enough?

Eli let the tape lie across his knee and looked at the line idly as if it were an animal not to be hurried. He believes in the covenant, he said finally. A man signs and things line up. He ties his acts like he ties a rope and he expects them to hold. But the covenants of the land are other deals. The land don't sign. The land holds.

Micah dropped to his haunches and set his elbow on his knee and watched Eli. You going to make me some scripture about trees? he said. You don't even go to meeting. You sit at the table like a judge and you rub your hands and you say that.

Eli hissed a breath; the faintest of smiles in the corner of his mouth. Words are things, he said. He had the habit of saying this as if it were a technical observation rather than an aphorism. We stack them. We make monuments with them. We make law. They stay.

Micah stood up then and walked a handful of paces down the row and reached up and fingered the bark of one of the trees. It was warm under his hand. He pressed his palm to the trunk and closed his eyes for a moment. He had been a boy once in the orchard; that boy was present like a portrait in a pocket of his flesh. He ran his fingers through a cluster of leaves and found a few small aphids clinging like tiny relics. The tree leaned from years of wind.

What did the old man leave? he asked. Not much. Other people say he left stories. He left dust. He left a bullet wedged in the hearth of his stove and an account with the bank and a catalog of things that need paying. We got his name on paper. We got the smell of his pipe. Reallie the thing is do we want to sink ourselves in a hole to keep a yard of grass that won't hold the water?

Eli cut a length of hose and slid it over the cracked plastic sleeve. He had an old way of doing this work where you made the quick decision and leant into it and did not waste time with rhetoric. His voice softened. The old man left more than he knew, he said. He left us. The orchard is a ledger. It keeps track.

Micah laughed that brittle laugh again and it sounded near to anger. A ledger doesn't buy tires, he said. It don't buy a new starter for the tractor. What if I want to go? What if I want to leave and see that city my cousin's always on about? We could take our share, split it, and neither one'd be owing the other. You could stay if you wanted. I ain't askin you to come. Just let me have my part.

Eli folded the tape and wound it around the coupling with precision. He tightened the clamp with the wrench until it bit. The water through the line eased and then thickened and the tree drank. A single leaf behind them twitched as if in a small convulsion. We are not talking of parting of things, he said. We are talking of the parting of ourselves. You want to go and the land remains. It will be there when you return and it will not call you by name. But do you not see that it is in us? That your hands are the hills and that your voice is the creek? Names are not debts. They are measures.

He raised his head and looked along the rows and with that slow patience of old men set about telling the story of the orchard as if he were reading it aloud from the ledger: the trees planted by a man from the Old County who had come with a horse and a wagon and a long memory of seasons; the little ditch that was first an experiment; the time the flood came and took half the western row and the way the neighbors came with men and sacks and buckets and helped rebuild; the year the frost took them all and how their father had worked nights with a gasoline pump and strings of light to keep the air warm. He spoke without ornament, as if the facts by their own weight might assemble significance.

Micah listened and his face changed, sometimes hardening, sometimes softening. He had a boy's appetite for parable but he would not be moved by it into what he called vanity. He said, Look, Eli, I know your stories. I got them tattooed on my ribs. I can tell you the year the pump failed. I can tell you the name of the man who mended the gate. I know it. But I don't want those things to measure whether I eat next winter. You can't live on a story. People come and take the land and then somebody else puts a brick and a light in it and it becomes something else. Who is to say this is not what's meant to happen?

Eli's fingers had found the rub of the hose and were smoothing the transition. You think the land means only what hands do upon it, he said. But that is a narrow reading. The land is the thing by which we answer. Something calls to us in it. You can either answer or you can step aside. The answer is not always given to be comfortable.

Micah spat again into the dust and then laughed and his laugh was a release. You talk sacred like that and the bank man will laugh at you when he sees the checks you sign, he said. He'll clip your name and put it in a file. When you die the county will come and plow the place under and your renters will claim there was never anything but a field. That frightens you? he asked. The thought of being so small.

Eli didn't smile. The sun had risen higher and the white light lay like a membrane over everything. It blew in upon them with the first insistence of the day and they felt it as a rough hand. Around the orchard the earth did not give. The water had been steady all spring but the well had begun to breathe and the pumping had a hollowness to it. The county said the aquifer was under stress. The neighbor with the new dirt knew people in the planning office. There was talk of subdivisions. Already the highway that had once been a county road had been widened and the stops at the nearest town were being deliberated with a kind of hunger. You could see the markers where the future roads would cut.

They ate noon in the shade of the barn, sitting on the lip of the hayloft like two vagrants. They had brought coffee in a dented thermos and bread wrapped in wax paper and a jar of the old man's peaches, the ones that had been canned with a sugar that pulled at the teeth. A holler of a dog somewhere behind the neighbor's fence, the quick sussuration of locusts, the sound of a pickup cutting a distant dust plume down the road. They ate slowly, discussing the repairs and the cost of parts. There were little acts of recollection: the time the younger had fallen from the oldest tree and lay under the wagon with a tooth knocked loose, the old man's blackened thumb where he'd gripped a hot pan, the yellowed photograph of the land when it had been full and fat.

Money entered the conversation again like a cold wind. Micah laid the paper the neighbor had left on the porch between them and pushed it toward Eli. The numbers made a neat dry geometry on the page: dollars, figures, the promise of a lump sum. He said, You sign and you get to leave. If you don't, we sit in the same house while the trough keeps draining.

Eli had not yet opened the paper. He set the thermos down and ran his thumb along the edge of the page as if reading the paper for texture. These things are paradox, he said. When you own the ground you are less free than you are when you do not. Free to sell. Free to leave. But being free in that sense is a shorn thing. You become an edition of yourself.

Micah's hand fluttered with frustration. You talk like the neighbor Harper's a moral test. He'll sign with a lawyer and he'll go home and put his name to it and go fishing and not care. He don't ask us a question. He offers us money. What answer do you expect other than yes, if I say yes? What desire do you have to bind me with your ideals, Eli? I'm not a book you can put on a shelf.

Eli laughed softly and the sound seemed mournful in the barn's shade. I am not binding you, he said. I am asking you to remember where you stand long enough to know what the cost will be. You keep saying what you'll get. Say instead what you will lose. That is a finer measure.

They were men who had lived in the same house all their lives and therefore were citizens of the same memory. They argued without rancor. At times they fell into the old roles: Eli the patient, Micah the hot. Yet there was also a tenderness in their words, a remnant of the boyhood when they had shared blankets and dolls and the old man had read to them. That tenderness made the argument worse, for it meant each knew the other's center and could not touch it without wounding.

In the afternoon they took the line up into the high part of the orchard where the main collar lay and the last of the underground pipes shifted. There the ground had been picked by moles and the roots had come up and heaved and the sun had browned the grass to a brittle straw. They waded in overgrown alders and found the break where the pipe had been crushed. The metal sleeve was gone and around the offending girth the roots had taken, a stubborn ring of growth that had strangled the plastic. Micah went back to the barn and came with a saw and a hatchet. Eli had the muscle and Micah had the speed.

They worked in tandem. Eli cut the roots that crossed the path like fingers; Micah pried the broken lengths apart and hauled the heavy worn pipe to the edge of the ditch. Sweat ran along the master lines of their backs and the sun capped them and the smell of pitch and rust rose from the fittings. When they had exposed the break they found a length of old iron that had been dug in when some earlier hand had attempted a more permanent repair. It was corroded and pitted but still stubborn as a habit. Eli looked at it and breathed and then with the slow ritual of men he had learned to see he set about coaxing it back into use.

Something about the work put thoughts into a smaller, more serviceable shape. They spoke less of sale and more of how to splice and fit and bring what water there was to the row that wanted it most. The job commanded them, and the argument subsided for a time under the authority of what instruments required. The sound of the saw was its own grammar.

When at last the pipe held and the water began to close the hunger of the orchard again their faces were wet and earthy and there was a kind of public happiness that comes from machines made to run. They sat back on their heels and watched the line and the drip and the leaves that lifted and sagged and lifted again as water that has been denied too long is pouring with prudence. All over the field one could see the faint distress turn to a minor relief.

Micah knocked his cap against his knee and then wiped the sweat off his brow with the back of his hand and pressed his hat down and laughed. See, he said. It comes. You can't have it all if you won't take the trouble. A fix here and a fix there, and the ground gets what it needs and we get to breathe. People worry like thieves. They count the pennies before the safe gets opened.

Eli let his hands rest in his lap and watched the water and then the trees. I know you want to go, he said. He said it like a man might offer a coat. If you go you'll be well. You will do the things you spoke of. You will be a man with a city in his bones and a pocketbook that opens for what is called life. But when you go you will not be rid of the land. You cannot be rid. The names travel. They find you in the crowd. They are like a birthmark.

Micah reached out and took a long drink from the thermos and then blew his breath out and tuned his head to the sound that the water made in the pipe. I don't want to be cursed, he said. I don't want the thing to follow me. I don't want some specter of a man I never saw tellin me how to mix my coffee in the mornin. You worry about blood and I worry about bills.

Eli said, You are not cursed. But men are haunted by the privilege of their birth. You have a birthright and a bill. Such are the terms. We must reckon.

They spoke of their father as if he were recumbent under a sheet, present in the room. He was at once a forensic fact and a myth. They remembered the small stubborn ritual of his ways: the cigarette stub he tucked in his ear while he read, the tear that sometimes wet his cheek as he watched the sun go down and the boys play in the dust. He had been a man of small and peculiar graces. He had also been a man who had, when the time came, left them the orchard without further instruction.

What did he want? Micah asked. Did he want us to keep it? Did he want us to break it off like a branch and hand it to the highest bidder? How do you know? It's easy to tell what you don't want. The old man had not left a letter with directives but he had left the weather and the ditch and the barn and the rust in the pump and the photographs and the habits. These things are what a man leaves when all else fails. They are his will in its most primitive form.

Eli said, He left us the task. He could not have done more. Whatever he meant he left as action. The task is a kind of language.

They sat then for a long time and watched the sun cross and the field breathe and a cloud of midges bloom and fold and unbloom over a clod of ground like a living thing. There was the thud of a truck in the distance and the sound of voices carried as if in a bottle. A child laughed as it chased a dog. The world went on indifferent to the arithmetic of their concern. It was a fact they had been given to abide.

Later, when the light had a knife edge to it and the heat had forgiven them for their labors, they took the pickup to the road that ran along the east boundary where the neighbor Harper had put a pile of flagged stakes. The stakes had been driven in a curious pattern, like the tentative fingers of a man testing where a house might stand. Harper was a round man, ruddy of face, with sleeves rolled and a grin as wide as a barn door. He came out to the roadside and wiped his hands on his jeans.

Boys, he said. How's the ditch? He had a manner that was all commerce and good humor and he regarded the brothers with a businessmanly affection, as if they were livestock he had admired from a distance and now desired to appraise.

Eli said little. Micah kept his fingers on the steering wheel and let Harper make the speech he had rehearsed. We believe this is perfect land for development, Harper said. You got access, you got beautiful aspect, you got a good road. We want to keep the trees where possible but create privacy with our fence and our plantings. We'll pay you fair. Cash, he hammered. No debts. No liens. Each of you takes what is due.

Eli's face was like a slate. He looked at the stakes and then at Harper and then at his brother. Tell me, he said to Harper without affect, what do you know of hunger? Harper blinked and then smiled. I know enough, he said. I know markets. I know my name will get on a map. I know the people who buy will not come to know your name. They will buy the view, maybe a story about an orchard, and that's their concern. They will pay for privacy and a lawn. That's the currency of our age.

Micah's fingers flexed on the wheel and there was a quiet violence in the contraction. You think you can buy a family's noon for a sum and call it square? he said. You put up trellis and lights and you stamp the field into rectangles and hand it off and you think it's done. But it stays on you. It's an account.

Harper laughed and it was a sound like a coin. I ain't buyin souls, he said. I'm buyin dirt. He pointed toward the ridge where the new subdivision would have a view and there was the shimmer of a future road on the horizon like a promise of light. Think of what you could do, he said. Pay off the debts. Move to town. Get that house you been talkin about. Stop living with the ghosts.

Eli said, There are ghosts of a different order. We need them.

Harper's grin narrowed in that moment as if some small logic had rebuked him. He patted the pickup's fender with a cheerful regard and said, Look, boys, the offer's on the table. Let me know. I'm a man of my word. He said it and he sounded as if he believed himself.

When he drove away the dust rose like a benediction and settled over the stakes. Micah watched the dust like a badger waiting for an opening. He stood then and walked to the first stake and touched it with the fingertips. Everybody's makin maps, he said. The world is getting smaller. It's all a grid and a phone and a string of lights to keep the dark at bay. Why should we be the last to hold on?

Eli put his hand on the stake too and felt the sun-blown splinter of it in his palm. He looked at Micah then and said, Because we are the medium of a thing. He used that word with the gravity of a man reciting scripture. The medium is what carries the other things. We are the medium of the orchard. Who else will be the medium if not us?

Micah laughed, softer now and almost ashamed of that softness. You make sanctity out of a ditch, he said. He wasn't unkind. But his sorrow was an animal behind a fence. If you let me walk I will walk. You can keep the fence and the ditch and the elbow grease and your photos. I will go. Maybe I will be happy. Maybe I will be no happier than a man who keeps seed by his bed and remembers he sold his past.

It was not that either of them believed in some ultimate checklist by which men were judged. They were both of the earth, and the earth is patient with petty longings. But there was in them a knowledge of a line that ran not only through loam but through the politics of their people's lives. You could sell a field to a man with money; you could not sell the aftertaste.

At dusk they walked the rows with a lantern and the lines of light made long shoals under the trees. The orchard had a last purple perfume as the day cooled and the fruit turned warm under the rind. They stopped at a trunk and sat on the same old cedar stump their father had cut in two seasons ago. Neither of them spoke for a while. The night made a soft pressure. Owls duetted in the far alders. The soft hum of the pump came from the well like something breathing.

Micah said, Tell me then, Eli. If you had the money and you had the choice and you could leave without a bone of regret, would you? The question had the cadence of a confession.

Eli turned his head and looked at the far horizon where the white road lay and the faint glow of the town's lamps were starting. I don't know, he said. He said it slowly as if weighing the syllables. There is a thing inside me that is as big as the land and as small as a coin. Sometimes it tells me to go. Sometimes it tells me to stay. When I was a boy I thought leaving was some evangelical freedom. Now I know leaving is a subtraction and staying is an arithmetic you do in a notebook that only you can read.

Micah closed his eyes for a moment and swallowed. The lantern cast his shadow long against the trunk and the shadow looked thinner than he felt. All I know is, he said, I don't want to be told I couldn't have tried. I don't want to be told I flinched. If we sign then we will have tried. If we don't then we're keepers of a museum of old chairs. Give me the choice. Let me have the shot.

Eli's hands were folded like harvest. He reached out and took Micah's hand in that brief blackness and held it. The touch was not melodramatic. It was the compact of brothers, ancient as debt and gift. We will do no such thing as make the other do what he will not, he said. He let his hand stay there until Micah relaxed and his shoulders fell like someone laying a burden down.

They made no bargain then. There is a solemnity in the act of not deciding, and men sometimes choose that by default. The lantern burned low and the night pressed cool on their faces and in their ears the orchard sounded like a congregation. When the stars appear the world is different; the gridlines blur and the city's glow becomes a rumor.

In the end they sat until their eyes watered from the smoke of the barn light and the lantern and then they went up to the house to sleep. Each slept in the rooms their father had slept in like a chain handing the torch. When the younger rose at dawn he moved about with the quick, furtive movements of a man who was unsatisfied. He left a note on the kitchen table that read I need to see it, and he folded it under a teaspoon.

Eli found the note and then pocketed it and made coffee and waited. He expected that he might see his brother in the distance or not at all. Hours later a car with city plates passed the front gate and the dust stood like a flag. The horizon kept its cold promise.

Days came and went in the fashion of weather. The neighbor's offer remained on the doorstep like a waiting animal. Men came by and looked at stakes and the surveyor marked things with paint like surgical cuts. The orchard drank what rain there was and the trees, that had been coaxed back for the moment, showed a pale resurgence. There was a small felicity to the work, so small that it might be overlooked by those who loved large counts. Yet in the small were great things. A graft taken and given, a trunk wrapped, a root pruned so that new life might come. Sometimes these acts are miracle enough.

On a late afternoon in June they sat by the ditch again. Between them lay the paper offer. Neither of them touched it. They had arguments yet to finish. They had confessions they were each reluctant to make. There were debts chained in ledgerbooks and there were dreams in a cousin's letters of a city made of light. There were images of grandchildren playing in a yard with a sprinkler and of someone else moving in with new curtains and long lawns and none of the ghosts.

Micah looked at the paper and then at the trees and then back at Eli. He said, You want to sign? He had no other words for the ask. It was a brute question, small as a coin, and in it lay all the other possibilities. You want to sell? I don't know, Eli said. He was honest. I don't know. I don't know what it is to give away the thing whose only argument is time. He let the silence grow and invited his brother to rule it.

Micah then did something he had not done in years: he laughed like a man who had found again the understanding of his own bones. He reached over and pushed the paper toward his brother and he said, We'll sleep on it. We'll let the field be for another night. He was half petition and half concession. He had decided for nothing more than a day's reprieve.

Eli did not lift the paper. He turned and looked down the line where the ditch died into the brush and the light was evening and the orchard's leaves were the color of the inner life of a coin. There are things that are decided by measure of water and measure of bread, he said. There are things that are decided by measure of those who come after us and by measure of the stories they will tell. He was speaking to the night and the night was listening.

They left the paper between them and walked the rows shoulder to shoulder. The orchard breathed around them like a slow beast. They were two men who had chosen, at least for the present, not to be finished. There was still a harvest to be coaxed and a milk of mornings to be tasted and debts to be met. The neighbor would wait unless hunger made him impatient and the county would grind its invoice with linear indifferent teeth. But for that day they would be the medium of the orchard, as Eli put it, and they would hold their hands to the work.

At the headland a nightjar winged across and the first stars came down like pins. The ditch gurgled and the trees sighed and the house behind them stood with its shuttered windows like a patient face. If some future man would one day read their names upon some filed paper he would not know the mornings they had in which they mended hose with callused fingers or the small reconciliations that had been made in the chill of dawn. Names do not carry suns. But the day had been made and they had lived in it and by living they had told one another that there is sometimes a reason to stay and sometimes a reason to go and that neither reason is wholly a virtue.

They sat there a while in the dark, shoulders squared to the night, and the orchard kept its counsel. The river of water they'd coaxed that day ran true and the trees drank as men do who have been denied and then are given again. Their mouths opened like the mouths of birds at feed. A moth beat at the lantern and then fell into the grass. On the porch an old pair of boots was set for the coming morning.

Tomorrow there would be another measure and maybe another argument. There would be phone calls and forms and the neighbor's truck idled on the road like an expectation. But for that night the brothers had chosen to hold the land and the paper both in abeyance. It was a small salvation. It was also an admission of the simple truth that sometimes a man must not decide his life in a single sitting. The orchard, as if in answer, sent up a wind. It lifted the leaves and they whispered like a congregation dismissed into the dark.
