The hotel had been rebuilt. That is, the façade everyone remembered from the photographs in the papers had been restored, the cornice curved the way it had curved in the black-and-whites that ran beside the account of the fire, and the brass letters above the porte-cochère were the same letters, though polished, and the doorman’s uniform was a deliberate echo of uniforms in which other men had posed decades earlier. There was a plaque — a neat bronze rectangle — set into the low wall at the edge of the drive. The plaque, read by a woman escorting a small boy who was neither interested in nor old enough to understand what the plaque said, commemorated “those affected by” the event of which everyone else said “the fire.” The plaque had the obligatory government-sounding phrase about resilience and the private-sounding phrase about commitment to the community. A modest flame motif had been incorporated into the hotel's new logo, which appeared on the linen napkins at the reception, on the menus, and on the small cards bound into white ribbon that told the guests what to do if they wished to leave a message for the newlyweds on a little oak tree that, if the season had been different, would have been planted in the hotel's new "memory garden" out back.

I stood in the porte-cochère for a long time with my bag at my feet and watched a man who was probably the hotel manager — he was a man who seemed to have been trained in the proper gestures for such positions; he made gestures the way some people make motions at the dinner table, as though he had made them many times and could now do them without thinking — salute the car that held the bride's mother and then, as if that was his duty, come over to stand beside me and ask if I was checking in. He looked past me, then at my handbag, which had been a small cheap thing when I bought it in the winter after the fire and which now bore a discreet logo the way other people's handbags bore logos. He pronounced my name. He had been given my name only twenty minutes earlier and had learnt to say it the way a person who has to remember new names does, turning it over without the pleasure of memory, as if it were a word he was not sure how to place.

"No," I said. "I'm not checking in. I'm here for the wedding."

He smiled. It is a smile designed to carry you over something awkward. "Welcome back," he said.

The phrase came out as if it had been written on the hotel's stationery and then read aloud. Welcome back. I had not been back. I had not been invited back. It is a small, ordinary thing to be welcomed into a place that is not yours — it is what we do when we meet for the first time — but the phrase, in that instant and that place, felt like a summation. I thought of the housekeepers' room where we used to keep the brooms and the rubber gloves and the iron. I thought of the aluminum carts with their stacked terry towels the way a cook thinks of his knives. I thought of the night of the fire: the report that went out, the telephone calls, the people who said they had seen us all come down the back stairs, the people who had written letters to the paper about heroism in very public, neat sentences. I thought of how, at the time, people had said, "People did what they could," and of how the phrase had meant for some "people" certain names; and for the rest of us — not names. Welcome back, said the manager. Welcome back, said the old woman with the face that had been cut by so many years of address-book courtesy that it now seemed to be a permanent expression.

It had been seven years. It could have been ten. Time is a slippery thing in the way disasters are measured; anniversaries are various, and newspapers mark them for a reason: to give you a day and a context in which to feel something public and to forget the rest. I had been twenty-six when the hotel burned and twenty-six is a year that insists on clarity. Coming back as a wedding guest at thirty-three, later, was not a spectacle I had planned. I had been invited, which is to say I had married well enough, or at least well enough for other people's calendars to include me. The envelope had had the return address of my husband or his employer. It had the polite calligraphy of hands accustomed to writing other people's names. It had the small gold monogram that still, to me, carried the smell of iron and the red dust of the plaster in the stairwell after the fire. The card said: We hope you will join us in celebrating the marriage of — and it listed two names that belonged to people in my present life who had no business pasting themselves into my earlier one. I thought of that then, waiting under the porte-cochère, and of the way, when you have done work that is meant to be invisible, you measure your day in things like returned towels and bedspreads folded the same way each time, and then the world that invited you to be invisible decides, for reasons of money or memory or other necessity, that it wants to be seen to have been attacked and then to have recovered.

The lobby was quiet in a practiced way. It had been refurnished with an attention to the fact that people will look at chairs and benches before they sit and will choose to sit on ones that will show their legs to flattering advantage and that will not squeak at the moment of a toast. The new chandeliers had the cautious éclat of something that was meant to make a photograph. There were decorative urns with flowers so arranged that the florist's name had been given in the program. The staff — men and women with the faces and the wardrobes of people who had been told precisely how to stand — motioned me to a place against the far wall. They handed me a menu with their gloved hands, and there was a line on the menu in smaller print that said, discreetly, that we would be served family-style. "Family-style" is the phrase people use these days when they do not feel obliged to say "we cannot afford passed hors d'oeuvres." A violinist in a suit tied in the especially straight way violinsts' suits are tied now stood in a corner and played something old in a way that avoided, specifically, the fuller sentiment of the song.

I had nothing to do. I had been dragged to the ceremony like a guest at a funeral who is told that attendance is expected because attendance is the social thing to do now. This is a misperception. People drag other people to weddings for reasons known only to them. I had not been part of the wedding couple's inner group. I had been, in the small way of those whose history is the hotel, a fact to be cursorily regarded, the way a newspaper photograph is regarded when no one will be sued.

They seated us in rows in a ballroom that had once been three rooms and now was one great neutral space. The chairs were draped in white; the aisle had a scattering of petals, which had been bought in bulk and then thrown carefully by someone who had perhaps been tasked earlier in the day with rolling the napkins. The guests began to take their places. At every wedding there is a moment when people who have been given the task of looking important take up their positions, and there are other people whose occupation has always been to watch the position-taking. The first people to come in were the relatives — the tight little knots of women who say "we're related" in the tone in which "we are" is said by people who have been rehearsing a script for years. There is a kind of vernacular of relation. The aunt who has been to enough weddings arrives in the same dress pattern she has used for at least three previous such ceremonies. The cousins arrive in versions of what the bridal magazines have decided this season means youth and distinction.

I sat among them, though I did not belong. I had on an old dress that had been pressed in the way you press garments when you are leaving them in the back of a closet for years and then take them out and iron them briefly with the flat iron that is used for sleeves and cuffs. People looked at me as one looks briefly at an acquaintance at a party in which one has a seat and needs the illusion of knowing everyone. A woman two seats down asked me — the way people ask you at these events a question they already think they know the answer to — "Do you know the family?" Her mouth formed the question in a way that invited a shared collapse of memory into the present.

"Just the hotel," I said.

"That is oddly close," she said. It was a small observation and without malice. People are instructed to make small observations at these times. It is part of the American way of reframing the private as the sociable. I said, as quietly as I could, "I used to work here."

There is a moment in a conversation like that when the listener guesses at what will be offered. There are standard narratives: we are told there will be recollections of the fire itself, of the heroics, of the names used in the paper. People have those phrases ready: "a terrible night," "did you go home?" "Were you hurt?" The ceremonial responses. I had prepared nothing for that kind of interaction because I had not expected to be called into any category of heroism or pathos and because, privately, the memory of that night had lost its grip on any demand for public description. It had become, for me, an incident countenanced only on certain days when some odour or the sound of a ladder would bring it back.

"Yes," I said finally. "I was a chambermaid."

She looked at me with the steady, slightly pitying gaze you reserve for small confessions of profession. "Do you still do it?"

"No."

She went back to the program and then whispered as if to inform me of something I did not need to know but which had been given to her as a token of intimacy with the event, "I read about the lawsuits. They said it was in the kitchen."

It was in the kitchen. Everyone knows where fires start. The truth that gets told in the paper is always more manageable than the truth that actually happened. In the papers, there were diagrams; there were maps of the smoke's way through the building. The paper images were neat, geometric. They fit into a column with other columns of things we take the time to say we care about. In private, the event does something else: it makes the air in a certain hallway taste like strange things you cannot name; it attaches to certain sheets the scent of melted plastic. The truth of a fire does not lend itself to diagrams. You cannot map where fear is. You can only mark what you can point to later: a burn on your palm, a scar behind the ear, the way you sometimes put your hand to a window to check whether glass is hot.

The ceremony began. There was a man at the front of the aisle who read from something that sounded like a prepared text. He had been chosen because he speaks well and has an association with the couple. Most of what happens at such moments has to do, in a trivial fashion, with whose voice has been approved as the authoritative one. He said about marriage that it was "the most visible of vows we make," which is a phrase that made me think of the hotel's façade and how visible it had been in the photograph after the fire, its face split in half by the black lines left by smoke. He spoke of "safety" in the same sentence. He was unaware — or aware but courteously so — that "safety" is a word that, at certain moments, can mean the very thing that fails you. He lifted his hands the way all people who speak in front of crowds lift their hands: he tried to control the cadences and the small silences. The bride and groom were young enough that you could attribute their composure to training in social scripts and only partly to ignorance.

I watched the blond of the bride's hair, how it caught the light from the new chandelier. I watched the groom's face, which was the kind of face that leads people to take him seriously in a world where seriousness is, at events like this, a currency. I watched the guests and thought of the hotel's old constant clientele: men and women who favored certain rooms on certain floors because they liked the view at certain hours of the evening. Those rooms, the ones we cleaned each morning, used to have names for some of us because we did not call one another as familiarly as found in other workplaces. People who live in a house of the same city for many years know the rhythm of the seasons. The hotel had its seasons. Now the seasons were to be carbon-copied into promotional material.

At the reception someone stood to make a speech. Stand-up speeches are a surprisingly revealing measure of social relationships. They tell you a lot about hierarchy and the way authority has been distributed at a wedding. He began by thanking "the hotel management," which was to say the owner and the corporation that had purchased the property and then the architects and the designers. He mentioned the hotel only as a stage on which history had been performed; he had a script about gratitude and commemoration. He spoke of the "community's support" after the fire, which meant, in practice, fundraisers and perhaps a volunteer day or two. He used the phrase "we would be remiss" in a way that demonstrated he had been coached in public platitudes.

The hotel's name was used with care. Sometimes in the years following the event the name of a place becomes a symbol. The hotel had become such a symbol. When it had burned, there were people who said we had to rebuild to show the city we would not be cowed. There were others who said the hotel should remain as a ruin or a memorial or perhaps be developed into condos. There was a split between those who thought of history as a marketing opportunity and those who saw history as an unpleasing residue. The hotel's new developers had, predictably, chosen "marketing opportunity." The new hotel had a meeting room named "The Old Lobby." I had preserved that name in the way a teacher preserves a habit. We printed that name on the bill of the person whose card had been left in the washing machine the night the flames began.

There was a woman at the table next to me whose hands were small and restless. She had taken in every detail of the room the way people at these events take in small details in order to write notes later about what the event "felt like." She asked — in a voice as steady as any person who has been given a role on a committee to perform — "Were you here that night?" I had answered this question a dozen times in different contexts, choosing different tones to see which people would recoil and which would find their decorative sympathy useful. I am not a storyteller by practice; I am someone who watches how other people tell their stories. I answered her with something brief because in that moment I did not wish to be the story.

"I was on duty," I said.

They used "duty" in the paper reports too. The word had the dry ring of the civic. Duty, hero, evacuation. We had lines in the paper. We had quotations that read well because a journalist had arranged them. We had a photograph of a woman holding a child's shoe. We had a man in uniform praising "the staff's quick action." The quote was attributed to the chief. He had been photographed looking the way chiefs look in photographs — decisive, turned slightly, as if toward the future.

At the bar a man ordered two gin and tonics and a third for his friend who would be home tomorrow by himself. He was the kind of man who has to make practical arrangements at a wedding. He had a face that gave the reassurance of a person who has never been told no for reasons that would challenge his view of the world. He had been at the hotel the night of the fire, he said. He had a room on the top floor. He laughed a laugh which was the laugh that men like him use when asked whether they had a story. "It was quite a thing," he said, and then he asked me whether the press had been hard on the hotel. The press always wants a villain. The hotel had had a villain at the time: a cook with a cigarette in the wrong place, a wiring problem in a closet, a negligent supplier. The press favours villains because villains fit the way narratives like to resolve themselves.

"You did not see it from the outside," he said, "how terrifying that column of smoke looked. They made such a fuss you thought the whole block was going to go." He told the story in the voice of someone describing a spectacle in which he had played no part. Spectacle is a safe form of engagement. It allows the spectator to assume a position of moral neutrality, as if the fire had been an interesting but ultimately harmless event. Nobody who has been through one of those things believes it is harmless. It makes a new rhythm for your life. It makes your old habits seem like relics. You stop assuming permanence. You remember the way objects have the terrible habit of becoming useless when the air changes.

There had been people who, after the fire, wrote letters to the hotel. They wrote with that mixture of sanctimony and nostalgia common to the public. They wrote that the hotel had been a "beacon of civility" and that they were "grateful" to have been unharmed. They were grateful because they were. They were grateful because others had been less fortunate. Gratitude is a complicated commodity. It arrives in the postmarked envelope with the words "So sorry" in neat hand and sometimes arrives with a check. I had received a check once; it had the corporate letterhead and a figure that could be described as "compensation" and then the careful sentence about being "sorry for any distress."

People behave at weddings the way people behave in corporate apologies: they choose their language. The newlyweds stood and made their vows. The bride spoke with the sort of crisp modulation one learns at school for public reading. She mentioned "safety" in an entirely different register from the man at the podium earlier. She said "home" is where "we build together." People applauded, that muted applause that sounds as if it has been rehearsed. The phrase "we build together" is perfectly suited to a building that was built over the bones of a rumor. The guests were the kind of people whose houses were used in the magazines and treated as if they were one another's props. The bride's mother wept in a way one learns when one wants to emphasize feeling without being messy. She had, two tables over, a woman with an apron who said "wash the faces too" when she had none of her former authority now.

When I was a chambermaid the public did not frequently address us directly, save to say, "Good morning," or "Can you bring an extra bath towel?" There were exceptional instances: the child who gave me a drawing and then told me, in that frank way children have, the ways in which they wanted to remake the world; the old man who, at three in the morning, had once asked me whether I thought the hotel would survive another winter. He had been drunk but he had been truthful; his face had worn that particular honesty of the old who speak without artifice. After the fire he had sent a postcard. He wrote in a large script: We are still here. That was the old man's way of being kind.

At the end of the night there was dancing. The band played songs whose chords are engineered to get people to move their feet with the same emotion that they used to buy bread with when they were children. People began to move forward into couples. The ritual of dancing is a ritual of mutual acknowledgment. It is also an exercise in showing how well you know how to touch and be touched in public. The woman who had asked me if I knew the family asked me to dance. I said no, not now. I told her I wanted to walk around the hotel, so she nodded in that way that permits a person to exit a social obligation.

The hallway on the south side — once a corridor that had been painted a pale yellow and which had had a small alcove with a telephone — had been turned into an outdoor terrace with glass doors that opened to a view the developers called "panoramic." There were planters with grasses and forsythia arranged in the modern style that suggests "nature" without any discomfiting signs. I went out and leaned on the low wall and looked down at the street. The city was, that evening, luminous in a way I had not seen for some time. People stood about on the sidewalk, couples moving as if they might stop to talk. There was a small child with a balloon who had been given the balloon by someone in a suit who had explained the physics of helium incorrectly and then laughed.

A cigarette man came out and stood at the end of the terrace the way men sometimes stand at the margins of a wedding as if to be an observer of humanity and to be near the exit. He had a face that wore the slight tiredness of one who has been covering events for a long time. He asked me, without introducing himself, whether it was true what had been written about the fire. He said this in the voice of a person trying to find an editor's line that will fetch the public: "Was there negligence?" He liked the smell of his cigarette and the way the smoke rose. He liked the chance to ask a question; some people are paid to ask questions, and he seemed to exist in that category.

"You'll find what you look for," I said. It is a sentence easy to say and not so easy to live.

He looked at me, his face eager with the hope of the straightforward answer. "So it wasn't someone's fault. It was a tragedy."

"Everything is someone's fault," I said. "And yet things happen."

I had been saved that night by something that might be called luck and by the hands of a woman who belonged to another staff. She had a way of hauling men and guests through the smoke the way mothers take children from a bath. She had been one who had answered the bell on the day shift when a fireman had come in to ask for the room keys. Her name was, I realized, the name that people in the press could not pronounce, and so they gave her a name that had only one syllable instead of the two that it had. You could tell the difference between the way the newspaper pronounced her name and the way the union would. Names straighten themselves out differently depending on the ear listening to them.

People who survive certain kinds of events begin to notice small things. They notice whether waiters fold the napkin in a way that suggests a calmness about the room; they notice whether a man at the next table chooses to talk loud enough for others to show he is not ashamed, or if he keeps his voice lower as if he is narrating something intimate. Survival makes certain objects into tests. People who have been through an event judge other people's responses by how carefully they regard random dangers. An escalator that has a loose rail becomes something to be discussed the way one speaks of one's debt. A roast that is too dry becomes a metaphor.

Someone at the hotel staff table — an employee I had not known but who must have been given the job of supervising this event — came by and spoke to me. "Would you like to see the old staff room?" she said. She had the polite, exact way of addressing a guest that has been taught in corporate hospitality schools. I answered that yes, I would, and followed her behind the ballroom into a corridor the way a person follows a guide through a museum. The staff room had been turned into an office for the managers of events, but there remained, in the far corner, a shelf where they kept a small stack of linen and an electric kettle. The kettle was new. In the corner was the scuffed linoleum where, before the renovations, we used to sit and pull on our stockings and discuss with a small conspiratorial courtesy the events of the day. The woman who guided me put her hand on a chair to indicate the room had been used by a team. Her hand was like the hands of other people in the hotel: it had been manicured just enough for the occasion.

"Do you ever come back?" she said.

I thought of the quilts that had been charred on the roof of the building. I thought of the way the radios in the staff locker had been used to hear live reports and the way my hands had shook while I folded damp sheets. "Sometimes," I said.

She told me their story. She had been here two years, she said. She had a sister who worked in housekeeping at a neighboring hotel. She described a child she wanted to enroll in a private school and the way that would be possible if she got a promotion. She looked at me the way people look at those who have known the place in another version and said, and I remember the phrase because it was used as if it were a test of character, "We all do what we can, right?"

We all do what we can. That was what the papers said. That was what the chief had said. It is an insinuating phrase. It demands no particulars and claims the moral high ground because it admits the minimal virtue of effort. Meanwhile, people who keep house and those who change sheets will always know the specifics of what has been done to save a place. They will remember the child's small hand. They will remember their own breath, the thrumming of their hearts and the taste of the air. They will remember small favors that were either repaid or forgotten. Memory is not a courtesy; it is an accumulation. It piles up whether the world likes it or not.

The wedding ended with champagne and a small, orderly cutting of the cake. There was commendable decorum. People lined up to kiss the bride and tell the couple how fortunate they were. I was offered the cake. I ate it and felt — in the strange way food sometimes registers — the thin sugar of a memory: sugar is one of the things people give guests when they want to make them forget bitterness. I had a cigarette that night in the alley that ran behind the hotel with a colleague from my current work who had less of an interest in the hotel than in gossip. She had been told, as I had been, that "it was a nice event." She laughed in a way that suggested she had made an economy out of attending events such as this. People make economies out of social appearances the way they make budgets for their houses.

I left without making apologies. I had no public role to perform. The hotel manager wrote an email the next morning thanking people for their attendance and including a photograph of the bride and groom posed with the hotel's staff in front of the new façade. Everyone clapped for the photograph. The photograph, like other photographs, was flat and full of light. The staff were arranged in rows as if they had been cast for a film. The woman who had saved me was in the back row with her hand on the shoulder of another woman and with a smile that was precise. No one in the photograph looked as if they had been involved in trauma. They were the staff in corporate mode: present, helpful, photographed.

I did not write to the paper. I did not make a speech. I walked the block in the dark and thought, for the first time in years, of the way water had sloshed in a bucket outside a side door the morning after the fire, when someone had begun, quietly and without fanfare, to clean. People come to these occasions and they leave with photographs and the sense that something has been preserved. They forget the smell of smoke in the morning, the way the sun hits an ash pattern, the way one corner of a room will never look the same again.

Outside, the city was exactly the same as the city always is when you leave an event: somebody's late-night light framing a silhouette in a window, a taxi idling, a couple arguing in a way that was both private and loud enough to be useful. I stood on the corner and then, on impulse, turned back. I walked through the lobby and into the small corridor that had once led to the employee rooms. The door that had once been marked "Housekeeping" was now a discreet unmarked panel; a broom leaned inside. I opened the door and stood in the small, cool space where we used to hang our coats. There was a smell leftover from the kitchen — citrus cleaner — a managed scent. It tried to erase the other one. The room looked as if it had been lit by someone who had just arrived and planned to leave. There was a small photograph on a shelf that I could not place. It had been left by somebody or it had been put there by a designer who'd thought a small image of an old staff party would make the space look lived-in.

I touched the photograph and saw my own life as if through a lens that softened and rearranged the harshness. I had been called back for a wedding that had nothing to do with the people who remember me for what I did. I had been invited because I had a place now in a social network that had the power to include and to exclude. I had been part of a house that had been remade as a name. The hotel had been rebuilt. They had done what they could. The plaque with its small flame was a suitable monument, a tasteful thing, good for a photograph.

When I left again I felt that the hotel's new light would follow me for a time. The city's air tasted of the high-pollen summer. I thought of the old man's postcard and of the child who gave me a drawing. I thought of the photograph in the staff room and of the way people at weddings are given a role and a cue as if they were actors. I walked past the little park where, years earlier, there had been a man who could not remember his address and who had been helped into a cab by one of the hotel's doormen. The man later sent a postcard.

I took a bus home. There are certain acts in life that are still, for me, easier than speech. Travel is one of them. I thought of the way we keep small tokens — receipts, a photograph, a matchbook — as if these things may one day identify us to ourselves. My bag still had the ribbon the hotel had tied on the napkin with the couple's initials. I took it out and folded it. It was the kind of object made of nothing and everything. It would sit on my dresser for a while until I could decide whether to keep it or throw it away.

At home I made tea, an act that required no conversation. Hot water takes something from the tea bag and leaves something else; this is like memory. You steep something in warmth and time and later you have a taste. I sat by the window and drank the tea and tried to measure what the night had meant. It was a small thing, perhaps, to go back as a guest. It was a small, circular motion. People do what they can, and then other people do what they can to be seen to have done it. The hotel had been rebuilt. The plaque had its neat language. The staff wore new uniforms and posed in a photograph. The woman who had saved me smiled from a back row.

I thought then of the old front desk ledger that we had once kept; names piled in a slow, obedient orthography. Once, when I was making up a room for a guest, there had been the entry of someone famous, someone whose name made us feel as if our small work mattered in a new currency, and we would whisper about it. That day we did not whisper about fame; we whispered about the smoke's color and about how quickly it had moved. Then the ledger had been charred at one corner and then bound in plastic and eventually it had been taken away by the hotel's management as evidence, in the way one takes evidence, and put into a file with clipped edges. Institutions gather objects and give them a context. They do not always preserve the people who created the objects.

I put the ribbon on my dresser and went to bed. When I turned off the light my room did not smell faintly of smoke. The next morning the sun fell thin through my window, and I thought of the plaque, the photograph, the manager's smile. I remembered with a particular clarity a small thing: the sound of the old woman's shoes as she ran down the back stairs the night the alarm had gone. She had a way of running as if she were an ordinary woman who had been given a script of bravery by circumstance. I had learned then, watching her, how certain characters in life are made up of ordinary materials and then find a way to become necessary.

There are people, when asked about the hotel, who will tell you it was a symbol. Symbols are useful. They allow people to feel a moral position without delivering the specifics of a life. They simplify. They reduce the hard edges. I thought of the child who had given me a drawing and of the old man who had written We are still here. I thought of how remaining is sometimes the only gesture of repair you can make. The hotel had been rebuilt. This was an act of remaining on a grand scale. There are smaller, less noticed acts: the woman who sits with the baby while the mother takes a shower; the man who returns a lost glove; the person who, when an alarm goes off, remembers the location of a staircase.

In the weeks after the wedding I received a photograph in the mail from the couple. It was one of those professional shots that had the couple in the center, smiling in a way that could not be improvised. Everyone in the photograph looked as if they had been paused for a long exposure and then allowed to resume their lives. The hotel's façade was behind them, crisp and whole in the bright afternoon light. There was a hand-scrawled note on the back: Thank you for coming. We are grateful for your presence. The note was in a hand that had learned the politeness of gratitude. I put the photograph on the shelf.

People called me, in the months that followed, to ask whether I would narrate what had happened that night in a small way for a reunion, for a charity booklet. My name had not been used in the hotel's publicity. I had not been given an honor or an award. I had been in a photograph made to show that we were everything tidy and proper. Those are the decisions organizations make. They are not wicked decisions; they are deliberate, and they are made for reasons: they are made to secure insurance, to placate a board, to reassure investors that a brand will not be eroded by memory.

I thought of these things lying in bed late at night. The window to my room is the kind that gives only a sliver of the outside; you cannot see the hotel from it. From my window I can see the roof of a neighbor's house and a small stand of trees that will later be amber in the fall. The hotel's light is, for my part, one of many in a city. For the public it is a landmark. For me it is a place where I worked and where, one night, elements rearranged themselves and took from us a direct line of ordinary things.

You are tempted, when you tell such a story, to end with a line that will make it moral. People need that. They like a wisdom picture. They prefer an explicit conclusion. I had no such line. I have a photograph and a plaque and a small ribbon and the sense, which is itself a kind of truth, that I will always be admitted somewhere between being a guest and being an instrument. There is a difference. The hotel had been rebuilt; it would be turned, in time, into an inviting place with rooms to be reserved and a website with a carefully curated gallery. People who work there will say — as they say in every hotel — that hospitality means a certain generosity. They will mean something practical by it. I will choose to believe this. I will also remember the sound of shoes on the back stairs and the way the old woman said, with a voice that did not need to be heard by a camera, "Come on."

You survive an event because someone calls your name, or because someone grabs your wrist, or because the way the smoke moves gives you an accidental advantage. You are rescued by particulars as often as by any more general sentiment. We will go back to places to celebrate something that is clean enough to be commemorated. We will put our initials on ribbon and we will plant trees that have plaques next to them that can be read at quick glance and then set aside. The hotel had been rebuilt. That is what the city wanted. That is what the brochures will always say when they tell the story. But in the small hours of the morning there are moments when the facts — the scar on my thumb, a sound in the hallway — come to mind as if to remind me that the world does not entirely change its mind because a plaque and a photograph say so. The little things are obstinate. They linger. They will, in their own time, be enough.
